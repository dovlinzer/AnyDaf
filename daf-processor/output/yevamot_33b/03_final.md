# Yevamot 33b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Chiyya vs. Bar Kappara

### Three Prohibition Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, רַבִּי חִיָּיא אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. בַּר קַפָּרָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. קָפַץ רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְנִשְׁבַּע: הָעֲבוֹדָה! כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי: שְׁתַּיִם. קָפַץ בַּר קַפָּרָא וְנִשְׁבַּע: הָעֲבוֹדָה! כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי: אַחַת. בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּטוּמְאָה. רַבִּי חִיָּיא אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. בַּר קַפָּרָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. קָפַץ רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְנִשְׁבַּע: הָעֲבוֹדָה! כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי: שְׁתַּיִם. קָפַץ בַּר קַפָּרָא וְנִשְׁבַּע: הָעֲבוֹדָה! כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי: אַחַת. זָר שֶׁאָכַל מְלִיקָה, רַבִּי חִיָּיא אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. בַּר קַפָּרָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. קָפַץ רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְנִשְׁבַּע: הָעֲבוֹדָה! כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי: שְׁתַּיִם. קָפַץ בַּר קַפָּרָא וְנִשְׁבַּע: הָעֲבוֹדָה! כָּךְ שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי: אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a non-priest who served** in some capacity in one of the Temple services, such as the burning of a burnt-offering on the altar **on Shabbat, Rabbi Ḥiyya says: He is liable** to receive punishment on **two** counts, both because he was a non-priest serving in the Temple and because he desecrated Shabbat. **Bar Kappara says: He is liable** on **only one** count. **Rabbi Ḥiyya jumped up and swore:** I swear **by the Temple service** that **this I heard from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: He is liable on **two** counts. **Bar Kappara jumped up and swore:** I swear **by the Temple service** that **this I heard from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: He is liable on **one** count. They disagreed over a similar issue as well, the case of a priest **with a blemish who served** in the Temple by sacrificing a public offering, which overrides ritual impurity, **while** he was **ritually impure. Rabbi Ḥiyya says: He is liable on two** counts, both for serving with a blemish and for being ritually impure. **Bar Kappara says: He is liable on only one** count, for serving with a blemish. **Rabbi Ḥiyya jumped up and swore:** I swear **by the Temple service** that **this I heard from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: He is liable on **two** counts. **Bar Kappara jumped up and swore:** I swear **by the Temple service** that **this I heard from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: He is liable on **one** count. They disagreed over a similar issue as well. This dispute is with regard to **a non-priest who ate a** bird sin-offering that was killed by **pinching** the back of its neck. It is permitted for priests to consume such an offering, but it is prohibited to consume any other bird killed in such a manner, since it was not ritually slaughtered. **Rabbi Ḥiyya says: He is liable** to receive punishment **on two** counts, both for being a non-priest who ate a priestly offering and for eating something that was not properly slaughtered. **Bar Kappara says: He is liable on only one** count. **Rabbi Ḥiyya jumped up and swore:** I swear **by the Temple service** that **this I heard from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: He is liable on **two** counts. **Bar Kappara jumped up and swore:** I swear **by the Temple service** that **this I heard from Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: He is liable on **one** count.

The sugya on 33b picks up in the middle of a discussion concerning *issur chal al issur* — the question of whether a second prohibition can take effect upon an object or person already subject to a prior prohibition. Three specific cases had been presented: a *zar* (non-*kohen*) who performs the *avodah* in the *Beit HaMikdash* on *Shabbat*; a *kohen* who is a *ba'al mum* and performs the *avodah* in a state of *tumah* during a circumstance of *tumah hutrah b'tzibbur*; and a *zar* who eats a bird upon which *melikah* had been performed — an act of slaughter that is valid for *kohanim* but renders the bird forbidden to non-*kohanim* at the very same moment it takes effect. The question in each case was whether the individual transgresses two distinct prohibitions or only one.

### Misattribution Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי — בְּאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי חִיָּיא סָבַר: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל מִיחַיַּיב תַּרְתֵּי, בַּר קַפָּרָא סָבַר: לָא מִיחַיַּיב אֶלָּא חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree?** The Gemara suggests that they disagree with regard to the issue of **a more inclusive prohibition, and** this dispute pertains to the status of a more inclusive prohibition specifically **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Ḥiyya holds that Rabbi Yosei** is of the opinion that **in** the case of **a more inclusive prohibition, one is liable on two** counts. **Bar Kappara holds** that Rabbi Yosei is of the opinion that **one is liable only on one** count.

The debate was attributed to Rabbi Chiyya and Bar Kappara, both of whom swore that Rebbe had taught them his ruling — Rabbi Chiyya reporting that Rebbe said two transgressions, Bar Kappara reporting that Rebbe said one. The *Gemara* had concluded that their dispute was really about *issur b'vas achas* — prohibitions that take effect simultaneously — and whether Rabbi Shimon, who generally holds *ein issur chal al issur*, would concede that when two prohibitions arrive at exactly the same moment, both take effect. That explanation seems coherent. But it raises a sharp difficulty: if Rabbi Chiyya is correct that even Rabbi Shimon would agree to liability in a case of *b'vas achas*, from where did Bar Kappara ever hear that Rebbe taught only one transgression? The *Gemara* asks directly: *l'Rabbi Chiyya, Bar Kappara shikora kameshakeir* — is Bar Kappara simply lying?

### Kolel vs. Bas Achas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, קָמִיפַּלְגִי בְּאִיסּוּר בְּבַת אַחַת, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא, קָא מִישְׁתְּבַע לְאַפּוֹקֵי לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מֵחֶזְקֵיהּ. אֶלָּא לְבַר קַפָּרָא — לְמָה לֵיהּ לְאִשְׁתְּבוֹעֵי? קַשְׁיָא. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְבַר קַפָּרָא, כִּי אַתְנְיֵיהּ רַבִּי לְדִידֵיהּ — אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, כִּי אַתְנְיֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא — אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר קַפָּרָא, שַׁקּוֹרֵי קָא מְשַׁקֵּר? אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כִּי אַתְנְיֵיהּ רַבִּי לְדִידֵיהּ, תַּרְתֵּי לִפְטוּר אַתְנְיֵיהּ, וְאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל אַתְנְיֵיהּ, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this must be explained differently: Rabbi Ḥiyya and bar Kappara **disagree** with regard to cases where two **prohibitions** take effect **simultaneously, and** this dispute pertains to the status of these prohibition specifically **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** It could be suggested that when the two prohibitions take effect simultaneously, Rabbi Shimon would concede that they both take effect. However, this explanation raises a difficulty as well: **Granted, according to Rabbi Ḥiyya** it is clear why **he was swearing.** He did so in order **to exclude** the understanding of **Rabbi Shimon's** opinion that was based on **his presumed** opinion. As it was known that Rabbi Shimon holds that a new prohibition cannot take effect where another prohibition exists; this is the default understanding of his opinion. Therefore, it was incumbent upon Rabbi Ḥiyya to take an oath in order to emphasize that despite Rabbi Shimon's general opinion, in cases where the prohibitions take effect simultaneously, he would concede that both prohibitions can take effect. **However, according to bar Kappara, why was it necessary to swear?** It would have sufficed for him simply to make his statement, as it concurs with the default understanding of Rabbi Shimon's opinion. The Gemara comments: Indeed, this is **difficult.** One can raise a difficulty with this explanation from a different angle as well: **Granted, according to bar Kappara, when Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught him,** he taught him **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who holds that two prohibitions cannot take effect even in cases when they occur simultaneously, and **when he taught** the ruling **to Rabbi Ḥiyya** it was **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** Accordingly, Rabbi Ḥiyya did in fact swear that he heard that one is liable only on one count, but he wrongly understood that this was Rabbi Shimon's opinion. **However, according to Rabbi Ḥiyya,** who holds that Rabbi Shimon concedes that in cases where the prohibitions take effect simultaneously one is liable for both, was **bar Kappara lying?** His statement would accord neither with Rabbi Shimon nor with Rabbi Yosei. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Ḥiyya** could have **said to you** that this is what transpired: **When Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught him,** bar Kappara, **he taught him two** cases involving **exemptions,** i.e., the cases of a non-priest who served in the Temple on Shabbat and that of the blemished priest who served while ritually impure. Both of these are cases of more inclusive prohibitions, and he was informing bar Kappara that Rabbi Shimon holds them liable on only one count, because Rabbi Shimon holds that a prohibition takes effect where another prohibition already exists only if the prohibitions take effect simultaneously, but not in cases of more inclusive prohibitions. **And he taught him** the ruling with regard to **a more inclusive prohibition,** that in such cases there is an exemption, **and** this ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And bar Kappara** then **saw** the case of **a non-priest who ate** a bird killed by **pinching, and since it was similar** to the previous cases, **he mixed them together.** Then, sometime later, it seemed to him as though he had in truth heard all of these cases together from Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **but** when **he examined this** last case, **he found** that it could exist **only** if the events occurred **simultaneously.** **And he concluded** that **since** the case of a non-priest eating a bird killed by pinching can occur only in a scenario where the prohibitions take effect **simultaneously,** the other cases are instances of **simultaneous** prohibitions **as well. And** similarly, **since** he was taught **to exempt** one individual from a second prohibition in **those cases, these cases** were taught **to exempt** one individual from a second prohibition **as well.** Therefore, according to Rabbi Ḥiyya, bar Kappara did not knowingly speak falsely. Rather, he heard certain matters from Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and then mistakenly merged with them other matters. As a result, he confused the issue.

The *Gemara* resolves this by having Rabbi Chiyya explain that Bar Kappara did not misrepresent Rebbe deliberately — he misremembered. Specifically: *ki asnaya Rebbe l'didi, tarti l'ftor asnaya* — when Rebbe taught Bar Kappara, and said one is exempt (i.e., only one transgression), he was teaching only two of the three cases, not all three.

The first two cases — the *zar* performing *avodah* on *Shabbat*, and the *ba'al mum* performing *avodah* in a state of *tumah* — can each be understood as sequential. In the first, a person performs *avodah* and *Shabbat* then arrives; in the second, a person is a *ba'al mum* and then becomes *tamei*. In both cases, a first prohibition is already in place and a second prohibition descends upon it afterward. This is the domain of *issur kolel* — where the arriving prohibition does not render the object forbidden to a wider set of people, but rather expands the category of forbidden acts while something already forbidden to the whole world becomes forbidden in a broader sense. In cases of *issur kolel*, even opinions that are more permissive regarding *issur chal al issur* may not grant double liability. Rebbe taught Bar Kappara these two sequential cases according to the view of Rabbi Shimon, who says that even *issur mosif* does not take effect — and certainly *issur kolel*, which is a weaker category, would not — and therefore in these two cases one is liable only once.

The critical distinction is that *issur kolel* is a concept that applies only in sequential settings: first prohibition A, then prohibition B descends and encompasses a broader class of forbidden acts. It is incoherent to speak of *issur kolel* when both prohibitions arrive simultaneously — in a *b'vas achas* scenario, there is no "first" and "second," no earlier prohibition that could block a later one. Bar Kappara heard Rebbe's teaching about the two sequential cases, understood that in those cases (as *issur kolel*) one is liable only once according to Rabbi Shimon, and then — as the *Gemara* explains — inadvertently mixed in the third case of the *zar* who ate the bird on which *melikah* was performed.

### Third Case Confusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר: מִדְּהָא בְּבַת אַחַת, הָא נָמֵי בְּבַת אַחַת. וּמִדְּהָנָךְ לִפְטוּר, הָנָךְ נָמֵי לִפְטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And he concluded** that **since** the case of a non-priest eating a bird killed by pinching can occur only in a scenario where the prohibitions take effect **simultaneously,** the other cases are instances of **simultaneous** prohibitions **as well. And** similarly, **since** he was taught **to exempt** one individual from a second prohibition in **those cases, these cases** were taught **to exempt** one individual from a second prohibition **as well.**

That third case — *melikah* by a non-*kohen* — is categorically different. The moment *melikah* is performed, the bird simultaneously becomes a valid *korban* (and thus forbidden to non-*kohanim*) and becomes forbidden in the sense relevant to the act of *melikah* itself. This case cannot be sequential; it is inherently *b'vas achas*. When Bar Kappara added it to the set in his memory, the combined set of three cases could no longer be characterized as *issur kolel* operating sequentially. The only common denominator he could identify across all three was *b'vas achas*. He then reasoned: since Rebbe taught that in these cases one is liable only once, and these cases are all *b'vas achas*, Rebbe must have ruled that even *b'vas achas* yields only one transgression.

The *Gemara* states this explicitly: *v'aydi d'damya lei arvibihu* — because the third case was similar to the other two (all involving acts normally permitted when done correctly, all involving non-*kohanim* or disqualified *kohanim*), Bar Kappara mixed it in. *V'lo ashkach ela b'vas achas* — examining the merged set, the only explanatory framework he found was *b'vas achas*. *V'savar mid'ha b'vas achas, ha nami b'vas achas* — he concluded that since the *melikah* case is *b'vas achas*, the whole teaching must concern *b'vas achas*. *U'mid'ha l'pattur, ha nami l'pattur* — and since Rebbe had taught exemption, he concluded that *b'vas achas* generally yields only one transgression. He had honestly misremembered — not lied — and that misremembering is what the *Gemara* sets out to explain.

## Three Issur Types

### Mosif, Kolel, Achas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: זָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, וּבַעַל מוּם שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּטוּמְאָה — יֵשׁ כָּאן מִשּׁוּם זָרוּת, וּמִשּׁוּם שַׁבָּת, וּמִשּׁוּם בַּעַל מוּם, וּמִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין כָּאן אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם זָרוּת וּבַעַל מוּם בִּלְבַד. וְאִילּוּ מְלִיקָה שַׁיְּירַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the statement of bar Kappara from a *baraita*: In the case of **a non-priest who served on Shabbat and** the case of **a blemished priest who served while ritually impure, there is** liability **here, due to** the prohibition against serving as **a non-priest; and due to** the prohibition against desecrating **Shabbat; and due to** the prohibition against serving as **a blemished priest; and due to** the prohibition against serving after contracting **ritual impurity.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. Rabbi Shimon says: There is** liability **here only due to** the prohibition against serving as **a non-priest and due to** the prohibition against serving as **a blemished priest.** The Gemara notes: **And yet** the case of a non-priest who ate a bird killed by **pinching was left out** and not mentioned among the cases subject to this dispute.

Having worked through the Bar Kappara episode, the *Gemara* pauses to present the systematic framework governing *issur chal al issur* in its three recognized forms. *Issur mosif* is when the newly arriving prohibition expands the range of persons to whom the object is forbidden — it "adds" to the audience of the prohibition. *Issur kolel* is when the new prohibition does not change who is forbidden the object, but broadens the category of acts forbidden in connection with it, encompassing it within a wider class. And *issur b'vas achas* refers to the case where two prohibitions descend simultaneously, with no temporal priority between them — the scenario where the very question of one prohibition "blocking" another loses its footing, because neither arrived first.

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן שַׁיְּירַהּ? אִילֵימָא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הַשְׁתָּא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל מִיחַיַּיב שְׁתַּיִם — בְּאִיסּוּר בַּת אַחַת מִיבַּעְיָא? אֶלָּא לָאו לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וּבְאִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל הוּא דְּפָטַר, אֲבָל בְּאִיסּוּר בְּבַת אַחַת — מִיחַיַּיב. תְּיוּבְתָּא דָּבָר קַפָּרָא תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **According to whom was this left out?** That is, according to the opinion of which *tanna* would this question arise? **If we say** that this was left out **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** it is difficult. **Now** that **Rabbi Yosei** holds, even **in** cases of **a more inclusive prohibition, that one is liable on two** counts, as was stated with regard to a non-priest who served on Shabbat, **is** it **necessary** to state that in the case **where the** additional **prohibition** takes effect **simultaneously** one would be liable on two counts? **Rather, is it not according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** that this case was left out, as Rabbi Shimon would concede that in this case one is liable on two counts? Therefore, Rabbi Shimon **exempts** one from liability for the second prohibition in cases of **more inclusive prohibitions, but in** cases of **simultaneous prohibitions** he would **deem** one **liable** on two counts. The Gemara summarizes: **The refutation** of the opinion **of bar Kappara is** indeed **a conclusive refutation,** and his statements are rejected.

Having established that the debate of Rabbi Chiyya and Bar Kappara concerns whether Rabbi Shimon would concede double liability in a case of *b'vas achas*, the *Gemara* attempts to test this with a *baraita*. The *baraita* states: a *zar* who performs *avodah* on *Shabbat*, or a *ba'al mum* who performs *avodah* in a state of *tumah* even when there is a *korban tzibbur* — in each case there are two transgressions: transgressing as a *zar* and transgressing *Shabbat*, or transgressing as a *ba'al mum* and transgressing *tumah*. This is the position of Rabbi Yose. Rabbi Shimon, however, says one is liable only for the first prohibition — the *zar* status or the *ba'al mum* status — not for the prohibition that arrived afterward.

Crucially, the *baraita* discusses only two of the three cases. The *melikah* case — the *zar* who ate the bird — is absent. The *Gemara* asks: *l'man shiyarah* — for whom's sake was it left out? It cannot have been omitted on account of Rabbi Yose, since Rabbi Yose, who holds *issur chal al issur* even sequentially and even in *issur kolel*, would certainly hold it in a case of *b'vas achas* as well — *hashta Rabbi Yose b'issur kolel mechayev shtayim, b'vas achas mibaya*. If he permits double liability when prohibitions arrive sequentially, he would surely permit it when they arrive simultaneously. There is thus no reason the *baraita* would have omitted the *melikah* case on Rabbi Yose's account.

### Shimon Exemption

It must therefore be Rabbi Shimon for whose sake the case was left out — meaning that Rabbi Shimon would not have the same ruling in the *melikah* case as in the other two. Since Rabbi Shimon says only one transgression in the sequential cases, the *baraita*'s omission of the *melikah* case implies that Rabbi Shimon would say something different there — and the *Gemara* concludes that he would say *two* transgressions when prohibitions arrive simultaneously. *U'b'issur kolel hu d'pattar* — it is specifically in the *issur kolel* sequential scenario that Rabbi Shimon exempts from double liability. *Aval b'issur b'vas achas mechayev* — but in *b'vas achas*, he concedes double liability. This yields the conclusion: *tyufta d'Bar Kappara tyufta* — a refutation of Bar Kappara's position.

It is worth flagging that the force of this proof depends on accepting the *Gemara*'s prior conclusion that the distinction between the *melikah* case and the other two cases is precisely the *b'vas achas* character of the former. Given that, the omission of *melikah* from the *baraita* is indeed significant evidence that Rabbi Shimon treats *b'vas achas* differently — and would hold double liability there. Whether this cleanly refutes Bar Kappara's specific position (as opposed to simply proving that Rabbi Shimon agrees *b'vas achas* yields two transgressions) is a fair question, but the *Gemara* presents it as a refutation.

## Zar on Shabbat

### Which Act Violates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָר שֶׁשִּׁימֵּשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, בְּמַאי? אִי בִּשְׁחִיטָה — שְׁחִיטָה בְּזָר כְּשֵׁרָה. וְאִי בְּקַבָּלָה וְהוֹלָכָה — טִלְטוּל בְּעָלְמָא הוּא. אִי בְּהַקְטָרָה — וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַבְעָרָה לְלָאו יָצָאתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question with regard to one of the laws mentioned above. The *baraita* mentioned the case of **a non-priest who served on Shabbat.** The Gemara wonders: **In what** form of service did he perform in the Temple? **If** this is referring to a case where a non-priest performed **the slaughtering** of the sacrificial animal, there would be no prohibition, as the **slaughtering by a non-priest is valid. And if** it is referring to **receiving** the blood **and carrying** the blood to the altar, while this is a case where he would be liable as a non-priest performing the service of a priest, insofar as Shabbat is concerned **this is a case only of moving,** and it does not entail a violation of any prohibited labor. **If** he served **by burning** the sacrificial portions on the altar, then this discussion follows the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, **but didn't Rabbi Yosei say** that the prohibition against **kindling on Shabbat was** singled **out** to teach that one who lights a fire on Shabbat merely violates **a prohibition?**

The *Gemara* now turns to a focused question that arises from one of the cases in the *baraita*: the *zar* who performs *avodah* on *Shabbat*. The ruling is that he transgresses twice — once for acting as a *zar* and once for *chillul Shabbat*. But this prompts a precise question: *b'mai* — in what act exactly did he violate *Shabbat*? Every prohibited *melachah* he might have performed in the context of *avodah* is subject to scrutiny.

If he performed *shechitah* — the answer is immediate: *shechitah b'zar kesherah* — a non-*kohen* may perform *shechitah* of a *korban*. This is actually a striking *halachah*: even in the *Beit HaMikdash*, even on *Shabbat* when the *avodah* is permitted, even a woman may perform *shechitah* of a *korban tzibbur*. So *shechitah* by a *zar* is not prohibited, and therefore cannot constitute a *Shabbat* violation in this context. If it was *kabalah* or *holachah* — catching the blood or carrying it to the *mizbeach* — that is merely *tiltul b'alma*, movement within an enclosed space, which does not rise to the level of a *Shabbat* violation in the usual sense. And if it was *haktarah* — burning the limbs on the *mizbeach* — one faces the position of Rabbi Yose himself, that *hava'arah l'lav nitna*: making a fire on *Shabbat*, according to Rabbi Yose, is only a *lav* and not a *chiyuv misah*, since the Torah singles it out explicitly — *lo tivaru eish b'chol moshvoseichem b'yom ha-Shabbat* — and Rabbi Yose derives from that singling-out that it was separated from the general category of *melachot* to teach that it is less severe. Since the discussion concerns equivalent-weight transgressions (carrying *chiyuv* for *korban* purposes), *haktarah* would not serve.

### Par on Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: בִּשְׁחִיטַת פָּרוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל, וּכְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שְׁחִיטַת פָּרוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּזָר פְּסוּלָה. אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא זָר? אֲפִילּוּ כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט נָמֵי! שֶׁזָּר אֶצְלוֹ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Yaakov said:** The service referred to **in** this case is **the slaughtering of the High Priest's bull** on Yom Kippur, **and this is in accordance with the statement of the one who says** that **the slaughtering of the High Priest's bull is disqualified** if performed **by a non-priest,** for this service is designated specifically for the High Priest alone. The Gemara asks: **If so,** that this is referring to the slaughtering of the High Priest's bull, then **why specifically** state **a non-priest? The same would hold true even** for **a common priest as well.** The Gemara answers: **This is referring to one who is as a non-priest in relation to** the High Priest and not to an actual non-priest.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov resolves the difficulty: the case is not *Shabbat* in the ordinary sense but *Yom Kippur*, and the specific act is *shechitah* of the *par* — the ox of the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. The *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* is performed exclusively by the *Kohen Gadol*, and according to the opinion that even *shechitah* of the *Kohen Gadol*'s *par* requires the *Kohen Gadol* himself, a *zar* performing that *shechitah* commits a prohibited act — and *shechitah* is universally agreed to constitute a *melachah* on *Shabbat* (or *Yom Kippur*). So the case involves a *zar* who performed the *shechitah* of the *par* on *Yom Kippur*: one transgression for acting as a *zar*, one for violating *Yom Kippur* through *shechitah*.

The *Gemara* then raises a further difficulty: *mai irya zar* — why is the case specifically a *zar*? If the ruling follows the opinion that any non-*Kohen Gadol* cannot perform this *shechitah*, then even an ordinary *kohen* (*kohen hedyot*) would be disqualified. The answer given is *she-zar etzlo ka'amar* — when the case says "*zar*," it means someone who is a *zar* relative to this particular *korban*, i.e., anyone who is not the *Kohen Gadol*. The *Gemara* itself notes that this is a significant stretch in the language.

### Rav Ashi Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: מִידֵּי חַטָּאוֹת קָתָנֵי, אוֹ לָאוֵי קָתָנֵי?! אֶלָּא אִיסּוּרֵי בְּעָלְמָא קָא חָשֵׁיב. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְקוֹבְרוֹ בֵּין רְשָׁעִים גְּמוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi strongly objects to this:** The previous rejection that was based on the idea that the act of burning was not a full-fledged prohibited labor is in fact groundless. **Is** the *tanna* **teaching** how many **sin-offerings** would be incurred, **or is he teaching** how many **negative mitzvot** are being violated? **Rather, he merely** enumerates **the prohibitions** violated by a certain action, without detailing their severities, and therefore the desecration of Shabbat involved in the Temple service can be any Shabbat violation. The Gemara asks: **What practical difference does it make** how many prohibitions are involved? The Gemara answers: It affects the decision whether or not **to bury him among the completely wicked.**

Rav Ashi cuts through the difficulty with a sharper observation. He asks: *midi chataos katani* — did the *baraita* say anything about how many *chataot* one must bring? *O lavei katani* — or about how many *lavin* were transgressed? *Ella issurei b'alma ka chashiv* — the *baraita* is simply counting how many prohibited acts were committed, not calibrating *chiyuvim* of lashes or *korbanos*. The *nafka mina*, Rav Ashi implies, is not the practical consequence of multiple sin-offerings or floggings — it is the more abstract question of how grievous a *rasha* one is for having committed one wrong or two. This observation, while somewhat reductionist in its immediate context, preserves the force of the debate: when the stakes are higher — multiple *chataot*, multiple sets of lashes — the same logic would apply with weightier consequences. Here, the practical implication is simply the moral accounting of the transgression.

## Wife Exchange Case

### Chuppah Exchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנַיִם שֶׁקִּידְּשׁוּ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, וּבִשְׁעַת כְּנִיסָתָן לַחוּפָּה הֶחְלִיפוּ אֶת שֶׁל זֶה לָזֶה וְאֶת שֶׁל זֶה לָזֶה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חַיָּיבִין מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ. וְאִם הָיוּ אַחִין — מִשּׁוּם אֵשֶׁת אָח. וְאִם הָיוּ אֲחָיוֹת — מִשּׁוּם ״אִשָּׁה אֶל אֲחוֹתָהּ״. וְאִם הָיוּ נִדּוֹת — מִשּׁוּם נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **two** men **who betrothed two women, and at the time that they entered the wedding canopy,** after the betrothal, the men **switched this** wife **with that** wife **and that** one **with this** one, **then these** two men **are liable for** engaging in forbidden sexual relations with **a married woman,** since each of them had intercourse with his fellow's wife. The act of betrothal is sufficient to prohibit a woman to all other men as a married woman. Therefore, when the women were switched, both men transgressed this violation. **And if they were brothers,** then they are also liable **for** forbidden sexual relations with **a brother's wife. And if** these women **were sisters,** then they are liable **for** taking **a wife and her sister** as well. **And if they were menstruating women,** they would be liable **for** intercourse with **a menstruating woman** as well.

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishnah*, whose subject matter is closely related to all that preceded it. The *Mishnah* states: *shenayim she-kiddeshu shtei nashim* — two men married two women; the men were unrelated to each other, the women were unrelated to each other. *Uvi-sh'at knisatan la-chuppah* — when the time came to enter the *chuppah* (a euphemism for the consummation of the marriage), *hechlifu et shel zeh la-zeh* — they accidentally exchanged wives. This is reminiscent of the exchange of Rachel and Leah, except that here it is more serious: each man has now had relations with the other man's wife. The critical point is that the *kiddushin* — the formal betrothal — had preceded the *chuppah*: each man was already fully married to his own wife when the exchange occurred. Whether the *chuppah* was a literal canopy under which faces were veiled or a euphemism for the bedroom, the legal reality is identical: each man had relations with another man's wife.

### Four Transgressions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַפְרִישִׁין אוֹתָן שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, שֶׁמָּא מְעוּבָּרוֹת הֵן. וְאִם הָיוּ קְטַנּוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיוֹת לֵילֵד — מַחֲזִירִים אוֹתָן מִיָּד. וְאִם הָיוּ כֹּהֲנוֹת, נִפְסְלוּ מִן הַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** following these forbidden sexual relations, **we separate** these women from their husbands **for three months, lest they were impregnated** by that forbidden act of intercourse. Doing so makes it possible to distinguish a child born of these relations, so that he could be rendered a *mamzer*. **And if they were female minors and unable to bear children, then we immediately return them** to their original husbands. **And if they were daughters of priests, they are** thereby **disqualified from eating of *teruma*.** By engaging in illicit sexual acts, they were rendered forbidden to priests and disqualified from eating *teruma*.

The *Mishnah* then enumerates the transgressions. *Harei elu chayavim mishum eshet ish* — each man transgresses the prohibition of adultery with a married woman. *V'im hayu achim, mishum eshet ach* — if the two men happened to be brothers, there is an additional transgression of relations with a brother's wife. *V'im hayu achayot, mishum isha el achotah* — if the two women happened to be sisters, there is a further transgression, since each man's wife is the other woman's sister, making the illicit partner his wife's sister. *V'im hayu nidot, mishum niddah* — and if the women were *nidot* at the time, there is a fourth transgression. These four can combine in a single case: four transgressions arising from a single act.

### Minors Exception

The *Mishnah* adds: *ve-im hayu ketanot she-ein re-uyot le-yeled, machzirin otan mi-yad* — if the women were minors who were incapable of bearing children, they are returned immediately to their lawful husbands. This exception follows from the principle that *pituy ketanah ones ninhu* — the seduction of a minor is treated legally as coercion, regardless of any apparent consent. A minor's consent is legally meaningless; it constitutes statutory rape in *halachic* terms. Accordingly, even if the act were intentional on the man's part, the woman — being a minor — would not be rendered forbidden to her husband, since her status is that of one who was violated against her will.

### Accident Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֶחְלִיפוּ. מִידֵּי בְּרַשִּׁיעֵי עָסְקִינַן?! וְתוּ, הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה חַטָּאוֹת — אִי בְּמֵזִיד מִי אִיכָּא קׇרְבָּן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: תְּנִי ״הוּחְלְפוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: In the mishna it states: **They switched** this one with that one. **Are we dealing with** such **wicked individuals** that they would intentionally switch their wives? **And furthermore,** consider **that which Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** on this subject: **There are sixteen sin-offerings here,** four sin-offerings for each of the men and four for each of the women. Yet, **if** they had acted **intentionally would there be an offering?** Sacrifices are brought only for unintentional acts. **Rav Yehuda said: Teach** instead **they were switched,** which indicates that the switching was not done intentionally; rather, the women were accidentally mixed up.

The *Mishnah* also specifies: *u-mafrishin otan sheloshah chodashim, shema me-ubarot hen* — the women are kept separate from their husbands for three months lest they be pregnant, since a pregnancy by the wrong man would create serious questions of lineage. The *Gemara* engages with the word *hechlifu* — they exchanged — and asks whether this implies intentional wife-swapping, which would be incongruent with the rest of the *Mishnah*. Rav Yehudah rules that the correct reading is *huchlefu* — they were exchanged, passively, accidentally. This reading is confirmed by the *Gemara* from the clause requiring three months of separation: *ve-ei be-meizid, mi sharya* — if it were intentional, how could the women go back to their husbands at all? An intentional act of adultery renders a woman forbidden to her husband. The fact that the *Mishnah* contemplates their return — conditional only on confirming the absence of pregnancy — demonstrates conclusively that the act was unintentional, and the participants are liable to a *korban chatat* rather than subject to the severer consequences of deliberate transgression.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אִם הָיוּ קְטַנּוֹת שֶׁאֵינָן רְאוּיוֹת לֵילֵד — מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתָן מִיָּד. וְאִי בְּמֵזִיד, מִי שַׁרְיָא? הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: פִּיתּוּי קְטַנָּה אוֹנֶס נִינְהוּ, וְאוֹנֶס בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מִישְׁרָא שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This, too, stands to reason from the fact that the latter clause teaches: If they were female minors and unable to bear children, then we immediately return them. And if** this had been done **intentionally, would it be permitted** to return a woman who had engaged in illicit sexual acts to her husband? The Gemara comments: **This** is **not difficult** and does not disprove the notion that the act was intentional. Even if the act was intentional, these women would be permitted to return to their husbands. This is because the **seduction of a minor girl is** considered **rape, and** after **rape** a woman **is permitted** to return **to an Israelite** husband.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּקָתָנֵי: מַפְרִישִׁין אוֹתָן שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים שֶׁמָּא מְעוּבָּרוֹת הֵן. הָא לָאו מְעוּבָּרוֹת — שַׁרְיָין, וְאִי בְּמֵזִיד מִי שְׁרֵי? אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הוּחְלְפוּ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** this can be derived from a different point **that** the mishna **teaches: We separate** these women from their husbands **for three months,** as **perhaps they** became **pregnant.** From here it can be deduced: **Were** it to become clear after three months that **they are not pregnant, they are permitted** to return to their husbands. **And if** they had acted **intentionally, would this be permitted? Rather,** must one **not conclude from here** that the mishna is referring to a case where **they were switched** inadvertently? The Gemara summarizes: Indeed, **conclude from here** that this is the case.

The *Gemara* also notes, from the *baraita* of Rabbi Chiyya, that if there are four prohibitions and four parties (two men, two women, each potentially liable), and each brings four *chataot*, the total is sixteen sin-offerings — *sheish esrei chataot*.

## Rabbi Meir Framework

### Four from One Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּמַאן הַאי תַּנָּא דְּאִית לֵיהּ אִיסּוּר כּוֹלֵל וְאִיסּוּר מוֹסִיף וְאִיסּוּר בַּת אַחַת? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: יֵשׁ אוֹכֵל אֲכִילָה אַחַת וְחַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ אַרְבַּע חַטָּאוֹת וְאָשָׁם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to Rabbi Ḥiyya, who teaches that this case incurs sixteen sin-offerings, **who is this *tanna* who holds** that a prohibition takes effect where another prohibition already exists for **a more inclusive prohibition, an expanded prohibition, and a simultaneous prohibition?** If these men are brothers, the moment that one betrothed a woman, that woman was immediately rendered forbidden to the second brother both as a married woman and as his brother's wife. When the second brother betrothed her sister, this added the prohibition pertaining to his wife's sister. This is a more inclusive prohibition because, as a result of this betrothal, the second brother is prohibited from engaging in sexual intercourse not only with his wife's sister, who is his brother's wife, but also with all of her other sisters. When this woman becomes a menstruating woman, she is forbidden to her husband as well, which is an expanded prohibition. Consequently, the mishna includes examples of all three types of prohibitions and asserts that they all take effect in this case. Therefore, the Gemara wonders which *tanna* holds that in each of these cases the prohibition takes effect even where another prohibition exists. **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said: This is** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **There is** one who performs a single act of **eating** an olive-bulk of food, **and he is liable to bring four sin-offerings and one guilt-offering.**

With the *Mishnah* established as a case of *shogegin* (inadvertent transgressors), the *Gemara* raises a conceptual question. How can a single act give rise to four distinct transgressions? The case in the *Mishnah* involves prohibitions that did not all exist simultaneously at the moment of betrothal; they accrued at different times. The woman was first an *eshet ish* — a married woman — from the moment of *kiddushin*. She then became an *achot isha* — a sister to someone's wife — only when her sister was also married. She became a *niddah* at a different moment entirely. Each of these prohibitions arrived at a different time. This is precisely the domain of *issur chal al issur*: can a later prohibition take effect upon a person or object already subject to a prior prohibition?

*U-man hai tanna d'it leh issur kolel v'issur mosif v'issur bas achas* — which *tanna* holds that all three categories of *issur chal al issur* are operative? Rav Yehudah said in the name of Rav: it is Rabbi Meir — *Rebbe Meir hi*.

### Chelev Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל חֵלֶב, וְהוּא נוֹתָר מִן מוּקְדָּשִׁין, בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיְתָה שַׁבָּת וְהוֹצִיאוֹ בְּפִיו — חַיָּיב. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵינוֹ מִן הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** How so? This *halakha* applies to one who is **ritually impure who ate** forbidden **fat** that was ***notar* from a consecrated offering,** i.e., it remained after the time when it was permitted to eat it, and this occurred **on Yom Kippur.** One who did this is liable to bring one sin offering for eating consecrated food while impure, one for eating forbidden fat, one for eating *notar*, and one for eating on Yom Kippur. He is also liable to bring a guilt-offering for misuse of consecrated items. In this case, it was prohibited to eat the fat from the moment the animal was born. When the animal was consecrated, the prohibition against deriving benefit from it took effect on the entire animal, rendering this an expanded prohibition. When the priest became ritually impure and thereby prohibited from partaking of all sanctified foods, a more inclusive prohibition took effect. The moment the fat was rendered *notar*, it became prohibited to offer it on the altar as well, so that that is another instance of an expanded prohibition. Lastly, on Yom Kippur, an additional, more inclusive prohibition took effect because on Yom Kippur it is prohibited to eat all food, even that which is not consecrated. **Rabbi Meir says:** There is one more sin-offering for which he may be liable. **If it was Shabbat and he carried** this olive-bulk of food from one domain to another **in his mouth, he is liable** for carrying out on Shabbat. The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Meir: Liability for the sin-offering that you added **is not** incurred **from** violation **of the** same **type** of prohibition. He is liable for carrying out the food, not for eating it.

The proof comes from a *baraita* in Rabbi Meir's name: *yesh ochel achilah achas v'chayav aleha arba chataot v'asham echad* — there is a case where one can eat a single item in a single act and incur four *chataot* and one *asham*. The scenario: one is *tamei* and eats *chelev* that comes from a *korban*, and this *chelev* is *notar* (left over beyond its permitted time), and it is from the sanctified *mukdashim*, and one does this on *Yom Kippur*. Each of these elements — eating *chelev*, eating as a *tamei* person from *kodshim*, eating *notar*, eating on *Yom Kippur* — independently carries *chiyuv karet* and obligates a *chatat*. The *asham* comes from *me'ilah*, the additional prohibition of deriving benefit from *hekdesh*. That is five in total — four *chataot* and one *asham*. Rabbi Meir adds: if it was *Shabbat* and the person was carrying the food in his mouth while walking through a public domain, there is a sixth. The Sages respond: *eino min ha-shem* — that is not of the same type, since the act of carrying is categorically distinct from the act of eating.

*Tosafot* engages seriously with the Sages' objection, suggesting that perhaps Rabbi Meir's point was that the act of swallowing constitutes a kind of *hanachah* — the placing-down that completes the *melachah* of carrying — making it more integrally connected to the act of eating than it might appear. This remains a somewhat difficult argument, and the *Gemara* does not fully resolve it.

### Chelev Analysis

The *baraita* involving Rabbi Meir's *chelev* case contains all three types of *issur chal al issur*. Rashi explains the structure as follows. The *chelev* begins as a non-sanctified animal (*chullin*), and at that stage the *chelev* is prohibited in eating but permitted in benefit. When the animal is sanctified (*hukdeshah*), an additional prohibition of *hana'ah* — benefit — takes effect on the *chelev*. Rashi identifies this as *issur mosif*, since the sanctification adds the prohibition of *issur hana'ah* on top of the existing *issur achilah*. It must be noted that this is a somewhat unusual application of *issur mosif*: the classic formulation of *issur mosif* involves the object becoming forbidden to a wider group of people, whereas here the object was already forbidden to everyone — the new prohibition simply adds a more stringent dimension (*issur hana'ah*) to the same object for the same people. Rashi's categorization should be noted as contested.

The *chelev* then becomes *notar*, left over past its permitted time. At that moment, as Rashi explains, the object becomes forbidden to the *mizbeach* as well — it can no longer be offered on the altar — *migo d'itosif issura la-mizbeach*. Since it is now forbidden to an additional party (the altar itself), this too constitutes *issur mosif* in the sense of expanding the domain of what is forbidden.

The *tamei* person then arrives and eats. Rashi identifies this as *issur kolel*: the *tamei* person was previously permitted to eat non-sanctified *chelev*, and now, having become *tamei*, he is forbidden from eating all *kodshim* — not just this piece of *chelev* but the entire category. The prohibition does not change the status of the *chelev* but expands the category of things forbidden to this person, which in turn includes this piece. That is the hallmark of *issur kolel*.

### Chelev Analysis

What, then, is the *issur bas achas* in the *chelev* case? Rashi's explanation is acknowledged to be complex. *Tosafot* proposes that Rabbi Meir's addition of *Shabbat* alongside *Yom Kippur* provides the *bas achas* element: on a day that is simultaneously *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur*, the two prohibitions of *Yom Kippur* and *Shabbat* arrive at exactly the same moment and are not sequential. This is the case of *bas achas* — both *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur* are in effect simultaneously, so there is no question of one blocking the other, and both are independently operative.

### Meir Following Whom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, הָאָמַר: טָעָה בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה — פָּטוּר. אֶלָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to clarify the opinion of Rabbi Meir: **And in accordance with whose opinion is** the statement of **Rabbi Meir? If** you say that **it is in accordance** with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** this is difficult. **Didn't** Rabbi Yehoshua **say that one who erred with regard to a mitzva is exempt** from bringing a sin-offering, i.e., one who unwittingly performed a transgression while intending to perform a mitzva is exempt from punishment? In the case in the mishna as well, the men intended to perform the mitzva of marriage but unwittingly violated a transgression because the wives were switched. Therefore, according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, they should be exempt from a sin-offering. **Rather,** this must be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who holds that even if one mistakenly transgressed while attempting to perform a mitzva, he is still obligated to bring a sin-offering.

The *Gemara* then asks: *l'Rabbi Meir aliba d'man* — Rabbi Meir's ruling that all four transgressions in the *Mishnah* are operative: does it align with Rabbi Eliezer or with Rabbi Yehoshua on the separate question that must now be introduced?

## Mitzvah Attempt

### Yehoshua Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ טָעָה בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה פָּטוּר, הָנֵי מִילֵּי גַּבֵּי תִּינוֹקוֹת — דִּזְמַנּוֹ בָּהוּל, אֲבָל הַאי, כֵּיוָן דְּאֵין זְמַנּוֹ בָּהוּל — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish,** resolve this differently and **say: Actually,** this can be explained even **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua. When Rabbi Yehoshua says that one who erred with regard to a mitzva is exempt, this applies** only **to** the case of **babies** who were mixed up in such a way that on Shabbat the *mohel* mistakenly circumcised a baby whose time had not yet come for circumcision. Since the *mohel* did not fulfill the mitzva of circumcision, he should be liable for performing prohibited labor on Shabbat. However, Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him from a sin-offering **since** he is **pressed for time.** Because circumcision must be performed only on the day specified, he was rushed and anxious to perform the circumcision, and due to this sense of urgency he erred and thought that the proper time was on Shabbat. **In this** case in the mishna, **however,** where the mistake involved marriage, **since** the brothers were **not pressed for time** Rabbi Yehoshua does **not** deem the brothers exempt.

There is an independent debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua: *to'eh bid'var mitzvah patur* — one who makes an error in the course of performing a *mitzvah* is exempt from bringing a *korban chatat*. Rabbi Yehoshua holds this exemption; Rabbi Eliezer does not. In the *Mishnah*'s case of the wife exchange, the man who inadvertently had relations with the wrong woman was attempting to fulfill the *mitzvah* of marital relations with his wife — *onah*, the conjugal obligation. If Rabbi Yehoshua exempts one who errs in the course of performing a *mitzvah*, why is the man in the *Mishnah* liable to a *chatat* at all?

The *Gemara* asks: if the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehoshua, the four *chataot* should be inapplicable — since the man erred while attempting a *mitzvah*. It must therefore be that the *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Eliezer, who does not grant this exemption. Alternatively — *i ba'is eima* — even if it follows Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption is not absolute.

### Brit Milah Case

Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption is illustrated by the case of a *mohel* who performs *brit milah* on *Shabbat* and accidentally circumcises the wrong infant — the baby born on Sunday rather than the one born on Saturday, whose *brit milah* is duly scheduled for that *Shabbat*. The *mohel* was attempting to perform a *mitzvah* and erred in its execution, and for this error he is exempt from a *chatat* according to Rabbi Yehoshua. Why? Because *d'zmano bahul* — there is a time pressure involved. The *Shabbat milah* must be performed on *Shabbat* specifically, and that urgency creates a mitigating context. Combined with the fact that the person was genuinely engaged in performing a *mitzvah*, the result is that the error falls into the category of an exempted inadvertence.

Two factors thus converge: the attempt to perform a *mitzvah*, and the urgency of a time-bound obligation. One may understand these as jointly producing a kind of *ones* — an externally imposed compulsion that reduces culpability — or alternatively as the *mitzvah*-attempt providing a moral dispensation that can only operate when the degree of negligence is sufficiently low. The time pressure reduces the fault level to the point where the *mitzvah* exemption can take hold.

### No Time Pressure

In the case of the wife exchange, neither mitigating factor is present in full. The man was indeed attempting to fulfill a *mitzvah* — marital relations — but there was no *zman u'vahul*, no time pressure compelling him to act at that moment. Marital relations are not a time-bound obligation in the same way as a *brit milah* scheduled for a specific *Shabbat*. Without the time pressure, the fault level is not mitigated enough for Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption to apply. Accordingly, even according to Rabbi Yehoshua, the man in the *Mishnah* would be liable to bring a *chatat*.

### Terumah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי תְּרוּמָה, דְּאֵין זְמַנָּהּ בָּהוּל, וְקָפָטַר! דִּתְנַן: הָיָה אוֹכֵל בִּתְרוּמָה, וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ פּוֹטֵר. הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בִּתְרוּמָה בְּעֶרֶב הַפֶּסַח עָסְקִינַן — דִּזְמַנָּהּ בָּהוּל. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּאִיסּוּר בַּת אַחַת, וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And yet** in the case of eating ***teruma*, where one is not pressed for time and** nevertheless Rabbi Yehoshua **exempts** him if the act involved a mitzva, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 72b): If a priest **was partaking of *teruma* and it became known that he was the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*** and thereby disqualified from the priesthood and prohibited from eating *teruma*, **Rabbi Eliezer deems** him **liable** to pay **the** value of the **principal and** an additional **fifth,** like any non-priest who unwittingly ate *teruma*. However, **Rabbi Yehoshua exempts** him because at the time of consumption he intended to perform a mitzva, for he thought that he was a priest eating of the *teruma*. The Gemara responds: **But wasn't it stated with regard to** that mishna: **Rav Beivai bar Abaye said: Here we are dealing with** leavened ***teruma*** that was being eaten by the priest **on the eve of Passover, for he is pressed for time.** In this case, he would rush to eat it so that it would not have to be burned. The Gemara offers a different attribution of the mishna: **And if you wish, say** a different explanation: The mishna is not according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who deems one liable in cases of more inclusive prohibitions and expanded prohibitions. Rather, the mishna is referring only to cases **of simultaneous prohibitions, and in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

The *Gemara* raises what appears to be a challenge: the case of *terumah*. A *kohen* eating *terumah* — which is a *mitzvah* — subsequently discovers that he is actually a *chalal* (the son of a divorced woman or a *chalutzah*), and therefore disqualified from *kehunah* and prohibited from eating *terumah*. Rabbi Yehoshua exempts him from having to pay the principal plus the additional fifth (*keren v'chomesh*) that a *zar* who inadvertently eats *terumah* would normally owe. Yet there is no particular time pressure in eating *terumah* — one can eat it at any time. Does this not contradict the claim that Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption requires *zman u'vahul*?

Rav Bibi bar Abaye resolves this by positing that the *terumah* case in question was specifically on *Erev Pesach*: the *kohen* was eating *chametz terumah* before the deadline and was under time pressure to consume it before *Pesach* began, creating exactly the *zman u'vahul* needed. Alternatively — *v'ibayis eima* — the *Mishnah*'s case of the wife exchange may be read as *issur bas achas*, as will now be explored, and the entire preceding analysis may be reconfigured accordingly.

## Bas Achas Reading

### Simultaneous Kiddushin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא כּוּלְּהוּ מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לְהוּ: דְּשַׁוִּינְהוּ שָׁלִיחַ, וְשַׁוּוֹ אִינְהוּ שָׁלִיחַ, וּפְגַע שָׁלִיחַ בְּשָׁלִיחַ. אֶלָּא נִדּוּת הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** with regard to **all of** the prohibitions in the mishna, **you can find that they** take effect simultaneously in the case **where** the men **appointed** a single **agent** to betroth the women for them, **and** the women **appointed an agent** to accept the betrothals for them, **and** one **agent encounters** the other **agent.** In this scenario, all the men become betrothed to all the women the moment that the agents meet, and the prohibitions all take effect simultaneously. **However,** with regard to the prohibition pertaining to **menstruating women, how can you find** the case where both women become menstruating women at the exact time of betrothal?

The *Gemara* now offers an alternative reading of the entire *Mishnah*: perhaps all four prohibitions — *eshet ish*, *eshet ach*, *achot isha*, and *niddah* — applied simultaneously, making this a pure case of *issur bas achas*. If so, the *Mishnah* could hold even according to Rabbi Shimon, who normally denies *issur chal al issur* — since Rabbi Shimon concedes double liability in *bas achas* cases, as the *baraita* proved earlier.

The challenge is to construct a scenario in which all statuses applied at literally the same moment. *Eshet ish* and *eshet ach* are not difficult to synchronize in principle — if two brothers betroth two women simultaneously, both statuses (*married woman* and *brother's wife*) arise at the same instant. Simultaneity of *kiddushin* can be achieved through a *shaliach*: the two brothers appoint a single representative, and the two women appoint a single representative, and the two *shaliachs* execute the *kiddushin* in a single transaction — *paga shliach b'shliach*. In that moment, each woman becomes simultaneously an *eshet ish* and an *eshet ach*, and additionally an *achot isha* (the wife's sister to her brother-in-law).

### Niddah Simultaneity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר רַב: בְּשׁוֹפְעוֹת מִתּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר — לְאִחַיּוֹבֵי אִינְהוּ, מִתּוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר לְאַחַר שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר — לְחַיּוֹבֵי אִינְהִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram said that Rav said:** This is referring to a situation **where** the women **continuously discharge** menstrual blood **from within the thirteenth** year of the brothers **until after** they reached the age of **thirteen,** in order **to deem** the men **liable** for the prohibition pertaining to a menstruating woman. At the moment that the boys reached maturity and the betrothals went into effect, the sisters were forbidden as menstruating women. And the women were continuously discharging blood **from within the twelfth** year of the women **until after** the age of **twelve, in order to deem** the women **liable** for engaging in relations as a menstruating woman. Therefore, if this took place in such a way that on the exact day when the men reached maturity, i.e., their thirteenth birthday, the women reached maturity, i.e., their twelfth birthday, and at that moment they were menstruating, then all of the prohibitions would take effect simultaneously and they would be liable on all counts, according to Rabbi Shimon as well.

The *niddah* status, however, is more difficult to synchronize. Either the woman was already a *niddah* before the *kiddushin* — in which case the *niddah* prohibition preceded the others and the question of *issur chal al issur* applies in reverse — or she became a *niddah* afterward, in which case it is again sequential. A stopwatch-precise coincidence of menstruation and *kiddushin* at the same moment seems impossible to engineer.

The *Gemara* resolves this with a striking answer attributed to Rabram in the name of Rav: *b'shofet mitoch gimel l'achar gimel* — the girl was a *niddah* from before her third birthday, and that *niddah* status continued past her third birthday. In practical terms: the girl experienced her first menstrual flow before turning three, and it continued through that birthday. This means that at the moment she turned three years old, she was simultaneously a *niddah* and, for the first time, subject to the full legal consequences of the prohibitions that had already been formally created.

This solution exploits a fundamental *halachah*: sexual relations with a girl under the age of three are not considered *bi'ah* — a legally cognizable act of intercourse — from the Torah's perspective. Consequently, even though the brothers had formally married these girls (their father acting as *shaliach* to accept the *kiddushin* on their behalf), the prohibitions of *eshet ish*, *eshet ach*, and *achot isha* were technically in place but had no practical operative force — because any physical act with them would not constitute *bi'ah*. The *niddah* prohibition similarly was in place but not yet operative as a practical matter.

The moment the girls turned three, all of these prohibitions became simultaneously operative: the *bi'ah* prohibition of *eshet ish*, *eshet ach*, and *achot isha* took effect at that same instant, and the *niddah* prohibition — which had been in effect since before the third birthday and had never been removed by *tevilah* — was equally operative at that moment. Thus, from the standpoint of practical *chiyuv*, all four prohibitions descended simultaneously, constituting a genuine *issur bas achas*.

The *Gemara* further notes that this analysis applies to the men's liability — *l'chiyuvinuhu* — which takes effect when the girls turn three. For the women's own liability, the relevant moment is when they reach *bat mitzvah* (twelve years old). For the *bas achas* analysis to hold for the women as well, the *niddah* status must have been continuously in effect from before age three through age twelve without any intervening *tevilah* — an extended period of nine years. The *Gemara* accepts this as the structure of the case.

## Pregnancy Concern

### First Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַפְרִישִׁים אוֹתָן. וְהָא אֵין אִשָּׁה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת בְּבִיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: שֶׁבָּעֲלוּ וְשָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **we separate** these women from their husbands for three months, as perhaps they became pregnant. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** it known that a virgin **woman does not become pregnant from her first sexual act? Rav Naḥman said that Rabba bar Avuh said:** This is referring to a situation **where they engaged in intercourse and then repeated** the act, so that it is possible that the women were impregnated during the second act of intercourse.

Returning to the three-month separation requirement, the *Gemara* confronts what might seem a logical difficulty. If the wife exchange was an isolated incident — a single mistaken act — why should there be a concern about pregnancy requiring three months of separation? The *Gemara* invokes a widespread belief of the ancient world: *ein ishah misaberet bi-vi'ah rishonah* — a woman does not conceive from the first act of intercourse. If this is so, the one-time error should not produce pregnancy, and no waiting period should be necessary.

The *Gemara* resolves this with the answer of Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: *sheba'u v'shanu* — they did not err only once; they had relations a second time as well. That second act creates the possibility of pregnancy and justifies the three-month separation. The *Gemara* then notes that if they had relations twice, Rabbi Chiyya's count of sixteen *chataot* might seem too low — shouldn't there be thirty-two? The answer is that the count of sixteen is reckoned from the first act alone, and the question of how many subsequent acts there were (and indeed, according to Rabbi Eliezer, how many distinct *kochos* — thrusts — occurred within a single act) is left aside. The *baraita* counts from the initial *bi'ah*.

### Tamar Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְתוּ, הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה חַטָּאוֹת, תְּלָתִין וְתַרְתֵּים הָוְיָין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if this is so, how can one explain **that which Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches: Here, then, are sixteen sin-offerings?** If there were indeed two acts of intercourse, then **there** should **be thirty-two** sin-offerings, since each prohibition was violated twice.

Rava raises a challenge to the principle that a woman cannot conceive from the first act of intercourse. The *Gemara* will raise this challenge, but we continue with the answer to the sixteen versus thirty-two *chataot* question first.

### Hymen Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ — לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דִּמְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל כֹּחַ וָכֹחַ — טוּבָא הָוְיָין. אֶלָּא דִּבְכֹחַ רִאשׁוֹן קָחָשֵׁיב, הָכִי נָמֵי, דְּבִיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה קָחָשֵׁיב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **And according to your reasoning, that** Rabbi Ḥiyya's enumeration of sin-offerings applies to all acts of intercourse, then **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who deems one liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each and every thrusting** movement during a single act of intercourse, there would **be many** prohibitions that were violated. This would greatly multiply the number of sin-offerings required. **Rather,** it must be that Rabbi Ḥiyya, with regard to Rabbi Eliezer's opinion, **is enumerating** only **the first thrusting** movement **in the intercourse. And so too,** with regard to the Rabbis' opinion, **he is enumerating** only **the first act of intercourse.** If there were two acts of intercourse, however, the number of sin-offerings would be doubled. **Rava said to Rav Naḥman:**

Rav answers: the discussion here doesn't address the specific case details about how Tamar conceived, but rather focuses on establishing the general principle about pregnancy and the counting of transgressions. The continuation with Rava's challenge will address the Tamar question directly.

## Zera Le-Vatalah

### Nursing Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* [The Talmud does not appear to discuss the nursing woman teaching in the provided Sefaria text for 33b. This section appears to relate to teachings that occur in a different part of the Talmud or may be a continuation beyond the scope of 33b.]

The *Gemara* now cites the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer regarding a nursing woman. The discussion of *zera l'vatalah* and related topics appears to extend beyond the daf provided in the source material.

### Withdrawal Critique

The Sages respond sharply to any practice involving early withdrawal or non-standard intercourse that results in the destruction of seed, calling it *zera l'vatalah* — the destruction of seed — which is prohibited.

### Tamar Proof

The *Gemara* connects the case of Tamar and Judah to the discussion of destroyed seed and external emission. Since Onan's sin is described with specific language about seed being destroyed, the comparison to practices involving withdrawal requires careful analysis of how the transgressions are similar and different.

The *Gemara* addresses how to understand Tamar's situation and Er and Onan's actions in the context of the laws regarding proper marital relations and the prohibition of *zera l'vatalah*.

*Tosafot* engages directly with the tensions in the *Gemara* regarding when *zera l'vatalah* is prohibited and under what circumstances it might be permitted, presenting multiple interpretive approaches.

### Rama's Ruling

This discussion leads to important *halachic* conclusions about the parameters of marital relations and the specific intent required for *zera l'vatalah* to be prohibited. The *Rama* rules in accordance with careful interpretation of *Tosafot*, establishing that the prohibition of *zera l'vatalah* is not triggered by the place or manner of seminal emission but only by the intent and habitual purpose of destroying seed to avoid procreation. This ruling is fundamental to understanding the *halachic* parameters of marital intimacy, and represents the authoritative *Ashkenazic* position on the question.
