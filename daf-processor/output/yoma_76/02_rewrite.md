# Yoma 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Manna Properties

### Dew Layers

The daf opens in the middle of the Talmudic discussion of the *man*, picking up from the contradiction between two verses. One verse states *v'ta'al shichvat hatal* — the layer of dew rose up in the morning — implying the *man* rested atop the dew. Another verse states *u'veredet hatal alav* — when the dew descended, it descended upon it — implying the *man* was beneath the dew. Rav Yosef bar Hanina resolves the contradiction: *tal milemala v'tal milmata* — there was dew both above and below. The *man* came packaged, as it were, in a protective envelope of dew. The Gemara adds that it was *domeh k'mi shemunach b'kufsa* — like something placed inside a box. It was not merely food; it was food presented beautifully, encased and protected.

### Machaspas Nature

The Torah describes the *man* as *dak mechuspas* — thin, like hoarfrost. The Gemara attempts to unpack the meaning of *mechuspas*. Reish Lakish reads it as a mnemonic: *nimoach* (dissolved) from the *mechas* portion, and *pas* from the hand — meaning it was something that would dissolve in the palm of one's hand. It melted upon contact, which raises the practical question of how one ate it at all, though the Gemara does not resolve this directly.

### 248 Limbs

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different reading of *mechuspas*: it refers to something *shenivla b'matayim arba'im u'shmoneh eivarim* — that was absorbed into the 248 limbs of the body. The *man* was so perfectly digested that it left no waste; it was entirely absorbed. The specific mention of the number 248 signals a *gematria*. The letters of *mechuspas* — two *samechs* (60 each = 120), *peh* (80), *mem* (40), and *chet* (8) — yield 248. The difficulty is that *mechuspas* as written appears to contain an extra *vav*, which would throw off the count. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds: *mechuspas ktiv* — it is written without the *vav*. Whether that is in fact the case in the Torah scroll is a matter for verification.

## Manna Spiritual

### Angels' Bread

The *tana'im* debate the meaning of the verse in Tehillim describing the *man* as *lechem abirim* — the bread of the strong ones. Rabbi Akiva interprets *abirim* as referring to the angels: *lechem shemalachei hashareit ochlim oto* — bread that the ministering angels eat. The *man* was thus angelic food, heavenly sustenance brought down to earth.

### Yishmael Critique

When this teaching was reported to Rabbi Yishmael, he sent back a pointed response: *Akiva ta'ita* — Akiva, you have erred. *Vechi malachei hashareit ochlim lechem?* — Do the ministering angels eat bread? The Torah itself testifies that Moshe Rabbeinu did not eat bread during his forty days on Har Sinai, and certainly angels do not eat.

### Limbs & Spirit

Rabbi Yishmael therefore reinterprets *abirim*: *al tikri abirim ela eivarim* — do not read "the strong ones" but "the limbs." *Lechem shenivla b'matayim arba'im u'shmoneh eivarim* — bread absorbed into the 248 limbs. This loops back to Rabbi Yochanan's teaching. It is worth noting that *eivarim* itself is close numerically — 253 — making it not quite a clean *gematria*, though the proximity is suggestive.

One might ask what Rabbi Akiva truly intended. This appears to tie into the broader methodological divide between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Yishmael operates on the principle of *dibra Torah kilshon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in human language — whereas Rabbi Akiva derives meaning from every detail of the text. Rabbi Akiva may have been trying to bridge heaven and earth: the eating of the *man* was not merely physical nutrition but something that brought one closer to a heavenly mode of existence. There is a spiritual dimension to the *man*, not merely a physical one.

### Malbim View

This reading gains support from the *Malbim*, who addresses the *man* directly and identifies two distinct dimensions within it. There is *lechem min hashamayim* — the physical aspect, which was equal for all — and a spiritual dimension reflected in the verse *vayilketu hamarbeh v'hamam'it*: those who were spiritually elevated received more in that dimension, while the physical portion remained uniform for all. Whether this constitutes the *pshat* of the verse is debatable, but it captures something important about what Rabbi Akiva is pointing toward: the *man* as spiritual sustenance, not merely physical nourishment, and the sense of dependence on God that eating it was meant to cultivate.

## Waste Problem

### Shovel Law

If the *man* was so completely absorbed into the body's 248 limbs that it left no waste, the Gemara faces an obvious difficulty: the Torah commands the Israelites *v'yated tihye lecha al azenecha* — you shall have a shovel upon your implements — to go out and dig when relieving oneself outside the camp. Why would such a law be necessary if the *man* left no residue? The Gemara answers: *devarim shetagrei umot ha'olam mochrin lahem* — the waste came not from the *man* itself but from other foods they purchased from merchants of the nations at the oases and encampments along the way. Of course, this invites the further question of why they needed to purchase food at all when they had the *man*. The answer, already established earlier in the tractate, is that despite the *man*'s miraculous capacity to taste like anything desired, they still craved the experience of other foods — real bread, familiar textures and appearances.

### Manna Abates

Rabbi Elazar ben Prata goes further. He says that even the food purchased from the nations — *af devarim shetagrei umot ha'olam mochrin lahem, man mefigan* — the *man* would abate and neutralize even those other foods, correcting their more mundane digestive properties. On this view, the miraculous absorption extended even to non-*man* food, as long as the *man* retained its special status.

### Sin & Loss

This leads to a sharp question: if even that was true, what then is the purpose of the shovel law? The Gemara answers: *leachar shesarchu* — after they sinned. Rashi explains that when the Israelites called the *man* *lechem hakelokel* — the cursed bread, the vile bread — it lost its miraculous properties. Their complaint degraded the food itself. They had something of unparalleled wonder, and their dissatisfaction caused it to fall from its level.

### 3 Parsa Walk

God's response, as the Gemara presents it, is striking. God says: I intended for you to be *kehamalachei hasharet* — like the ministering angels. Note the irony: this is precisely the image that Rabbi Yishmael had objected to when Rabbi Akiva used it. Yet now the Gemara itself employs it, as God's own description of what the Israelites could have been. Because they failed to appreciate this, *achshav ani matriach otam shalosh parsaot* — now I will burden them with a three-*parsah* walk to relieve themselves. Rabbah bar bar Chana testifies that he personally saw the distance between Beit HaYeshimot and Avel HaShittim — the two ends of the encampment — and it was indeed three *parsaot*, twelve *mil*, approximately twelve kilometers.

### Latrine Placement

A *braita* adds that the Israelites could not relieve themselves in front of or to the sides of the camp, because the *anan* — the pillar of cloud — might move in that direction. They were required to go only behind the camp, in the direction from which they had come, since it was known the nation would not retrace its steps. This meant that someone stationed at the far front of the camp might have to walk the full three *parsaot* to reach the designated area behind the camp. This is, to put it plainly, a startling ruling. Every other miracle described here is one thing, but making a person walk such a distance for such a basic need is genuinely difficult to accept at face value.

## Manna Complaint

### Soul Dried

The Gemara cites the Israelites' complaint: *v'atah nafshenu yeveishah, ein kol, bilti el haman eineinu* — now our soul is dried up, there is nothing, nothing but the *man* before our eyes. Rashi's comment here is particularly evocative: *man bashacharit, man ba'arvit, man hayom, man emesh, man de'etmol* — *man* in the morning, *man* in the evening, *man* today, *man* yesterday, *man* the day before. The monotony was existentially unbearable for them.

### Bloating Fear

But the Gemara probes deeper into their complaint. *Assi man ze shetifach bimeihem* — they could not accept the miraculous nature of the *man* because they feared it would bloat and expand inside them. *Klum yesh yelud isha shemachnis v'eino motzi* — is there any child of a woman, any human being, who can consume without eventually expelling? Their inability to perceive the miracle as a miracle, their anxiety about what was happening inside their bodies — this was the essence of their complaint, not merely boredom with the taste.

### Joshua Portion

The Gemara revisits Rabbi Yishmael's response to a related matter, and this section introduces a new *davar acher*: *lechem abirim achal ish zeh Yehoshua* — the *ish* who ate the bread of the strong ones was Yehoshua. *Sheyorad lo man kineged kol Yisrael* — his portion of *man* was equivalent in quantity to the entire Jewish people. The derivation uses a *gezerah shavah*: the word *ish* appears here, and *ish* appears in the verse describing Yehoshua bin Nun — *ish asher ruach bo* — a man in whom there is spirit. Rashi reads this quite literally: every Israelite received one *omer*, and before Yehoshua's tent was deposited the equivalent of two million *omers*. The evident symbolism is that the leader is *kineged kol hador* — the weight of the entire generation rests upon him; he represents them all. It is notable, though, that the Gemara singles out Yehoshua, who had not yet formally assumed leadership.

The Gemara asks: why not derive this about Moshe instead, of whom it also says *veha'ish Moshe anav me'od*? The answer is technical: *danin ish me'ish v'ein danin ish mizeh ha'ish* — one derives a *gezerah shavah* using the plain word *ish*, not the expanded form *zeh ha'ish*, which is a different formulation.

## Daily Trust

### Why Daily?

The students of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai posed the following question: *mipnei mah lo yarad lahem leYisrael man pa'am achat beshanah?* — why was the *man* not provided once a year? Surely it would have been simpler to deliver a full year's supply at once rather than requiring the Israelites to go out and collect it each morning.

### King Analogy

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai responds with an analogy. A king who provides his son with a full year's food budget will see that son only once a year — when the next year's payment comes due. But a king who provides only a daily allowance will be visited by his son every single day. *Af Yisrael* — so too the Jewish people: a father with four or five children *hayah do'eg v'omer, shema lo yered man lemachar* — would be anxious each evening, fearing the *man* might not descend the next morning, that his whole family would starve. *Nimtzu kulam mechavnim et libam la'avihem shebashamayim* — and so every single day they turned their hearts toward their Father in Heaven.

This is, of course, the *pshat* of the relevant verses. The Torah states *vaya'ancha vayar'ivacha vayachilcha et haman... anotcha ulenasotcha* — He afflicted you, and made you hungry, and then fed you the *man*, in order to afflict you and test you. And the famous conclusion: *ki lo al halechem levado yichyeh ha'adam ki al kol motza pi Hashem yichyeh ha'adam* — man does not live by bread alone but by all that comes forth from the mouth of God. The daily collection — *veyilketu devar yom beyomo lema'an anasenu hayelech betorati im lo* — was itself a test of whether they would follow God's Torah.

### Anxiety vs Faith

Yet the Gemara's framing is worth examining carefully. The *mashal* of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai emphasizes anxiety — the daily uncertainty, the fear that the *man* might not come — as the mechanism producing religious devotion. This is one possible reading of the *pesukei*m: uncertainty and worry drive the Israelites to keep their hearts directed toward God.

But there is a meaningfully different reading. The deeper purpose of the *man*'s daily provision may have been not to induce anxiety but to cultivate genuine *emunah* and *bitachon* — faith and trust. The prohibition on gathering extra, the guarantee that *Erev Shabbat* would yield a double portion, the fact that the *man* did not appear on *Shabbat* — all of these called for active trust, not merely fearful dependence. The person of developed faith does not wake up each morning anxious about whether the *man* will come; rather, he has internalized the truth that God provides, and he goes out confident. These are related dispositions — both involve daily orientation toward God — but they represent significantly different religious personalities. One model aims to keep a person perpetually nervous as an instrument of behavioral compliance; the other aims at genuine, settled faith as the goal of religious life. The *pesukei*m arguably point more toward the latter, even if the Gemara's *mashal* foregrounds the former.

### Fresh & Light

The Gemara offers two additional explanations for daily provision, introduced by *davar acher*. First: *she-hayu ochlim oto k'she-hu cham* — so that they would eat the *man* freshly, like warm bread delivered each morning. The experiential reality of fresh bread matters, even if a miracle could theoretically preserve it. Second: *mipnei masa ha-derech* — so they would not have to carry a full year's supply across the wilderness. These explanations are notably more modest in their spiritual ambition. They speak to the aesthetic and practical dimensions of the experience rather than the theological message of dependency. Compared to the first explanation, they represent a significant shift in register.

## 60 Amot Height

### Height Claim

The Gemara introduces a scene: Rabbi Tarfon, Rabbi Yishmael, and several elders were sitting together engaged in *Parashat HaMan*. Among them was Rabbi Elazar HaModai. He spoke up and declared: *man sheyarad leYisrael hayah gavo'ah shishim amah* — the *man* that descended for the Jewish people was sixty *amot* high. This is in the genre of wonder-descriptions that the Gemara has been accumulating: pearls came with it, diamonds came with it, and now it stood ninety to a hundred feet tall.

### Tarfon Challenge

Rabbi Tarfon immediately challenged him: *Modai, ad matai atah megavev devarim u'mevi aleinu?* — how long will you pile up words and pile things upon us? Where are you getting this from? The challenge is pointed: Rabbi Elazar HaModai's claim sounds like aggadic embellishment without textual grounding.

### Flood Parallel

Rabbi Elazar HaModai responds: *Rabbi, mikra ani doresh* — I derive it from a verse. His argument begins with the flood narrative. The Torah states that during the flood *chamesh esreh amah milemala gavru hamayim va-yechassu heharim* — the waters rose fifteen *amot* above and covered the mountains. The immediate question is: fifteen *amot* above what? If measured from the valley floor, the water level would differ from place to place and the *tevah* could not navigate a sea of inconsistent depths. *Ve-khi mayim shavin shavin kayamin?* — do waters stand at equal levels from different measurements simultaneously?

The answer is that first, *nivke'u kol ma'aynot tehom rabah* — all the springs of the great deep burst open, filling the world until the waters reached the summit of every mountain on earth, leveling off at the height of the highest peak. Only then did the floodwaters rise an additional fifteen *amot* above that level. Thus, the fifteen *amot* were measured above the mountaintops — which means the total volume of water was vastly greater than fifteen *amot*, encompassing the full height of all the mountains plus fifteen more. This digression is necessary for Rabbi Elazar HaModai's argument, because the fifteen *amot* represents the increment above the highest point, which will become the unit of comparison.

Having established this, the Gemara now invokes the principle: *eizo mida meruba, mida tova o mida puranyut?* — which is greater, God's measure of goodness or His measure of punishment? The answer is well-established: the measure of goodness exceeds punishment. This is demonstrated by comparing *poked avon avot al banim... al shileshim ve'al rive'im* — punishment extending to four generations — with *notzer chesed la'alafim* — lovingkindness extending to two thousand generations.

### Window-Door Ratio

With this principle established, the Gemara compares the language used for the flood with the language used for the *man*. By the flood: *va'arubot hashamayim niftachu* — the windows of heaven were opened. By the *man*: *vayitzav shechakim mima'al, va-daltei shamayim patach* — God commanded the heavens above and opened the doors of heaven, *vayamter aleihem man leechol* — and rained down *man* upon them to eat. The flood opened windows; the *man* opened doors.

How many window-panes fit in a door? The Gemara calculates: four. Two doors thus equal eight window-panes. There were two *arubot* opened for the flood and two doors opened for the *man*, but since each door contains four windows, the two doors equal eight window-equivalents — four times the two windows of the flood. Since the flood waters rose fifteen *amot* above the mountaintops, and the *man* was four times as great, the *man* reached sixty *amot*. *Venimtza man leYisrael gavo'ah shishim amah* — thus the *man* stood sixty *amot* high.

### Visible to Kings

A *baraita* cites Isi ben Yehuda with an alternative formulation: *man sheyarad leYisrael haya mitgaber ve'oleh ad she'ro'u oto kol malchei mizrach uma'arav* — the *man* that descended for Israel rose so high that all the kings of East and West could see it. This is derived from the verse in Tehillim: *ta'aroch lefanai shulchan neged tzorerai* — You have set a table for me before my enemies. The table that God set was the *man*, visible to all the opposing kings of the world. The Gemara's discussion of this verse leads to a brief tangent: *Abaye* notes that *kosi revaya* — my cup overflows — where *revaya* in *gematria* equals *resh* (200) + *vav* (6) + *heh* (5) + *yud* (10) = 221. Thus the cup of David HaMelech in the world to come will hold 221 *log*.

### Rain Objection

The Gemara now raises objections against Rabbi Elazar HaModai's derivation. First: *hatam b'arba'im yomin* — there, by the flood, the rain fell over forty days. *Hacha b'chad sha'ata* — here, the *man* descended in a single moment. Even if it fell at a greater rate than the rain, accumulation takes time; what falls in one moment cannot equal what accumulated over forty days. Second: *hatam lechulei alma* — there, the rain fell over the entire world. *Hacha leYisrael lechodei* — here, the *man* fell for Israel alone. The scale is incomparable. Moreover, the Gemara adds, there was more surplus created by the flood waters, which themselves diminished over time, whereas the *man* was collected and consumed.

Nevertheless, the Gemara concludes that Rabbi Elazar HaModai's derivation stands on its own terms — *peticha peticha gamar* — he infers from the language of opening in one place to the language of opening in the other, and on that basis derives the sixty *amot*. The objections challenge the magnitude of the comparison but do not dislodge the hermeneutical move.

## 5 Afflictions

### 5 vs 6 Count

Having concluded the extended discussion of the *man*, the Gemara turns to the *halacha* of Yom Kippur. The question posed is: *hani chamisha inuyim keneged mi?* — these five afflictions, corresponding to what? Rav Chisda answers: corresponding to five occurrences of the language of *inuy* in the Torah — *be-asor, ach be-asor, Shabbat shabbaton, ve-Shabbat shabbaton, ve-asah lachem* — the various formulations in *Acharei Mot*, *Emor*, and *Pinchas* where Yom Kippur is commanded. Count them and there are five.

But immediately the Gemara notes an apparent contradiction: *hani chamisha hava ve-anan shis tnan* — these are five, yet our *Mishna* teaches six afflictions. The *Mishna* enumerates: eating and drinking (which is effectively two), plus anointing, bathing, wearing leather shoes, and conjugal relations — six in total. Tosafot notes that there is in fact a sixth verse as well — *ki kol hanefesh asher lo te'uneh* — but that verse states the punishment for non-compliance rather than commanding affliction. There are five commandments to afflict, and six *inuyim* listed in the *Mishna*. How does Rav Chisda's count of five reconcile with the *Mishna*'s six?

### Eating-Drinking

The resolution is that eating and drinking are counted as a single category — one form of *inuy* — rather than two. Drinking is subsumed within eating: *shtiyah shehi bichlal achilah*. Rav Chisda thus counts: one (eating-drinking) plus four others = five, matching the five verses.

It should be noted, however, that this is purely for the purpose of counting against the five verses. Halachically, eating and drinking do not combine: if one eats the requisite quantity minus a marginal amount and drinks the requisite quantity minus a marginal amount, he has not transgressed either prohibition, since the two quantities do not aggregate into a combined violation. They are distinct prohibitions with distinct thresholds, even while for purposes of counting the *inuyim* they are treated as one.

Reish Lakish provides the derivation that drinking is included under the rubric of *achilah*: *minayan l'shtiyah shehi bichlal achilah?* — the verse states *ve-achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha ma'asar degancha tiroshcha ve-yitzharecha* — you shall eat before God your *ma'aser* of grain, *tirosh*, and oil. Since *tirosh* is wine — a beverage — and the verse uses the verb *achilah* to describe it, we see that eating can encompass drinking.

### Wine-Mead

The Gemara challenges this: *mi mai?* — how do we know *tirosh* here means wine in its liquid, drinkable form? Perhaps it means *anigron* — a dish of vegetables steeped in wine — in which case it would be an act of eating, not drinking, and the extension to drinking would be unproven. Rabbah bar Shmuel defines terms: *anigron* is the liquid of cooked beets; *achsidron* is the liquid of other cooked vegetables. On this reading, perhaps the verse merely teaches that wine may be consumed as a component of a solid food.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov therefore adduces a different verse: *ve-natata hakesef bechol asher te'aveh nafshecha* — you shall spend the *ma'aser sheni* money on whatever your soul desires — cattle, sheep, wine, and *shichar*. The verse ends *ve-achalta lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — and you shall eat before God. Now *shichar* — beer or mead — is unambiguously a beverage, and the verse applies *achilah* to it. This establishes that the verb *achilah* encompasses drinking.

The Gemara again challenges: perhaps even *shichar* was consumed as an *anigron*-type dish, mixed into food. The response: *shichar ketiv* — the word *shichar* indicates *midi de-meshakker*, something with the capacity to intoxicate. Intoxication results from drinking large quantities, not from eating foods with trace alcoholic content. If the Torah says *shichar*, it means a drink consumed in drinking quantities. The further challenge — *dilma deveilah keilit?* — perhaps it refers to pressed *Keilit* dates, which a *baraita* teaches could also cause intoxication and were included in the prohibition of *yayin ve-shichar al tesht* for a *kohen* entering the *Mikdash* — is met with a *gezerah shavah*: *yalif shichar shichar minazir*. The *nazir* is prohibited from *yayin ve-shichar*, and there the context is clearly liquid; the juxtaposition of *shichar* with *yayin* in the same verse establishes that *shichar* means a drinkable beverage, not a solid food. This clinches the derivation that drinking falls within the category of *achilah*.

### Wine Names

Having established the derivation, the Gemara pauses to examine the names the Torah uses for wine. *Ve-amai kari leh yayin?* — why is wine called *yayin*? *Yayin shemevi yilalah la-olam* — wine brings wailing to the world. Rashi connects *yilalah* to *ta'aniyah*, mourning and lamentation. There is also the tradition identifying the *etz hadaat* of *Gan Eden* as a *gefen*, a vine — which fits with this etymology: from the earliest chapters of human history, wine has been the source of catastrophic moral failure.

*Ve-amai kari leh tirosh?* — why is wine called *tirosh*? *Shechol hamitgareh bo na'aseh rash* — whoever competes with it, whoever gives himself over to it, becomes poor. Indulgence in wine destroys a person financially and morally. The root *rash* — poverty — is embedded in the word.

This leads to a remarkable observation by Rav Kahana: *tirosh* is written in the Torah without the *vav* — *tirash* — but read with the *vav* — *tirosh*. The defective spelling yields *rash*, poverty; the full reading yields *rosh*, a head, a leader. *Zacha na'aseh rosh* — if one merits, if one drinks with discipline and responsibility, wine elevates; *lo zacha na'aseh rash* — if one does not merit, it impoverishes. Wine is not simply negative; it has dual potential depending on the character of the person engaging with it.

This duality is echoed in a further observation attributed to Rava: *yayin yesamach levav enosh* — wine gladdens the heart of man. The word *yesamach* can be pointed as *yesamach* (gladdens) or *yeshameach* (from *shimmamon*, desolation). *Zacha mesamcho* — if one merits, wine gladdens; *lo zacha meshammemo* — if one does not merit, it brings desolation. Rava himself testifies personally: *chamra ve-reichani pikchin* — wine and fragrant spices have sharpened my mind and aided my learning.

The observation that the Gemara uses the language of *zacha* and *lo zacha* — worthy and unworthy — is worth dwelling on. This is not simply about moderation as a behavioral choice. There is an element of predisposition at work: not everyone who overindulges is making a free choice in the same way, and not everyone with a certain temperament succumbs. *Zacha* implies something more like a constitutive quality of character, not merely the outcome of a single decision.

## Bathing & Oil

### Daniel Fast

The Gemara turns to locating the biblical sources for the remaining *inuyim* — bathing and anointing. The proof-text is drawn from Daniel. During his period of self-affliction, Daniel declares: *lechem chamudot lo achalti, u-vasar ve-yayin lo va el pi, ve-soch lo sachti* — I did not eat desirable bread, meat and wine did not enter my mouth, and I did not anoint myself. Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat explains that *lechem chamudot* — bread of desire — means that Daniel abstained even from fine white wheat bread, not merely from rich or elaborate foods. He was on an austere subsistence regimen.

### Oil Affliction

How do we know that Daniel's abstention from these things constitutes *inuy*? The verse later in Daniel reads: *va-yomer elai al tira Daniel, ki min hayom asher natata et libcha le-havin u-le-hitanot lifnei Elohecha, nishme'u devarecha* — from the day you set your heart to understand and to *lehitanot* — to afflict yourself — before your God, your words have been heard. The angel describes Daniel's conduct as *lehitanot*, affliction. Since Daniel explicitly included *ve-soch lo sachti* among his abstentions, and those abstentions as a whole are called *inuy*, we learn that refraining from anointing is itself a form of *inuy*. This establishes the *d'oraita* basis — or at minimum the *asmachta* — for the prohibition of *sicha* on Yom Kippur.

It is worth noting that for a modern audience the prohibition of anointing requires some contextual grounding. In antiquity, oil was not merely cosmetic; it was the functional equivalent of soap. The Romans used oil as a cleansing agent: hot oil was applied to the body to loosen dirt and then scraped off with an implement called a *strigil*. Archaeological reconstructions of ancient bathhouses include these implements as standard equipment. Anointing was thus an integral part of the bathing and cleansing process, not a luxury supplement to it.

### Bathing Source

Having established anointing from Daniel, the Gemara asks: *rechitza minalan?* — where do we derive bathing? Rav Zutra the son of Rav Tuvia cites the verse: *vatavo chamayim b'kirbo ke-shemen b'atzmotav* — and it came like water within him and like oil into his bones. The comparison between water and oil — water enters from outside, just as oil is rubbed onto the outside of the body — implies that bathing with water is parallel to anointing with oil, and thus both fall under the same category of *inuy*.

### Oil as Drink

The Gemara then presents a *baraita* that uses the same verse in a completely different direction: *minalan le-sicha ke-shtiyah be-Yom HaKippurim?* — where do we derive that anointing is like drinking on Yom Kippur? From the same verse: *vatavo chamayim b'kirbo ke-shemen b'atzmotav* — water inside, oil in the bones — teaching that rubbing oil into one's skin is comparable to drinking, in the sense that both involve the body absorbing a substance. On this reading, the verse does not parallel bathing to anointing; it parallels anointing to drinking.

The consequence is significant: if *sicha* is *ke-shtiyah*, anointing is a form of drinking, which would make the prohibition of *sicha* considerably more serious — it is not merely an additional *inuy* but a quasi-form of consumption, parallel to the most stringent prohibition on Yom Kippur.

At this point Rav Ashi intervenes with an alternative derivation of *rechitza*: *migufa d'kra shamia lei* — bathing can be inferred from Daniel's own verse. Daniel says *ve-soch lo sachti* — the doubled formulation (*soch lo sachti* rather than simply *ve-lo sachti*) encompasses more than simple anointing. Since anointing and bathing are functionally intertwined — oil was used as part of the cleansing process — the expanded language of Daniel's declaration includes bathing within the prohibition of anointing.

### D'oraita Debate

The concept of *sicha ke-shtiyah* requires clarification, because it is not universally applied in *halacha*. Ordinarily, we do not say *sicha ke-shtiyah*: a *nazir* who rubs wine on his skin has not violated the prohibition on drinking wine; one who rubs *chelev* on his skin has not violated the prohibition on consuming *chelev*. The principle is invoked only in specific contexts — primarily where the issue is *inuy* (affliction, as on Yom Kippur) or where the question is one of deriving benefit. In those cases, the definition of the prohibited activity is broad enough to include topical absorption.

This leads directly to the central *machlokes rishonim*: are the *inuyim* of Yom Kippur — beyond eating and drinking — *d'oraita* or *derabbanan*? The Gemara's language, drawn from verses in the Torah and derived through what appear to be genuine *drashot*, sounds like *d'oraita*. And indeed, some *rishonim* hold that all five *inuyim* carry biblical force.

However, Rabbeinu Tam (in the *Tosafot* on the preceding *daf*, 75a, in the middle of the discussion beginning *minayan le-sicha ke-shtiyah*) explicitly states: *u'peirash Rabbeinu Tam d'chol inuyim d'Yom HaKippurim lav ninhu ela miderabbanan, d'kra asmachta be-alma, le-var me-achilah u-shtiyah* — all the *inuyim* of Yom Kippur are merely rabbinic, and the verses are no more than *asmachta*, except for eating and drinking which are clearly biblical. His proof: the Gemara itself permits a person covered in filth to wash it off, and a person with scabs to apply oil. *Mi sheyesh lo chatatim be-rosho, sach ke-darko ve-eino choshesh* — one with scabs on his head may anoint in the normal manner without concern. If the prohibition were biblical, how could such exceptions be permitted?

### Pleasure vs Need

There is, however, a third approach — one that does not require framing the permitted cases as exceptions to a rabbinic prohibition. Rather, the very *definition* of what is prohibited may be the key. What the Torah prohibits may be specifically *rechitza* or *sicha* that constitutes *ta'anug* — pleasure, indulgence — as opposed to *rechitza* or *sicha* undertaken to remove filth or to address a medical condition. Washing off dirt is not pleasurable bathing in the relevant sense; applying oil to a wound or scab is not pleasurable anointing. On this reading, the prohibition is *d'oraita*, but its scope is definitionally limited to acts of luxury and comfort. The permitted cases are not exceptions but simply fall outside the definition from the outset.

This definitional approach carries important implications going forward. As the Gemara continues to address the details of which acts are permitted and which are prohibited on Yom Kippur, two questions will recur: first, whether a given leniency is an *exception* to the prohibition or a case that was never within its *definition*; and second, whether the prohibition in question is of biblical or rabbinic origin. Both questions remain live and contested among the *rishonim*, and the Gemara's treatment sets the stage for that ongoing analysis.
