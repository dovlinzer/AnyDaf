# Menachot 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Last 8 Verses

### 8 Verses Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל, אָמַר רַב: שְׁמֹנָה פְּסוּקִים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, יָחִיד קוֹרֵא אוֹתָן בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת. כְּמַאן? דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yehoshua bar Abba says** that **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says:** With regard to the last **eight verses of the Torah** (Deuteronomy 32:5–12), a single **individual reads them in the synagogue,** as that section may not be divided between two readers. The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this said? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion about how a *sefer Torah* must be written, building on the preceding *gemara* about Moshe's ascent to receive the Torah. The discussion now turns to a theologically and halachically rich topic: the status of the last eight *pesukim* of the Torah.

The chain of tradition runs as follows: *Amar Rav Yehoshua bar Abba amar Rav Giddel amar Rav* — Rav taught that the final eight *pesukim* of the Torah, an individual reads them in the *beit knesset* (*yachid korei otan beveit haknesset*). The precise meaning of *yachid korei otan* will be unpacked shortly, but the *gemara* first asks: *ke-man* — like whom does this ruling go? The answer it proposes: *de-lo ke-Rabbi Shimon* — apparently not like Rabbi Shimon.

### Rabbi Yehudah View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וַיָּמׇת שָׁם מֹשֶׁה עֶבֶד ה׳״, אֶפְשָׁר מֹשֶׁה חַי וְכָתַב ״וַיָּמׇת שָׁם מֹשֶׁה״? אֶלָּא עַד כָּאן כָּתַב מֹשֶׁה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ כָּתַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And Moses the servant of the Lord died there"** (Deuteronomy 34:5). **Is it possible** that after **Moses died, he** himself **wrote: "And Moses died there"? Rather, Moses wrote** the entire Torah **until this** point, and **Joshua bin Nun wrote from this** point **forward;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And some say** that **Rabbi Neḥemya** stated this opinion.

The *gemara* then introduces the underlying dispute by way of a *baraita*. The verse in question is *vayamot sham Moshe eved Hashem* — "And Moshe the servant of God died there in Arvot Moav." This verse opens the final eight *pesukim* of Devarim. The *baraita* poses the obvious difficulty: *efshar Moshe chai ve-katav vayamot sham Moshe* — is it really possible that Moshe was alive and wrote the words "and Moshe died there"? That would have been, at the time of writing, not merely emotionally devastating but factually false.

Rabbi Yehudah resolves the problem directly: *ella ad kan katav Moshe, mikan ve-eilakh katav Yehoshua bin Nun* — up until this point Moshe wrote the Torah; from here onward, Yehoshua bin Nun wrote the final eight *pesukim*. Some attribute this position to Rabbi Nechemia rather than Rabbi Yehudah, or to both. By contemporary standards, attributing any portion of the Torah's authorship to Yehoshua might sound like the height of *kefira*, and yet here Rabbi Yehudah advances exactly that position.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֶפְשָׁר סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה חָסֵר אוֹת אַחַת? וּכְתִיב ״לָקֹחַ אֵת סֵפֶר הַתּוֹרָה הַזֶּה וְשַׂמְתֶּם אֹתוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said to him: Is it possible** that the **Torah scroll** was **missing a single letter? But it is written** that God instructed Moses: **"Take this Torah scroll and put it** by the side of the Ark of the Covenant" (Deuteronomy 31:26), indicating that the Torah was complete as is and that nothing further would be added to it.

Rabbi Shimon responds sharply: *efshar sefer Torah chaser oti echat* — is it conceivable that the *sefer Torah* should be missing even a single letter? He marshals a verse in support: *lakoach et sefer ha-Torah ha-zeh ve-samtem oto* — "take this Torah scroll and place it near the Ark." These were Moshe's own words to the Levites. If Moshe called it a *sefer Torah* — a complete Torah scroll — he clearly had written the entire thing before handing it over. The final eight *pesukim* must therefore have been written by Moshe as well.

This argument carries both a practical dimension — Moshe's use of the phrase *sefer Torah* implies a complete text — and a deeper theological resonance. Is it even conceivable that the Torah received through Moshe was not fully given by God through him? The entire *sefer Torah* must have come from Moshe.

### Writing with Tears

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, עַד כָּאן – הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אוֹמֵר וּמֹשֶׁה כּוֹתֵב וְאוֹמֵר, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אוֹמֵר וּמֹשֶׁה כּוֹתֵב בְּדֶמַע, כְּמָה שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לָהֶם בָּרוּךְ מִפִּיו יִקְרָא אֵלַי אֵת כׇּל הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה וַאֲנִי כֹּתֵב עַל הַסֵּפֶר בַּדְּיוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon explains: **Rather, until this** point, i.e., the verse describing the death of Moses, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, dictated and Moses wrote** the text **and repeated** after Him. **From this** point **forward,** with regard to Moses' death, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, dictated and Moses wrote with tears** without repeating the words, due to his great sorrow. **As it is stated there** with regard to Jeremiah's dictation of the prophecy of the destruction of the Temple to Baruch ben Neriah: **"And Baruch said to them: He dictated all these words to me, and I wrote them with ink in the scroll"** (Jeremiah 36:18), but he did not repeat the words after Jeremiah.

But this immediately raises the difficulty of how Moshe could write something untrue. The *gemara* answers that up until these final eight *pesukim*, the process of transmission was: *Hakadosh Barukh Hu omer u-Moshe kotev ve-omer* — God would dictate, Moshe would repeat the word aloud and then write it. This practice of vocalizing before writing is in fact what scribes do today when writing a *sefer Torah*, *mezuzot*, or *tefillin*, in order to guard against error.

When it came to the final eight *pesukim*, however, the formulation changes: *Hakadosh Barukh Hu omer u-Moshe kotev be-dema* — God dictated and Moshe wrote with tears (*be-dema*). The *gemara* draws support from a verse in Yirmiyahu describing Barukh, Yirmiyahu's scribe: *mi-piv yikra elai et kol ha-devarim ha-eleh va-ani kotev al ha-sefer be-diyo* — "From his mouth he would read to me all these words and I would write them in the scroll with ink." The contrast, as Rashi reads it, is that Barukh says he wrote — but does not say he repeated the words back aloud first. The passage concerned tragedy and lamentation, and so the process of verbalization gave way to silent, tearful writing.

### Rashi & Maharsha

According to Rashi, the plain meaning of *be-dema* is that Moshe was weeping as he wrote. It was so painful to write the words "and Moshe died there" that he could not bring himself to vocalize them. The transmission was still entirely from God to Moshe; Moshe wrote every word — but instead of repeating them aloud, he wept as he wrote. The picture is deeply poignant. Rashi's reading, however, does not fully address the underlying theological difficulty: how could Moshe write something that was, at the moment of writing, not yet true?

The Maharsha, not in this *gemara* but in Bava Batra where the same passage appears, offers a brilliant reading that addresses this directly. He argues that Rabbi Shimon is making two distinct points that differentiate the final eight *pesukim* from everything that preceded them.

The first distinction concerns the process of vocalization. Throughout the Torah, the formulation is *Moshe omer ve-kotev* — Moshe would say and then write. For the final eight *pesukim*, it becomes simply *kotev* — he wrote, without saying. This departure from the established mode of transmission was itself a signal — a kind of flag embedded in the very process of writing — that these *pesukim* were different in nature, that they described events that had not yet occurred.

The second and even more striking distinction is the Maharsha's reading of *be-dema* in contrast to *be-diyo*. The *gemara* in Yirmiyahu uses the phrase *kotev be-diyo* — writing with ink. Here, the Torah says *kotev be-dema* — writing with tears. The Maharsha understands this not merely as a description of emotional state but as a description of the medium: Moshe actually wrote the final eight *pesukim* in tears, not in ink. It was, in effect, invisible ink. The *sefer Torah* was complete — Moshe had written every word — but the final eight *pesukim* were written in tears, and Yehoshua subsequently filled them in with ink. This reading beautifully holds the tension between two truths: the *sefer Torah* was entirely written by Moshe, and yet Moshe could not have written those words in the normal sense while they remained unfulfilled.

The Vilna Gaon is reported to hold a similar position. He understands *be-dema* as referring to a teardrop that had not yet fully formed into the precise shape of letters, and Yehoshua subsequently shaped those drops into the proper letters of the text. However one reads the specific mechanism, both approaches resolve the same tension: the entire Torah came through Moshe, but the final eight *pesukim* were written in a qualitatively different mode that acknowledged their as-yet-unrealized character.

### Yachid Kore Otan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הוֹאִיל וְאִישְׁתַּנִּי – אִישְׁתַּנִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now states its inference: **Shall we say that** the ruling of Rav that the last verses of the Torah are read by only one reader is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** since according to Rabbi Shimon these verses are similar to all other verses of the Torah, as they were all written by Moses? The Gemara answers: **You** may **even say** that Rav's ruling was stated in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon; since they differ** from the rest of the Torah in one way, as Moses wrote them without repeating the words, **they differ** from the rest of the Torah in this way as well, and they may not be divided between two readers.

The *gemara* then returns to the question with which it opened: the principle of *yachid korei otan* seems to reflect the position of Rabbi Yehudah, who holds that those *pesukim* were written by Yehoshua and therefore carry a different status. But the *gemara* says *afilu teima Rabbi Shimon* — even according to Rabbi Shimon, who holds that Moshe wrote everything, one could still affirm the special status of *yachid korei otan*. The reason: *ho-il ve-ishtani ishtani* — since they were changed, they are different. Whether through the absence of vocalization, through the medium of tears, or through whatever the *gemara* understood to distinguish those eight *pesukim*, their altered mode of transmission confers upon them a distinct status — and that distinction expresses itself in the rule of *yachid korei otan*.

What does *yachid korei otan* mean in practice? Rashi explains that it means only a single individual may receive all eight *pesukim* as a single *aliyah*. They cannot be divided between two *aliyot*. Because they constitute a distinct and singular unit, they must be treated as one block, and this reflects their unique status. This reading is apparently also that of the Yerushalmi.

Tosafot records an alternative interpretation in the name of Rav Meshullam, suggested by Rabbeinu Tam: *yachid korei otan* means that the *ba'al korei* would not read alongside the *oleh* — the person called up to the Torah would have to read it himself, without the assistance of the reader. There is something almost poignant in that reading — a certain aloneness in those final *pesukim*, as though the reader stands alone before the words of Moshe's death. Tosafot itself, however, objects that in the time of the *gemara* there was no *ba'al korei* at all — each *oleh* read for himself — so the distinction the *gemara* draws cannot have been contrasting those two modes. Tosafot ultimately agrees with Rashi: the rule is that one *oleh* receives all eight *pesukim* together.

### Rambam Ruling

The Rambam rules on this in Hilchos Tefilla, Perek 13, Halacha 6. He writes: *shmoneh pesukim she-besof ha-Torah mutar likros osam be-veit ha-knesset be-pachot me-asarah* — the final eight *pesukim* of the Torah may be read in the synagogue with fewer than ten. We *pasken* like Rabbi Shimon, the Rambam affirms — *af al pi she-kol ha-Torah hayu Moshe mi-pi ha-Gevurah amram* — even though the entire Torah was spoken by God through Moshe, including these final eight *pesukim* — nevertheless, *ho-il u-mashmaam she-heim achar misat Moshe, harei nishtanu* — since they convey the sense of events that took place after Moshe's death, they are different. And because they are different, *mutar le-yachid likros otan* — they may be read without a *tzibur*.

This is a striking ruling. The Rambam locates the distinctive status of the final eight *pesukim* not in the mode of their writing — not in tears versus ink, not in the absence of vocalization — but simply in the jarring dissonance of their content: they sound as though they were written after Moshe died. And from this difference in character, the Rambam derives a practical leniency — that they can be read without a full *minyan*.

### Raavad Critique

The Raavad is sharply dismissive. He writes: *lo shamanu davar zeh me-olam* — we have never heard of such a thing. His reading of *yachid korei otan* is simply that of Rashi: you do not interrupt it; one *oleh* receives all eight together. He then adds, in the spirit of Tosafot's observation, that the local practice is indeed that the *oleh* reads it himself without a *chazzan* reading alongside him — not as the explanation of the *gemara*, but as an additional layer of the *minhag* — again evoking that sense of solitude and weight in those final *pesukim*.

Raavad closes with a pointed rejoinder to the Rambam: *u-mah she-katav hu inyan zarus hu me-od* — what the Rambam wrote is particularly bizarre, *ve-tzibur heichan halchu* — and where did the *tzibur* go? If you have just been reading the Torah with a full *minyan* for the preceding *aliyot*, why would you suddenly be reading the final eight *pesukim* without one? While it is theoretically possible — a small *minyan* in which one person departs — it seems strange to formulate a special halachic status around such a marginal case.

## Buying vs Writing

### Purchasing Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל, אָמַר רַב: הַלּוֹקֵחַ סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה מִן הַשּׁוּק – כְּחוֹטֵף מִצְוָה מִן הַשּׁוּק, כְּתָבוֹ – מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ קִיבְּלוֹ מֵהַר סִינַי. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אִם הִגִּיהַּ אֲפִילּוּ אוֹת אַחַת – מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו כְּאִילּוּ כְּתָבוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua bar Abba says** that **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says: One who purchases a Torah scroll in the marketplace is akin to one who snatches a mitzva in the marketplace,** as the proper manner in which to perform the mitzva of writing a Torah scroll is to write one for himself. And if he himself **writes** a Torah scroll, **the verse ascribes him** credit **as though he received it at Mount Sinai. Rav Sheshet says: If he emended even a single letter** of the Torah scroll, thereby completing it, the verse **ascribes him** credit **as though he had written it** in its entirety.

The *gemara* now turns to a different aspect of the *mitzvah* of *sefer Torah*, introducing a chain of teaching: *Rami bar Abba amar Rav Giddel amar Rav* — Rav taught that one who purchases a *sefer Torah* from the marketplace is like one who snatches a *mitzvah* from the marketplace (*ke-chotef mitzvah min ha-shuk*). The image is telling. Purchasing an existing *sefer Torah* involves no creative effort; the Torah scroll simply changes hands. You have acquired a *mitzvah* — presumably there is a *mitzvah* in owning a *sefer Torah* — but you have contributed nothing to the world of Torah. The word *chotef*, "snatches," even carries a slightly unflattering connotation: someone else may have lost it while you gained it.

### Writing Merit

By contrast, one who actually writes a *sefer Torah* — *kasvo* — *ma'aleh alav ha-katuv ke-ilu kiblo me-Har Sinai* — Scripture considers it as though he received it from Har Sinai. The act of writing creates a profound personal connection to the words themselves. One does not merely acquire the physical object; one participates in the very transmission of Torah. That intimate engagement is what the *gemara* expresses with the phrase *ke-ilu kiblo me-Har Sinai* — as though he himself stood at Sinai and received it. This is even more than commissioning a *sefer Torah*, where at least a new scroll is brought into the world through one's investment — writing it oneself establishes a degree of ownership over the words that cannot be replicated by a transfer of funds.

### Correcting Letter

Rav Sheshet adds a further nuance: *im higiah afilu ot achat, ma'aleh alav ha-katuv ke-ilu ketavo* — if one corrected even a single letter in a *sefer Torah*, Scripture considers it as though he wrote the entire scroll. This is a one-degree upgrade, not two: correcting a letter is treated as equivalent to writing the scroll, but not as equivalent to receiving it at Sinai — that higher level is reserved for one who writes the whole thing from start to finish. The logic is that correcting an error transforms the scroll from unusable to usable; one has performed a genuine act of writing, not merely a transfer. People who participate in a *sefer Torah* dedication by "buying a letter" and guiding the *sofer*'s hand in writing it participate in something of this spirit — a personal connection through direct involvement in even a single letter.

## Scroll Specs

### Column Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (סִימָן סגל״ם) תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עוֹשֶׂה אָדָם יְרִיעָה מִבַּת שָׁלֹשׁ דַּפִּין וְעַד בַּת שְׁמֹנָה דַּפִּין, פָּחוֹת מִיכֵּן וְיָתֵר עַל כֵּן לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** § Before continuing its discussion of the *halakhot* of writing a Torah scroll, the Gemara presents **a mnemonic** for the upcoming *halakhot*: ***Samekh*, *gimmel*, *lamed*, *mem*. The Sages taught: A person may prepare** for a Torah scroll **a sheet** of parchment of any size **from three columns and until eight columns,** but **one may not prepare** a sheet of parchment that has **less than** three **or more than** eight columns.

The *gemara* now proceeds to the physical specifications of a *sefer Torah*. A *baraita* states: *oseh adam yeriah mi-bat shalosh dapim* — one may make a single *klaf* (parchment panel) with a minimum of three columns, *ve-ad bat shemonah dapim* — and a maximum of eight. The *sefer Torah* is composed of individual *klafim* sewn together, and each *klaf* must have a reasonable number of columns — not so few that the columns become excessively wide, and not so many that they become excessively narrow.

*Pachot mi-kan ve-yater mi-kan lo ya'aseh* — more than eight or fewer than three columns in a single *klaf* one should not do. It should be noted that most of these instructions have the character of *le-chatchila* guidance — aesthetic and practical standards for proper Torah writing — rather than absolute rules whose violation would render the scroll *pasul*.

### Column Width

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יַרְבֶּה בְּדַפִּין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה אִגֶּרֶת, וְלֹא יְמַעֵט בְּדַפִּין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעֵינָיו מְשׁוֹטְטוֹת, אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן ״לְמִשְׁפְּחוֹתֵיכֶם לְמִשְׁפְּחוֹתֵיכֶם לְמִשְׁפְּחוֹתֵיכֶם״ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And he may not increase** the number **of columns,** e.g., by writing eight columns on a narrow sheet of parchment, **since** then each column **has the appearance of a missive** due to its narrow lines. **And he may not decrease** the number **of columns,** e.g., by writing three columns on a wide sheet of parchment, **since** then the lines will be so wide that the reader's **eyes** will **wander,** as it will be difficult to find the beginning of a line. **Rather,** the ideal width of a line is, **for example,** where one can write **"*lemishpeḥoteikhem*," "*lemishpeḥoteikhem*," "*lemishpeḥoteikhem*,"** for a total of **three times.**

Rashi's explanation of *ve-lo yarbeh ba-dapim* — do not have more columns than is appropriate for the *klaf* — is that even within the range of up to eight columns, a small *klaf* should not be forced to accommodate six, seven, or eight narrow columns, because then it will resemble a letter (*iggeret*), which was apparently written in narrow columns. Conversely, *ve-lo yama'it ba-dapim* — do not have fewer columns than is appropriate — because overly wide columns cause the eyes to wander (*einav meshottetot*): reading to the far end of a very long line, the reader must then return all the way to the beginning of the next line and risks losing his place.

The ideal column width is specified by reference to the word *le-mishpachoteichem*: the column should be wide enough to fit three iterations of this ten-letter word — *le-mishpachoteichem le-mishpachoteichem le-mishpachoteichem* — giving an optimal column width of thirty letters. Tosafot, in the name of Rabbeinu Tam, qualifies this: the thirty-letter standard applies only when writing in a particularly narrow script. When the letters are large and thick, as is common in beautiful *sifrei Torah*, the eye does not wander as much, and the column may be correspondingly wider.

### Dividing Klaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִזְדַּמְּנָה לוֹ יְרִיעָה בַּת תֵּשַׁע דַּפִּים, לֹא יַחְלוֹק שָׁלֹשׁ לְכָאן וְשֵׁשׁ לְכָאן, אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע לְכָאן וְחָמֵשׁ לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** If one **happened** to acquire **a sheet** of parchment that **has** space for **nine columns,** exceeding the eight-column limit, **he should not divide** it into two sheets of parchment with **three** columns **here and six** columns **there; rather,** he should divide it into two sheets of parchment with **four** columns **here and five** columns **there,** so that the two sheets will be similar in width.

If one happens to obtain a piece of *klaf* that could accommodate nine columns, one does not want to use it as a single panel with nine columns. It must be cut. But how should it be divided? *Lo yachlok shalosh le-kan ve-shesh le-kan* — do not divide it into a three-column piece and a six-column piece. Rather, *ela arba le-kan ve-chamesh le-kan* — four and five, or five and four. The *klafim* should be of roughly similar sizes, and a four-five division is visually more balanced than a three-six division.

### End Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בִּתְחִלַּת הַסֵּפֶר, אֲבָל בְּסוֹף הַסֵּפֶר – אֲפִילּוּ פָּסוּק אֶחָד וַאֲפִילּוּ דַּף אֶחָד. פָּסוּק אֶחָד סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: פָּסוּק אֶחָד בְּדַף אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement** that the sheet must contain a minimum of three columns **said?** It is said with regard to sheets **at the beginning** and middle **of the scroll. But at the end of the scroll,** a sheet may consist of **even one verse, and even one column.** The Gemara asks: Can it **enter your mind** to say that a sheet may contain **one verse? Rather, say** that it may consist of even **one verse on one column.**

An important exception applies at the conclusion of the *sefer Torah*. *Ba-meh devarim amurim bi-tchilat ha-sefer* — the rule that *klafim* must have between three and eight columns applies throughout the *sefer* until its very end. But *aval be-sof ha-sefer* — at the end, *afilu pasuk echad ve-afilu daf echad* — even a single *pasuk* in a single column is permissible.

The *gemara* itself challenges this: *pasuk echad salka da'at?* — can one really have just one *pasuk* in a column? The question, as some *meforshim* understand it, arises from the earlier teaching that the text must reach the bottom of the column. If you have only one *pasuk* left on your final *klaf*, how do you reconcile placing a single *pasuk* in a column while also filling the column to the bottom? The *gemara* clarifies: *ela eima pasuk echad be-daf echad* — one *pasuk* filling one column. That is, if the final *klaf* has only one *pasuk* remaining, the scribe writes it in very large letters, one word per line if necessary, stretching that single *pasuk* to fill the entire column from top to bottom. Some *rishonim* specify that this is done with notably large letters.

### Margin Sizes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיעוּר גִּלָּיוֹן מִלְּמַטָּה – טֶפַח, מִלְּמַעְלָה – שָׁלֹשׁ אֶצְבָּעוֹת, וּבֵין דַּף לְדַף – כִּמְלֹא רֶיוַח רוֹחַב שְׁתֵּי אֶצְבָּעוֹת, וּבַחוּמָּשִׁין מִלְּמַטָּה – שָׁלֹשׁ אֶצְבָּעוֹת, מִלְּמַעְלָה – שְׁתֵּי אֶצְבָּעוֹת, וּבֵין דַּף לְדַף – כִּמְלֹא רֶיוַח רוֹחַב גּוּדָל.
>
> **Translation:** **The measure of** the **margin** of a Torah scroll is as follows: The size of the **lower** margin is **one handbreadth [*tefaḥ*].** There is a requirement for a large margin there, so that a reader not inadvertently rest his arm on the writing. The size of the **upper** margin, which is less susceptible to that occurrence, is **three fingerbreadths [*etzba'ot*], and** the space **between each column** is **equal to the full width of two fingerbreadths. And with regard to** one of **the five** books of the Torah that is written as an independent scroll, the size of the **lower** margin is **three fingerbreadths,** the size of the **upper** margin is **two fingerbreadths, and** the space **between each column** is **equal to the full width of a thumb-breadth [*gudal*].**

The *gemara* now specifies the margin dimensions. *Shiur gilyon* — the minimum margins: the bottom margin is a *tefach* (four finger-breadths); the top margin is three finger-breadths (*shalosh etzba'ot*), slightly smaller. The reason the bottom margin is larger is presumably that the scroll is more likely to be handled, rested upon, or rolled up from below, warranting greater protection. *U-vein daf le-daf ki-melo ruchaw shtei etzba'ot* — the space between adjacent columns is two finger-breadths in width.

For *chumashim* — bound codices rather than scrolls — the margins are somewhat smaller: the bottom margin is three finger-breadths, the top is two, and the inter-column space is the width of a thumb. These proportional reductions reflect the different physical handling of a codex. As with the column rules, these margin specifications have the character of proper conduct and aesthetic standard — considerations of *kavod* and ease of reading — rather than absolute conditions of validity, though this is not explicitly stated.

### Letter Spacing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵין שִׁיטָה לְשִׁיטָה כִּמְלֹא שִׁיטָה, וּבֵין תֵּיבָה לְתֵיבָה כִּמְלֹא אוֹת קְטַנָּה, וּבֵין אוֹת לְאוֹת כִּמְלֹא חוּט הַשַּׂעֲרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the space **between** one **line** of a Torah scroll **and** the following **line** must be **equal to** the space of **a full line, and** the space **between** one **word and** the following **word** must be **equal to a full small letter, and** as for the space **between** one **letter and** the following **letter,** it is sufficient for it to be **equal to a full hairbreadth.**

*U-vein shita le-shita ki-melo shita* — between each line of text, the space should equal the height of one line of text; this is what we would call double-spacing. *U-vein teiva le-teiva ki-melo ot ketana* — between each word, the space must be at least the width of a small letter, such as a *yud*, so that words are clearly distinguishable from one another. A *sefer Torah* in which the words are not clearly separated is not *kosher*. *U-vein ot le-ot ki-melo chut ha-sa'arah* — between each letter within a word, even a hairbreadth of space suffices, but they must be distinct. Letters that touch and are not clearly separate render the scroll *pasul*.

### Letter Size Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַל יְמַעֵט אָדָם אֶת הַכְּתָב, לֹא מִפְּנֵי רֶיוַח שֶׁל מַטָּה, וְלֹא מִפְּנֵי רֶיוַח שֶׁל מַעְלָה, וְלֹא מִפְּנֵי רֶיוַח שֶׁבֵּין שִׁיטָה לְשִׁיטָה, וְלֹא מִפְּנֵי רֶיוַח שֶׁבֵּין פָּרָשָׁה לְפָרָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The *halakhot* of the margins notwithstanding, **a person may not reduce** the size of **the writing** in a manner that the size of the writing is not consistent, **not in order to** ensure the correct amount of **space for the lower** margin, **nor in order to** ensure the correct amount of **space for the upper** margin, **nor in order to** ensure the correct amount of **space between** one **line and** the following **line, nor in order to** ensure the correct amount of **space between** one **passage and** the following **passage,** as this is not aesthetically pleasing.

*Al yima'et adam et ha-ktav lo mi-pnei revach shel mata ve-lo mi-pnei revach shel ma'alah* — a scribe must not make his letters smaller than the norm of the column's script in order to achieve the required bottom or top margin. If the scoring of the *klaf* was done slightly too close to the edge, and the scribe realizes he will not achieve the full required margin, he must nonetheless maintain consistent letter size throughout. *Ve-lo mi-pnei revach she-bein shita le-shita* — nor should he shrink the letters to achieve proper double-spacing between lines. *Ve-lo mi-pnei revach she-bein parsha le-parsha* — nor to create proper white space between one *parsha* and the next. The letter size established at the outset of a column must be maintained uniformly, regardless of the resulting margin or spacing dimensions.

## Word Overflow

### 5-Letter Word

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִזְדַּמְּנָה לוֹ תֵּיבָה בַּת חָמֵשׁ אוֹתִיּוֹת – לֹא יִכְתּוֹב שְׁתַּיִם בְּתוֹךְ הַדַּף וְשָׁלֹשׁ חוּץ לַדַּף, אֶלָּא שָׁלֹשׁ בְּתוֹךְ הַדַּף וּשְׁתַּיִם חוּץ לַדַּף.
>
> **Translation:** If **one happens upon a word that comprises five letters** and cannot be written in its entirety within the column, **he may not write two** letters **within the column and three outside of the column,** in the margin. **Rather,** he should write **three** letters **in the column and two outside of the column.**

The *gemara* now addresses what happens when a word does not fit within the column boundary. In the absence of hyphens and without electronic typesetting, the scribe must manage overflow manually.

The rule for a five-letter word that reaches the end of a column: *lo yichtov shtayim betoch ha-daf ve-shalosh chutz la-daf* — do not write two letters inside the column and three outside. Rather, *ela shalosh betoch ha-daf ve-shtayim chutz la-daf* — three letters must fall within the column and two may extend beyond it. The principle is that the majority of the word must remain within the column itself. While ideally the text should be both right and left justified, when that cannot be achieved, the overflow is acceptable provided that the majority of the overflowing word sits within the column boundary. This is the transition from the end of 30a to the top of 30b.

### 2-Letter Word

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִזְדַּמְּנָה לוֹ תֵּיבָה בַּת שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת – לֹא יִזְרְקֶנָּה לְבֵין הַדַּפִּין, אֶלָּא חוֹזֵר וְכוֹתֵב בִּתְחִילַּת הַשִּׁיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **he happens** upon **a word that comprises two letters** and cannot be written in its entirety within the column, **he may not cast it** in the margin **between the** two **columns; rather, he should return and write** the word **at the beginning of the** following **line.**

A two-letter word presents a different problem. *Lo yizrakena levein ha-dapin* — do not think that because two letters may overhang the column, you may place an entire two-letter word entirely in the inter-column space. That is prohibited. *Ela chozer ve-kotev bi-tchilat ha-shita* — instead, go back and begin writing the word at the start of the next line.

This ruling may seem obvious — if a five-letter word must have its majority inside the column, certainly a two-letter word cannot be placed entirely outside — but the *gemara* states it explicitly, presumably to forestall the mistaken inference that since two letters are allowable outside, two-letter words may be placed there in their entirety.

## Name Corrections

### Erasing Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַטּוֹעֶה בַּשֵּׁם, גּוֹרֵר אֶת מַה שֶּׁכָּתַב, וְתוֹלֶה אֶת מַה שֶּׁגָּרַר, וְכוֹתֵב אֶת הַשֵּׁם עַל מְקוֹם הַגְּרָר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף תּוֹלִין אֶת הַשֵּׁם. רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: אַף מוֹחֵק וְכוֹתֵב.
>
> **Translation:** § **One who mistakenly** omitted **the name** of God and wrote the next word before discovering his error should **scrape off that which he wrote, and suspend** the words **that he scraped off** above the line, **and write the name** of God **upon the place that had been scraped;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says: One may even suspend the name** of God above the line, without scraping off the word that was written in its place. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** Not only may one scrape off the dry ink of the next word, but one may **even wipe away** the word while the ink is still wet **and write** the name of God in its place.

The scenario is this: the scribe was meant to write *vayedaber Hashem el Moshe leimor* but accidentally wrote *vayedaber el Moshe leimor*, omitting the divine name. The name must somehow be inserted. What are the options?

Rabbi Yehudah's position: *gorer ma she-katav* — scrape away (with a dry scraping instrument) what has been written. Since one cannot practically erase back to *vayedaber* and redo everything from that point if many lines have been written since, the procedure is more targeted. The scribe scrapes away *el Moshe leimor*, writes the divine name on the line in its place, and then *toleh et ma she-garar* — suspends the scraped text (*el Moshe*) between the lines, in the inter-column double-spacing, in a position that indicates clearly that those words follow the divine name on the line. The result on the line reads *vayedaber Hashem*, while between the lines the words *el Moshe* are written in a way that marks them as the continuation, followed by *leimor* back on the line itself. This is analogous to the caret insertion mark in written correction. Rabbi Yehudah's view is that the divine name must be on the line itself in its proper position, and the displaced words are accommodated between the lines.

Rabbi Yitzchak takes a different approach: *af mochek ve-khotev* — one may simply erase (*mechikah*, as opposed to scraping) the displaced text and rewrite the name directly on the line. The precise distinction between *gorer* and *mochek* is not entirely clear — *gorer* refers to scraping after the ink has dried, while *mochek* may leave more of a stain — but the practical import of Rabbi Yitzchak's ruling is that one may correct the line straightforwardly by erasing the displaced text and writing the name cleanly in its place.

### R. Shimon Shezuri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַשֵּׁם כּוּלּוֹ תּוֹלִין, מִקְצָתוֹ אֵין תּוֹלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says:** A scribe **may suspend the entire name** of God above the line, but he **may not suspend part of** the name of God above the line.

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri articulates a qualification to the *toleh* approach: *kol ha-shem kulo tolin, miktzato ein tolin* — one may suspend the entire divine name between the lines, but one may not suspend part of it. The divine name may not be split, with part between the lines and part on the line itself. It is a refinement of the position that allows *tiluy* — suspension — of the name, insisting that if one uses this method the name must appear as a complete, intact unit wherever it is placed.

### Rabbi Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין אֶת הַשֵּׁם לֹא עַל מְקוֹם הַגְּרָר וְלֹא עַל מְקוֹם הַמְּחָק, וְאֵין תּוֹלִין אוֹתוֹ. כֵּיצַד עוֹשֶׂה? מְסַלֵּק אֶת הַיְרִיעָה כּוּלָּהּ וְגוֹנְזָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Meir:** A scribe **may not write the name** of God **either upon the place that had been scraped or upon the place that had been wiped away, and** he **may not suspend it** above the line, as none of these options exhibit sufficient respect for the name of God. **What should** the scribe **do? He should remove the entire sheet** of parchment **and inter it.**

Rabbi Meir advances the most stringent position of all: *ein kotsvin et ha-shem lo bi-mekom ha-garar ve-lo bi-mekom ha-machak* — one may not write the divine name either where it has been scraped or where it has been erased. *Ve-ein tolin oso* — and one may not suspend it between the lines either. If none of these corrective methods are available, *keitzad oseh* — what does one do? *Mesalek et ha-yeriah kulah u-gonzah* — one must remove the entire *klaf* and put it in *genizah*. There is no permissible repair; the *klaf* must be replaced entirely. The concern underlying Rabbi Meir's position is that any erasing or scraping in the vicinity of the divine name — or any suspension that leaves the name in an improper position — is impermissible, and since there is no way to correct the error without one of these prohibited acts, the entire *klaf* must go.

### Amoraic Rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב, הֲלָכָה: תּוֹלִין אֶת הַשֵּׁם. רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל, הֲלָכָה: מוֹחֵק וְכוֹתֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the final halakhic ruling: **Rav Ḥananel says** that **Rav** says: The ***halakha*** is that one **suspends the name** of God above the line. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Shmuel** says: The ***halakha*** is that one may even **wipe away** the word while the ink is still wet **and write** the name of God in its place.

The *gemara* then records two *amoraic* rulings. Rav Chananel in the name of Rav rules: *halacha tolin et ha-shem* — the law follows the position that suspends the divine name between the lines. Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel rules: *halacha mochek ve-khotev* — the law is to erase and rewrite on the line.

### Shimon Shezuri Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא מָר הֲלָכָה כְּמָר, וּמָר הֲלָכָה כְּמָר, מִשּׁוּם דְּאָפְכִי לְהוּ. אָמַר רָבִין בַּר חִינָּנָא, אָמַר עוּלָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why is it necessary to state the actual opinions? **Let** this **Sage,** Rav Ḥananel in the name of Rav, **say** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of this **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei; **and** let this **Sage,** Rabba bar bar Ḥana in the name of Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Shmuel, say that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of that **Sage,** Rabbi Yitzḥak. The Gemara answers: **Since** there are those **who reverse** the opinions of the *tanna'im*, they needed to state the opinions explicitly. **Ravin bar Ḥinnana says** that **Ulla says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri; and moreover,** not only is the *halakha* in accordance with his opinion with regard to this matter, but in **any place where Rabbi Shimon Shezuri taught** a *halakha*, the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with his** opinion.

The *gemara* asks why each *amora* stated his position as a *halacha* ruling rather than simply saying "the law follows Rabbi Yehudah" or "the law follows Rabbi Yitzchak." The answer: *mishum de-ichalfei* — because there was confusion about which *tanna* held which position. To avoid ambiguity arising from a misattributed *tannaic* name, each *amora* stated the ruling itself rather than identifying the *tanna* whose position he endorsed.

A final ruling is then recorded: Ulla adds a sweeping general principle: *ve-lo od, ela kol makom she-shana Rabbi Shimon Shezuri, halacha kimoto* — and not only here, but wherever Rabbi Shimon Shezuri states an opinion — in a *Mishna* or a *baraita* — the law follows him.

## Shimon Shezuri Analysis

### Anchoring Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַהָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר כׇּל הַשֵּׁם כּוּלּוֹ תּוֹלִין, מִקְצָתוֹ אֵין תּוֹלִין – וְהָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה תּוֹלִין אֶת הַשֵּׁם, וְרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה מוֹחֵק וְכוֹתֵב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **To which** statement of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri is this referring? **If we say** that it is referring **to** the statement **here,** where **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says:** A scribe **may suspend the entire name** of God above the line, but he **may not suspend part of** the name of God above the line, that is difficult: **But wasn't it stated with regard to that** *baraita* that **Rav Ḥananel says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is that one **suspends the name** of God above the line, **and Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Shmuel** says: The ***halakha*** is that one may even **wipe away** the word while the ink is still wet **and write** the name of God in its place?

The *gemara* now asks: *ahai* — on what case was Ulla's sweeping statement anchored? Ulla said two things: the law follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri in the matter at hand, and in general the law follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri wherever he appears. But what was the specific ruling that prompted the general declaration? Where did Ulla actually *anchor* his blanket statement?

The *gemara* then attempts to identify that anchor case by examining each case where Rabbi Shimon Shezuri holds an opinion, and testing whether Ulla's statement could have been made in reference to it. The methodology is elegant: if Ulla's statement was about a given case, one would expect Ulla to have been in conversation with other *Amoraim* who discussed that same case. But Ulla's statement appears to have been made independently of any other *amora's* ruling. Therefore, any case where another *amora* already ruled like Rabbi Shimon Shezuri cannot be the anchor of Ulla's statement.

### Get Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתַהּ, הוּא נָמֵי לֵימָא. אֶלָּא אַהָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמְסוּכָּן. הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, זְעֵירִי אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, וְאִם אִיתַהּ – הוּא נָמֵי לֵימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is so** that when Rabbi Ḥanina said that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri he was referring to this matter, then **let** Ravin bar Ḥinnana **also say** along with those *amora'im* that the *halakha* is that one suspends the entire name of God above the line, but not a part of the name. **Rather,** say that Rabbi Ḥanina's statement that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri was referring not to the discussion here, but was stated **with regard to this** mishna (*Gittin* 65b): **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says:** Even **in the case of one who is dangerously ill** who gives that instruction, they write the bill of divorce and give it to his wife. **But wasn't it** already **stated with regard to that** mishna that **Ze'eiri says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina** says: The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri? And if it is so** that when Rabbi Ḥanina said that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri he was referring to this matter, then **let** Ravin bar Ḥinnana **also say** along with Ze'eiri that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri.

The *gemara* first suggests that Ulla's statement was anchored in the following *Mishna*. Originally it was taught that someone being led out in chains to be executed (*ha-yotzei be-kolar*) who said "write a *get* for my wife" — his statement is treated as authorization not only to write and sign the *get* but also to deliver it, even though he did not explicitly say "deliver it." The reason for this leniency is contextual: one does not speak of writing a *get* when about to be executed unless one intends it to be delivered; he simply did not articulate the full instruction because he was understandably distracted.

Subsequently, the sages extended this ruling: *af ha-mifaresh ve-ha-yotzei be-shayara* — even someone embarking on a sea voyage or joining a caravan may rely on the same principle. Here too, the urgency of departure and the risk of not returning provide sufficient context to interpret his words as full authorization to write, sign, and deliver the *get*.

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri extends the principle further: *af ha-mesukan* — even someone who is ill or in danger, though not with the same degree of immediate urgency as someone being led to execution or racing to catch a departing ship, is nonetheless in a situation of sufficient contextual pressure that his words are interpreted as full authorization. Perhaps this is where Ulla's general endorsement of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri was anchored?

The *gemara* rejects this: *ha itmar alah* — this case already had a ruling issued upon it. *Zeiri amar Rabbi Chanina, halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon Shezuri* — Zeiri in the name of Rabbi Chanina ruled that the law follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri in the case of the *mesukan*. If Ulla's blanket statement was anchored in this case, he should have said it together with Zeiri's ruling. Since Ulla's statement stands independently, it cannot be referring to this case.

### Terumah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַהָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן כִּיפָּר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי: פּוֹל הַמִּצְרִי שֶׁזְּרָעוֹ לְזֶרַע, מִקְצָתוֹ הִשְׁרִישׁ לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וּמִקְצָתוֹ אַחַר רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה – אֵין תּוֹרְמִין מִזֶּה עַל זֶה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין תּוֹרְמִין וּמְעַשְּׂרִין לֹא מִן הֶחָדָשׁ עַל הַיָּשָׁן וְלֹא מִן הַיָּשָׁן עַל הֶחָדָשׁ. וְהָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי, וְאִם אִיתָא – הוּא נָמֵי לֵימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** say that Rabbi Ḥanina's statement was **with regard to this** *halakha*: **Rabbi Yosei ben Keifar says in the name of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri:** If one **planted a cowpea** plant **for** its **seed,** i.e., not to be eaten as a vegetable but for one to either eat or plant its seeds, and **some of** the plants **took root before Rosh HaShana, while some of** them took root only **after Rosh HaShana, one may not separate *teruma*** or tithes **from this for that, as one may not separate *teruma* or tithes from the new** crop **for the old or from the old** crop **for the new.** The Gemara asks: **Wasn't it** already **stated with regard to** those *mishnayot* that **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri? And if it is so** that when Rabbi Ḥanina said that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri he was referring to these *mishnayot*, then **let** Ravin bar Ḥinnana **also say** along with Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri.

The *gemara* proposes another candidate: *terumat ma'aser shel demai she-chazra li-mkomah* — the case of *terumat ma'aser* of *demai* that fell back into the produce.

The background: *demai* is produce purchased from an *am ha-aretz* — an ordinary farmer not part of the rabbinic community — regarding whom we are uncertain whether he separated *ma'aser* and *terumat ma'aser*. We assume he separated *terumah* but not necessarily the tithe and its priestly portion. If the buyer purchased such produce and *Shabbat* arrived before he had separated *ma'aser* and *terumat ma'aser*, the rabbis were lenient and allowed him to ask the seller whether he had already separated them; if the seller said yes, the buyer could rely on him. Tosafot adds a reference from the Yerushalmi that *eimat Shabbat al amei ha-aretz* — the awe of *Shabbat* makes an *am ha-aretz* more likely to tell the truth; and Rashi adds that this leniency also assumes there is nothing else to eat.

Now: what if the buyer did separate *terumat ma'aser* before *Shabbat*, but then the *terumah* fell back into the produce and mixed in? The entire mixture is now forbidden, since *terumah* may not be eaten by a non-*kohen* and it is mixed throughout. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri rules: *af be-chol sho'alo ve-ochlo al piv* — in this situation, even on a weekday one may ask the *am ha-aretz* seller and rely on his statement that he had already separated *ma'aser* previously, thereby retroactively rendering the buyer's own separation of *terumat ma'aser* unnecessary, and the entire produce is permitted. The leniency is extended beyond *Shabbat* need to weekday cases involving significant loss.

This could be the anchor case for Ulla's ruling. But again the *gemara* rejects it: *amar Rabbi Yochanan, halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon Shezuri be-mesukan u-ve-terumat ma'aser shel demai* — Rabbi Yochanan already ruled like Rabbi Shimon Shezuri in both the *mesukan* case and this *terumat ma'aser* case. If Ulla's statement referred to either of these, he should have been in conversation with Rabbi Yochanan. Since he was not, neither can be the anchor.

### Legume Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵךְ קַח מִן הַגּוֹי״, קָסָבַר יֵשׁ קִנְיָן לְגוֹי בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהַפְקִיעַ מִיַּד מַעֲשֵׂר, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ מִן הַפְּטוּר עַל הַפְּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that Rabbi Tarfon **said to him: Go and take** produce **from a gentile** and separate tithes from it on behalf of the untithed produce that is intermingled in the majority of tithed produce. Accordingly, Rabbi Tarfon **holds** that **a gentile has acquisition** of land **in Eretz Yisrael to abrogate** the sanctity of the land, thereby removing it **from** the obligation to **tithe** its produce. **And** therefore, if Rabbi Shimon Shezuri takes produce from a gentile, **he is considered,** by Torah law, **to be** separating tithes **from exempt** produce **on behalf of exempt** produce, while all of it is obligated in tithes by rabbinic law.

The *gemara* now proposes yet another candidate. Rabbi Yosei ben Kipar says in the name of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri: the case of the *pul ha-mitzri* — the Egyptian legume — that was planted for its seeds. When a crop is planted for its seeds, the obligation of *terumot u-ma'asrot* attaches when the plant takes root, not when it is picked. If some of the plants in a given row took root before Rosh Hashanah and others took root after, the produce belongs to two different titheing years — some to year two and some to year three — and one may not tithe from one year's produce on behalf of another year's.

The problem is that all the plants grow together in a mixed row, and it is impossible to know which plants belong to which year. *Ein tormin mi-zeh al zeh le-fi she-ein tormin u-ma'asrin lo min ha-chadash al ha-yashan ve-lo min ha-yashan al ha-chadash* — one may not take *terumah* from the new on behalf of the old or from the old on behalf of the new.

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's ruling: *tzovro gorno le-tocho* — harvest everything together into the same threshing floor, then take *terumah* and *ma'aser* from the combined pile. One assumes that the mixture is proportionally distributed — if, say, sixty percent of the plants belong to the prior year and forty percent to the new year, then the portion of *ma'aser* taken from the combined pile will be proportionally sixty percent from the old year and forty percent from the new, and each portion will count for its appropriate year.

### Multiple Discussions

The *gemara* immediately raises the same objection it has raised to each prior candidate: *amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yochanan, halacha Rabbi Shimon Shezuri* — Rabbi Yochanan already ruled like Rabbi Shimon Shezuri in the Egyptian legume case as well. *Ve-im ita, hu nami leima* — if Ulla's statement was anchored in this case, he too should have been part of that discussion. Since he was not, this cannot be the anchor case either.

The pattern is now fully exposed. Ulla made a sweeping declaration that the law follows Rabbi Shimon Shezuri — both in the case at hand and wherever he appears. Yet every case in which Rabbi Shimon Shezuri expresses a view has *already* been the subject of an *amoraic* ruling by another *amora*. If Ulla had been referring to any one of those cases, one would have expected his ruling to appear in conversation with those other *amoraim*. But Ulla's statement stands alone, disconnected from all prior *amoraic* discussions. The challenge, therefore, is to identify a case where Rabbi Shimon Shezuri holds an opinion but no other *amora* has ruled upon it — that would be the true anchor of Ulla's blanket principle. The *gemara* leaves this as the question at the close of the daf.
