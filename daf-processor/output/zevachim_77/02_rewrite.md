# Zevachim 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Metzora Oil Procedures

### Metzora Stipulation

The daf opens by resuming a discussion that arose in passing from the broader theme of *mevi'in kodashim l'veit hapsul*. The case under examination involves a person uncertain whether he is a *metzora*. He brings an *asham* and stipulates that if he is not in fact a *metzora*, the animal shall serve as a *shelamim*. The animal is then treated with the stringencies of both categories simultaneously. The Gemara had been working through the practical consequences of this arrangement, particularly regarding the *log* of oil that accompanies the *metzora* purification rite. The conclusion reached was that one brings the *log* and treats it both as the oil used in the *metzora* purification and as a *nedavah* free-will offering — functioning similarly to a *minchah*, from which a *kometz* is taken and placed on the *mizbeach*.

### Kli Sharet Sanctification

The Gemara had to work out how both functions could be served by a single *log*. According to Tosafos, the difficulty is that *metzora* oil may not be burned on the altar as an *isheh*, since it was designated for a different sacrificial purpose — the prohibition of *kodesh mimenu l'ishin*. The resolution was that the oil is burned on the altar as fuel, not as an offering proper, to cover the scenario in which it turns out to be *metzora* oil. The *log* is then refilled, and that replenished oil is used in the sprinkling ceremony.

Rashi posed an independent difficulty: once some of the oil has already been offered on the altar, if this turns out to have been *nedavah* oil, how may the remnant on the altar be used in the subsequent ritual? To this the Gemara answered that in the scenario where the oil is not needed as *metzora* oil, one stipulates that it be treated as inconsequential — effectively like water — and it carries no further significance.

### Extra Oil Problem

The Gemara at the top of 77a now presses further: *v'ika shiyayim d'ba'i michlinehu — v'ika hach purta d'lo kamitzei lei*. After all the procedures are completed, what is done with the remnant of the oil? Normally it would be eaten. But recall that at the second stage, extra oil was poured in — oil needed in case the man is indeed a *metzora*. In the scenario where he turns out not to be a *metzora* and the oil functions as *nedavah* oil, the *kometz* was taken only from what was originally there. The additional oil that was poured in never had a *kometz* taken from it. How, then, may that extra oil be eaten together with the remainder? The *kohanim* would be eating what might be *chullin* mixed with *kodesh*.

### Redemption Solution

The Gemara's answer is *d'parik lei* — one redeems the added portion. In the scenario where the man is not a *metzora*, that extra oil was added after the *nedavah* process was complete. One stipulates at the time of adding it: if this man is a *metzora*, let this oil be fully sanctified through the *kli sharet* and used for the *metzora* ritual; if he is not a *metzora*, I do not intend for the *kli sharet* to sanctify it, and after the *metzora* procedure it will be redeemed on money, which money will go toward another *nedavah*.

Rashi notes an immediate difficulty with this redemption: once something has been sanctified in a *kli sharet*, it cannot simply be redeemed. But the Gemara clarifies that *kli sharet eino mekadesh ela l'daat* — a service vessel sanctifies only when one intends it to do so. The stipulation therefore controls whether the vessel sanctifies at all.

The Gemara then raises the problem of *chullin* in the *azarah*. Having redeemed the added portion, one now has a mixture of ninety percent *nedavah* oil that has had its *kometz* taken, and ten percent *chullin*. Does bringing *chullin* into the *azarah* constitute a violation? The Gemara considers two possibilities: if one redeems the oil while it is outside the *azarah*, *ipsulei b'yotzei* — the whole mixture becomes invalid by having left the sacred precincts. If one redeems it while inside the *azarah*, the oil was brought in while sanctified; it became *chullin* only subsequently, while already inside — *v'chullin mimeila havyan*. Since one did not carry *chullin* into the *azarah* but rather carried in sanctified oil that only later became *chullin*, the transgression is not triggered.

### Shimen Oil Nedavah

One final difficulty presents itself. The entire scheme depends on the stipulation that if the man is not a *metzora*, the oil will function as a *nedavah*. But Rabbi Shimon — whose ruling this mishna follows — himself holds that one may not *lechatchila* donate oil as a *nedavah*. How can this stipulation be part of the procedure at all? The Gemara answers: *tikunei gavra shani* — saving a person from uncertain *metzora* status is a special circumstance. Since *bedi'avad* an oil *nedavah* is certainly valid, and since we are constrained by the need to provide this person with a legitimate path forward, even Rabbi Shimon would permit it here.

## Asham Talui Question

### Talui Alternative

Rav Rechumi was sitting before Ravina and transmitted the following question in the name of Rav Huna bar Tachlifa. Setting aside the oil entirely and returning to the stipulation regarding the animal itself: the mishna has the person stipulate that if he is not a *metzora*, the *asham* shall be a *shelamim*. Rav Huna bar Tachlifa asks — why go all the way to a *shelamim*? Why not stipulate: if I am a *metzora*, this is my *asham metzora*, and if I am not a *metzora*, this shall be an *asham talui*? An *asham talui* is the guilt-offering brought for a doubtful transgression. Indeed, there is an opinion of Rabbi Eliezer that since one can never entirely exclude the possibility of having sinned unknowingly, one may bring an *asham talui* on any day without any concrete basis for concern. Why does the mishna not employ this option and remain entirely within the category of *asham*?

The inference drawn was: *shma mina man tanna d'palig alei d'Rabbi Eliezer Rabbi Shimon* — since the mishna (authored according to Rabbi Shimon's view) does not offer the *asham talui* option, we may deduce that Rabbi Shimon rejects Rabbi Eliezer's position and holds that an *asham talui* requires a concrete, identified basis for concern. That is why he had to resort to a *shelamim* instead.

### Shimon Rejects Talui

This inference seemed solid. The *asham talui* option would have been logically available; the fact that it was passed over implies a principled rejection of the view that one may bring an *asham talui* without a concrete cause.

### Sheep vs. Ram

Ravina responded sharply: *Torah Torah* — what happened to basic Torah knowledge? *Imi b'dachrei michalfei lach?* Did you confuse a sheep and a ram? An *asham metzora* is a one-year-old sheep. An *asham talui* is a two-year-old ram. One cannot stipulate that the same animal serve as either one, because they are entirely different animals. The only legitimate alternative use for a sheep is a *shelamim*, since a *shelamim* may also be a sheep. The option of *asham talui* was never available not because of any position regarding Rabbi Eliezer's ruling, but simply because the species and age requirements are incompatible. It is a striking reminder that even a great *amora*, absorbed in theoretical possibilities, can momentarily overlook the concrete specifics of *halacha*.

## Chatat Olah Mix

### Limbs Mixed

With the digression concluded, the Gemara moves to the next mishna, which shifts from animals mixed together to the cut limbs of sacrifices mixed together. The mishna states: *eivrei chatat shenitarvu b'eivrei olah* — the limbs of a *chatat*, which the *kohanim* would eat, become mixed with the limbs of an *olah*, which are to be burned on the altar. The problem is acute: the *kohanim* cannot eat the mixture, lest they eat *olah* meat; but they cannot simply burn it all on the altar, since that would mean burning *chatat* meat, which is designated for the *kohanim* to eat — a violation of the prohibition of *kodesh mimenu l'ishin*.

### Eliezer Firewood

Rabbi Eliezer says: *yitein l'ma'ala* — go ahead and place it all on the altar fire. *V'ro'eh ani et b'sar hachatat milma'ala k'ilu hen eitzim* — I regard any *chatat* meat placed on the fire not as an offering to God but as fuel. The *olah* meat is an offering; the *chatat* meat is merely combustible material feeding the fire. Rabbi Eliezer is not troubled by the destruction of *chatat* meat per se — the concern is only that one may appear to be offering something to God that is not designated as an offering, and that concern is resolved by declaring it to be fuel.

### Sages Notar

The Sages disagree. One cannot simply relabel *chatat* meat as firewood; meat placed on the altar fire is problematic if it has no sacrificial standing and was designated for eating. Instead they say: *te'avor tzuratan v'yeitzu l'veit hasreifah* — allow the mixture to remain until the following morning, by which point it will have the status of *notar* (sacrificial leftovers that have become time-invalid), and then burn it all outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — at the *beit hasreifah*.

The obvious question presents itself: is not allowing something to become *notar* itself a violation of *lo totiru mimenu*? The Gemara does not press this point, apparently assuming that since there is no permissible alternative course of action, one is in the position of an *onus* — coerced by circumstance — and the principle of *shev v'al ta'aseh* applies. One's hands are tied halachically, and the *notar* status arises without any affirmative act of transgression.

## Pasuk Exegesis

### Eliezer Derivation

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Eliezer?* What is his textual basis? The answer is the verse in Leviticus 2:11–12: *ki kol se'or v'chol dvash lo taktiru mimenu isheh l'Hashem; korban reishis takrivu osam l'Hashem v'el hamizbe'ach lo ya'alu l'rei'ach nicho'ach*. The verse forbids burning *chametz* and honey (*se'or v'chol dvash*) as an offering. They may be brought as *bikurim* — first-fruit offerings — but may not be put on the altar. Crucially, the verse says *lo ya'alu l'rei'ach nicho'ach* — they shall not ascend as a pleasing odor. Rabbi Eliezer derives from the qualification *l'rei'ach nicho'ach* that the prohibition applies only when something is being offered for its sacrificial purpose, as a pleasing odor to God. *L'rei'ach nicho'ach ee atah ma'aleh aval atah ma'aleh l'shem etzim* — for the purpose of a pleasing odor you may not place it, but for the purpose of firewood you may.

On this reading, the more general prohibition of *kodesh mimenu l'ishin* — which forbids burning the remnant of something from which a part was already offered — should similarly yield to the *l'rei'ach nicho'ach* qualification. If one burns the *chatat* meat not as an offering but as fuel, no prohibition is violated.

### Sages Counter

The Sages read the verse differently. They invoke the word *osam* — "those" — in the phrase *korban reishis takrivu osam l'Hashem*: *mi'eit Rachmana osam* — the Torah restricts the *l'rei'ach nicho'ach* leniency specifically to those items the verse is literally discussing — *se'or v'dvash*, chametz and honey (and by extension the *shtei halechem* of Shavuot and *bikurim*). These, if placed on the altar as firewood, are permitted. But the prohibition derived by inference from *mimenu isheh l'Hashem* — which forbids burning the remainder of something from which an *isheh* was offered — is not subject to the firewood leniency. The word *osam* delimits the leniency to the explicit subject of the verse.

The result is paradoxical: the items explicitly prohibited by the verse — chametz and honey — may be placed on the altar as firewood; the items prohibited only by inference — remnants of sacrifices — may not. It is counterintuitive, yet this is the Sages' position.

### Eliezer Reply

Rabbi Eliezer responds by redeploying the word *osam*, but in the opposite direction: *osam hu d'lo aleh l'rei'ach nicho'ach aval atah ma'aleh l'shem etzim — aval midi achrinei lo*. He reads *osam* as restricting the ramp prohibition (*el hamizbeach lo ya'alu*) rather than the firewood leniency. The chametz and honey — those items explicitly named — may not be placed even on the ramp of the altar, as the verse includes the ramp in *el hamizbe'ach*. But items prohibited only by inference, such as the remnants of a sacrifice, are forbidden only on the altar proper, not on the ramp.

The Sages, in turn, read *osam* as yielding two simultaneous teachings: first, that the ramp prohibition is stricter for chametz and honey than for inferred categories; and second, that the firewood leniency is available only for chametz and honey and not for sacrifice remnants. *Tarti shma mina* — both conclusions are derived from the same word.

Rabbi Eliezer's position, once one accepts that the verse teaches the firewood leniency at all, has its own internal logic: if firewood is permitted for chametz and honey, it should be equally permitted for sacrifice remnants. The Sages insist that the word *osam* confines the leniency exclusively to the explicit subjects of the verse.

## Explicit vs. Inferred

### Rashi Strictness

The distinction the Gemara draws is between the explicit prohibition in the verse and the prohibition derived by inference. For the explicit subject of the verse — chametz and honey — the prohibition of placing them on the altar is treated more severely: it extends even to the ramp. For items whose prohibition is derived from the verse by inference — such as the remnants of a sacrifice — the prohibition is confined to the altar proper and does not extend to the ramp.

### Sages Firewood

According to the Sages, the firewood leniency flows in the opposite direction. Because the verse says *l'rei'ach nicho'ach*, limiting the prohibition to a sacrificial-offering context, this leniency applies only to the explicit subject of the verse — chametz and honey. One who places chametz or honey on the altar as firewood does not violate the prohibition. But one who burns the remnants of a sacrifice as firewood does violate it, because the firewood leniency was never extended by the verse to inferred prohibitions. This produces the ironic structure: what is strictly forbidden by explicit text may nevertheless be placed as firewood; what is forbidden only by inference may not.

## Beraita Levels

### Three Severity

The Gemara notes: *matnisin d'lo k'hai tanna* — our mishna does not align with the following *tanna*, as preserved in a *beraita*. The *beraita* presents a three-tiered structure of cases rather than the two-tiered structure of our mishna. The three cases are: (1) limbs of a *chatat* mixed with limbs of an *olah*; (2) limbs of an unblemished *olah* mixed with limbs of a blemished one (*ba'alat mum*); (3) limbs of a kosher animal mixed with limbs of an animal used in bestiality (*rovea* or *nirba*). Each level represents an escalating degree of disqualification — respectively: a technical halachic incompatibility, a physical-aesthetic blemish, and a moral-sexual corruption.

### Agreement Extremes

On the two extremes, the *beraita* records no dispute. At the lenient end, where *chatat* and *olah* limbs are mixed, both Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages agree that one may proceed: the limbs from the inappropriate category are treated as firewood. At the strict end, where an animal involved in bestiality is part of the mixture, all agree that nothing may be placed on the altar — not even as firewood.

### Debate Locus

The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages, according to this *beraita*, falls in the middle case: unblemished and blemished *olah* limbs mixed together. Here Rabbi Eliezer permits treating the blemished limbs as firewood and placing everything on the altar, while the Sages forbid it. Our mishna, by contrast, has Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages debating by the *chatat-olah* mixture and has Rabbi Eliezer himself conceding by the blemished case. The *beraita* thus represents a different tradition of where exactly the debate is located.

## Aesthetic vs. Moral

### Huna Minor Blemish

The Gemara now asks: according to the *beraita*'s version, what distinguishes the blemished animal from the one involved in bestiality, such that the firewood leniency applies in one case but not the other? Rav Huna offers an answer: *bedukin sheba-ayin va-aliba d'Rabbi Akiva* — the case involves minor ocular blemishes of the eye, and is ruled according to Rabbi Akiva, who holds that such minor blemishes, once they have ascended the altar, need not be removed (*im alu lo yerdu*). Because this type of blemish is not a severe invalidity, the animal retains a degree of fitness for the altar, which distinguishes it from an animal involved in bestiality, which is absolutely disqualified.

### Firewood Challenge

The Gemara immediately challenges this: *eima d'amar Rabbi Akiva bedi'avad; lechatchila mi amar?* Rabbi Akiva's ruling that such an animal need not be removed applies only *bedi'avad* — after the fact. He does not permit placing it on the altar *lechatchila*. The only thing that would permit it *lechatchila* in this case is the firewood rationale. But if firewood is what permits it, then what functional difference is there between a blemished animal and one used in bestiality? Both are equally unfit *lechatchila*.

### Pappa Ramp

Rav Pappa proposes an alternative resolution: *hacha bemai askinan kegon she-alu agabei kevesh* — the case involves limbs that were already placed on the ramp. Once they are on the ramp, Rabbi Akiva's rule applies, and they need not be removed. This permits moving them to the fire — and this logic applies to the minor-blemish case but not to the bestiality case. Hence the distinction.

### Exegesis Mum

The Gemara rejects Rav Pappa's solution: *ei hachi afilu beinihu* — if that is the reasoning, it has nothing to do with the mixture at all. If a minor-blemished animal's limbs were already on the ramp, Rabbi Akiva would permit moving them to the fire even without any mixture being involved. The issue of *ta'arovet* — mixture — would be irrelevant.

The Gemara therefore turns to exegesis: *ella ta'ima d'Rabbi Eliezer mi'eit Rachmana mum bam*. The verse reads: *ki mashchatam bahem mum bam lo yeiratze lachem* — "for their corruption is in them; their blemish is in them (*mum bam*); they will not be accepted for you." The emphasis is on *bam* — in them, when the blemish is specifically and identifiably located in the animal being offered. The derivation is: *hu d'lo yeratzu ha yedei ta'arovet yeratzu* — when the blemish is specifically identified in a particular animal, it may not be offered; but when the pieces are mixed and the blemish cannot be pinpointed to any particular limb, the restriction does not apply and the mixture may be offered.

### Eliezer Acceptable

This is a striking turn in the Gemara's reasoning. The position attributed to Rabbi Eliezer is no longer that the blemished limbs are placed on the altar *as firewood* — it is that when the limbs are mixed, the blemished pieces are actually *acceptable as a legitimate offering*. *Mum bam* — the blemish is in them — is what disqualifies. When it is no longer possible to identify which piece bears the blemish, the disqualification lapses, and the mixed pieces may be offered as *korbanot mamash*.

### Sages Blemish

The Sages counter with their own reading of *mum bam*: the word *bam* does not mean "when specifically identified in them." Rather, it means "while the blemish is present in them." The verse is saying: as long as the blemish exists and has not passed, the animal is unacceptable. Once the blemish resolves on its own, the animal becomes acceptable. This is a ruling about the temporal persistence of the blemish, not about whether the blemish is individually identifiable. The mixture, in the Sages' view, changes nothing.

### Eliezer Bam

Rabbi Eliezer presses the linguistic argument further. The verse uses two words: *bam* (in them) and elsewhere *bahem* (in them, plural). *Mibam bahem* — one word addresses the case where the blemish is present and the animal is specifically identified (*bahem*), while the other addresses the case where the blemish is present but the pieces are mixed and no single piece is individually identified as the blemished one (*bam*). Each word carries a distinct teaching, and together they establish the distinction Rabbi Eliezer draws between an identified and an unidentified blemish.

The Sages do not accept this as two separate teachings: *bam bahem lo darshi* — they do not derive independent rulings from the two phrasings.

### Firewood Compromise

Having established Rabbi Eliezer's position that the mixed blemished pieces are actually *kosher* as an offering, the Gemara asks from the Sages' perspective: even if you reject Rabbi Eliezer's textual argument, why not at least permit the firewood rationale as a compromise? The Sages respond: *hacha meisi hatam lo meisi* — here it is disgusting (*ma'is*); there (by *chatat* and *olah*) it was not disgusting. The aesthetic repugnance of having pieces from a blemished animal on the altar prevents the Sages from applying even the firewood leniency in this case.

## Assumption Strategy

### Limbs Blemished

We now reach the next mishna, which addresses precisely the case that the *beraita*'s version of the debate — and our mishna's version as well — agrees is problematic: *eivrei olah temimah* mixed with *eivrei ba'alat mum*. Unblemished *olah* limbs are mixed with limbs from a blemished *olah*. Since the blemish renders those pieces *ma'is* and the firewood leniency is unavailable (according to the Sages) or unnecessary (according to Rabbi Eliezer, who considers the mixed pieces actually valid), the question is what practical course of action the *kohanim* should follow.

### Bad Already

Rabbi Eliezer says: *im kareiv rosh echad meihem yikrevu kol haroshim kulam* — if one head from the group has already been placed on the altar fire, the rest of the heads may be offered as well. Similarly, if the legs of one animal have been offered, all the legs may follow. The logic is an assumption strategy: since something was already placed on the altar without prior knowledge of which piece was blemished, we retroactively assume that the blemished piece was the one already offered. This removes the identification of the blemish from the remaining pieces, and they may now legitimately be brought up. Rabbi Eliezer requires some prior act to trigger this assumption — the act of having already offered one piece — because without it, the *ma'is* concern stands fully.

### Sages Burn

The Sages reject the assumption strategy entirely: *afilu karvu kulam chutz me'echad meihem yeitzei l'veit hasreifah* — even if all but one have already been offered, the remaining piece must be taken out and burned at the *beit hasreifah*. No matter how many have preceded it, one may never say regarding the last piece: the blemished one must have already been done. The Sages do not permit retroactive assumptions to override the concrete uncertainty that remains. So long as one piece remains unaccounted for, it might be the blemished one, and it may not be placed on the altar *lechatchila*.

---

## Assumption Strategy (II)

### Pairs Requirement

Rav Elazar clarifies Rabbi Eliezer's position: *lo hichshir Rabbi Eliezer ela shnayim shnayim* — Rabbi Eliezer permits proceeding only when the limbs are brought up two at a time. When one brings a pair, two considerations operate simultaneously: the general assumption that the blemished animal was already offered in an earlier round, and additionally, the knowledge that of any given pair, at least one is certainly from an unblemished animal, which lends support to treating the other as likely acceptable as well.

### One Pair

Rabbi Yirmiyah challenges this from the mishna itself: the Sages say that even if all but one have been offered, that one must go to the *beit hasreifah* — implying that Rabbi Eliezer would permit offering that final single piece. But if Rabbi Eliezer requires pairs, how can he permit offering a lone remaining piece? Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Tachlifa resolves this: *mai echad zug echad* — when the mishna says "one remains," it means one *pair* remains, not one individual limb. The mishna, read carefully, always envisions the pieces being brought in pairs, and Rabbi Eliezer's leniency applies exclusively in that configuration.

## Blood Mixtures

### Sight Principle

The Gemara now transitions to a new and far-reaching mishna dealing with blood mixed with other liquids. The fundamental conceptual principle established here is that when dealing with mixtures in a non-food context, the operative sense is sight rather than taste. In the laws of food, a prohibited substance mixed into a permitted one is evaluated by whether it imparts flavor — *ta'am*. In the sacrificial context, where the blood is not food, flavor is irrelevant. What determines whether the blood retains its identity — and may therefore be thrown on the altar — is whether it remains visually detectable: *mar'it dam*.

### Blood Water

The first case: *dam shenitarev bemayim* — sacrificial blood mixed with water. *Im yesh bo mar'it dam* — if the appearance of blood is still discernible, *kasher* — it is valid and may be thrown on the altar. This is the paradigm case establishing the *mar'it dam* principle.

### Blood Wine

The mishna then presents a significant *chidush*: *nitarev beyayin* — the blood is mixed with wine. Since wine is a dark red liquid, the blood may no longer be visually detectable even if substantial blood is present. Does the blood simply lose its identity? The mishna rules: *ro'in oto ke'ilu hu mayim* — we regard the wine *as if it were water*, and ask: had this been water, would the blood have been visible? If the answer is yes, the blood is deemed present even within the wine, and the mixture may be thrown on the altar.

This is a striking halachic fiction. In reality, the blood is not visible. Yet the mishna permits treating it as present by applying a counterfactual: *had it been water*. What authorizes this counterfactual reasoning as a basis for leniency is one of the distinctive features of this sugyah.

### Blood Blood

The same conditional visibility rule applies when blood is mixed with the blood of non-sacrificial animals — whether domesticated animals (*behema*) or deer (*chaya*). *Ro'in oto ke'ilu hu mayim* — again one asks whether the *korban* blood would be detectable had the other liquid been water. If yes, the blood is treated as present and the mixture is valid.

This has a parallel in the laws of *kashrut*: when a prohibited substance falls into a permitted substance of the same type — as when *treif* chicken falls into kosher chicken soup — taste cannot distinguish them. Nevertheless, the rule of *min b'mino* requires us to evaluate the mixture as if the two were different substances, which preserves the forbidden item's identity and prevents it from being nullified by the majority. Here the *mishna* applies the same logic in the lenient direction: evaluate as if the opaque liquid were transparent water, and if the blood would be visible under those conditions, treat it as present.

### Blood Blood

The same conditional visibility rule extends to blood mixed with the blood of non-sacrificial animals or deer: *ro'in oto ke'ilu hu mayim*, whether the *korban* blood would have been detectable had the other liquid been water. If yes, the blood is treated as present and the mixture may be offered.

### Yehuda Identity

Rabbi Yehuda goes further: *ein dam mevatel dam* — blood never nullifies blood. Even if the amount of *korban* blood is so small relative to the other blood that, had the mixture been with water, the *korban* blood would not have been visible, Rabbi Yehuda holds that when blood is mixed with blood, the *korban* blood retains its identity absolutely. Blood and blood can never effect mutual nullification.

### Spill Invalid

If the sacrificial blood becomes mixed with blood from invalid sacrifices — *dam pesulin* — even if the *korban* blood is still deemed present by the *mar'it dam* criterion, the mixture may not be thrown on the altar, because the blood of invalid sacrifices may not be offered. The ruling is *yishafech la'amah* — the mixture is spilled into the channel that carries it out of the *Beit HaMikdash*. Similarly, if the *korban* blood is mixed with *dam hatamtzit* — the residual blood that drains out after the life-force blood has been collected, which has no sacrificial standing — *yishafech la'amah* as well.

### Eliezer Permit

Rabbi Eliezer dissents regarding the mixture with *dam hatamtzit*: *Rabbi Eliezer machshir* — Rabbi Eliezer permits throwing this mixture on the altar. The precise grounds for his leniency in this case will require further analysis.

### Halakha Validity

The *halakha* as stated in the mishna is that *im lo nimlach venatan kasher* — if one did not pause to deliberate and simply threw the mixture on the altar, the offering is valid after the fact. Since the *korban* blood is still deemed present according to the *mar'it dam* criterion or its counterfactual extension, the act achieves its sacrificial purpose *bedi'avad*. The debate between the Sages and Rabbi Eliezer concerns whether one is permitted to proceed *lechatchila* with the mixture that includes *dam hatamtzit*.

The conceptual architecture of this mishna is of broad significance for the laws of mixtures throughout *halacha*. Two features distinguish it from the food-law model: first, validity is determined by sight rather than taste; and second — and more strikingly — the counterfactual visibility test is applied to create leniency rather than stringency. In food law, the analogous reasoning (*min b'mino*) preserves the identity of a forbidden substance in order to prohibit. Here, the same structural reasoning preserves the identity of a desired substance in order to permit. This sugya will be taken up further in the following session.
