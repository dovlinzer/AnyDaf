# Eruvin 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Principles of Psak

### Lenient Opinion Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב וְרַבִּי זְרִיקָא אָמְרוּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, וּכְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מֵחֲבֵרָיו, וּכְרַבִּי מֵחֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara discussed the principles cited with regard to halakhic decision-making, it cites additional principles. **Rabbi Ya'akov and Rabbi Zerika said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** in disputes **with** any individual **Sage, and** the *halakha* **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** even in disputes **with** other **Sages, and the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi in disputes **with** any individual **Sage.**

The daf before us — 47a — continues a discussion that occupied much of the previous daf regarding foundational principles of *psak halacha*. The dominant principle, already familiar, is that in matters of rabbinic law (*derabanan*) one follows the lenient opinion (*le-kula*). A critical qualification, however, is that this principle applies specifically when the decisor has genuinely exhausted his analysis and finds himself unable to determine the correct ruling. As the Rambam frames it, when one does not know toward which direction the law leans — *eini yodea le-heichan hadin noteh* — the first obligation is to apply one's own judgment. Only when genuinely stymied does the principle of *halacha divrei ha-meikel* take effect. Furthermore, even in rabbinic matters, this leniency does not apply when it would mean following a lone opinion against many (*yachid bi-mkom rabbim*) — with one notable exception: in matters of *eruvin* and *aveilus*, the lenient view is followed even against the majority, without the Gemara specifying why.

### Minority Opinions

A related principle discussed on the previous daf concerns relying on a minority opinion in a time of pressing need (*sha'at ha-dechak*). This raises two questions: first, whether such reliance is permitted even in Torah-level prohibitions (*de'oraita*) or only in rabbinic ones — the context of the preceding discussion was specifically rabbinic; and second, what makes a minority opinion a legitimate *da'at yachid* that retains its standing as a viable minority view rather than one that has been effectively rejected by tradition. The authority of the *Shulchan Aruch* and its contemporary application to these principles was also addressed.

### Tannaitic Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּלָּשׁוֹן הַזֶּה אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. הַשְׁתָּא בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לֵיתָא, בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In the case of a dispute between **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda;** in the case of a dispute between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei; and, needless to say,** in the case of a dispute between **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** As **now,** if **in disputes with Rabbi Yehuda,** the opinion of Rabbi Meir **is not** accepted as law, **need it** be stated that **in disputes with Rabbi Yosei,** Rabbi Meir's opinion is rejected? Rabbi Yehuda's opinion is not accepted in disputes with Rabbi Yosei.

The Gemara then reviewed the established rules for adjudicating *machloket tannaim*: when Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir dispute, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda; when Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda dispute, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda; and there are parallel rules regarding Rabbi Akiva *me-chavero* and Rabbi Yosi *me-chavero*.

### Rav Mesharshya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: לֵיתַנְהוּ לְהָנֵי כְּלָלֵי. מְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא הָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiya said: These principles** of halakhic decision-making **are not** to be relied upon. The Gemara asks: **From where does Rav Mesharshiya** derive **this** statement?

The Gemara concluded the previous daf with a challenge from Rav Mesharshya, who sought to demonstrate that these principles are not actually in force by citing cases where we rule contrary to them. The Gemara's response was the principle of *heicha d'itmar itmar, heicha d'lo itmar lo itmar*: where an explicit ruling exists, that ruling stands — but that does not undermine the general principles, which govern precisely those cases where no explicit ruling has been given and one needs guidance on how to decide. The existence of exceptions, whether explicit rulings that go against the general principle or cases where we have *paskined* otherwise for particular reasons, does not mean the principles lack force in cases where they are the only available guidance.

## Testing Principles

### Exception Cases

The Gemara on 47a now turns to an extended examination of the principles by running through a series of *mishnayot*. This daf is notably sparse in *Tosafot*, for good reason: it consists almost entirely of a long chain of cases. The Gemara's method is twofold. First, it attempts to prove the principles are not operative by citing cases where we rule against them. The ready answer in each such case is that the existence of explicit rulings or recognized exceptions does not invalidate the general principle — these rules apply when nothing else determines the outcome.

### Cases Requiring Ruling

The Gemara then presses from the opposite direction: if the principles genuinely hold, why would an Amora ever need to issue an explicit ruling in a case where the principles already determine the outcome? The very act of issuing such a ruling seems to imply that the principles alone are insufficient — that without the explicit statement, one might not have known how to rule. This forms the core tension the Gemara will work to resolve across the cases that follow.

## Private/Public City

### Reshut Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עִיר שֶׁל יָחִיד וְנַעֲשֵׂית שֶׁל רַבִּים — מְעָרְבִין אֶת כּוּלָּהּ. שֶׁל רַבִּים וְנַעֲשֵׂית שֶׁל יָחִיד — אֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶת כּוּלָּהּ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן עוֹשֶׂה חוּצָה לָהּ, כָּעִיר חֲדָשָׁה שֶׁבִּיהוּדָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ חֲמִשִּׁים דָּיוֹרִין — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת שֶׁל שְׁנֵי בָתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The *mishnah* teaches: **If a city that belongs to a** single **individual** subsequently **becomes** one that **belongs to many** people, **one may establish an *eiruv*** of courtyards **for all of it.** But if the city **belongs to many** people, **and it** falls into the possession **of a** single **individual, one may not establish an *eiruv* for all of it, unless he excludes** from the *eiruv* an area the size **of the town of Ḥadasha in Judea, which contains fifty residents;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** The excluded area need not be so large; rather, **Rabbi Shimon says: [three courtyards]** each **containing two houses** are sufficient for this purpose.

The first case is drawn from a *mishnah* the Gemara introduces with the words *ela mei-ha*. The *mishnah* discusses a city that was private (*shel yachid*) and became public (*shel rabbim*), or vice versa. The interpretation of "private" and "public" here depends on one's broader understanding of *reshut ha-rabbim*. Rashi ties this to his position that a true *reshut ha-rabbim* requires 600,000 people: a public city is one large enough to constitute a genuine *reshut ha-rabbim*, and a private city is one that is not. Most *Rishonim* hold that ownership is irrelevant to this determination — what matters is use and traffic. The Ramban, however, takes a distinctive view that ownership is relevant, and reads the *mishnah* literally as addressing the question of whether a city is publicly or privately owned.

### Transition Cases

The *mishnah* presents two scenarios. In the first, a privately owned or insufficiently populous city becomes a public city — and the *mishnah* rules that one may still make an *eruv* for the entire city, a ruling that itself requires explanation given the public character now assumed by the city. The second scenario, which is the Gemara's focus here, involves the reverse: a city that was public and has now become private, meaning that in principle an *eruv* should now be permitted. Nevertheless, the *mishnah* rules that one may not make an *eruv* for the entire city. One must leave a portion outside the *eruv* as a *zecher* — a memorial reminder — lest people come to think that one may make an *eruv* over a genuinely public city. The *mishnah* specifies the minimum size of the excluded area: it must be at least the size of a new city in Yehudah, which the *mishnah* defines as containing fifty residents — a figure that reflects a very different scale of settlement than our own.

### Yehuda vs Shimon

Rabbi Yehuda requires that the excluded area contain fifty residents. Rabbi Shimon, at the top of 47a, takes a far more lenient position: the excluded area need only consist of three *chatzeros*, each containing two houses — that is, three courtyards with two houses each. This is a considerably smaller area than what Rabbi Yehuda requires.

### Rav's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וּמַאן פְּלִיג עֲלֵיהּ — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לֵיתַנְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said** that **Rav said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon; and who disagrees with** Rabbi Shimon on this matter? It is **Rabbi Yehuda. Didn't you say:** In disputes between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? Rather, can we not conclude from** this mishna that these principles should **not** be relied upon?

Rav Chama bar Guria reports in the name of Rav that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon. The Gemara immediately raises the difficulty: the one who disputes Rabbi Shimon here is Rabbi Yehuda, and one of the established principles is that when Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon dispute, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda. Why then did Rav rule like Rabbi Shimon? The Gemara dismisses this as a problem by invoking the principle of *heicha d'itmar itmar, heicha d'lo itmar lo itmar*: the existence of an explicit ruling in favor of Rabbi Shimon is precisely the kind of case where the rule is overridden. The rules apply by default; explicit rulings stand on their own.

## Absent Householder

### Eruv Participation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: הַמַּנִּיחַ אֶת בֵּיתוֹ וְהָלַךְ לִשְׁבּוֹת בְּעִיר אַחֶרֶת, אֶחָד נָכְרִי וְאֶחָד יִשְׂרָאֵל — אוֹסֵר לִבְנֵי חֲצֵירוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נָכְרִי — אוֹסֵר, יִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין דֶּרֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל לָבֹא בַּשַּׁבָּת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ הִנִּיחַ אֶת בֵּיתוֹ וְהָלַךְ לִשְׁבּוֹת אֵצֶל בִּתּוֹ בְּאוֹתָהּ הָעִיר — אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר, שֶׁכְּבָר הִסִּיחַ דַּעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the proof is **from that which we learned** elsewhere in a mishna: With regard to **one who left his house** without making an *eiruv* of courtyards, **and established residence** for Shabbat **in a different town, whether** he was **a gentile or a Jew,** his lack of participation **prohibits the** other **residents of the courtyards** in which he has a share to carry objects from their houses to the courtyard, because he did not establish an *eiruv* with them, and failure to include a house in the *eiruv* imposes restrictions upon all the residents of the courtyard. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** **Rabbi Yehuda says: His** lack of participation **does not prohibit** the others to carry, since he is not present there. **Rabbi Yosei says:** Lack of participation in an *eiruv* by **a gentile** who is away **prohibits** the others to carry, because he might return on Shabbat; but lack of participation by **a Jew** who is not present **does not prohibit** the others to carry, **as it is not the way of a Jew to return on Shabbat** once he has already established his residence elsewhere. **Rabbi Shimon says: Even if he left his house and established residence** for Shabbat **with his daughter in the same town, his** lack of participation **does not prohibit** the residents of his courtyard to carry, even though he is permitted to return home, **because he has already removed it,** i.e., returning, **from his mind.**

The Gemara moves to the next case with *ela mei-ha*. The background principle is that all residents of a *chatzer* — a courtyard surrounded by walls whose houses open onto a common yard — must participate together in an *eruv* in order to be permitted to carry in that shared space. Although technically *reshut ha-yachid*, the common courtyard is used by multiple residents whose domains remain in some sense distinct, and rabbinic law requires collective unification through the *eruv*. If even one resident refuses to participate, his distinct claim to the shared space frustrates the unification and invalidates the *eruv* for everyone.

### Absence Issue

The question the *mishnah* addresses is what happens when a resident is not present for *Shabbat* at all — he left to spend *Shabbat* in another city. Since he is not actively using the *chatzer*, perhaps his theoretical ownership of a distinct portion is irrelevant in practice, and the remaining residents can proceed with their *eruv* without him. The *mishnah* asks this both for a Jew and a non-Jew who have left: does either of them still prevent the *eruv*?

### Rabbi Meir

Rabbi Meir's position is that both a Jew and a non-Jew who have left still prevent the *eruv* from taking effect. Their ownership of a distinct portion in the *chatzer* remains legally relevant regardless of their physical absence.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees entirely: neither the absent Jew nor the absent non-Jew prevents the *eruv*. Since they are not present for *Shabbat*, their portion is treated as effectively ownerless for this *Shabbat*, and the remaining residents may proceed.

### Rabbi Yosi

Rabbi Yosi draws a distinction: an absent non-Jew still prevents the *eruv*, but an absent Jew does not. The reasoning for the distinction is that a non-Jew can travel on *Shabbat* — he might ride out and return even on *Shabbat* itself — and therefore his potential presence on *Shabbat* cannot be discounted. A Jew, by contrast, does not travel on *Shabbat*; if he has left the city, he will not return. Rabbi Shimon goes further still: even if a Jew has merely gone to spend *Shabbat* at his daughter's house within the same city, he does not prevent the *eruv*, because *hisiyach da'ato* — he has mentally disengaged from his own home. When one goes to spend *Shabbat* elsewhere, even locally, the entire framework of *Shabbat* — the meals, the presence, the plans — is organized around that other place. For a non-Jew, by contrast, *Shabbat* is no different from any other day, and physical departure does not imply the same mental disengagement. (The *mishnah*'s choice to specify his daughter's house rather than his son's house likely reflects the social reality that married daughters live with their husbands and a father going there would not return home, whereas sons might continue living in the same household, making the disengagement less complete.)

### Rav Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וּמַאן פְּלִיג עֲלֵיהּ — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said** that **Rav said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And who disagrees with him?** It is **Rabbi Yehuda. Didn't you say:** When there is a dispute between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?** This teaches that one cannot rely upon these principles.

Once again, Rav Chama bar Guria reports in the name of Rav that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon. The Gemara raises the difficulty in doubled form: not only do we have the principle that Rabbi Yehuda beats Rabbi Shimon, but here we also have the principle that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi *me-chavero*. Both principles point away from Rabbi Shimon, yet Rav rules like him. As Rashi notes, one could have raised both objections together. The Gemara again answers with *heicha d'itmar itmar*: these are exceptions where an explicit ruling exists, and the existence of such explicit rulings does not undermine the principles in cases where no explicit ruling has been given.

## Presence vs Bread

### Poor Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזֶהוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ הֶעָנִי מְעָרֵב בְּרַגְלָיו. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אָנוּ אֵין לָנוּ אֶלָּא עָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And that is what** the Sages meant when **they said: A pauper can establish an *eiruv* with his feet;** that is to say, he may walk to a place within his Shabbat limit and declare: Here shall be my place of residence, and then his Shabbat limit is measured from that spot. **Rabbi Meir says: We** apply this law **only to a pauper,** who does not have food for two meals; only such a person is permitted to establish his *eiruv* by walking to the spot that he wishes to acquire as his place of residence.

The Gemara presses once more with *ela mei-ha*, this time turning to the *mishnah*'s teaching: *ha-ani me'arev be-raglav* — a poor person makes his *eruv techumin* with his legs rather than with bread. The mechanism of *eruv techumin* establishes one's permitted *Shabbat* boundary by positioning food or oneself at a point up to 2000 *amot* from the city before *Shabbat* begins. Normally the *eruv* is established with bread placed at that point; but one may also establish it with one's own physical presence, simply being at that location when *Shabbat* begins.

### Only Poor

Rabbi Meir's position is that this option — making an *eruv techumin* through personal presence rather than food — is available only to a poor person who has no bread to spare. The general rule requires bread, and only the economic necessity of the poor person justifies the departure from that requirement.

### All Can Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד עָנִי וְאֶחָד עָשִׁיר. לֹא אָמְרוּ מְעָרְבִין בְּפַת אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל עַל הֶעָשִׁיר, שֶׁלֹּא יֵצֵא וִיעָרֵב בְּרַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This allowance applies **both to a pauper and to a wealthy person.** Indeed, **they said** that **one can establish an *eiruv* with bread only in order to** make placing an *eiruv* **easier for a wealthy person, so that he** need **not** trouble himself and **go out and establish an *eiruv* with his feet,** but the basic *eiruv* is established by walking to the spot one will acquire as his place of residence.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees, saying the option is available to anyone, whether poor or wealthy: *echad ani ve-echad ashir*. His reasoning is that the institution of *eruvin* with bread was itself a *leniency* introduced for the wealthy — to spare them the trouble of going out and standing at the boundary until *Shabbat* begins. They can instead send someone to place their bread there, or place it before *Shabbat* without remaining. This means that making an *eruv* with personal presence is the more basic and universally available option, and the bread *eruv* is the facilitation that was added for convenience. Any person who prefers to use his physical presence may do so.

### Rav's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַתְנֵי לֵיהּ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי לְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: אֶחָד עָנִי וְאֶחָד עָשִׁיר. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב, סַיֵּים בָּהּ נָמֵי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi** once **taught** this law to **Ḥiyya bar Rav in the presence of Rav,** saying: This allowance applies **both to a pauper and to a wealthy person, and Rav said to him:** When you teach this law, **conclude also with** this ruling: The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi was teaching this *mishnah* to Chiya bar Rav in the presence of Rav, presenting Rabbi Yehuda's view that the option is available to all. Rav instructed Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi that when he teaches this *mishnah*, he should append the ruling: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — we rule like Rabbi Yehuda.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי, וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I need a second** ruling? **Didn't you** already **say:** When there is a dispute between **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?** The fact that Rav needed to specify that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda on this matter indicates that he does not accept the general principle that when there is a dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda, the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

This produces the second direction of the Gemara's challenge. Now we are not dealing with an exception to the rules; we are dealing with a case that the rules already resolve. The rule is that when Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda dispute, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda. So *pshita* — why did Rav need to say so? As *Tosafot* phrases the underlying tension: *tarti lama li* — why do we need both the general principle and an explicit ruling to the same effect? The *Gemara* asks *u-mai koshya*: is this really difficult?

## Mishnah vs Baraita

### Rav vs Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּילְמָא רַב לֵית לֵיהּ לְהָנֵי כְּלָלֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this reasoning: **What is the difficulty** here? **Perhaps Rav does not accept these principles,** but the other Sages accept them.

The Gemara's answer is that perhaps Rav himself does not hold of these general principles at all. It was Rabbi Yochanan who articulated the *klalim*, and perhaps they reflect only his approach. When Rav explicitly ruled like Rabbi Yehuda, he did so because without his statement there would be no way to know where the *halacha* fell — since the general principles are not operative from his perspective. What Rav Mesharshya was therefore saying, the Gemara now clarifies, is not that these principles never apply, but that they are not universally held. Rabbi Yochanan may hold of them; Rav does not. The rules are thus not a consensus position, and knowing that Rabbi Yochanan formulated them does not mean one can rely on them in all contexts or according to all authorities.

## Three Month Wait

### Paternity Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: הַיְּבָמָה לֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ וְלֹא תִּתְיַיבֵּם עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ לָהּ שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara brings a proof **from that which we learned** in another mishna with regard to a woman waiting for her brother-in-law, i.e., a woman whose husband died without children but who is survived by a brother. The brother-in-law is obligated by Torah law either to perform levirate marriage with his deceased brother's widow, or to free her to marry others by participating in *ḥalitza*. **The woman waiting for her brother-in-law may neither participate in *ḥalitza* nor undergo levirate marriage until three months have passed** following her husband's death, due to concern that she may be pregnant from him, in which case she is exempt from levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*. After the three-month waiting period it will become clear whether she is pregnant from her husband.

The Gemara continues to the next *mishnah* with *ela mei-ha*. The new topic is the *halacha* requiring women to wait three months after widowhood or divorce before remarrying. The case of the *yevamah* is presented first: a woman whose husband died childless, leaving a surviving brother who must perform either *yibum* or *chalitzah*. The *mishnah* rules that neither *yibum* nor *chalitzah* may be performed until three months have elapsed from the husband's death. The concern is determining whether the widow is pregnant. If she is, *yibum* would constitute the grave prohibition of *eishas ach* — the biblical prohibition of a brother's wife — since *yibum* is mandated only in the absence of children, and a pregnant widow is not childless. Performing *chalitzah* when pregnant would be similarly problematic: the *chalitzah* would not have been necessary, yet someone might later see that she married a *kohen*, not knowing that the *chalitzah* was nullified by the subsequent discovery of her pregnancy, and draw the false conclusion that a *chalutzah* is permitted to a *kohen*.

### Blanket Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן שְׁאָר כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים לֹא יִנָּשְׂאוּ וְלֹא יִתְאָרְסוּ עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ לָהֶן שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, אֶחָד בְּתוּלוֹת וְאֶחָד בְּעוּלוֹת, אֶחָד אַלְמָנוֹת וְאֶחָד גְּרוּשׁוֹת, אֶחָד אֲרוּסוֹת וְאֶחָד נְשׂוּאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, all other women may not be married or** even **betrothed until three months have passed** following their divorce or the death of their husbands, **whether** they are **virgins or non-virgins, whether** they are **widows or divorcees,** and **whether** they became widowed or divorced when they were **betrothed or married.** In all cases, the woman may not marry for three months. Otherwise, if she is within the first three months of her pregnancy from her first husband, and she gives birth six months later, a doubt would arise as to the identity of the father. The Sages did not differentiate between cases where this concern is applicable and where it is not; rather, they fixed a principle that applies universally.

The *mishnah* then generalizes: *ve-chen shaar kol ha-nashim* — so too all other women whose marriages ended through death or divorce must not enter into *nisuin* or even *eirusin* until three months have elapsed. The stated concern in these cases is paternity: if a woman remarries quickly and a child is born, it may be unclear whether the child belongs to the first or second husband — a matter of enormous *halachic* significance. Understanding *nisuin* as the concern is natural, since it is through *nisuin* that husband and wife begin living together and the possibility of pregnancy arises. The extension to *eirusin* — the formal betrothal that does not yet involve cohabitation — is a *lo plug*: the Rabbis did not want to permit *eirusin* lest it lead to permitting *nisuin*.

The *mishnah* applies this rule without distinction: *echad betulot ve-echad be-ulot* — whether or not the woman is known to have had marital relations; *echad almanot ve-echad gerushot* — whether she is a widow or a divorcee; and even *echad arusot ve-echad nesuot* — whether her prior relationship was one of *nisuin* or merely *eirusin*. This last category is where the blanket rule becomes most striking: if she was only an *arusah* to her first husband — betrothed but never having lived with him or had relations — there is no logical basis for concern about pregnancy from the first husband. Similarly, if the second relationship is only *eirusin* with no intention of immediate *nisuin*, the paternity concern does not apply. Yet the *mishnah*'s blanket formulation covers all cases alike. This is the *lo plug* principle at its most sweeping: rather than require courts and communities to navigate complex distinctions, a uniform rule is imposed even where its underlying rationale does not apply. The practical implications are serious — a seventy-year-old woman receiving a *get* is still told she must wait three months — and the Gemara will engage with how far this blanket rule actually extends.

### Rabbi Yehuda Logical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נְשׂוּאוֹת — יִתְאָרְסוּ. וַאֲרוּסוֹת — יִנָּשְׂאוּ, חוּץ מֵאֲרוּסָה שֶׁבִּיהוּדָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלִּבּוֹ גַּס בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** A woman who had been **married** when she became widowed or divorced **may be betrothed** immediately, as couples do not have relations during the period of their betrothal. However, she may not marry until three months have passed, in order to differentiate between any possible offspring from the first and second husband. A woman who had only been **betrothed** when she became widowed or divorced **may be married** immediately, as it may be assumed that the couple did not have relations during the period of their betrothal. This is **except for a betrothed woman in Judea, because** there the bridegroom's **heart is bold,** as it was customary for couples to be alone together during the period of betrothal, and consequently there is a suspicion that they might have had relations, in which case she might be carrying his child. However, no similar concern applies in other places.

Rabbi Yehuda takes the logically differentiated position. He holds that *nesuot yisarsu ve-arusot yinaseu*: a woman who had *nisuin* with her first husband may have *eirusin* with the second — since the two are not yet cohabitating — and a woman who had only *eirusin* with the first husband may have *nisuin* with the second — since there is no possibility of pregnancy from the first. Rabbi Yehuda adds one exception: *arusa she-bi-Yehuda* — an *arusah* in Yehudah is treated with greater stringency, because in Yehudah it was customary for the *chatan* and *kallah* to spend time alone together even before *nisuin*, creating the possibility of relations having occurred after betrothal.

### Rabbi Yosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים יִתְאָרְסוּ, חוּץ מִן הָאַלְמָנָה — מִפְּנֵי הָאִיבּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: All the women** listed above **may be betrothed** immediately, because the decree applies only with regard to marriage; this is **except for a widow,** who must wait for a different reason, **because** of the **mourning** for her deceased husband.

Rabbi Yosi goes further in the lenient direction: *kol ha-nashim yisarsu* — all women may have *eirusin* immediately, without waiting three months. She agrees with Rabbi Yehuda that *eirusin* poses no genuine concern for any of these women. Her sole exception is the widow who is still in the period of *aveilus* for her first husband: *chutz min ha-almana mipnei ha-eivel* — a widow should not enter into *eirusin* during her mourning period. This is a distinct concern, rooted not in paternity but in the integrity of mourning. Whether obligations of *pru u-rvu* or other considerations might in some circumstances override the concern of *aveilus* is a matter explored at length in *Tosafos* and by Rabbeinu Tam, but that discussion lies beyond our present focus.

### Yochanan Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִינַן: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לָא עָל לְבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא. אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַסִּי דַּהֲוָה קָאֵים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֲמוּר בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אֲמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. מִכְּלָל דִּיחִידָאָה פְּלִיג עֲלֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And we said** with regard to this: It once happened that **Rabbi Eliezer did not come to the study hall. He met Rabbi Asi, who was standing,** and **said to him: What did they say** today **in the study hall? He said to him** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** Rabbi Eliezer asked: **By inference,** can it be inferred from the fact that the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion that only **a single** authority **disagrees with him?**

Rabbi Elazar had been absent from the *beit midrash* on the day this ruling was discussed. He encountered Rabbi Assi and asked him for a summary of what had been taught. Rabbi Assi reported that Rabbi Yochanan had ruled: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yosi* — we rule like Rabbi Yosi. The Gemara notes that implicit in this explicit ruling is the assumption that the position opposing Rabbi Yosi is a *da'at yachid* — a lone opinion. Both Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yehuda agree that *eirusin* is permitted immediately; the only opposing voice is the anonymous *stam* of the *mishnah* that prohibits it. The question of whether the *stam* represents a *da'at yachid* or a *da'at rabbim* is thus significant in evaluating how widely the opposing position reaches.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי? וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resumes its question: **Why do I need** Rabbi Yoḥanan to state that the *halakha* is in accordance with Rabbi Yosei? **Didn't you say:** In a dispute between **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** and therefore the *halakha* should be in accordance with him here as well? This implies that the principle is not to be relied upon.

Now the challenge runs in the other direction. Rabbi Yochanan was himself the one who articulated the general principle that when Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosi dispute, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi. The *stam* of the *mishnah*, which forbids *eirusin*, has been identified as representing a *lo plug* position — precisely the kind of categorical decree (*gezeira*) characteristically associated with Rabbi Meir. Why then did Rabbi Yochanan need to issue an explicit ruling? The principle already dictates the outcome: Rabbi Yosi against Rabbi Meir — follow Rabbi Yosi.

## Mishnah vs Baraita (II)

### Baraita Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי — בְּמַתְנִיתִין, אֲבָל בְּבָרַיְיתָא — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: It was** nonetheless **necessary** to issue this ruling, **it** could have **entered your mind to say** that **this** principle **applies** only with regard to disputes **in the Mishna. But** with regard to disputes **in a *baraita*, say no,** the principle does not apply. Therefore, Rabbi Yoḥanan **is teaching us** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei in this case as well.

Abaye provides the resolution. He explains: *itztarich* — the explicit ruling was necessary. One might have thought that the principle by which Rabbi Yosi's position prevails over Rabbi Meir's applies only when the dispute is recorded in a *mishnah* — since *mishnayot* were edited with great care and the attribution of positions to named Tannaim can be relied upon with confidence. A *baraita*, however, was not edited with the same precision, and there is room to doubt whether the attributed positions are correctly recorded. As Rashi explains, when it comes to a *baraita*, even if the *klalim* would otherwise apply, there is insufficient certainty that we have the names right to rely on the principle alone. The Gemara's earlier cases were all drawn from *mishnayot*; the case of the three-month wait and Rabbi Yosi's position in the *baraita* requires an explicit ruling because one cannot automatically extend the *klalim* to *baraitot* with the same confidence.

This answer also explains why one might have been uncertain about whether to follow Rabbi Yosi here even though the general principle would seem to apply: the *klalim* may not apply to *baraitot* with the same certainty as to *mishnayot*.

### Shmuel's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: הָנֵי כְּלָלֵי לָאו דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל נִינְהוּ, דְּהָא רַב לֵית לֵיהּ הָנֵי כְּלָלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Since no proof has been found to support Rav Mesharshiya's statement that there are no principles for issuing halakhic rulings, the Gemara emends his statement. **Rather, this** is what Rav Mesharshiya **is saying: These principles were not accepted by all** authorities, **as** in fact **Rav did not accept these principles,** as demonstrated above.

There is an additional reason why Rabbi Yochanan's explicit ruling was needed. Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel rules: *halacha ke-Rabbi Meir bi-gzeirotav* — we follow Rabbi Meir specifically when he is making a *gezeira*, a categorical preventive decree. This is precisely what is happening here: Rabbi Meir's insistence on a uniform three-month rule regardless of circumstances is not a substantive argument about the likelihood of pregnancy in any particular case. It is a *lo plug* decree — a *gezeira* designed to keep the rule simple and universally applicable. According to Shmuel's principle, this is exactly the type of ruling where we follow Rabbi Meir.

### Contradiction

This creates a direct conflict between two operative principles: the *klal* that we follow Rabbi Yosi against Rabbi Meir, and Shmuel's principle that we follow Rabbi Meir in his *gezeirot*. Both principles apply here, and they point in opposite directions. It was therefore essential for Rabbi Yochanan to state explicitly that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi — because without that explicit ruling, one might have applied Shmuel's competing principle instead. The explicit statement is thus not redundant but necessary precisely because two rules are in tension and only a direct ruling can resolve the conflict.

## Pagan Markets

### Permitted Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: הוֹלְכִין לְיָרִיד שֶׁל נׇכְרִים וְלוֹקְחִים מֵהֶן בְּהֵמָה וַעֲבָדִים וּשְׁפָחוֹת, בָּתִּים שָׂדוֹת וּכְרָמִים, וְכוֹתֵב וּמַעֲלֶה בְּעַרְכָּאוֹת שֶׁלָּהֶן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמַצִּיל מִיָּדָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the proof that these principles do not apply is **from that which was taught** in the following *baraita*: **One may go to a fair of** idolatrous **gentiles and buy animals, slaves, and maidservants from them,** as the purchase raises them to a more sanctified state; and he may buy **houses, fields, and vineyards** from them, due to the mitzva to settle Eretz Yisrael; **and he may write** the necessary deeds **and confirm** them **in their** gentile **courts** with an official seal, even though this involves an acknowledgement of their authority, **because it is as though he were rescuing** his property **from their hands,** as the court's confirmation and stamp of approval prevents the sellers from appealing the sale and retracting it.

The Gemara introduces the next case with *ela mei-ha*, now turning to a *baraita*. The *baraita* teaches that one may attend non-Jewish market days (*yarid shel nochrim*) for the specific purpose of purchasing certain categories of items that we prefer to see under Jewish ownership: servants, animals, houses, fields, and vineyards in the Land of Israel. Normally, participation in such market days raises concerns because these markets were organized in connection with pagan festivals. Yet the *baraita* permits attendance when the purpose is acquiring items that bring important resources under Jewish control — servants and animals who are thereby brought under the wings of the *Shechina* in a Jewish household, and real estate in *Eretz Yisrael* which Jewish ownership helps preserve. Furthermore, the *baraita* permits registering such transactions in non-Jewish courts — *ke-chotev u-ma'aleh be-arkaot shelahem* — even though Jews are generally discouraged from using non-Jewish legal forums. The justification in both cases is *ke-matzil mi-yadam*: one is effectively rescuing these resources from non-Jewish hands, which overrides the usual concerns.

### Kohen Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיָה כֹּהֵן — מִטַּמֵּא בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָדוּן וּלְעַרְעֵר עִמָּהֶן. וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁמִּטַּמֵּא בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ — כָּךְ מִטַּמֵּא בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he is a priest, he may become ritually impure** by going **outside Eretz** Yisrael, where the earth and air are impure, in order **to litigate** with them **and to contest** their claims. **And just as** a priest **may become ritually impure** by going **outside Eretz** Yisrael, **so may he become ritually impure** for this purpose by entering **into a cemetery.**

The *baraita* continues with a further allowance: if the person seeking to acquire land in Israel is a *kohen*, he may travel to *chutz la'aretz* for the purpose of defending the acquisition in non-Jewish courts, even though *chutz la'aretz* carries the status of *tumah* and *kohanim* are normally forbidden from leaving *Eretz Yisrael* and thereby becoming *tameh*. He may even enter what is classified as a *beit ha-pras* — a field with rabbinic-level grave *tumah* — though the *baraita* clarifies that entering a full *beit ha-kevarot* with biblical *tumah* would not be permitted for this purpose.

### Marriage Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִטַּמֵּא לִישָּׂא אִשָּׁה, וְלִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֵימָתַי — בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין מוֹצֵא לִלְמוֹד, אֲבָל מוֹצֵא לִלְמוֹד — לֹא יִטַּמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** a priest **may** likewise **become ritually impure** and leave Eretz Yisrael in order **to marry a woman or to study Torah** there. **Rabbi Yehuda said: When** does this allowance apply? When **he cannot find** a place **to study** in Eretz Yisrael. **But** if the priest can find a place **to study** in Eretz Yisrael, **he may not become ritually impure** by leaving the country.

The *baraita* adds two further circumstances under which a *kohen* may leave *Eretz Yisrael* and become *tameh* in *chutz la'aretz*: to get married, and to learn Torah. These correspond to the two purposes for which one may sell even a *Sefer Torah* — the two supreme personal obligations that override otherwise operative restrictions.

### Yehuda Restricts

Rabbi Yehuda qualifies the allowance to travel to *chutz la'aretz* for Torah study: *eimatai? bi-zman she-ein motze lilmod* — this is permitted only when no adequate *yeshiva* exists in *Eretz Yisrael*. If a sufficiently good *yeshiva* can be found in the Land, there is no justification for leaving. Notably, Rabbi Yehuda does not apply this same restriction to marriage — presumably he accepts that the consideration of finding a suitable spouse is not bounded by the same geographic logic.

### Yosi Allows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף בִּזְמַן שֶׁמּוֹצֵא לִלְמוֹד — נָמֵי יִטַּמָּא, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מִן הַכֹּל זוֹכֶה אָדָם לִלְמוֹד. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּיוֹסֵף הַכֹּהֵן שֶׁהָלַךְ אֵצֶל רַבּוֹ לְצַיְדָּן לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Even when he can find** a place **to study** Torah in Eretz Yisrael, **he may also** leave the country and **become ritually impure, because** a person does not merit to learn from everyone,** and it is possible that the only suitable teacher for him lives outside of Eretz Yisrael. **And Rabbi Yosei reported** in support of his position: **It once happened that Yosef the priest went to his teacher in Tzeidan,** outside Eretz Yisrael, **to learn Torah,** although the preeminent Sage of his generation, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, lived in Eretz Yisrael.

Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *af bi-zman she-motze lilmod nami yitameh* — even if there is a good *yeshiva* in *Eretz Yisrael*, the *kohen* may still go to *chutz la'aretz* to learn Torah. His reasoning is *she-ein min ha-kol zocheh lilmod* — not everyone merits to learn from just any teacher. Even if the *yeshiva* in Israel is excellent by any objective standard, a student has the right to seek out the particular *rebbe* from whom he personally can best learn, and he cannot be told that the presence of other teachers nearby is sufficient. Rabbi Yosi then cites a precedent: the story of Yosef ha-Kohen, who traveled to his teacher in Sidon — which is in *chutz la'aretz* — for the purpose of Torah study.

### Yochanan Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וּלְמָה לִי? וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** about this: **The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** The Gemara asks: **Why** was it necessary for Rabbi Yoḥanan to issue this ruling? **Didn't you say:** In disputes between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** and so it should be obvious that this *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion? Apparently, this principle is not accepted.

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yosi* — we follow Rabbi Yosi. The challenge is immediately apparent: this is a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi, and the established principle is that when they dispute, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi. Why did Rabbi Yochanan need to state the ruling explicitly?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי — בְּמַתְנִיתִין, אֲבָל בְּבָרַיְיתָא — אֵימָא לָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: It was** nonetheless **necessary** to issue this ruling, **it** could have **entered your mind to say** that **this** principle **applies** only with regard to disputes **in the Mishna. But** with regard to disputes **in a *baraita*, say no,** the principle does not apply. Therefore, Rabbi Yoḥanan **is teaching us** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei in this case as well.

Abaye's answer is the same as before: the principle applies with confidence to *mishnayot*, where the editorial care of Rabbi Yehuda ha-Nasi ensures that the names and positions are correctly attributed. A *baraita*, however, was not edited with the same rigor — as Rashi explains, the concern is not that the *baraita* is unreliable in a wholesale sense, but that it is not reliable enough to automatically apply the *klalim* without additional confirmation. Therefore, Rabbi Yochanan's explicit ruling was necessary to establish that we follow Rabbi Yosi even in this *baraita*.

## Non-Jewish Objects

### Shmuel Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חֶפְצֵי נָכְרִי אֵין קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to addressing acquisition of residence. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Objects belonging to a gentile do not acquire residence** and do not have a Shabbat limit, either on their own account or due to the ownership of the gentile. Accordingly, if they were brought into a town from outside its limits, a Jew may carry them two thousand cubits in each direction.

The Gemara now shifts to a separate topic that will occupy the remainder of the daf: the *techum* status of objects owned by non-Jews. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel states: *cheftzei nochri ein konim shevita* — objects owned by non-Jews do not acquire a fixed place. This means that such objects, when they come into a Jew's possession or vicinity on *Shabbat*, are not limited to the 2000 *amot* that objects owned by Jews are restricted to. They have no *techum* limit.

### No Owner Techumin

The reasoning emerges from the general principle that owned objects acquire their *Shabbat* rest in accordance with the *techum* of their owner. The owner of these objects is a non-Jew, who has no *Shabbat techum* restriction at all — a non-Jew may travel wherever he wishes on *Shabbat*. Therefore, objects owned by a non-Jew, being defined by their owner's *techum*, are similarly without limit.

The Gemara sharpens the point by asking according to whom Shmuel's ruling must be directed. If according to the Rabbis — who hold that ownerless objects (*cheftzei hefker*) do not acquire a fixed place — then Shmuel's ruling seems obvious. If ownerless objects, which have no owner to define their *techum*, have no 2000-*amot* limit, then objects owned by a non-Jew, whose owner's *techum* is unlimited, should *a fortiori* have no limit. The Gemara therefore concludes that Shmuel's statement must be directed according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who holds that ownerless objects *do* acquire a fixed place, with a 2000-*amot* limit measured from where they were when *Shabbat* began. Shmuel comes to teach that even Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri would agree that objects owned by a non-Jew are different: since they have an owner, and that owner's *techum* is unlimited, the objects themselves are not restricted to 2000 *amot*.

### Baraita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הַשּׁוֹאֵל כְּלִי מִן הַנׇּכְרִי בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְכֵן הַמַּשְׁאִיל לוֹ לַנׇּכְרִי כְּלִי מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב וְהֶחְזִירוֹ לוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְהַכֵּלִים וְהָאוֹצָרוֹת שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם — יֵשׁ לָהֶן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ. וְנׇכְרִי שֶׁהֵבִיא לוֹ פֵּירוֹת מִחוּץ לַתְּחוּם — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יְזִיזֵם מִמְּקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita*. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** With regard to a Jew **who borrowed a utensil from a gentile on a Festival, and similarly** with regard to a Jew **who lent a utensil to a gentile on the eve of a Festival and** the gentile **returned it to him on the Festival, and** likewise **utensils or bins that acquired residence within the** city's Shabbat **limit,** in all these cases the utensils **have,** i.e., can be moved, **two thousand cubits in each direction. But if a gentile brought** the Jew **produce from outside** the Shabbat **limit,** the Jew **may not move it from its place.**

The Gemara raises a challenge from a *baraita*. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar rules regarding two scenarios: one where a vessel was borrowed from a non-Jew during *Yom Tov* — meaning the object began *Yom Tov* in the non-Jew's possession — and one where a vessel was lent to a non-Jew before *Erev Yom Tov* and returned during *Yom Tov*, again meaning it began *Yom Tov* in non-Jewish possession. In both cases, the *baraita* states: *yesh lahem alpayim amah le-chol ruach* — they have 2000 *amot* in every direction from where they began *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov*. The *baraita* continues: a non-Jew who brought fruit from outside the *techum* — meaning the fruit began *Shabbat* more than 2000 *amot* outside the city — may not be moved from its place. This strongly implies that objects owned by a non-Jew at the onset of *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* do have a *techum* of 2000 *amot*, contrary to Shmuel's ruling.

### Positions Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי חֶפְצֵי נׇכְרִי קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה, הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי הִיא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי חֶפְצֵי הַנׇּכְרִי אֵין קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה, הָא מַנִּי — לָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי וְלָא רַבָּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted if you say that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri holds** that **objects that belong to a gentile acquire residence,** one can say that **this** *baraita* is in accordance with **whose** opinion? **It** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** that even a gentile's objects acquire residence. **However, if you say that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri holds** that **objects belonging to a gentile do not acquire residence,** in accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** *baraita*? It is **neither** in accordance with that of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri nor** that of **the Rabbis.**

The Gemara works through the implications. If one follows Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position that *cheftzei hefker* acquire a fixed place — 2000 *amot* from where they are — then the *baraita*'s ruling that non-Jewish objects are similarly limited makes sense: Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri extends the same logic from ownerless objects to objects owned by a non-Jew, treating them as analogously fixed. The *baraita* would then reflect Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's view. But if one holds, following the Rabbis, that *cheftzei hefker* do not acquire a fixed place, then the *baraita*'s ruling that non-Jewish objects have 2000 *amot* fits neither the Rabbis nor Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri in the way Shmuel described him. The Gemara therefore says: according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, this *baraita* is readily understood. And Shmuel's ruling — that non-Jewish objects have no *techum* limit — was issued according to the Rabbis, but was nonetheless necessary because one might have thought there is a reason to treat non-Jewish-owned objects more like Jewish-owned objects due to possible confusion.

### Confusion Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי חֶפְצֵי הַנׇּכְרִי קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר כְּרַבָּנַן, וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ: לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא — מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: גְּזֵירָה בְּעָלִים דְּנׇכְרִי אַטּוּ בְּעָלִים דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֶפְצֵי נׇכְרִי קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה, גְּזֵירָה בְּעָלִים דְּנׇכְרִי אַטּוּ בְּעָלִים דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** say that **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri holds** that **a gentile's objects acquire residence,** and that **Shmuel,** who said that they do not acquire residence, **spoke in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis. And** with regard to **that which was difficult for you:** According to **what *halakha*,** we must ask — **you might have said** that the Sages should issue a **decree** in the case of **gentile owners** that his objects acquire residence in his location and that they may not be carried beyond two thousand cubits from that spot, lest people carry objects belonging to **a Jewish owners** beyond their two-thousand-cubit limit. Therefore, it **is teaching us** that no decree was issued. **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin,** however, **said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Objects that belong to a gentile** indeed **acquire residence,** due to the aforementioned **decree** issued in the case of **gentile owners due to** the case of **Jewish owners.**

This brings the Gemara to a third position. Rav Chiya bar Avin in the name of Rabbi Yochanan states: *cheftzei nochri konim shevita* — objects owned by non-Jews do have a 2000-*amot* limit. The reason is *gezeira ba'alim de-nochri atu ba'alim de-Yisrael* — a rabbinic decree that we treat ownership by a non-Jew analogously to ownership by a Jew, out of concern that people will confuse the two situations. Once an object is known to be owned — rather than ownerless — people might not stop to ask whether it is Jewish-owned or non-Jewish-owned, and allowing non-Jewish-owned objects to travel without restriction might lead to the erroneous conclusion that Jewish-owned objects can similarly be brought from beyond the *techum*. Rabbi Yochanan therefore decrees a 2000-*amot* limit even for non-Jewish objects.

The result is two reasons to hold that non-Jewish objects have a *techum* limit: the approach of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who treats such objects like *cheftzei hefker* with a 2000-*amot* limit measured from the object itself; and Rabbi Yochanan's approach, which is grounded in the rabbinic concern about confusion with Jewish-owned objects and presumably measures the 2000 *amot* from where the owner was when *Shabbat* began, since the logic is based on analogy to Jewish-owned objects.

## Rams Case

### Rava Allows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ דִּכְרֵי דַּאֲתוֹ לִמְבָרַכְתָּא, שְׁרָא לְהוּ רָבָא לִבְנֵי מָחוֹזָא לְמִיזְבַּן מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **certain rams were brought to** the town of **Mavrakhta** on Shabbat. **Rava permitted the residents of Meḥoza to purchase them** and take them home, although Mavrakhta was outside the Shabbat limit of Meḥoza and could be reached by the residents of Meḥoza only by way of an *eiruv* of Shabbat limits.

Against this background the Gemara presents a practical incident. Rams were brought to a city called Mavrakhta, having come from outside the *techum*. Rava permitted the residents of Mechuza to purchase them — presumably on *Yom Tov*, since rams would be *muktzeh* on *Shabbat*, and there are recognized allowances for purchasing food items on *Yom Tov* in an indirect fashion, such as taking the object and settling accounts afterward.

### Ravina Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חֶפְצֵי נׇכְרִי אֵין קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה. וְהָא שְׁמוּאֵל וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֶפְצֵי נׇכְרִי קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה, גְּזֵירָה בְּעָלִים דְּנׇכְרִי אַטּוּ בְּעָלִים דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל?!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rava: What is your reasoning** in permitting these rams? You must rely upon that which **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Objects belonging to a gentile do not acquire residence,** and so they are permitted even if they were brought to Meḥoza from outside the Shabbat limit. **Isn't** the principle, in disputes between **Shmuel and Rabbi Yoḥanan,** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan? And Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin** already **said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Objects that belong to a gentile acquire residence,** based on **a decree** in the case of **a gentile owner, due to** the case of **a Jewish owner.** The *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion.

Ravina challenged Rava: what is the basis for the permission? If Rava's ruling relies on Shmuel's position that *cheftzei nochri ein konim shevita* — that non-Jewish objects have no *techum* limit — that would justify the purchase. But the established rule is that when Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan dispute, the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan. And Rabbi Yochanan holds that non-Jewish objects do have a 2000-*amot* limit, as a *gezeira* paralleling Jewish-owned objects. These rams were brought from outside the *techum* and should be forbidden to be moved or purchased because they have traveled beyond their permitted 2000 *amot*.

### City as Four Amot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: לִיזְדַּבְּנוּ לִבְנֵי מְבָרַכְתָּא, דְּכוּלַּהּ מְבָרַכְתָּא לְדִידְהוּ כְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava reconsidered and said: Let** the rams **be sold** only **to the residents of Mavrakhta.** Although the rams acquired residence, and may be moved only four cubits as they were taken beyond their Shabbat limit, the legal status of **all Mavrakhta is like four cubits for them.** However, they may not be sold to the residents of Meḥoza, as the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

Rava's response draws on the foundational rule established at the very beginning of this *perek*: an object that was taken *chutz la-techum* and placed within a city is treated as having the entire city as its four *amot*. Once an object that originated outside the *techum* is brought within a walled city, it may be moved throughout the entire city — the city functions as an equivalent of the four *amot* that any person or object retains even when it has gone *chutz la-techum*. Rava therefore says: grant Ravina's premise entirely, and accept that these rams have gone *chutz la-techum* under Rabbi Yochanan's ruling. Nonetheless, once they have entered Mavrakhta — *dekula Mavrakhta ke-arba amot damya* — the entire city of Mavrakhta counts as their four *amot*, and anyone within the city may use them. Rashi specifies that this rule presupposes Mavrakhta was a walled city, and *Tosafot* concurs. Several *Rishonim*, however, raise the question of whether an unwalled city might similarly function as a unified domain for this purpose — a question with practical implications for cases such as being stranded in a city when *Shabbat* begins unexpectedly.

## Fishnet Boundary

### Fishnet Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: חֵרֶם שֶׁבֵּין תְּחוּמֵי שַׁבָּת — צָרִיךְ מְחִיצָה שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל לְהַפְסִיקוֹ. מְחַיֵּיךְ עֲלֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya taught** a *baraita*: **A water-filled ditch [*ḥerem*] that** lies **between** two **Shabbat limits requires** **an iron partition to divide it** into two separate areas, so that the residents of both places may draw water from it. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** would **laugh at this** teaching, as he deemed it unnecessary.

The Gemara presents its final case. Imagine a body of water positioned exactly at the boundary between the *techumin* of two different cities — so that the water on one side belongs to the *techum* of city A, and the water on the other side belongs to the *techum* of city B. A fishnet is laid across the middle of the water, marking the boundary line. The net itself creates no legal separation; it merely makes visible the problem that exists regardless.

The problem is this: the water of the lake is owned by the residents of the respective cities, and just as their personal movement is limited to 2000 *amot* from the city, so too their water — being owned — is limited to the *techum* of the city. The water on city A's side should not mix with the water on city B's side, because taking water from one side to the other would be moving it *chutz la-techum*. Yet water flows freely, and the net does nothing to stop it.

### Iron Wall

Rabbi Chiya says: *cherem shebein techumei Shabbat tzarich mechitza shel barzel lehafsika* — a fishnet dividing *techumin* requires an iron wall to create a meaningful separation. In other words, without such a wall, the waters are hopelessly mixed, and neither city can use the water freely.

### Yosi Laughs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי טַעְמָא קָא מְחַיֵּיךְ? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּתָנֵי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי לְחוּמְרָא, וְאִיהוּ סְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּרַבָּנַן לְקוּלָּא. ומִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר לְקוּלָּא, מַאן דְּתָנֵי לְחוּמְרָא מְחַיֵּיךְ עֲלֵהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why did** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, **laugh? If you say** that it is **because** Rabbi Ḥiyya **taught** the *baraita* **stringently, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** saying that ownerless objects acquire a place of residence, **and** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina **holds leniently, in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** and says that those objects do not acquire residence, this is difficult. Just **because he holds leniently, does he laugh at one who teaches stringently?**

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina laughed at this ruling. The Gemara asks why. One possibility is that he was mocking Rabbi Chiya for holding a strict position — requiring an iron wall — when by contrast Rabbi Yosi himself holds a lenient position, perhaps treating the water as analogous to *cheftzei hefker* without a *techum* limit. The Gemara pushes back on this: just because you hold a lenient position does not mean you may laugh at someone who holds a stricter one. A lenient position is legitimate, but so is a strict one, and ridicule is not warranted. And this is indeed a striking moment in the *sugya*, because the entire discussion earlier in the daf emphasized *halacha ke-divrei ha-meikel be-eruv* — that in *eruvin* we follow the lenient view. Yet here we see that there are indeed positions within the domain of *eruvin* that rule strictly, and mockery of such positions is rejected.

### Water Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא, מִשּׁוּם דְּתַנְיָא: נְהָרוֹת הַמּוֹשְׁכִין וּמַעְיָינוֹת הַנּוֹבְעִין — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּרַגְלֵי כׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** he must have laughed for a different reason, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Flowing rivers and streaming springs are like the feet of all people,** as the water did not acquire residence in any particular spot. Consequently, one who draws water from rivers and springs may carry it wherever he is permitted to walk, even if it had previously been located outside his Shabbat limit. According to Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, the same *halakha* should apply to the water in the ditch.

The real reason Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina laughed, the Gemara explains, is different. There is a *baraita* that teaches: *naharot ha-moshchin u-ma'yanot ha-novim harei hen ke-raglei kol adam* — flowing rivers and springs have no *techum* limitation; they belong to everyone. And while the Gemara considers whether perhaps the waters in question were stationary rather than flowing, the deeper point is that the Rabbis adopted a general policy of leniency in matters involving water. This emerges from the famous exchange between Rav Tabla and Rav: Rav Tabla asked whether a *mechitza teluyah* — a suspended wall that does not reach the ground — may serve as a valid *mechitza* on dry land. Rav answered that a suspended wall is valid only over water (*ba-mayim*), not on dry land. As the Gemara puts it: *ela kal hu she-hikilu chachamim ba-mayim* — this is a special leniency the Rabbis introduced specifically for bodies of water. They were willing to treat a non-continuous wall as legally sufficient over water in a way they would not accept on land, precisely because the alternative would deny communities access to water entirely.

By this same logic, Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina laughed at the requirement for an iron wall: given the Rabbis' established leniency regarding barriers and water, one should be able to use even an imperfect division — such as a fishnet or a reed wall — to serve as a sufficient *mechitza* between the two *techumin*. The fact that water passes through the net is no objection, because the Rabbis apply a fictional-wall standard over water; what matters is the defined separation, not its physical impermeability. Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina's point is thus that Rabbi Chiya's iron-wall requirement is not merely strict, but fails to account for the established rabbinic leniency in this domain — and that is what he found worthy of challenge.
