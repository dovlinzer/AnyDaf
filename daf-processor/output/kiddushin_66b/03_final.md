# Kiddushin 66b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Abaye-Rava Debate

### One vs. Two Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אִשְׁתּוֹ זִינְּתָה בְּעַד אֶחָד וְשׁוֹתֵק, מַהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נֶאֱמָן. רָבָא אָמַר: אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. הָוֵי דָּבָר שֶׁבְּעֶרְוָה, וְאֵין דָּבָר שֶׁבְּעֶרְוָה פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If a husband is told **by one witness** that **his wife committed adultery, and** the husband remains **silent, what is** the *halakha*? **Abaye said:** The witness is **deemed credible. Rava said: He is not deemed credible.** Why not? Because **it is a matter involving forbidden relations, and there is no matter** of testimony **for forbidden sexual relations** that can be attested to by **fewer than two** witnesses.

The daf opens with a debate between Abaye and Rava concerning the evidentiary standards required in matters of personal status — specifically, whether one witness suffices or whether two are required. The question is not merely about the formal act of *kiddushin* or *gittin*, where it is already established that two witnesses are needed for the act to take effect, but about proof after the fact: can one witness, even an uncontested one, establish a fact bearing on personal status? Abaye holds that one witness would be enough in such circumstances. Rava disagrees, invoking the principle of *ein davar shebiervah pachot mishnayim* — no matter touching on *erva*, personal status, or sexual propriety can be established by fewer than two witnesses. According to Rava, this principle operates in both directions: two witnesses are required both to effectuate such an act and to prove that it occurred.

The scope of the discussion extends well beyond *gittin* and *kiddushin* proper. Among the examples discussed: whether a woman was taken captive, which would render her forbidden to a *kohen*, or whether a woman committed adultery, which would render her forbidden to her husband. In all such cases, Rava insists, the standard of proof remains two witnesses.

### Rava's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, דִּתְנַן: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מַעֲשֶׂה בִּמְגוֹרָה שֶׁל דִּסְקִים בְּיַבְנֶה שֶׁהָיְתָה עוֹמֶדֶת בְּחֶזְקַת שְׁלֵימָה, וּמָדְדוּ וְנִמְצֵאת חֲסֵירָה –
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** **From where do I say** my claim that one does not rely on a lone witness in matters of forbidden relations? **As we learned** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon said: An incident** occurred **with regard to** a water **reservoir of Diskim in Yavne, which had the presumptive status of** being **complete,** i.e., they thought it contained forty *se'a*, the requisite amount for a ritual bath, **and they measured it** after a time **and it was found to be deficient,** as it contained less than that amount.

The Gemara opens with *mina meimra* — where do we derive this? — and points to a *mishnah* (also found in the *Tosefta*). The citation is introduced with *tennan*, indicating a *mishnah*-level source.

### Mikvah Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל טְהָרוֹת שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל גַּבָּהּ הָיָה רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן מְטַהֵר, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְטַמֵּא. אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: מִקְוֶה זֶה בְּחֶזְקַת שָׁלֵם הוּא עוֹמֵד, מִסָּפֵק אַתָּה בָּא לְחַסְּרוֹ – אַל תְּחַסְּרֶנּוּ מִסָּפֵק. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אָדָם זֶה בְּחֶזְקַת טָמֵא הוּא עוֹמֵד, מִסָּפֵק אַתָּה בָּא לְטַהֲרוֹ – אַל תְּטַהֲרֶנּוּ מִסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all** immersions of **ritual purification performed in** the reservoir before it was measured, **Rabbi Tarfon would render** them **ritually pure, and Rabbi Akiva** would **render** them **ritually impure.** The two Sages discussed the matter. **Rabbi Tarfon said: This ritual bath retained the presumptive status of** being **whole** throughout this period, and **you are coming to** declare it **deficient** in the past **out of uncertainty. Do not deem it deficient out of uncertainty. Rabbi Akiva said** in response: **This person** who immersed himself in that ritual bath **retained the presumptive status of** being **ritually impure** before he immersed. **You are coming to purify him out of uncertainty. Do not deem him ritually pure out of uncertainty.**

The *mishnah* presents the following case: there was a *mikvah* in a place called Diskim — Rashi understands this as either a personal or a place name — located in Yavneh. The *mikvah* had stood under the presumption (*chazakah*) of being *shalem*, complete, containing the required forty *se'ah* of valid water. At some point, a *chacham* measured it and found it deficient. The question becomes: what is the status of everything done using this *mikvah* since the last time it was verified as complete?

Rabbi Tarfon rules *metaher* — he considers everything done with the *mikvah* to have been valid. His reasoning rests on the principle of *chazakah*: the *mikvah* had an established status of completeness. A doubt about when exactly it became deficient does not override that status; we assume the *mikvah* remained *shalem* until the last possible moment, i.e., until the moment it was found to be lacking. As he articulates it: *mikvah zeh bechezkat shalem hu omed* — this *mikvah* stands in its presumption of completeness. One cannot retroactively undermine it on the basis of uncertainty.

Rabbi Akiva rules *metamei* — he considers everything done since the last verification to be suspect. His reasoning is that there is a competing *chazakah* to consider: *adam zeh bechezkat tamei hu omed* — the person who immersed had a pre-immersion status of *tumah*. That status too is established. The question of whether the person became *tahor* depends entirely on whether the *mikvah* was *kasher* at the time of immersion. Since that is now uncertain, one cannot presume the person's status changed. The doubt cuts both ways, and the question becomes which *chazakah* prevails.

### Kohen Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: מָשָׁל לְעוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ בֶּן חֲלוּצָה, שֶׁעֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said** in response: There is **a parable** that illustrates this. A priest was **standing and sacrificing** offerings **on the altar, and it became known that he is the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*.** The *halakha* is **that his** earlier **service** before this discovery remains **valid.**

Unable to resolve the competing *chazakot* directly, Rabbi Tarfon proposes an analogy: *mashal le'omed umakriv al gabbe hamizbeach* — imagine a *kohen* who has been standing and offering *korbanot*, and it then becomes known that he is a *ben gerushah* or *ben chalutzah*, i.e., a *chalal*. In that case, his *avodah* until that point is *kesheirah*. This is not merely a matter of presumption but a special *gezeirat hakatuv*, derived from a *pasuk*, that retroactive invalidation does not apply once the *avodah* was performed in ignorance. Rabbi Tarfon invokes this case to suggest a general policy consideration: just as one cannot issue recall notices invalidating years of priestly service, so too one cannot retroactively invalidate months of *taharot* performed using the *mikvah*.

There is, as the lecturer acknowledges, a notable imprecision in the analogy. The *mikvah* case involves a *safek* — genuine uncertainty about when the deficiency arose — whereas the *kohen* case involves a situation where the *pesul* was present throughout, and we are simply applying a special legal rule that suspends retroactive invalidation. Nonetheless, Rabbi Tarfon's point has a certain policy logic: the societal consequences of wholesale retroactive invalidation support leaning toward the *chazakah* that the *mikvah* remained *kasher*.

### Baal Mum Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: מָשָׁל לְעוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְנוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא בַּעַל מוּם, שֶׁעֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said:** A more accurate **parable** is that of a priest who was **standing and sacrificing on the altar, and it became known that he is blemished.** In this case, the *halakha* is **that his** earlier **service** is **disqualified.**

Rabbi Akiva responds with a counter-analogy: *mashal l'ma hadavar domeh* — imagine instead a *kohen* offering *korbanot* who is discovered to have a *mum*, a bodily blemish. In that case, his *avodah* is retroactively *pesulah*. This is a striking contrast: one might have supposed that being a *chalal* — lacking the fundamental *kedushat kehunah* — is a more severe disqualification than having a *baal mum*, who in many respects retains *kedushat kehunah* (he shares in the *korbanot*, he is subject to the restrictions of *kohanim*). And yet the *halacha* is precisely the reverse: for the *chalal*, past *avodah* is valid; for the *baal mum*, it is not.

Rabbi Akiva presses the point further. He offers a second distinction: *mikvah pesulah begufa, baal mum pasul begufo* — the *mikvah*'s deficiency is intrinsic to itself, and the *baal mum*'s deficiency is intrinsic to his own body. By contrast, the *ben gerushah* or *ben chalutzah* is *pesul me'acherim* — the disqualification comes from outside, from what his parents did. This structural parallel between the *mikvah* case and the *baal mum* case suggests that the *mikvah* should follow the rule of the *baal mum*, under which retroactive invalidation applies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: אַתָּה דִּימִּיתוֹ לְבַעַל מוּם, וַאֲנִי דִּמִּיתִיו לְבֶן גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ לְבֶן חֲלוּצָה, נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said: You compared** the case of a ritual bath found to be deficient **to** that of **a blemished** priest, **whereas I compared it to** the case of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza*. Let us see to which** case **it is similar.**

Rabbi Tarfon then states the question openly: *nireh l'mi domeh* — let us determine which analogy is more apt. If the *mikvah* case more closely resembles *ben gerushah*, we apply that rule; if it more closely resembles *baal mum*, we apply that rule.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִתְחִיל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לָדוּן: מִקְוֶה פְּסוּלוֹ בְּיָחִיד, וּבַעַל מוּם פְּסוּלוֹ בְּיָחִיד, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בִּשְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva began to analyze** the matter: **The disqualification of a ritual bath** is **by** the testimony of **an individual,** as witnesses are not required to establish that a ritual bath is deficient, **and** likewise **the disqualification of a blemished** priest with regard to performing the Temple service **is** by the testimony of **an individual. And do not let** the *halakha* of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* prove** otherwise, **as his disqualification is by** the testimony of **two** witnesses.

Rabbi Akiva then proceeds to argue the comparison systematically: *mikvah pesulah beyachid, baal mum pasul beyachid* — the *mikvah*'s deficiency can be established by a single person's testimony, and the *baal mum*'s deficiency likewise. Whereas *ben gerushah* and *ben chalutzah* — matters of personal status touching on *erva* — require two witnesses. The *mikvah* thus structurally resembles the *baal mum*, not the *ben gerushah*. One therefore applies the *baal mum* rule, and retroactive invalidation follows.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: מִקְוֶה פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ, בַּעַל מוּם פְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ בֶּן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ מֵאֲחֵרִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: עֲקִיבָא! כׇּל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמְּךָ כְּפוֹרֵשׁ מִן הַחַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** one can say: **The disqualification of a ritual bath is due to** the bath **itself,** and similarly, **the disqualification of a blemished** priest is **due to** the priest **himself. And do not let** the *halakha* of **the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* prove** otherwise, **as his disqualification is due to others,** i.e., through his mother. **Rabbi Tarfon said to him: Akiva, anyone who separates from you, it is as though he has separated from life** itself.

Rabbi Tarfon famously capitulates: *kol haparesh mimcha k'poresh min hachayim* — anyone who separates from you is as one who separates from life itself. The argument was conceded.

### Contested Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בְּיָחִיד הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּקָא מַכְחֵישׁ לֵיהּ, מִי מְהֵימַן? אֶלָּא דְּשָׁתֵיק,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the topic at hand: **What are the circumstances** with regard to **this blemished** priest **whose disqualification is by** means of **an individual** witness? **If** the priest **denies his** claim, maintaining that he is not blemished, **is** a lone witness **deemed credible? Rather,** it must be **that** the priest remains **silent** and therefore is considered to have admitted to the accusation.

The Gemara now takes stock of the actual probative value of this exchange for Rava's principle. The question is: in what circumstance does the *baal mum* case require only one witness? The Gemara asks: *hai baal mum d'pesulah beyachid heichi dami?* — in what scenario does a single person's testimony suffice for a *baal mum*? If the accused is contesting the testimony, we would not ordinarily credit a single witness against a denial. It must therefore be the case — says Rava — that the scenario is one of uncontested testimony: *ela d'shatik*, the *kohen* is not disputing it. And yet even such an uncontested single witness does not suffice for *ben gerushah* or *ben chalutzah*. This demonstrates that in matters of personal status, even uncontested testimony from a single witness is insufficient. Two witnesses are required, and that is Rava's proof.

### Uncontested Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם דְּקָא מַכְחֵישׁ לֵיהּ, וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ: ׳אַמַּאי מְהֵימַן׳ – דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁלַח אַחְוִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye** could have **said** in response to this proof: **Actually,** both cases deal **with** an individual **who denies** the witness's testimony, **and that which you said: Why is** the witness **deemed credible** when he says that this priest is blemished, it is referring to a case **where** the witness **said to him: Remove** your clothes and **show** us that you are not blemished.

Abaye responds: *l'olam d'ka machish lei* — in fact, the *kohen* is contesting the testimony. So how does the single witness prevail? *D'amar lei shlach achvei* — because the witness can say: if you are telling the truth that you have no *mum*, strip and demonstrate it. The single witness is believed not because the testimony is uncontested in the ordinary sense, but because there is a mechanism for verification: the subject's refusal to demonstrate constitutes corroboration of the witness's claim.

This explains the second distinction Rabbi Akiva drew: *baal mum pesulah begufo* — the *mum* is on the person's body, and is therefore in principle demonstrable. The *mikvah*'s deficiency is similarly intrinsic and demonstrable. Whereas *ben gerushah* and *ben chalutzah* — *pesulah me'acherim* — involve a fact about parentage that cannot be demonstrated physically. If a witness says this man is a *ben gerushah* and the *kohen* denies it, there is nothing to show. Therefore a single witness, even a contested one, does not suffice; two are required.

Abaye's position thus becomes: the distinction is between contested and uncontested testimony, not between one witness and two in the context of personal status per se. When testimony is contested and cannot be demonstrated or refuted, two are needed — as by *ben gerushah*. When testimony can be verified by physical demonstration, one witness may suffice even in a contested setting — as by *baal mum*. But for personal status where no physical verification is possible, one witness — contested or not — is insufficient.

The Gemara concludes that the debate between Abaye and Rava on this point remains unresolved by this source alone. We do *posken* like Rava — *ein davar shebiervah pachot mishnayim* applies both to effectuating and to proving matters of personal status — but the logical proof from this passage is not airtight.

One practical extension of this debate is worth noting: the principle applies not only to cases of invalidation but also in situations where a person must prove something favorable — a woman proving she is divorced, a convert proving the fact of conversion. The standard of proof is accordingly robust.

### Kohen Ben Gerusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן גְּרוּשָׁה וּבֶן חֲלוּצָה דַּעֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה, מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהָיְתָה לּוֹ וּלְזַרְעוֹ אַחֲרָיו״ – בֵּין זֶרַע כָּשֵׁר וּבֵין זֶרַע פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to the *halakha* of the *baraita*: **And from where do we** derive **that the service of the son of a divorced woman or the son of a *ḥalutza* is valid** after the fact? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: As the verse states** with regard to Pinehas the priest: **"And it shall be for him and his seed after him** the covenant of an everlasting priesthood" (Numbers 25:13), which includes **both fit seed and unfit seed.** This teaches that the service of a priest is valid after the fact even if he was disqualified.

The Gemara closes out this section by establishing the underlying source for the rule that a *kohen* discovered to be a *ben gerushah* or *ben chalutzah* does not have his prior *avodah* retroactively invalidated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מֵהָכָא: ״בָּרֵךְ ה׳ חֵילוֹ וּפֹעַל יָדָיו תִּרְצֶה״ – אֲפִילּוּ חוּלִּין שֶׁבּוֹ תִּרְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel's father said** that the proof text is **from here:** It states in the blessing of the tribe of Levi: **"Bless, Lord, his substance [*ḥeilo*], and accept the work of his hands"** (Deuteronomy 33:11). The word *ḥeilo* is expounded as including **even** the service of **his profane ones [*ḥullin*],** which God will **accept** after the fact.

Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel derives it from the verse regarding Pinchas: *v'haytah lo u'l'zaro acharav berit kehunat olam* — it shall be for him and for his descendants after him a covenant of eternal priesthood — which is interpreted to apply *bein zera kasher bein zera pasul*, to both valid and disqualified offspring. Even a *chalal* retains enough of the *kehunat olam* to validate past *avodah* performed in ignorance.

The father of Shmuel derives it from *barech Hashem cheilo u'fo'al yadav tirtzeh* — bless his service-strength and may the work of his hands find favor — understanding *cheilo* as a wordplay on *chulin*, and reading the verse as: even if it turns out there is *chulin* in him — i.e., he is a *chalal* — his *avodah* was *tirtzeh*, found acceptable, until the point of discovery.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: מֵהָכָא: ״וּבָאתָ אֶל הַכֹּהֵן אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם״, וְכִי תַּעֲלֶה עַל דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁאָדָם הוֹלֵךְ אֵצֶל כֹּהֵן שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּיָמָיו? אֶלָּא זֶה כָּשֵׁר וְנִתְחַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai said:** The source is **from here,** a verse stated with regard to first fruits: **"And you shall come to the priest who shall be in those days"** (Deuteronomy 26:3). **But can it enter your mind that a person can come to a priest who is not** alive **in his days?** What then is the meaning of the phrase "in those days"? **Rather, this** is referring to **a fit** priest **who** later **became** established as **a *ḥalal*.** All his previous days of service are considered those of a fit priest.

Rabbi Yannai derives it from *u'vata el hakohen asher yihyeh bayamim hahem* — you shall go to the *kohen* who is in your days. Obviously one can only go to the *kohen* available at the time; the verse thus validates the *avodah* of whichever *kohen* served during a given period, even if he was later discovered to be disqualified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעַל מוּם דַּעֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה, מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לָכֵן אֱמֹר הִנְנִי נֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת בְּרִיתִי שָׁלוֹם״ – כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׁלֵם וְלֹא כְּשֶׁהוּא חָסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis of the *baraita*. **From where do we** derive **that the service of a blemished** priest **is** retroactively **invalid? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: As the verse states** with regard to Pinehas: **"Wherefore say: Behold, I give to him My covenant of peace [*shalom*]"** (Numbers 25:12), which means that he receives the covenant **when he is whole [*shalem*], but not when he is** blemished and **lacking** a limb.

For the *baal mum*, the Gemara inverts the derivation. Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel reads *lachen emor hineni noten lo et briti shalom* — the covenant of peace — as applying *kshehu shalem*, only when he is physically complete, unblemished. The word *shalom* in this verse is written with a *vav ketia*, a broken or truncated *vav*, as a scribal tradition indicating that the wholeness the verse describes is conditional on physical integrity. Once there is a *mum*, the condition fails, and past *avodah* is retroactively *pesulah*.

The lecturer acknowledges difficulty with the internal logic of this distinction: intuitively, a *chalal* — who lacks *kedushat kehunah* entirely — seems more fundamentally disqualified than a *baal mum*, who retains *kedushat kehunah* and participates in *korbanot*. And yet the *halacha* holds the *baal mum*'s past *avodah* invalid while validating the *chalal*'s. The best available explanation tracks the distinction between something demonstrable and present — the *mum* was on the body, in principle perceptible — versus something more abstract and genealogical, which could not have been known without information from others. But the lecturer notes that this explanation remains imperfect.

## Yichus Rules

### No Aveirah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְאֵין עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר. וְאֵיזֶה זוֹ – זוֹ כֹּהֶנֶת, לְוִיָּה, וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִית שֶׁנִּשְּׂאוּ לְכֹהֵן, לֵוִי, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There is a principle with regard to the *halakhot* of lineage: **Any case where there is betrothal,** i.e., where the betrothal takes effect, **and** the marriage involves **no transgression** by Torah law, the lineage of **the offspring follows the male,** his father. **And** in **which** case **is this** applicable? For example, **this** is the case with regard to **the daughter of a priest;** or **the daughter of a Levite; or the daughter of an Israelite, who married a priest, a Levite, or an Israelite.** In all these cases the child's lineage is established by his father's family.

The next *mishnah* opens the broader inquiry into *yichus* — genealogical status — which encompasses the questions of *kohen*, *levi*, *Yisrael*, *mamzer*, *chalal*, and related categories. The *mishnah* articulates a general principle: *kol makom sheyesh kiddushin v'ein aveirah, havalad holech achar hazachar* — wherever *kiddushin* is binding and no transgression occurred, the child's status follows the father.

### Paternal Descent

The illustrative case: a woman who is a *kohenet*, *leviyah*, or *Yisraelit* marries a man who is a *kohen*, *levi*, or *Yisrael*. The child follows the father's status in all cases, regardless of the mother's status. This is the straightforward application of paternal descent in a permitted marriage.

### Aveirah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְיֵשׁ עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם. וְאֵיזֶה זוֹ – זוֹ אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַמְזֶרֶת וּנְתִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמַמְזֵר וּלְנָתִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And any case where there is** a valid **betrothal and** yet **there is a transgression, the offspring follows the flawed** parent. **And** in **which** case **is this** applicable? For example, **this** is the case of **a widow** who is married **to a High Priest,** or **a divorced woman or a *ḥalutza*** who is married **to a common priest,** or **a *mamzeret* or a Gibeonite woman** who is married **to an Israelite,** or **an Israelite woman** who is married **to a *mamzer* or to a Gibeonite.** In these situations the child inherits the status of the blemished parent.

The *mishnah* continues: *v'kol makom sheyesh kiddushin v'yesh aveirah, havalad holech achar hapagum* — wherever *kiddushin* is binding but a transgression occurred, the child follows the disqualification. It does not matter whether the *pesul* comes from the father's side or the mother's side; the stigma passes to the child.

The illustrative cases of forbidden *kohen* marriages: an *almanah* with a *kohen gadol*, or a *gerushah* or *chalutzah* with a *kohen hedyot*. In these unions *kiddushin* is technically binding (as these are *issurei lavin*, not *issurei karet*), but transgression has occurred. The child does not inherit the precise status of *gerushah* per se, but becomes a *chalal* — disqualified from priestly service and the associated privileges and restrictions. The concept is that the disqualifying status passes on in some form regardless.

Similarly, if a woman who is a *mamzeret* or *netinah* marries a *Yisrael* (or vice versa — a *mamzer* marrying a *bat Yisrael*), *kiddushin* is binding, but the child is a *mamzer*. The principle of *holech achar hapagum* applies directly: the child carries the disqualified status.

### No Kiddushin/Others Poss…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מִי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ עָלָיו קִידּוּשִׁין, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לָהּ עַל אֲחֵרִים קִידּוּשִׁין – הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in **any** case **where** a woman **cannot** join in **betrothal with** a particular man, as the betrothal does not take effect, **but she can** join in **betrothal with others,** i.e., the woman is considered a member of the Jewish people and can marry other Jews, in these cases **the offspring is a *mamzer*.**

The *mishnah* then addresses a woman with whom *kiddushin* is not binding at all regarding this particular man, but who could be validly married to others: *havalad mamzer*. The child is a *mamzer*. The paradigm case here is a married woman — her husband could marry her, but she cannot create *kiddushin* with this other man. Any issue of such a union, being from *arayot*, is a *mamzer*.

### Matrilineal Descent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מִי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ לֹא עָלָיו וְלֹא עַל אֲחֵרִים קִידּוּשִׁין – הַוָּלָד כְּמוֹתָהּ. וְאֵיזֶה זֶה – זֶה וְלַד שִׁפְחָה וְנׇכְרִית.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in **any** case **where** a woman **cannot** join in **betrothal with him or with others, the offspring is like her.** He is not considered his father's son at all, but has the same status as his mother. **And** in **which** case **is this** applicable? **This is the offspring of** a Canaanite **maidservant or a gentile woman,** as her child is a slave or a gentile like her. If he converts, he is not a *mamzer*.

The final category: *kol mi she'ein lah lo alav v'lo al acherim kiddushin, havalad kamosah* — any woman with whom no *kiddushin* is possible at all, with anyone, and the child follows the mother's status. The cases: a non-Jewish woman, or a slave. No Jew can contract valid *kiddushin* with either. The child accordingly is not a *mamzer* — that category presupposes a prohibited but potentially valid Jewish marital nexus — but is rather a non-Jew or a slave, following the mother.

This is, as the Gemara will shortly elaborate upon, the foundational principle of matrilineal descent. Where no *kiddushin* can exist, the child's status is determined entirely by the mother.

## Rule Exceptions

### Shimon's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּלָלָא הוּא דְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְאֵין עֲבֵירָה הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that **any case where there is betrothal** and the marriage involves no transgression, the lineage of the offspring follows the male. **Rabbi Shimon said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: Is** it an established **principle** that **any case where there is betrothal and there is no transgression the offspring** invariably **follows the male? But**

The Gemara immediately challenges the *mishnah*'s framing as a universal principle. Rabbi Shimon asks Rabbi Yochanan: *klala hu?* — is this really a complete and reliable rule?

### Ger-Mamzeret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא מַמְזֶרֶת, דְּיֵשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְאֵין עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם, דְּתַנְיָא: גֵּר שֶׁנָּשָׂא מַמְזֶרֶת – הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** there is the case of **a convert who married a *mamzeret*, where there is** a valid **betrothal and there is no transgression,** as they are permitted to marry each other, and yet **the offspring follows the flawed** lineage and is a *mamzer*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a convert who married a *mamzeret*, the offspring is a *mamzer*.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

The challenge: consider a *ger* who married a *mamzeret*. According to some opinions, such a marriage is permitted — a *ger* is allowed to marry a *mamzeret*. If so, there is *kiddushin* and there is no *aveirah*, yet the child is still a *mamzer*. The child follows the *pagum* despite the absence of transgression — which contradicts the first clause of the *mishnah*.

A *baraita* is cited: *ger shenasa mamzeret, havalad mamzer* — if a *ger* marries a *mamzeret*, the child is a *mamzer*. This is the position of Rabbi Yose.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא? מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: גֵּר לֹא יִשָּׂא מַמְזֶרֶת, וְיֵשׁ קִידּוּשִׁין וְיֵשׁ עֲבֵירָה – הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Rabbi Shimon: **Do you maintain** that **the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei?** Not so; **the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says: A convert may not marry a *mamzeret*, and** therefore in this case **there is betrothal and there is a transgression,** which is why **the offspring follows the flawed** lineage.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *mi savart masnitin Rabbi Yose hi?* — who says our *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yose? The *mishnah* may reflect the view of Rabbi Yehudah, who holds that a *ger* is not permitted to marry a *mamzeret* in the first place. According to Rabbi Yehudah, such a union involves *kiddushin* with *aveirah*, and therefore the child following the *pagum* is entirely consistent with the *mishnah*'s second clause. The apparent exception dissolves once we assign the *mishnah* to Rabbi Yehudah rather than Rabbi Yose.

### Kol Makom Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנְיַיהּ! תְּנָא ״כׇּל מָקוֹם״ דְּסֵיפָא, לְאֵתוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **let** the mishna **teach** this case as one of its examples. The Gemara answers: The mishna **taught** the principle: **Any case** where there is betrothal and a transgression, **in the latter clause,** precisely **to include** this kind of case.

The Gemara then takes up the question of completeness. Even if the *mishnah*'s rule is internally consistent, there are cases that fit the rule but are not listed as examples, and cases that are exceptions to the rule that are also unlisted. How do we account for both?

The Gemara answers with a dual hermeneutical move. The phrase *kol makom* — wherever — at the opening of each clause signals a general principle intended to include additional cases beyond those explicitly listed. The phrase *v'eizu zu* — and which is this? — following each list signals that the list is exhaustive for its particular formulation, thereby implicitly acknowledging that other cases may deviate from the rule.

The Gemara proceeds through candidate cases. A *chalal* who marries a *bat Yisrael*: *kiddushin* is binding, no *aveirah*, and the child follows the father — he is a *chalal*. This is an application of the rule not listed in the *mishnah*. A *Yisrael* who marries a *chalala*: *kiddushin* is binding, no *aveirah*, and the child follows the father — the child is *kasher*, not a *chalala*. This too is an application of the rule not listed.

### Chalal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, וּתְנָא ״אֵיזוֹ זוֹ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** a different answer: **Actually,** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and** it **taught** the phrase: In **which** case **is this** applicable, **to exclude** cases of betrothal without a transgression other than those listed in the mishna.

The Gemara asks: if these are applications of the rule, why were they not listed explicitly? The answer is that they cannot be framed neatly within the *mishnah*'s formulation. The *mishnah* lists cases of *kohenet*, *leviyah*, and *Yisraelit* marrying a *kohen*, *levi*, or *Yisrael* — implying that any of these women could marry any of these men. But a *chalala* cannot marry a *kohen*. Were the *mishnah* to include *v'chalala* in the list of women who married a *kohen*, *levi*, or *Yisrael*, it would imply that a *chalala* marrying a *kohen* is among the scenarios — which is forbidden. The *mishnah* cannot include the case without distorting its own framing. This is, as the Gemara acknowledges, a satisfying answer: literary considerations of economy and consistency preclude the explicit listing.

### Framing Problem

This observation generalizes. The *mishnah* is constructed as a tight, elegant set of rules. Cases that fit the rule but require syntactic contortions to include are simply absorbed into the scope of *kol makom* without being named. Cases that are exceptions are similarly handled by *eizu zu*, which marks the list as the intended application rather than an exhaustive catalogue of all possible cases. The strategy is: *tana kol makom l'atuyei* — the *tanna* uses *kol makom* to include unlisted applications; *tana eizu zu l'motei* — and uses *eizu zu* to exclude, or rather to acknowledge, unlisted exceptions.

### Mitzri Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִצְרִי שֵׁנִי שֶׁנָּשָׂא מִצְרִית רִאשׁוֹנָה – בְּנָהּ שְׁלִישִׁי הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But there is** also the *halakha* **of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, as Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **a second-generation Egyptian man,** i.e., the son of an Egyptian convert, **who married a first-generation Egyptian woman,** a woman who herself converted, **her son is** considered **a third-generation** Egyptian who may marry a Jew of unflawed lineage.

Another application of the rule is now introduced from a tradition of Rabba bar bar Chana citing Rabbi Yochanan: *Mitzri sheini shenasa Mitzrit rishona, benah shelishi havei* — a second-generation *Mitzri* who marries a first-generation *Mitzrit* produces a third-generation child. The background rule is that a *Mitzri* convert may not marry into the Jewish community until the third generation: *dor shelishi yavo lahem bikhal Hashem*. The question is how to count generations when two converts of different generational remove marry each other. The answer: the child's generation is counted by the father's status. A second-generation *Mitzri* father and a first-generation *Mitzrit* mother produces a third-generation child — following the father. This is another application of the rule of paternal descent in a permitted marriage with no *aveirah*. Why was it not listed? The *kol makom* of the *reisha* includes it.

### Converting Nations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִיכָּא, דְּכִי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בָּאוּמּוֹת – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר. נִתְגַּיְּירוּ, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַפָּגוּם שֶׁבִּשְׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But there is** the following case, which apparently contradicts the principle of the mishna: **As when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael **he** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: With regard to the nations,** i.e., if members of two different nations married when they were gentiles, **follow the male** to determine the status of their child. If these members of two different nations **converted, follow the flawed** lineage **of the two.**

A tradition brought in the name of Ravin citing Rabbi Yochanan introduces a case that functions as an exception rather than an application: when members of non-Jewish nations convert, and two converts of different national backgrounds marry — for example, an *Edomi* convert and a *Mitzri* convert — one asks what status their child bears, given that different nations have different generational thresholds for full integration. The rule here, says Ravin, is *halach achar hapagam shebishneihem* — we follow whichever of the two parents carries the greater disqualification, the greater *pagum*. Even if no *aveirah* was committed in the marriage, the child follows the more disqualified parent. This is an exception to the rule that in a permitted marriage one follows the father. It is accounted for by *eizu zu l'motei* — the *eizu zu* formulation acknowledges the existence of such exceptions without enumerating them.

### Category Resolution

The Gemara now resolves how the various cases map onto the categories under Rabbi Yehudah versus Rabbi Yose. According to Rabbi Yehudah, who holds that a *ger* may not marry a *mamzeret*, the union of *ger* and *mamzeret* is one of *kiddushin v'yesh aveirah*, and the child following the *pagum* fits the second clause of the *mishnah*. The *kol makom* of the *reisha* includes the *chalala* and *Mitzri* cases as unlisted applications of paternal descent. The *eizu zu* of both clauses covers the exceptions — most notably the *umot shenitgayru* case of nations that converted.

According to Rabbi Yose, who holds the *ger-mamzeret* union is permitted, that case is an exception: no *aveirah*, yet the child follows the *pagum*. It is covered by *eizu zu* as an unlisted exception. But the Gemara notes that under Rabbi Yose's reading, the *kol makom* of the *seifa* — the clause about *kiddushin v'yesh aveirah* and *holech achar hapagum* — has nothing additional to include. The Gemara deflects: *aidi d'tana reisha eizu zu, tana seifa eizu zu; aidi d'tana reisha kol makom, tana seifa kol makom* — the parallel structure of the *mishnah* requires that each clause employ both formulations, even if in a given clause one of them does no independent work. The literary symmetry of *kol makom* and *eizu zu* appearing in each half of the *mishnah* is itself sufficient justification for their inclusion, regardless of whether both are needed for substantive legal purposes in every case.

## Non-Jewish Nations

### Tribal Paternity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּמּוֹת – הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הַזָּכָר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the nations,** i.e., if members of two different nations married when they were gentiles, **follow the male** to determine the status of their child.

The Gemara turns to the governing principle for non-Jews: *b'umos halach achar hazachar* — among the nations, one follows the father in determining tribal identity. The question of national or tribal membership for non-Jews is determined patrilineally.

### Cana'anite Status

The Gemara distinguishes between native *Kena'anim* — members of the seven nations — and non-native *Kena'anim*, those who happen to dwell in the land but whose fathers are not from the seven nations. The legal significance pertains to the laws of slavery: the Torah permits purchasing *Kena'ani* slaves from among the peoples dwelling in the land, but the application of this permission turns on the national identity of the person in question. The Gemara asks: from where do we know that a child born to a *Kena'ani* woman by a foreign father is not considered a *Kena'ani* for these purposes?

### Slavery Proofs

The *talmud lomar*: *vegam mibnei hatoshavim hagarim imachem* — from among the children of the residents who dwell among you — you may acquire them as slaves. The verse distinguishes those who are resident sojourners in the land from native inhabitants. The Gemara understands this distinction as tracking the father's origin: if the father is foreign, the child, though born in the land, is not a native *Kena'ani*. The verse *mehem tiknu* — from them you may acquire — applies to such a child, and the implication is that the child's status is determined by the father's national identity.

### Reverse Case

The reverse case: a *Kena'ani* man who fathers a child with a non-*Kena'ani* woman. The verse *asher holidu b'artzechem* — who were born in your land — is read to indicate that being physically born in the land is not determinative; what matters is whether the father is from *Kena'an*. If the father is a *Kena'ani*, the child is considered to be living in *Kena'an* in the relevant sense, because the child's national identity follows the father. *Aval lo min hagarim b'artzechem* — but not from those who are merely dwelling in the land — distinguishes the case where the father is foreign: that child, though resident in the land, does not carry *Kena'ani* identity.

The Gemara acknowledges the circularity here: the *pesuk* does not explicitly mention the father as the determining factor; rather, the presumption that status follows the father is read into the verse, and then read back out of it as proof. Nevertheless, the result is consistent: for non-Jewish nations, tribal or national identity is determined patrilineally. The discussion of what occurs when such individuals convert — how to reckon the status of a child born to, for example, an *Edomi* convert and a *Moavi* convert — is deferred to the following session.
