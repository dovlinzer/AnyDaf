# Niddah 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tliyah & Attribution

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הִשְׁאִילָה חֲלוּקָהּ לְנׇכְרִית אוֹ לְנִדָּה — הֲרֵי זוֹ תּוֹלָה בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where a woman **lent her garment to a gentile woman or to a menstruating** Jewish **woman,** and after the borrower returned the garment the owner wore it and then discovered a blood stain, she **attributes** the blood stain **to** the gentile or the menstruating woman.

Today's daf is Niddah 60b, and we pick up at the Mishnah toward the bottom of the amud. We had a brief digression discussing the question of a woman bleeding from a wound — a case where there is a basis to attribute the blood to a non-uterine source, some internal injury. Now we return to our main theme: *kesamim*, the laws of stains.

### Tliyah Basics

One of the central principles governing stain law is *tliyah* — attribution, the notion of "blaming it on someone else." Until now, the "someone else" has been a party who really does not lose anything by the attribution: the butcher, the husband with the bloody nose, the woman with a wound that can reopen. These are essentially win-win scenarios. Now we turn to cases of *tliyah* where the picture is more complicated — where the attribution is not simply costless to all parties involved.

### Mishnah: Lending & Attribution

The Mishnah states: *hishilah chaluka l'nochrit* — she lent her garment to a non-Jewish woman — *o l'niddah* — or to a *niddah* — and then received the garment back, wore it, and a stain was discovered. The problem is that she did not inspect the garment before lending it. The question is whether the stain originated from her or from the non-Jewish woman or the *niddah* to whom it was lent.

The answer is straightforward in terms of who loses: attributing it to the *niddah* costs her nothing, since she is already *tamei*. Attributing it to the non-Jewish woman likewise carries no practical consequence, since the relevant laws of *niddah* do not apply to non-Jewish women at a biblical level, and whatever rabbinic consideration applies does not change her status in a meaningful way. Therefore, *harei zo tola bah* — she may attribute the stain to the other woman.

What is notable here is that we do not require that it be *more likely* the stain came from the other woman. In the case of the non-Jewish woman, there is not necessarily any stronger circumstantial reason to suppose it came from her. Nevertheless, since attributing it to her is more *convenient* and no one loses as a result, we do so. This is an extension of the principle of leniency that underlies the entire category of stain law.

### Who Loses Analysis

One might ask why the Mishnah even mentions the non-Jewish woman — after all, she may not have been menstruating at the time. The answer is precisely the point: it does not matter whether she was actively menstruating. Just as the Jewish woman who finds a stain cannot definitively confirm she bled, so too the non-Jewish woman cannot confirm she did not. The stain could have come from either. Since attributing it to the non-Jewish woman costs her nothing — her status remains unchanged — we do so and the Jewish woman may regard herself as *tehorah*.

This is a broad leniency. The entire logic of *kesamim* is rabbinic, and wherever a basis for leniency presents itself — including the availability of a party who does not lose by attribution — we avail ourselves of it.

## Three Women Case

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים שֶׁלָּבְשׁוּ חָלוּק אֶחָד, אוֹ שֶׁיָּשְׁבוּ עַל סַפְסָל אֶחָד, וְנִמְצָא עָלָיו דָּם — כּוּלָּן טְמֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** In a case of **three women who wore one garment or who sat on one bench [*safsal*],** one after the other, and the garment, or bench, was examined before the first of them donned it, or sat on it, and it was clean, and after the third one removed the garment, or stood up, **a blood** stain **was discovered on** the garment or on the bench, **all** the women **are ritually impure.**

The Mishnah now addresses a more complex scenario: *gimel nashim shelovshu chaluk echad* — three women shared one garment, passing it among themselves without inspecting it in between — or *sheyashvu al safsal echad* — they sat on one chair without an intervening inspection. A stain is subsequently found. Who is responsible?

### Equal Parties Rule

Since there is no basis to attribute the stain to one woman more than another, and since saying it came from any particular woman would impose a status of *tum'ah* on her without greater justification than the others, one cannot legitimately attribute the stain to any single party. The result: *kulan teme'ot* — they all become *teme'ot*.

This might seem surprising. One might think that since each woman can point to the others as equally plausible sources, perhaps they should all be regarded as *tehorah* — each benefiting individually from the doubt. But this reasoning fails.

### Two Roads Analogy

The classic illustration is the case of two roads, one of which has a corpse buried beneath it. If each traveler came individually to ask, we would rule *tahor* for each based on *chezkat taharah* and the principle that *safek tum'ah b'reshut harabim sefeko tahor*. But if they present themselves simultaneously, it becomes absurd to declare both *tahor*, since we know with certainty that one of them walked over the buried corpse. So too here: even if they do not appear simultaneously, there is a limit to how far attribution can stretch when all parties are equal candidates. When no one woman is a more appropriate candidate than another, attribution to any one of them at the expense of the others cannot be justified, and all lose equally.

### Rabbi Nechemia & Mekabel Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָשְׁבוּ עַל סַפְסָל שֶׁל אֶבֶן אוֹ עַל הָאִיצְטְבָא שֶׁל מֶרְחָץ — רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה מְטַהֵר. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה — אֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל כְּתָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** If **they sat on a stone bench or on the bench [*ha'itzteva*] of a bathhouse,** neither of which can become ritually impure, the first because it is stone and the second because it is attached to the floor of the bathhouse, and a blood stain was found on one of those benches, **Rabbi Neḥemya deems** all three women **ritually pure, as Rabbi Neḥemya would say: Any item that is not susceptible to ritual impurity is not susceptible to** ritual impurity due to blood **stains.** The decree of impurity due to blood stains was limited to items susceptible to ritual impurity.

The Mishnah then introduces a different scenario: *yashva al safsal shel even o al istaba shel merchatz* — if the women sat on a stone bench or on a bathhouse bench. These are objects that either because they are stone or because they are attached to the ground and therefore not considered independent vessels, do not receive *tum'ah* — they are *eino mekabel tumah*. In such a case, *Rabbi Nechemia metaher* — Rabbi Nechemia rules the women are *tehorah*, even if it was only one woman sitting there. The reason follows in the next section.

## Mekabel Tumah Principle

### Stain Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה — אֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל כְּתָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Any item that is not susceptible to ritual impurity is not susceptible to ritual impurity due to blood stains.

Rabbi Nechemia's position is encapsulated in a foundational principle: *kol davar she'eino mekabel tumah eino mekabel ketamim* — anything that cannot become *tamei* is not subject to the laws of stains. This is a principle of enormous practical importance in *hilchot ketamim*.

### Tosafos Analysis

Tosafos explains what makes this principle structurally significant. One might ask: why should the *tum'ah*-susceptibility of the *object* determine whether the *woman* is *teme'ah*? The woman's status should be the primary concern. Tosafos answers that this reveals something fundamental about the category of *kesamim* itself: the entire framework of stain law was rooted in the world of *tumah v'taharah*. The analysis begins with the status of the stain on the object. If we cannot declare the object *tamei*, we cannot proceed to declare the woman *teme'ah*. The woman's status is derived from the object's status, not assessed independently. When the chain of *tum'ah* cannot begin with the object, it cannot reach the woman.

### Modern Applications

This has direct implications for contemporary *halacha*. The question of whether materials such as plastic, polyester, or nylon are *mekabel tumah* is not merely academic. A stain found on a plastic or synthetic garment would, under Rabbi Nechemia's principle, not render the woman *teme'ah*, provided those materials are not *mekabel tumah* — not even *mekabel tumah derabbanan*, since the accepted ruling follows the stricter standard of requiring *mekabel tumah* even rabbinically.

### Relevance Today

This is why, despite the general practice of not studying *Taharot* in depth given its limited direct applicability today, one cannot fully navigate *hilchot niddah* without recourse to these principles. The laws of *kesamim* require knowing what is *mekabel tumah*. Similarly, related areas — such as the requirement for a *chatzitzah*-free immersion in the *mikveh* and the laws of *sechach* — also hinge on questions drawn from *hilchot kelim* and *mekabel tumah*. These chapters of *Shas* that might seem remote turn out to be essential.

## Rav's Restriction

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: בְּנׇכְרִית הָרוֹאָה. מִמַּאי? דּוּמְיָא דְּנִדָּה: מָה נִדָּה דְּקָחָזְיָא, אַף נׇכְרִית דְּקָא חָזְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** The ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to a gentile woman** who is of age and already **seeing** a flow of menstrual blood, i.e., she has had a flow of menstrual blood. The Gemara explains: **From where** does Rav know that this is referring to a gentile woman who once experienced a flow of blood? He infers from the mishna that the gentile woman it mentions is **similar to a menstruating woman: Just as a menstruating woman** is one **who sees,** i.e., who has already experienced bleeding, **so too,** the mishna is referring to an adult **gentile woman who** already **sees** menstrual blood.

The Gemara now turns back to the question of attribution to non-Jewish women. *Amar Rav benachrit hara'ah* — Rav limits the Mishnah's leniency. One cannot attribute a stain to just any non-Jewish woman merely because it is convenient. The non-Jewish woman must be someone who has already previously menstruated. She need not be actively menstruating at the time, but she cannot be someone for whom menstruation would be entirely implausible — a young girl who has never yet had a period. Rav derives this by analogy: *dumya deniddah* — just as when we attribute it to a *niddah* we mean a woman who has been seeing blood, *af nachrit dekahazya* — so too with the non-Jewish woman, she must be in the relevant category of someone who has menstruated.

### Rav Sheshet Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כִּי נָיֵים וְשָׁכֵיב רַב אַמְרַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. דְּתַנְיָא: תּוֹלָה בְּנׇכְרִית. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: בְּנׇכְרִית הָרְאוּיָה לִרְאוֹת. וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא בִּרְאוּיָה לִרְאוֹת, אֲבָל רוֹאָה — לָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says: I say** that **when Rav was dozing or sleeping he said that *halakha*,** i.e., it is an error. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a woman loaned her garment to a gentile and subsequently found a blood stain on it, **she attributes** the stain **to the gentile woman.** This *tanna* deems it permitted for her to attribute the blood to any gentile woman, regardless of her age or her likelihood of bleeding. **Rabbi Meir** disagrees and **says:** This applies specifically **to a gentile woman who is fit to see** menstrual blood. **And** the Gemara adds that **even Rabbi Meir says only that** the gentile woman must be **fit to see** menstrual blood, **but** he too agrees that **it is not necessary** for her to be **seeing** blood at that time. She does not have to have actually experienced bleeding at some point.

*Amar Rav Sheshet Rav namnumi namnem* — Rav Sheshet dismisses Rav's position sharply: Rav must have been dozing when he said this, since it conflicts with an explicit *braita*. The *braita* states: *tola b'nochrit* — you may attribute it to a non-Jewish woman. *Rabbi Meir omer b're'uyah lirot* — Rabbi Meir adds that she must at least be of the appropriate age, i.e., fit to be menstruating. This formulation suggests that the *Rabbanan* require nothing at all, while Rabbi Meir — seemingly the stricter opinion — requires only that she be of menstruating age, not that she have already menstruated. If even Rabbi Meir does not demand prior menstruation, how can Rav make such a demand?

### Rabbi Meir Debate

The Gemara pauses to clarify who is being strict and who is being lenient. *Ve'afillu Rabbi Meir lo ka'amar ella b'ru'uyah lirot* — even Rabbi Meir, who appears strict by requiring age appropriateness, does not go so far as to require actual prior menstruation. So Rav's position seems unsupportable.

### Rav Papa Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְתִסְבְּרַאּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְחוּמְרָא? רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְקוּלָּא! דְּתַנְיָא: אֵינָהּ תּוֹלָה בְּנׇכְרִית, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: תּוֹלָה. וְאֶלָּא קַשְׁיָא הָךְ! תָּרֵץ הָכִי: וְהִיא שֶׁרוֹאָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: בִּרְאוּיָה לִרְאוֹת, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָהּ רוֹאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response to Rav Sheshet's challenge: **And** how can **you understand** that **Rabbi Meir** is coming **to be stringent?** Rav Sheshet maintains that according to the first *tanna* of the *baraita* she can attribute the blood stain to any gentile, whereas Rabbi Meir rules stringently that she may attribute it only to a gentile who is fit to experience bleeding. This is incorrect, as **Rabbi Meir** is actually coming **to be lenient.** In other words, the first *tanna* is more stringent, as he deems it permitted for her to attribute the blood stain only to a gentile woman who had experienced a menstrual flow at least once. By contrast, Rabbi Meir rules that she may attribute the stain to a gentile woman who is old enough to experience bleeding, even if she has never experienced a menstrual flow. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: A woman who loaned her garment to a gentile woman and subsequently finds a blood stain on it **may not attribute** the stain **to** the **gentile woman. Rabbi Meir says** that **she may attribute** the blood stain to the gentile woman. **But if so,** the first *baraita*, which states that according to the first *tanna* she may attribute the blood stain to any gentile woman, is **difficult.** You must **answer like this:** According to the first *tanna* she may attribute the blood to any gentile woman provided that **she sees,** i.e., that she once experienced bleeding. By contrast, **Rabbi Meir says** a more lenient opinion, **that** she may attribute it to the gentile woman provided that **she is fit to see** menstrual blood, **and** this is the *halakha* **even though she has not** actually **seen** menstrual blood yet.

*Amar Rav Papa utisbara Rabbi Meir lechumra* — Rav Papa corrects the reading entirely: you have misidentified who is strict and who is lenient. Rabbi Meir is actually the *lenient* opinion. A second *braita* makes this explicit: *ein toleh b'nochrit* — you may not attribute a stain to a non-Jewish woman, as the majority position. *Rabbi Meir omer toleh* — Rabbi Meir says you may. Rabbi Meir is the more permissive voice.

This reframes the debate entirely. The *Rabbanan* hold that attribution to a non-Jewish woman is only valid when she has actually had a prior period — her status must be established, not merely theoretical. Rabbi Meir holds that it suffices that she be of the age at which menstruation is appropriate, even without prior menstruation having occurred. So when the first *braita* reads "Rabbi Meir says she must be of the age to see," this is not Rabbi Meir being stricter than the *Rabbanan* — it is Rabbi Meir being *more lenient*, requiring only age appropriateness where the *Rabbanan* require actual prior menstruation.

Thus Rav's ruling aligns with the *Rabbanan* against Rabbi Meir. The principle underlying this dispute is clear: *tliyah* requires not only that no one loses by attribution, but also that the attribution not be entirely implausible. One cannot attribute a stain to a woman for whom menstruation would be absurd. The question is merely how much of a track record is needed.

## Unequal Loss Debate

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תּוֹלָה בְּשׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם, בַּשֵּׁנִי שֶׁלָּהּ. וּבְסוֹפֶרֶת שִׁבְעָה שֶׁלֹּא טָבְלָה, לְפִיכָךְ הִיא מְתוּקֶּנֶת וַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ מְקוּלְקֶלֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ תּוֹלָה, לְפִיכָךְ שְׁתֵּיהֶן מְקוּלְקָלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *baraita* with regard to a woman who loaned her garment to another woman who was a lesser *zava*: The lender **may attribute** the blood stain on the loaned garment **to a woman who observes** a clean **day for** each **day** she experiences a discharge, if the blood is found **on her second** day, i.e., the day after she had a discharge, despite the fact that she does not have a presumptive status of seeing blood. It is nevertheless considered that her uterus is open and the likelihood is that she will experience bleeding. **And** likewise, she may also attribute the blood stain if she loaned her garment **to a woman counting seven** clean days **who did not immerse** in a ritual bath, and who will now have to count another seven clean days. **Therefore,** the status of the one who loaned the garment **is fixed, and** the status of **the other** woman **is ruined** and she must begin her counting again; this is **the statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: She may not attribute** the blood flow to either of these women. **Therefore,** the statuses of **both** the woman who loaned the garment and the one who borrowed it **are ruined,** in that both women are deemed impure.

The Gemara now addresses the most contested terrain: cases where attribution *does* hurt the other party, but hurts her less than it would hurt the woman attributing. The Gemara cites a *braita*: *toleh b'shomeret yom k'neged yom b'sheni shelah* — you may attribute it to a *shomeret yom k'neged yom* on her second day.

To set up the case: the woman who lent her garment is entirely *tehorah* — she is not a *niddah* and is in the middle of her cycle. The borrower is a *shomeret yom k'neged yom* — a woman who had a *zavah*-type stain one day, and is now on the following day, the "watch day." If she does not bleed today, she may immerse. The garment is returned and a stain is found. If the stain came from the lender, she becomes a *niddah* and must count seven days. If it came from the borrower, the borrower loses her clean "watch day" and must wait one more day. The loss is radically asymmetric: one day versus seven days.

The *braita* also addresses the case of a *soferet shivah she-lo tavlah* — a woman who has completed her seven clean days but has not yet immersed. If the stain came from the lender, the lender becomes a *niddah* with a seven-day count. If it came from the borrower, she must restart her seven-day count entirely. Here the loss is more symmetric — seven days lost either way — but with one crucial distinction: the borrower is still *teme'ah* regardless, since she has not yet immersed. The lender is *tehorah*. There is thus at least a structural asymmetry even where the day-count loss appears equal.

### Rabban Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין, שֶׁתּוֹלָה בְּשׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם, בָּרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁלָּהּ. וּבְיוֹשֶׁבֶת עַל דַּם טוֹהַר, וּבִבְתוּלָה שֶׁדָּמֶיהָ טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** both Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **agree that** a woman who lends her garment **may attribute** any blood stain found on it **to a woman** who **observes a day for a day** if it is **on her first** day of the discharge. In this case the status of the woman who borrowed the garment is no more ruined than it was already, as either way she can become pure on the following day. **And** they also agree **in** a case where the woman who borrowed the garment was a woman after childbirth **who is observing** the period **of** the **blood of purity.** During these days, attributing the blood to her does not ruin her status, as any blood she emits is pure and does not affect her status. **And** similarly, the lender may attribute the blood stain **to a virgin** who engages in intercourse for the first time, **as her blood is pure,** as there is an assumption that it is hymenal bleeding rather than menstrual blood.

*Lefikach hi metukenet v'chaverta mekulkelet, divrei Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — therefore, she is spared and her friend bears the loss. That is the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. His reasoning is that since one party has less to lose — either fewer days or is already *teme'ah* in any event — there is sufficient basis to attribute the stain to her and spare the other party entirely.

### Rabbi's Position

*Rebi omer eina tola* — Rebi disagrees. Even unequal loss is still loss. Once there is any real cost imposed on the other party by attribution, no matter how much smaller that cost is compared to what the attributing woman would face, Rebi refuses to permit attribution. *Lefikach shteihem mekulkalot* — therefore both women suffer, the lender becoming a *niddah* and the borrower losing her clean day or restarting her count, because the stain cannot be attributed to either one definitively.

### Agreement Cases

Despite their dispute, Rebi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel agree in cases where the attribution costs the other party nothing at all. *Shomeret yom k'neged yom b'rishon shelah* — if the borrower had her staining event today, she is on the first day, and saying the stain is hers costs her nothing, since she stained anyway. *O b'yoshevet al dam tohor* — or if she is in the *dam tohor* period following childbirth, the thirty-three or sixty-six days during which blood does not render her *teme'ah*, then attributing the stain to her has no consequence for her. *O b'vatula shedam'eha tehorim* — or if she is a woman who has recently married and whose bleeding is presumed to be hymenal, then again she loses nothing by attribution.

In all these cases, everyone agrees: where attribution is costless, attribute freely. The debate between Rebi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is only where there is genuine, if asymmetric, loss.

The Gemara then asks why the phrase *lefikach* — "therefore" — appears in both Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling and in Rebi's ruling, since in each case the conclusion would seem to follow obviously from the stated position. The answer is that each *lefikach* serves to exclude a possible alternative rule. One might have thought that even if attribution to the other woman is rejected, one would resolve the stain by applying some independent rule — such as assigning responsibility to whichever woman was in physical possession of the garment at the moment of discovery. The *lefikach* in Rebi's formulation rules this out: once attribution fails, both women are *teme'ot*, and no alternative distributional rule comes into play.

## Two Roads Case

### Case Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: טָמֵא וְטָהוֹר שֶׁהָלְכוּ בִּשְׁנֵי שְׁבִילִין, אֶחָד טָהוֹר וְאֶחָד טָמֵא — בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **Rav Ḥisda says:** In a case of two individuals, one of whom was **ritually impure and** the other of whom was **pure, who walked on two paths, one** of which was **pure and** the other **one impure** due to a corpse buried there, and neither remembers which path he took, and afterward they handled items of ritual purity, e.g., the portion of produce designated for the priest [*teruma*] or consecrated items, **we have arrived at the dispute** between **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, if one of the two individuals was already impure it can be assumed that he was the one who walked along the ritually impure path, and the other individual remains pure. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi claims that there is no assumption that the one who was pure retains that state, as it is equally possible that he walked along the ritually impure path.

*Amar Rav Chisda*: the Gemara now revisits the case of two roads — one *tamei*, one *tahor* — but with a new twist. We have one *tamei* person and one *tahor* person, both of whom walked down two paths, one of which contained a buried corpse and one of which did not, and neither remembers which path he walked. The most convenient attribution is that the *tamei* person walked the *tamei* path and the *tahor* person walked the *tahor* path. There are also independent legal grounds for leniency: the *tahor* person has a *chezkat taharah*, and if this is *reshut harabim*, *safek tum'ah b'reshut harabim sefeko tahor* would apply.

Rav Chisda says this is subject to the debate of Rebi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The *tamei* person is already *tamei*, but if he walked the *tamei* path — that of *tum'at met* — he now faces a lengthier purification process. If he walked the *tahor* path, the attribution to him may cost him additional days of *tum'ah* that he would not otherwise have had to observe. Is that loss enough to preclude attribution under Rebi's view, thereby requiring both men to treat themselves as having walked the *tamei* path?

Rav Chisda holds that this is precisely the Rebi–Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel debate. The *tamei* person is not in the same position as someone who has no loss whatsoever, so attribution to him is not uncontested.

### Rav Adda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַדָּא: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ כִּי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ, הָכָא מַאי נָפְקָא לַן מִינַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda objects to this** suggestion of Rav Ḥisda, claiming that one cannot compare the two cases. It is possible that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **states** his *halakha* **only there,** with regard to a woman who observes a day for a day, as she can immerse in a ritual bath at any time, and **therefore both** women **are like each other,** i.e., both have a presumption of ritual purity. But **here,** in the case of the two individuals walking on two paths, **what** practical **difference** does it make to the one who was previously impure if he remains ritually impure? Since there is no change of status, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would agree in that case that it can be assumed that the individual who was previously impure was the one who walked on the impure path.

*Matkif lah Rav Adda*: Rav Adda challenges the analogy. He argues that the *shomeret yom* case, which animated the original dispute, is structurally different from the two-roads case. In the *shomeret yom* scenario, one might say Rebi refused attribution not because he was unwilling to impose any loss on the other party, but because the *shomeret yom* was essentially already on the cusp of *taharah* — she only needed to go to the *mikveh* — and therefore was essentially equivalent in status to the *tehorah* woman. If the two parties are effectively equivalent, there is no basis for preferring to attribute to one over the other.

The two-roads case might be genuinely different. The *tamei* person is truly *tamei*, not on the threshold of purity. He has real loss to sustain by having the *tamei* path attributed to him, but his loss is modest compared to the *tahor* person's loss. If Rebi's reasoning was about essential equivalence, not about any loss at all, then Rebi might actually *permit* attribution in the two-roads case, since the parties are genuinely unequal in ways beyond mere day-counts.

### Interpretation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִסְדָּא, סוֹף סוֹף אִיהִי טְבִילָה בָּעֲיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara asks: How would **Rav Ḥisda** respond to this claim? Rav Ḥisda would answer that a woman who observes a day for a day is also not fully pure, as **ultimately she requires immersion** in a ritual bath to complete her purification, and yet Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi still rejects the attribution of the blood flow to her. Accordingly, the two cases are comparable and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would not assume that the individual who was already impure was the one who walked along the path that was impure.

*Rav Chisda* responds: *sof sof itvilah ba'aya* — in the end, the *shomeret yom* still needs to immerse. She is not *tehorah*. Her remaining obligation is real. Rebi's refusal to attribute, therefore, was not premised on her being essentially *tahor*. Rather, it was premised on his unwillingness to impose *any* incremental loss on a party through attribution. On that understanding, the two-roads case is indeed subject to the same debate: Rebi would say no attribution even there, because the *tamei* person would suffer at least some additional days, while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would say attribute because the loss is asymmetric in favor of leniency.

The disagreement between Rav Chisda and Rav Adda is thus a dispute about what animated Rebi's position: was it an absolute rule against imposing *any* loss via attribution, or was it a narrower principle that applies only when the parties are essentially equivalent in status?

## Doubtful Tamei Cases

### Talui Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: טָמֵא וְטָהוֹר, וַאֲפִילּוּ טָהוֹר וְתָלוּי, שֶׁהָלְכוּ בִּשְׁנֵי שְׁבִילִין, אֶחָד טָמֵא וְאֶחָד טָהוֹר — תּוֹלֶה טָמֵא בְּתָלוּי וְטָהוֹר בְּטָהוֹר, לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** In a case of two individuals, one of whom was **ritually impure and** the other of whom was **pure, or even** where one was **pure and** the other was impure due to **uncertainty, who walked on two** different **paths, one** of which was **impure and** the other **one pure,** and neither remembers which path he took, one may **attribute** by assuming that **the impure** path was the one traversed **by** the individual who was impure due to **uncertainty, and** the **pure** path was traversed **by** the one who was **ritually pure.** And **everyone agrees** with this ruling, i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi agrees with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in this case. This statement is in accordance with Rav Adda's objection, not in accordance with the suggestion of Rav Ḥisda.

*Amar Rebbi Yosi berabbi Chanina*: the Gemara introduces yet another variation. What if it is not a *tamei* and *tahor* person on two paths, but a *tahor* person and a *talui* — someone whose status is doubtful, who may or may not be *tamei*? They walk down two paths, one *tamei* and one *tahor*, and we do not know which walked where. Can we attribute the *tamei* path to the doubtful person and the *tahor* path to the clearly *tahor* person?

Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Chanina rules: *toleh tamei b'talui v'tahor b'tahor* — yes, attribute the *tamei* path to the *talui* and the *tahor* path to the *tahor*. Moreover, *v'divrei hakol* — this is the unanimous position of all opinions, including Rebi.

### No Additional Loss

The reason is critical: the *talui* must treat himself as *tamei* regardless. His doubtful status already obligates him to observe the restrictions of *tum'ah*. Declaring him to have walked the *tamei* path imposes no additional loss upon him — he was already treating himself as *tamei*. Since he suffers no incremental loss by the attribution, even Rebi would agree it is permitted. This is a precise application of the principle that costless attribution is universally accepted.

This reasoning assumes the case concerns the *first day* — if the *talui* would lose additional days by the attribution, the debate would re-emerge.

### Ketem B'Ketem Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר לֵיוַאי: מַהוּ לִתְלוֹת כֶּתֶם בְּכֶתֶם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yehuda bar Livai: What is** the *halakha* **with regard to attributing** a blood **stain to** a woman who is already impure due to having seen a blood **stain?**

This leads naturally to a more subtle question: *mahu litlos ketem b'ketem* — may one attribute a stain found on a shared garment to a woman who has herself found a stain on a different garment of hers? On the surface, the answer would seem to be yes: if the other woman already has a stain, she becomes *teme'ah* regardless, so attributing this additional stain to her costs her nothing. But a competing consideration arises: the basis for *tliyah* has always required some meaningful identification of the other woman as a *plausible* source of bleeding. A *niddah* is clearly in the context of bleeding. A woman counted in her *shivah neki'im* is still within the *tum'ah* of prior bleeding. But a woman who merely found a *ketem* — a stain on her own garment — is in a more ambiguous position. The nature of *kesamim* is that we do not say definitively that she bled; we say only that a stain was found and she must be treated accordingly. Can such a woman serve as the basis of attribution for another's stain?

## Ketem Attribution

### Yochanan Query

The *baraita* that was raised previously posed this exact question in two different ways. Let me now examine the resolutions.

### Rabbi's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין תּוֹלִין. מָה טַעַם? לְפִי שֶׁאֵין תּוֹלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda bar Livai **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan: She **may not attribute** this blood stain to a woman who was already impure due to having seen a blood stain, and both women are ritually impure. Rabbi Yoḥanan asked Rabbi Yehuda bar Livai: **What is the reason** that she may not attribute the blood stain to her? Rabbi Yehuda bar Livai answered: It is **because** in this case one **may not attribute** the new blood stain to that other woman, as her previous stain might have come from an external cause.

*Amar leih ein toleh* — the answer given is that one may not attribute a stain to a woman who had only a *ketem*. *Mah ta'am* — why? *Lefi she'ein toleh* — the reason given, that "you do not attribute," seems at first glance circular, but the Gemara elaborates through its subsequent discussion.

The underlying logic, as the Gemara frames it through its treatment of the *aliba d'Rebbi* question, is this: According to Rebi, attribution is already highly restricted. If even when a woman is definitively known to be bleeding, Rebi is unwilling to attribute a stain to her when she would suffer any incremental loss — certainly when her bleeding status is itself uncertain, based only on a *ketem* she found, Rebi would not permit attribution. *Hashta mah hatam dikachazya migufah* — now, there where she actually bled from her body, Rebi would still not always allow attribution; *hacha d'alma kachazya*, here where the blood might have come from elsewhere entirely, he certainly would not allow it. So according to Rebi, *ein toleh b'ketem* — definitively.

The more interesting question is according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who is generally more willing to permit attribution. *Ki tibei lach aliba d'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the real question is for Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position. *Hatam hu dikachazya migufah* — in the normal attribution cases, the woman to whom we attribute at least has a known history of bleeding, so it is not implausible. *Aval hacha d'me'alma kachazya* — here, where the source of the stain on her own garment is also uncertain, perhaps even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would say that attribution is impermissible — *lo talya*. Or perhaps it makes no difference — *o dilma lo shana* — and attribution is still permitted since she suffers no incremental loss.

### Braita Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: אֵין תּוֹלִין כֶּתֶם בְּכֶתֶם, הִשְׁאִילָה חֲלוּקָהּ לְנׇכְרִית אוֹ לְיוֹשֶׁבֶת עַל הַכֶּתֶם — הֲרֵי זוֹ תּוֹלָה בָּהּ. הָא גּוּפַהּ קַשְׁיָא: רֵישָׁא אָמְרַתְּ אֵין תּוֹלִין, סֵיפָא אָמְרַתְּ תּוֹלִין!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised an objection to** Rabbi Yehuda bar Livai from the following *baraita*: A woman who loans her garment to another and subsequently finds a blood stain on it **may not attribute** the blood **stain to** a woman who was impure due to having previously seen a blood **stain.** But if **she loaned her garment to a gentile woman or to** a woman who was **observing** days of impurity **due to** having seen a blood **stain, she may attribute** the blood stain found on the garment to that other woman. Before explaining the objection, the Gemara first analyzes the *baraita*. **This** *baraita* **itself** is **difficult.** In **the first clause you said** that a woman who loans her garment to another and subsequently finds a blood stain on it **may not attribute** the blood stain to a woman who was impure due to having previously seen a blood stain, whereas in **the latter clause you said** that she **may attribute** the blood stain to such a woman. Rabbi Yoḥanan explains this contradiction: **This** is **not difficult. This** first clause of the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and that** latter clause is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

The Gemara raises a *braita* that appears to contradict the ruling: *ein tolin ketem b'ketem* — you do not attribute a stain to a woman with a stain. So far so consistent. But the *braita* continues: *hishila chaluka lenochrit o leyoshevet al hakesem, harei zo tola bah* — if she lent her garment to a non-Jewish woman *or* to a woman who was *yoshevet al ha-ketem* (sitting on a stain, i.e., treating herself as *teme'ah* due to a stain), she may attribute it to her. This directly contradicts the first clause — first the *braita* says you cannot attribute to a woman with a stain, then it says you can. *Ha gufa kashya* — the *braita* itself is internally contradictory.

### Resolutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי — הָא בָּרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁלָּהּ, הָא בַּשֵּׁנִי שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** an alternative resolution of the contradiction: Both **this** clause **and that** clause are in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The difference is that **this** latter clause, where she may attribute the blood stain to the other woman, is referring to a case where the blood stain was found **on** that woman's **first** day, when she had just found the blood stain and is impure for that day. Since she is in any case impure for that day, she is not adversely affected by having the new stain attributed to her. The case where the blood stain may not be attributed to the other woman is a case where the blood stain in question was found **on her second** day, i.e., the day after she found the blood stain, when she is not impure but merely requires immersion. The lender may not attribute the new blood stain to her, as that would render her ritually impure for an extra day.

*Ha lo kashya* — the Gemara offers a first resolution: the two clauses represent the positions of Rebi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel respectively. The first clause reflects Rebi's view that *ein toleh ketem b'ketem*; the second reflects Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's permissive stance.

*Ika de'amri* — an alternative reading: both clauses reflect Rebi's position, but in different cases. *Ha b'rishon shelah ha b'sheni shelah* — the first clause discusses the second day of the *ketem*-woman's count, when she would lose a day if attribution is made; the second clause discusses the first day, when she loses nothing. According to this reading, Rebi would permit attribution on the first day, when it is costless, but would prohibit it on the second day, when there is loss. Notably, this reading also raises the question of whether Rebi's standard for *ketem b'ketem* attribution tracks his general principle about loss.

### Retroactive Discovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הָא וְהָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל — וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן לְמַפְרֵעַ, כָּאן לְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** yet another resolution of the *baraita*: Both **this** first clause **and that** latter clause are in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and it** is **not difficult. Here,** in the latter clause of the *baraita*, when she may attribute the stain to a woman who had seen a blood stain, it is referring to rendering that woman impure **retroactively** with regard to the status of pure items that she had already touched before the stain was found on the garment she borrowed. This attribution of the blood stain to the woman who was already impure due to seeing a blood stain does not harm her impure status in any way, as items she had previously touched were already considered impure. **There,** in the beginning of the *baraita*, where the ruling is that the lender may not attribute the blood stain to a woman who was already impure, it is referring to her own status **with regard to the future.** With regard to the future, one may not attribute the stain to woman who had already seen a blood stain, as this attribution would ruin that woman's counting of pure days.

*Rav Ashi amar*: Rav Ashi offers yet another resolution, attributing both clauses to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *va'hu v'ha Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. The difference lies not in who the opinions are, but in the timing of the second stain's discovery. *Kan lemapre'a kan lehaba* — one clause speaks of retroactive determination, the other of forward-looking assessment.

Tosafos explains this distinction clearly. When the first garment's stain is found, a ruling must be made at that moment. If, at that same moment, the other woman has already found a stain on a second garment of hers, then that pre-existing stain is available as a basis for attribution, and one may attribute the first garment's stain to her — *lemapre'a*. But if the other woman discovered her own stain only *after* the first garment's stain had already been assessed — *lehaba* — that later discovery cannot retroactively revise the earlier ruling. Status determinations are fixed at the moment of assessment; they cannot be revised on the basis of facts that emerge subsequently.

This is a striking principle. It is not about ascertaining physical facts — if it were, later evidence would always be relevant. It reflects the formal character of halachic status determinations: once a ruling is made under the conditions that existed at the time, it is not undone by subsequent discoveries. The rules are formal, not merely evidentiary.

## Braita Reinterpretation

### Abaye Reframes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא! אָמַר רָבִינָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הִשְׁאִילָה חֲלוּקָהּ לְנׇכְרִית — בַּעֲלַת כֶּתֶם הֲרֵי זוֹ תּוֹלָה בָּהּ. וְהָא ״אוֹ לְיוֹשֶׁבֶת עַל הַכֶּתֶם״ קָתָנֵי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אוֹ לְיוֹשֶׁבֶת עַל דַּם טוֹהַר — בַּעֲלַת כֶּתֶם תּוֹלָה בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the objection: The *baraita* has been resolved, but **in any case** everyone agrees that according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel she may attribute the stain to a woman who had previously seen a blood stain. This presents **a difficulty** to the answer of Rabbi Yehuda bar Livai, i.e., that according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel one may not attribute the blood stain in such a case. **Ravina said:** It is **not difficult,** as **this** is what the *baraita* **is saying** in its latter clause: If **she loaned her garment to a gentile woman,** then with regard to defining the lender as one **who had seen** a blood **stain,** the lender **may attribute** the stain **to** the gentile woman. If so, it cannot be inferred from the *baraita* that according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel one may attribute a blood stain to another woman who was already impure due to a blood stain. The Gemara raises an objection: **But** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **teaches: Or to** a woman who was **observing** days of impurity **due to** having seen a blood **stain,** i.e., it mentions another woman who was already impure due to having seen a blood stain. Ravina explains that **this** is what the *tanna* **is saying:** If she loaned the garment to a gentile woman **or to** a woman after childbirth **who is observing** the period **of** the **blood of purity,** then with regard to defining the lender as **a woman who saw** a blood **stain, she may attribute** the stain to the gentile or to the woman observing the days of ritually pure blood, and the lender remains pure.

*Amar Abaye lo kashya* — Abaye resolves the internal contradiction in the *braita* by reinterpreting its text entirely. The two cases in the *braita*'s permissive clause are not "a non-Jewish woman" and "a woman sitting on a stain" as two separate parallel cases. Rather, the *braita* should be read as follows: *hishila chaluka lenochrit ba'alat ketem* — she lent her garment to a non-Jewish woman who had a stain, or *leyoshevet al dam tohor* — to a woman who was in her *dam tohor* period (the thirty-three or sixty-six post-childbirth days when blood is not *tamei*). Under this reading, the permissive clause does not involve attribution of a *ketem* to a woman whose only basis is another *ketem*. The non-Jewish woman also has a *ketem*, which is irrelevant since her *tum'ah* status is not governed by Jewish law in the same way; and the *dam tohor* woman has no loss from attribution regardless.

### Principle Maintained

This re-reading preserves the principle *ein tolin ketem b'ketem* intact. The *braita*'s permissive clause, properly understood, does not actually permit attributing a stain to a woman whose only relevant status is having found a stain. In both permitted cases, there is an additional feature — non-Jewish status or *dam tohor* period — that independently justifies attribution. The formal principle that stain-to-stain attribution is impermissible remains undisturbed.

## Nechemia Extended

### Scriptural Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה? דִּכְתִיב ״וְנִקָּתָה לָאָרֶץ תֵּשֵׁב״ — כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּשְׁבָה לָאָרֶץ — נִקָּתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya. **Rav Mattana says: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Neḥemya? As it is written:** "And her gates shall lament and mourn; **and clean she shall sit upon the ground"** (Isaiah 3:26). This teaches that **once she sits on the ground,** which is not susceptible to ritual impurity, **she shall be clean,** i.e., pure.

Returning to Rabbi Nechemia's principle, the Gemara now examines its precise scope and derivation. *Amar Rav Matna mai ta'ama deRebbi Nechemia* — what is the textual basis for Rabbi Nechemia's position? The verse states *v'niketa la'aretz teshev* — she shall be cleansed, and she sits on the ground. If she sat on the ground — ground being paradigmatically *eino mekabel tumah*, as it is not a vessel — and found a stain, *v'niketa* — she is clean. Tosafos adds, as noted earlier, that this reveals the entire framework: the category of *kesamim* was constituted within the architecture of *tumah v'taharah*, and if the object cannot be declared *tamei*, the woman cannot be declared *teme'ah*.

### Pottery Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מְטַהֵר הָיָה רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אֲפִילּוּ בַּאֲחוֹרֵי כְּלִי חֶרֶס. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לִיגְזוֹר גַּבּוֹ אַטּוּ תּוֹכוֹ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says: Rabbi Neḥemya would deem her ritually pure even** if she sat **on the exterior** of an inverted **earthenware vessel.** Since an earthenware vessel becomes impure only if an impure item enters its airspace, its exterior is not susceptible to ritual impurity and therefore it does not render a woman who sees a blood stain on it ritually impure. The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** Rabbi Neḥemya himself said that she does not become impure if a blood stain is found on an item that is not susceptible to ritual impurity. What does Rabbi Ḥanina add to that statement? The Gemara answers: This observation is necessary, **lest you say: Let** the Sages **decree** that she becomes impure from a blood stain found on **the exterior** of an earthenware vessel, **due to** its similarity to a blood stain found on **the interior,** which would render her impure. Therefore, Rabbi Ḥanina **teaches us** that there is no such decree.

*Amar Rav Huna amar Rabbi Chanina*: Rabbi Nechemia's principle applies even to the outer side of a pottery vessel (*achorei kli cheres*). Pottery vessels become *tamei* only through their interior; the outer surface does not become *tamei*. Therefore, a stain found on the outer surface of a pottery vessel does not render the woman *teme'ah*, even though the vessel *as a whole* is in the category of objects that receive *tum'ah*. The relevant question is whether *this specific surface* is *mekabel tumah*, not merely whether the object generically is.

*Peshita* — one might think this is obvious. The Gemara responds: *mahu deteima ligzar gabo atu tocho* — one might have thought to issue a rabbinic decree treating the outer surface as equivalent to the inner surface, lest people become lenient about stains found inside. *Ka mashma lan* — the Gemara informs us that no such decree was issued.

### Rag Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְטַהֵר הָיָה רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה בְּמַטְלָנִיּוֹת שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ, דְּלָא חַזְיָין לָא לַעֲנִיִּים וְלֹא לַעֲשִׁירִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Rabbi Neḥemya would deem her ritually pure** if she saw blood stains **on small rags that do not have** an area of **three** fingerbreadths **by three** fingerbreadths, **as** these rags **are suitable** for use **neither for the poor nor for the wealthy.**

*Amar Abaye*: Rabbi Nechemia similarly rendered *tahor* a stain found on a rag that was less than three finger-widths by three finger-widths — *matlit she'ein bah shalosha al shalosha*. Such a rag is too small to be considered a usable item — *delo chazi lo le'aniyim v'lo le'ashirim*, it has no utility for poor or rich alike. Since it is too small to be classified as a vessel susceptible to *tum'ah*, a stain found on it does not implicate the woman. This further sharpens the principle: it is not enough that the material is one that *generically* can become *tamei*; the specific object must actually be of sufficient character to *receive tum'ah* under the laws of *kelim*.

## Historical Precedent

### Nachman Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר רַב מַתְנָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: אַבָּא תָּנֵי מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים וְטִמְּאוּם, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Rav Mattana taught in the name of Rav:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya. Rav Naḥman said** to Rav Ḥiyya bar Rav Mattana: **Father teaches** the following *baraita*: **An incident** of this kind **came before the Sages,** involving two women who found a blood stain on an item that was not susceptible to ritual impurity, **and** the Sages **deemed** both women **ritually impure,** in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, who disagree with Rabbi Neḥemya. **And** yet **you say** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya?**

*Darash Rabbi Chiya bar Rav Mattana mishmeih d'Rav halacha k'Rabbi Nechemiah* — Rav ruled publicly that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Nechemia. Rav Nachman immediately challenged this: *Abba tani ma'aseh ba lifnei chachamim v'tim'um* — my father (as a *tanna*) taught a *braita* in which a similar case came before the Sages and they declared the women *teme'ot*. How can one rule like Rabbi Nechemia if the *chachamim* themselves ruled against him in practice?

### Grinding Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים שֶׁהָיוּ טוֹחֲנוֹת בְּרֵחַיִים שֶׁל יָד, וְנִמְצָא דָּם תַּחַת הַפְּנִימִית — שְׁתֵּיהֶן טְמֵאוֹת, תַּחַת הַחִיצוֹנָה — הַחִיצוֹנָה טְמֵאָה וְהַפְּנִימִית טְהוֹרָה, בֵּינָתַיִם — שְׁתֵּיהֶן טְמֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What is** that incident in question? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **two women who were grinding with a hand mill** and were standing next to each other, **and blood was found beneath the** woman on the **inside,** i.e., the woman standing closest to the mill, **they are both ritually impure.** The reason is that the woman standing further away pushes in to get closer to the mill, and therefore the blood stain could be from either of them. But if blood was found **beneath** the woman on **the outside,** i.e., the woman standing further from the mill, the woman on **the outside is impure and** the woman on **the inside is pure.** If the blood was found **between them, they are both impure.**

The *braita* describes two women grinding grain at a hand mill — *shte nashim she-hayu tachanot b'rechayim shel yad*. One woman was positioned closer to the millstone (*pnimit*, inner), the other farther away (*chitzonah*, outer), though the outer woman sometimes needed to move inward to place grain or gather the flour.

If blood was found under the inner woman: *shtehen teme'ot* — both are *teme'ot*, because the outer woman also moved into the inner space, making her an equal candidate. If found under the outer woman: *ha-chitzonah teme'ah v'ha-pnimit tehorah* — only the outer one is *teme'ah*, since the inner woman had no occasion to move outward. Here the attribution follows not mere convenience but the logic of physical probability. If found precisely between both women: *shtehen teme'ot* — again both, since neither is a stronger candidate.

### Olive Leaf Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה וְנִמְצָא דָּם עַל שְׂפָתָהּ שֶׁל אַמְבָּטִי, וְעַל עָלֶה שֶׁל זַיִת בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁמַּסִּיקוֹת אֶת הַתַּנּוּר, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים וְטִמְּאוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **There was an incident and blood was found on the edge of a bathtub, and** in another case a blood stain was found **on an olive leaf at the time that** the women **were kindling the oven. And the incident came before the Sages and** they **deemed** both women **ritually impure.** Since an olive leaf is not susceptible to ritual impurity, this *baraita* is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya.

The *braita* continues with two additional instances. Blood was found on the rim of a bath (*sfata shel ambati*), or on an olive leaf during the heating of an oven — *al aleh shel zayit b'sha'ah she-hisiku et ha-tanur*. In the olive-leaf case, oven-heating leaves were gathered beneath, and blood was found on one of them. *U-va ma'aseh lifnei chachamim v'tim'um* — when such cases came before the Sages, they declared the women *teme'ot*. Yet an olive leaf is presumably not *mekabel tumah*. This seems to contradict ruling like Rabbi Nechemia.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב מְטַמֵּא, וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה מְטַהֵר, וְהוֹרוּ חֲכָמִים כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav Mattana answered: **It is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** whether or not the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Neḥemya. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a blood stain is found on an item that is not susceptible to ritual impurity, **Rabbi Ya'akov deems her impure, but Rabbi Neḥemya deems her pure. And the Sages ruled** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya.**

*V'mishni tanna'ei hi* — the Gemara resolves the contradiction: the position of the majority (*chachamim*) is itself a debate among *tanna'im*. Another *braita* explicitly states: *Rabbi Yaakov metamei v'Rabbi Nechemia metaher v'horu chachamim k'Rabbi Nechemia* — Rabbi Yaakov renders *tamei*, Rabbi Nechemia renders *tahor*, and the *chachamim* ruled in accordance with Rabbi Nechemia. Since we have a *braita* in which the majority explicitly aligned with Rabbi Nechemia, the ruling follows Rabbi Nechemia. The earlier instances where *chachamim* rendered *teme'ot* represent a tannaic dispute, and the decisive ruling sides with the lenient position.

## Kesamim Criteria

### Three Leniencies

With Rabbi Nechemia's principle now firmly established, the Gemara has effectively articulated three major structural leniencies in the law of *kesamim*. First, there is a minimum size requirement: the stain must be larger than a *geris* (the volume of a louse). Second, it must be found on an object that is *mekabel tumah*. Third — coming up in the Mishnah — it must be on non-colored clothing, since on colored fabric the blood may not be visible or distinguishable.

### Tzara'at Parallel

Each of these leniencies can be explained on its own terms. The *geris* size threshold relates to the louse — a common source of red stains. The *mekabel tumah* requirement follows from the Tosafos analysis of the framework. The colored-clothing criterion follows from the need to visually confirm the stain's character. Yet taken together, these three criteria map precisely onto the three conditions for *tum'at nega'im* — the *tzara'at* stains: a minimum size exceeding a *geris*, occurrence on clothing, and occurrence only on white (non-colored) garments. As Tractate *Nega'im* presents it, those same criteria govern the assessment of stains of *tzara'at*.

### Formal vs. Practical

This convergence raises a fundamental question: are the criteria of *kesamim* independently arrived at through practical halachic reasoning, or are they shaped by a formal structural analogy to *tum'at nega'im*? Opinions in the *mishnayot* are distributed — Rabbi Nechemia articulates the *mekabel tumah* principle, Rabbi Chanina ben Antigonus articulates the stain principle elsewhere — suggesting the framework was assembled from multiple voices. Yet the perfect alignment of all three criteria with the *nega'im* framework suggests that something more formal may be operating. The size of a *geris* is also a standard threshold across various areas of *tumah v'taharah*, suggesting again that formal criteria and practical considerations are deeply intertwined in this area of law.

## Three in One Bed

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים שֶׁהָיוּ יְשֵׁנוֹת בְּמִטָּה אַחַת, וְנִמְצָא דָּם תַּחַת אַחַת מֵהֶן — כּוּלָּן טְמֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **three women who were sleeping in one bed and blood was discovered beneath one of them, all of them are ritually impure.**

The Gemara moves to the next Mishnah: *shalosh nashim she-hayu yeshenot b'mita achat* — three women who were sleeping in one bed, and blood was found beneath one of them.

### All Become Tmeia

*Kulan teme'ot* — all three are *teme'ot*, because during sleep they presumably moved around, and the blood could have originated from any of them. The position of the stain beneath one of the women does not conclusively identify her as the source, since movement in sleep is assumed. If it were established that they lay rigidly in fixed positions and could not have moved, the analysis would differ, but that is the subject of later Gemara discussion.

### Individual Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּדְקָה אַחַת מֵהֶן וְנִמְצֵאת טְמֵאָה — הִיא טְמֵאָה, וּשְׁתֵּיהֶן טְהוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** If when the blood was discovered **one of them examined** herself **and discovered that she was impure** due to menstruation, **she is impure and the** other **two are pure.**

*Badka achat mehen v'nimtzet teme'ah* — if one of the women performed an internal examination after waking and found herself to be a *niddah*, then *hi teme'ah v'shtehen tehorot* — she alone is *teme'ah* and the other two are *tehorot*. The positive identification of one woman as the source of the blood removes the uncertainty and frees the others.

### Attribution Between

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוֹלוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to three women who were sleeping in one bed and blood was discovered beneath one of them, that if one of them is unfit to menstruate **they attribute** the blood **to each other,** i.e., to the women who are fit to menstruate.

*V'tolot zo b'zo* — under other circumstances, which the Gemara will specify, one woman may attribute the stain to another based on differential likelihood. This is the *tolot zo b'zo* principle: when the women are not equal candidates — one pregnant, one not; one postmenopausal, one not; and so on — the woman who is a *less* likely source of the stain may attribute it to the woman who is a *more* likely source.

### Equal Unsuitability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לֹא הָיוּ רְאוּיוֹת לִרְאוֹת — רוֹאִים אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ הֵן רְאוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** all three women **were not fit to see** the flow of blood, e.g., they each belonged to one of the categories listed in the mishna on 7a, **one considers them as though they were fit,** and all three are impure, because the blood must have originated from one of them.

*V'im lo hayu re'uyot lirot* — if none of the women were in a state in which they would be expected to have their cycles, all being pregnant for example, then paradoxically *re'uyot k'ilu hen re'uyot* — they are treated as though they are all equally suitable. Since no one woman is a more likely candidate than another, they cannot attribute to one another, and all remain in the status of *teme'ot*. This is the final clause of the Mishnah and will be revisited at the close of the Gemara's discussion.

## Checking Timing

### Instantaneous Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: וְהוּא שֶׁבָּדְקָה עַצְמָהּ בְּ״שִׁיעוּר וֶסֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one of the women examined herself when the blood was discovered and found that she was impure due to menstruation, she is impure and the other two are pure. In this regard, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** The other two women may attribute the blood to the one who examined herself only **when she examined herself within** the brief **period of time** needed for the onset of **menstruation.** But if she checked herself after this time, although she is impure, the other women are also impure, due to uncertainty.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav*: the ability to identify one woman as the source of the blood and thereby exempt the others depends on a critical condition — *v'hu she-badka atzma b'shiur veset* — she must have checked herself *instantaneously*, within the *shiur veset*, the minimal time interval following the moment of discovery. Only if she checks immediately and finds she is *niddah* can we retroactively conclude that she was *niddah* at the time the stain was found. If she delayed her examination, then even if she finds herself to be *niddah*, we cannot conclude she was *niddah* at the relevant moment — she may have become *niddah* after the stain was discovered, and so the stain cannot be attributed to her.

This is not merely an abstract distinction. It goes to the heart of how status determinations work in *hilchot niddah*: a woman's *niddah* status is established at the moment of confirmed *re'iyah*, and attribution of past events depends on whether that status was established at the relevant time.

### Chatat Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּבַר פְּדָא, דְּאָמַר: כֹּל שֶׁבַּעְלָהּ בְּחַטָּאת — טׇהֳרוֹתֶיהָ טְמֵאוֹת. בַּעְלָהּ בְּאָשָׁם תָּלוּי — טׇהֳרוֹתֶיהָ תְּלוּיוֹת, בַּעְלָהּ פָּטוּר — טׇהֳרוֹתֶיהָ טְהוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rav **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **bar Padda, who said:** There are three time periods with regard to defining a woman's ritual-purity status if she sees blood after engaging in intercourse. The shortest is the period of time required for the onset of menstruation, i.e., for menstrual bleeding to begin. The next shortest is the time it would take the woman to get out of bed after intercourse and wash her private parts. The longest period is any time longer than that. If a woman finds blood after intercourse within the period of time needed for the onset of menstruation, she must have been impure during intercourse. With regard to **any** woman **whose husband** would be liable **to** bring **a sin offering** if he had engaged in intercourse with her, because she found the blood within that short period, if she had not been engaging in intercourse, but rather had been preparing food in a state of ritual purity, and then she found blood within that short period, all the **pure items** that she touched **are ritually impure.** **By contrast, with regard to any woman whose husband** would be liable **to** bring **a provisional guilt offering** if he had engaged in intercourse with her, as it is uncertain whether he violated a transgression for which he would be liable to bring a sin offering, if she had not been engaging in intercourse, but rather had been preparing food in a state of ritual purity and then found blood within that same time period, the status of all the **pure items** she touched is **suspended,** as their status is uncertain. They are not burned but they may not be eaten either. Finally, with regard to any woman **whose husband** would be **exempt** from bringing any offering, i.e., where she finds blood after a longer time period, if she had not been engaging in intercourse, but rather had been preparing food in a state of ritual purity, and then found the blood after a longer time period, all the **pure items** she touched remain **pure.**

*Savar lah k'Bar Pada* — Rav Yehuda's ruling follows Bar Pada's framework. *D'amar kol she-ba'alah b'chatat tissaref terumah* — whatever scenario would obligate the husband to bring a *chatat* (sin offering) after relations with his wife who was found to be *niddah* also obligates the burning of *terumah* she handled. The two cases are parallel: the husband's *chatat* obligation and the *terumah*'s *tum'ah* status are determined by the same standard of when the woman's *niddah* status is established.

### Bar Pada Framework

Bar Pada's framework operates in three tiers. If the woman checked *immediately* after relations — within the *shiur veset* — and found she was *niddah*, the husband brings a *chatat*, since we conclude she was *niddah* during relations. Correspondingly, any *terumah* she handled during that same window is burned. If she checked after a slightly longer delay — producing a state of doubt (*safek*) about whether she was already *niddah* during relations — the husband brings an *asham talui*, and the *terumah* or *taharot* she handled are in a suspended (*taluy*) state. If she waited long enough that we simply cannot say she was *niddah* during relations — treating her as having become *niddah* afterward — the husband is entirely exempt, and the things she handled are *tehorot*.

The application to the three-women case is direct: only in the first tier, where the check was truly instantaneous, can we definitively attribute the stain to the woman who found herself *niddah* and thereby exempt the other two.

## Blood Prevention

### Yoshia Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בַּעְלָהּ בְּחַטָּאת — טׇהֳרוֹתֶיהָ תְּלוּיוֹת. בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם, אֵימַר שַׁמָּשׁ עַכְּבֵיהּ לְדָם, [אֲבָל] הָכָא, אִם אִיתַהּ (דְּהָוֵי) [דַּהֲוָה] דָּם, מַאן עַכְּבֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Oshaya says:** There is no connection between her husband's obligation to bring an offering and the ritual-purity status of the items she handled. **Even** if **her husband** is liable **to** bring **a sin offering,** i.e., when she discovered the blood within the period of time needed for the onset of menstruation, the status of all the **pure items** she touched before she discovered the blood is **suspended,** as their status is uncertain. Rabbi Oshaya explains his reasoning. **Granted, there,** in the case of intercourse, where the husband must bring an offering, one can **say** that the male **organ prevented the blood** from emerging from her body, and therefore it is clear that she was impure beforehand. **But here,** in the case of preparing food in a state of ritual purity, **if it is so that there was blood** coming out of her while she was preparing the food, **what prevented** the blood from emerging from her body?

Rabbi Yoshia's principle provides the physiological basis for why an instantaneous post-coital check can establish *niddah* status *during* relations rather than merely *after* them. The principle: *emar shamash ikvo l'dam* — relations themselves prevented the blood from emerging. The blood was present, but was held back by the physical fact of relations. Upon separation, it emerged immediately. Therefore, finding blood immediately after relations is effectively equivalent to blood having been present *during* relations.

### Child & Elder Mashal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דוֹמֶה? לְיֶלֶד וְזָקֵן שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהָיוּ בַּדֶּרֶךְ — יֶלֶד שׁוֹהֶא לָבֹא, נִכְנְסוּ לָעִיר — יֶלֶד מְמַהֵר לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says a parable** in explanation **of** the opinion of **Rabbi Oshaya: To what is this matter comparable? To a child and an old man who are walking along the road. As long as they are on the road,** the **child delays his arrival,** i.e., he walks at the pace of the old man. But once **they enter the city,** the **child hastens his arrival,** and runs on ahead. Similarly, as soon as the couple has finished engaging in intercourse, the blood comes quickly, but the blood is hindered from coming out during intercourse.

*Amar Rabbi Yirmia mashal d'Rabbi Yoshia l'mai davar domeh* — Rabbi Yirmia offers an analogy. A child and an elder are walking together along a road toward a city. As long as they are walking together on the road, the child walks respectfully beside the elder, matching his pace. *Nichnasu la'ir* — the moment they enter the city and the companionship is dissolved, *yeled memaher lavo* — the child runs off immediately. The restraint was the joint circumstance of the road; its removal triggers immediate, rapid action. So too: the physical circumstance of relations restrained the blood; its cessation brought the blood immediately.

### Eye & Tears Mashal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דוֹמֶה? לְאָדָם שֶׁנּוֹתֵן אֶצְבַּע בָּעַיִן, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאֶצְבַּע בָּעַיִן — דִּמְעָה שׁוֹהָא לָבֹא, נָטַל הָאֶצְבַּע — דִּמְעָה מְמַהֶרֶת לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye** also **says a parable** in explanation **of** the opinion of **Rabbi Oshaya: To what is this matter comparable? To a man who puts** his **finger in** his **eye. As long as** his **finger is in** his **eye,** the **tears delay their arrival** and remain in the eye. But as soon as **he** has **removed his finger** the **tears hasten their arrival.**

*V'amar Abaye mashal d'Rabbi Yoshia l'mai davar domeh* — Abaye offers a second analogy. If one presses a finger against the eye, tears will not flow — the pressure prevents their emergence — but the moment the finger is removed, *dim'ah memaheret lavo* — the tears come immediately. Again: the restraint removed, the immediate consequence follows.

### Distinction from Others

This reasoning applies specifically to post-coital checking. In other contexts — such as handling *terumah* — if there is a delay, we cannot invoke this principle. There was no physical impediment holding back the blood; if blood appears now, it appeared now, not earlier. The uniqueness of the sexual context is that the physical act of relations itself creates a documented physiological basis for saying that blood present immediately afterward was already present *during*. Without that basis, even an instantaneous check does not retroactively establish prior *niddah* status. This is why the standards diverge between the husband's *chatat* and other cases, and why Rabbi Oshaya could argue that even if the husband brings a *chatat* — the most stringent tier — the *terumah* she handled may still only be in a state of *safek tum'ah* rather than certain *tum'ah*, since the *terumah* was not handled during an act of relations.

## Tolot Criteria

### Pregnant Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוֹלוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד תּוֹלוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ? עוּבָּרָה וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ עוּבָּרָה — תּוֹלָה עוּבָּרָה בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ עוּבָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **How do they attribute** the blood **to one another?** If one of the women is **pregnant and** one **is not pregnant,** the **pregnant** woman **may attribute** the blood **to the** woman **who is not pregnant.**

*Tanu Rabbanan keitzad tolot zo b'zo* — the Sages taught: how does the attribution among the women in the bed work? *Ubarah v'she'eina ubarah* — if one woman is pregnant and one is not, *tolah ubarah b'she'eina ubarah* — the pregnant woman may attribute the stain to the non-pregnant one. Pregnancy significantly reduces the likelihood of uterine bleeding, making the non-pregnant woman the more plausible source.

### Nursing Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנִיקָה וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ מְנִיקָה — תּוֹלָה מְנִיקָה בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ מְנִיקָה. זְקֵנָה וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ זְקֵנָה — תּוֹלָה זְקֵנָה בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ זְקֵנָה. בְּתוּלָה וְשֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּתוּלָה — תּוֹלָה בְּתוּלָה בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּתוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** If one of the women is **nursing** and the other **is not nursing, the nursing** woman **may attribute** the blood **to the** woman **who is not nursing.** If one of the women is **old** and no longer experiences bleeding regularly, **and** the other one **is not old,** the **old** woman **may attribute** the blood **to** the woman **who is not old.** Likewise, if one of the women is **a virgin,** in this context, one who has not yet experienced bleeding due to her youth, **and** the other woman **is not a virgin,** in this context, one who has experienced bleeding, the **virgin may attribute** the blood **to** the woman **who is not a virgin.**

*Menika v'she'eina menika* — if one woman is nursing and one is not, *tolah menika b'she'eina menika* — the nursing woman may attribute the stain to the non-nursing woman. Nursing similarly suppresses uterine bleeding, making the non-nursing woman the stronger candidate.

*Zekena v'she'eina zekena* — postmenopausal versus premenopausal: *tolah zekena b'she'eina zekena* — the postmenopausal woman may attribute the stain to the premenopausal woman, since uterine bleeding is no longer expected of the postmenopausal woman.

*Besulah v'she'eina besulah* — here the term *besulah* is used in the sense of *mesuleket damim*, a woman who has not yet begun to menstruate, as opposed to one who has already established her cycle. *Tolah besulah b'she'eina besulah* — the pre-menarche woman may attribute the stain to the post-menarche woman.

### Equal Status Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוּבָּרוֹת, שְׁתֵּיהֶן מְנִיקוֹת, שְׁתֵּיהֶן זְקֵנוֹת, שְׁתֵּיהֶן בְּתוּלוֹת — זוֹ הִיא שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: לֹא הָיוּ רְאוּיוֹת לִרְאוֹת — רוֹאִים אוֹתָן כְּאִילּוּ הֵן רְאוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If both** women **are pregnant,** or **both** women **are nursing,** or **both** women **are old,** or **both** women **are virgins,** in **this** case the *halakha* is **as we learned** in the mishna: If both women **were not fit to see** menstrual blood, **one considers them as though they were fit,** and all three are impure, because the blood must have originated from one of them.

*Hayu shteian ubarot, shteian menikot, shteian zekenos, shteian besulot* — if both women share the same status in every relevant category, then *zu hi she-shaninu* — this is the case we stated in the Mishnah: *lo hayu re'uyot lirot, ro'im k'ilu hen re'uyot* — since they are all equally unsuitable candidates in the same way, they are treated as though all equally suitable, and *kulan teme'ot* — all become *teme'ot* with no basis for attribution in any direction.
