# Sanhedrin 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boel Aramit Status

### Intro & Question

The daf opens on the final line of 81b with the principle of *kanayim pogim bo* — the doctrine that zealots may act extrajudicially against certain transgressors — and the paradigmatic case through which that doctrine is explored: *v'haboh al ha'aramit*, one who engages in relations with a non-Jewish woman.

The discussion transitions immediately to the top of 82a, where Rav Kahana poses a question to Rav.

### Rav Kahana's Q

*Ba'ei minei Rav Kahana me'Rav*: Rav Kahana asked Rav — if the *kanayim* do not act, what is this person's legal status? What precisely is the sin, and what punishment does he incur? The underlying difficulty is that the biblical prohibition of *lo titchaten bam* addresses intermarriage in the context of *ishus*, of a quasi-matrimonial relationship. But where a man merely fornicates — casual relations outside any such context — what exactly is the transgression, and what is its formal severity? *Mahu?*

Rav, however, could not answer: *inshi Rav l'gmarei* — Rav forgot his tradition on the matter.

### Dream & Pasuk

The resolution came through an unusual channel. *Akri'u l'Rav Kahana b'chelmeih* — a *pasuk* was read to Rav Kahana in a dream:

> בָּגְדָה יְהוּדָה וְתוֹעֵבָה נֶעֶשְׂתָה בְיִשְׂרָאֵל וּבִירוּשָׁלַיִם כִּי חִלַּל יְהוּדָה קֹדֶשׁ ה' אֲשֶׁר אָהֵב וּבָעַל בַּת אֵל נֵכָר
> *(Malachi 2:11)*

Rav Kahana came to Rav and reported the dream. *Ad d'kavi Rav l'gmarei* — this prompted Rav to recover his forgotten teaching, and he proceeded to interpret the verse.

### Rav's Drasha

Rav reads the verse as encoding four distinct transgressions. *Bagdah Yehudah* — "Judah betrayed" — *zu avoda zarah*, this refers to idolatry, as confirmed by another verse: *u'v'chen bagadetem bi beit Yisrael neum Hashem* — "you have betrayed Me, O house of Israel." The language of betrayal (*bagidah*) captures the essence of our covenantal relationship with God: to worship foreign gods is to commit the ultimate act of infidelity.

*V'toevah naasta b'Yisrael u'biYerushalayim* — "an abomination was committed in Israel and Jerusalem" — *zeh mishkav zachor*, this refers to male homosexual intercourse, as the Torah states: *v'et zachar lo tishkav mishkevei isha toevah hi*. Both of these initial references describe sins that are *chayav misah*.

*Ki chillel Yehudah kodesh Hashem* — "for Judah has desecrated what is holy to God" — *zu zonah*, this refers to prostitution, as the verse states: *lo tiheyeh kadeshah mibnot Yisrael v'lo yiheyeh kadesh mibnei Yisrael*. Notably, this transgression is not *chayav misah*; it is a *lav*.

Finally, *u'vaal bat el nechar* — "and he has coupled with the daughter of a foreign god" — *zeh haboh al hakutit*, one who has relations with a non-Jewish woman. The continuation of the verse reads:

> יַכְרֵת ה' לָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂנָּה עֵר וָעֹנֶה מֵאָהֳלֵי יַעֲקֹב וּמַגִּישׁ מִנְחָה לַה' צְבָאוֹת
> *(Malachi 2:12)*

Rav interprets this to mean: if he is a Torah scholar, *lo yiheyeh lo er b'chachamim v'oneh b'talmidim* — he will have no standing among the sages, no regular place among students; he will be cut off from the *beit midrash*. If he is a *kohen*, *lo yiheyeh lo ben magish mincha l'Hashem tzevaot* — he will have no son to bring offerings on the altar.

This raises a striking question: the punishment described here does not obviously constitute *chiyuv misah*. The verse's phrasing about being "cut off" is ambiguous — is this *karet* or merely the loss of social status? And between the clear capital cases at the beginning of the verse and this final case, there is the intermediate category of *zonah*, a mere *lav*. The *rishonim*, as we shall see the *gemara* return to, feel that the doctrine of *kanayim pogim bo* — which effectively licenses taking the transgressor's life — cannot be coherently grounded unless we assume this person bears at minimum some sense of *chiyuv misah bidei shamayim*. That forces a focus on the word *yicharet* in the verse — which at least implies *karet* — and that is the direction in which the *halacha* will ultimately be read.

### Intermarriage & G-d

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba adds a dimension beyond formal penalty. *Kol ha-ba al kutit ke'ilu mis'chaten ba-avoda kochavim* — whoever has relations with a non-Jewish woman, it is as though he has made the idols themselves his *machatanim*, his family by marriage. The source: *u'vaal bat el nechar* — he has coupled with the daughter of a foreign god. The *gemara* immediately asks: *ve-chi bas yesh lo l'el nechar?* Does a foreign deity actually have a daughter? The point, of course, is not literal. Rather, through this relationship one draws the entire apparatus of foreign worship into the fabric of the Jewish family. This is why intermarriage has always been perceived — rightly — as an existential threat to Jewish continuity and identity, far beyond whatever formal halachic category it may occupy. One is not merely transgressing a prohibition; one is importing *avoda zara* into the household of Israel.

### Yehoyakim Story

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba continues with a separate teaching. He reports that written on the skull of Yehoyakim — one of the late kings of Judah during the period of the First Temple, who permitted idolatrous worship to continue despite prophetic rebuke — were the words *zot ve-od acheres*: this punishment, and yet another one is still to come.

The grandfather of Rabbi Pereda — identified in the context as Rabbi Chiya bar Abba himself, though the *gemara* curiously does not name him as such — *ashkecha le-hahu gulgolta de-vashadya be-sha'arei Yerushalayim*: he found a skull that had been cast out and left to roll about at the gates of Jerusalem. He tried to bury it: *kavra*, and then *ve-hadar n'vag* — it resurfaced. He buried it again, and again it pushed its way back up. At that point he recognized it must be something extraordinary.

*Hai gulgolta shel Yehoyakim* — this must be the skull of Yehoyakim, as the prophet had declared: *kevuras chamor yikaver, sachov vehashlech mehaleh l'sha'arei Yerushalayim* — he shall be buried with the burial of a donkey, dragged and cast out from the gates of Jerusalem. A divine decree had been set: his body would not rest in the earth.

Yet even recognizing this, he felt compelled to show the former king some dignity. *Malka hu ve-lav orech ara l'vizzuyei* — he was nonetheless a king, and it is not fitting to degrade him so. So he took the skull, wrapped it in silk, and placed it in a box in his room.

His wife discovered it, and — naturally — went to her neighbors with the alarming find. They drew the worst possible conclusion: *hai de-it'sa kamaisa* — this must be the skull of his first wife, whom he apparently cannot let go. So she threw it into the oven and burned it to ash. *Ve-hainu dich'siv al gulgolto: zot ve-od acheres* — that was the *ot acheres*, the second punishment: not only was he denied burial, but his very remains were ultimately incinerated.

When Rabbi Chiya bar Abba came, he said: now I understand what was written on it.

### Hasmon. Edict

The *gemara* returns to the formal question of *boel aramit*. When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported: *beit dino shel Chashmonai gazru* — the *beit din* of the Hasmonean era issued a rabbinic enactment. It is noteworthy that the *gemara* attributes this specifically to the Hasmoneans — a period of religious reformation and heightened concern about Hellenization and cultural assimilation.

The edict: *haba al hakutit chayav alei mishum nashka* — one who has relations with a non-Jewish woman is treated rabbinically as having violated four prohibitions, encoded in the acronym *nashka*: *niddah* (even though the laws of *niddah* technically apply only to a Jewish woman, she is treated as one rabbinically); *shifcha* (a slave woman, who is categorically forbidden); *goya* (for her very status as a non-Jew, which in the Torah applies only to marriage, but is extended rabbinically to casual relations as well); and *alef* for *eshet ish* (a married woman — even though her marriage is not technically recognized as *ishus* in the full halachic sense, we treat her as a married woman for this purpose).

When Ravin came, he reported a variant: *mishum nashgaz* — replacing *eshet ish* with *zonah* (prostitute), yielding *niddah, shifcha, goya, zonah*. *Aval mishum ishut leit lah* — he does not recognize even a rabbinic status of *eshet ish* for a non-Jewish woman. His reasoning: non-Jewish men do not hold their wives to fidelity, so there is no meaningful marriage to recognize; instead, we assign her the status of a *zonah*.

Rav Dimi, by contrast, holds the opposite: *nashayehu vadai lo mafkere* — non-Jewish husbands do in fact take their wives' fidelity seriously, and therefore one should recognize *eshet ish* rather than *zonah*.

All of this elaborates the formal rabbinic prohibitions that underlie the case where *kanayim pogim bo* applies. As the *gemara* notes, it is striking that intermarriage is treated with a social severity exceeding many formal transgressions of far greater halachic weight. The answer lies in the existential dimension already articulated — this is a genuine threat to Jewish continuity — and it is that dimension which the doctrine of *kanayim pogim bo* expresses at its most dramatic level.

## Kanayim Framework

### No Ruling Given

*Itmar nami* — it was also taught: Rav Chisda said, *haba limalech ein morin lo* — if someone comes to ask whether he may go and kill a person caught in the act of relations with a non-Jew, we do not rule for him that it is permitted. The entire mechanism of *kanayim pogim bo* operates outside the formal legal system. We are, in a sense, retrospectively accepting what the zealot has done — even, in the right circumstances, praising it — but we cannot prospectively endorse it. There is an inherent tension here: the *halacha* exists, yet we refuse to teach it to one who asks. To license vigilantism openly would be to invite disorder and create a population of self-appointed executioners. So the principle remains operative yet judicially untransmitted.

Rabba bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *haba limalech ein morin lo*.

### Zimri & Pinchas

And not only that, but the *gemara* goes further to illustrate how radically the operative window is circumscribed. *Ela she-im pirash Zimri ve-harago Pinchas neherag alav* — if Zimri had separated from Kozbi and Pinchas killed him when they were no longer in the act, Pinchas would have been guilty of murder. *Neherag alav.* He would be subject to execution.

The doctrine of *kanayim pogim bo* applies — if it applies at all — only at the precise moment of intercourse. Miss that window by even a moment, and what was a potential act of *kana'uth* becomes unlawful killing. The narrowness of this ruling is striking: not only do we refuse to give advance permission, but the conditions for post-facto endorsement are extraordinarily tight.

### Rodeph Status

There is a further, and quite remarkable, dimension. *Nehepach Zimri ve-harago le-Pinchas ein neherag alav* — if Zimri had turned around and killed Pinchas as Pinchas was coming for him, Zimri would not have been executed for it. Why? *She-harei rodeph hu* — because Pinchas in that moment is a *rodeph*, a pursuer seeking his life. From a purely legal standpoint, even at the very moment that *kanayim pogim bo* could apply — even while Pinchas is entirely within whatever latitude exists for zealot action — Zimri retains the right of self-defense, because Pinchas is pursuing him with lethal intent.

The *gemara* is here holding two ideas simultaneously: we can, after the fact, accept or even honor what Pinchas did; and yet, from within the legal system at that identical moment, Pinchas was a *rodeph* whom Zimri could lawfully kill. These two frames coexist, each fully operative. The *gemara* is not resolving the tension so much as insisting that both propositions are true at once.

## Pinchas Narrative

### Judges Confront

The *gemara* now turns to the biblical narrative itself. *Vayomer Moshe el shophtei Yisrael: hargu ish anashav ha-nitzmadim le-va'al pe'or* — Moshe said to the judges of Israel: each of you kill those of your men who attached themselves to Baal Peor. Trials began and executions were carried out.

### Zimri's Act

*Halach shivto shel Shimon etzel Zimri ben Salu* — the tribe of Shimon went to Zimri. They said to him: *hen danin dinei nefashot ve-atah yoshev ve-shotek?* — they are executing people, presumably from our own tribe, and you sit here silent?

*Mah asah* — what did Zimri do? *Amad ve-kibetz esrim ve-arba'ah elef mi-Yisrael* — he gathered twenty-four thousand Israelites. The *gemara* identifies these as the very twenty-four thousand who would die in the ensuing plague, presuming them to have been perpetrators themselves. *Ve-halach etzel Kozbi* — and he went to Kozbi, the daughter of Tzur, one of the princes of Midian.

He said to her: *hashmi'i li* — be with me. She responded: *bat melech ani* — I am a princess. *Ve-chen tzivani abba: lo tishme'i ela le-gadol she-ba-hem* — my father instructed me to submit to no one less than the greatest among them, meaning Moshe. Zimri replied: *af hu nasi shevet hu* — I too am a tribal chieftain. *Velo od ela she-hu gadol mimenu* — indeed I am greater than Moshe. *She-hu sheni la-beten* — I, as head of Shimon, am the second tribe; Moshe, as head of Levi, leads only the third. Therefore, by her father's own standard, she should comply with him.

*Tafshah be-vloritah* — he seized her by her hair, and *ve-hevi'ah etzel Moshe* — brought her before Moshe. Addressing him as *ben Amram* — conspicuously stripping him of the title of *Moshe Rabbeinu* — he challenged: *zu asurah o muteret?* Is this woman forbidden to me or permitted? *Ve-im tomar asurah* — and if you say forbidden — *bat Yitro mi hitirah lach?* Who permitted you the daughter of Yitro? Rashi comes quickly to Moshe's defense, noting that the daughter of Yitro had stood at Har Sinai and entered the covenant, thereby becoming Jewish. But the challenge had its effect.

### Moshe Lost

*Ba-otah sha'ah nitalmah mimenu halacha* — at that moment, the *halacha* escaped Moshe. This was not, apparently, the question of why he had been permitted to marry the daughter of Yitro, but rather the operative *halacha* of what to do about Zimri — the principle of *kanayim pogim bo* itself. Perhaps because he was being personally attacked, perhaps for some other reason, Moshe could not access the ruling.

One might pause here: God had already commanded through Moshe to judge and execute those who attached themselves to Baal Peor. Why, then, is *kanayim pogim bo* described as extra-legal? The answer the *gemara* implies is that such a command would require a full judicial process — witnesses, trial, formal verdict. What Pinchas did was something else: an immediate, extrajudicial act. That is why it required its own distinct category of *halacha*.

*Ga'u kulam bivchiyah* — they all broke out weeping. *Ve-hainu dich'siv: ve-hemah vochim petach ohel mo'ed* — as the verse states, "and they were weeping at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting."

### Pinchas Acts

*Ve-cheitiv vayar Pinchas ben Elazar* — and the verse says: Pinchas son of Elazar saw. *Mah ra'ah?* — what did he see? Here the *gemara* records three interpretations.

Rav says: *ra'ah ma'aseh ve-nizkar halacha* — he saw the act unfolding and remembered the *halacha* of *kanayim pogim bo*. He then went to Moshe: *achi avi abba* — brother of my grandfather, for Pinchas was the son of Elazar the son of Aharon, making Moshe his great-uncle. It is interesting that he does not say *Moshe Rabbeinu*, especially against the backdrop of Zimri's contemptuous *ben Amram*. He asked: *lo kach limadtani bi-ridtecha me-Har Sinai?* — is this not what you taught me when you came down from Har Sinai? *Haba al ha-kutit kanayim pogim bo.* Moshe responded: *karyana de-igarta ihu lehevei parvenka* — the reader of the message should be its executor. Notably, Moshe did not say "yes, do it" — that would violate *ein morin lo* — but rather pointed back to Pinchas as the one who had articulated the ruling.

Shmuel offers a different reading of what Pinchas saw. He was guided by the principle: *ein chochmah ve-ein tevunah ve-ein etzah neged Hashem* — there is no wisdom, no understanding, and no counsel against God. *Kol makom she-yesh chillul Hashem ein cholkin kavod la-rav* — whenever there is a desecration of God's name, one does not defer to religious authority that is failing to act. Pinchas did not look up a *halacha*; he rose because he knew he had to defend God's honor.

Rabbi Yitzchak said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *ra'ah she-ba malach ve-hishchit ba-am* — he saw the angel coming to destroy among the people; the plague was beginning. He understood that something had to be done immediately to stop it.

Three distinct accounts: a man acting from precise legal knowledge; a man acting from moral imperative; a man acting from urgent necessity in the face of catastrophe.

*Vayakom mitoch ha-edah vayikach romach be-yado* — he rose from the congregation and took his spear. The *gemara* derives from the phrase *mitoch ha-edah* — from the midst of the congregation — that *ein nichnasim bi-khli zayin le-veit ha-midrash*: one does not bring weapons into the *beit midrash*. He had to first leave the study setting before arming himself.

*Shalaf shinana, hinichah be-unkalo* — he removed the spearhead and placed it in his cloak, concealing it. He then leaned on the wooden shaft as though it were a walking stick, entering the territory of the tribe of Shimon while saying: *heichan matzinu she-shivto shel Levi gadol mi-shel Shimon?* — where have we ever found that Levi is greater than Shimon? He was posing as someone who also wanted to participate. The tribe of Shimon said: *hanichu lo, af hu la-asot tzorcho nichnas* — let him through, he too wants to take care of his needs. The onlookers reportedly remarked: *habitu perushim et ha-davar* — even the separated ones, even the pious, are doing this now. And so he was admitted.

### Six Miracles

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *shisha nissim na'asu lo le-Pinchas* — six miracles were performed for Pinchas in the course of his act.

First, that Zimri could have separated from Kozbi and did not, which would have immediately stripped Pinchas of any license to act.

Second, that Zimri could have spoken up — he was surrounded by twenty-four thousand tribal members — and did not call for help when he saw Pinchas draw the spear.

Third, *she-kivein be-zachruto shel ish u-vi-nekevutah shel isha* — that his spear passed precisely through their genitalia as they were engaged in the act, so that everyone could see the nature and justice of what had occurred. This is understood by the *gemara* as implicit in how the *pesukim* can be read, and is directly relevant to the parameters of *kanayim pogim bo*: the act had to be witnessed in flagrante.

Fourth, *she-lo nishmetu min ha-romach* — they did not slip off the spear. Pinchas carried them out of the tent on the spear so the people could witness it, and miraculously they remained impaled upon it.

Fifth, *she-ba malach ve-higbiya et ha-mashkof* — the angel came and raised the lintel of the tent's doorway so that Pinchas, carrying the spear with both bodies upon it, could pass through without stooping, which would have caused them to fall.

Sixth, *she-ba malach ve-hishchit ba-am* — the angel began bringing the plague to the people, distracting the crowd and preventing them from attacking Pinchas in retaliation.

### Divine Praise

*Ba ve-chavtan lifnei ha-Makom* — Pinchas then brought the two of them and cast them down before God. He cried out: *Ribono shel olam! Al elu yiplu esrim ve-arba'ah elef mi-Yisrael?* — Master of the Universe, for these two, twenty-four thousand of Israel should fall? The verse confirms: *vayihyu ha-meitim ba-mageifah esrim ve-arba'ah elef*. The *gemara* reads the verse *vaya'amod Pinchas vayefalel* — Pinchas stood up and *vayefalel* — not, Rabbi Elazar insists, in the sense of *vayitpallel*, he prayed, but in the sense of *pelilot*, he made an argument. *Melamed kivyachol she-asah pelilot im kono* — he argued with God, challenging the justice of so many deaths.

*Bikshu malachei ha-sharet le-dachfo* — the ministering angels sought to push Pinchas away. Who is this human to argue with God? God said to them: *hanichu lo — kanai ben kanai* — leave him alone; he is a zealot, son of a zealot. Rashi reads this as a reference to Shimon and Levi at the incident of Dinah, though it might equally evoke the Levites who rose at Har Sinai. *Meshiv cheimah ben meshiv cheimah* — one who turns away Divine wrath, descended from one who turned away Divine wrath: Aharon, who brought the *ketoret* to halt a plague, was Pinchas's great-grandfather. Now Pinchas himself has acted to stop one. He comes by his *kana'uth* honestly, and it is entirely *le-shem shamayim*.

### Tribe Contempt

The other tribes, however, were not so generous in their assessment. *Hitchilu shevatim mevazim* — the tribes began to mock him. *Ra'item ben Puti zeh* — have you seen this son of Puti? The reference is to the verse identifying Pinchas's grandfather on his mother's side: *Elazar lakach lo mi-bnot Putiel lo le-isha* — Elazar had married from the daughters of Putiel. The *gemara* identifies Putiel with Yitro, *she-pittem agalim la-avoda zara* — who fattened calves for idol worship. So they sneered: this descendant of an idol-worshipper's family — *ve-harag nasi shevet mi-Yisrael?* — has killed a prince of a tribe of Israel?

*Ba ha-katuv ve-yichasao* — the verse comes and traces his lineage to defend him: *Pinchas ben Elazar ben Aharon ha-kohen*. Not through his mother, but through his grandfather: he is the grandson of Aharon ha-kohen. God said to Moshe: *hekdem lo shalom* — go and bring him peace first.

### Covenant Peace

*She-ne'emar: lachen emor hineni nosein lo et briti shalom* — as it is said: "Therefore declare: I hereby give him My covenant of peace." The atonement that Pinchas brought through his act, the *gemara* states, was to be a source of ongoing atonement: *ve-ra'uyah kapara zo she-tehei mechaperes ve-holeches le-olam* — this atonement was fitting to endure for all time, as expressed by *bris kehunas olam*: an eternal covenant of priesthood. There is something profoundly suggestive in the fact that the man of violence is given a covenant of peace — and indeed, in the book of Yehoshua, Pinchas ultimately becomes a diplomat, a man of peace. The warrior becomes the negotiator; that trajectory is already embedded in the divine response here.

## Names & Acts

### Gematria 424

Rav Nachman said in the name of Rav: what is meant by the verse *zarzir masnayim o tayish u-melech al-kum imo* (Proverbs 30:31)? The plain sense speaks of a greyhound, a he-goat, and a king whom none can stand against — impressive and slightly puzzling company. The *gemara* reads the verse as a reference to the story of Zimri.

*Arba meot ve-esrim ve-arba be-ilot ba'al oso rasha oso ha-yom* — that wicked man had relations 424 times on that day. The *gematria* of the word *zarzir* is 424. The word *masnayim* refers to the hips — the act of intercourse. Zimri is the *zarzir masnayim*. *Ve-himtin lo Pinchas* — and Pinchas waited for him, reading *masnayim* now as the root *himtin*, waited — until *ad she-tashash kocho*, until Zimri was exhausted. *Ve-hu eino yodea she-melech al-kum imo* — but Pinchas did not realize he did not have to wait; the King against Whom none can rise was with him all along, and God would have protected him regardless.

A *baraita* of Rabbi Yosef offers a different count: not 424 but 60 acts of intercourse, *ad she-na'aseh ke-beitza muzeret* — until he became like a scrambled or spoiled egg, meaning either his body or his semen was spent and he was left utterly weakened. *Ve-hi hayeta ke-aruga malei mayim* — and she was like a garden bed filled with water. Rav Kahana adds: *u-moshava beit sa'ah* — her capacity was a *beit sa'ah*, an area of considerable size. Rabbi Yosef further teaches: *kavrah shelo amah amah* — the dimensions attributed to her were an *amah* wide. These are the *gemara*'s very graphic descriptions of the flagrant nature of their fornication.

### Kozbi Shvilnai

Rav Sheshes teaches: *lo Kozbi shemah* — her name was not actually Kozbi. *Ella Shvilnai* — her true name was Shvilnai, daughter of Tzur. Shvilnai, as the *gemara* will show, was a name that had entered popular speech as a byword for a notorious prostitute.

*Ve-lamah nikra shemah Kozbi?* — why then is she called Kozbi? *She-kozva aviha* — because she betrayed her father: her father had instructed her to submit only to Moshe, and she allowed herself to be taken by Zimri. Alternatively — *davar acher* — the name *Kozbi* can be read as *kos bi im zeh*: "slaughter for me through this one," meaning she told her father that through this fornication she would bring destruction upon Israel.

*Ve-hainu de-amri inshi* — and because her real name was Shvilnai, she appears in the following popular sayings: *bein kani le-urvani Shvilnai mai ba'ya?* — between the reeds and the willows, what is Shvilnai doing there? The implication is obvious: what else would she be doing in a secluded spot among the reeds and willows? Similarly: *be-hadi kilfei di-knei Shvilnai mai ba'ya?* — among these peeled reeds, what is Shvilnai doing? Same meaning, same insinuation. *Gafta le-ima* — she has brought her mother along into her evil as well; Rashi explains that calling her a *zonah* was taken to implicate her mother as a *zonah* also.

### Zimri's Names

Rabbi Yochanan states: *chamesh shemos yesh lo* — Zimri bore five names.

He was called *Zimri* — *al she-na'asah ke-beitzah muzeret*, because through the act he was reduced to the state of a spoiled egg.

He was called *ben Salu* — *al she-histil avonot shel mishpachto*, Rashi explaining that he caused people to investigate or speak about the sins of his family, bringing disgrace upon his lineage.

He was called *Shaul* — *al she-hish'il atzmo le-dvar aveirah*, he lent himself out to a matter of sin.

He was called *ben ha-Kena'anit* — *al she-asah ma'aseh Kena'an*, he acted with the licentiousness of Canaan, Canaan being classically associated with sexual transgression in the Torah.

*U-mah shemo?* — and what is his real name? *Shelumiel ben Tzurishaddai shemo* — his name is Shelumiel ben Tzurishaddai, who appears in the book of Bamidbar as the designated chieftain of the tribe of Shimon at the time of the offerings of the *nesi'im*. The *gemara* identifies Zimri with that figure. It is an interesting footnote that the Yiddish word *shlemiel* appears to derive from *Shelumiel* — and the *gemara*'s association of that name with Zimri gives the etymology a certain dark resonance.

## Kohen Tumah

### Kohen Head

The *gemara* turns to another case in the *Mishnah*: a *kohen* who performs the *avodah* in a state of *tumah*. The *Mishnah* teaches that his fellow *kohanim* — specifically the younger priests, *pirchei kehunah* — would not bring him to *beit din*, but would take him outside the *azarah* and crack open his head with logs.

### Divine Death Q

Rav Acha bar Hunah asked Rav Sheshes: *Kohen she-shimmesh be-tumah — chayav misah bidei Shamayim o ein chayav misah bidei Shamayim?* — is this transgression deserving of divine death, or not? Because the question is: on what basis do his fellow priests crack open his head? Rav Sheshes responds: *tanisuha* — the answer is in the *Mishnah* itself, and the question can be resolved by reasoning from it.

### Lashing Logic

If we say he is *chayav misah bidei Shamayim*, then *nishvkei de-liktil bidei Shamayim* — let God handle it. Why do the priests act? And yet if we say he is not *chayav misah bidei Shamayim* — the *gemara* presses back in the other direction as well. *Midei dumi de-amar Rachmana patur ve-anan katlinan leh?* — could it be that God exempts him and we execute him without any underlying sense of *chiyuv misah?* Surely that cannot be right either.

The *gemara* raises the case of *kippah*: *mi she-lakah ve-shanah beit din machnisim oto la-kippah* — one who is flogged and repeats his transgression is placed in the *kippah* and caused to die. But surely he is not formally *chayav misah* for the transgression that earned him lashes. The *gemara* pushes back: *Rachmana patrei ve-anan katlinan leh?*

The answer given is that of Rabbi Immi citing Reish Lakish: *be-malkut she-nitnah le-karet askinan* — we were discussing specifically lashings that are given for a sin that is *chayav karet*. The person is ultimately a *bar katla*, deserving of death at some level. That is exactly how the case of *kippah* was explained earlier. So too, the underlying assumption in all these cases is that there must be some form of *chiyuv misah* or *chiyuv karet* lurking beneath the surface.

## Death Framework

### Stolen Vessel

The *gemara* now conducts a rapid review of all the *Mishnah*'s cases against this framework. *Ve-ha-gonev et ha-kasvah* — what about stealing the *kli sharet*, the sacred vessel? Rav Yehudah said: *bi-kheli sharet askinan, u-remizah, velo yavo'u lirot ke-vala et ha-kodesh va-yamuto* — we connect it to the *pasuk* of "swallowing up the holy thing, and they shall die." There is a textual link to death even here.

### Cursing Kosem

*Ve-ha-mekalel et ha-kosem* — and what of cursing with the prognosticator, the *kosem*? This was not addressed when the *Mishnah* was first introduced, so the *gemara* addresses it now. *De-tani Rav Yosef: yakeh kotzam et kosmo* — Rav Yosef taught in a *baraita* that the *kotzam* should smite the *kosmo*, meaning God. *De-machzi ke-mevarekh et ha-Shem* — it resembles blasphemy. Thus, even if not formally *chayav misah*, the act is sufficiently proximate to blasphemy that some connection to the death penalty is implied.

### Karet Focus

*Ve-ha-boel aramit?* — and what of *boel aramit*, the case that opened the entire discussion? *Akriyuh le-Rav be-chelmeih ve-akriyuh li-kra* — the *pasuk* was read to Rav in his dream, and he remembered his tradition. Rashi, commenting on this passage, makes explicit what the *gemara* had only gestured toward earlier: *akriyuh le-Rav be-chelmeih — yikharet Hashem — amar: bar karet hu* — they read to Rav in his dream *yikharet Hashem*, and he said: this man is subject to *karet*. Whereas the *drasha* earlier had focused on the loss of status among scholars and the inability to bring offerings, the *rishonim* — and Rashi here — insist that the operative reading must emphasize the *karet* dimension of the verse. The principle of *kanayim pogim bo* cannot be coherently grounded without assuming some form of *chiyuv karet* or *chiyuv misah*.

### Chiyuv Mitah

The *gemara*'s overall posture, then, is this: it remains somewhat uncertain whether to read all these cases as requiring a clear *chiyuv misah* or as demonstrating that we can at times impose death even without it. But the consistent thrust of the *gemara*'s analysis has been to find, in each case, some textual or logical hook that ties the transgression to at least a heavenly death or *karet*. In the case of *boel aramit*, that hook is the word *yikharet* in the verse from Malachi. In the other cases, the connections vary — whether through *pesukim* about swallowing holy things and dying, or through proximity to blasphemy — but the pattern is consistent: the *gemara* refuses to endorse extrajudicial lethal action against someone unless there is, at some level, a basis for regarding him as deserving of death.
