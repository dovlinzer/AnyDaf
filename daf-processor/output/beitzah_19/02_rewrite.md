# Beitzah 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tevilat Keilim Erev YT

### Bein Hashmashot Issue

The daf begins on the second line of 19b. A *baraita* is introduced: *keli she-nitma b'erev Yom Tov ein matbilin oso bein hashmashos* — a vessel that became *tamei* on *erev Yom Tov* may not be immersed during *bein hashmashos*. The preceding discussion had established that *tevilat keilim* is prohibited on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, and had explored various technical distinctions: whether the vessel became *tamei* on *Yom Tov* or before it, and whether the immersion is being performed for the sake of the water (which is permitted) or for the sake of the vessel itself (which is not). The present *baraita* addresses a case of *safek*: since the vessel became *tamei* on *erev Yom Tov*, one cannot immerse it during *bein hashmashos*, because it may already be *Yom Tov*, making the immersion an *issur d'Rabbanan*.

### R. Shimon Shezuri

*Rabbi Shimon Shezuri omer: af be-chol ein matbilin oso mipnei she-tzarich ha'erev shemesh* — Rabbi Shimon Shezuri goes further and says that even on a weekday one may not immerse a vessel during *bein hashmashos*, because the vessel requires *ha'erev shemesh* — the onset of nightfall — to attain full *taharah*. Just as a person who immerses in a *mikvah* achieves complete *taharah* only at nightfall (a distinction relevant for eating *terumah* and *kodshim*, though not practically for a woman who is a *niddah*), so too a vessel requires *ha'erev shemesh*.

The concern, as Rashi explains, is that someone immersing a vessel at *bein hashmashos* is evidently planning to use it that very night. Since he dunked the vessel precisely at *bein hashmashos*, it is possible that nightfall had already arrived, meaning he may never actually have had *ha'erev shemesh*. One might ask: the same concern should apply to someone immersing at night, who also clearly wants to use the vessel that evening. But *bein hashmashos* is particularly dangerous, because the person can convince himself that he got the immersion in before nightfall and that *ha'erev shemesh* therefore applies, when in fact it may not. Those who require *ha'erev shemesh* are admittedly a limited population — people eating *terumah* or *kodshim* — and one might think such people know the law. But the concern is not ignorance so much as self-persuasion: convincing oneself that the timing worked out when it may not have.

### Tanna Kamma Query

The *Gemara* presses: *u'ma Tanna Kamma lo ba'ei ha'erev shemesh*? Does the first *tanna* not require *ha'erev shemesh*? That is, how does the *Tanna Kamma* address the concern Rabbi Shimon Shezuri raises about weekday immersions at *bein hashmashos*?

### Machshavto Nikares

Rava answers: *ashkachinhu l'Rabbanan d'bei Rav d'yasvi v'ka'amri* — he found the rabbinic students sitting and saying that the debate turns on the principle of *machshavto nikares mitoch ma'asav* — one's intention is visible through one's actions. The question is how much one can infer, from the way a person is behaving, that he is fully aware of the applicable *halacha*. The case: *kegon d'nakit mana b'yadeih ve-rahit ve-azil bein hashmashos l'atbolei* — he is holding a vessel and running to reach the *mikvah* at *bein hashmashos*. The act of running demonstrates some urgency. One view: *mada yada d'ba'ei ha'erev shemesh* — the fact that he is running shows he understands he must arrive before nightfall if he wants nightfall to count as *ha'erev shemesh*. Rashi notes that even if he arrives and realizes he has missed the ideal timing, he is already there with his vessel and will proceed to immerse; but the running itself shows he understands the law, and thus no *gezeirah* is needed.

### Intent & Knowledge

The opposing view holds: *machmas melacha hu d'karahit* — people run because they have work to do, not because they understand the significance of nightfall. The running proves nothing about his *halachic* awareness. The dispute is therefore whether one can reliably read a person's intent and knowledge from the manner in which he acts.

The *Gemara* clarifies that the parties do not disagree when the intent is unambiguous. Rather, *ki peligi* — where they disagree — is in a case where the person's level of knowledge is genuinely uncertain from his behavior alone.

### Pachot Mik'adasha

The specific case of uncertain knowledge: someone whose vessel became *tamei* through contact with less than a *k'adasha* — less than a lentil-size portion of a dead rodent — came to the rabbis to ask whether his vessel in fact contracted *tumah* from an amount smaller than the minimum. The answer is no, but he had another vessel that required immersion.

One view holds: *mi d'hai lo gamir* — someone who does not know the basic *halacha* of the minimum size for *tumah* clearly lacks knowledge of the laws of *tumah ve-taharah* generally, and therefore we may assume he also does not know the requirement of *ha'erev shemesh*. Accordingly, we do not permit such a person to immerse at *bein hashmashos*, lest he come to use the vessel before full *taharah* is achieved.

The opposing view holds: *ha hu d'lo gamir* — that particular law, the minimum size, he does not know, but that is because, as Rashi says, it is not written explicitly in the *Torah*. The requirement of *ha'erev shemesh*, by contrast, is written explicitly and is more commonly known. We do not impute ignorance of one law from ignorance of another.

This whole passage strikes one as curious. The *Gemara* appears to be discussing a policy of whether to permit immersion at *bein hashmashos*, yet it frames the question as an assessment of a specific individual's knowledge. If the person is standing right in front of the rabbis asking about *pachot mik'adasha*, why not simply inform him of the *halacha* of *ha'erev shemesh* directly? What does it mean to condition the policy on an assessment of whether his ignorance of one *halacha* implies ignorance of another? And even if the policy is to deny him access to the *mikvah* based on his apparent ignorance, how would he ever come to learn the rule at all? The phrase *machshavto nikares mitoch ma'asav* is worth noting independently, as it appears in other *halachic* contexts as well — for instance, in discussions of whether a minor can be presumed to possess *da'at* based on the evident thoughtfulness of his behavior.

## Mi-Gav L'Gav & Michiburah

### Mi-Gav L'Gav

The *Mishnah* had stated briefly that one may immerse vessels *mi-gav l'gav* and *mi-chaburah l'chaburah*. A *Tosefta* explains: *keitzad mi-gav l'gav* — what does "from back to back" mean? If someone had purified his vessels for use in his *gat* (olive press) and then decided to use them instead in his *bet ha-bad* (wine press), and wishes to re-immerse them before doing so — *gezunterheit*, let him. This is true even though there is no *halachic* requirement to do so: both contexts involve the same level of *taharah*, and the vessels are already *tahor*. The person has simply adopted a personal practice of immersing all vessels before any new use. Since the *tevilah* is entirely unnecessary, he may go ahead and perform it on *Yom Tov*.

### Michiburah L'Chiburah

*Keitzad mi-chaburah l'chaburah* — what does "from group to group" mean? Rashi specifies this refers to *Pesach* groups: *hayah ochel ba-chaburah zo ve-rotzeh le'echol ba-chaburah acheret* — someone was intending to eat with one group and then wished to change to another group. Since once one has actually begun eating with a group one may not transfer the *Pesach* to another *chaburah*, the *Mishnah* must be speaking of a case where he had merely intended to eat with one group and changed his mind before the *shechitah*. He now wishes to immerse his vessels anew before joining the second group — perhaps out of a practice of immersing vessels before joining a *Pesach* group. *Ha-reshut be-yado* — he may do so.

### Non-Halachic Tevilah

This leads to a striking observation. Every concern the *Gemara* raised earlier about *tevilah* on *Yom Tov* — that one might come to carry in a public domain, or that it looks like one is performing a *tikkun* — applies equally in these cases. The person is carrying his vessel to the *mikvah* for a purpose that serves no *halachic* function. Yet because this is not a *halachically* required *tevilah*, it is permitted. The same internal logic governs other distinctions in the preceding discussion: whether the vessel became *tamei* on *erev Yom Tov* or on *Yom Tov* itself, whether the source of *tumah* was an *av ha-tumah* or a *sheni le-tumah* — distinctions that map onto which immersions are permitted and which are not, even though the underlying concerns seem equally present in every case. The *Gemara* is operating with a formalistic principle: where the *tevilah* is technically not required, the concerns are set aside.

## Shelamim & Olot YT

### Beit S. vs. Beit H.

The *Gemara* now returns to a central *Yom Tov* debate of the *masechta*. *Beit Shammai omrim: mevi'im shelamim ve-ein somchin aleihem, aval lo olot* — Beit Shammai holds that *shelamim* (peace offerings) may be brought on *Yom Tov*, since their meat will be eaten and thus constitute a *tzorech Yom Tov*, but *semicha* — the act of pressing one's full weight on the animal — may not be performed, as it constitutes a form of using an animal on *Yom Tov*, which is rabbinically prohibited. Moreover, *olot* (burnt offerings) may not be brought at all, since no food benefit accrues to the offerer.

*U-Veit Hillel omrim: mevi'im shelamim ve-olot ve-somchin aleihem* — Beit Hillel holds that both *shelamim* and *olot* may be brought, and that *semicha* may be performed as well. The debate thus involves two questions: whether an *olah* — from which one derives no food — may be brought as a legitimate use of *melachah* on *Yom Tov*, and whether *semicha* is a prohibited use of an animal on *Yom Tov*.

This is worth placing in a broader context. This dispute is one of the earliest recorded debates in rabbinic history. The tractate *Chagigah* lists a chain of the *Nasi* and *Av Beit Din* going back centuries, all of whom debated the question of whether one performs *semicha* on *shelamim* brought on *Yom Tov*. It is somewhat puzzling that this particular question — which is, after all, a rabbinic concern — became the defining controversy across so many generations. It is also worth observing how much of *Masechet Beitzah* as a whole consists of Beit Shammai–Beit Hillel disputes, from *d'oraita* questions about carrying and bringing *korbanot*, to *d'Rabbanan* questions like the egg and *eruv tavshilin*. That a single *masechta* should be so thoroughly organized around this one set of disputants is itself remarkable and, as yet, not readily explained.

One further technical note: since *semicha* is not *me'akev* — it does not invalidate the offering if omitted — Beit Shammai's position that one may bring *shelamim* without *semicha* is coherent. The act can simply be performed on *erev Yom Tov* instead.

### Ulla's View

Ulla refines the scope of the dispute: *machloket be-shalmei chagigah lismoch ve-olat re'iyah le-hakriv* — the debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel concerns specifically *shalmei chagigah* (the festival peace offerings incumbent upon every pilgrim, based on the verse *va-chagosem oto chag la-Shem*, which obligates bringing *shelamim* as part of the festival celebration) and *olat re'iyah* (the burnt offering that fulfills the commandment *lo yeira'u fanai reikam*, that one shall not appear before God empty-handed).

According to Ulla, both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that *shalmei chagigah* may be brought on *Yom Tov* — it is a festival obligation and its meat is eaten, making the rationale for *Yom Tov* permission straightforward. Their dispute about *shelamim* is limited to the question of *semicha*. On *olot*, the dispute is whether *olat re'iyah* — a genuine *Yom Tov* obligation — may be brought on *Yom Tov* itself. Beit Hillel says yes; Beit Shammai says no, let it be deferred to the days following *Yom Tov*.

This also illuminates the position of Beit Shammai with respect to *Shavuot*, where there is no *Chol ha-Mo'ed*. According to Beit Shammai, one could never actually fulfill the obligation of *olat re'iyah* on *Shavuot* itself; it would always have to be made up in the days following, by an extension learned from the extra days of *Pesach* and *Sukkot*.

The underlying disagreement: Beit Shammai reads *va-chagosem oto chag la-Shem* as limiting permissible activity to the *chagigah* itself — *chagigah in, olat re'iyah lo*. Beit Hillel reads the phrase *la-Shem* expansively — *kol de-la-Shem*, any *korban* that is brought for God's honor as part of the *chag* may be brought. But with respect to *nedarim u-nedavot* — freewill offerings unrelated to the festival — *divrei ha-kol ein krevin be-Yom Tov*, all agree they may not be brought.

This conclusion about *nedarim u-nedavot* also connects to the earlier discussion in the *masechta* about the principle of *mitoch she-hutrah le-tzorech hutrah nami she-lo le-tzorech* — the rule that what is permitted for one purpose on *Yom Tov* may be permitted even for other purposes. The *Gemara* there concluded that one who brings an *olat nedavah* on *Yom Tov* would be liable to *malkut* according to Beit Shammai but not according to Beit Hillel. The present discussion, which says even Beit Hillel agrees *lechatchila* that unrelated offerings cannot be brought, is consistent: Beit Hillel's greater leniency, rooted in *mitoch*, means that *bedi'avad* one might not be penalized, but certainly *lechatchila* such offerings are not to be brought.

One might also ask why Beit Shammai, even without *mitoch*, should object to a *shelamim* that is not a *shalmei chagigah*, given that the offerer will eat its meat — seemingly no different from slaughtering an animal at home. The answer must be that the eating of a *shelamim* is understood as eating in the context of a *korban*: it is not primarily about satisfying hunger but about bringing an offering to God, with the eating as a secondary dimension. It is even somewhat ironic that Beit Shammai, who does not hold of *mitoch*, ends up not allowing *shelamim* that are not *shalmei chagigah*, when one might have thought that the *shelamim*'s eating component would make it the paradigmatic case of *tzorech Yom Tov*. But *shalmei simchah* — the peace offerings of rejoicing — which we will encounter shortly, bring the two elements together: the *shelamim* dimension and the *simchat ha-chag* dimension. Without that combination, even *shelamim* in isolation do not suffice.

### Braita Alternative

A *braita* is cited in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: *lo necheleku Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel al olah she-einah shel Yom Tov she-einah kreivah be-Yom Tov* — there was no dispute that an *olah* unrelated to *Yom Tov* may not be brought. And: *al shelamim she-hein shel Yom Tov she-kreivim be-Yom Tov* — there was also no dispute that *shelamim* that are festival obligations may be brought. The debate, says this *baraita*, was twofold: *al olah she-hi shel Yom Tov* — an *olah* that is a *Yom Tov* obligation (*olat re'iyah*), and *shelamim she-einam shel Yom Tov* — *shelamim* not connected to the festival at all. Beit Shammai says neither may be brought; Beit Hillel says both may.

This version diverges from Ulla's reading. On the *olah*, the two agree. But this *baraita* has Beit Hillel permitting even *shelamim* that are unrelated to the festival, because in the end the offerer will eat the meat — and it has Beit Shammai prohibiting even such *shelamim*. This is a direct contradiction to Ulla's claim that even Beit Hillel would concede that unrelated *shelamim* cannot be brought.

### Tannai Dispute

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve the contradiction by proposing an emendation of the *baraita*: *targima ve-eima hachi* — reinterpret the text. Under the revised reading, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar is made to say that there was no dispute that *olah* and *shelamim* unrelated to *Yom Tov* cannot be brought, and no dispute that *shelamim* related to *Yom Tov* can be brought. The sole subject of debate is *olat re'iyah*, precisely as Ulla had said. This resolution effectively cuts and pastes the *baraita* to align it with Ulla. The straightforward reading of the *baraita*, however, is that Beit Hillel would permit even unrelated *shelamim* — for the reason already stated, that one will eat the meat.

### Rav Yosef

Rav Yosef's response to the contradiction is blunter: *Tannai shakalat me'alma* — do not think there are no tannaim who disagree with Ulla. There are other tannaitic opinions in the world; finding one that contradicts Ulla merely establishes that it is a *tannaic* dispute. The *baraita* of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar represents one view; Ulla's reading reflects another. The matter is *tannai*.

### Shelamim YT

A further *baraita* is cited: *shlamim ha-ba'im machmat Yom Tov be-Yom Tov* — *shelamim* brought specifically for the sake of *Yom Tov*, that is, *shalmei chagigah* or *shalmei simchah* as we will see. *Beit Shammai omrim: somech aleihem me-erev Yom Tov ve-shochtan be-Yom Tov* — Beit Shammai holds that *semicha* must be performed on *erev Yom Tov*, since it cannot be done on *Yom Tov* itself, but the actual slaughter occurs on *Yom Tov*. *U-Veit Hillel omrim: somech aleihem be-Yom Tov ve-shochtan be-Yom Tov* — Beit Hillel holds that *semicha* may be performed on *Yom Tov* as well. So what is debated is an *Yom Tov*-related *shelamim*; the question is only whether *semicha* is permitted.

### Nedarim V'Nedavot

The *baraita* continues: *aval nedarim u-nedavot divrei ha-kol ein krevin be-Yom Tov* — but freewill sacrifices unrelated to *Yom Tov*, all agree may not be brought. This supports Ulla's position. Both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that *shelamim* that carry no connection to the *chag* — even though their meat will be eaten — may not be brought on *Yom Tov lechatchila*. As noted, even if Beit Hillel's *mitoch* principle might provide a *bedi'avad* defense, it does not license *lechatchila* conduct.

The *baraita* then moves to a seemingly unrelated case: *ein mevi'in toda be-Chag ha-Matzot mipnei chametz she-bah* — a *toda* (thanksgiving offering), which is a type of *shelamim* requiring both *matzah* and *chametz* loaves, may not be brought on *Pesach* because its *chametz* component cannot be presented. And *ve-lo be-Atzeret mipnei she-hu Yom Tov* — nor may it be brought on *Shavuot*, because it is *Yom Tov*. This establishes that a *toda*, which is a *shelamim* not connected to the festival, may not be brought on *Yom Tov*. *Aval mevi adam todato be-Chag ha-Sukkot* — but it may be brought during *Sukkot*.

When it says *ein mevi'in toda be-Chag ha-Matzot*, one might think this is obvious since the *chametz* loaves cannot be presented on *Pesach*. In fact, the *Gemara* clarifies that the ruling extends even to the fourteenth of Nisan — *erev Pesach* — based on the principle *ein mevi'im kodashim l'veit ha-pasul*: one does not bring sacrifices into a situation where they are likely to become invalid. If one brings a *toda* in the morning of the fourteenth, there is barely enough time to eat all the *chametz* loaves before midday, risking that they will become *notar* and require burning. This is also the reason we do not recite *Mizmor le-Todah* on *erev Pesach*.

## Toda & Bal Te'acher

### Toda Fulfillment

Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon introduces a further distinction: *mevi adam todato be-Chag ha-Sukkot ve-yotzei bo yedei chovato mishum simchah ve-ein yotzei bo mishum chagigah* — a person may bring his *toda* on *Sukkot* and thereby fulfill his obligation of *simchah*, but he does not thereby fulfill his obligation of *chagigah*. This requires some unpacking.

There are two distinct categories of festival *shelamim* incumbent on a pilgrim. One is *shalmei chagigah*, grounded in the verse *va-chagosem oto chag* — the obligation to bring *shelamim* as an expression of the *chag* itself. The other is *shalmei simchah*, grounded in *ve-samachta atah u-veitecha* — the obligation of *simchat Yom Tov*, rejoicing on the festival. While *shalmei chagigah* focuses on the act of bringing the *korban*, *shalmei simchah* is primarily about eating meat that was brought as a *korban* in the context of God's presence. The distinction is significant: it is specifically eating the meat of a *korban* that constitutes the fulfillment, not merely eating meat. This is why *Chazal* teach that in the absence of the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, *simchah* for men is expressed through wine — the eating of *korban* meat being no longer available. (The *Rambam*'s *girsa* adds meat alongside wine, but our *girsa* in the *Gemara* mentions only wine.)

Now, if someone had already accepted an obligation to bring a *toda* — perhaps due to a dangerous journey from which he was saved — and he brings it on the *regel*, may he count that as his *shalmei simchah* as well? Yes: since *shalmei simchah* is fundamentally about eating meat that has been brought as a *shelamim* in a *kodesh* context, there is no reason one cannot eat the *toda* meat and simultaneously fulfill *simchah*. But can he use his *toda* to fulfill *shalmei chagigah*? No. *Shalmei chagigah* is a *davar she-be-chovah* — an independent obligation — and *ve-chol davar she-be-chovah eino ba ela min ha-chulin*: an obligatory offering can only be fulfilled by something not already dedicated to satisfying a prior obligation. One cannot discharge one obligation through another preexisting one.

When the *Gemara* asks whether this ruling about *chagigah* is so obvious as to require no statement, it answers: *lo tzericha* — yes it does, because it comes to tell us that even if one *explicitly stipulated* at the time of accepting the *toda* that he intends it also to satisfy his *chagigah* obligation, it still cannot. And this is what leads into the discussion of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan.

### Abaye's Frame

At this point, Rav Zeira raises an objection to Rabbi Shimon's position — in Tosafot's reading of the text, where Rabbi Shimon says that if you cannot bring the *toda* on *Pesach* and *Shavuot*, you cannot bring it on *Sukkot* either. *Ha-shta shulusei mesaltinan* — we allow gathering wood from forests on *Chol ha-Mo'ed*, a form of labor with considerable latitude. *Ve-nedarim u-nedavot mibaya* — and you mean to tell me one cannot bring a *toda* on *Chol ha-Mo'ed Sukkot*?

Abaye resolves this: *be-hakravah kulei alma lo pligi de-sharei* — regarding the actual bringing of the *toda* on *Chol ha-Mo'ed Sukkot*, nobody disputes that it is permitted. The *baraita* was never discussing that. *Ki pligi le-makam aleih be-val te'acher* — the entire dispute in this *baraita* concerns the prohibition of *bal te'acher*, the *Torah* prohibition against delaying the fulfillment of one's vows: *ki tidor neder la-Shem Elokecha lo te'acher le-shallemo*.

Abaye's framing is not merely an *ukimta* for this one line; Rashi emphasizes that Abaye is explaining the fundamental subject matter of the entire *baraita*. Ask yourself: what was this *baraita* really about? The answer, says Abaye, is *bal te'acher*. *Chazal* understood the prohibition as linked to the *regalim* — the pilgrimages — because the three *regalim* represent one's greatest opportunities to present himself at the *Beit ha-Mikdash* and discharge his obligations. The question is: what constitutes the cycle? How many *regalim* must pass before one transgresses *bal te'acher*?

### Tanna Kamma

The *Tanna Kamma* holds: *shalosh regalim amar Rachmana afilu shelo kesidran* — the *Torah* requires three *regalim*, and the order does not matter. Once three *regalim* have passed — regardless of which *regel* one started from — without one having brought his *korban*, he is in violation. In the case of a *toda*, which cannot be brought on *Pesach* (due to *chametz*) or on *Shavuot* (because it is *Yom Tov*), only *Sukkot* provides an actual opportunity. Therefore: *Sukkot* is the one *regel* where one must bring it, and if *Sukkot* passes without the offering being brought, *bal te'acher* has been violated.

### R. Shimon

Rabbi Shimon holds: *kesidran in, shelo kesidran lo* — only the sequence matters. One is not in violation unless the three *regalim* have passed *ke-sidran*, in order, beginning with *Pesach*. Using Tosafot's *girsa* of the *baraita*: *kol she-ba be-Chag ha-Matzot u-ve-Chag ha-Shavuot ba be-Chag ha-Sukkot* — any offering that can be brought on *Pesach* and *Shavuot* must then be brought on *Sukkot* or one is in violation. But if the obligation did not yet exist on *Pesach* and *Shavuot* — if one's vow was taken after those occasions — then *Sukkot* is not yet the deadline; another full cycle remains. Rashi reads the language this way: if you were already obligated on *Pesach* and had the opportunity on *Shavuot*, then *Sukkot* is your last chance. But if you were not yet obligated at those earlier times, you will have another opportunity.

The language is admittedly awkward to map cleanly onto the *baraita*'s words, but the principle is clear: *kesidran* is required, and *shelo kesidran* does not trigger *bal te'acher*.

### R. Elazar b. Shimon

Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon offers yet a third position, and a separate *baraita* is cited in his name to clarify it: *lomar she-zeh acharon* — the reason the *Torah* mentioned *Sukkot* again at the end of its list is to say that *Sukkot* is the final one, the concluding deadline. This aligns with Rabbi Shimon's view that order matters and *Sukkot* closes the cycle. But Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon goes further: *lomar she-zeh gorem* — *Sukkot* alone is the cause, the decisive point. *Sukkot* is the universal deadline for all outstanding obligations, regardless of when the vow was made. If one made a *neder* the day before *Sukkot*, he must bring it on *Sukkot* or be in violation. If he made it the day after *Sukkot*, he has until the following *Sukkot*. The deadline is *Sukkot*, period, independent of the cycle of the other *regalim*.

This position may evoke the dual calendrical framework in the *Torah*: alongside the calendar that begins with *ha-chodesh ha-zeh lachem* — where *Pesach* is the starting point of the *regalim* — there is also the calendar in which *Sukkot* is *tekufat ha-shanah* or *be-tzet ha-shanah*, the close of the year, associated with *Rosh Hashanah* and the end of the annual cycle. Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon draws on this notion: *Sukkot* is always the year's terminus for *korbanot*.

The *Gemara* then connects this back to our question of *shelamim* on *Yom Tov*. Since Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon says one must bring one's *toda* on *Sukkot*, and since *Chol ha-Mo'ed Sukkot* would be no different from the *Tanna Kamma*'s position (about which there is no dispute), his statement must mean something more — namely, that one can bring it even on *Yom Tov* of *Sukkot* itself. This would make him the tannaitic source for the view that *nedarim u-nedavot* — unrelated *shelamim* — may be brought even on *Yom Tov*, in contrast to the position that all agree they may not. The *Gemara* acknowledges that this reading is somewhat forced: Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon was primarily discussing *bal te'acher*, not *Yom Tov* policy. But because the *Gemara* has been reading the entire *baraita* through the lens of Abaye's *bal te'acher* framework, it draws Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon into the earlier dispute as well.

To summarize the landscape: the *olah* unrelated to *Yom Tov* cannot be brought, and even Beit Hillel agrees *lechatchila*. The *olat re'iyah* is debated between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. *Shalmei chagigah* and *shalmei simchah* everyone agrees can be brought; the debate is only about *semicha*. The key question is whether *shelamim* not related to *Yom Tov* — represented paradigmatically by the *toda* — can be brought on *Yom Tov*. According to Ulla (and the *baraita* that supports him), everyone agrees they may not. According to the straightforward reading of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's *baraita*, Beit Hillel would permit them. And according to the *Gemara*'s reading of Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon's position, he is the *tanna* who holds they can be brought even on *Yom Tov* itself.

## Tenai & Ma'aseh Order

### Toda L'Chagigah

The *Gemara* returns to the question raised by Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish to Rabbi Yochanan: *ha-omer harei alai toda ve-etzei bah yedei chagigah* — someone declares, "I accept the obligation of a *toda*, and I intend to use it to fulfill my *chagigah* obligation." Or: *hareini nazir ve-agaleach demi ma'aser sheni* — I am a *nazir*, and when I bring the *korbanot* of *nezirut*, I will use my *ma'aser sheni* funds for them. In both cases, the person stipulates at the moment of accepting his vow that he will simultaneously discharge another obligation. What is the *halacha*?

Rabbi Yochanan responded: *neder ve-eino yotzei* — the vow takes effect, but the *toda* cannot be used to fulfill the *chagigah*; *nazir ve-eino megaleach* — he is a *nazir*, but he cannot use *ma'aser sheni* money for his *korbanot*. The *Gemara* notes that this ruling is not merely about *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* — stipulating against a *Torah* rule — though that is one way to frame the problem. Rashi's explanation goes to the order of declaration: the moment one says *harei alai toda* or *hareini nazir*, that declaration takes immediate effect. The *al menat* that follows comes too late; the obligation is already binding. If so, the logic of the ruling is not about substance but sequence.

### Neder & Tenai

If the problem is the order of the statements — the obligating declaration preceded the condition — then reversing the order might produce a different result. This is the key principle of *tenai kodem le-ma'aseh*: the condition must precede the act to be operative. A condition stated after the binding act cannot retroactively limit or redirect that act.

### Schiv Mera

This principle is illustrated through the story of a *shechiv mera* — a person on his deathbed — whose declarations carry the special legal status of *matnat shechiv mera*, a deathbed gift that takes effect immediately (so as not to agitate the dying person). The case: *hahu gavra de-amar lehu* — a certain man said to those gathered around him, *havu lei arba me'ah zuzie le-ploni ve-linsov barti* — give 400 *zuz* to a certain person, and let him marry my daughter.

Rav Pappa ruled: *arba me'ah shakil* — he takes the 400 *zuz*; *u-varti ei ba'ei nasiv ve-ei ba'ei lo nasiv* — but with respect to the daughter, he may marry her if he wishes and need not if he does not. The two statements are independent: the 400 *zuz* was stated first and became binding as soon as it was uttered; the subsequent mention of the marriage cannot retroactively make the money conditional upon it.

But if the man had said instead, *linsav u-havu lei* — let him marry my daughter and then give him 400 *zuz* — then *ei nasiv shakil ve-ei lo nasiv lo shakil*: if he marries her he gets the money; if he does not, he does not. There the condition of marriage was stated first, before the grant of money, and so the money was understood from the outset as conditional upon the marriage.

### Mareimar & Ravina

Mareimar was teaching this ruling in his own name — *mi-shamei de-nafshei* — describing the principle about the *toda* and the *chagigah* obligation. Ravina said to Mareimar: *atun hachi matnitu lah* — you teach it as an anonymous ruling. *Anan ke-di ba'ei minei Reish Lakish me-Rabbi Yochanan matninan lah* — we have this teaching with attribution, as a question that Reish Lakish posed to Rabbi Yochanan.

### Tosafot Synthesis

Tosafot draws the natural conclusion: if the order of declaration determines the outcome, then reversing it should be effective. Specifically, someone who says *al menat she-etzei bo yedei chagigah, harei alai toda* — stipulating the condition *before* accepting the *toda* obligation — should indeed be able to use the *toda* to fulfill his *chagigah*. The condition preceded the act, so the act was accepted from its inception in the context of the condition.

Tosafot acknowledges that this case is not quite the same as a typical *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah*, where the problem is that the stipulation contradicts the substance of a *Torah* rule. Here, the question is purely about sequencing. The broader principle is the *halachic* framework of *tenai kodem le-ma'aseh* — the condition must precede the deed — which applies throughout *hilchot tena'im*. Where the sequence is respected, the condition can shape the legal effect of the act; where it is not, the act takes hold unconditionally and the subsequent condition is inoperative.
