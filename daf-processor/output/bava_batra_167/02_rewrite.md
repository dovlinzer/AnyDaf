# Bava Batra 167 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Signature Display Rules

### Displaying Signature Loc…

The daf opens with a teaching of Abaye concerning the proper way to display one's signature to a *beit din*. The practical need for this arises from a scenario the Gemara describes: Reuven and Shimon have both signed a *shtar*, but Shimon has since died and no one in the world can identify Shimon's signature except Reuven and one other person. Since Reuven cannot both testify to Shimon's signature and validate his own — for that would mean three-quarters of the *shtar* is being validated through Reuven's testimony alone — a workaround is required. Reuven and the other witness testify to Shimon's signature, while Reuven contrives to let the *beit din* observe his own handwriting directly, without formal testimony. He enters the courtroom, begins practicing his signature, "accidentally" drops the paper, and walks away. The *beit din* then examines the writing on its own initiative, gaining firsthand familiarity with his signature without any testimonial act on his part.

This illustrates why one might wish to place one's signature on record before *beit din* in advance. Abaye's rule addresses how this should be done: *hai man deba'ei lemachvei chasimus yadei beveit dina, lilechei besof megilta* — whoever wishes to display his signature to *beit din* should sign at the top of a piece of parchment, not at the bottom.

### Blank Document Risk

The reason is straightforward and sobering. If one signs at the bottom of a blank piece of parchment, a finder could write above the signature whatever he wishes — a loan document, a sale, an IOU — and the signature would appear to authenticate the fabricated text. The Gemara reinforces this concern by invoking an established rule: *hotzi alav kesav yado shehu chayav lo, gova minechasim bnei chorin* — if someone produces another person's handwritten note acknowledging a debt, it may be used to collect from that person's unencumbered assets. The principle underlying this is *hoda'as ba'al din keme'ah edim dami*: a person's own admission is as effective as the testimony of a hundred witnesses. The note does not create a lien against property sold to third parties — it is, after all, only the debtor's own self-testimony and lacks the public notice of a witnessed document — but it does allow collection from freely available assets. Even if only the signature is in the debtor's hand and the body of the text was written by someone else, the signature is sufficient. This makes a signed blank page a genuinely dangerous instrument, and Abaye's rule is designed to prevent it.

### Tax Collector Story

The Gemara illustrates this with a memorable story. A certain *buzbina* — a tax collector — came before Abaye and made what sounded like a generous offer: he wished to see Abaye's signature so that when Torah scholars presented a note bearing Abaye's name, he would recognize it and exempt them from the tax. He handed Abaye a piece of parchment. Abaye began to sign at the top — leaving no blank space above — whereupon the tax collector began pulling the document, trying to maneuver Abaye into signing somewhere in the middle, where there would be blank space above the signature. Abaye recognized the trick immediately and said to him: *kvar kadmucha rabanan* — the Rabbis already anticipated you. The very rule that Abaye himself had taught had now saved him from being manipulated by the man asking him to apply it.

## Document Protection Rules

### Numbers at Line Ends

Abaye offers a second protective rule: *mitlat va-ad asara lo lichtov besof shita* — one should not write the numbers three through ten at the end of a line in a legal document. The concern is forgery by addition.

### Forgery via Yud Addition

The mechanism is elegant and troubling. In Aramaic, the numbers three through ten can each be multiplied by ten through the simple addition of a *yud* and a *nun* (or in some forms a *yud* and a *mem*): *tlat* becomes *tlatin* (thirty), *arba* becomes *arba'im* (forty), *chamesh* becomes *chamishim* (fifty), *shesh* becomes *shishim* (sixty), *sheva* becomes *shiv'im* (seventy), *tesha* becomes *tish'im* (ninety), *eser* becomes *esrim* (twenty). If the numeral falls at the end of a line, a forger can squeeze in a *yud-nun* in the margin or overhang — far less visibly intrusive than inserting an entirely new word like *me'ot* (hundreds), which would obviously stand out. One should note that *shmoneh* (eight) is apparently excluded from this concern, possibly because its terminal *hei* cannot be transformed into a *yud-nun* in the same way. Similarly, *echad* and *shnayim* cannot be inflated to *achad asar* or *esrim* by such a simple addition, so they pose no such risk. The range of three through ten is therefore the zone of vulnerability.

### Repetition Remedy

What if, despite best efforts, the number has already been written at the end of a line and nothing can be done about it? The Gemara prescribes a remedy: *mehadar lei lediburei t'rei vetlata zimni* — write the amount again two or three times later in the document. The principle is that we always follow the final repetition, analogous to how we write both numerals and words on a modern check, supplemented by a drawn line. The rationale is that among multiple repetitions, at least one will fall in the middle of a line, making forgery by marginal insertion impossible and thereby anchoring the authentic amount.

### Orchard Erasure Case

The Gemara now presents two case studies in forgery detection. In the first, a *shtar* recorded the sale of a *tilta b'pardisa* — a third of an orchard. The fraudster took the *bet* of *bi-pardisa* and erased both its roof (the horizontal top stroke) and its foot (the bottom stroke), leaving only a vertical line — transforming *bi-pardisa* into *u-pardisa*, "and the orchard." The document now appeared to convey not merely a third of one orchard but a third of some property *and* the entire orchard — a far more valuable transaction.

When the case came before Abaye, he examined the document and challenged the claimant: *mai ta'ama revach lei alma l'hai vav?* — why does this *vav* have so much room to breathe? Why is there such conspicuous space between the *vav* and the rest of the word *pardisa*? Had the *bet* originally stood there, the remaining vertical stroke would naturally be set apart from what followed, whereas a genuine *vav* would sit flush within the word. The physical spacing was telltale. Abaye had the claimant bound and administered lashes, and he admitted the forgery.

The Gemara leaves an important question unresolved, and it is worth pausing on it. What if the claimant had not admitted? Could Abaye — or any *dayan* — dismiss the *shtar* based solely on this visual anomaly? After all, a scribe might occasionally leave extra space inadvertently, and spacing alone is not conclusive proof. The Rambam, in *Hilchot Malveh v'Loveh* (27:12), addresses the obligation of careful scrutiny: *u'mitikun hashtarot l'hitbonen beshtar bevavin vezayinim shelo yihyu dechukim bein hatevot shema zayif vehosif zo* — it is a corrective institution of *shtar* law that one must carefully examine every *vav* and *zayin* in the document to ensure they are not squeezed between words, lest a forgery added them; *v'lo yihyu meruchakim*, and one must equally check that no letter is suspiciously separated from its neighbors, lest someone erased part of a letter — for example a *hei* or a *chet* — and left only one leg in the place of a *vav*. The Rambam's formulation makes clear that this scrutiny is mandatory, but it does not fully answer the threshold question: how much suspicion is sufficient for a *dayan* to dismiss a *shtar* outright? This remains a genuine gap in the analysis.

### Three Brothers Case

The second case involves a different kind of addition. A *shtar* read *manat Reuven v'Shimon achei* — the portion belonging to Reuven and Shimon, the brothers — which had been sold to a third party. Reuven and Shimon, it transpired, had a third brother named Acha. Reuven and Shimon jointly owned one field; all three brothers together owned a different, presumably more valuable field. The fraudster added a *vav* to the word *achei* — "brother" — transforming it into *v'Achi*, "and Acha," so that the *shtar* now read as if Reuven, Shimon, *and* their brother Acha had all sold together, which would convey the more valuable jointly-held field.

When this came before Abaye, he asked the same diagnostic question: *mai ta'ama dachik lei alma l'hai vav?* — why is this *vav* so crowded? Why is it squeezed so tightly against the surrounding letters? The compression was the giveaway. The man admitted after being bound. And here too the same open question stands: without a forced admission, what authority does the *dayan* have to act on visual suspicion alone, and what is the required threshold of proof?

## Forgery Detection

### Rambam's Scrutiny Rules

The Rambam's formulation quoted above — requiring the *dayan* to examine spacing both for suspicious compression and suspicious separation — captures the principle at work in both cases just described. The two anomalies are inverses of one another: a letter squeezed in (*dachik*) indicates an insertion; a letter standing conspicuously apart (*merubach*) indicates an erasure of the surrounding material. The Rambam instructs that this analysis must extend to the entire document and every form of writing. What the Gemara does not spell out, and what the Rambam likewise leaves implicit, is the operative standard of proof that translates a *dayan*'s suspicion into a definitive ruling — particularly when the party against whom the *shtar* is being used affirmatively claims forgery and the visual evidence is suggestive but not conclusive.

### Rava's Signature Case

A striking Gemara follows. A certain *shtar* bore the signatures of Rava and Rav Acha bar Adda. When it came before Rava in his capacity as *dayan* — itself an interesting question touching on the principle of *ed na'aseh dayan*, though the Gemara does not pursue it — Rava examined his own signature and said: *dein chatimat yada didi* — yes, this is my signature. *Mihu, kamei d'Rav Acha bar Adda lo chatmi li me'olam* — but I have never co-signed a *shtar* together with Rav Acha bar Adda. One possible reading of *kamei* ("before" or "in the presence of") suggests Rava's statement was more precise: he never signed a document prior to Rav Acha bar Adda — meaning he always signed only after his co-signatory, never first. This reading helpfully explains how Rava could be so confident: he would know from practice that he had never affixed his name to a document alongside this particular colleague.

The forger was bound and admitted the deed. Rava then turned to him with a sharp practical question: *bishlama didi zayafta* — perhaps I can understand that you forged my signature, if you are skilled enough. *Ela d'Rav Acha bar Adda* — but how did you replicate the signature of Rav Acha bar Adda, whose hand trembles with age or illness? The forger explained: *anchi yadai amitzra* — he placed his hand on a rope, like a suspension bridge, and as people walked across and the rope swayed and jiggled, he signed in that shaking motion. An alternate version has him suspended from a well-rope, swinging freely as he wrote. People will go to extraordinary lengths to produce a convincing forgery.

This prompts an important discussion. Once a *shtar* has been *mekuyam* — validated by *beit din* — does the signatory himself retain any *ne'emanus* to contest it? It seems clear that once witnesses have validated his signature and *beit din* has confirmed the document, the signatory's own subsequent claim that the signature is not his cannot simply override the validation. The principle of *keivan shehigid shuv eino chozer umagid* — once a witness has testified, he cannot retract — is relevant here, though the precise application is not fully worked out by the Gemara. What if independent witnesses later came forward claiming to have seen the forgery? That would function more like a *psul* external to the testimony itself, somewhat analogous to the process of *hazamah*, where witnesses are disqualified by a showing that they were elsewhere. These questions are raised but remain open.

## Documents Without Parties

### Get Without Wife

The Gemara transitions to a new Mishnah: *kotvin get l'ish af al pi she'ein ishto imo* — a *get* may be written for a husband even though his wife is not present. Since *get* is a unilateral act by the husband, the wife's agreement is not required; he will hold the *get* and present it to her when he is ready to divorce her. Of course, he must still authorize the writing himself.

### Tosafot on Dating Risk

Tosafot raises a difficulty: if the *get* is written now but delivered months later, it will appear that the divorce took effect at the earlier date. This creates at minimum the problem of protecting *lakuchos* — third-party purchasers — who may have acquired property from the husband after the date written in the *get* but before its actual delivery. If it appears the wife was divorced earlier, she might be deemed to have had usufruct rights (*peiros*) during that intervening period, prejudicing buyers who relied on the husband's ownership.

The *Ri* in Tosafot answers: *lo makish inish pur'anusa l'nafshei* — a person does not invite misfortune upon himself. Having a *get* written is considered a bad omen; no one does it in advance of genuine intent to divorce imminently. We therefore assume that whenever a *get* is written, it will be given very shortly thereafter. If there is specific evidence to the contrary — for instance, a *get* found on the floor under suspicious circumstances — suspicion may arise. But as a baseline assumption, we are not concerned. Tosafot further notes that for *gittin* transmitted from abroad (*gittei habaim mimedinas hayam*), the unavoidable delay is publicly known — the entire process of overseas transmission through *beit din* is notorious — so the apparent pre-dating creates no practical problem: *kala is lehu*, their circumstances are well known.

### Receipt Without Parties

The Mishnah continues: *v'hashover l'isha* — one may also write a *shover* (receipt for *ketubah* payment) for the woman even though her husband is not present. After a husband pays his wife's *ketubah*, the wife may either return the *ketubah* document itself or write a receipt acknowledging payment. If she comes to the scribes before payment and asks that a receipt be prepared in advance — to be handed over upon actual payment — the scribes may comply. The reasoning: the only person who might be hurt by a premature or fraudulent use of this receipt is the woman herself. If she gives the receipt before receiving payment, she loses her claim; no one else is injured. The Mishnah also specifies: *uvilvad sheyehu makirin* — provided the scribes recognize the relevant parties. Who precisely must be recognized, and in which direction, becomes the subject of Gemara discussion below.

As for scribal fees: *v'haba'al noten sachar* — the husband pays. This is consistent with the general principle that the beneficiary of the transaction pays. The husband benefits from the receipt because it extinguishes his *ketubah* obligation; that he pays is clear. Why he also pays the scribal fees for the *get* itself, given that the beneficiary of the *get* is less obvious, is left for the Gemara to work through.

### Debt Document Rules

The Mishnah proceeds: *kotvin shtar l'loveh af al pi she'ein malveh imo* — scribes may write a loan document for the borrower even without the lender present, since the borrower is only incriminating himself and the lender has no reason to object. Conversely, *v'ein kotvin l'malveh ad sheyehe loveh imo* — they may not write for the lender without the borrower present, since the lender could be fabricating a debt that the borrower never authorized. The borrower pays the fees: *v'haloveh noten sachar* — not because the borrower benefits from the *shtar* (it works against him), but because he benefits from the underlying transaction. He received money without paying interest; his is the financial gain.

### Sale Document Rules

The same logic extends to sales: *kotvin shtar l'mocher af al pi she'ein loke'ach imo* — a sale document may be written for the seller without the buyer's presence. If the seller wishes to have a document prepared stating that he sold his property to a named buyer, there is no harm; the buyer's interests are not prejudiced. But *v'ein kotvin l'loke'ach ad sheyehe mocher imo* — one may not write the document for the buyer without the seller, since the seller might deny the sale entirely and the document would be fraudulent. The buyer pays the fees: *v'haloke'ach noten sachar* — he is the transactional beneficiary. It might seem as though the seller benefits by receiving money, but it is the buyer who acquires property and for whom the documentary record provides protection.

### Two-Party Documents

Certain documents, however, are bilateral by nature, and neither party can initiate without the other. *Ein kotvin shtar eirusin v'nisuin ela mida'at shneihem* — a betrothal agreement (*tenai'm*) or marriage contract (*ketubah*) may be written only with the knowledge and presence of both parties, since both sides undertake obligations and both benefit. Similarly, *ein kotvin shtar arisut v'kablanut ela mida'at shneihem* — a sharecropping agreement (*arisut*) or a work contract (*kablanut*) requires both parties. A contract that specifies mutual obligations — payment on one side, labor on the other — cannot be prepared at the initiative of either party alone. The *m'kabel* (the one taking on the job) pays the fees in this case, being viewed as the more needy party who gains the employment opportunity. Likewise, *ein kotvin shitrei beirurin v'chol maaseh beit din ela midaat shneihem* — documents concerning the selection of judges or court proceedings that affect both litigants require the consent of both, and in that case *ush'neihem notnim sachar*, both parties share the cost.

### Two Copies Rule

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds a practical observation about these two-party documents: *lishneihem kotvin shenayim, l'zeh l'atzmo ul'zeh l'atzmo* — for documents that affect both sides, two copies are prepared, one for each party. Since each side has obligations and rights recorded in the document, each side naturally needs its own copy. This point, while apparently obvious, is significant enough that it required explicit statement — since the earlier discussion of one-party documents did not address which party retains the single copy.

## Party Recognition Issue

### Recognition Initial Rule

The Gemara now explores the Mishnah's requirement that scribes recognize the parties. Rav Yehuda cites Rav: *uv'vada d'makir shem ha'ish b'get v'shem ha'isha b'shovar* — in the case of the *get*, the scribes need only recognize the man's name; in the case of the receipt (*shover*), they need only recognize the woman's name. The reasoning is intuitive. When a man comes to request a *get* for his wife, the scribes know him — that is sufficient. If he names his wife as Sarah, they will write that name. If he has no wife by that name, the document will be useless. What harm could come from not knowing the wife? Similarly, when a woman requests a receipt for her *ketubah*, the scribes recognize her; her husband's identity is not their concern, since the document only affects her and her husband.

### Identical Names Problem

Three Amoraim — Rav Safra, Rav Acha bar Rav Huna, and Rav Huna bar Chinana — sat together with Abaye present and raised an objection. Focusing on the *get*: *v'lichosh, dilma katav gita?* — why are we not concerned that the following fraud could occur? A man — not Reuven Jacobson, but someone else entirely — approaches the scribes and asks them to write a *get* naming his wife as Sarah. The scribes recognize this man as Reuven Jacobson and write accordingly. He then delivers this *get* not to his own wife, but to the wife of another Reuven Jacobson in the same town — a woman who also happens to be named Sarah. She now appears divorced when she is not, and may go on to collect her *ketubah* under false pretenses or worse. The parallel concern applies to the *shover*: a woman gives the scribes a false husband's name, the receipt is prepared, and it is delivered to a different man who shares that name, fraudulently extinguishing a real *ketubah* obligation.

### Both Names Required

Abaye responded that Rav had in fact said the opposite of what was initially quoted in his name: *shem ha'ish b'get vehu hadin leshem ha'isha* — both the husband's name and the wife's name must be recognized by the scribes when writing a *get*; *shem ha'isha b'shovar vehu hadin leshem ha'ish* — and both names must be recognized for the *shover* as well. The initial one-sided formulation was a misattribution or simplification. Recognition of both parties is required.

### Same City Same Names

This, however, does not fully solve the problem. The Gemara now raises the scenario of *shnei Yosef ben Shimon hadarim be'ir achas* — two couples in the same city where both the husbands and wives share identical names. Even if the scribes recognize that the man before them is indeed named Yosef ben Shimon and that his wife is indeed named Miriam, there may be another couple in the same city with precisely those names. A *get* written for one couple could be handed to the other.

It is worth noting a subtle but telling observation: the Gemara frames this problem entirely in terms of the husband's name — *shnei Yosef ben Shimon* — without mentioning the wives' shared names as part of the problem's description, even though the fraud being contemplated necessarily depends on the wives' names also matching. Tosafot raises a broader question at this point: perhaps a *get* or *shtar* is intrinsically *pasul* whenever its text could equally describe two different parties — regardless of any fraudulent intent — since it is ambiguous on its face. That question is bracketed by the Gemara here, which focuses on the practical concern of misuse rather than inherent invalidity.

### Both Couples Present Rule

Rav Acha bar Rav Huna cites Rav's ruling: *ein megarshin neshoseihen ela ze bifnei ze* — in a city where two couples share identical names, neither husband may divorce his wife except in the presence of the other couple. The logic, while not made fully explicit by the Gemara, appears to be that requiring both couples to appear together before *beit din* at the moment of divorce creates a verification mechanism. When the divorced woman later presents the *get*, she will need witnesses who can testify not only to the act of divorce but to the fact that the other couple was present and that she is indeed the correct recipient.

One might still question what this achieves. If a woman produces a *get* and claims divorce, the *beit din* knowing about the two identically named couples will presumably insist on witnesses who can testify to the circumstances of the divorce. What the requirement of both couples being present ultimately accomplishes is to make the act of fraud harder to execute without detection. But the honest challenge remains: a determined fraudster could still claim the required circumstances were satisfied and dare the court to disprove it. The Gemara does not fully resolve how this rule prevents rather than merely complicates fraud.

### Name Adoption Fraud

A further scenario: what if someone travels to a different city — where he is unknown — and begins representing himself as Yosef ben Shimon? His real name is something else entirely. He manages to establish this false identity, then approaches a *sofer* in that city, claims to be Yosef ben Shimon, and obtains a *get* written in that name. He returns to his home city and delivers this *get* to the wife of the real Yosef ben Shimon. When she presents it, the *beit din* of the home city will not even think to apply the two-couples rule, since to their knowledge there is only one Yosef ben Shimon in their town — the fraudster has constructed his identity in a different jurisdiction entirely.

### Thirty-Day Rule

Rav Huna bar Chinana cites Rav's response: *kol shehuchzak shemo ba'ir shloshim yom ein chosheshin lo* — a person whose name has been established in a city for thirty days, we are no longer concerned about this fraud. The Rashbam explains: if the ruse must be sustained for thirty days before a *get* can be written, the fraudster is exposed to a prolonged period during which his scheme might unravel — people might recognize him, word might travel back to his home city, or other circumstances might expose him. A fraud that can be executed in a single day is a practical threat; one that requires sustained impersonation over a month is sufficiently unlikely that we do not legislate against it. This does not mean the fraud is impossible; it means the risk is tolerable.

### Name Response Test

What if a stranger arrives in town and has not yet been there thirty days? The Gemara asks: *lo itchazak mai?* — what is the law when thirty days have not yet elapsed? Abaye proposes a test: *dekaru lei ve'ani* — call out the name and see if he responds. If someone walking down the street turns around when called by a name, that is evidence that the name is genuinely his. The reflex response to one's own name is deeply ingrained and is difficult to suppress or fake.

### Deceiver Objection

Rav Zvid rejects this: *ramai beramutei zahir* — a deceiver is careful in his deception. Someone who has gone to the effort of constructing a false identity will know to turn around when his assumed name is called; indeed, he will have been practicing and anticipating exactly this. The name-response test therefore provides no reliable information, and the conclusion follows: if a person has not been established in a city for thirty days, a *get* simply cannot be written for him there.

## Witness Retraction Case

### Woman Retraction Claim

The Gemara now presents a complex case involving Rav Yirmeyah bar Abba and a *shover* he had signed. A woman's husband produced the receipt in *beit din*, claiming that she had been paid her *ketubah*. The receipt bore Rav Yirmeyah's signature as a witness. The woman, however, claimed: *lav ana havai* — that was not me. She maintained that when Rav Yirmeyah and his co-witnesses signed the receipt, a different woman had appeared before them, falsely representing herself as the husband's wife.

### Witness Doubt Response

Rav Yirmeyah acknowledged that he himself had harbored doubts — that is, he had thought, even at the time the case came before *beit din*, that this woman did not appear to be the same woman from the earlier signing. *Ana nami amri lehu lav ihi* — I too said to my co-witnesses that she doesn't seem to be the same woman. But his co-witnesses had explained: *mikhashi dekhachsha* — she has simply grown thin, or aged; *uvagra lah kalah* — and her voice has deepened. It is the same woman, and she is lying.

### Torah Scholar Exception

Abaye addressed the question of whether Rav Yirmeyah's partial retraction could stand. The general rule is *keivan shehigid shuv eino chozer umagid* — once a witness has testified, he may not retract. Yet here the Gemara seems to permit the ultimate resolution in favor of the receipt's validity, grounded in a specific circumstance: *tzurba merabanan lav orchei lemidak* — a Torah scholar does not typically scrutinize women. Rav Yirmeyah had not been looking at her; he was listening to her voice, which had changed. His co-witnesses, who had looked at her, recognized her. His doubt was a product of his own *tzni'us* practices rather than genuine uncertainty about her identity. Under these circumstances, the final position — accepting the co-witnesses' explanation that she was the same woman — is allowed, because there is compelling circumstantial evidence explaining why Rav Yirmeyah's impression was mistaken.

### Unresolved Principles

This case leaves several important questions unresolved. First, on the principle of *keivan shehigid*: the rule is generally understood as an ironclad procedural bar — once testimony is accepted in *beit din*, it cannot be withdrawn regardless of subsequent explanations. Does this case establish that *keivan shehigid* can be overridden by sufficiently compelling circumstantial evidence explaining the error? If so, what is the threshold? This seems to cut against the formalistic understanding of the rule. Second, a more basic question: why is Rav Yirmeyah's initial doubt — his claim that this is a different woman — treated as significant at all? If the *shover* bears his validated signature, his subsequent second-guessing of his own signing would itself seem to trigger *keivan shehigid*; he would be retracting a previously established act of testimony. Part of the answer may lie in the distinction between formal testimony delivered in *beit din* and signatures on a *shtar*: the latter may not rise to the same level of *edut* for purposes of the *keivan shehigid* rule, and the question of whether the *shtar* had already been formally *mekuyam* before Rav Yirmeyah expressed his doubts is material. Whether the principle of *keivan shehigid* applies to witnesses retracting their signatures on documents, as distinct from their oral testimony before a court, and whether circumstantial evidence bearing on the reliability of that signature can reopen the question — all of this remains, as the Gemara closes, unresolved.
