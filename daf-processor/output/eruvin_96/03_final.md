# Eruvin 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Tefillin Pairs Debate

### Finding Tefillin Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ זוּג אֶחָד אִין, טְפֵי — לָא. לֵימָא תְּנַן סְתָמָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that a person who finds phylacteries in a field may carry them by pairs, indicating that **one pair, yes,** it may be carried; however, **more** than one pair, **no,** they may not be carried. The Gemara asks: **Let us say** that **we learned the unattributed** mishna **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** despite the principle that an unattributed mishna usually reflects Rabbi Meir's opinion.

The daf begins at the bottom of 95b, picking up from a *mishnah* concerning one who finds *tefillin* on the street without an *eruv* — no walls, no enclosure — and must bring them indoors. The solution the *mishnah* contemplates is wearing them. But the question then becomes: may one wear a single pair at a time, or two pairs simultaneously? That is the debate the *gemara* has been unpacking.

### Shabbat as Zman Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר שַׁבָּת לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא, וְכִי שְׁרוֹ רַבָּנַן לְעִנְיַן הַצָּלָה — דֶּרֶךְ מַלְבּוּשׁ בִּמְקוֹם תְּפִילִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, he holds** that **Shabbat is not a time for phylacteries, and when the Sages permitted** one to don phylacteries **for the purpose of rescue,** it was only by donning them in the **manner** that one typically wears **clothing,** i.e., in the appropriate **place** for **phylacteries.**

The *gemara* has been presenting the debate through two lenses. The first concerns whether *Shabbat* is a *zman tefillin* — a time when *tefillin* properly apply. Ironically, if *Shabbat* is indeed a *zman tefillin*, one is limited to wearing a single pair, because the act is viewed through the lens of the *mitzvah* itself, and the *mitzvah* calls for one pair. If *Shabbat* is not the time of the *mitzvah*, however, two pairs becomes theoretically permissible — not because of any special dispensation, but because wearing *tefillin* at that point is regarded as something closer to wearing an unusual garment than to performing a *mitzvah*. More than two pairs, even on this view, would not work: there is only a proper anatomical place for two pairs of *tefillin* to sit, and wearing them anywhere else would be like tying a tie around one's leg — not considered wearing at all.

The second dimension is *bal tosif*, the prohibition against adding to a *mitzvah*. Adding to a *mitzvah* is most potent when one does so during the *mitzvah*'s own time and alters its parameters — taking two *lulavim* simultaneously on *Sukkos*, for instance. If one were to take a *lulav* on an ordinary weekday, it would be hard to call that *bal tosif*; it would be strange, but without the core *mitzvah* as a foundation, there is nothing to expand. Accordingly, if *Shabbat* is not the time of *tefillin*, wearing two pairs is less likely to constitute *bal tosif*, since one is not building upon the *mitzvah* at all. If it were *bal tosif*, and if *Shabbat* were the *zman tefillin*, the extra pair would be clearly superfluous — creating not only a *bal tosif* problem but presumably a *Shabbat* violation as well, since the second pair would be rendered obviously extraneous.

### Kavanah in Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שַׁבָּת זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא, וְהָכָא בְּמִצְוֹת צְרִיכוֹת כַּוּוֹנָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי. תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: לָצֵאת בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the dispute should be understood as follows. **Everyone agrees that Shabbat is a time for phylacteries, and here they disagree with regard to** whether or not fulfillment of **mitzvot requires intent. The first *tanna* holds: To fulfill** a mitzva one **needs intent.**

Continuing from two lines at the bottom of 95b, the *gemara* now offers an alternative framing: *dekhulei alma Shabbos zman tefillin hu* — suppose everyone agrees that *Shabbat* is indeed the time for *tefillin*. How then could wearing two pairs ever be permitted? The *gemara* says: *vehakha bemitzvos tzrichos kavanah kamiflgi* — the debate here concerns whether *mitzvos* require *kavanah*, intention, in order to be fulfilled. *Tanna Kamma* holds that to be *yotzei* a *mitzvah* one needs *kavanah*, and here *kavanah* is absent. *Rabban Gamliel* holds that *kavanah* is not required.

### Rashi on Kavanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: לָא בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabban Gamliel holds:** In order to fulfill a mitzva, **one does not need intent.**

*Rashi* explains the underlying logic: the assumption at this stage is that what is required to *fulfill* the *mitzvah* is also what is required to *transgress* the prohibition of *bal tosif*. If *kavanah* is not needed to fulfill the *mitzvah*, then the act is defined as a *mitzvah* act even without intention — and that same definition would apply to a second pair of *tefillin* as well. Wearing two pairs would then mean one is expanding the *mitzvah*, constituting *bal tosif*. But if *mitzvos tzrichos kavanah* — if the *mitzvah* is only defined as such with intent — then without intent, the second pair is also not classified as part of the *mitzvah* act. Since one is wearing two pairs not for the purpose of the *mitzvah* but simply to transport them indoors, one cannot be said to be expanding the *mitzvah*, and there is no *bal tosif*. Whatever defines the fulfillment of the *mitzvah* likewise defines what constitutes the *mitzvah* act — and therefore, where *kavanah* is required, one would not be *over* on *bal tosif* without it.

## Bal Tosif Transgression

### Bal Tosif Without Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָצֵאת לָא בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה, וְהָכָא לַעֲבוֹר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תּוֹסִיף" קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: לַעֲבוֹר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תּוֹסִיף" לָא בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **everyone agrees** that **to fulfill** a mitzva **one does not need intent, and here they disagree with regard to** the condition needed **to violate** the prohibition: **Do not add** to mitzvot of the Torah. **As the first *tanna* holds** that **one does not need intent to violate** the prohibition: **Do not add** to mitzvot.

At the top of 96a, the *gemara* sharpens the analysis with yet another framing. One could say: *dekhulei alma latzet lo ba'ei kavanah* — everyone agrees that fulfilling the *mitzvah* at the right time in the right way does not require *kavanah*. The act, properly done at the proper moment, more or less defines itself as the *mitzvah* act. But perhaps more is needed to be *over* on *bal tosif*, because there one is doing something wrong — incorporating an additional element into the *mitzvah* that does not naturally belong there — and that incorporation requires an active intention to bring it within the scope of the *mitzvah*.

### Tanna Kamma vs Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: לַעֲבוֹר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תּוֹסִיף" בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabban Gamliel holds** that in order **to violate** the prohibition: **Do not add** to mitzvot, **one needs intent** to perform a mitzva.

With this framing, *vehakha la'avor mishum Bal Tosif kamiflgi* — the debate is specifically about what is needed to transgress *bal tosif*. *Tanna Kamma* holds that to be *over* on *bal tosif* one does not need *kavanah*. Since the *mitzvah* itself is fulfilled without *kavanah*, and the additional element is performed alongside it, that element too becomes incorporated into the *mitzvah* act without any intent being necessary. Extra *tefillin*, or a *lulav* held in addition to one's own, becomes part of the *mitzvah* framework regardless of intention — and so wearing two pairs of *tefillin* would constitute *bal tosif* and presumably a *Shabbat* violation as well.

*Rabban Gamliel* holds that even though the base *mitzvah* is fulfilled without *kavanah*, an additional element does not become part of the *mitzvah* unless one intends it to be so. When one picks up one's *lulav* without thinking about the *mitzvah*, one is still *yotzei* — that is the *mitzvah* itself. But a second *lulav* held at the same time does not become incorporated into the *mitzvah* without an active intention. Similarly, a second pair of *tefillin* worn only to carry them indoors does not, absent such intent, become part of the *mitzvah* act — and without that, there is no *bal tosif*, and presumably no *Shabbat* violation either. The second pair can be regarded as an unusual garment, nothing more.

### Two Pairs No Bal Tosif

The underlying point across all these approaches is consistent: how can one wear two pairs of *tefillin* without violating *bal tosif*? Either because *Shabbat* is not the *zman* of the *mitzvah* at all, or because even if it is, the *mitzvah* requires *kavanah* which is absent here, or — the sharpest version — because even if *mitzvos* generally do not require *kavanah* for fulfillment, incorporating an additional element into the *mitzvah* act does require active intention, and without such intention, the second pair remains entirely outside the scope of the *mitzvah*.

## Bal Tosif Shelo BiZmano

### Transgressing Out of Sea…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִי דִּסְבִירָא לַן דְּשַׁבָּת זְמַן תְּפִילִּין — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא לַעֲבוֹר בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה וְלָא לָצֵאת בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה. וְהָכָא בְּ"לַעֲבוֹר שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנּוֹ" קָמִיפַּלְגִי. תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: לָא בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the dispute may be explained as follows. **If we were to maintain** that **Shabbat is** a fit **time for** donning **phylacteries, everyone** would **agree** that **one does not need intent to violate** the prohibition against adding to mitzvot, **nor does one need intent to fulfill** a mitzva. In this case, one's intention has no bearing on his action. **However, here, they disagree with regard to** the condition for **violating** the prohibition against adding to a mitzva **not in its** proper **time,** i.e., when a mitzva is performed not at its prescribed time. **The first *tanna* holds** that if the act of a mitzva is performed not in its proper time, **one does not need intent;**

The *gemara* now raises a further possibility. Perhaps the debate is not about *kavanah* at all, but about something even more basic: *vekhakha be-la'avor shelo bizmano kamiflgi* — can one even be *over* on *bal tosif* when performing an act *shelo bizmano*, outside the time when the *mitzvah* applies? The earlier reasoning assumed that if *Shabbat* is not the *zman tefillin*, one is entirely safe, because the act is not even viewed through the *mitzvah* lens — comparable to taking a *lulav* during an ordinary weekday. But the *gemara* now questions whether that is actually so. Is it possible to violate *bal tosif shelo bizmano*?

### Intent Outside Appointed…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: לַעֲבוֹר שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנּוֹ בָּעֵי כַּוּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabban Gamliel holds that to violate** the prohibition against adding to a mitzva **not in its** proper **time, one needs intent** to fulfill the mitzva.

The *gemara* suggests that this is precisely what *Tanna Kamma* and *Rabban Gamliel* disagree about. *Tanna Kamma* holds that even outside the *mitzvah*'s proper time, one can be *over* on *bal tosif* without *kavanah* — the mere act of wearing two pairs of *tefillin* on *Shabbat*, even if *Shabbat* is not the *zman tefillin*, suffices to constitute a transgression. *Rabban Gamliel* holds that outside the *mitzvah*'s time, *kavanah* is specifically required to be *over* — and since there is no such intent here, two pairs may be worn freely.

### Sukkah Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר זוּג אֶחָד נָמֵי לָא. וְעוֹד: הַיָּשֵׁן בַּשְּׁמִינִי בְּסוּכָּה יִלְקֶה! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִדְשַׁנִּינַן מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this last explanation the Gemara asks: **If so, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** one should **not even** don **one pair** of phylacteries. According to Rabbi Meir's opinion, one who does so violates the prohibition against adding to mitzvot merely by donning one pair, since he is fulfilling the mitzva of phylacteries at a time when he is not commanded to do so. **And furthermore,** according to this opinion, **one who sleeps in a *sukka* on the Eighth Day** of Assembly **should be flogged** for violating the prohibition against adding to mitzvot, as he adds to the mitzva of: "You shall dwell in booths for seven days" (Leviticus 23:42). Yet the Sages instituted that outside of Eretz Yisrael, Jews must observe *Sukkot* for eight days, even though one who sleeps in a *sukka* on the eighth night outside of Eretz Yisrael transgresses a Torah law. **Rather, it is clear as we originally answered,** i.e., you must accept one of the other explanations.

The *gemara* immediately challenges the position that one could violate *bal tosif shelo bizmano* without *kavanah*: *ee hakhi*, if that were so, *ve-Rabbi Meir zuga chada nami lo* — according to *Tanna Kamma* (associated here with Rabbi Meir), one should not even be permitted to wear a single pair of *tefillin* on *Shabbat*. If transgressions of *bal tosif shelo bizmano* require no *kavanah*, then even the one pair would be problematic.

Furthermore: *ha-yashen bi-shmini be-sukka yelakeh* — one who sleeps in the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret* should be liable. Biblically, the *mitzvat sukkah* ends after the seventh day of *Sukkos*. Yet the *gemara* elsewhere assumes that one may dwell in the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret* because of *sefeika de-yoma* — the doubt about which day is the final day of the holiday — and also because *Chazal* explicitly ordained it. Could one be *over* on *bal tosif* under those circumstances? After all, one lacks any intention to add to the *mitzvah*: one is dwelling in the *sukkah* because one was told to, or because of calendar uncertainty — not because one wishes to expand the *mitzvah* of *sukkah* from seven days to eight. If *bal tosif shelo bizmano* required no *kavanah*, there would be no way to avoid the violation.

The *gemara* concludes from this that clearly, *shelo bizmano*, one cannot be *over* on *bal tosif* without *kavanah*. Without the intention to add — whether one is wearing *tefillin* on *Shabbat*, sitting in the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret*, or taking a *lulav* on an ordinary day — there is no *bal tosif* violation whatsoever.

This discussion also raises an interesting question about a case where one does have *kavanah*: if one sat in the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret* with the explicit intention that the biblical *mitzvah* of *sukkah* should apply for eight days, that would indeed be *bal tosif*. Similarly, if one put on *tefillin* on *Shabbat* with the intention that the *mitzvah* applies seven days a week, one would be *over*. But if one took a *lulav* today — many months removed from *Sukkos* — and declared it a *mitzvah* on its own terms, that might not be *bal tosif* at all, since one has not expanded the base *mitzvah* from its natural scope; one has simply created an entirely new and separate ritual. *Bal tosif*, as *Chazal* understand it, is defined narrowly as expanding a *mitzvah* from its base — not inventing a new observance entirely unmoored from the original. If, however, one were to do it on the day immediately following *Sukkos*, or on each successive day until it connected back, then one would be expanding the *mitzvah*'s scope and might indeed be *over*.

### Original Approach Confir…

The *gemara* concludes: *ela mehvarta keshaninan mei'ikara* — it is better to understand the matter as originally stated. If *Shabbat* is definitively not the *zman tefillin*, one is entirely safe with no *bal tosif* concerns whatsoever. All the debates reduce to: either *Shabbat* is not the *zman tefillin* — in which case wearing two pairs carries no *bal tosif* risk — or *Shabbat* is the *zman tefillin*, in which case the question is what is required to constitute *bal tosif* and whether the second pair without intent triggers it.

## Finding the Tanna

### Rabbi Akiva Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ שַׁבָּת זְמַן תְּפִילִּין? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. דְּתַנְיָא: "וְשָׁמַרְתָּ אֶת הַחֻקָּה הַזֹּאת לְמוֹעֲדָהּ מִיָּמִים יָמִימָה". "יָמִים" — וְלֹא לֵילוֹת. "מִיָּמִים" — וְלֹא כׇּל יָמִים, פְּרָט לְשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** Since the topic of phylacteries was discussed, the Gemara continues to explore this issue. **Whom did you hear** who said that **Shabbat is** a fit **time for** donning **phylacteries?** It is **Rabbi Akiva, as it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the end of the section in the Torah beginning with: "Sanctify all firstborns to me" (Exodus 13:2), which deals with the mitzvot of the Paschal lamb and phylacteries: **"And you shall observe this ordinance in its season from year [*miyamim*] to year"** (Exodus 13:10), which indicates that these mitzvot apply during the **days** [***yamim***] **and not** during the **nights.** Furthermore, the letter *mem* in **"from year" [*miyamim*]** teaches: **But not** on **all days;** this **excludes Shabbat and Festivals,** on which phylacteries are not worn. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.**

Having established the debate's framework, the *gemara* turns to identify which *tanna* holds which position with respect to *Shabbat* and *zman tefillin*. The *gemara* asks: *u'man shamat lei Shabbos zman tefillin* — who is it that holds *Shabbat* is the time of *tefillin*? The first candidate raised is Rabbi Akiva.

The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *v'shamarta es hachukah hazos l'mo'adah miyamim yamimah*. This verse appears at the close of one of the four *parshiyos* of *tefillin* — specifically the *parsha* at the end of *Parashas Bo*. In context, the *peshat* of the *pesukim* refers to the *korban Pesach*, but since it is one of the four places in the *Torah* where the text introduces the language of a sign on one's hand and a memorial between one's eyes, it is also read as referring to *tefillin* themselves. The *braita* derives from the words *miyamim yamimah*: *yamim* — days and not nights, thus excluding nighttime from the *mitzvah* of *tefillin*; *miyamim yamimah* — some days and not all days, thus excluding *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* from the *mitzvah*. That is the position of Rabbi Yosei HaGlili.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֱמַר חוּקָּה זוֹ אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן פֶּסַח בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: This ordinance is stated only with regard to the Paschal** lamb, and it does not refer to phylacteries at all.

Rabbi Akiva disputes this reading entirely: *lo ne'emar chukah zo ela l'inyan Pesach bilvad* — this ordinance, the word *chukah*, is said only with respect to the *korban Pesach*. The verse is not speaking about *tefillin* at all. Consequently, according to Rabbi Akiva, there is no scriptural basis to exclude *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* from the *zman tefillin* — and no basis, from this verse, to exclude nighttime either.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן: הַפֶּסַח וְהַמִּילָה מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה — לֵימָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא — כֵּיוָן דְּמוֹקֵי לָהּ בְּפֶסַח, לָאו נָמֵי אִיכָּא, כִּדְרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **that** which **we learned** in a mishna that **the Paschal** lamb **and circumcision are positive mitzvot, let us say that** this statement is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.** The reason for this claim is **that if** you say this teaching is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, since he establishes** this verse as referring **to the Paschal** lamb, this would mean that in failure to bring this offering there **is also** the violation of **a negative** mitzva, **in accordance with** the principle **that Rabbi Avin** said that **Rabbi Elai said.**

The *gemara* then digresses briefly onto a related point: the *mishnah* in another context states that *Pesach* and *milah* are the only positive commandments for which one incurs *karet* without the transgression of a *lo ta'aseh*. The question is raised whether this is consistent with Rabbi Akiva, since according to him, the verse *v'shamarta es hachukah hazos* — which ordinarily introduces a negative prohibition (*hashamer* connoting a warning not to transgress) — would apply to *Pesach*, creating a *lav*. The *gemara* answers by invoking the principle of Rabbi Avin citing Rabbi Ila'i: *kol makom she'ne'emar hashamer pen v'al, eino ela b'lo ta'aseh* — wherever the *Torah* says "guard yourself lest" or "you shall not," it is a negative prohibition; but *hashamer* in the context of a positive command functions as a positive exhortation. Guarding oneself not to transgress is a negative prohibition; guarding oneself to perform is a positive commandment. This resolves the side issue and returns the discussion to the main question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, "הִשָּׁמֶר" דְּלָאו — לָאו, "הִשָּׁמֶר" דַּעֲשֵׂה — עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Even if you say** that **Rabbi Akiva** holds that no negative mitzva applies to the Paschal lamb, it is not difficult, as an additional principle must be taken into account. Although it is true that the term **observe with regard to a negative** mitzva indicates the presence of another **negative** mitzva; that same term **observe** with regard to **a positive** mitzva has the force of **a positive** mitzva, as the Torah is warning adherents to take special care in the observance of a mitzva.

### Shabbat Not Zman Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שַׁבָּת זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא?! וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַנִּיחַ אָדָם תְּפִילִּין בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְהָיָה לְךָ לְאוֹת עַל יָדְךָ", מִי שֶׁצְּרִיכִין אוֹת, יָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ — שֶׁהֵן גּוּפָן אוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the issue at hand: **And** does **Rabbi Akiva** really **hold that Shabbat is a time for** donning **phylacteries? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says:** I **might** have thought that **a person should don phylacteries on *Shabbatot* and Festivals.** Therefore, **the verse states: "And it shall be for a sign for you on your arm,** and for a remembrance between your eyes, so that God's law shall be in your mouth; for with a strong arm God brought you out of Egypt" (Exodus 13:9). The obligation to don phylacteries applies when Jews **require a sign** to assert their Judaism and their status as the Chosen People, i.e., during the week, **excluding** Shabbat and Festivals, **as they are themselves signs** of Israel's status as the Chosen People and a remembrance of the exodus from Egypt.

The *gemara* now presses further: does Rabbi Akiva really hold that *Shabbat* is a *zman tefillin*? A *braita* is cited in which Rabbi Akiva himself addresses the question directly: *yachol yaniach adam tefillin b'shabatot u'v'yamim tovim?* — perhaps one should put on *tefillin* on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*? Rabbi Akiva answers with a *drashah* on the verse *v'hayah l'cha l'os al yadecha*: *tefillin* are to be a sign on one's hand — and this applies to *mi she'tzrichin os*, those who need a sign. *Yatz'u elu she'hen gufan os* — excluded are those days that are themselves a sign. *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, being themselves an *os*, do not require the additional sign of *tefillin*; not only is one not required to wear *tefillin* on those days, but one is actually prohibited from doing so. Wearing *tefillin* as a sign on a day that is itself a sign would seem to imply that the day's own *os* is insufficient — and that is treated as an actual prohibition, perhaps a kind of *bal tosif*, perhaps an undermining of the sanctity of *Shabbat* as an *os* in its own right.

*Rashi* offers a striking description of what the *os* of *tefillin* signifies: *yamim sheYisrael tzrichin le'amid os al atzmam lehakir shehem machazikim b'Toraso shel haKadosh Baruch Hu* — these are days on which Israel needs to establish a sign upon themselves to demonstrate that they embrace God's commandments. There is something active and powerful in that formulation: wearing *tefillin* is not merely a mark of chosenness but a physical demonstration of one's embrace of God's *mitzvos*, done *al yadecha*, directly on one's body.

The question naturally arises: how does the exclusion extend to *Yom Tov*? The verse *ki os hi beini uveineichem* speaks most explicitly of *Shabbat*. The extension to *Yom Tov* is more difficult to ground textually, though *Rashi* simply states that *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov* are themselves a sign between God and Israel, citing that verse. The question of *Chol HaMoed* is even more disputed, and notably the *gemara* never resolves it directly — a silence that became the opening through which a major *machlokes Rishonim* developed, one in which *Kabbalah* played a significant role.

*Tosafos* addresses *Chol HaMoed* at the top of the page. Their initial instinct is that *tefillin* should not be worn on *Chol HaMoed*, since the day carries its own markers of special status — the prohibition of certain *melacha*, and the active rituals of *sukkah* during *Sukkos* and the prohibition of *chametz* during *Pesach* — all of which render the day recognizably distinct without the *os* of *tefillin*. Yet *Tosafos* also cites a *Yerushalmi* whose plain meaning seems to support wearing *tefillin* on *Chol HaMoed*, and he notes *vekhen ama davar* — that this was the common practice. *Tosafos* himself acknowledges the tension: logical analysis would suggest not wearing them, yet the practice was to wear them. This became one of the most consequential divergences between Ashkenazi and Sephardi communities — with the *Zohar*'s position subsequently tipping the balance decisively for many communities away from wearing *tefillin* on *Chol HaMoed* altogether.

So the conclusion emerges clearly: Rabbi Akiva himself holds that *Shabbat* is *lav zman tefillin* — not the time for *tefillin* — based on the principle that *Shabbat* is itself an *os*. We have thus not yet identified the *tanna* who holds that *Shabbat* is the *zman tefillin*.

### Rabbi Natan Night Tefill…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא דְּתַנְיָא: הַנֵּיעוֹר בַּלַּיְלָה, רָצָה — חוֹלֵץ, רָצָה — מַנִּיחַ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי נָתָן. יוֹנָתָן הַקִּיטוֹנִי אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַנִּיחִין תְּפִילִּין בַּלַּיְלָה. מִדְּלַיְלָה לְתַנָּא קַמָּא זְמַן תְּפִילִּין — שַׁבָּת נָמֵי זְמַן תְּפִילִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, it is this *tanna*,** Rabbi Natan, who maintains that Shabbat is a fit time for donning phylacteries, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who is awake at night, if he wishes he** may **remove** his phylacteries, and **if he wishes he** may continue to **don** them, and he need not worry about violating the prohibition against adding to mitzvot. This is **the statement of Rabbi Natan. Yonatan HaKitoni says: One** may **not don phylacteries at night. From** the fact **that according to the first *tanna*,** Rabbi Natan, **night is** a fit **time for phylacteries,** it may be inferred that **Shabbat, too, is a time for** donning **phylacteries,** as Rabbi Natan evidently does not accept Rabbi Yosei HaGelili's limitation based on the phrase: From year to year.

The *gemara* turns to another candidate: *ela hai tanna hu* — perhaps it is the following *tanna*. A *braita* teaches: *haneior balayla*, one who is awake at night while wearing *tefillin* — *ratza choletz ratza maniach*, if one wishes one may remove them, and if one wishes one may keep them on. This is the opinion of Rabbi Natan. And since Rabbi Natan permits actively wearing *tefillin* at night, this implies that *layla zman tefillin* — nighttime is a time for *tefillin* according to his view, which in turn implies that the verse of *miyamim yamimah* is not applied to exclude nighttime from the scope of the *mitzvah*.

It must be noted that there is a major debate about nighttime and *tefillin*. Everyone agrees that the practice is not to wear *tefillin* at night — but the reason is disputed. Is it because nighttime is biblically excluded by *miyamim yamimah*, making it simply not a *zman tefillin*? Or is it that biblically the *mitzvah* could apply at night, but *Chazal* were concerned one might fall asleep while wearing them and pass gas in an undignified manner? The position of Rabbi Natan, which permits putting them on *lechatchila* at night for one who is wide awake, reflects the view that nighttime is biblically the *zman tefillin* — the concern is only practical, and if one is fully alert, that concern is removed. The *gemara* therefore reasons: *mi'd'layla l'tanna kamma zman tefillin hu, Shabbos nami zman tefillin* — if nighttime is included in the *zman tefillin* for this *tanna*, then presumably the verse of *miyamim yamimah* plays no limiting role at all, and *Shabbat* too would be the *zman tefillin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ לַיְלָה זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא, שַׁבָּת לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא. דְּהָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר: לַיְלָה זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא, שַׁבָּת לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention: This is not a conclusive proof, as **perhaps he holds** that although **night is** a fit **time for phylacteries, Shabbat is not** a fit **time for phylacteries. As we** have **heard that Rabbi Akiva said** that **night is a time for phylacteries,** because he does not accept the limitation of "from days to days," and yet he maintains that **Shabbat is not a time for phylacteries,** as no sign is required on Shabbat.

The *gemara* rebuts this: perhaps one can hold that nighttime is the *zman tefillin* without holding that *Shabbat* is. We know that Rabbi Akiva himself held *layla zman tefillin* — since he rejected the verse of *miyamim yamimah* as applying to *tefillin* entirely, reading it instead as referring to the *korban Pesach*, nighttime is implicitly included. And yet Rabbi Akiva explicitly ruled that *Shabbat* is *lav zman tefillin* based on the principle of *os*. The two exclusions are thus independent: nighttime is excluded only by *miyamim yamimah* — a ground Rabbi Akiva does not accept — while *Shabbat* is excluded by the *os* principle, which is distinct and which Rabbi Akiva does accept. The two positions do not travel together as a package.

We therefore still have not identified the *tanna* who holds that *Shabbat* is the *zman tefillin*.

## Women Tefillin Status

### Michal and Yona Stories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַאי תַּנָּא הִיא דְּתַנְיָא: מִיכַל בַּת כּוּשִׁי הָיְתָה מַנַּחַת תְּפִילִּין וְלֹא מִיחוּ בָּהּ חֲכָמִים, וְאִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל יוֹנָה הָיְתָה עוֹלָה לָרֶגֶל וְלֹא מִיחוּ בָּהּ חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** we must say that **it is this *tanna*** who maintains that Shabbat is a time for phylacteries, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Michal, daughter of Kushi,** King Saul, **would don phylacteries, and the Sages did not protest against her** behavior, as she was permitted to do so. **And** similarly, **Jonah's wife would undertake the Festival pilgrimage and the Sages did not protest against her** practice.

The *gemara* introduces yet another candidate. A *braita* is cited: *Michal bas Kushi haysa menachas tefillin*, Michal the daughter of Kushi was accustomed to wearing *tefillin*, *v'lo michu bah chachamim* — and the Sages did not object. *Rashi* explains that Kushi here refers to Shaul — there is a *pasuk* that uses the term *Kushi* to describe Shaul — making this the famous Michal, daughter of the king. Additionally: *v'ishto shel Yona haysa ola la-regel*, the wife of the prophet Yonah would make the pilgrimage to Yerushalayim for the *regel*, and the Sages likewise did not object.

### Women Obligated Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּלֹא מִיחוּ בָּהּ חֲכָמִים — אַלְמָא קָסָבְרִי מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the fact **that the Sages did not protest against** Michal's donning phylacteries, **it is apparent that these** Sages **hold** that phylacteries **is a positive mitzva not bound by time,** i.e., it is a mitzva whose performance is mandated at all times, including nights and Shabbat.

The *gemara* attempts to infer from these two stories: *mi'd'lo michu bah chachamim* — since the Sages did not object, presumably these women were performing a *mitzvah* they were obligated to perform. *Alma kasavri mitzvas asei she'lo ha-zman grama hi* — this implies that *tefillin* is not a time-bound *mitzvah*, since if women are fully obligated, that obligation must apply even on *Shabbat*, meaning *Shabbat* is indeed *zman tefillin*. If so, we have found our *tanna*.

The problem, of course, is that if women were genuinely obligated, the *gemara* would expect not merely *lo michu bah*, but an affirmative statement that women are required to wear *tefillin* — with every woman doing so as a matter of course. The phrasing of non-objection already begins to suggest something more like permissive tolerance than obligatory fulfillment.

### Women Exempt Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: נָשִׁים סוֹמְכוֹת רְשׁוּת? לָא סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּלָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְלָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to refute this. **But perhaps** that *tanna* **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said:** It is **optional** for **women** to **place** their hands on the head of a sacrificial animal before it is slaughtered. Here too, perhaps it is optional for women to don phylacteries. The Gemara answers: **This cannot enter your mind, as neither Rabbi Meir holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, nor does Rabbi Yehuda hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** as the Gemara proceeds to prove.

The *gemara* resolves this by pointing to the case of Yonah's wife and the *regel*. *Regel* is clearly a time-bound *mitzvah* — indeed, the Torah explicitly says *yeira'eh kol zechurcha*, "all your males shall appear," making the obligation explicitly gendered. No one would suggest that women are obligated in *aliyah la-regel*. And yet the Sages did not object to Yonah's wife making the pilgrimage. Why not? Because being exempt does not necessarily mean one is forbidden — one may choose to fulfill a *mitzvah* from which one is exempt, and the Sages would not object.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: נָשִׁים סוֹמְכוֹת רְשׁוּת?
>
> **Translation:** **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said:** It is **optional** for **women** to **place** their hands on the head of a sacrificial animal before it is slaughtered.

*Dekhulei alma lo ba'ei kavanah* — if this is true of *aliyah la-regel*, it is equally true of *tefillin*. Michal and Yonah's wife were both exempt from time-bound *mitzvos*, and both chose to fulfill them anyway. *Lo michu bah chachamim* does not mean they were obligated; it means the Sages permitted their voluntary performance. This resolves the story: *tefillin* is time-bound, *Shabbat* is *lav zman tefillin*, and women are exempt — but the Sages did not object to their choosing to perform the *mitzvah* regardless.

### Exempt Woman Perform

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דִּתְנַן: אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַתִּינוֹקוֹת מִלִּתְקוֹעַ. הָא נָשִׁים מְעַכְּבִין — וּסְתָם מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Neither Rabbi Meir holds in accordance** with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as we learned** in a mishna: **One** need **not prevent children from sounding** the *shofar* on Rosh HaShana. Although there is an element of prohibition in sounding the *shofar* when there is no obligation to do so, since the children will one day be obligated to sound the *shofar*, one need not prevent them from doing so and learning. It may be inferred from here that **one must prevent women** from sounding the *shofar*. **And an unattributed mishna** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** indicating that according to Rabbi Meir, a woman does not even have the option of performing a time-bound positive mitzva.

The *gemara* then raises the question that has been lurking beneath the surface throughout: does being exempt simply mean one is not required, or does it mean one is actually prohibited from performing the *mitzvah*? The *mishnah* under consideration in the broader context — which is attributed by convention to Rabbi Meir, as an unattributed *mishnah* reflects his position — states: *ein m'akvin es hatinokos mil'itkoa*, one does not prevent children from blowing a *shofar* on *Rosh Hashana*, as part of their *chinuch*. The *diyuk* is: by implication, *nashim m'akvin* — women one would prevent. This suggests that a *mitzvah* from which women are exempt is not merely optional but actually impermissible for them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: "דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל ... וְסָמַךְ", בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל סוֹמְכִין וְאֵין בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל סוֹמְכוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: נָשִׁים סוֹמְכוֹת רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Nor does Rabbi Yehuda hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as it was taught** in the *Sifra*, the halakhic *midrash* on Leviticus. The verse states: **"Speak to the sons of Israel…and he shall place** his hands on the head of the burnt-offering" (Leviticus 1:2–4). By inference, **the sons of Israel place** their hands, **but the daughters of Israel do not place** their hands. **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** It is **optional** for **women** to **place** their hands on the head of a sacrificial animal before it is slaughtered.

Similarly, the *braita* from the *Sifra* on *Vayikra* states: *b'nei Yisrael somchin v'ein b'nos Yisrael somchos* — Israelite males perform *smicha* on their *korbanos*, but women do not. Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon add: *nashim somchos reshus* — women may nonetheless do so if they choose. The *tanna kamma* who does not offer this qualification, and whose *Sifra* position is attributed to the school of Rabbi Yehuda (*stam Sifra* follows Rabbi Yehuda), appears to hold that exempt women are not merely excused but actually barred. The *gemara* concludes from these sources that neither Rabbi Meir nor Rabbi Yehuda follow the position of Rabbi Yosi — both hold that a woman exempt from a *mitzvah* is also prohibited from performing it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּסְתָם סִיפְרָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַמּוֹצֵא תְּכֵלֶת בַּשּׁוּק, לְשׁוֹנוֹת — פְּסוּלוֹת, חוּטִין — כְּשֵׁרִין. ... עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בַּחֲדָשׁוֹת וִישָׁנוֹת, אֲבָל בְּאִשָּׁה לָא פְּלִיגִי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא הוּא, וְכׇל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁאֵין הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And who is** the author of **an unattributed *Sifra*?** It is **Rabbi Yehuda.** This teaching proves that Rabbi Yehuda maintains that women do not have the option of placing their hands on a sacrifice. Neither Rabbi Meir nor Rabbi Yehuda accepts Rabbi Yosei's opinion that it is optional for women to perform time-bound positive mitzvot; therefore, the *tanna* who cited their opinions that a woman may bring in phylacteries on Shabbat maintains that the mitzva of phylacteries is not time-bound and is in effect even on Shabbat, which is why even women are obligated. ... Analysis of this *Tosefta* indicates that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda **disagree only with regard to** the issue of **new** phylacteries **and old ones; however, with regard to a woman** bringing in the phylacteries, **they do not disagree** that it is permitted. **Learn from it** that this *tanna* maintains that donning phylacteries **is a positive mitzva not bound by time, and** since **women are obligated in every positive mitzva not bound by time,** a woman may don these phylacteries and walk into the town.

This creates a powerful inference regarding *tefillin*: since both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda nevertheless rule that a woman who finds *tefillin* on *Shabbat* may wear them — the first permitting it regardless of whether old or new, the second permitting it for old pairs — they cannot be treating it as something a woman is merely tolerated in doing despite her exemption. If they held that exempt women are prohibited, and yet they allow women to wear *tefillin*, it must be because women are not exempt at all. Women must be fully obligated in *tefillin*. And if women are obligated in *tefillin*, then *tefillin* is not a time-bound *mitzvah* — and *Shabbat* is *zman tefillin*. So we have finally found our *tanna*: both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda hold *Shabbat* is *zman tefillin* and women are *chayav*. This is a striking conclusion — effectively half of the canonical Tannaitic all-star team.

### Exempt Woman Perform

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: נָשִׁים סוֹמְכוֹת רְשׁוּת? לָא סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּלָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְלָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to refute this. **But perhaps** that *tanna* **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said:** It is **optional** for **women** to **place** their hands on the head of a sacrificial animal before it is slaughtered. Here too, perhaps it is optional for women to don phylacteries. The Gemara answers: **This cannot enter your mind, as neither Rabbi Meir holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, nor does Rabbi Yehuda hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,**

The *gemara* continues to press whether *lo michu bah chachamim* can itself bear the weight of the inference. Could one not simply say that even Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda — despite generally prohibiting exempt women from performing *mitzvos* — simply tolerated it here? The *gemara* says *lo salka datchach*: it is untenable to read the cases that way, because we have explicit evidence that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda both hold that exempt women are prohibited. The *mishnah* about *shofar* and the *Sifra* on *smicha* make their positions clear. Knowing that, the fact that they still permit women to wear *tefillin* can only mean they regard women as *chayavos* — obligated.

### Rashi Niros Mosifos

The broader question of whether exempt women may perform *mitzvos* they are not obligated in generates a major debate between *Rashi* and *Tosafos* with significant *halachic* ramifications. *Rashi*, in the relevant passage, explains that the problem with exempt women performing such *mitzvos* is that they *niros k'mosifos* — they appear to be adding. Even though it is not technically *bal tosif* (since an exempt person has no base *mitzvah* to begin with), the act of someone performing something outside their obligation creates the appearance of expanding the *mitzvah*'s scope. According to *Rashi*, this would be a global concern: women should refrain from performing any *mitzvah* from which they are exempt, because in every case it would look like an improper addition.

### Tosafot Context Problems

*Tosafos*, followed by the great majority of the *Rishonei Ashkenaz*, rejects this global approach. The concern is not uniform; rather, each case of a *mitzvah* that exempt women are said to be prohibited from involves a *specific contextual problem* that would not exist if the woman were obligated but emerges precisely because she is not.

Consider *tekias shofar*: producing sound from a musical instrument on *Yom Tov* is ordinarily prohibited. The only reason it is permitted on *Rosh Hashana* is because of the *mitzvah*. If a woman is exempt from the *mitzvah*, that justification evaporates — and the playing of the *shofar* on *Yom Tov* becomes problematic in its own right. The prohibition is not based on *niros k'mosifos* in the abstract, but on a specific prohibition that the obligation would otherwise override.

Similarly with *smicha*: leaning one's weight upon the *korban* in the *azarah* carries the appearance of *shevus b'kodshim* — using a sacred animal as a support — which is at least rabbinically problematic. When one is obligated in *smicha*, this concern is overridden. When one is exempt, the local problem resurfaces.

By this approach, *mitzvos* that have no such attendant problems are entirely unaffected by a woman's exempt status. Sitting in a *sukkah*, hearing *tekias shofar* when men are in any case blowing, taking a *lulav*, wearing *tzitzis* — none of these carry the kind of local issue that would become activated by the woman's exemption. According to *Tosafos*, a woman may freely perform these *mitzvos*, and there is no general principle barring her from doing so.

### Tefillin Guf Naki

This brings us to *tefillin*. What is the specific contextual problem that *Tosafos* identifies for exempt women who wish to wear *tefillin*? The issue is *guf naki* — the requirement to maintain bodily cleanliness and to avoid passing gas while wearing *tefillin*. As is well known, this concern is the reason why *tefillin* are no longer worn throughout the entire day as they once were: the *gemara* itself treats *tefillin* as an exceptionally demanding *mitzvah* in this regard, which is also why the practice of starting *chinuch* for *tefillin* begins only a short time before *Bar Mitzvah*, far later than for other *mitzvos*. When one is obligated in *tefillin*, this challenge is an obligation one accepts and works to meet. When one is exempt, the difficulty remains but the obligation that would justify confronting it is absent — and therefore, according to Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda as read by *Tosafos*, the exempt woman should not take on the challenge.

This is not a gender-specific concern in *Tosafos*'s framing: it is the same concern that applies to everyone, overridden for obligated men but not available to override for exempt women. That said, it should be acknowledged that in later *Ashkenazic* practice — particularly in the *Rema* — the notion of *guf naki* in the women's context expanded well beyond the original concern about passing gas, becoming entangled with questions of *niddah* and a broader culture of women maintaining distance from certain holy activities during their menstrual cycle. This expansion, while influential in shaping communal practice, goes considerably beyond the *gemara*'s original framing.

It is worth noting that the entire debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda on one hand and Rabbi Yosi on the other — about whether exempt women are prohibited or merely excused — maps directly onto the contemporary question of women and *tefillin*. Rabbi Yosi's position, that women may perform *mitzvos* from which they are exempt, permits women to wear *tefillin*. Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda's position prohibits it — but on the narrower *Tosafos* reading, the prohibition is specifically because of *guf naki*, which raises the further question of whether that concern is sufficient to bar a woman who is genuinely careful in this regard.

### Bracha Exempt Women

The question of *tefillin* and *bracha* is directly addressed by *Tosafos* in the first note at the bottom of 96a. *Tosafos* cites Rabbeinu Tam: *de-mutar le-nashim le-varech al kol mitzvat asei she-hazman gerama af al gav de-peturot* — it is permitted for women to make a *bracha* on all time-bound positive commandments even though they are exempt. The proof from the stories of Michal and Yonah's wife is that they were clearly performing the *mitzvah*, and by implication, making the appropriate *bracha* — *ke-ama de-hachi she-osa mi-stama ka-mevarechet*. This is the classic Ashkenazi position: women recite the *bracha* on *mitzvos* from which they are exempt.

Sephardi practice, following the *Rambam*, is that women do not make a *bracha* on such *mitzvos*, since one cannot say *asher kiddishanu bemitzvosav vetzivanu* — that He has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us — if one has not in fact been commanded.

The Ashkenazi position offers two explanations for why a woman may say *vetzivanu*. The first is collective: *vetzivanu* refers to *Klal Yisrael* as a whole; even if I personally was not commanded, we as a people were, and I speak within that collective framework. The second and more substantive explanation is that women were in fact obligated but were given an exemption — and there is a meaningful difference between never having been obligated and having been obligated with an exemption available. One who is obligated but chooses not to take the exemption re-enters the category of the obligated, and may therefore say *vetzivanu* with full force. This understanding also gives greater weight to the religious act itself: the woman performing a *mitzvah* under this conception is not doing something optional in a casual sense, but is taking on an obligation that has real standing — and that in turn reduces the force of arguments that *guf naki* or other concerns should categorically override her choice to perform.

The practical consequences of the Sephardi position are significant. When no *bracha* is made, the entire cultural investment in the act diminishes. An exchange is noted in which a Sephardic woman of the household, during *Sukkos*, was offered the opportunity to take the *lulav* and responded with complete incomprehension — *why would I do that?* — having no framework within which the act carried meaning. This stands in contrast to the Ashkenazi approach, where the *bracha* expresses and reinforces the genuine religious value of the woman's voluntary performance of the *mitzvah*.

## Blue Threads Evidence

### Marketplace Techeiles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַמּוֹצֵא תְּכֵלֶת בַּשּׁוּק, לְשׁוֹנוֹת — פְּסוּלוֹת, חוּטִין — כְּשֵׁרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** With regard to **one who one finds** fabric dyed **sky blue in the marketplace,** if he found **strips** of combed and dyed wool **they are unfit** for use as ritual fringes. The sky blue threads used in ritual fringes must be spun and dyed for the purpose of the mitzva, and these strips might have been dyed for a different purpose. However, if one found sky blue **threads, they are fit** for use in ritual fringes, as it can be assumed they were prepared for that purpose.

The *gemara* shifts to a related evidentiary question. Rabbi Elazar rules: *ha-motzei techeiles ba-shuk* — one who finds blue threads in the marketplace. The governing question is whether these threads were dyed *lishmah* — for the sake of the *mitzvah* of *tzitzis* — since *techeiles* must be so prepared to be valid. How much evidence of proper preparation does one need before relying upon what one finds?

### Thread Preparations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא לְשׁוֹנוֹת, דְּאָמַר: אַדַּעְתָּא דִגְלִימָא צַבְעִינְהוּ? חוּטִין נָמֵי, נֵימָא: אַדַּעְתָּא דִגְלִימָא טְוִינְהוּ! בִּשְׁזוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **strips** that renders them unfit? It is because Rabbi Elazar **said: One dyed** the strips **with the intent** to use them **for a cloak.** If so, with regard to **threads as well, let us say: One spun them with the intent** to use them **for a cloak.** In that case, they too would be deemed unfit. The Gemara answers: Here, it is referring to threads that are **twisted,** which are not typically used for weaving.

Rabbi Elazar establishes a graduated standard. Simple strips or lengths of fabric — *leshonos* — are *pesul*, invalid, since one might reasonably assume they were dyed to make a blue garment. Twisted threads — *chutim* — are *kasher*, valid for *tzitzis*, since it takes more effort to produce twisted threads, and one would presumably not bother unless intending them for the *mitzvah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁזוּרִים נָמֵי, נֵימָא: אַדַּעְתָּא דְשִׂיפְתָּא דִגְלִימָא עַיְיפִינְהוּ! בְּמוּפְסָקִין, דְּכוּלֵּי הַאי וַדַּאי לָא טָרְחִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **twisted** threads **as well, let us say** that **one twisted them with the intent** of attaching them to **the hem of a cloak** as ornamentation. The Gemara answers: It is referring to twisted threads **that were cut** into short strings suitable for use as ritual fringes, **as people certainly do not exert** themselves and fashion fringes of a cloak to resemble ritual fringes.

The *gemara* challenges this reasoning: perhaps twisted threads too were made for some other purpose — perhaps for sewing a blue garment? The *gemara* responds: *bishzurin* — the standard of the strings is that of fully twisted cords, like our *tzitzis*. But even twisted cords could have been made as decorative trim along the edge of a garment. The *gemara* answers: *bimfaksikin* — if the strings have already been cut to the precise length appropriate for *tzitzis*, that resolves the matter.

### Effort Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* דְּכוּלֵּי הַאי וַדַּאי לָא טָרְחִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **as people certainly do not exert** themselves and fashion fringes of a cloak to resemble ritual fringes.

*Dechulei hai vadai lo tarchai inshi* — to that full extent of effort, people simply do not bother unless they are producing them specifically for the sake of the *mitzvah*. Dyeing, spinning into a twisted cord, and cutting to the precise right length — each step adds specificity; together they constitute sufficient evidence that the object was prepared *lishmah*.

### New Tefillin Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְכִי אָדָם טוֹרֵחַ לַעֲשׂוֹת קָמֵיעַ כְּמִין תְּפִילִּין? דִּתְנַן: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים, בִּישָׁנוֹת. אֲבָל בַּחֲדָשׁוֹת — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The assertion that people do not exert themselves is problematic, **as** by the same token, **does a person exert** himself **to fashion an amulet in the form of phylacteries?** Nevertheless, the Sages were concerned that an object that appears to be phylacteries might actually be a different object. **As we learned** in a mishna: **In what** case **is this statement** that one is permitted to carry phylacteries inside on Shabbat **said?** It is **with regard to old** phylacteries. **However, with regard to new** ones, **he is exempt** from the obligation to bring them in, as it is possible that they are not phylacteries but amulets in the form of phylacteries.

Rava then raises an obvious question: if that graduated effort is the evidential standard, why can one not apply the same logic to a brand new pair of *tefillin*? Surely the effort involved in producing a pair of *tefillin* — assembling the boxes, inserting the *parshiyos*, tying the straps — vastly exceeds that involved in cutting blue threads to length. *Vechi adam tore'ach la'asot keme'a kemin tefillin?* Would anyone go to the trouble of fabricating an amulet that looks exactly like *tefillin*?

And yet the *mishnah* itself states: *bame de'amru, biyeshanot* — the ruling that one may carry them in applies specifically to old *tefillin*, implying that new-looking *tefillin* do not carry the same presumption. Rav Zeira dispatches his son Ava to cite a *braita* in the *beit midrash* that explicitly states the principle — cut strings are *kasher* because *ein adam tore'ach* — as a way of asserting the foundational rule. Rava is unimpressed: *umishum detani lei Ava brei deRav Zeira, kifenan lei?* — just because Rav Zeira's son recites a *braita*, should we simply capitulate? The *braita* may establish the principle for *techeiles*, but it does not answer why that same principle cannot be applied to brand new *tefillin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא לְאַהֲבָה בְּרֵיהּ: פּוֹק תְּנִי לְהוּ: הַמּוֹצֵא תְּכֵלֶת בַּשּׁוּק, לְשׁוֹנוֹת — פְּסוּלִין, חוּטִין מוּפְסָקִין — כְּשֵׁירִין, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין אָדָם טוֹרֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to his son Ahava: Go out and teach** this *baraita* **to** the Sages. With regard to **one who finds** fabric dyed **sky blue in the marketplace,** if he finds **strips, they are unfit** for use in ritual fringes; however, if he finds **threads cut** into short strings, **they are fit** for use in ritual fringes **because a person does not exert** himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמִשּׁוּם דְּתָנֵי לַהּ אַהֲבָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא כֵּיפֵי תְּלָא לַהּ?! וְהָתְנַן: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּישָׁנוֹת, אֲבָל בַּחֲדָשׁוֹת — פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And because Ahava, son of Rabbi Zeira, taught** this *halakha*, **has he hung** ornamental **rings on** that line of reasoning, i.e., does that constitute an absolute proof? The difficulty posed by Rava from the mishna is not resolved by the *baraita*, **as we learned: In what** case **is this statement** that one is permitted to carry phylacteries inside **said?** It is **with regard to old** phylacteries. **However, with regard to new** ones, **he is exempt.** Apparently, there is concern lest one exert himself to fashion an object similar to one used in a mitzva.

The answer, and the resolution of why new-looking *tefillin* cannot be presumed genuine despite the considerable effort involved in their production, is left as a *cliffhanger* — to be continued on the following *daf*.
