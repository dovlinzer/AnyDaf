# Makkot 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lav Hanitak Le'aseh

### Definition & Examples

We open on daf 16 in the midst of the *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — and this is, as one might say, the welcome-to-Gemara roller coaster. The subject is *lav hanitak le'aseh*: cases in the Torah where a negative commandment is paired with a positive one, such that if the transgressor does the forbidden act, the Torah then instructs him to perform a remedial positive act. The classic illustration is *shiluach haken*: the Torah prohibits taking the mother bird together with her young, and immediately pairs that prohibition with the positive command *shaleach teshalach et ha'em* — you shall send the mother away. Another paradigmatic example, which opens our sugya at the top of daf 16, is the verse in Shemot 12 concerning the Pesach sacrifice: *velo totiru mimenu ad boker*, do not leave over from it until the morning — and yet *vehanotar mimenu ad boker ba'esh tisrofu*, whatever remains until the morning shall be burned in fire. The Torah thus attaches a positive obligation to the negative prohibition.

### Core Dispute

The central dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerns precisely when *malkot* — lashes — are incurred for a *lav hanitak le'aseh*. The basic framework: ordinarily, negative commandments carry lashes. But a *lav* that is linked to a positive commandment does not generate lashes, because the person retains the potential to perform the corrective *aseh*. The question is: at what point does that potential expire, and the lashes kick in?

Reish Lakish holds that as soon as the person has violated the *lav* and has not yet performed the *aseh*, he is already liable. Rabbi Yochanan holds the opposite: only when the possibility of fulfilling the *aseh* has been entirely cancelled — actively and irrevocably — does he become liable for lashes. Until that moment, the *lav* and the *aseh* remain connected, and no lashes apply.

### Lav Categories

To appreciate the structure of the dispute, one must recall two foundational principles. *Lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — a negative commandment whose transgression involves no physical act — does not carry lashes. *Lav she'yesh bo ma'aseh* — a prohibition transgressed through an active deed — does carry lashes. The case of *lav hanitak le'aseh* introduces a third, more complicated category, which is why the sugya demands such careful unpacking.

### Lav + Aseh

The Gemara presents the verse regarding the Pesach sacrifice: *velo totiru mimenu ad boker, vehanotar mimenu ad boker ba'esh tisrofu*. This is adduced as a case of *lav hanitak le'aseh*. The text says *bah hakatuv liten aseh achar lo ta'aseh lomar she'ein lokin alav divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — the Torah came to place a positive act after the negative, to teach that one does not receive lashes for it: these are the words of Rabbi Yehuda. As we shall see, Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish each draw their respective positions from different teachings of Rabbi Yehuda — and a tension between two of his rulings will have to be resolved.

## Rabbi Yochanan

### Pesach Example

Rabbi Yochanan reads the *peshat* of this passage with precision. The Gemara states: *Rabbi Yochanan dayek hachi — ta'ama de'va katuv, ha lo va katuv lokei*. The reason lashes do not apply is specifically because the Torah wrote the positive commandment — *ba'esh tisrofu* — providing a remedial act. Had the Torah not provided this *aseh*, the violator would indeed receive lashes. This implies that it is the existence of the *aseh* — and the ongoing potential to fulfil it — that exempts from *malkot*. The violator retains that exemption as long as the *aseh* can still be performed.

### Hatra'at Safek

This leads directly to the question of *hatra'at safek* — a doubtful or uncertain forewarning. For lashes to apply, *hatra'ah* — a valid warning — is required. In a *lav hanitak le'aseh*, if I warn someone that he is about to transgress the prohibition, my warning is necessarily uncertain: I cannot tell him definitively that he has transgressed, because he still has time to fulfil the *aseh*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, this *hatra'at safek* is nonetheless considered a valid *hatra'ah* — and lashes apply once the *aseh* becomes impossible to fulfil.

## Reish Lakish

### Lav She'ein Bo

Reish Lakish reads the same Gemara differently. The Gemara states: *ve'Reish Lakish dayek hachi — ta'ama de'va katuv, ha lo va katuv lokei*. For Reish Lakish, the reason that lashes apply when the text does not provide a remedial *aseh* is that without it, this is a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — a prohibition violated without a physical act. Ordinarily, *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh ein lokin alav*; yet the Gemara qualifies this, and the discussion proceeds to the further implication: *hatra'at safek*, for Reish Lakish, does *not* constitute a valid *hatra'ah*. These seemingly opposed conclusions — lashes applying earlier in Reish Lakish's view but *hatra'at safek* not being valid — generate the structure of the coming analysis.

### Makeh Aviv

To examine the status of *hatra'at safek*, the Gemara pivots to a case concerning *makeh aviv ve'imo* — one who strikes his father and mother. A *baraita* teaches: *hika zeh ve'chazar ve'hika zeh, kilel zeh ve'chazar ve'kilel zeh, hika shneihem be'vat achat o kilel shneihem be'vat achat — chayav*. The case involves a child whose paternity is uncertain: the mother divorced one man and remarried shortly thereafter, such that when a child was born, it is unclear whether the child belongs to the first or second husband. If this child struck or cursed one putative father and then the other — or struck or cursed both simultaneously — one of them is certainly his father and he is liable. The reason: *hatra'at safek shema hatra'ah* — a doubtful warning counts as a warning.

## Makeh Aviv Case

### Paternity Doubt

The scenario is as follows. A woman was married to, say, Reuven. They divorced, and without waiting the requisite three months for establishing paternity, she remarried — let us say Levi. A child was born. The child may belong to Reuven, in a pregnancy of nine months, or to Levi, in a pregnancy of seven months. This child now strikes Reuven, having been forewarned; returns and strikes Levi; or strikes both simultaneously; or curses one and then the other or both at once. In all these scenarios he is declared *chayav*, because at least one of the two men struck is certainly his father, and the warning given — even if directed at someone who may not be the father — counts as a valid forewarning.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Rule

Rabbi Yehuda, however, introduces a critical distinction: *be'vat achat chayav, zeh achar zeh patur*. If both were struck simultaneously, he is liable; if sequentially, he is exempt. The reason: when the acts are sequential, *hatra'at safek* does not constitute a valid *hatra'ah* — the warning preceding each individual act was directed at a person who may not be the father at all, and that is not a sufficient forewarning. When both are struck simultaneously, the single warning covers a single moment in which one father is certainly being struck.

This ruling stands in tension with the position Reish Lakish derives from Rabbi Yehuda in the context of *lav hanitak le'aseh*. The Gemara thus presents *trei tanna'ei aliba de'Rabbi Yehuda* — two different traditions in the name of Rabbi Yehuda — and resolves: *ei le'Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, trei tanna'ei aliba de'Rabbi Yehuda; ei le'Rabbi Yochanan, lo kashya — ha deideh, ha de'rabeih*. For Reish Lakish, the conflict reflects a genuine dispute among transmitters of Rabbi Yehuda's view. For Rabbi Yochanan, there is no conflict: one ruling reflects Rabbi Yehuda's own position, the other reflects his teacher's.

## Negative Commandment

### Negative Commands

The Gemara now cites a broader principle transmitted through a chain of Amoraim: *Rav Idi bar Avin amar Rav Amram amar Rav Yitzchak amar Rav Yochanan* — and Rav Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Yossi ha-Glili: *kol lo ta'aseh she'ba'Torah* — every negative commandment in the Torah — *lav she'yesh bo ma'aseh lokin alav, lav she'ein bo ma'aseh ein lokin alav*. If transgressed through a physical act, lashes apply; if not through an act, they do not.

### Exceptions

Three explicit exceptions to the general rule that *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* carries no lashes are listed: *chutz min ha'nishba u'meimar ve'ha'mekalel et chaveiro ba'Shem* — one who swears falsely, one who performs *temurah* by substituting one sanctified animal for another, and one who curses his fellow using God's name. These three carry lashes despite involving no physical act.

### Rabbi Yehuda Issue

This generates a *kashya de'Rabbi Yehuda a'de'Rabbi Yehuda* — a contradiction between two positions attributed to Rabbi Yehuda. In the case of *lo totiru*, Rabbi Yehuda rules that no *malkot* apply — it is a *lav hanitak le'aseh*. But in the case of the son striking both putative fathers, *malkot* do seem to apply. Striking another person is *lav she'yesh bo ma'aseh*, and the *hatra'ah* issue works differently in each context. The Gemara registers this tension as requiring resolution.

## Resolution

### Reish Lakish Position

For Reish Lakish, the resolution is that *hatra'at safek* counts as a valid *hatra'ah*. He learns the principle of *lav hanitak le'aseh* from one strand of Rabbi Yehuda's teaching and the principle of *hatra'at safek* from another, treating the apparent contradiction as reflecting different *tanna'im* transmitting Rabbi Yehuda's view.

### Rabbi Yochanan Position

For Rabbi Yochanan, there is no contradiction at all: *lo kashya — ha deideh, ha de'rabeih*. One ruling reflects Rabbi Yehuda's own position, the other reflects his teacher's position, and Rabbi Yochanan consistently attributes each to its proper source. Rabbi Yochanan's specific contribution is understanding the *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* dimension in this context: the striking of the putative father is indeed *lav she'yesh bo ma'aseh*, and the *hatra'at safek* functions as a valid *hatra'ah* in his view.

## Overview Examples

### Two Views

To summarize before proceeding to the cases: as long as the *lav hanitak le'aseh* remains connected to the possibility that the person will yet fulfil the positive commandment — and that possibility has not been cancelled — no lashes apply. This is articulated through the formulas *kiyamo ve'lo kiyamo* (whether he fulfilled or did not fulfil the *aseh*) on Reish Lakish's side, and *bitlo ve'lo bitlo* (whether he actively cancelled or did not cancel the *aseh*) on Rabbi Yochanan's side. For Reish Lakish, the moment the *aseh* is not fulfilled, liability begins. For Rabbi Yochanan, liability begins only when the person has done something to actively and irrevocably foreclose the possibility of fulfilling the *aseh*.

### Four Examples

The Gemara will examine four primary examples of *lav hanitak le'aseh* and test whether each satisfies both frameworks — the *kiyamo ve'lo kiyamo* approach and the *bitlo ve'lo bitlo* approach. The four cases are: *shiluach haken* (sending away the mother bird), *lo tigzol* (robbery and its return), the case of *ones she'gerash* (the rapist who divorces the woman he was obligated to keep), and *pe'ah* (the corner of the field). The critical question in each case is: at what point can the *aseh* no longer be performed — and was that point brought about by the person's own action?

## Shiluach Haken

### Torah Passage

The Gemara cites *tanan hatam — hanotel em al habanim*, and the relevant Torah passage is read: *ki yikarei kan tzippor lefanecha baderech, bechol etz o al ha'aretz, efrochim o beitzim, veha'em rovetzet al ha'efrochim o al habeitzim, lo tikach ha'em al habanim. Shaleach teshalach et ha'em, ve'et habanim tikach lach, lema'an yitav lach veha'arachta yamim*. The structure is clear: a negative commandment — do not take the mother together with the young — paired with a positive commandment — send the mother away. This is the textbook case of *lav hanitak le'aseh*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

*Rabbi Yehuda omer lokeh ve'eino meshaleach*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the violator receives *makot* and does *not* send the mother away. His reasoning is that the order of the verse is to be read in reverse: one is first supposed to send the mother, and only then may one take the young. Under this reading, the *aseh* was supposed to precede the *lav*, and therefore the *aseh* cannot serve as a corrective to the *lav* after the fact — it does not cancel it. Consequently, lashes apply.

### Chachamim

The Chachamim read the verse in its straightforward order: first the prohibition against taking the mother, then the positive command to send her. *Vechachamim omrim meshaleach ve'eino lokeh* — one sends the mother and does not receive lashes. They articulate the general rule: *zeh haklal — kol mitzvat lo ta'aseh sheyesh bah kum aseh ein chayavim aleha* — any negative commandment that carries with it a positive remedial act does not generate liability for lashes, so long as the *aseh* remains available.

### Two Cases

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan — ein lanu ela zot ve'od acheret*. Rabbi Yochanan offers what should be the most reassuring statement in the entire sugya: there are only two cases of *lav hanitak le'aseh* in which *malkot* actually apply, this one and one other. When Rabbi Elazar asked where the other case is, Rabbi Yochanan said: go look for it. *Nafak dak ve'ashkach* — he went, searched, and found it. The rest of the sugya is devoted to identifying why only two cases ultimately result in *malkot*, as all the other candidates fail the *bitlo ve'lo bitlo* test for one reason or another.

## Ones She-Gerash

### Context

The case of *ones she'gerash* — the rapist who divorces the woman he was compelled to marry — is introduced as a candidate for the second case of *lav hanitak le'aseh* where lashes apply. The Torah states *lo tuchal le'shalcha kol yamecha* — you may never send her away for all the days of your life. This negative commandment is paired with an implicit positive: he must maintain her as his wife. While the moral dimension of this case is deeply troubling by modern sensibilities, the Talmudic logic reflects a protective mechanism for the woman in the social context of the ancient world, where a woman who had been violated had severely diminished marriage prospects, and the Torah therefore imposed on the perpetrator a lifelong obligation.

### Israelite vs. Kohen

The Gemara distinguishes: *im yisrael hu — machzir ve'eino lokeh*. If he is an ordinary Israelite, he can bring her back — he can still fulfil the positive *aseh* — and he therefore does not receive lashes. *Ve'im kohen hu — lokeh ve'eino machzir*. If he is a *kohen*, once he has divorced her she becomes a divorcee, and a *kohen* is forbidden to marry a divorcee. He can no longer fulfil the remedial *aseh*, so he receives lashes. This makes it a case where the *aseh* has been cancelled.

### Kiyam Problem

The Gemara asks: this works well for the opinion that follows *kiyamo ve'lo kiyamo* — since the *aseh* is no longer fulfilled, lashes apply. But for the opinion that requires *bitlo ve'lo bitlo* — that the person must have *actively* cancelled the *aseh* — this case poses a difficulty. In *shiluach haken*, the cancellation is clear: if one kills the mother bird, one has actively destroyed the object of the *aseh*. But in *ones she'gerash*, how does one actively cancel the *aseh* in a comparable way? You cannot kill the woman — *de'i de'katla, kam leih biderabbah mineih*, he would be liable for a far graver offense. What act, analogous to killing the bird, forecloses the possibility of ever returning her?

### Solutions

*Amar Rav Shimi me'Chozna — kegon she'kibla kiddushin me'acher*. Rav Shimi suggests: the cancellation occurs when she has accepted *kiddushin* from another man — she is now betrothed to someone else and he can no longer take her back. But this is challenged: *Rav Ashi amar — shavitah shali'ach? Ei hi ka'mavatla leih, ei lo shavitah shali'ach, kol kimeineih ve'lo klum hi*. Did he appoint her as his agent to accept betrothal on her behalf? If she herself is the one nullifying his obligation, that is not *his* act of cancellation. And he cannot have appointed himself an agent to facilitate her betrothal to another, since he has a prior binding obligation to her.

An alternative solution is then offered: *ela amar Rav Shimi mi'Naharde'a — kegon she'hadira b'rabim*. What if he made a public vow — a *neder b'rabim* — that he will never take her back? This vow, if irrevocable, would foreclose the *aseh*.

## Vow Annulment

### Neder Types

The Gemara distinguishes between two types of vow: *neder she'hudar b'rabim* — a vow made publicly — and *neder al da'at rabim* — a vow made with the agreement or understanding of the community, incorporating the community's will into its very fabric. The question is which of these cannot be annulled.

### Ameimar

*De'amar Ameimar: hilcheta — neder she'hudar b'rabim yesh lo hafarah; al da'at rabim ein lo hafarah*. Ameimar's ruling: a vow made publicly *can* be annulled — one can return to the public setting and undo it. But a vow made *al da'at rabim* — with the community's assent incorporated into its very terms — cannot be annulled, since it is no longer the individual's vow alone to retract. It is only this latter type that would permanently foreclose the man's ability to fulfil the *aseh* of taking back the woman he violated.

## Gezel, Mashkon

### Gezel

The Gemara asks: are there not more examples? *Ve'ha'ika siman — gezel, mashkon, u'fe'ah*. Taking these in order: *gezel* — the Torah says *lo tigzol*, do not rob, and then *ve'heshiv et ha'gezeilah*, return what you have taken. This is a *lav hanitak le'aseh*, and it works straightforwardly for both the *kiyamo ve'lo kiyamo* and *bitlo ve'lo bitlo* frameworks.

### Mashkon

*Mashkon*: the Torah says *lo tavo el beito la'avot avoto, hashev tashiv lo ha'avot k'vo ha'shemesh* — do not enter the poor person's home to seize his collateral, but return it to him at sunset. Again, a negative paired with a positive. *U'mashkachat lah be'kiymo ve'lo kiymo u'vitlo ve'lo bitlo* — this case can be found to work under both formulas. However, a complication arises: *hatam keivan de'chayav be'tashlumin ein lokeh u'meshalem* — since the violator is liable for monetary restitution, he cannot receive both lashes and payment. The rule is that one does not receive both *malkot* and *tashlumim* (with the sole exception of *motzi shem ra*). Therefore *gezel* and *mashkon* may not be the best examples — the person can always pay the monetary equivalent, and the *aseh* is never truly foreclosed.

### Ger Dies

*Matkif lah Rabbi Zeira: ha'ika mashkono shel ger u'met ha'ger* — what about the collateral of a *ger* who dies? A convert who dies leaves no halachic heirs; his estate is ownerless. If someone holds a *ger*'s collateral and the *ger* dies, the collateral cannot be returned — to whom would one return it? The possibility of fulfilling the *aseh* has been foreclosed.

### Not Bitul

The Gemara responds: *hatam gavra bar tashlumi hu, ve'shibuda de'gera hu de'ka paka* — the person was always liable for monetary payment, and it is the *ger*'s death that dissolves the lien. The point is that the *ger*'s death was not caused by the violator's own action. Unlike the case of killing the mother bird — where the person actively destroys the object of the *aseh* — the *ger* dying on him is not something he did. Furthermore, even if one destroyed the *mashkon* itself, one can still pay its monetary value, meaning the *aseh* is never truly, actively cancelled by the person. *Gezel* and *mashkon* therefore fail the *bitlo ve'lo bitlo* test, and do not generate *malkot* under Rabbi Yochanan's framework.

## Pe'ah

### Torah Requirement

The Gemara then turns to *pe'ah*: *ve'ha'ika pe'ah de'rachmana amar lo techaleh pe'at sadecha... le'ani ve'la'ger ta'azov otam* — do not destroy the corner of your field; leave it to the poor person and to the stranger. Again, a negative and a positive. *De'mashkachat lah be'kiymo ve'lo kiymo, bitlo ve'lo bitlo* — this case, the Gemara suggests, can work under both formulations.

### Time Framework

The question is: how much time does the person have to fulfil the *aseh*, and when is it actively foreclosed? The Gemara cites a *mishnah*: *mitzvat pe'ah le'hafrish min ha'kamah* — ideally, the *pe'ah* is given from the standing grain. *Lo hifrish min ha'kamah — mafrish min ha'omerim* — if he cut the whole field without leaving a corner, he may give from the sheaves. *Lo hifrish min ha'omerim — mafrish min ha'keri ad she'lo mireach* — he may give from the pile of grain before it is smoothed and rendered liable for tithes.

### Making Dough

*Mircho — ma'aser ve'noten lo*: once it has been processed to the point of *miru'ach*, it must be tithed, and the tithe goes to the Levi. But can one still give *pe'ah* at this stage? The discussion presses further: when does the window definitively close?

### Rabbi Yishmael

*Ki de'Rabbi Yishmael de'amar: af mafrish min ha'isah* — Rabbi Yishmael extends the obligation even further, holding that one may still give *pe'ah* from the dough. By this point, the grain has been transformed into a fundamentally different substance — no longer standing sheaves, no longer loose grain, but *isah*, dough.

### Challah Exclusion

*Ve'de'Rabbi Yishmael nami mashkachat lah de'achal isah* — but even within Rabbi Yishmael's extended framework, one can find a case of active cancellation: if the dough has been rendered *challah* — set aside as *challah* and thus subject to the prohibition of consumption without separation — then one can no longer distribute it to the poor as *pe'ah*. The form has been changed so fundamentally, and a new *issur* has been created, that the *aseh* of *pe'ah* is now actively foreclosed. This is the *bitul* we have been looking for.

## Conclusion

### Only Two Cases

*Ela zot ve'od acheret* — and so Rabbi Yochanan is vindicated. After examining all the candidates — *ones she'gerash*, *gezel*, *mashkon*, and *pe'ah* — we are left with exactly two cases of *lav hanitak le'aseh* in which a person can genuinely, actively foreclose the *aseh* and thereby incur *malkot*. The first is *shiluach haken*: if one kills the mother bird, one has destroyed the very object of the positive commandment and can never fulfil it. The second is *pe'ah*: if one processes the grain all the way to dough and then renders it *challah*, one has actively transformed the *pe'ah* into something that can no longer be distributed as required. In all other cases, either the *aseh* remains perpetually available (and no *bitul* is possible), or any apparent *bitul* is not attributable to the person's own act.

## Vow Annulment (II)

### Teacher Case

There remains one loose end from the case of *ones she'gerash*: we concluded that a *neder al da'at rabim* could theoretically foreclose the *aseh*, making it a third case of *malkot* — yet Rabbi Yochanan said there are only two. The resolution: even a *neder al da'at rabim* can be annulled when the matter involves a *dvar mitzvah*. The Gemara illustrates this with a famous story: *ki ha de'hahu makrei dardekei de'hava pasha beinokei* — there was a teacher of young children who was harsh and neglectful with his students. Rav Acha imposed a *heider* on him, barring him from teaching. Later, Ravina reinstated him, because no teacher of equivalent skill could be found — *de'lo ishtakach de'dayek kavatei*. Since the children's education constitutes a *dvar mitzvah*, the *neder* could be annulled, and the man was reinstated. The same logic applies to *ones she'gerash*: even if the man took a *neder al da'at rabim* never to take her back, since taking her back is itself a *dvar mitzvah* — the positive commandment *lo tuchal le'shalcha* — the *neder* can be annulled, and the *aseh* remains theoretically available. Therefore, this case does not generate *malkot*, and we are left with only two.

## Eating Prohibited

### General Rule

The Mishnah returns to the broader category of those who receive lashes: *ha'ochel nevelot u'treifot shekatzim u'remasim* — one who eats animals that died without proper *shechita*, *treif* animals, *shekatzim* and *remasim*, the various categories of prohibited creeping things. The Gemara now examines specific cases within this category, moving from the general to the particular.

### Binta

*Amar Rav Yehuda: hai man de'achal binta devei karva — malkin leih mishum sheretz hashortetz al ha'aretz*. Rav Yehuda rules that one who eats the *binta* — a small creature found inside cabbage — receives lashes on account of the verse *kol sheretz hashortetz al ha'aretz sheketz hu lo ye'achel*, in Vayikra 11. A subsequent report notes: *hahu de'achal binta devei karava — venagdei Rav Yehuda*, indicating that Rav Yehuda actually administered lashes in such a case.

Tosafot raises the obvious question: if this is simply a *sheretz gamur*, why does the Gemara need to tell us? The answer Tosafot provides is that the *binta* may be a small fish — *kemo dag katan hanimtza bamachreshah* — a creature that, had it been found in water, would have been permitted because it possesses *kaskasim*, scales. Found outside the water, however — inside the ground or inside a head of cabbage — it is no longer a kosher fish. The novelty is that one might have thought its former status as a potentially kosher species exempts it; the Gemara teaches that it does not, and *malkot* apply.

### Sheratzim Lashes

*Amar Abaye: achal putita — lokei arba'ah*. One who swallows a *putita* whole — which Rashi identifies as a water creature — receives four sets of lashes. *Achal nemalah — lokei chamesh mishum sheretz hashortetz al ha'aretz*. Eating an ant incurs five sets of lashes. *Tzirah — lokei shesh mishum sheretz ha'of*: eating a wasp incurs six sets of lashes, with the additional count coming from the category of *sheretz ha'of*, flying creeping things. The reason for swallowing whole is that the laws of *kezayit* — the minimum volume for liability — apply when eating pieces; a whole living creature, however, constitutes a *biryah* and generates its own liability regardless of size.

### Lo Teshaktzu

*Amar Rav Achai: hamash'he et nekavav over mishum lo teshaktzu* — one who unduly delays attending to bodily needs transgresses the prohibition *lo teshaktzu*, do not make yourselves abominable. Whether this transgression generates *malkot* is implied by the context.

### Bloodletter Vessel

*Amar Rav Bibi bar Abaye: hai man de'shati be'karna de'umna — ka'avar mishum de'lo teshaktzu*. One who drinks from the vessel of a bloodletter — a vessel used in the course of medical bloodletting — also transgresses *lo teshaktzu* by virtue of the disgusting nature of the act.

### Smashed Ants

*Amar Rava bar Rav Huna: rissek tesha nemalim ve'hevi echad chai ve'hishliman le'kezayit — lokei vav*. If one crushed nine ants and added one living ant, bringing the total to a *kezayit*, he receives six sets of lashes. The breakdown: *chamesh mishum biryah* — five for the whole creature (*biryah*), and one for the *kezayit* of the crushed ants.

### Quantity Dispute

*Neveilah — Rabba amar Rabbi Yochanan: afilu shenayim vehu. Rabbi Yosef amar: afilu echad vehu. Ve'lo paligi — ha be'rabbata, ha be'zutarta* — Rabba citing Rabbi Yochanan says the case involves two crushed creatures; Rabbi Yosef says even one. The Gemara resolves: they do not actually disagree. One ruling refers to large ants, the other to small ones. The practical upshot: one must not eat revolting things. The halachic scaffolding in which one receives six *malkot* and requires prior warning for each — while vivid — reflects the Gemara's systematic precision in applying the principles of liability to every conceivable case.

## Tevel & Tithe

### Tevel Maaser

*Achal tevel u'maaser rishon* — among those who receive lashes are those who eat *tevel*, untithed produce, and *maaser rishon*, first tithe. *Amar Rav: achal tevel shel maaser ani — lokeh* — one who eats produce that should have been given as *maaser ani*, the poor tithe, receives lashes.

### Rabbi Yosi

*Amar Rabbi Yosi: yachol lo yehei chayav ela al ha'tevel she'lo huram mimenu kol ikkar* — one might have thought that liability arises only for produce from which no separation whatsoever has been made. But Rabbi Yosi extends the liability: even if *terumah gedolah* was separated but not *maaser rishon*, or *maaser rishon* but not *maaser sheni*, or even if *maaser ani* specifically was not separated — in all these cases, the produce retains its status as *tevel* and may not be eaten.

### Tithe Obligation

The textual derivation proceeds through a *gezerah shavah*: the verse states *lo tuchal le'echol bish'arecha* — you may not eat within your gates — and elsewhere the Torah states *ve'achlu bish'arecha ve'save'u* in reference to *maaser ani*. The verbal parallel between *bish'arecha* in the prohibition and *bish'arecha* in the description of *maaser ani* links the two: the prohibition of eating *tevel* extends to produce from which *maaser ani* has not been separated. *Ve'amar Rachmana lo tuchal* — and the Torah says you may not. The presence of this negative commandment is what generates *malkot* for eating such produce.
