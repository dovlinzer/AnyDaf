# Menachot 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pesach & Non-Sanctified

### Intro: Pesach Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְתַרְבִּיצָא אֲמוּר: וְכִי דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ? בִּכְלָלָא אִיתְּמַר, פֶּסַח כּוּלֵּיהּ חֲדָא מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And in the study hall [*uvetarbitza*]** they discussed the statement of the mishna that the *halakha* that all obligatory offerings must be brought from non-sacred property is derived from the Paschal offering of the generations, and **they said: But can a matter derived via juxtaposition,** i.e., the Paschal offering of the generations, whose *halakha* is derived by juxtaposition from the Paschal offering in Egypt, **again teach a matter via** another **juxtaposition?** The Gemara responds: This is not considered an instance of a matter derived by juxtaposition teaching a matter derived by another juxtaposition. Rather, the statement of the mishna that all offerings are juxtaposed to the Paschal offering **was stated in a general** manner, since **the entire Paschal offering is one matter.** The Paschal offering in Egypt and the Paschal offering of the generations are both referred to as a Paschal offering.

The daf opens in the middle of a sustained debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva concerning the source for the principle that a *korban pesach* may only be purchased from non-sanctified, non-ma'aser sheini funds. Rabbi Eliezer derives this rule from the *Pesach Mitzrayim*, while Rabbi Akiva rejects that inference on the grounds that one cannot apply a principle derived from a *ma'aseh she-ee efshar* — a situation that was impossible under normal circumstances — to a *ma'aseh efshar*, a situation that is entirely possible. The Gemara had already established that Rabbi Eliezer's derivation rests on an explicit hekesh linking the two cases, and invokes the famous principle *Ein meshivin al ha-hekesh*: once a biblical linkage is explicit, objections based on logical inconsistency no longer apply.

### Chozer u'Melamed

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara now wraps up this discussion with a question posed u've-tarbitza — in the academy, among the students, in the sense of *harbatzat Torah*. The question is: ve-chi davar ha-lamed be-hekesh chozer u-melamed be-hekesh? We learned *Pesach Dorot* from *Pesach Mitzrayim* through a hekesh; can we then take *Pesach Dorot* and apply its principles to all other korbanot through another hekesh? The Gemara answers: be-khulala it-mar — it was stated as a general category. Kula chada milta hi: all instances of *korban pesach* constitute a single unified category. Even though a hekesh was required to extend the law from *Pesach Mitzrayim* to *Pesach Dorot* — because the one applied only once in history while the other is a general ongoing obligation — the derivation does not constitute daisy-chaining from A to B to C. Rather, it is a single category being made whole, and from that unified Pesach category one may legitimately extend principles to all other korbanot.

### Rabbi Akiva's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, פֶּסַח דְּאֵינוֹ בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהָא דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה לָעֹלָה וְלַמִּנְחָה וְלַחַטָּאת וְלָאָשָׁם וְלַמִּלּוּאִים וּלְזֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **Rabbi Akiva,** who rejects the proofs of Rabbi Eliezer, **from where does he** derive **that the Paschal offering comes only from non-sacred** money? The Gemara responds: Rabbi Akiva **derives it from that which Shmuel said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer:** It is stated: **"This is the law of the burnt offering, of the meal offering, and of the sin offering, and of the guilt offering, and of the inauguration offering, and of the sacrifice of peace offerings"** (Leviticus 7:37). This verse connects all of the specified offerings, such that individual aspects of each offering are applicable to all of the offerings.

Rabbi Akiva does not dispute the ruling itself — that a Pesach, and by extension all obligatory korbanot, must come from non-ma'aser sheini funds. His disagreement is solely with the source. He cannot learn it from *Pesach Mitzrayim*, which is a she-ee efshar case. Instead, the Gemara explains, nafka lei me-ha d'amar Shmuel mishum Rabbi Elazar — he derives it from a statement of Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Elazar. That statement will emerge from a general pasuk that simultaneously teaches numerous laws shared across all korbanot: *Zot ha-Torah la-olah ve-la-minchah ve-la-chatat ve-la-asham ve-la-miluim u-le-zeivach* ha-shelamim. The Gemara will mine each entry in that list for a law that is explicit in one korban and then, by virtue of the pasuk's linkage, applied to all others. Notably, an almost identical exposition of this same pasuk appears near the end of Masechet Zevachim — the same literary phenomenon by which a summing-up verse generates a comprehensive set of shared halakhot toward the close of the tractate.

## Zot HaTorah Pasuk

### Zot HaTorah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עוֹלָה״ – מָה עוֹלָה טְעוּנָה כְּלִי, אַף כֹּל טָעוּן כְּלִי. מַאי הִיא? אִילֵימָא מִזְרָק, גַּבֵּי זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִיבּוּר נָמֵי כְּתִיב ״וַיָּשֶׂם בָּאַגָּנֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara details these aspects. The verse states: "Of the **burnt offering,"** to teach that all of the offerings are like a burnt offering in that **just as a burnt offering requires a utensil** in its preparation, **so too do all** animal offerings **require a utensil. What** is the **utensil? If we say** it is **a bowl,** a utensil used for collecting the blood, as is learned from the burnt offerings that were sacrificed at Mount Sinai, that cannot be correct, as a utensil for collecting blood does not need to be learned from a burnt offering. **With regard to communal peace offerings it is also written:** "And they offered burnt offerings, and they sacrificed peace offerings…**And Moses took half of the blood and put it in basins"** (Exodus 24:5–6).

The Gemara begins with olah: mah olah te'unah keli — just as an olah requires a vessel, so too every korban requires a vessel. The Gemara first considers whether the vessel in question is a mizrak, a bowl for catching the blood. After Har Sinai, it says, the Torah records that they slaughtered olot and shelamim and placed the blood ba-aganot — in bowls — and from there one might derive that blood-catching requires a vessel. The Gemara objects: but that is written by shelamim as well, not uniquely by the olah. The vessel we are focused on, then, is something specific to the olah: the sakin, the slaughter knife.

### Olah: Knife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא סַכִּין, וְעוֹלָה גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁלַח אַבְרָהָם אֶת יָדוֹ וַיִּקַּח אֶת הַמַּאֲכֶלֶת לִשְׁחֹט אֶת בְּנוֹ״, וְהָתָם עוֹלָה הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעֲלֵהוּ לְעֹלָה תַּחַת בְּנוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the term utensil must be referring to **a knife,** as the slaughter may be performed only with a knife and not with a sharp stone or reed. The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **a burnt offering itself, from where do we** derive that it must be slaughtered with a knife? This is learned from **that which is written: "And Abraham stretched forth his hand, and took the knife to slaughter his son"** (Genesis 22:10); **and there,** Abraham was sacrificing **a burnt offering, as it is written: "And offered it up for a burnt offering instead of his son"** (Genesis 22:13).

The Gemara then asks: ulo'ah gufah minalan — how do we know the olah itself required a knife-vessel? The source is va-yishlach Avraham et yado va-yikach et ha-machelet lishchot et bno: Abraham reached out his hand and took the machelet — a notably graphic term for a knife, one that evokes the act of cutting into flesh — to slaughter his son. The Gemara establishes that Yitzchak was being treated as an olah: after the akeida, the Torah says regarding the ram va-ya'alehu le-olah tachat bno, he offered it as an olah in place of his son, which confirms that the son had been intended as an olah. Since Abraham used a knife for what was being treated as an olah offering, we derive that an olah requires a knife.

This source is striking — and more than a little disturbing. The derivation of korban law from the akeida is unavoidable: olah tachat bno makes the substitutionary logic explicit. The Gemara does not shy away from this, even as we might prefer to bracket it.

A closer look reveals that this passage also bears on the question from Masechet Chulin of whether the slaughter knife must be detached — mechubar le-karka — and we learn from Abraham that it must be. But our Gemara is adding something beyond mere detachment. The word kli suggests that for a korban, the knife must meet the standard of a vessel — something fashioned to function as a knife — as opposed to, say, a sharpened rock. Tosafos raises a further question: does the korban requirement extend to a kli sharet, a sanctified vessel? The Gemara here says only kli, not kli sharet, and it is not obvious from the akeida passage that the machelet was sanctified. Tosafos attempts to derive it from an extra word but acknowledges the difficulty. In practice, there are three distinct levels: the general shechitah requirement that the knife be detached; the korban requirement that it be a proper fashioned vessel; and the debated requirement among Rishonim that it also be a kli sharet.

### Abraham & Isaac

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִנְחָה״ – מָה מִנְחָה אֵינָהּ נֶאֱכֶלֶת אֶלָּא לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה, אַף כֹּל אֵין נֶאֱכָלִין אֶלָּא לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה. מַאי? אִי חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם – בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to expound the aforementioned verse (Leviticus 7:37). When the verse mentions **a meal offering,** it teaches that **just as a meal offering is eaten only by males of** the **priesthood** (see Leviticus 6:9–11), **so too are all** of the offerings mentioned in this verse **eaten only by males of** the **priesthood.** The Gemara asks: With regard to **what** offering **is it** that this *halakha* must be derived? **If** one suggests that it is with regard to the **sin offering and** the **guilt offering,** this *halakha* **is explicitly written** of them. With regard to the sin offering, it is stated: "Every male among the priests may eat it" (Leviticus 6:22), and with regard to the guilt offering, it is stated: "Every male among the priests may eat of it" (Leviticus 7:6).

The Gemara proceeds to minchah: mah minchah einah ne'echelet ela le-zichrei kehunah — just as a minchah is eaten only by male kohanim, so too af kol eino ne'echal ela le-zichrei kehunah — anything from among the korbanot that goes to the kohanim is restricted to the male kohanim. This differs from terumah, which the kohen may feed to his entire household including women and children; the portions of the korbanot allocated to kohanim are reserved for the males exclusively.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִיבּוּר – מֵרִבּוּיָא דִּקְרָאֵי אָתֵי: ״בְּקֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ כׇּל זָכָר (בַּכֹּהֲנִים) יֹאכַל אֹתוֹ״, לִימֵּד עַל זִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִיבּוּר שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱכָלִים אֶלָּא לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **If** one suggests that the *halakha* must be derived with regard to **communal peace offerings,** i.e., the two lambs that were sacrificed as communal offerings on *Shavuot* together with the offering of the two loaves (see Leviticus 23:19), this *halakha* **is derived from** the **amplification of the verse** stated with regard to meal offerings, sin offerings, and guilt offerings. The verse states: **"In a most sacred place shall you eat of it; every male may eat it"** (Numbers 18:10), and it is taught in a *baraita*: The verse **teaches with regard to communal peace offerings that they are eaten only by males of priestly** families.

The Gemara then asks: by which korban is this actually needed? If we are speaking of a chatat or asham, the law is stated explicitly in the relevant pesukim. The one case where this is not explicit is zivchei *shalmei tzibbur* — the communal shelamim, a category whose only real member in practice is the *kivsei atzeret*, the lambs of Shavuot, since these go to the kohanim rather than to the owners. The Gemara responds that me-ribuyah de-kra atya — we actually derive this from an extra expression in the pasuk in *Parshat Korach*, where in reiterating all the priestly entitlements the Torah says kol zachar yochal oto after already having said tochelen — the repetition of "eat it" comes to include *shalmei tzibbur* among those eaten only by male kohanim. The Gemara therefore concludes tannai hi: it is a tannaic dispute as to which pasuk one learns this from — some derive it from the minchah entry in Zot HaTorah, others from the extra formulation in *Parshat Korach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, אִיכָּא דְּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא, וְאִיכָּא דְּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **It is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. There is** one *tanna* **who cites it,** the *halakha* that only males of priestly families may eat of the communal peace offering, **from here,** i.e., the precedent mentioned explicitly with regard to the meal offering; **and there is** one *tanna* **who cites it from there,** i.e., the amplification of the verse stated with regard to meal offerings, sin offerings, and guilt offerings.

### Mincha: Male Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חַטָּאת״ – מָה חַטָּאת מְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבִלּוּעַ, אַף כֹּל מְקַדֵּשׁ בְּבִלּוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues expounding the verse: "This is the law of the burnt offering, of the meal offering, and of the sin offering, and of the guilt offering, and of the inauguration offering, and of the sacrifice of peace offerings." **"Sin offering"** teaches: **Just as** with regard to **a sin offering,** whatever it touches **is sanctified through** the substance that becomes **absorbed, so too** for **all** offerings mentioned in this verse, whatever they touch **is sanctified through** the **absorbed** portions.

Ve-la-chatat: mah chatat mekadesh be-valua af kol mekadesh be-valua. Just as a chatat sanctifies by absorption, so too any korban sanctifies through absorption. The Torah states by the chatat: kol asher yiga be-visarah yikdash — anything that touches the flesh of the chatat becomes holy. The Gemara interprets this as yikdash lihyot kamoha: it becomes holy in the same mode as the chatat itself. If the chatat was valid, an item that absorbed some of its juices adopts its restrictions — for instance, if the chatat was a one-day sacrifice, the item that absorbed from it may only be eaten for one day. Ve-im pasul, if the chatat was itself invalid, then tipasel — the other item also becomes invalid. This is the principle of mekadesh be-valua: sanctification through being absorbed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב בְּחַטָּאת, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב בְּמִנְחָה. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּמִנְחָה – מִשּׁוּם דְּרַכִּיכָא בָּלְעָה, אֲבָל חַטָּאת אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּחַטָּאת – מִשּׁוּם דְּבָשָׂר אַגַּב דְּשַׁמִּין (קָדִיר) [קָרִיר], אֲבָל מִנְחָה אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And it was necessary to write** the *halakha* of absorption **with regard to a sin offering, and it was necessary to write** the *halakha* of absorption **with regard to a meal offering. As, had the Merciful One written** this *halakha* only **with regard to a meal offering,** I would say **that since it is soft, it is absorbed** and therefore sanctifies what it touches; **but** with regard to the meat of **a sin offering, I would say** that it does **not** sanctify what it touches. **And had the Merciful One written** this *halakha* only **with regard to a sin offering,** I would say that it is **because, on account of its fattiness, the meat penetrates [*kadeir*]** into whatever it touches and sanctifies it; **but** with regard to **a meal offering, I would say** that it does **not** sanctify what it touches. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the Torah to write both.

One might ask why this is a special korban principle at all, since the general laws of mixtures already mandate that when something is not batel it conveys the more stringent status. The answer is that by korbanot this applies even in cases where under ordinary mixture principles the forbidden substance would have been batel — the Torah overrides the normal threshold. That expanded stringency is the chiddush of mekadesh be-valua, and af kol mekadesh be-valua extends it to all korbanot.

### Chatat: Sanctifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָשָׁם״ – מָה אָשָׁם אֵין שָׁפִיר וְשִׁלְיָא קָדוֹשׁ בּוֹ, אַף כֹּל אֵין שָׁפִיר וְשִׁלְיָא קָדוֹשׁ בּוֹ. קָסָבַר: וַלְדוֹת קָדָשִׁים בַּהֲוָיָיתָן הֵן קְדוֹשִׁים, וְדָנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **"Guilt offering"** teaches: **Just as** with regard to **a guilt offering, a fetal sac and a placenta are not sacred within it,** because a guilt offering is always male and as such never holds a fetal sac or a placenta, **so too** for **any** of the offerings mentioned in the verse, **a fetal sac and a placenta are not sacred** if found **within it.** The Gemara notes: Evidently, this *tanna* **holds** that with regard to **offspring of sacrificial** animals, **they are sanctified** only **as they are** from the moment of their births, but not in utero. **And** he also holds that **one derives** the **possible from** the **impossible,** so that the *halakha* of a fetal sac and a placenta in the case of female animals may be derived from the *halakha* of a male animal.

Ve-la-asham: mah asham ein shapir ve-shilya kadosh bo — just as by an asham the placenta and embryo carry no sanctity, af kol ein shapir ve-shilya kadosh bo — by no korban do the placenta and embryo carry sanctity. The logic is that an asham must be male, so a placenta and embryo could never exist in an asham in the first place. Applying this to a female korban like a chatat, we learn that even there, if one finds an embryo or placenta inside the animal, it possesses no kedushah: it need not be offered on the mizbeach, and it has none of the attendant restrictions of korban meat. As Rashi notes in Zevachim, the question of whether one must even remove the kidneys and fat of the embryo is itself resolved — the answer is no, since the embryo has no sanctity whatsoever.

The Gemara specifies the two assumptions underlying this derivation: first, that veladot kodashim be-havayatan hen kedoshin — the offspring of a consecrated animal acquires its kedushah only upon birth, not while still in the womb. Second, ve-danin efshar mi-she-ee efshar — one may derive a law applicable in a possible case from a situation that is impossible. This second assumption, notably, is the very principle Rabbi Akiva rejects in the earlier debate. The Gemara is careful to note that the entire braita here is amar Shmuel mishum Rabbi Elazar, i.e., it proceeds from the school of Rabbi Eliezer, who does accept danin efshar mi-she-ee efshar. Rabbi Akiva's own derivations will be addressed separately.

### Asham: No Embryo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִלּוּאִים״ – מָה מִלּוּאִים מוֹתְרֵיהֶן בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, וְאֵין בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים מוֹתְרֵיהֶן בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, אַף כׇּל מוֹתְרֵיהֶן בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, וְאֵין בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים מוֹתְרֵיהֶן בִּשְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Inauguration offering"** teaches: **Just as** with regard to the **inauguration offering,** the rams and the bread of which were brought during the seven days of inauguration of the Tabernacle and which the priests ate, **their leftovers** were disposed of **by incineration,** as is stated: "And if any of the flesh of the inauguration offering, or of the bread, remain until the morning, then you shall burn the remainder with fire" (Exodus 29:34), **and** there were **no living animals** counted **among their leftovers** disposed of **by incineration, so too** for **all** offerings mentioned, **their leftovers are** disposed of **by incineration, and there are no living animals** counted **among their leftovers** disposed of **by incineration.** Accordingly, if one sanctifies two animals so that either one may be brought if the other is lost, when one animal is sacrificed, the surviving animal is not killed and incinerated.

Ve-la-miluim: mah miluim mosreihen bi-sreifa — just as by the miluim sacrifices, the leftover meat is burned, ve-ein ba'alei chayim mosreihen bi-sreifa — but live animals are not burned as notar. The Torah says explicitly to Aaron and his sons by the miluim: if anything is left over from the meat, burn it in the fire. While the law of notar applies broadly, the explicit command to burn notar appears not by every case but specifically here by the miluim. By virtue of Zot HaTorah, this applies to all korbanot: af kol mosreihen bi-sreifa — notar meat of any korban is burned.

The second half of the rule — that live animals are not treated as notar subject to burning — arises from the case of a korban that becomes "leftover" not as meat but as a live animal. The classic instance is when one designates a replacement for a lost korban, then recovers the original: one of the two is a kind of leftover. Rashi notes that the Torah never actually addresses the case of burning a live animal by the miluim; shelo matzinu sreifa ba-miluim ela be-vasar le-achar shechitah. The Torah speaks only of burning the remaining meat after shechitah, not of any living animal. So this too is in the category of she-ee efshar — the case simply does not arise. The rule is therefore: notar meat is burned across all korbanot; this does not extend to a live animal that has been rendered "leftover."

### Miluim: Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁלָמִים״ – מָה שְׁלָמִים מְפַגְּלִין וּמִתְפַּגְּלִין, אַף כֹּל מְפַגְּלִין וּמִתְפַּגְּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **"Peace offering"** teaches: **Just as** with regard to the **peace offering,** its components may **render** components of the offering ***piggul* and** are **rendered *piggul*, so too** with regard to **all** offerings mentioned in this verse, their components **render** components of the offering ***piggul* and are rendered *piggul*.**

The final term in the pasuk is ul-zeivach ha-shelamim: mah shelamim mifaglim u-mitfaglim — just as shelamim both creates piggul and becomes piggul itself, so too af kol mifaglim u-mitfaglim. The paradigm case of piggul in the Torah is the shelamim, and from there the principle extends to all korbanot.

Tosafos explains the formulation mifaglim u-mitfaglim in light of the fundamental structure of piggul: for piggul to apply, there must be two distinct components — an act being performed (such as zerikah, the sprinkling of the blood) that is matir, i.e., permits, something else (such as the basar, the meat). If a kohen sprinkles the blood with intent to eat the meat outside its proper time, that invalid intent is mefagel the basar. This is why a *minchat kohen*, which is entirely burned and has no portion to be "permitted" by the burning of the kometz, cannot have piggul — there is no two-part structure. The expression mifaglim u-mitfaglim therefore means: it creates piggul in the remaining portion, and correspondingly that portion itself becomes piggul. This applies to all korbanot that share the two-part structure.

## Rabbi Akiva's Chatat

### Shelamim: Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה כּוּ׳ מִנְחָה״ – מָה מִנְחָה מְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבִלּוּעַ, אַף כֹּל מְקַדֶּשֶׁת בְּבִלּוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Akiva** that the verse states: **"This is the law** of the burnt offering, of the meal offering, and of the sin offering, and of the guilt offering, and of the inauguration offering, and of the sacrifice of peace offerings" (Leviticus 7:37). From the term **"meal offering"** it is derived: **Just as** with regard to **a meal offering,** whatever it touches **is sanctified through** the substance that becomes **absorbed,** as it is stated: "Whatever shall touch them shall be sacred" (Leviticus 6:11), **so too** for **all** offerings mentioned in this verse, whatever they touch **is sanctified through** the **absorbed** portions.

The Gemara now introduces a parallel braita: be-matnitin tana mishmeih de-Rabbi Akiva — taught in the name of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva proceeds through the same pasuk of Zot HaTorah but draws different lessons from each term. The olah entry is apparently skipped, perhaps because the knife-vessel law is the same; we proceed directly to minchah.

Rabbi Akiva learns mekadesh be-valua — sanctification through absorption — not from the chatat (as Rabbi Eliezer's tradition did) but from the minchah, where it is equally written: kol asher yiga bahem yikdash. The Gemara then asks why the Torah found it necessary to write the rule both by the chatat and by the minchah. The answer is that each case carries a unique rationale that would not have been extended to the other: had the Torah written it only by the minchah, one might have thought the principle applies because the minchah is soft and readily absorbs — mishum de-rachicha balah — but that a chatat, being meat, would not. Had the Torah written it only by the chatat, one might have thought the principle applies because meat is fatty and penetrates — mishum de-vasar agav de-shamein kadir — but that a minchah, being soft and flour-like, would not. Tzricha: both were required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב בְּחַטָּאת, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב בְּמִנְחָה. דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּמִנְחָה – מִשּׁוּם דְּרַכִּיכָא בָּלְעָה, אֲבָל חַטָּאת אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּחַטָּאת – מִשּׁוּם דְּבָשָׂר אַגַּב דְּשַׁמִּין (קָדִיר) [קָרִיר], אֲבָל מִנְחָה אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And it was necessary to write** the *halakha* of absorption **with regard to a sin offering, and it was necessary to write** the *halakha* of absorption **with regard to a meal offering. As, had the Merciful One written** this *halakha* only **with regard to a meal offering,** I would say **that since it is soft, it is absorbed** and therefore sanctifies what it touches; **but** with regard to the meat of **a sin offering, I would say** that it does **not** sanctify what it touches. **And had the Merciful One written** this *halakha* only **with regard to a sin offering,** I would say that it is **because, on account of its fattiness, the meat penetrates [*kadeir*]** into whatever it touches and sanctifies it; **but** with regard to **a meal offering, I would say** that it does **not** sanctify what it touches. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the Torah to write both.

One difficulty remains in this reasoning. The rationale offered for the minchah is that "it absorbs" — but the direction of transfer in mekadesh be-valua is that the minchah's juices penetrate something else, not that the minchah takes on substance from something else. A minchah's softness would make it more susceptible to absorbing from other things, which is actually the lesser chiddush. The chiddush is precisely that when a drop of minchah gets absorbed into something else, the minchah's kedushahstatus follows it. The Gemara's formulation here — de-rachicha balah — does not map cleanly onto that direction, and the precise logic remains difficult.

### Akiva's Chatat Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חַטָּאת״ – מָה חַטָּאת אֵינָהּ בָּאָה אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין, וּבַיּוֹם, וּבְיָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית, אַף כֹּל אֵינוֹ בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין, וּבַיּוֹם, וּבְיָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית.
>
> **Translation:** The cited *baraita* continues: **"Sin offering"** teaches: **Just as a sin offering is brought only from non-sacred** animals, **and** it is sacrificed specifically **in the daytime, and** its service must be performed **with** the priest's **right hand, so too all** offerings mentioned **are brought only from non-sacred** animals, **and** are sacrificed specifically **in the daytime, and** each one's service must be performed **with** the priest's **right hand.**

This is the derivation the entire discussion was building toward. Rabbi Akiva reads the chatat entry of Zot HaTorah as follows: machatat einah ba'ah ela min ha-chulin — a chatat comes only from non-sanctified funds. And from the pasuk's linkage, af kol einah ba'ah ela min ha-chulin — so too all obligatory korbanot must come from non-ma'aser sheini funds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַטָּאת גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהִקְרִיב אַהֲרֹן אֶת פַּר הַחַטָּאת אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״ – ״לוֹ״ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְלֹא מִשֶּׁל מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **a sin offering itself, from where do we** derive that it is brought only from non-sacred animals? **Rav Ḥisda said:** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And Aaron shall present the bull of the sin offering, which is his"** (Leviticus 16:11). This teaches that the animal must come **from his** cattle, **and not from** money with which the second **tithe** has been redeemed.

How do we know this specifically of the chatat? Rabbi Chisda teaches: the pasuk in the Yom Kippur passage says ve-hikriv Aharon et par ha-chatat asher lo — "that is his" — lo mishelo, from his own personal funds, ve-lo mishel ma'aser, and not from any other source including ma'aser funds. From the word lo we derive that the chatat must come from the kohen's own non-designated funds. By virtue of Zot HaTorah, this extends to all korbanot.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּיּוֹם – מִ״בְּיוֹם צַוֹּתוֹ״ נָפְקָא! כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary to derive from the *halakha* of a sin offering that an offering is sacrificed **in the daytime?** Isn't this principle **derived from** the expression: **"On the day of His commanding"** (Leviticus 7:38), which is understood to be referring to all offerings? The Gemara answers: Indeed, the *baraita* **cited** the principle from the model of a sin offering **for no reason [*kedi*],** and it was mentioned here on account of the other principles.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיָדוֹ הַיְמָנִית – מִדְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה נָפְקָא, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר אֶצְבַּע וּכְהֻנָּה – אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יָמִין! כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why must the *baraita* teach that the *halakha* of the sin offering teaches that the rites of offerings must be performed **with** the priest's **right hand?** Isn't this **derived from** the statement **of Rabba bar bar Ḥana? As Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Reish Lakish says:** In **any place** in the Torah **that it is stated** that an action is performed with **a finger, or** that it is performed by **priesthood,** the *halakha* is that the rite is performed **only** with the **right** hand. This is derived from the Torah's statement with regard to the leper: "And the priest shall dip his right finger" (Leviticus 14:16). The Gemara answers: The *baraita* **cited** the principle from the model of a sin offering **for no reason,** since it is actually derived from Rabba bar bar Ḥana's statement.

The Gemara notes that two additional laws were mentioned in conjunction with the chatat: that it must be brought in the daytime, and that the kohen must use his right hand. Both, however, are ultimately superfluous as derivations from this context: the daytime requirement is already learned from the pasuk mi-yom tzavoto — "on the day God commanded" — a general rule for all korbanot; and the right-hand requirement is derived from the principle of Rabba Bar Bar Channah quoting Reish Lakish that wherever the Torah mentions etzba and kehunah together it refers to the right hand. The Gemara concludes regarding both: kidi nasba — they were included gratuitously. The essential and purposeful derivation from the chatat entry is solely the requirement of chulin funds.

### Akiva: Mincha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָשָׁם״ – מָה אָשָׁם עַצְמוֹתָיו מוּתָּרִין, אַף כֹּל עַצְמוֹתָיו מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The cited *baraita* continues: **"Guilt offering"** teaches: **Just as** with regard to **a guilt offering, its bones** have no sanctity and **are permitted** for any use, **so too** with regard to **any** mentioned offering, **its bones are permitted.**

Ve-la-asham: *mah Asham atzmotav mutarin* — just as by an asham the bones are permitted for personal use, af kol atzmotav mutarin — so too by all korbanot the bones are permitted. This means that the bones, like the hide, are given to the kohanim without any of the restrictions that apply to the korban's meat — no time limit, no geographic restriction, and no obligation to treat them with kedushah. They may be used for whatever purpose one wishes.

This rule is effectively self-evident for most korbanot, since the pesukim speak of the kohanim eating the meat and implicitly exclude non-meat components from the same restrictions. The significant case is the olah, which is entirely burned on the mizbeach. One might have thought that since the entire olah must go up in flames, even the bones are included. This derivation teaches otherwise: even by the olah, the bones are excluded from the korban's sanctity and need not be placed on the mizbeach. The kohen may take the bones home and use them freely.

## Pesach: Shelamim

### Akiva: Chatat Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הַאי ״וְזָבַחְתָּ פֶּסַח״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מִנַּיִן לְמוֹתַר הַפֶּסַח שֶׁקָּרֵב שְׁלָמִים? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזָבַחְתָּ פֶּסַח לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ צֹאן וּבָקָר״, וַהֲלֹא אֵין פֶּסַח בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים וּמִן הָעִזִּים! אֶלָּא מוֹתַר הַפֶּסַח יְהֵא לְדָבָר הַבָּא מִן הַצֹּאן וּמִן הַבָּקָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **Rabbi Akiva,** who derives this from the verse: "This is the law of…the sin offering," **that** verse: **"And you shall sacrifice the Paschal offering,"** **what does he make of it,** i.e., what does he derive from it? **He requires it for that** which was stated **by Rav Naḥman, as Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: From where** is it derived **that a leftover Paschal offering,** an animal consecrated but not ultimately sacrificed on Passover eve, **is sacrificed as a peace offering** afterward? It is derived from **that** which **is stated: "And you shall sacrifice the Passover offering unto the Lord, your God, of the flock and the herd."** The verse is difficult: **But isn't a Paschal offering brought only from the sheep and from the goats? Rather,** it is derived from here **that a leftover Paschal offering should be** sacrificed **as** an offering **brought** both **from the flock and from the herd,** i.e., a peace offering.

The Gemara now turns to Rabbi Akiva's reading of the pasuk in Devarim: u-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar. Rabbi Eliezer had used the word bakar — cattle — to extend the principles of Pesach to all other korbanot, since a Pesach cannot come from cattle. Rabbi Akiva, who cannot accept that usage (it would constitute learning from a she-ee efshar case), instead applies the pasuk mi-bayei lei le-chide-Rav Nachman — to teach the ruling of Rav Nachman.

Rav Nachman, quoting Rabbah bar Avuha, asks: *minayin le-*motar pesach* she-karev shelamim* — how do we know that what is left over from a *korban pesach* is brought as a shelamim? *motar pesach* refers to the classic case of a Pesach that was lost, replaced by a substitute, and then recovered — one of the two animals must now be dealt with, and it has the status of "leftover." The answer: she-ne'emar u-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar. Since a Pesach cannot come from bakar, the word bakar must be teaching something else: that the *motar pesach* — which can no longer be a Pesach — becomes a korban of the type that comes from both tzon and bakar, namely a shelamim. The Pesach is similar to a shelamim in that it is eaten by its owners, and when it can no longer function as a Pesach it becomes precisely that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מִדַּאֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל נָפְקָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם מִן הַצֹּאן קׇרְבָּנוֹ לְזֶבַח שְׁלָמִים״, וְאָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: דָּבָר הַבָּא מִן הַצֹּאן יְהֵא לְזֶבַח שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is it derived from here** that a leftover Paschal offering is sacrificed as a peace offering? **It is derived from** the verse **that Shmuel's father** cites: **As it is written:** "And **if his offering for a sacrifice of peace offerings** to the Lord **is of the flock"** (Leviticus 3:6); **and Shmuel's father said:** This teaches that an offering **that is brought** only **from the flock,** i.e., the Paschal offering, **will be a sacrifice of peace offerings.**

The Gemara then challenges: but do we not already derive this from the pasuk cited by the father of Shmuel? For it is written by the shelamim: *im min ha-tzon korbano* le-zevach shelamim. The father of Shmuel asks why the Torah says *im min ha-tzon* rather than *im min ha-kevasim* — why the broad "flock" rather than the specific "sheep"? Because davar ha-ba min ha-tzon — something that comes only from the flock. What comes only from flock animals? The Pesach — it comes only from sheep or goats. Yehei le-zevach shelamim: if it can no longer be brought as a Pesach, it becomes a shelamim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא! וְהָתַנְיָא: ״כֶּבֶשׂ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַפֶּסַח לְאַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But still** it must be asked: **Is it derived from here? It is derived from there,** from the verse cited in the following *baraita*. **And isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: Even though the verse already states that peace offerings come from the flock, as it is written: "And if his offering for a sacrifice of peace offerings to the Lord be of the flock, male or female, he shall sacrifice it without blemish" (Leviticus 3:6), the verse goes on to specify: "If he bring a lamb for his offering…and if his offering be a goat" (Leviticus 3:7–12). The word **"lamb"** is written **to include the Paschal offering in** the requirement that the **fat tail** be sacrificed on the altar, which is written subsequently with regard to a peace offering (Leviticus 3:9), since this *halakha* is not mentioned in the verses concerning the Paschal offering.

A third pasuk is then cited from a braita: the Torah says by the shelamim: im keshev hu makriv — if it is a sheep he brings. Le-rabbot et ha-Pesach la-alyah — this comes to include the Pesach in the requirement of burning the fat tail. And subsequently: ke-she-hu omer im keshev — when it says if it is a sheep — le-havi Pesach she-avrah shenato — this includes a Pesach whose year has passed, and shelamim ha-ba'im machmat Pesach — shelamim that arise on account of the Pesach. Le-khol mitzvot shelamim — they are subject to all the rules of shelamim: semichah, nesachim, tenufat chazeh ve-shok. These are things one does not do with a Pesach itself but does with a Pesach that has transformed into a shelamim. The pasuk then says ve-im ez — if it is a goat — and this interruption, hifsik ha-inyan, teaches *she-einah te'unah alyah* — a goat's small tail is not burned, unlike a sheep's fat tail.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אִם כֶּבֶשׂ״ – לְהָבִיא פֶּסַח שֶׁעָבְרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, וּשְׁלָמִים הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת פֶּסַח, לְכׇל מִצְוַת שְׁלָמִים: שֶׁיִּטְעֲנוּ סְמִיכָה, וּנְסָכִים, וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **When** the verse **states: "If** he brings **a lamb,"** it is **to include in all the mitzvot of peace offerings a Paschal offering whose** first **year has passed** and is therefore too old to be sacrificed as a Paschal offering, **and peace offerings brought due to a Paschal offering.** Specifically, this indicates **that they require placing hands** on the head of the offering, **libations, and the waving of** the **breast and thigh.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְאִם עֵז״ – הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן, לִימֵּד עַל הָעֵז שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה אַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And when** the verse **states:** "And **if** his offering is a **goat," it interrupted the** previous **matter** and **taught that the** sacrifice of **a goat does not require** that the **fat tail** be burned on the altar. In any event, the verse indicates that a Paschal offering that was disqualified as such because it has reached its second year, i.e., the leftover of a Paschal offering, is sacrificed as a peace offering. It may therefore be asked: Why are there three verses to indicate this one *halakha*?

### Akiva: Asham Bones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּלָתָא קְרָאֵי כְּתִיב: חַד לְעִבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְעִבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, וְחַד לְעִבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא עִבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, וְחַד לְלֹא עִבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא עִבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** none of these derivations are superfluous, as **three verses are written** that teach the *halakha* that a Paschal offering that is sacrificed not on Passover eve is sacrificed as a peace offering. **One** verse teaches this *halakha* in a case where **its time** of sacrifice, Passover eve, **has passed, and its** first **year has** also **passed,** disqualifying it for sacrifice as a Paschal offering. **And one** verse teaches the *halakha* in a case where **its time** of sacrifice **has passed, but not its** first **year. And** the third **one** teaches a case where **neither its time** of sacrifice **nor its** first **year has passed,** but it was sacrificed before Passover eve.

The Gemara asks: tlata kra'ei ktivu — there are three pesukim, each apparently teaching that a *Pesach becomes* a shelamim. Why are all three needed? Chad le-avar zmano ve-avrah shnato — one addresses the case where Pesach has already passed and the animal is also more than a year old, rendering it entirely disqualified from ever serving as a Pesach. Mishum de-idchei lei le-gamrei — since it has been completely rejected from the category of Pesach, its transformation into shelamim seems obvious.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן עִבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְעִבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִידְּחִי לֵיהּ לִגְמָרֵי, אֲבָל עִבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא עִבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, דַּחֲזֵי לְפֶסַח שֵׁנִי, אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** all these verses **are necessary. As had the Merciful One written** only the case **where** both **its** first **year and its time** of sacrifice **have passed,** one could say that only such a Paschal offering should be sacrificed as a peace offering, **as it was completely rejected from** its status as **a Paschal offering; but** in a case where **its time** of sacrifice **has passed but its** first **year** has **not** passed, in **which** case it is still **fit to** be sacrificed as a Paschal offering on the **second *Pesaḥ*, I would say** that it is **not** sacrificed as a peace offering.

Ve-chad le-avar zmano ve-lo avrah shnato — a second is needed for when Pesach has passed but the animal is still within its first year. Here, d'chazi le-Pesach sheni — it could in theory be used for Pesach Sheni, the makeup Pesach of the fourteenth of Iyyar. Since it has not been fully rejected from potential Pesach use, one might have thought it does not become a shelamim if slaughtered in the interim. The second pasuk teaches that it does.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן עִבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא עִבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, דְּאִידְּחִי לֵיהּ מִפֶּסַח רִאשׁוֹן, אֲבָל לֹא עִבְּרָה זְמַנּוֹ וְלֹא עִבְּרָה שְׁנָתוֹ, דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְפֶסַח רִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי חֲזֵי, אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Torah **had taught us** only that a leftover Paschal offering **whose time has passed but whose year has not passed** is sacrificed as a peace offering, one might think that this is **because** the Paschal offering **was rejected from the first *Pesaḥ*; but** in a case **where neither its time** of sacrifice **nor its** first **year have passed,** in **which** case it is still **fit to** be sacrificed as **a Paschal offering** on Passover eve, **I would say** that it is **not** sacrificed as a peace offering. Therefore, all three verses **are necessary.**

Ve-chad de-lo avar zmano ve-lo avrah shnato — the third is needed for when it is the right age and Pesach has not yet arrived — it is, say, Purim. The animal is fully eligible to serve as a Pesach in a few weeks. One might have thought that if slaughtered now it carries the status of a Pesach. The third pasuk teaches that even here, if brought before Pesach, it has the status of a shelamim and is not treated as a Pesach. Tzricha: all three cases required their own derivation.

The summary of this extended discussion is as follows. The entire debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva ultimately yields the same ruling through different sources: an obligatory korban cannot come from ma'aser sheini funds. Rabbi Eliezer derives it from *Pesach Mitzrayim*; Rabbi Akiva derives it from the chatat entry of Zot HaTorah. As for the difficult pasuk u-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar, Rabbi Eliezer reads bakar as extending Pesach principles to all other korbanot; Rabbi Akiva reads it as establishing that a *motar pesach* — in any of its three forms — takes on the status of a shelamim.

## Minchot: Sources

### Motar Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל קׇרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּיבּוּר וְהַיָּחִיד בָּאִין מִן הָאָרֶץ וּמֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, מִן הֶחָדָשׁ וּמִן הַיָּשָׁן, חוּץ מִן הָעוֹמֶר וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, שֶׁאֵינָן בָּאִין אֶלָּא מִן הֶחָדָשׁ וּמִן הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All communal and individual** meal **offerings** may **come from** produce grown in **Eretz** Yisrael **and from outside Eretz** Yisrael, **from the new** crop, i.e., the current year's crop, **and from the old** crop from previous years. This is the *halakha* of all meal offerings **except for the *omer*,** i.e., the measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan, **and the two loaves,** i.e., the communal offering brought on the festival of *Shavuot*, **as they come only from the new** crop **and from Eretz** Yisrael.

A new perek begins, redirecting the discussion back to the laws of menachot. Kol korbanot ha-tzibbur ve-ze ha-yachid — all communal and individual korbanot, by which the mishna means all communal and individual menachot in context — ba'im min ha-aretz u-mi-chutz la-aretz: the grain used for them may come from Israel or from outside Israel. Min ha-chadash u-min ha-yashan: they may come from new grain or old, recently harvested or stored.

### Three Pesukim

*[Continued from above]*

Chutz min ha-omer u-shtei ha-lechem — with the exception of the Omer and the *shtei halechem* brought on Pesach and Shavuot respectively. She-einam ba'im ela min ha-chadash: these two must come from new grain, since their entire purpose is to represent the new barley and wheat harvest. U-min ha-aretz: they must also come from grain grown in Eretz Yisrael, since they are the first harvest of the Land. These are the only two menachot so restricted.

### Pesach Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן אֵינָן בָּאִין אֶלָּא מִן הַמּוּבְחָר, וְאֵיזֶהוּ מוּבְחָר שֶׁלָּהֶם? (מִכְנֵיס וְזַטְחָא) [מִכְמָס וְזוֹנֵחָא] – אַלְפָּא לַסֹּלֶת, שְׁנִיָּיה לָהֶן – עֲפוֹרַיִים בַּבִּקְעָה. כׇּל הָאֲרָצוֹת הָיוּ כְּשֵׁרוֹת, אֶלָּא מִכָּאן הָיוּ מְבִיאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And all** meal offerings **come only from the optimal-**quality grain. **And which** places have the **optimal** grain **for them?** Fields in **Makhnis and Zateḥa** are the **primary [*alfa*]** source **for fine flour. Secondary to them** is **Aforayim in the valley.** A**ll the regions were valid** as the source of the grain**, but** it is **from here,** the primary and secondary places, that **they would bring** grain, because it was of optimal quality.

Ve-khulam einam ba'im ela min ha-muvchar — and for all menachot, one must use choice flour. Ve-eizeh u muvchar shelahem? — what counts as the choicest? Machnis ve-Zatcha, two locations in Eretz Yisrael famed for superior grain — some manuscripts read Michmas u-Meyunicha or Machmas ve-Zanocha. Whatever the precise identification, the Mishnah describes them as alfa le-solet — grade A flour, the alpha of fine flour. Shniyah lahem, second to them in quality, is Aforayim ba-bika, from the valley region called Aforayim. Kol ha-aratzot hayu kesherot — all lands were technically valid, ela mi-kan hayu mevi'im — but this is where they would actually bring from, as these produced the best grain. Whether this final specification refers to all menachot or specifically to the Omer and *shtei halechem* is not entirely clear from the mishna. Rashi reads kol artzot shel Eretz Yisrael kesherot, limiting himself to Eretz Yisrael, which suggests he is speaking of the Omer and *shtei halechem* specifically, the only menachot required to come from Israeli grain.

## Omer & Shtei HaL

### Grain Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: עוֹמֶר הַבָּא מִן הַיָּשָׁן – כָּשֵׁר, שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם הַבָּאוֹת מִן הַיָּשָׁן – כְּשֵׁרוֹת, אֶלָּא שֶׁחִיסֵּר מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that the *omer* meal offering and the two loaves are prepared only from the new crop. The wording of the mishna indicates that this is an essential requirement. The Gemara notes: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this** following ***tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **An *omer*** meal offering **that comes from the old** crop is **valid.** Similarly, **the two loaves that come from the old** crop **are valid, but** by bringing them from the old crop one **lacks** the proper fulfillment of its **mitzva.**

The Gemara now challenges the mishna: matnitin de-lo ke-hai tanna — our mishna does not accord with the following tanna. For a braita teaches: Omer ha-ba min ha-yashan kasher — if the Omer is brought from last year's grain, it is valid. Shtei ha-lechem ha-ba'ah min ha-yashan kesherah — and likewise the *shtei halechem* brought from old grain is valid. Ela she-chisar mitzvah — one has merely failed to fulfill the mitzvah optimally. This stands in clear tension with our mishna, which states that these two must come from new grain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹמֶר – דִּכְתִיב ״תַּקְרִיב אֵת מִנְחַת בִּכּוּרֶיךָ״, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides the biblical sources for the rulings of the *baraita*: The source for the ruling concerning the ***omer*** meal offering is **as it is written:** "And when you shall bring a meal offering of first fruits to the Lord, it is ripened grain, toasted over fire, even groats of the fresh ear, **you shall bring the meal offering of your first fruits"** (Leviticus 2:14). The superfluous repetition of the term "you shall bring" teaches that the *omer* is valid **even** if brought **from** an old crop that was stored away in **the attic.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם – דִּכְתִיב: ״מִמּוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם תָּבִיאּוּ״, וְלֹא מִן חוּצָה לָאָרֶץ. ״מִמּוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The source for the ruling concerning the **two loaves** is **as it is written:** "And you shall offer a new meal offering to the Lord. **From your dwellings you shall bring** two wave-loaves" (Leviticus 23:16–17). The term "your dwellings" is a reference to Eretz Yisrael. Therefore, the verse indicates that the two loaves must be brought from grain grown there, **and not from outside of Eretz** Yisrael. Furthermore, the term **"from your dwellings"** teaches that the offering may come from any grain grown in Eretz Yisrael **and even from** an old crop that was stored away in **the attic.**

How does the braita permit this? By the Omer the Torah says takriv et minchat bikurecha — you shall bring your first-fruits offering — and from the additional verb takriv the braita derives that one may bring it even afilu min ha-aliyah, even from stored grain in the attic. Similarly by the *shtei halechem*, the Torah says mi-moshvoteichem tavi'u — from your dwelling places you shall bring — and from the inclusive word tavi'u (you shall bring) the braita derives that one may bring it from anywhere, even stored grain, though mi-moshvoteichem simultaneously excludes grain from chutz la-aretz.

### Choice Flour

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, שֶׁכׇּל שֶׁאַתָּה מֵבִיא מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר – הֲרֵי הוּא כָּזֶה! אִם כֵּן, לִיכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״תָּבִיא״, מַאי ״תָּבִיאּוּ״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי. וְהָכְתִיב ״רֵאשִׁית״? לְמִצְוָה. הָכְתִיב ״חֲדָשָׁה״? הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks how two *halakhot* can be derived from the same term: **Didn't you** already **expound that** term to teach that one can use grain only if it is grown in Eretz Yisrael? How can you also derive from it that the grain can be brought from an old crop? The Gemara explains: That is derived from the next term, as **the verse states:** "From your dwellings **you shall bring"** (Leviticus 23:17), which teaches that one may bring them from any grain grown in Eretz Yisrael **and even from** an old crop that was stored away in **the attic.** The Gemara questions this answer: **But that** term **is necessary** to teach **that any** leavened bread offering **that you bring in another instance,** i.e., the loaves of the thanksgiving offering, **is to be like this** offering of the two loaves, i.e., the same requirements apply to it (see 77b). How, then, can you expound the term to also teach that grain from an old crop can be used for the two loaves? The Gemara explains: **If so,** that the term is written only to teach about the requirements for other leavened bread offerings, then **let the verse write: You shall bring [*tavi*],** using the singular form. For **what** reason then, does it write: **"You shall bring [*tavi'u*],"** using the plural form? It is written so that one can **learn from it two** different *halakhot*. The Gemara questions the ruling of the *baraita* that the *omer* and the two loaves are valid even if brought from an old crop: **But isn't** the term **"first" written** with regard to both the *omer* and the two loaves? This indicates they must come from the new crop. The *omer* is referred to as "the first of your harvest" (Leviticus 23:10), and the two loaves are referred to as "an offering of the first" (Leviticus 2:12). The Gemara answers: The term indicates that only the new crop should be used, but that is only **for** the proper fulfillment of the **mitzva.** If an old crop was used, the offerings are still valid. The Gemara asks: But **isn't** the term **"new" written** with regard to the two loaves: **"A new** meal offering" (Leviticus 23:16), which indicates that only the new crop can be used? **That** term **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*:

### Old Grain Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אָמְרוּ: שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם הַבָּאוֹת מִן הַיָּשָׁן – כְּשֵׁרוֹת, וּמָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״חֲדָשָׁה״? שֶׁתְּהֵא חֲדָשָׁה לְכׇל הַמְּנָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Akiva said** that even if **the two loaves are brought from the old** crop, they are **valid. How do I realize** the meaning of: **"A new** meal offering"? This teaches that the two loaves **are to be the first of all the** other **meal offerings.** No other meal offerings may be brought from the new crop until the meal offering of the two loaves has been brought.

Rabbi Natan ve-Rabbi Akiva amru: Shtei ha-lechem ha-ba'ot min ha-yashan kesherot — Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Akiva both hold that *shtei halechem* brought from old grain is nonetheless valid. U-mani mekayem chadasha — how then do they understand the requirement that it be chadasha? She-tehei chadasha le-khol ha-menachot: it must be new relative to all other menachot — meaning that until the *shtei halechem* is brought, no other minchahmay be offered from the new grain's harvest. The word chadasha does not mandate that the *shtei halechem* itself come from new grain; it mandates that it inaugurate the new grain season for all other menachot. Paradoxically, on this view the *shtei halechem* need not itself consist of new grain, yet once it is brought, all other menachot may now use the new grain. The requirement of chadasha is thus relational — it defines the Shtei HaLechem's role as the gateway to the new grain season — rather than intrinsic to the composition of the offering itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בְּחָדָשׁ,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara defines the limits of the dispute between the mishna and *baraita*: **They disagree only with regard to** whether it is essential for the *omer* and the two loaves to be brought from **the new** crop.

A final note on the mishna's opening statement: although it says kol korbanot ha-tzibbur ve-ha-yachid, the context makes clear that menachot are the subject. Tosafos raises the question of analogous rules for animal korbanot — whether there are limitations regarding where the animals come from or distinctions between new and old. He concludes from the Tosefta that kol korbanot ha-tzibbur ve-ha-yachid ba'in me-eretz u-mi-chutz la-aretz afilu mi-ben ovdei kochavim: all animal korbanot may come from Israel, from outside Israel, and even from non-Jews. The notable exceptions are the bechor and *ma'aser behemah*, where there may be restrictions, a topic that will be addressed more fully in *Masechet Bechorot*.
