# Meilah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Me'ilah

### Me'ilah Overview

*Masechet Me'ilah* is distinctive in several respects. Like a number of other tractates, its language departs from the standard diction and grammatical patterns one expects from the Bavli, which has led to the longstanding discussion of whether its final editing was carried out in a different Babylonian academy — whether *Nehardea*, *Sura*, or *Pumbedita*. The *Rashi* on this tractate is standard *Rashi*, and the *Tosafot* here is a mix: some sections are more in the nature of commentary, others involve genuine critical analysis. It is also worth noting — though it pertains to a different tractate — that while the *Rashi* on the opening *daf* of *Kereisos* appeared unusual, as one continued through the tractate it read as authentically Rashi's hand, and the notion that *Rashi* on *Kereisos* is not genuinely his seems to be an overstatement.

As for the substance of the *masechet*, its subject is the Torah prohibition of *me'ilah*: the misappropriation of consecrated property. When one derives benefit from *hekdesh* unintentionally — *beshogeg* — the offender brings a *korban* of *asham me'ilah*, repays the principal, and adds a *chomesh*, an extra fifth (which is in actuality a quarter of the new total). When the violation is intentional — *bemezid* — there is no *korban* and no *chomesh*; there is only repayment of the principal. It is worth pausing over this asymmetry: the *shogeg* case carries more formal consequences. This parallels what was discussed throughout *Kereisos*, where the requirement of *kappara* and atonement can sometimes produce weightier obligations than a deliberate transgression.

### Intentional/Unintentional

This distinction has an important terminological implication throughout the *mishnah* and *gemara*. When the text declares *ma'al* or *lo ma'al* regarding a given case, one must not read *lo ma'al* as meaning that the act was *mutar*. If someone acted *bemezid*, we say *lo ma'al* — meaning the formal category of *me'ilah*, with its obligation of *asham me'ilah* and additional *chomesh*, does not apply — but that is far from saying the act had no consequences. Repayment is still required, and there may well be other *issurim* that apply. *Lo ma'al* has a narrow, technical definition: it means only that the specific obligations of *korban* and *chomesh* are not triggered.

### Me'ilah De'rabbanan

Similarly, there is the concept of *me'ilah de'rabbanan*, which requires careful definition. One must distinguish it from a simple *issur hana'ah de'rabbanan*. In a case where the *gemara* says *lo nehenin velo mo'alin*, the first clause — *lo nehenin* — is a rabbinic prohibition on deriving benefit; nothing more is implied. *Me'ilah de'rabbanan*, by contrast, is a rabbinic extension and expansion of the *de'oraisa* category, carrying substantially greater halachic weight. As the *gemara* established in the previous *daf*, in a case of *me'ilah de'rabbanan* there is no *korban* and no *chomesh* — those belong exclusively to *me'ilah de'oraisa* — but there remains an obligation to repay the principal. The rabbis clearly understood this as a distinct category, and they deliberately refrained from adding the *chomesh* in order to mark the difference from *de'oraisa* liability.

### Theft vs. Benefit

On a conceptual level, a fundamental question runs through the entire *masechet*: how does one understand the essential character of the *aveirah* of *me'ilah*? Is it a form of theft from *hekdesh* — an act defined by taking something that belongs to the divine treasury? Or is it better understood as deriving benefit from something to which one had no entitlement, without necessarily causing a loss? The two framings are not interchangeable, and the tension between them will resurface throughout the tractate.

### Shiur & Kedushah

The *shiur* of *me'ilah* is a *prutah*, which monetizes the prohibition — unlike, say, an *issur achila* measured in *kezayis*. But one must ask: does the *prutah* measure what was taken from *hekdesh*, or what the person benefited? The difference is not merely theoretical. Consider a case where one drinks from a *kos* belonging to *hekdesh*: the person derived benefit, yet *hekdesh* suffered no loss. Yet *me'ilah* applies nonetheless. Conversely, one might transfer an object of *hekdesh* as a gift — causing *hekdesh* a loss — without personally benefiting. These cases pull in different directions depending on which model one adopts.

This question was explored in *Kereisos* as well, where the *gemara* raised the matter of market value versus actual benefit. For most purposes, the operative question is not what the object is worth on the market but whether the person derived *hana'at shaveh prutah* — benefit worth a *prutah*. If the object is *assur behana'ah* and has no market value but was consumed and the person genuinely benefited, *me'ilah* applies. The market-value framing is more relevant when the conception of *me'ilah* skews toward theft rather than unauthorized benefit.

### Kodshei HaShem

A central prerequisite for *me'ilah de'oraisa* is that the object be *kodshei Hashem* — fully consecrated to God in a direct and exclusive sense. This is why there is no *me'ilah* on the meat of *kodashim kalim* that may be eaten, nor on the meat of a *chatat* or *asham* after *zerikat hadam*, when it becomes *mutar* to the *kohanim*. Let us trace the spectrum:

At one end stands the *olah*: from the moment of sanctification through its complete burning, no person has any legitimate right to it (with the narrow exception of the skin), and *me'ilah* applies throughout. At the other end, *shlamim*: as *kodashim kalim*, the offering is conceptually associated with the owner — some even hold *mamon be'alim*, that it remains in a meaningful sense the owner's property — and *me'ilah* does not apply to the meat. The sole exception within *shlamim* is the *imurin*, the portions designated for the *mizbe'ach*, and even those acquire their consecrated status only after *shechita* and *zerikah* render them fit for the altar.

The middle cases — *chatat* and *asham* — are *kodshei kodashim*, fully God's in their fundamental conception. The *kohanim*'s right to eat the meat is a *heter* that comes only after *zerikat hadam*; until that point, even the meat is subject to *me'ilah*. Once the blood has been sprinkled validly, the meat becomes *mutar la'kohanim* and loses its *me'ilah* status.

### Masechet Structure

With this in mind, one should understand what to expect from the tractate as a whole. A striking feature of *Me'ilah* is that the act of *me'ilah* itself — the scenarios in which one actually becomes *mo'el* — appears only toward the very end of the *masechet*. The opening portions are largely devoted to defining whether various objects carry *kedushah* and, if so, what level of *kedushah* they possess. These are prerequisites for *me'ilah*, not *me'ilah* itself. Questions abound: if one consecrates a tree and fruit grows from it, is the fruit subject to *me'ilah*? If one consecrates a well and rainwater collects, does *me'ilah* apply to the water? These involve derivative and indirect *kedushah*. The first two-thirds of the *masechet*, broadly speaking, engages the objects and their *kedushah*; the actual law of *me'ilah* arrives at the end.

## Psulo BaKodesh

### Psulo Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ: כׇּל שֶׁהָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה, וְכׇּל שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in another mishna (*Zevaḥim* 68b) **with regard to** the same disqualified bird offerings: For **any** one of them **whose disqualification** occurred **in the sacred** area, i.e., the Temple courtyard, e.g., if the nape of its neck was pinched at night, **it does not render** the **garments** of one who swallows it **impure** when the meat is **in the throat. And** for **any** one of them **whose disqualification did not** take place **in the sacred** area, **it does render** the **garments** of one who swallows it **impure** when the meat is **in the throat.**

With this background in place, we turn to the concept that dominated the first *daf* and continues into the present discussion: *psulo bakodesh*. This phrase refers to a category of ritual disqualification — *pesul* — that arises from a technical fault in the performance of the *avodah* within the *Mikdash* itself. It is distinguished sharply from a *pesul* that removes the offering entirely from the realm of *kodesh* — such as slaughtering it outside the *Beit HaMikdash* entirely, or the animal dying on its own. In *psulo bakodesh*, the error occurs within the framework of the temple service; the offering remains, as it were, within the ballpark.

This distinction has direct consequences for *me'ilah*. Even a *korban* that is *pasul* — that cannot be offered — retains *me'ilah* status so long as it qualifies as *psulo bakodesh*. And the operative test for whether something qualifies is the principle of *im alu lo yerdu*: if an item was placed on the *mizbe'ach*, it is not removed. If the halacha dictates that it may remain on the *mizbe'ach* once placed there, this demonstrates that it is still sufficiently within the domain of *kodshei Hashem* to carry *me'ilah*. Conversely, if it must be removed, that indicates it has moved outside the relevant category.

An additional dimension of this principle bears emphasizing: for *kodshei kodashim* such as a *chatat* or *asham*, the *pesul* also has a further implication. In a valid offering, *zerikat hadam* would render the meat *mutar la'kohanim* and remove its *me'ilah* status. But when the *korban* was performed under *pesul* — wrong location, *pigul*, and the like — the meat is never rendered permitted. It was never made *mutar* through a valid *zerikah*, and therefore it remains *me'ilah* indefinitely. This is itself part of the *chiddush* of the *mishnah*: not merely that *me'ilah* continues to apply despite the *pesul*, but that it applies permanently to the meat.

There is also the corresponding point for *kodashim kalim*: in a valid offering, *zerikat hadam* would bring the *imurin* into *me'ilah* status by rendering them fit for the *mizbe'ach*. A flawed *zerikah* cannot accomplish this transformation.

### Mishnah Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן, אֵין מְטַמְּאִים בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה וּמוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, חוּץ מֵחַטַּאת הָעוֹף שֶׁעָשָׂה לְמַטָּה, כְּמַעֲשֵׂה חַטַּאת הָעוֹף לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna on *Zevaḥim* 66b, which deals with bird offerings that were disqualified for having either the napes of their necks pinched or their blood sprinkled in the wrong place, teaches: **And all of** the offerings mentioned in that mishna do **not** render one who swallows their meat **ritually impure** to the extent that his **garments are rendered impure** when it is **in the throat.** Although the offering is disqualified, since the nape of its neck was pinched as part of the rite one is not rendered impure by swallowing an olive-bulk of its meat, as is the *halakha* with regard to a carcass of a kosher bird. **And** one who derives benefit from these offerings is liable for **misusing them.** This is the *halakha* concerning all bird offerings, **except for the bird sin offering that one performed below** the red line, **according to the procedure of a bird sin offering,** and **for the sake of a sin offering.** Since it was sacrificed correctly, its meat is permitted to the priests and is no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse.

Against this background, Rav Yosef challenged Rabbah — who had maintained that if one slaughtered a *korban* in the wrong location, it would need to be removed from the *mizbe'ach* — through a tightly constructed chain of three *mishnayot* linked together by the principle of *chada migo chada*: one is learned from the other in sequence, so that Mishnah A illuminates Mishnah B and Mishnah B illuminates Mishnah C.

The first *mishnah* in the chain addresses the case of a bird offering whose *melikah* was performed in the wrong location — whether an *olat ha'of* that should have been performed above but was done below, or vice versa. The *mishnah* rules *bekhulan ein metamei begadim beveit habli'ah*: in all such cases, consuming the bird does not render one's garments *tamei* upon swallowing. This is the halacha associated with *neveilah* — a strangled bird. The ruling thus implies that despite the invalid *melikah*, the bird is not classified as *neveilah*. Because the error was a technical ritual fault committed within the *Mikdash* — *psulo bakodesh* — the identity of the offering is not fundamentally altered. Additionally, *umo'alin bahen*: *me'ilah* still applies, with one exception. A *chatat ha'of* performed correctly — below on the *mizbe'ach*, for the sake of a *chatat* — whose meat is eaten by the *kohanim* does not carry *me'ilah* once that *heter achilah* takes effect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי: כׇּל שֶׁהָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – אִם עָלוּ, לֹא יֵרְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in yet another mishna (*Zevaḥim* 84a): With regard to **any** offering **whose disqualification** took place **in the sacred** area, the sacred area renders the offering acceptable, and **if** such offerings **ascended** onto the altar they **shall not descend.**

The second *mishnah* in the chain contributes the conceptual vocabulary: *kol she'psulah bakodesh*, any case that falls within the category of *psulah bakodesh*, does not cause *tum'at begadim* — it is not treated as *neveilah*. This second *mishnah* does not merely repeat the fact pattern; it provides the conceptual label. Where the first *mishnah* described specific actions (wrong location, etc.), the second *mishnah* names the principle: *psulah bakodesh*.

The third *mishnah* then delivers the clinching rule: *kol she'hayah psulah bakodesh im alu lo yerdu* — anything classified as *psulah bakodesh*, if it ascended the *mizbe'ach*, does not come down. Stringing all three together: *psulah bakodesh* → no *tum'at neveilah* → remains *me'ilah* → stays on the *mizbe'ach im alu*.

### Mizbe'ach Test

These three principles — *psulah bakodesh*, *im alu lo yerdu*, and *me'ilah* — are thus shown to be logically intertwined. The *mizbe'ach* functions as a kind of test. If the principle of *im alu lo yerdu* applies — if the item, once placed on the *mizbe'ach*, may remain there — this demonstrates its continued status as *kodshei Hashem* for purposes of *me'ilah*. The *mizbe'ach* absorbing and retaining the offering is evidence of its minimal, residual qualification within the sacred domain, even in a state of *pesul*. The converse is equally true: if the item must come down from the *mizbe'ach*, this shows it has fallen outside even the minimal threshold, and *me'ilah* would not apply.

### Rabbah v. Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְרַבָּה חֲדָא מִגּוֹ חֲדָא, וַחֲדָא מִגּוֹ חֲדָא:
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the dispute with regard to offerings that were slaughtered in the wrong place and then were brought up to the altar. Rabba maintains that such offerings are removed from the altar while Rav Yosef rules that once they ascended the altar they do not descend from it and are burned. **Rav Yosef raised an objection to Rabba, one** source **from** another **one, and** that **one from** another **one,** i.e., his objection is based upon the combination of several sources.

Rabbah had maintained that when a *korban* is slaughtered in the wrong place (*shachata badarom*), the item must be removed from the *mizbe'ach* if placed there. In Rabbah's understanding, that case does not qualify as *psulo bakodesh* in the relevant sense. The syllogism assembled by Rav Yosef through the three *mishnayot* now bears directly on this position. If the item must come down, Rabbah is implicitly saying it is *not psulo bakodesh*. But the second *mishnah* established that what is *not psulo bakodesh* causes *tum'at neveilah* — meaning it has lost its sacred identity entirely. And the first *mishnah* established that in such cases there is no *me'ilah*. Therefore, on Rabbah's own logic, there should be no *me'ilah* in the case of *shechita badarom* — yet the *mishnah* treats *me'ilah* as applying. Furthermore, the *mishnah*'s declaration of no *me'ilah* appears categorical, encompassing both *de'oraisa* and *de'rabbanan* dimensions. Rabbah's attempt to preserve *me'ilah de'oraisa* while conceding removal from the *mizbe'ach* cannot be sustained.

## Bamat Yachid Case

### Rabbi Elazar Q.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּפְלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עוֹלַת בָּמַת יָחִיד, שֶׁהִכְנִיסָהּ לִפְנִים – קְלָטוּהָ מְחִיצוֹת לְכׇל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara notes: **And that** matter with regard to **which Rabba and Rav Yosef disagree** is **obvious to Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar says:** In the case of **a burnt offering** consecrated to be sacrificed **on a personal altar,** during the period when it is permitted to do so, **that one brought inside** the Temple courtyard, it is **admitted by the partitions** of the courtyard **for all matters;** and from that point onward it has the status of a burnt offering sacrificed in the Temple with regard to all *halakhot*.

Following the resolution of the Rabbah–Rav Yosef debate, the *gemara* introduces a related question posed by Rabbi Elazar. Rabbi Elazar addresses the case of an *olat bamat yachid* — an *olah* that was consecrated for a private *bamah* (*bamat yachid*). This was permitted during the period when *bamot* were allowed, such as when the central *mishkan* was at *Nov* or *Givon*. During that era, a person could sanctify an offering for his own private *bamah*, with considerably fewer formal requirements: no need for a *kohen*, no *klei shareit*, no obligation to slaughter in the north even for an *olah*. A great deal of latitude was permitted.

Now suppose such a person — having sanctified his *olah* for his private *bamah* — decided to bring it instead to the central *mishkan* at *Nov* or *Givon*. The *halacha* is *kaltuhu mechitzot le'kol davar*: the environs of the central sanctuary absorb the offering in every respect, meaning it is now fully subject to all the requirements of that place.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: [עוֹלַת בָּמַת יָחִיד שֶׁהִכְנִיסָהּ, שֶׁנִּפְסְלָה] – עָלוּ, מַהוּ שֶׁיֵּרְדוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: In this regard, **Rabbi Elazar asks:** In the case of **a burnt offering** consecrated to be sacrificed **on a personal altar that one brought into** the Temple courtyard **and** it **was rendered unfit** for sacrifice, e.g., it was slaughtered in the south rather than its proper place in the north, **if** its limbs **ascended** the altar to be sacrificed, **what is** the *halakha* **as** to whether **they should descend?** Although the animal was disqualified due to the change in location, had it been slaughtered in the south on a personal altar it would not have been disqualified.

The question posed by Rabbi Elazar is: *olat bamat yachid she'hichnisuha she'nifselah alu mahu she'yerdu*? — if the offering was brought inside and was then disqualified in a way that would *not* have constituted a *pesul* had it been offered on the private *bamah* — for instance, it was slaughtered in the south, which at a *bamat yachid* would have been entirely permissible — and it was then placed on the *mizbe'ach*, does it come down or not?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לַחֲדָא, מִכְּלַל דְּאִידַּךְ פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, אִי כְּרַבָּה אִי כִּדְרַב יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **From the fact that** Rabbi Elazar **asked** this question only **with regard to this one** case, one can conclude **that** in **the other** case, where the burnt offering was consecrated for sacrifice in the Temple, the *halakha* of the limbs that ascended the altar is **obvious to him.** He **either** holds that the *halakha* is that they are not sacrificed, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabba, or** he maintains that they are sacrificed, **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Yosef.**

The *gemara* observes: *midhekam'i ba'ei lei* — from the very fact that Rabbi Elazar poses this more complex question, it appears he takes for granted the answer to the simpler, baseline case of *shachata badarom* by an ordinary *korban*. He finds that simple case obvious — though we do not know whether he holds like Rabbah or like Rav Yosef — and moves directly to ask about the harder case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא מִגּוֹ חֲדָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this inference, as it is possible that Rabbi Elazar was actually uncertain with regard to that case as well; but **he raises one** dilemma **as a result of the other.** In other words, Rabbi Elazar was asking two questions, one predicated on the other, and the second question is valid regardless of whether he holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabba or Rav Yosef.

The *gemara* then suggests: perhaps Rabbi Elazar does not take the baseline case for granted at all. Perhaps he is asking a *chada migo chada* question — first asking what the halacha is in the base case, and then on the basis of that answer asking what happens with *bamat yachid*. Just because he phrases the question as the harder case does not necessarily mean the easier case is settled.

## Psulo BaKodesh (II)

### Tiyuvta de'Rabbah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** By citing all these *mishnayot*, Rav Yosef demonstrates that an offering that is slaughtered in the wrong place is not removed from the altar, contrary to Rabba's opinion. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rabba** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

The *tiyuvta* against Rabbah having been established through the chain of three *mishnayot*, the *gemara* declares *tiyuvta de'Rava tiyuvta* — a definitive refutation. This closes the debate cleanly, and the result sharpens the general principle considerably. The concept of *kodshei Hashem* is now defined with greater precision: beyond the basic *korban*, what constitutes *kodshei Hashem* for *me'ilah* purposes is determined by the *mizbe'ach*. If a *pesul* is of the *psulo bakodesh* variety — technical enough that the item may remain on the *mizbe'ach* if placed there — it retains its status as *kodshei Hashem* and carries *me'ilah*. Once the *pesul* is so severe that the item must come down from the *mizbe'ach*, it can no longer qualify as *kodshei Hashem* for *me'ilah* purposes, even though it retains some degree of *kedushah* in other respects.

## Bamat Yachid Case (II)

### Absorption Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבָּה הָתָם אִם עָלוּ – יֵרְדוּ, מְחִיצָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ – פָּסְלָה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לֹא פָּסְלָה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: Even if Rabbi Elazar holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabba, that in a regular case the limbs are removed from the altar, one can claim that **Rabba says** that **if** the limbs **ascend** the altar **they shall descend only there,** i.e., if the burnt offering was consecrated for sacrifice in the Temple. In that case, the owner consecrated the offering with the intention of sacrificing it within the **partition** of the courtyard, where slaughtering is performed **properly,** in the north. Consequently, if it was slaughtered in the south, that **renders** the offering entirely **disqualified,** and therefore even if it ascended the altar it must descend. But in a case where he consecrated the offering in order to sacrifice it **improperly** on a personal altar, slaughtering it in the south **does not render** the offering entirely **disqualified,** and therefore if it ascends the altar it does not descend.

Returning to Rabbi Elazar's question about the *olat bamat yachid*, the *gemara* articulates two competing frameworks for thinking about why the case might go either way.

The first framework explains why Rabbah might rule more leniently in this case than in the standard case. In the baseline case of *shachata badarom*, the offering was fully subject to the requirements of the *Mikdash* from the outset: *mechitza ketikna paslah* — the walls of the sanctuary, functioning properly, impose all the relevant requirements, and a violation of those requirements is a real *pesul*. But here the offering was originally consecrated as *kodushat bamat yachid*, where slaughtering in the south would have been entirely acceptable. One might therefore argue that this *pesul* is weaker — that the original *kedushah* of the *bamat yachid* extends greater latitude, and it should stay on the *mizbe'ach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִילְמָא: אֲפִילּוּ לְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמַר אִם עָלְתָה – לֹא תֵּרֵד, מְחִיצָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ – קָלְטָה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לָא קָלְטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps,** the opposite is the case: **Even according to** the opinion of **Rav Yosef, who said** that **if** it **ascends** the altar it **shall not descend,** that *halakha* applies only in a case where the owner consecrated the offering in order to sacrifice it within the **partition** of the courtyard, in which case it was consecrated **properly.** In such an instance, the offering is **admitted** by the partitions of the courtyard, and therefore even in a case where it was disqualified, if it ascended the altar it does not descend. By contrast, in a case where he consecrated it in order to sacrifice it **improperly** on a personal altar, the offering is **not admitted** at all by the partitions of the courtyard, which means that even if it ascends the altar it must descend.

The second framework explains why even Rav Yosef — who holds *im ala lo yered* in the standard case — might say this case is *worse* and that the offering must come down. The logic runs as follows: *mechitza ketikna kaltah, shelo ketikna lo kaltah* — the walls of the central sanctuary "absorb" an offering that was sanctified for that place from the beginning, giving it a right, as it were, to remain on the *mizbe'ach* once placed there. But an offering originally sanctified for a *bamat yachid*, which was never fundamentally a *Mikdash korban*, was never absorbed by the *mechitzot* in the relevant sense. It lacks the entitlement to remain, because it did not start as a *Mikdash korban* in the first place.

The result is that the case can legitimately be asked either way, and both directions carry genuine halachic logic. Asking the harder question does not, therefore, signal that the easier question has already been resolved.

One might ask: when a person consecrates an offering for a *bamat yachid*, does he necessarily intend to confine it to that context? Perhaps a person who consecrates *stam* intends simply to sanctify the animal generally, retaining the freedom to bring it wherever he wishes — and the *bamat yachid* designation is therefore not a constraining framework. This is a genuine conceptual difficulty, and the *gemara* does not resolve it.

### Teiqu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved **[*teiku*]**.

The *gemara* concludes: *teiqu* — the question remains unresolved. Whether an *olat bamat yachid* brought into the *Mikdash* and disqualified in a way that would have been permissible on a private *bamah* must be removed from the *mizbe'ach* or may remain there is left as an open question.

## Zrikat Pigul

### Rav Giddal Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: זְרִיקַת פִּיגּוּל אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא מִידֵי מְעִילָה בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא לִידֵי מְעִילָה בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the misuse of offerings that were rendered unfit, **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav said: Sprinkling** the blood of **an offering** with **the intent to consume it after its appointed time [*piggul*],** in a case where there was *piggul* intent during the slaughter as well, **does not remove** the meat of the offering **from** the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse, in** the case of **offerings of the most sacred order.** The reason is that its meat is still classified as "the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15), since there was no proper sprinkling that permitted the priests to partake of the offering, as it is *piggul*. Similarly, this sprinkling **does not bring** the sacrificial portions **of offerings of lesser sanctity into** the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** as only a valid sprinkling confers that status.

With the *psulo bakodesh* discussion concluded, the *gemara* transitions to a new statement. Rav Giddal said in the name of Rav: *zerikat pigul eina motzi midei me'ilah be'kodshei kodashim* — a *zerikah* performed with *machshevet pigul* (intent to eat the offering *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo*) is not a valid *zerikah* and does not remove *me'ilah* status from the meat of *kodshei kodashim*. Since the *zerikah* is *pigul* and therefore invalid, it does not render the meat *mutar la'kohanim*, and so the meat remains perpetually under *me'ilah*. Similarly, such a *zerikah* does not bring the *imurin* of *kodashim kalim* into *me'ilah* status: *ve'eino mevi lidei me'ilah bekodashim kalim*. A valid *zerikah* is what normally renders the *imurin* fit for the *mizbe'ach* and thereby subject to *me'ilah*; a *zerikat pigul* accomplishes none of this.

At first glance this seems merely to restate what the *mishnah* already established. What is the *chiddush*?

### Tosafot Chiddush

*Tosafot* explains the *chiddush* as follows. There is a well-established principle in the laws of *pigul*: an offering cannot acquire *pigul* status unless it was, apart from the *pigul* intention itself, otherwise fit — *meratzeh le'pigulo*. The *zerikah* must have been performed correctly in all other respects; a *zerikah* accompanied by an additional *pesul*, such as *chutz limkomo*, would not create *pigul* at all. The *zerikah* of *pigul* is thus, by definition, a *zerikah* that was performed correctly in all respects other than the disqualifying intention. The *hava amina* would therefore be: since *zerikat pigul* is a *zerikah* that is, in every formal sense, *meratzeh le'pigulo* — considered fit enough to generate *pigul* status — perhaps we should extend that significance and say that *zerikat pigul* is meaningful enough to affect *me'ilah* status as well. Rav Giddal in the name of Rav rules that this is not the case. *Zerikat pigul* may suffice to create *pigul*, but it does not suffice to change the *me'ilah* status of the meat or of the *imurin*.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתֵיב אַבָּיֵי וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַתּוֹדָה לִפְנִים, וְלַחְמָהּ חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – לֹא קִדֵּשׁ אֶת הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Abaye sat and related this *halakha*,** which Rav Giddel said that Rav said. **Rav Pappa raised an objection to Abaye** from a mishna (*Menaḥot* 78b): With regard to **one who slaughters the thanks offering** in its proper place **inside** the Temple courtyard, **and** at that time, **its** forty accompanying **loaves** were **outside the wall,** where it is prohibited to partake of the loaves, **the loaves are not sanctified** by this slaughtering.

Abaye was transmitting this tradition of Rav Giddal in the name of Rav when Rav Pappa challenged him from the following *mishnah*: *hashochet et hatoda bifnim velachmah bachutz* — if one slaughters a *toda* inside the *Mikdash* while its accompanying bread is outside, *lo kiddesh halachem* — the *shechita* does not sanctify the bread. The *toda* is accompanied by bread, and the *shechita* of the *toda* is what elevates the bread from *kedushat damim* to *kedushat haguf*. But if the bread is outside, it is disconnected from the *korban* and the *shechita* does not affect it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחָטָהּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרְמוּ פָּנֶיהָ בַּתַּנּוּר, וַאֲפִילּוּ קָרְמוּ כּוּלָּן חוּץ מֵאַחַת מֵהֶן – לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָהּ חוּץ לִזְמַנָּהּ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמָהּ – קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **if he slaughtered** the thanks offering **before the surface** of the loaves **formed a crust in the oven, and** this is the *halakha* **even** if the surface of **all** the loaves **formed a crust except for one of them, the loaves are not sanctified,** even those that had already formed a crust. If the priest **slaughtered** the thanks offering with the intent to partake of it or to burn the portions consumed on the altar **beyond its** designated **time** and it was thereby rendered *piggul*, **or outside its** designated **area** and was disqualified, **the loaves are sanctified** and are subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. This is presumably the case even if he also sprinkled the blood with *piggul* intent, as the mishna does not differentiate in this regard.

Similarly, if the bread was not yet fully baked — if the crust had not yet formed (*ad shelo karmu paneha batannur*), or if even one loaf among them lacked its crust (*afillu karmu kullan chutz me'achas mehen*) — the bread is not sanctified. The requirement is all-or-nothing.

Critically for our discussion, the *mishnah* continues: *shachat chutz lizmano ve'chutz limkomo* — if one slaughtered the *toda* with a *machshevet pigul* of *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* — *kiddesh halachem*, the bread is sanctified. So a *shechitat pigul* is sufficient to generate *kedushat haguf* in the bread. The implication appears to be that *pigul* acts are still halachically significant for purposes of *kedushah* — and if for *kedushah*, why not for *me'ilah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא פִּיגּוּל מַיְיתֵי לַהּ לִידֵי מְעִילָה! אִישְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **Evidently,** sprinkling the blood of offerings of lesser sanctity with the intent of ***piggul* subjects** offerings of lesser sanctity **to** the *halakhot* of **misuse.** This apparently contradicts the opinion that Abaye stated that Rav Giddel said that Rav said. Abaye **was silent,** and was unable to resolve the difficulty against Rav's statement.

Abaye was silenced. He went before Rabbi Abba, who then reformulated the response.

It should be noted, as *Tosafot* emphasizes, that the bread was never subject to *me'ilah* to begin with — it is always *mutar ba'achila*, either to the *be'alim* or to the *kohanim*, and is therefore never *kodshei Hashem* in the *me'ilah* sense. The argument from the bread case is therefore analogical in nature, not direct, and the *gemara* will ultimately reject the analogy.

### Shechita v. Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When** Abaye **came before Rabbi Abba** and reported Rav Pappa's comments, Rabbi Abba **said to** Abaye: That is not a difficulty, as Rav Giddel said that the intent of *piggul* **during sprinkling** does not subject offerings of lesser sanctity to the *halakhot* of misuse. Since the offering is rendered full-fledged *piggul* and is disqualified, it cannot become subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. By contrast, the mishna in *Menaḥot* is referring specifically to the intent of *piggul* during slaughtering, whereas there was no such intent during sprinkling. In such a case, the sprinkling was performed with proper intent, and therefore it subjects the offering to the *halakhot* of misuse.

Rav Ashi offered the following response: *amar lei, b'zerikah* — the distinction lies in the *zerikah*. Following *Tosafot*'s reading, the argument runs as follows. The *hava amina* in the original statement of Rav Giddal in the name of Rav was specifically about *zerikat pigul* — that since *zerikat pigul* is *meratzeh le'pigulo*, perhaps it should affect *me'ilah* status. The answer is that it does not. *Zerikat pigul* and *shechitat pigul* are not the same for these purposes.

The key lies in the moment at which *me'ilah* status changes. In the *avoda* of *kodashim*, what changes the *me'ilah* status? For *kodshei kodashim*, a valid *zerikah* causes the meat to become *mutar la'kohanim* and thereby exit *me'ilah*. For *kodashim kalim*, a valid *zerikah* renders the *imurin* fit for the *mizbe'ach* and thereby enters *me'ilah*. In both directions, it is *zerikah* that is the operative moment of change. At that moment — when the *zerikah* is performed — if it is a *zerikat pigul*, the *pigul* is fully operative, and therefore the *zerikah* is entirely ineffective at changing the *me'ilah* status. This is not so for *shechita*: *shechita* is not ordinarily the moment at which *me'ilah* status changes, and a *shechitat pigul*, as will be explained shortly, does not carry the fully operative force of *pigul* at that stage.

## Pigul Labeling

### Ula's Kometz Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרָבָא: וְהָא אָמַר עוּלָּא: קוֹמֶץ פִּיגּוּל שֶׁהֶעֱלוֹ לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – פָּקַע פִּיגּוּלוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rava,** with regard to this explanation of Rav Giddel's statement: **But doesn't Ulla say:** In the case of **a handful** that is ***piggul*,** which is unfit for sacrifice, **that was brought up onto the altar** to be sacrificed and was burned by the fire of the altar, **its *piggul*** status **lapsed from it,** and it is fit for sacrifice?

The question of when *pigul* status is operative — at *shechita* or only at *zerikah* — is developed through a separate principle. Rav Ashi invoked the teaching of Ulla: *kometz pigul she'he'elah legavei mizbe'ach paka pigulo mimenu* — a *kometz* that was designated as *pigul* and then placed upon the *mizbe'ach*, once burned on the *mizbe'ach*, loses its *pigul* designation. The significance here is the term *kometz pigul* — a *kometz* that has already been *labeled as pigul*, even before the offering's *avodah* is complete.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְמִיצָה הַיְינוּ שְׁחִיטָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** since the **removal of** the **handful** in the case of the meal offering **is** the same as **slaughtering** animal offerings, i.e., it is the equivalent rite, and in this case the improper intent during the removal of the handful rendered the meal offering *piggul*, the same should apply to slaughtering in the case of an animal offering: The sacrificial portions of the offerings of lesser sanctity should be rendered full-fledged *piggul* by slaughtering alone with the intent of *piggul*, regardless of the intent during sprinkling. If so, Rabbi Abba's answer above is incorrect.

The *kometz* corresponds to *shechita* in the process of a *mincha* — it is the initial act, analogous to *shechita*, prior to the *haktara* on the *mizbe'ach* (analogous to *zerikah*). Yet we call it *kometz pigul* already, before the *avodah* is finished.

The force of this observation is that we label an act as *pigul* before the *zerikah* is complete, which would seem to undercut the reasoning that *shechitat pigul* cannot be called *pigul* at that moment. If we call the *kometz pigul*, we are calling *shechita*-stage acts *pigul* already.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיסּוּרָא דְּמַיְיתֵי לִידֵי פִיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Ashi: This is not a difficulty, as Ulla did not mean that the intent during the removal of the handful alone rendered the meal offering full-fledged *piggul*. Rather, he meant that **a prohibited act** was performed upon it **which brings** it to a status of ***piggul*,** but the full status of *piggul* is attained only if there is also improper intent at the time of the sacrificing of the handful, which is equivalent to sprinkling in the case of an animal.

The response is: *isurei d'mati le'pigul* — when we use the term *kometz pigul* or similar formulations before the *avodah* is finished, we do not mean the offering has actually achieved *pigul* status in a legally binding sense. We mean it is a *kometz* that is on track to become *pigul*. The terminology is anticipatory, not constitutive.

### Ilfa Two Avodot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְהָאָמַר אִילְפָא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט סִימָן רִאשׁוֹן חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וְסִימָן שֵׁנִי חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ״, אֲבָל בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עֵירוּבֵי מַחְשָׁבוֹת הֲוַאי!
>
> **Translation:** There is a dispute in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 29b) with regard to two consecutive improper intentions. Rabbi Yehuda maintains that if there first was intent to partake of an offering or to burn the portions consumed on the altar beyond its designated time, which renders it *piggul* and therefore one who consumes it would be liable to receive *karet*, and then there was intent to perform that act outside its designated area, which merely disqualifies the offering, it is *piggul*. But if the order is reversed, it is merely disqualified. The Rabbis rule that in either case it is only disqualified. With that mishna in mind, **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: But doesn't Ilfa say:** This **disagreement** applies **in** a case where the different intentions occurred during the performance of **two** different sacrificial **rites?** Ilfa elaborated: **For example, if one said: I am hereby slaughtering** the **first one of the organs that must be severed in ritual slaughter,** i.e., either the trachea or the esophagus, with the intention of consuming the offering **beyond its** designated **time; and** he then slaughtered its **second** organ with the intention of consuming it **outside its** designated **area,** then these *halakhot* apply. **But** if both intentions occurred **in** the course of **one rite,** e.g., during the slaughtering of the same organ, then **everyone,** including Rabbi Yehuda, agrees that this **constitutes a mixture of intentions,** and the offering is not rendered *piggul*. This example indicates that slaughtering alone with intent beyond its designated time renders the offering *piggul*, contrary to the opinion of Rav Giddel.

Ravina pressed further. He invoked a ruling associated with Ilfa regarding cases where there are two distinct *avoda* acts, each carrying a different *machshava*: for instance, one performed *shechita* with a thought of *chutz lizmano* (a *pigul* intent) and then performed *zerikah* with a thought of *chutz limkomo* (a *pesul* intent, but not *pigul*). The question is whether the *chutz limkomo* thought at *zerikah* prevents the offering from acquiring *pigul* status. Ilfa rules that this is a genuine dispute — *machlokes bishtei avodos*. The two acts are treated as distinct, and the *machshavot* at each stage interact in a complex way.

Crucially, Ilfa gives a concrete example of *shtei avodos* even within a single *shechita*: if one declared, *hareini shochet siman rishon chutz lizmano, siman sheni chutz limkomo* — the first *siman* of the *shechita* with *chutz lizmano*, the second *siman* with *chutz limkomo* — this counts as *shtei avodos*. The two *simanim* are treated as distinct acts. This matters because it implies that even at the stage of *shechita*, the precise content and sequence of *machshavot* is tracked in a way that affects *pigul* status — which in turn suggests we are already treating the *shechita* acts as relevant to *pigul* determination at the time they occur.

### Ravina Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי, לְכִי זָרֵיק תִּיגַּלֵּי מִילְּתָא. אִי בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת, אִי בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi answered Ravina: **Indeed,** Rav Giddel agrees with this ruling. Although he maintains that slaughtering alone does not render an offering *piggul*, nevertheless, he agrees that **when** the blood is **sprinkled** afterward with *piggul* intent, it **will be revealed** whether there was *piggul* intent in the course of one rite or two rites. Consequently, **if** both intentions occurred **during one** sacrificial **rite,** everyone agrees that the offering is not *piggul*, despite the fact that the sprinkling was performed with *piggul* intent. But **if** the two intentions took place **during two** different sacrificial **rites,** then the question of whether it is *piggul* or merely disqualified is subject to the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis. Yet, the offering cannot be established as *piggul* until after the sprinkling.

Ravina's objection is pointed: if one maintains that *pigul* status is not fully determined until *zerikah*, and that prior language is merely anticipatory, why would the order of *machshavot* at *shechita* matter? If *pigul* status is only determined at the moment of *zerikah*, then what was thought at *shechita* versus what was thought at *zerikah* should be assessed from the perspective of that later moment. It should always be a case of *shtei avodos*, since the *machshavot* at *zerikah* are always distinct from those at *shechita*. The very fact that the dispute is framed around what happened at *shechita* implies that *pigul* identity is being tracked and attributed from the moment of *shechita* — not merely anticipated, but substantially established.

### Retroactive Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, גַּבֵּי תוֹדָה נָמֵי, עַד דְּזָרֵיק!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But **if so, with regard to a thanks offering,** where it is stated that if the priest slaughtered it with the intent to partake of it or to burn the portions consumed on the altar beyond its designated time, then the loaves are sanctified (see 3b), the intention only at the time of the slaughtering should **also** not render the offering *piggul* **until** the blood is **sprinkled.** Since the mishna is apparently referring to a regular case of *piggul*, it must be speaking of a situation where the sprinkling was also performed with the *piggul* intent, which is how Ilfa explained the mishna on *Zevaḥim* 29b. If so, according to Rav Giddel, who maintains that slaughtering with the intent of *piggul* does not bring offerings of lesser sanctity into the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, how could the loaves be considered sanctified?

The resolution is as follows: *lechi zarik igla'ei milsa ei be'avodah achas ei bishtei avodos* — when the *zerikah* is finally performed, it retroactively reveals what occurred: whether it was a case of *avodah achas* (a single act) or *shtei avodos* (two distinct acts). The *zerikah* is still required to complete and vest the *pigul* status, and one is not *chayav karet* for eating the offering until the *zerikah* is done. But critically, the *label* of *pigul* does not affix at the moment of *zerikah* going forward — it applies retroactively to the moment of *shechita*. This is why order matters: the question of which *machshava* came first is assessed at the time the *machshavot* occurred, not at the time of *zerikah*.

This retroactive model has direct implications for the bread case. The claim was that *shechitat pigul* could sanctify the bread because at the moment of *shechita* the *pigul* was not yet fully operative. The objection was: but once *zerikah* is done, retroactively it was *pigul* from *shechita* — so how did the bread get sanctified? The *gemara* now has to address this directly.

## Zrikat Pigul (II)

### Toda-Bread Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״קָדַשׁ״ – לִיפָּסֵל, דְּבָעֵי שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What** does the mishna mean when it says that the loaves are considered **sanctified?** It does not mean that they are sanctified in the sense that they are subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. Rather, it means that they are sanctified to the extent that they have the possibility **to be disqualified,** to the extent **that** they **require burning** afterward.

The challenge from the *toda*-bread case now returns for its final resolution. If *pigul* status retroactively dates to the moment of *shechita*, one might ask: does this mean that the *shechita* retroactively fails to sanctify the bread? Rav Ashi's answer is: *mai kiddesh lifaseil ulvei sreifa* — the *shechita* did sanctify the bread, but only to the degree of making it *pasul* and subject to burning. The *shechitat pigul* is halachically significant — it achieves *kedushat haguf* in the sense that the bread can now become *pasul belina* and must be destroyed — but it does not achieve the level of sanctification that would be relevant to *me'ilah*.

This is the critical distinction. *Me'ilah* requires that an offering be genuinely *kodshei Hashem* in the operative sense — that the *avodah* be fully valid and capable of producing the relevant status changes: meat becoming *mutar la'kohanim*, or *imurim* becoming fit for the *mizbe'ach*. A *shechitat pigul* or *zerikat pigul* can produce partial halachic effects — it can produce *kedushat haguf* in the sense of *pesul* and *sreifa* — but it cannot produce the full, valid transformation of status that *me'ilah* law requires. The analogy from the bread case ultimately fails precisely because the bread never carried *me'ilah* to begin with; its sanctification through *shechitat pigul* is a question of *kedushah* in a narrower sense, not of *me'ilah*. For *me'ilah*, the standard remains a completely valid *avodah*.
