# Meilah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Core Concepts

### Textual Background

Masechet Meilah presents some notable features that distinguish it from the standard Babylonian Talmud text. Like certain other tractates, the language deviates from what one expects in terms of grammar, diction, and overall style — a phenomenon that has prompted discussion among scholars as to whether the final editing was carried out in a different Babylonian academy, whether Nehardea, Sura, or Pumbedita. The consensus today leans toward attributing the unusual linguistic character to the distinctive editorial environment of one of these schools rather than to an Eretz Yisrael provenance, though the question has been raised. The *Rashi* throughout is standard *Rashi*, and the *Tosafos* here is *Tosafos Shantz* — a mix of commentary-style *Tosafos* and more critical analytical engagement.

### Meilah Definition

The *issur d'Oraisa* of *me'ilah* operates as follows: when someone misuses *hekdesh* unintentionally — *b'shogeg* — they are obligated to bring an *asham meilos*, pay back the principal, and add a *chomesh*, an extra fifth (which is technically a quarter of the new total). If the violation was committed deliberately — *b'meizid* — there is no *korban* and no fifth; restitution of the principal alone is required. It is therefore worth noting that acting *b'shogeg* carries more formal consequences in terms of *kapparah* and atonement, a dynamic familiar from earlier discussions in Masechet Keritot.

This also clarifies what the Mishnah and Gemara mean when they use the term *lo ma'al*. That phrase does not indicate that the act was permitted. If someone acted *b'meizid*, we say *lo ma'al* — meaning they do not fall within the Torah's specific category of *me'ilah* in the sense of incurring the obligation of an *asham meilos* and the additional fifth. But the act is certainly not *muttar*, and there may be other consequences. The term *lo ma'al* is narrow in its application.

### Meilah d'Oraita vs d'Rabbanan

Alongside *me'ilah d'Oraisa*, there exists a concept of *me'ilah d'rabbanan*. This is distinct from a simple *issur hana'ah*: when the Gemara says *lo nehenin*, that is an *issur d'rabbanan* against deriving benefit, but the weight of *me'ilah d'rabbanan* is greater. The Rabbis effectively expanded the *d'Oraisa* category and gave it additional force. As the Gemara established yesterday, in cases that technically fall outside *me'ilah d'Oraisa*, the Rabbis may still designate the situation as *me'ilah d'rabbanan*. Since it is *d'rabbanan*, there is of course no *korban* and no fifth — the Gemara made that explicit — but the Rabbis clearly created a distinct category, evidenced by the fact that they still required repayment of principal. This makes *me'ilah d'rabbanan* meaningfully different from a mere prohibition against *hana'ah*.

### Theft vs Benefit

On a conceptual level — a question to be examined more thoroughly in a later *perek* — there is a fundamental question about how to understand *me'ilah*: is it a form of stealing from *hekdesh*, or is it primarily about deriving benefit from something that does not belong to one, without that necessarily constituting theft? The practical difference emerges in a neat test case: if one drinks from a vessel of *hekdesh*, one has benefited, yet *hekdesh* has not lost anything tangible. Nevertheless, there is *me'ilah* in such a case. Conversely, if one takes money belonging to *hekdesh* and transfers it to another person as a gift, one has not personally benefited, yet one has clearly acted in a manner resembling theft. It may be that there are in fact two conceptually distinct dimensions of *me'ilah*, and the question of how to characterize the *aveirah* is itself significant.

### Shiur and Kedushat Hashem

The *shiur* of *me'ilah* is a *prutah*. This is not measured like a *shiur* of *issur achila* by a *kezayis*, but by monetary value. The relevant question is whether one frames this as: how much money has been removed from *hekdesh*, or how much benefit has one derived from something to which one had no right? If the focus is on benefit, then one could in theory benefit a *prutah*'s worth from an object that has no market value — for instance, if the object is *assur b'hana'ah* and belongs to the *mizbei'ach*, like an *olah*, it carries no market value, yet if one eats it, one has received *hana'ah* of a *prutah*. The measurement would accordingly focus on the benefit derived rather than the market value of the object itself. This distinction was discussed in Keritot in connection with the question of whether the object must be worth a *prutah* — and the answer, at least for most scenarios, is that the operative measure is the benefit, not the market value.

This is directly relevant to the Gemara under discussion, which addresses *korbanot* that are *pasul* — slaughtered at night, with the *zerikah* performed in the wrong place, or with a *machsheves piggul* — yet to which *me'ilah* still applies. The reason is that the question of *me'ilah* is not about the object's value but about whether a *prutah*'s worth of benefit was derived. And the only genuinely relevant question becomes whether one can characterize the object as *kodshei Hashem* — not merely as *kadosh* in some sense, but as specifically designated to God at a level that triggers *me'ilah*.

### Objects & Korban Types

The objects to which *me'ilah* applies are determined by the type of *korban* in question. There is no *me'ilah* on the portions of *korbanot* that may be eaten — for example, the meat of a *shelamim*, or the meat of a *chatat* and *asham* after the blood has been sprinkled and the meat becomes permitted to the *kohanim*. *Me'ilah* requires something that is entirely devoted to God — *mikodshei Hashem*. 

The clearest case is *bedek ha-bayis*: one has no rights to it whatsoever. Among *korbanot*, *me'ilah* applies to the portions destined for the *mizbei'ach*. For *shelamim*, which is *kodshim kalim* and is viewed as essentially belonging to the owner — some hold it is *mamon be'alim*, and in any case it is not fundamentally characterized as God's — there is no *me'ilah*, with the exception of the *imurin*. And even the *imurin* acquire their specific designation only after the *shechita* and *zerikah*, at which point they are fit to go upon the *mizbei'ach*. At the other extreme is the *olah*, to which no one has any claim at any stage (apart perhaps from the hide), and therefore *me'ilah* applies from the moment of sanctification all the way through to the burning.

The intermediate cases are *chatat* and *asham*, which are *kodshei kodashim*. Fundamentally they begin as entirely God's, as opposed to *kodshim kalim*. The *kohanim*'s eating of the meat is a *heter* that comes later — after *zerikat ha-dam*, the meat of the *chatat* that becomes permitted to the *kohanim* is no longer subject to *me'ilah*. Everything else, however, remains *me'ilah*. Until the *zerikah*, even the meat is considered entirely God's, and then through *zerikah* it becomes *muttar la-kohanim*.

### Masechet Framework

One should be prepared for the fact that most of the first *perakim* of the tractate are not about the act of *me'ilah* itself but about defining the objects and examining the different types of *kedushah* they possess. Questions arise such as: if one sanctifies a tree and fruit grows from it, is the fruit subject to *me'ilah*? If one sanctifies a well and rainwater gathers in it, is there *me'ilah* on the rainwater? These concern cases where *kedushah* is derivative or arises indirectly. Anticipate, then, that the first two-thirds of the tractate are predominantly concerned with the objects of *kedushah*, and the act of *me'ilah* itself receives its fullest treatment toward the end.

One final point bears directly on the Gemara we are about to enter. The *shiur* of *me'ilah* is normally a *prutah*, but a question arose in Keritot about whether the measure is the object's market value or the benefit derived. For most of our discussion, the focus is on whether one derived a *prutah* of benefit — not on the market value of the object. This is especially relevant because the Gemara here addresses *korban pasul* — a *korban* slaughtered at night, or with *zerikah* in the wrong place, or with *machsheves piggul*. Such a *korban* began as *kadosh* and *assur*, is now *pasul* and will have to be burned, yet *me'ilah* still applies — because it is not about the object's value but about whether a *prutah* of benefit was derived.

The only truly relevant question, which becomes central to the first several chapters of *me'ilah*, is whether the object can be labeled *kodshei Hashem*. This is not equivalent to asking whether it is *kadosh* — of course it remains *kadosh*; we did not make it *chullin*. Rather, the question is whether something so fundamentally wrong was done to it that, despite its continued *kedushah*, it loses the specific designation of *kodshei Hashem* for *me'ilah* purposes. One example is the case of an animal that was slaughtered entirely outside the *Beis Hamikdash*: it retains *kedushah* and remains subject to *issur hana'ah*, but it is no longer *kodshei Hashem*. It has been so profoundly misdirected and will be *holech l'ibbud* — destroyed — that it no longer carries that designation for purposes of *me'ilah*.

The first observation the Gemara made yesterday, then, is this: when things go wrong in a manner that is still *within the ballpark* — what is called *psulo bakodesh*, a technical ritual flaw in how the *avodah* was performed rather than something that takes the *korban* entirely out of the *mikdash* context — it is not the same as slaughtering outside or allowing it to die. A *psulo bakodesh* flaw does not strip the *korban* of its *kodshei Hashem* status, and *me'ilah* therefore still applies. And the test case for whether something is *psulo bakodesh* is whether *alu lo yardu* — if once placed on the *mizbei'ach* it may remain there, that shows it is still sufficiently within the category of *kodshei Hashem*, even if it should not have been placed there to begin with.

## Rabbah vs Rav Yosef

### Three Mishnayot Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְרַבָּה חֲדָא מִגּוֹ חֲדָא, וַחֲדָא מִגּוֹ חֲדָא:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection to Rabba, one** source **from** another **one, and** that **one from** another **one,** i.e., his objection is based upon the combination of several sources.

The Gemara now states: *eiseivi Rav Yosef le-Rava* — Rav Yosef challenged Rava, who had maintained that if one slaughtered in the wrong place, the principle of *alu lo yardu* applies. The challenge is introduced with the formulation *chada migu chada ve-chada migu chada* — one built from one and one built from one. That is to say: three Mishnahs are strung together in sequence. Something is derived from Mishna A that clarifies what Mishna B is saying, and from what we learn in Mishna B we are able to understand what Mishna C is saying — and from Mishna C, a pointed question can be raised. The logic proceeds through all three in order before the challenge lands.

### Melikah & Non-Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן, אֵין מְטַמְּאִים בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה וּמוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, חוּץ מֵחַטַּאת הָעוֹף שֶׁעָשָׂה לְמַטָּה, כְּמַעֲשֵׂה חַטַּאת הָעוֹף לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna on *Zevaḥim* 66b, which deals with bird offerings that were disqualified for having either the napes of their necks pinched or their blood sprinkled in the wrong place, teaches: **And all of** the offerings mentioned in that mishna do **not** render one who swallows their meat **ritually impure** to the extent that his **garments are rendered impure** when it is **in the throat.** Although the offering is disqualified, since the nape of its neck was pinched as part of the rite one is not rendered impure by swallowing an olive-bulk of its meat, as is the *halakha* with regard to a carcass of a kosher bird. **And** one who derives benefit from these offerings is liable for **misusing them.** This is the *halakha* concerning all bird offerings, **except for the bird sin offering that one performed below** the red line, **according to the procedure of a bird sin offering,** and **for the sake of a sin offering.** Since it was sacrificed correctly, its meat is permitted to the priests and is no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse.

The first Mishna states: *ve-chulan ein mitamin begadim beveit hablia* — all of them do not cause *tumah* to garments through swallowing. The subject is birds — an *olas ha-of* or *chatas ha-of* — where the *melikah* was performed in the wrong place. For a bird slaughtered outside the context of a *korban*, the halachic category would be *neveilah*, and the *halacha* of a *neveilah* bird is that eating it causes *tumah* to garments at the moment of swallowing. The statement *lo mitame begadim beveit hablia* is therefore a way of saying: this bird does not carry the *halacha* of *neveilah*, at least for this purpose.

One might have thought that performing a *psul melikah* — in the wrong place — renders the bird a *neveilah*. The Mishna says no: because it is *psulo bakodesh*, a technical ritual flaw in how the *avodah* was performed rather than something analogous to strangling the bird outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, it does not fully change the bird's identity. We do not say it is as though it was strangled. The *psul melikah* is enough within the ballpark that the bird will not be considered a *neveilah*. Hence *ve-chulan* — all birds where the *melikah* was done incorrectly — *ein mitamin begadim beveit hablia*, they do not render one *tamei* through swallowing, and additionally *u-moalin bahen* — there is still *issur me'ilah* — precisely because it is *psulo bakodesh*, and although it cannot be brought as a valid *korban*, the *me'ilah* status remains intact.

The one exception is a *chatas ha-of* where everything was in fact done correctly: below, on the lower half of the *mizbei'ach*, as required, and for the sake of a *chatas*. In such a case, the *kohanim* eat the meat, and therefore the meat of the correctly offered *chatas ha-of* is not subject to *me'ilah*. But setting that aside, the fundamental principle is that an *of* done incorrectly retains *issur me'ilah* because it is *psulah bakodesh*, which is insufficient to remove it from the category of *kodshei Hashem*.

### Psulah Bakodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ: כׇּל שֶׁהָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה, וְכׇל שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in another mishna (*Zevaḥim* 68b) **with regard to** the same disqualified bird offerings: For **any** one of them **whose disqualification** occurred **in the sacred** area, i.e., the Temple courtyard, e.g., if the nape of its neck was pinched at night, **it does not render** the **garments** of one who swallows it **impure** when the meat is **in the throat. And** for **any** one of them **whose disqualification did not** take place **in the sacred** area, **it does render** the **garments** of one who swallows it **impure** when the meat is **in the throat.**

The Gemara then notes that another Mishna explicitly conceptualizes this: *kol shepsula bakodesh* — any case that falls within the category of *psula bakodesh* — *eina metamei begadim b'veit habliah*. Where a technical ritual error is made in performing the *melikah*, we do not classify the bird as a *neveilah*, and it will not cause *tumah b'veit habliah*. The contribution of this second Mishna over the first is not just restating the rule for the wrong-place case, but providing the conceptual label: *psulah bakodesh*. The first Mishna described what went wrong; the second Mishna names the category — a technical problem in how the ritual was performed within the *kodesh* context. This framing of *psulah bakodesh* then becomes the operative concept for what follows.

### Alu Lo Yardu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי: כׇּל שֶׁהָיָה פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – אִם עָלוּ, לֹא יֵרְדוּ. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in yet another mishna (*Zevaḥim* 84a): With regard to **any** offering **whose disqualification** took place **in the sacred** area, the sacred area renders the offering acceptable, and **if** such offerings **ascended** onto the altar they **shall not descend.** The first mishna establishes that birds disqualified because their rites were performed in the wrong place are subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. The second mishna teaches that these same birds are considered items whose disqualification occurred in the sacred area. The last mishna establishes that if items whose disqualification occurred in the sacred area ascend the altar, they do not descend. By citing all these *mishnayot*, Rav Yosef demonstrates that an offering that is slaughtered in the wrong place is not removed from the altar, contrary to Rabba's opinion. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rabba** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

The third Mishna states: *kol shehayah psula bakodesh im alu lo yerdu* — anything in the category of *psulah bakodesh*, once it goes up on the *mizbei'ach*, does not come down. Conversely, *ve-chol shelo hayah psula bakodesh* — if the *psul* was not of the *bakodesh* variety, as when the bird was strangled or something of that nature — *metamei begadim b'veit habliah*, it would be a *neveilah*. The correlation is thus complete: *psulah bakodesh* means it is not considered a *neveilah*, *me'ilah* applies, and *alu lo yardu*.

### Rav Yosef's Inference

Stringing all three Mishnahs together yields the following conclusion: if something is *psulah bakodesh*, there is still *me'ilah*, and if it is *psulah bakodesh*, it remains on the *mizbei'ach* once placed there. Conversely, if it is not *psulah bakodesh*, it is treated as a *neveilah*, there is no *me'ilah*, and it is removed from the *mizbei'ach*.

Now what had Rava maintained? Rava had said that when one slaughters in the wrong place, the *korban* must be taken down from the *mizbei'ach*. If it must come down, that is precisely because we are *not* calling it *psulah bakodesh* — for the principle is that *psulah bakodesh* remains on the *mizbei'ach*. But if we are not calling it *psulah bakodesh*, then by the rule of the second Mishna, there is no *me'ilah*. How, then, can Rava say there is still *me'ilah* in such a case? The argument flowing through the three Mishnahs neatly demonstrates: if the *korban* must be removed from the *mizbei'ach*, one is effectively conceding it is not *psulah bakodesh*, and if it is not *psulah bakodesh*, it cannot be *kodshei Hashem* for purposes of *me'ilah*.

### Rava's Acceptance

*Tiyuvta de'Rava tiyuvta* — the Gemara accepts this as a definitive refutation, and Rava's position is overturned. The significance is that we now have a clear articulation of the concept of *kodshei Hashem*: what constitutes *kodshei Hashem* — beyond the basic *korban* — is whether the object remains in the domain of the *mizbei'ach*. If it is *psulah bakodesh* such that it is permitted to remain on the *mizbei'ach* even though it should not have been placed there, it is still *kodshei Hashem* for *me'ilah*. Once it is not even close enough for that — once one would remove it from the *mizbei'ach* — it is no longer *kodshei Hashem*, even though it technically retains *kedushah*.

It is also worth noting that Rava had attempted to counter the earlier form of this argument by saying: even if it is not *me'ilah d'Oraisa*, there is *me'ilah d'rabbanan*. But the Mishna's statement of *ein me'ilah* appears categorical — not *d'Oraisa* and not *d'rabbanan*. The rule is: if something is indicated as not *psulah bakodesh* by virtue of being taken off the *mizbei'ach*, there is no *me'ilah* at all, neither *d'Oraisa* nor *d'rabbanan*, in that specific scenario.

## Olat Bamot Yachid Case

### Bamot Yachid Background

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּפְלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עוֹלַת בָּמַת יָחִיד, שֶׁהִכְנִיסָהּ לִפְנִים – קְלָטוּהָ מְחִיצוֹת לְכׇל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And that** matter with regard to **which Rabba and Rav Yosef disagree** is **obvious to Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar says:** In the case of **a burnt offering** consecrated to be sacrificed **on a personal altar,** during the period when it is permitted to do so, **that one brought inside** the Temple courtyard, it is **admitted by the partitions** of the courtyard **for all matters;** and from that point onward it has the status of a burnt offering sacrificed in the Temple with regard to all *halakhot*.

The Gemara now transitions to a related question raised by Rabbi Elazar. Its framing presupposes one side of the Rabbah–Rav Yosef debate, which shows that Rabbi Elazar took that base case as settled and was asking about a more complex scenario built on top of it.

Some background is necessary. During the period of Nob and Givon, there was both a central *mishkan* and individual *bamot yachid* — private altars that individuals were permitted to use (*she'as heter bamos*). When bringing on one's private *bamah yachid*, a wide range of leniencies applied: no *kohen* was required, no *klei shares* were needed, there was no obligation to slaughter *b'tzafon* for an *olah*, and one had considerably more latitude overall. At the same time, the central *mishkan* at Nob and Givon had stricter requirements and could receive certain *korbanot* — *chovos*, *korban tzibur* — that could not be brought on a private *bamah*.

### Bamah Sanctified, Mikvash…

Rabbi Elazar's case is this: *olas bamas yachid shehichnisah lifnim* — an *olah* that was sanctified for one's own private *bamah yachid*, and then the owner decided to bring it to the central *mizbei'ach* at Nob or Givon. Having sanctified it for the *bamah yachid*, the owner had greater latitude — no requirement of *b'tzafon*, no strict *makom* requirements. But now that the animal has actually been brought to the central *makom*, one must follow the rules of that place.

### Mechitzot Kaltu

The *halacha* is *kaltuha mechitzos l'kol davar* — the enclosing walls of the central *mishkan* absorb it for every purpose. That is to say: being brought within that space means one is now governed by all the requirements of that place. The environment of the central *mizbei'ach* at Nob and Givon absorbs the *korban* and demands that its service conform to those stricter rules.

### Rabbi Elazar's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: [עוֹלַת בָּמַת יָחִיד שֶׁהִכְנִיסָהּ, שֶׁנִּפְסְלָה] – עָלוּ, מַהוּ שֶׁיֵּרְדוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: In this regard, **Rabbi Elazar asks:** In the case of **a burnt offering** consecrated to be sacrificed **on a personal altar that one brought into** the Temple courtyard **and** it **was rendered unfit** for sacrifice, e.g., it was slaughtered in the south rather than its proper place in the north, **if** its limbs **ascended** the altar to be sacrificed, **what is** the *halakha* **as** to whether **they should descend?** Although the animal was disqualified due to the change in location, had it been slaughtered in the south on a personal altar it would not have been disqualified.

Given that the *mechitzos kaltu* — that once brought within the central *makom* the animal is now subject to full requirements — Rabbi Elazar asks: *olas bamas yachid shehichnisua she'nifselah alu mah she-yardu* — what is the *halacha* if this *olah*, after being brought inside, was rendered *pasul* in a way that would not have been a *psul* had it remained a *bamah yachid* offering?

As *Rashi* explains, the case is specifically one where, for example, the animal was slaughtered *badarom* — in the south rather than the north. On a *bamah yachid* there is no *makom hamikdash*, no *tzafon* requirement, so slaughtering in the south would have been perfectly acceptable. But now that it has been brought to the central *makom* and is subject to those rules, that *shechita badarom* constitutes a *psul*. The question: if it was put on the *mizbei'ach*, does it stay or come down?

### Identity vs Entitlement

This is a genuinely complex question because one can argue it in two directions — and remarkably, one can construct an argument each way whether one holds like Rabbah or like Rav Yosef.

Starting with the easier direction: Rabbah had said that in a standard case of *shechitah badarom*, the animal must come down from the *mizbei'ach*. But one might say that Rabbah's reasoning was *bimechitzah kitiknah paslah* — it was the full *mikdash* environment, demanding *b'tzafon*, that made the *shechitah badarom* a genuine *psul* that mandates removal. By contrast, *shelo kitiknah*, in a case that was not originally within those standard requirements — where the *kedushah* was for a *bamah yachid* that permitted slaughter anywhere — perhaps Rabbah would say *lo paslah*, it is not so severe a *psul*, and it may remain.

Conversely, one can argue from Rav Yosef's direction. Rav Yosef holds that in a standard case *im alah lo yerad* — once it goes up, it stays. But perhaps even Rav Yosef would say this *bamah yachid* case is *worse*. Why? Because *mechitzah kitiknah kaltah*: when a *korban* was sanctified for and brought entirely within the *mikdash* context, the *mechitzot* absorb it and grant it, so to speak, the standing to remain on the *mizbei'ach* once placed there. But *shelo kitiknah lo kaltah*: if the *korban* was fundamentally sanctified for outside — for a *bamah yachid* — and was merely brought in afterward, it was never a *mikdash korban* at its core. It therefore lacks the entitlement, the right of belonging, that would allow it to remain on the *mizbei'ach*. The question thus turns on two distinct frameworks: one based on the *identity* of the *psul* (is the flaw mild enough given the original latitude?), the other on *entitlement* (does the *korban*'s origin grant it standing to remain?).

This also raises a question worth noting: one might argue that when someone sanctifies an animal for a *bamah yachid*, they are not necessarily excluding the *Beis Hamikdash* — they may simply be sanctifying it *stam*, intending to bring it wherever they choose. If so, the very framing of this as a special *bamah yachid* *kedushah* is itself subject to question. Rabbi Elazar's raising of the question at all implies that he treated *bamah yachid* sanctification as a meaningful distinct category with its own identity.

### Teiku Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לַחֲדָא, מִכְּלַל דְּאִידַּךְ פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, אִי כְּרַבָּה אִי כִּדְרַב יוֹסֵף. חֲדָא מִגּוֹ חֲדָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבָּה הָתָם אִם עָלוּ – יֵרְדוּ, מְחִיצָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ – פָּסְלָה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לֹא פָּסְלָה, אוֹ דִילְמָא: אֲפִילּוּ לְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמַר אִם עָלְתָה – לֹא תֵּרֵד, מְחִיצָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ – קָלְטָה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לָא קָלְטָה. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this inference, as it is possible that Rabbi Elazar was actually uncertain with regard to that case as well; but **he raises one** dilemma **as a result of the other.** In other words, Rabbi Elazar was asking two questions, one predicated on the other, and the second question is valid regardless of whether he holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabba or Rav Yosef. **Even if Rabbi Elazar holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabba, that in a regular case the limbs are removed from the altar, one can claim that Rabba says** that **if** the limbs **ascend** the altar **they shall descend only there,** i.e., if the burnt offering was consecrated for sacrifice in the Temple. In that case, the owner consecrated the offering with the intention of sacrificing it within the **partition** of the courtyard, where slaughtering is performed **properly,** in the north. Consequently, if it was slaughtered in the south, that **renders** the offering entirely **disqualified,** and therefore even if it ascended the altar it must descend. But in a case where he consecrated the offering in order to sacrifice it **improperly** on a personal altar, slaughtering it in the south **does not render** the offering entirely **disqualified,** and therefore if it ascends the altar it does not descend. **Or perhaps,** the opposite is the case: **Even according to** the opinion of **Rav Yosef, who said** that **if** it **ascends** the altar it **shall not descend,** that *halakha* applies only in a case where the owner consecrated the offering in order to sacrifice it within the **partition** of the courtyard, in which case it was consecrated **properly.** In such an instance, the offering is **admitted** by the partitions of the courtyard, and therefore even in a case where it was disqualified, if it ascended the altar it does not descend. By contrast, in a case where he consecrated it in order to sacrifice it **improperly** on a personal altar, the offering is **not admitted** at all by the partitions of the courtyard, which means that even if it ascends the altar it must descend. The Gemara concludes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved **[*teiku*]**.

The Gemara concludes: *teiku* — the question is left unresolved.

## Zrikat Piggul & Meilah

### Rav's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: זְרִיקַת פִּיגּוּל אֵינוֹ מוֹצִיא מִידֵי מְעִילָה בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא לִידֵי מְעִילָה בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel says** that **Rav said: Sprinkling** the blood of **an offering** with **the intent to consume it after its appointed time [*piggul*],** in a case where there was *piggul* intent during the slaughter as well, **does not remove** the meat of the offering **from** the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse, in** the case of **offerings of the most sacred order.** The reason is that its meat is still classified as "the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15), since there was no proper sprinkling that permitted the priests to partake of the offering, as it is *piggul*. Similarly, this sprinkling **does not bring** the sacrificial portions **of offerings of lesser sanctity into** the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** as only a valid sprinkling confers that status.

Having completed the discussion of *psulah bakodesh* and *alu lo yardu*, the Gemara now addresses a distinct but related question. The Mishna's point was not only that these *psulim* do not strip an animal of *kodshei Hashem* status, but that when things are done *b'psul*, certain status changes that would ordinarily occur do not take place. For *kodshei kodashim* like a *chatat* or *asham*, the meat ordinarily ceases to be *me'ilah* after *zerikat ha-dam* permits it to the *kohanim*. But if the service was performed *b'psul* — wrong *makom*, *piggul*, etc. — the meat is never rendered *muttar*, so it remains subject to *me'ilah* permanently. The *chiddush* of the Mishna is not merely that it doesn't lose its *me'ilah* status, but that the meat stays *me'ilah* indefinitely, since the *zerikah* never created the *heter achilah*.

With this as background, Rav Gidel transmits a ruling from Rav: *zrikas piggul eina motzi midei me'ilah b'kodshei kodashim* — if the *zerikah* of the blood was performed with a *machsheves piggul* — thinking to eat the meat *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* — it is not a valid *zerikah*, and therefore does not remove *me'ilah* from the meat of *kodshei kodashim*. The meat remains *me'ilah* since it was never rendered *muttar la-kohanim*.

### Kodshim Kalim Corollary

Correspondingly, *zrikas piggul* also does not create *me'ilah* in *kodshim kalim*. For *kodshim kalim*, the *zerikah* ordinarily triggers *me'ilah* on the *imurin*, since after the *zerikah* the *imurin* are designated for the *mizbei'ach*. But a *zrikas piggul* — an invalid *zerikah* — cannot effect that transformation. The *imurin* of *kodshim kalim* thus do not attain *me'ilah* status through a *zrikas piggul*. 

Both of these rulings appear to follow straightforwardly from the principle of our Mishna: a *psul* in the service does not change the existing *me'ilah* status either way. So what is the *chiddush* of Rav Gidel's statement, if it merely restates the Mishna?

### Tosafot's Hava Amina

*Tosafos* explains the *chiddush* as follows. There is a principle in the laws of *piggul* that requires *meratzeh l'pigulo* — the other *avodos* must have been performed correctly in order for *piggul* to take effect. *Piggul* requires a kind of *ritzuy*; there must be enough valid service surrounding the *machsheves piggul* for it to establish the *korban* as *piggul*. The *machsheves piggul* cannot be embedded within a completely invalid service. This concept is sometimes called *nitpal l'pigulo*.

Given this, one might have had the following *hava amina* specifically regarding *zrikas piggul*: the *zerikah* itself is the act that constitutes *meratzeh l'pigulo* — it is the *zerikah* that establishes the final *piggul* status of the *korban*. Since the *zerikah* plays this elevated role of finalizing *piggul*, perhaps it should also be counted as significant — as if it were a properly functioning *zerikah* — for all other *halachos*, including *me'ilah*. The *zerikah* would then be considered meaningful enough to remove *me'ilah* from the meat of *kodshei kodashim*, or to create *me'ilah* on the *imurin* of *kodshim kalim*. 

Rav Gidel in the name of Rav therefore comes to tell us: no. Despite its role in establishing *piggul*, a *zrikas piggul* does not carry over that significance to the laws of *me'ilah*. The *me'ilah* status is unaffected by the *zerikah*.

## Toda & Bread Case

### Toda Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתֵיב אַבָּיֵי וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא, אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַתּוֹדָה לִפְנִים, וְלַחְמָהּ חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – לֹא קִדֵּשׁ אֶת הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָהּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרְמוּ פָּנֶיהָ בַּתַּנּוּר, וַאֲפִילּוּ קָרְמוּ כּוּלָּן חוּץ מֵאַחַת מֵהֶן – לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָהּ חוּץ לִזְמַנָּהּ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמָהּ – קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Abaye sat and related this *halakha*,** which Rav Giddel said that Rav said. **Rav Pappa raised an objection to Abaye** from a mishna (*Menaḥot* 78b): With regard to **one who slaughters the thanks offering** in its proper place **inside** the Temple courtyard, **and** at that time, **its** forty accompanying **loaves** were **outside the wall,** where it is prohibited to partake of the loaves, **the loaves are not sanctified** by this slaughtering. Likewise, **if he slaughtered** the thanks offering **before the surface** of the loaves **formed a crust in the oven, and** this is the *halakha* **even** if the surface of **all** the loaves **formed a crust except for one of them, the loaves are not sanctified,** even those that had already formed a crust. If the priest **slaughtered** the thanks offering with the intent to partake of it or to burn the portions consumed on the altar **beyond its** designated **time** and it was thereby rendered *piggul*, **or outside its** designated **area** and was disqualified, **the loaves are sanctified** and are subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. This is presumably the case even if he also sprinkled the blood with *piggul* intent, as the mishna does not differentiate in this regard.

The Gemara now records that Abaye was sitting and teaching this tradition — *Rav Gidel amar Rav* — when Rav Pappa challenged him with the following Mishnah. 

The Mishna states: *ha-shochet es ha-toda bifnim ve-lachmah bachutz lo kidesh ha-lachem* — if one slaughters the *toda* inside the *mikdash* while the bread accompanying it is outside, the bread is not sanctified. The *toda* comes with bread, and through the *shechita* of the *toda* the bread is elevated from *kedushas damim* to *kedushas ha-guf*. But for this sanctification to occur, the bread must be in the proper place at the time of *shechita*. If the bread is outside the *mikdash*, it is not connected to the *korban* and the *shechita* does not affect it. Similarly, if the *shechita* took place before the bread had formed a crust in the oven — *ad shelo kermu paneha ba-tanur* — the bread has not yet attained the status of *lechem* and is not sanctified. And even if all but one of the loaves had formed a crust, the Mishna rules *lo kidesh ha-lachem* — none of it is sanctified, since the entire complement of bread must be present and ready; it is all or nothing.

### Piggul Slaughter Case

The Mishna then continues: *shachatah chutz lizmano ve-chutz limkomo* — if the *toda* was slaughtered with a *machsheves piggul*, thinking to eat it *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo*, what happens to the bread? The ruling is *kidesh ha-lachem* — the bread is nonetheless sanctified. A *shechitas piggul*, despite being invalid for the *korban* itself, is still considered significant enough for the purpose of *kedushas ha-guf* of the bread.

### Pappa's Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא פִּיגּוּל מַיְיתֵי לַהּ לִידֵי מְעִילָה! אִישְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **Evidently,** sprinkling the blood of offerings of lesser sanctity with the intent of ***piggul* subjects** offerings of lesser sanctity **to** the *halakhot* of **misuse.** This apparently contradicts the opinion that Abaye stated that Rav Giddel said that Rav said. Abaye **was silent,** and was unable to resolve the difficulty against Rav's statement.

From this Mishnah, Rav Pappa draws an inference: *shechitas piggul* is halachically significant — it has an effect in the domain of *kedushah*. If *shechitas piggul* can sanctify the bread to *kedushas ha-guf*, this would suggest that a *piggul* act of service carries some meaningful weight for *halacha*.

### Analogy Question

The challenge then follows by analogy: if *shechitas piggul* is significant enough to effect *kedushah*, why do we say that *zrikas piggul* — or, for that matter, *shechitas piggul* in the context of *me'ilah* — has no effect? Why should *piggul* service be meaningful for *kedushah* in one domain but entirely meaningless for *me'ilah*? The argument seems to favor the position that *piggul* acts of service are not as categorically inert as Rav Gidel's ruling implies. Abaye had no ready response — *ishtik*, he was silent.

### Shechita vs Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When** Abaye **came before Rabbi Abba** and reported Rav Pappa's comments, Rabbi Abba **said to** Abaye: That is not a difficulty, as Rav Giddel said that the intent of *piggul* **during sprinkling** does not subject offerings of lesser sanctity to the *halakhot* of misuse. Since the offering is rendered full-fledged *piggul* and is disqualified, it cannot become subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. By contrast, the mishna in *Menaḥot* is referring specifically to the intent of *piggul* during slaughtering, whereas there was no such intent during sprinkling. In such a case, the sprinkling was performed with proper intent, and therefore it subjects the offering to the *halakhot* of misuse.

Rav Ashi then proposed a distinction. The key, he argued, lies in the difference between *shechita* and *zerikah*. The statement of Rav Gidel concerned *zrikas piggul* — a *zerikah* performed with *machsheves piggul*. And the *hava amina* addressed by *Tosafos* was specifically about *zrikas piggul* perhaps being more significant given its role as *meratzeh l'pigulo*. The case of the *toda* bread involves *shechitas piggul*, not *zrikas piggul*. These two must be distinguished — and the Gemara will go on to develop precisely why they are treated differently.

## When Piggul Labeled

### Piggul Labeling Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרָבָא: וְהָא אָמַר עוּלָּא: קוֹמֶץ פִּיגּוּל שֶׁהֶעֱלוֹ לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – פָּקַע פִּיגּוּלוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ, וּקְמִיצָה הַיְינוּ שְׁחִיטָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rava,** with regard to this explanation of Rav Giddel's statement: **But doesn't Ulla say:** In the case of **a handful** that is ***piggul*,** which is unfit for sacrifice, **that was brought up onto the altar** to be sacrificed and was burned by the fire of the altar, **its *piggul*** status **lapsed from it,** and it is fit for sacrifice? **And** since the **removal of** the **handful** in the case of the meal offering **is** the same as **slaughtering** animal offerings, i.e., it is the equivalent rite, and in this case the improper intent during the removal of the handful rendered the meal offering *piggul*, the same should apply to slaughtering in the case of an animal offering: The sacrificial portions of the offerings of lesser sanctity should be rendered full-fledged *piggul* by slaughtering alone with the intent of *piggul*, regardless of the intent during sprinkling. If so, Rabbi Abba's answer above is incorrect.

Rav Ashi now poses a deeper challenge. The distinction proposed — that *shechitas piggul* can work for *kedushas ha-guf* of the bread because at the moment of *shechita*, the *piggul* is not yet fully established — rests on the premise that something is not yet labeled *piggul* at the time of *shechita*. The *piggul* status, on this view, only fully crystallizes at the time of *zerikah*, once the service is complete. Therefore at the moment of *shechita*, when the bread is sanctified, the *korban* has not yet become *piggul* in any full sense — it carries only a *machsheves piggul*, an intent, not the status itself. One can thus distinguish: the bread gains its *kedushas ha-guf* at the moment of *shechita*, when *piggul* has not yet been vested, whereas *me'ilah* status changes are determined by *zerikah*, at which point *piggul* is fully operative.

But Rav Ashi challenges this logic: is it truly accurate to say that something is not called *piggul* before the *zerikah* is complete?

### Kometz Piggul Case

To press the point, Rav Ashi cites a statement of Ulla: *kometz piggul she-he'elo legabei mizbei'ach paka pigulo mimenu* — if one took the *kometz* from a *minchah* on which a *machsheves piggul* was had — thinking to eat the *minchah chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* — and then placed that *kometz* on the *mizbei'ach*, the *piggul* status is extinguished once it burns there. The point of the citation is not the ruling about the *mizbei'ach* but what the *kometz* is called before being placed there: *kometz piggul*. We label it *piggul* already. And *kmitzah*, the taking of the *kometz*, is the analogue of *shechita* — it is the first major act of service for a *minchah*, just as *shechita* is the first act for an animal *korban*. Yet we call the *kometz piggul* before the *haktarah* — the burning, which is the analogue of *zerikah* — has been performed. This would suggest that the label *piggul* does attach at the early stage of service, contradicting the premise that at *shechita* the *korban* is not yet *piggul*.

### Labeling vs Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: אִיסּוּרָא דְּמַיְיתֵי לִידֵי פִיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Ashi: This is not a difficulty, as Ulla did not mean that the intent during the removal of the handful alone rendered the meal offering full-fledged *piggul*. Rather, he meant that **a prohibited act** was performed upon it **which brings** it to a status of ***piggul*,** but the full status of *piggul* is attained only if there is also improper intent at the time of the sacrificing of the handful, which is equivalent to sprinkling in the case of an animal.

The Gemara responds: *asokei de-masik lei piggul* — the use of the label *kometz piggul* is not a precise technical statement; it means a *kometz* that *will become piggul*, not that it already possesses *piggul* status in full. We do not literally mean to say it is *piggul* at this stage. The Gemara then adds: *ve-ha katani* — but it was further taught (on *14a*) regarding this *kometz*: *im acheirim mevi lidei piggul hu atzmo lo kol shekein* — if the *kometz* causes other things to become *piggul*, surely it itself should have *piggul* status. This seems again to imply that the *kometz* itself is labeled *piggul* from the stage of *kmitzah*. The Gemara responds similarly: *ha nami issura demati lei lidei piggul* — again, what is meant is not that it is fully *piggul* yet but that it is on the track to becoming *piggul*. The label is anticipatory, not technically precise.

### Ilfa's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר אִילְפָא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּשְׁתֵּי עֲבוֹדוֹת, כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט סִימָן רִאשׁוֹן חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וְסִימָן שֵׁנִי חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ״, אֲבָל בַּעֲבוֹדָה אַחַת – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עֵירוּבֵי מַחְשָׁבוֹת הֲוַאי!
>
> **Translation:** There is a dispute in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 29b) with regard to two consecutive improper intentions. Rabbi Yehuda maintains that if there first was intent to partake of an offering or to burn the portions consumed on the altar beyond its designated time, which renders it *piggul* and therefore one who consumes it would be liable to receive *karet*, and then there was intent to perform that act outside its designated area, which merely disqualifies the offering, it is *piggul*. But if the order is reversed, it is merely disqualified. The Rabbis rule that in either case it is only disqualified. With that mishna in mind, **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: But doesn't Ilfa say:** This **disagreement** applies **in** a case where the different intentions occurred during the performance of **two** different sacrificial **rites?** Ilfa elaborated: **For example, if one said: I am hereby slaughtering** the **first one of the organs that must be severed in ritual slaughter,** i.e., either the trachea or the esophagus, with the intention of consuming the offering **beyond its** designated **time; and** he then slaughtered its **second** organ with the intention of consuming it **outside its** designated **area,** then these *halakhot* apply. **But** if both intentions occurred **in** the course of **one rite,** e.g., during the slaughtering of the same organ, then **everyone,** including Rabbi Yehuda, agrees that this **constitutes a mixture of intentions,** and the offering is not rendered *piggul*. This example indicates that slaughtering alone with intent beyond its designated time renders the offering *piggul*, contrary to the opinion of Rav Giddel.

Ravina then raises a further challenge to Rav Ashi from a teaching of Ilfa. 

### Single vs Two Avodos

Ilfa addresses a debate in the laws of *piggul* concerning cases where multiple *machshavos* are present. If the *shechita* was performed with a *machsheves chutz lizmano* — which is *piggul* — and the *zerikah* was performed with a *machsheves chutz limkomo* — which is a *psul* but not *piggul* — the question is whether the presence of this second invalid *machshava* disrupts the formation of *piggul*. The rule of *meratzeh l'pigulo* requires that the surrounding service be done correctly; another *psul machshava* might be said to prevent the *korban* from being *piggul*. Ilfa rules: *machlokes b'shtei avodos* — this debate applies when the two problematic *machshavos* occur across two distinct *avodos* (such as *shechita* and *zerikah*). But if both problematic *machshavos* occurred within the same *avoda* — for example, during both *simanim* of a single *shechita*, one with *chutz lizmano* and one with *chutz limkomo* — *de'kulei alma eiruv machshavos hu*: everyone agrees that constitutes a *eiruv machshavos*, a mixture of intentions, and the *korban* is not *piggul*. The reason is that when they occur in the same *avoda*, even if sequentially, they are treated as simultaneous contaminating thoughts.

### Retroactive Labeling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי, לְכִי זָרֵיק תִּיגַּלֵּי מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi answered Ravina: **Indeed,** Rav Giddel agrees with this ruling. Although he maintains that slaughtering alone does not render an offering *piggul*, nevertheless, he agrees that **when** the blood is **sprinkled** afterward with *piggul* intent, it **will be revealed** whether there was *piggul* intent in the course of one rite or two rites. Consequently, **if** both intentions occurred **during one** sacrificial **rite,** everyone agrees that the offering is not *piggul*, despite the fact that the sprinkling was performed with *piggul* intent. But **if** the two intentions took place **during two** different sacrificial **rites,** then the question of whether it is *piggul* or merely disqualified is subject to the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis. Yet, the offering cannot be established as *piggul* until after the sprinkling.

Why is this relevant here? Because the debate about *shtei avodos* — two separate acts of service — presupposes that the *piggul* identity of the *korban* is already being shaped by what happens at *shechita*. If *piggul* status were deferred entirely to the moment of *zerikah*, the *machshava* at *shechita* would be irrelevant, and every case would effectively be a *single avoda* question turning only on what was thought at *zerikah*. The fact that the Sages debate the order and interplay of *machshavos* across *shechita* and *zerikah* demonstrates that *piggul* identity begins to form at *shechita*. Ravina thus presses: you cannot say the label of *piggul* is entirely deferred to the *zerikah* moment.

Rav Ashi concedes the point: *lechi zarak tigli milsa* — when one finally performs the *zerikah*, the *piggul* status is revealed retroactively. The label of *piggul* goes back to the *shechita* moment in a retroactive sense — its full *piggul* designation crystallizes at *zerikah* but anchors retroactively to the earlier act. This is a significant concession: the *korban* does come to be labeled *piggul* retroactively from the moment of *shechita*, once *zerikah* is completed.

### Bread Meilah Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, גַּבֵּי תוֹדָה נָמֵי, עַד דְּזָרֵיק! מַאי ״קָדַשׁ״ – לִיפָּסֵל, דְּבָעֵי שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But **if so, with regard to a thanks offering,** where it is stated that if the priest slaughtered it with the intent to partake of it or to burn the portions consumed on the altar beyond its designated time, then the loaves are sanctified (see 3b), the intention only at the time of the slaughtering should **also** not render the offering *piggul* **until** the blood is **sprinkled.** Since the mishna is apparently referring to a regular case of *piggul*, it must be speaking of a situation where the sprinkling was also performed with the *piggul* intent, which is how Ilfa explained the mishna on *Zevaḥim* 29b. If so, according to Rav Giddel, who maintains that slaughtering with the intent of *piggul* does not bring offerings of lesser sanctity into the status of being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, how could the loaves be considered sanctified? **What** does the mishna mean when it says that the loaves are considered **sanctified?** It does not mean that they are sanctified in the sense that they are subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. Rather, it means that they are sanctified to the extent that they have the possibility **to be disqualified,** to the extent **that** they **require burning** afterward.

This concession, however, creates a problem for the earlier resolution of the *toda* bread case. The explanation had been: when the *toda* was slaughtered with *machsheves piggul*, the bread was sanctified at the moment of *shechita*, and at that moment the *korban* was not yet labeled *piggul* — so the *shechita* could still effect *kedushas ha-guf*. But now Rav Ashi has conceded that the *piggul* label does apply retroactively from *shechita*. Does this mean the bread is retroactively not sanctified? The Gemara presses this: *ee hachi gabi toda nami ad d'zarik* — if so, in the *toda* case as well, once the *zerikah* is performed retroactively the *shechita* was *piggul*, so how did the bread become sanctified?

### Kedushat Haguf vs Meilah

The Gemara's final answer: *mai kideish l'pasel u'bisreifa* — what the *shechitas piggul* effected was sanctification only to the degree that the bread becomes *pasul* and must be burned. It attained *kedushas ha-guf* in the sense of susceptibility to *psul b'linah* and similar *disqualifications* — but this is categorically different from what is required for *me'ilah*.

*Tosafos* had already noted from the outset that the bread of the *toda* is never subject to *me'ilah* in the first place, since it is always *muttar b'achilah* — either to the *be'alim* or to the *kohanim*; the bread was never *kodshei Hashem* in the *me'ilah* sense. The argument from the bread case was therefore always an argument by analogy: if *shechitas piggul* is meaningful for *kedushah*, why not for *me'ilah*? The Gemara now shows the analogy breaks down. *Shechitas piggul* can effect *kedushas ha-guf* to the degree of making the bread susceptible to *psul*. But for *me'ilah*, what matters is a wholly different threshold: what changes status for *me'ilah* is either that the meat of *kodshei kodashim* becomes *muttar la-kohanim*, or that the *imurin* of *kodshim kalim* become fit for the *mizbei'ach*. For *either* of those changes to occur, the *zerikah* must be genuinely *kasher*. A *zrikas piggul* — an invalid *zerikah* — cannot effect that transformation. The moment of *zerikah* is when *piggul* is fully operative, and therefore at precisely the moment when *me'ilah* status might otherwise change, the *piggul* is fully in force and the change does not occur. The *toda* bread case, by contrast, concerns *kedushah* at the moment of *shechita*, which is a distinct question from *me'ilah*, and the analogy therefore cannot be sustained.
