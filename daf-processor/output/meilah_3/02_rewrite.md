# Meilah 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Meila Concepts

### Opening Remarks

We continue today with *Meilah*, daf 3. As will become apparent, the daf is considerably shorter than yesterday's, which means we will not face as many challenges covering the material. This is partly because there is substantially more *Tosafos* here, and the *sugyos*, as one will find, are somewhat more dense and challenging, though many of these *Tosafos* function more as commentary than critical analysis — a mix of both approaches. The *Tosafos* here is *Tosafos Shantz*, and the *Rashi* is the standard *Rashi*. It is worth noting in passing that although the first daf of *Rashi* on *Kerisos* seemed unusual and some have questioned its authenticity, as one continues through that *masechta* it reads very much like the genuine *Rashi*. *Meilah* presents no such ambiguity.

One feature worth noting about *Meilah* as a *masechta* is that its language is not quite what one expects from the standard *Bavli* in terms of certain grammatical usages and diction, which has led to discussions about whether its final editing was done in a different Babylonian academy — whether *Nehardea*, *Sura*, or *Pumbedita*. The reader will notice something slightly unusual in the language as we proceed.

### Meila d'Oraisa Definition

The basic subject is the *issur d'Oraisa* of *meila*: when someone misuses *hekdesh* unintentionally, *b'shogeg*, the consequences are that they bring a *korban asham meilos* and pay back the principal with an added *chomesh* — an extra fifth, which is in fact a quarter of the principal. If the violation was deliberate, *b'meizid*, there is no *korban* and no *chomesh*; the principal alone is repaid. It is a striking feature of *halacha* that acting *b'shogeg* carries heavier formal consequences, but this fits within the broader framework of *kappara* and atonement that we encountered in *Kerisos*: sometimes the need for atonement itself generates additional obligations.

### Lo Ma'al Category

It is important to clarify the technical meaning of *lo ma'al* as it appears throughout the *Mishnah* and *Gemara*. When the *Gemara* states *lo ma'al* in a given case, it does not mean that what was done was *muttar*. If someone acted *b'meizid*, we would say *lo ma'al* — not that there are no consequences, but that one does not fall within the Torah's formal category of *meila* that obligates an *asham meilos* and the payment of an additional fifth. *Lo ma'al* is thus a very narrow designation, and keeping this in mind is essential to understanding the entire *masechta*.

### Meila d'Rabbanan

There is also a concept of *meila d'rabbanan*. This is more than a simple *issur d'rabbanan* of deriving benefit — more than the formula *lo nehenin*, which indicates an *issur hana'ah*. *Meila d'rabbanan* expands the *d'Oraisa* category and gives it substantially more weight. Cases that technically fall outside *meila d'Oraisa* may nonetheless be designated *meila d'rabbanan*. Since it is *d'rabbanan*, there is of course no *korban* and no *chomesh* — as the *Gemara* established yesterday — but it is a more weighty designation than a simple *issur hana'ah*. The Rabbis required repayment of the principal, thereby placing it in a distinct category, while deliberately distinguishing it from the *d'Oraisa* framework.

### Conceptual Frameworks

Conceptually, a question runs through the entire *masechta* — one we will not analyze at length now but will return to in a later *perek* — namely, to what degree one conceptualizes *meila* as a form of stealing from *hekdesh*, and to what degree it is better understood as deriving unauthorized benefit from something that does not belong to you, without the full character of theft. A neat test case arises when one derives benefit but *hekdesh* suffers no loss — for example, drinking from a *kos shel hekdesh*: one has benefited, yet *hekdesh* has not been diminished, and there is nonetheless *meila*. Conversely, one might transfer something belonging to *hekdesh* to another person as a gift without personally benefiting at all, yet one has effectively stolen from *hekdesh*. These two scenarios suggest there may be two distinct types of *meila*, and that question of conceptualization runs beneath the surface of the *masechta*.

### Shiur: Prutha Measure

The *shiur* of *meila* is a *prutah*. This immediately monetizes the violation in a way that is unlike, say, an *issur achila* measured by a *kezayis*. But whether the *prutah* is measured by the benefit I derived or by the market value of the object I took is not a trivial question. One can imagine benefiting a *prutah*'s worth without the object having any market value — for instance, an *olah* that is *assur b'hana'ah* and has no commercial value, yet if one were to eat it, one has derived a *prutah* of benefit. The measure, therefore, is benefit derived, not market value of the object — though in cases where *meila* resembles theft more than unauthorized enjoyment, market value may be the more relevant consideration. This point was discussed at length in *Kerisos* and is directly relevant to the *Gemara* before us, which deals with *korbanos psulin* — offerings that began as *kodesh* and *assur* but became *pasul* — and which nonetheless retain *meila* not because of their value but because the question is whether I benefited a *prutah*.

### Kedoshei Hashem

This brings us to what becomes a central question across the first four *perakim* or so of *Meilah*: whether an object can be designated *kodshei Hashem*. This is not the same as asking whether it is *kadosh* — of course it retains *kedusha*, we have not made it *chullin* — but whether it retains the specific designation of *kodshei Hashem* for purposes of *meila*. Some things, though still *kadosh*, have been so misdirected that they no longer bear this designation. *Hekdesh* that was slaughtered entirely outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, for example, or animals that died — although an *issur hana'ah* may still apply, such objects are *holech l'ibbud* and are no longer *kodshei Hashem* for *meila* purposes. As the *Gemara* established yesterday, the relevant test is *alu lo yardu*: if an object may remain on the *mizbeach* once placed there, it is sufficiently within the category of *kodshei Hashem* that *meila* still applies. A *psul* of this kind — a technical failure in the performance of the *avodah* rather than a fundamental disqualification — is called *psulo bakodesh*.

### Korban Classifications

The *masechta* is not primarily about the act of *meila* but about defining the objects and their levels of *kedusha* — which is a prerequisite for *meila*. *Meila* does not apply to parts of *korbanos* that may be eaten, such as the meat of a *shelamim*, the meat of a *chatas* or *asham* after *zerikas hadam*, when those portions become *muttar la-kohanim*. *Meila* applies only to what is wholly devoted to *Hashem*.

The paradigmatic case of *meila* is *bedek ha-bayis*, which one has no right to at all, or the portions of *korbanos* destined for the *mizbeach*. By *shelamim*, which is *kodashim kalim* and is seen as fundamentally belonging to the owner — even according to those who do not hold *mamon be'alim*, it is certainly not fundamentally God's — there is no *meila*, except for the *imurin*. And even the *imurin* only receive their designation after *shechita* and *zerika*, when they become fit for the *mizbeach*. An *olah*, by contrast, belongs entirely to God from the moment of sanctification: no one has any right to it at any stage, and therefore *meila* applies from the moment it is sanctified through the moment it is burnt. The intermediate cases are the *chatas* and *asham*, which are *kodshei kodashim* — fundamentally God's — though the *kohanim*'s eating of their meat is a *heter* that arises after *zerikas hadam*. Before the *zerika*, the meat is *assur*; after the *zerika*, the meat becomes *muttar la-kohanim* and is no longer subject to *meila*, while everything else remains *meila*.

The first two-thirds of the *masechta* are thus largely occupied not with the act of *meila* but with defining objects and their *kedusha*, raising questions such as: if one sanctifies a tree and fruit grows from it, is the fruit *meila*? If one sanctifies a well and rainwater gathers, is there *meila* on the rainwater? These questions of derivative or indirect *kedusha* dominate the early *perakim*.

## Psulo Bakodesh & Alu Lo…

### Rava vs. Rav Yosef

Yesterday's *daf* was largely occupied with this question of *psulo bakodesh* and its relationship to *kedoshei Hashem*, centering on a debate between *Rava* and *Rav Yosef* over whether *korbanos* that were *pasul* due to *shechita b'darom* — slaughtering in the southern rather than northern part of the *azarah* — must be removed from the *mizbeach* if placed there. *Rava* maintained that they must descend; *Rav Yosef* maintained that once placed on the *mizbeach* they remain — *alu lo yardu*. The principle of *alu lo yardu* functions as the test of *kedoshei Hashem* status: if an object may remain on the *mizbeach* once placed there, this demonstrates that despite its *psul* it is sufficiently within the category of *kodshei Hashem* for *meila* to apply. Once it does not remain — once it must be removed — the implication is that it has fallen out of the *kodshei Hashem* category for *meila* purposes, even though it retains some form of *kedusha*.

### Chada Migu Chada Method

The *Gemara* on our daf, 3a, opens with *Rav Yosef* challenging *Rava*'s position by stringing three *mishnayos* together using the method of *chada migu chada v'chada migu chada*: learn something from *Mishna* A that clarifies *Mishna* B, then from *Mishna* B something that clarifies *Mishna* C, and then pose the challenge from *Mishna* C. The three *mishnayos* are assembled to demonstrate that *psulo bakodesh* and *alu lo yardu* are logically linked — and that this linkage demolishes *Rava*'s position.

### Bird Shechita Case

The first *mishna* in the chain: if one performs *melika* of a bird — either an *olas ha-of* or a *chatas ha-of* — in the wrong location, whether too high or too low on the *mizbeach*, the ruling is *ve-chulan ein mitamin begadim b'veit hablia* — they do not render one's garments *tamei* when swallowed, which is the way of stating that the bird does not have the status of *neveilah*. One might have assumed that an invalid *melika* in the wrong location renders the bird a *neveilah*, since that would be the result of an improper slaughter. The *mishna* rules otherwise: because the *psul* occurred within the framework of the sacred service — a technical ritual failure, not a slaughter outside the *Beis HaMikdash* — it does not entirely transform the identity of the bird. We do not say it is as though one had strangled it. The technical term is *psulo bakodesh*, and as a consequence the bird will not generate *tumah b'veit hablia* and retains *issur meila*. The *mishna* further notes that *u-moalin bahen* — there is still *meila* — with one exception: a *chatas ha-of* performed correctly below on the *mizbeach* for the sake of a *chatas*, precisely as required, since in that case the *kohanim* eat the meat and therefore the meat is not subject to *meila*.

### Psulah Bakodesh Definiti…

The second *mishna* conceptualizes what the first described: *kol she-psula bakodesh eina metamei begadim b'veit hablia* — anything whose *psul* occurred within the sacred context does not render garments *tamei* when swallowed. What this *mishna* contributes to the argument is not new information but a conceptual label: the first *mishna* described a specific error — wrong location of *melika* — and the second *mishna* names this category *psulah bakodesh*. A technical ritual failure in the manner of performing the *avodah* is *psulah bakodesh*, distinct from a more fundamental disqualification such as slaughtering outside the *Beis HaMikdash* entirely.

### Alu Lo Yardu Principle

The third *mishna* then states: *kol she-hayah psula bakodesh im alu lo yerdu* — anything in the category of *psulah bakodesh*, once placed on the *mizbeach*, does not come down. Conversely, *v'chol shelo hayah psula bakodesh metamei begadim b'veit hablia* — anything whose *psul* was not *bakodesh* is treated as a *neveilah* and does generate *tumah*. The three *mishnayos* together establish the complete chain: *psulah bakodesh* correlates with not becoming *neveilah*, not becoming *neveilah* correlates with retaining *meila* status, and *psulah bakodesh* correlates with *alu lo yardu*.

### Rav Yosef's Challenge

The logical proof against *Rava* now follows directly. If *rava* says that a *korban* slaughtered in the wrong place must be taken down from the *mizbeach*, he is implicitly saying it is not *psulah bakodesh* — because the established principle is that *psulah bakodesh* remains on the *mizbeach*. But if it is not *psulah bakodesh*, then the *mishna*'s principle implies there is no *meila* — and yet *Rava* was willing to concede *meila* in such a case, at least *d'rabbanan*. The problem is that the *mishna*'s statement *ein meila* appears categorical, excluding both *d'Oraisa* and *d'rabbanan* *meila*. Thus *Rav Yosef* demonstrates through the chain of three *mishnayos* that one cannot simultaneously hold that a *korban* must descend from the *mizbeach* and that *meila* still applies, even *d'rabbanan*.

### Rava's Concession

The *Gemara* accepts this as an absolute refutation: *tiyuvta de'Rava tiyuvta*. *Rava*'s position is rejected. This is significant for the general principle of *kedoshei Hashem* that underlies our *mishna*: the test of *alu lo yardu* is not merely a parallel ruling but is the operative indicator of *kodshei Hashem* status for *meila*. If a *korban* that is *psul* may nonetheless remain on the *mizbeach* — even though it ought not to have been placed there — it is sufficiently within the category of *kodshei Hashem* that *meila* applies. Once it cannot remain on the *mizbeach*, it is no longer *kodshei Hashem* for *meila* purposes, even though it retains *kedusha* in some form, and neither *d'Oraisa* nor *d'rabbanan* *meila* applies.

## Olat Bamos Yachid Query

### Bama Yachid Case Setup

Following the resolution of the *Rava–Rav Yosef* debate, the *Gemara* introduces a question posed by *Rabbi Elazar*: *olas bamas yachid shehichnisah lifnim* — what is the *halacha* for an *olah* that was sanctified for a private *bamah* and subsequently brought into the central *mikdash*?

### She'at Heter Bamos

The historical background: during the period of *she'at heter bamos*, when private altars were permitted, a central *mishkan* existed — for example, at *Nob* and *Givon* — but there was as yet no permanent *Beis HaMikdash*. During such a period, one was permitted to offer on one's private *bamah yachid*, and this came with substantial latitude: no requirement for a *kohen*, no requirement for *klei shares*, no requirement to slaughter in the north even for an *olah*. A *korban* brought at the central location, by contrast, was subject to all the standard requirements. The question involves someone who sanctified an *olah* for his private *bamah* — which was entirely permitted — and then chose to bring it to the central *mizbeach*.

### Kaltuha Mechitzos

Once such a *korban* enters the precincts of the central *mikdash*, the principle is *kaltuha mechitzos l'kol davar* — the walls of the central location absorb it, and from that point forward it must conform to all the requirements of the *mikdash*. This principle is established at the very end of *Zevachim*. The question then becomes: having been absorbed by the central *mikdash*, if the *korban* is now offered in a manner that would have been perfectly valid at the *bamah yachid* but is a *psul* at the central *mikdash* — such as slaughtering in the south rather than the north — what is the *halacha* if it is subsequently placed on the *mizbeach*? Does it remain, or must it descend?

### Elazar's Question

*Rabbi Elazar*'s question is: *olas bamos yachid shehichnisua she-nifselah, alu mah she-yardu* — if such an *olah* was *nifselah* in a way that would not have been a *psul* at the *bamah yachid*, and was then placed on the *mizbeach*, does it descend? As *Rashi* explains, the *psul* at issue is specifically one that would not have invalidated the *korban* at the *bamah yachid* — for example, slaughtering in the south, which is entirely acceptable outside the framework of the *mikdash* where there is no *makom hamikdash*. The question is thus how much weight we give to the original *kedusha* of the *bamah*, which allowed greater latitude, versus the *kedusha* of the location into which it was brought, which imposes stricter requirements.

### Question Presumption

The *Gemara* notes: *mi'de'ka mibaya lei l'chada* — since *Rabbi Elazar* is asking only the complex *bamah yachid* case, *michlal d'idach pshita lei* — the base case of a standard *korban* slaughtered *b'darom* and placed on the *mizbeach* appears obvious to him. Whether he would resolve it like *Rava* or like *Rav Yosef*, the *Gemara* cannot determine, since the base case could have been obvious either way. The *Gemara* then raises the possibility that he may in fact be asking a double question — *chada mi-go chada ka-mibaya lei* — first seeking to know the *halacha* in the base case of *shachatah b'darom*, and then, having received that answer, inquiring about the special *bamah yachid* case. So the mere fact that he frames his question around the more complex case does not necessarily establish that the base case was settled in his mind.

### Two Interpretive Paths

The *Gemara* lays out why the *bamah yachid* case could be argued in either direction even given a settled resolution of the base case. According to *Rava*'s framework — where a standard *shechita b'darom* requires descent from the *mizbeach* — one might nonetheless say that the *bamah yachid olah* should be permitted to remain. Why? *Bimchitzah kitiknah paslah, shelo kitiknah lo paslah*: *Rava*'s reasoning in the standard case was that everything was *bimchitzah kitiknah* — done within the environment of the *mikdash* under its requirements — and violating those requirements is a real *psul*. But when the *korban* was originally sanctified for a framework that never required *b'tzafon* slaughter and allowed far greater latitude, perhaps the *psul* of *badarom* is not so grave, and it should be permitted to remain on the *mizbeach*.

Conversely, even according to *Rav Yosef* — who holds that in the standard case *alu lo yardu* — one might argue that this *bamah yachid* case is actually worse. *Mechitzah kitiknah kaltah*: the principle of *kaltuha mechitzos* works precisely because everything about the *korban* was directed toward the *mikdash* from the outset, which gives the *korban* an entitlement, as it were, to the protections of the *mizbeach* once placed there. But a *korban* that was originally sanctified for a *bamah* — an outsider, fundamentally not a *mikdash korban* — lacks that entitlement. *Shelo kitiknah lo kaltah*: the walls did not truly absorb it in the sense that would give it a right to remain on the *mizbeach*. The question is therefore genuinely open from both sides.

### Teiku: No Resolution

The *Gemara* concludes: *teiku*. The question remains unresolved. The entire discussion of *Rava* and *Rav Yosef* — whether *alu lo yardu* applies to a *korban shechuta b'darom* — is thus concluded, and with it the establishment of the operative principle: *psulo bakodesh* and *alu lo yardu* are the markers of *kodshei Hashem* status for *meila*. What remains open is the edge case of an *olah* sanctified for a *bamah yachid* and brought to the central *mikdash*.

## Zrikas Piggul & Meila

### Rav Gidel Statement

The *Gemara* now turns to an independent statement: *amar Rav Gidel amar Rav* — *zrikas piggul eina motzi midei me'ilah*. If the *zerika* of the blood was performed with a *machsheves piggul* — an intent to eat the meat *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* — this does not constitute a valid *zerika*, and accordingly it does not change the *meila* status of the *korban*.

### Kodashei Kodashim

The first application: *eina motzi midei me'ilah b'kodshei kodashim*. For a *chatas* or *asham*, a valid *zerika* would have rendered the meat *muttar la-kohanim* and thereby removed the *meila* that applied to it. A *zrikas piggul*, however — since it is invalid — does not accomplish this. The meat of *kodshei kodashim* remains *meila* just as it was before the *zerika*, because the *zerika* was a *zrikas piggul* and the meat was never made *muttar la-kohanim*.

### Kodashim Kalim

The second application goes in the opposite direction: *v'eino mevi lidei me'ilah b'kodashim kalim*. By *kodashim kalim*, a valid *zerika* would bring the *imurin* into the category of *meila*, since they are now designated to go onto the *mizbeach*. A *zrikas piggul*, being invalid, does not accomplish this either. The *imurin* of *kodashim kalim* do not attain *meila* status through a *zrikas piggul*.

### Tosafos Chiddush

The statement seems on its surface merely to restate the principle of our *mishna* — that when *avodah* is performed with a *psul*, the *meila* status does not change. *Tosafos* clarifies the genuine *chiddush*: the novel claim of *Rav Gidel amar Rav* concerns specifically *machsheves piggul* at the moment of *zerika*. There is a principle in the laws of *piggul* that a *korban* is not considered to have attained the status of *piggul* unless all other *avodos* were performed validly — a concept expressed as *meratzeh la-piggulo*: the *korban* must have been sufficiently accepted through its service, apart from the *piggul* thought, to establish the *piggul*. The *zerika* itself is the *avoda* that provides *meratzeh la-piggulo* — it is what gives the *piggul* its force. One might therefore have reasoned that since the *zerika* simultaneously establishes the *piggul* and provides its own *ritzuy*, it should be treated as sufficiently valid to effect changes in *meila* status, just as it effects *piggul* status. *Rav Gidel amar Rav* teaches that this reasoning is wrong: even *zrikas piggul*, despite functioning as *meratzeh la-piggulo* for purposes of establishing *piggul*, does not function as a valid *zerika* for *meila* purposes.

## Rav Pappa Challenge

### Toda Bread Case

*Rav Pappa* challenged *Abaye* — who had been transmitting *Rav Gidel*'s statement — from the following *mishna*: *ha-shochet es ha-toda bifnim v'lachmah bachutz lo kidesh ha-lachem* — if one slaughters a *toda* inside while its bread is outside the *Mikdash*, the bread is not sanctified to *kedushas ha-guf*. Until the *toda* is slaughtered, the bread possesses only *kedushas damim*; it attains *kedushas ha-guf* through the *shechita* of the *korban*. If the bread is outside, it is not connected to the *korban* at the moment of *shechita* and so is not elevated. Similarly, *shachatah ad shelo kermu paneha ba-tanur* — if the bread had not yet formed a crust in the oven, it is not yet legally considered *lechem*, and so *lo kidesh ha-lachem*. If even one loaf of the required set lacked its crust, none of the bread is sanctified — it is all or nothing.

### Inference Challenge

The *mishna* then states: *shachatah chutz lizmano v'chutz limkomo, kidesh ha-lachem* — if one slaughtered with a *machsheves piggul*, the bread is nonetheless sanctified. The inference *Rav Pappa* draws is that *shechitas piggul* is apparently significant enough to effect *kedusha* — and if it effects *kedusha*, one might expect it to have implications for *meila* status as well. This appears to contradict *Rav Gidel*'s statement that a *zrikat piggul* does not effect changes in *meila* status.

### Bread Analogy Limit

*Tosafos* notes from the outset that the challenge is in fact an argument by analogy rather than a direct contradiction, and the *Gemara* will eventually expose the limits of that analogy. The bread of a *toda* is never subject to *meila* to begin with, because it is *muttar b'achila* — either to the *be'alim* or to the *kohanim*; it is never *kodshei Hashem* in the relevant sense. The argument is therefore not that *shechitas piggul* creates *meila* on the bread, but that *shechitas piggul* appears to be halakhically significant for purposes of *kedusha* — and the question is why this significance would not extend to *meila* status as well.

### Abaye's Consultation

*Abaye* was silent — *ishtik* — unable to immediately resolve the challenge. He subsequently sat before *Rabbi Abba* to discuss the matter, and it is *Rav Ashi*'s response to *Rava* that carries the analysis forward.

## Zrika vs. Shechita

### Zrika Determinance

*Rav Ashi* resolves the challenge with a single word: *bi-zrika* — the distinction lies at the moment of *zerika*, not *shechita*. The analysis, elaborated along the lines of *Tosafos*, runs as follows. The *machsheves piggul* at *shechita* and the *machsheves piggul* at *zerika* are not equivalent. The critical moment for *meila* status — the moment at which the status of a *korban* changes, either removing *meila* from the meat of *kodshei kodashim* by making it *muttar la-kohanim*, or imposing *meila* on the *imurin* of *kodashim kalim* by dedicating them to the *mizbeach* — is *zerika*. At the moment of *zerika*, *piggul* is fully in force and therefore cannot effect any valid change in *meila* status. But at the moment of *shechita*, when the bread of the *toda* gains *kedushas ha-guf*, the *piggul* has not yet fully vested: *piggul* requires the completion of the *avodah*, and at the point of *shechita* alone it is only *machsheves piggul*, not full *piggul*. Therefore the *shechita* was able to sanctify the bread, because at that moment the *piggul* had not yet crystallized.

### Kometz Piggul Case

*Rav Ashi* himself then raises a difficulty against this answer. The principle he has articulated is that something should not be labeled *piggul* until the *zerika* is completed. But we find the expression *kometz piggul* — a *kometz* taken from a *minchah* with *machsheves piggul* is referred to as *piggul* even before it has been offered on the *mizbeach*. And *v'kmitzah hainu shechita* — the *kemitzah* from a *minchah* is analogous to *shechita* in a *zevach*. If something can be called *piggul* from the moment of *kemitzah*, why are we saying that *piggul* is not operative until *zerika*?

### Kometz Piggul Clarificat…

The *Gemara* responds: *asokei de-masik lei piggul* — when we call it *kometz piggul*, we are not being technically precise. We mean a *kometz* that will become *piggul*, not that it is already *piggul* in the full halakhic sense. The label is anticipatory, not definitive. We are not formally designating it as *piggul* at that early stage.

### Ravina's Zevachim Case

*Ravina* poses a further challenge to *Rav Ashi* from a passage in *Zevachim*: *v'ha amar Ilfa machlokes b'shtei avodos*. There is a well-known debate about what happens when a *korban* is slaughtered with a *machshava chutz lizmano* — the *piggul* thought — and the *zerika* is performed with a different problematic thought, *chutz limkomo*, which is a *psul* but not *piggul*. The question is whether that second *psul* at *zerika* prevents the *piggul* established at *shechita* from taking effect, on the grounds that *piggul* requires all other *avodos* to have been performed validly — *meratzeh la-piggulo*.

### Ilfa's Two Avodos

*Ilfa*'s ruling distinguishes between two scenarios. If the *chutz limkomo* thought came at *shechita* first, and the *chutz lizmano* thought came at *zerika* afterward, everyone agrees it is not *piggul* — the *korban* was already *pasul* before the *piggul* thought was introduced. But if the *chutz lizmano* thought came first at *shechita*, and the *chutz limkomo* thought came second at *zerika*, there is a *machlokes* about whether the subsequent *psul* at *zerika* blocks the *piggul* established at *shechita*. However, if both conflicting thoughts arose during the same *avoda* — even sequentially, even if one occurred slightly before the other — *kulei alma* agree: *eiruv machshavos hu*, it is a mixture of thoughts, and the *korban* is certainly not *piggul*. The debate is only when they occur at two distinct *avodos*.

### Retroactive Labeling

*Ravina*'s challenge to *Rav Ashi* is this: *Ilfa*'s entire framework presupposes that the question of *piggul* turns on what occurred at *shechita* — which thought came first — and that this matters for the label of *piggul*. This implies that *piggul* can be attributed retroactively to the moment of *shechita*, based on what happens later at *zerika*. If that is so — if the label of *piggul* is not held in abeyance until *zerika* but applies retroactively at the *shechita* moment once *zerika* is completed — then the whole basis for distinguishing the *toda* bread case collapses. One had argued that at the moment of *shechita* the bread was sanctified because *piggul* was not yet operative. But if *piggul* applies retroactively to the moment of *shechita* once the *zerika* completes, then retroactively the bread's sanctification moment was one of *piggul* — and perhaps it should not have been sanctified.

The *Gemara*'s response is that this retroactive labeling is indeed accepted: *piggul* does apply retroactively to the *shechita* moment once the *zerika* confirms it. The concession is significant.

### Return to Bread Case

This forces a return to the *toda* bread question. If retroactively, upon the completion of *zerika*, the *piggul* status applies back to *shechita* — why did the *shechita* sanctify the bread? The answer, as *Tosafos* anticipated from the beginning, is that the cases are not truly parallel. The *shechita* of a *piggul* can effect sanctification of the bread to the status of *kedushas ha-guf*. But *kedushas ha-guf* is not the same as *meila*. The argument had been framed as if sanctification for *kedusha* purposes and sanctification for *meila* purposes are equivalent, and the *toda* bread case was offered as evidence that *piggul* has halakhic force sufficient to matter for *meila*. That analogy fails.

### Shechita Meila Distincti…

The final answer: *shechitas piggul* can sanctify the bread *l'pasel u'bisreifa* — to give it *kedushas ha-guf* in the limited sense that it becomes susceptible to *psul b'lina* and must be burned if left overnight — but this is distinct from *meila*. For *meila* status to change, a completely valid *avodah* is required. The moment that effects *meila* changes is *zerika*, and at *zerika* the *piggul* is fully operative and undeniably invalid. A *zrikas piggul* cannot make the meat of *kodshei kodashim* *muttar la-kohanim* and thereby remove *meila*, nor can it bring the *imurin* of *kodashim kalim* into *meila* status. The case of the bread demonstrates only that *shechitas piggul* has some halakhic force for purposes of *kedusha* — a *kedusha* of the limited kind sufficient to create *psul b'lina* — but this does not translate to the question of *meila*, which requires a genuinely valid *avodah* to effect any change in status.
