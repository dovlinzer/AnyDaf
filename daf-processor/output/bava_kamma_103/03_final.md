# Bava Kamma 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shinui Koneh Review

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִיקַּח לוֹ חִטִּין – וְלָקַח מֵהֶם שְׂעוֹרִין, שְׂעוֹרִין – וְלָקַח מֵהֶם חִטִּין; תַּנְיָא חֲדָא: אִם פָּחֲתוּ – פָּחֲתוּ לוֹ, וְאִם הוֹתִירוּ – הוֹתִירוּ לוֹ; וְתָנֵי חֲדָא: אִם פָּחֲתוּ – פָּחֲתוּ לוֹ, וְאִם הוֹתִירוּ – הוֹתִירוּ לָאֶמְצַע!
>
> **Translation:** **to purchase wheat for him,** which he plans to sell at a profit, **and** instead the agent **purchased barley with** the money, or if he gave his agent money to purchase **barley and** instead **he purchased wheat with** the money, two *baraitot* issue discordant rulings with regard to the *halakha*. **It is taught** in **one** *baraita* that **if there was a loss, the loss is for** the agent, **and if there was a profit,** the **profit is for** the agent as well, as he is considered to have abrogated his status as an agent by deviating from the instructions. **And it is taught** in **one** *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Metzia* 4:20) that if there was an agreement that both parties would split the profits from the transaction, **if there was a loss, the loss is for** the agent, **and if there was a profit,** the **profit** goes **to the middle,** i.e., the agent and the investor split the profit.

Today's learning is dedicated to the memory of all those who perished in the tragedy of September 11 and those who gave their lives to save others. We pick up on 102b, the second wide line, in the middle of a discussion arising from a *mishna* concerning an agent who was given money to purchase one commodity and instead purchased another — wheat instead of barley, or barley instead of wheat — and the question of what consequences follow from that deviation.

### Shinui Koneh Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְהָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. הָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר שִׁינּוּי קוֹנֶה; וְהָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר שִׁינּוּי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** To reconcile the *baraitot*, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is **not difficult. This** first *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and this** second *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** Rabbi Yoḥanan explains his rationale: **This** first *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that **a change** by an agent that deviates from the terms of his assignment means that the agent **acquires** the item in question for himself, as in the case of one who was paid to dye wool red but dyed it black. In this case as well, when the agent purchases wheat instead of barley, or vice versa, he acquires the grain for himself. **And this** second *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that **a change** by an agent which deviates from the terms of his assignment does **not** mean that the agent **acquires** the item in question for himself. It is acquired by the one who appointed the agent, and they split the profits as agreed. But the agent is held responsible for any loss incurred, due to his deviation from the instructions.

The *gemara* sought to connect this case to the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir regarding *shinui koneh* — whether a transformation in an object effects an acquisition. The comparison is admittedly strange. The classic case of *shinui koneh* involves a physical object given to someone who changes it: wood given to make a chair, wool given to be dyed. In those cases there is a tangible raw material that the person can be said to acquire through the act of transformation. Here, however, what exactly is being acquired? Money is given for investment, and when the agent invests it contrary to instructions, what is the object of acquisition? The parallel is therefore of an unusual character, but the *gemara* treated it as a meaningful analogy nonetheless.

The logic runs as follows. If one holds *shinui koneh* — as Rabbi Meir does in the case of a craftsman — then by analogy, when an agent acts contrary to his *shlichus*, he is operating outside the scope of his agency and is, in some sense, taking personal possession of the money and what he purchases with it. If, on the other hand, one holds that *shinui* is not *koneh*, then the agent remains within the framework of the agency even when he deviates from the precise instructions, and the consequence of the deviation is not that he takes possession but simply that any resulting loss falls upon him rather than the principal.

### Money Investment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר – אֶלָּא בְּמִידֵּי דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ, אֲבָל לִסְחוֹרָה – לָא אָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar objects to this** explanation: **From where** do you say that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda extends to the case of these *baraitot*? **Perhaps Rabbi Meir says** that a change by an agent from the terms of his assignment results in the acquisition of the item by the agent **only with regard to an item that is fit for** use by the one who appointed the agent **himself,** such as wool dyed the wrong color, which is now not usable for its intended purpose. **But** Rabbi Meir **does not say** his ruling with regard to an item purchased **for** subsequent sale as **merchandise,** since it is intended to be sold for a profit in any event, and the transaction would take effect as agreed upon initially, with the agent held responsible for any loss incurred.

A further nuance was that even according to the opinion that *shinui* is normally *koneh*, this case of monetary investment may be different. The reason: the principal who gave the money to invest does not particularly care what it is invested in — he simply wants a profitable return. Since his real intention is to make money and not to specify the precise investment vehicle, acting outside the stated instructions does not necessarily remove the agent from the scope of his *shlichus* when the investment proves successful. The obligation shifts, however, when the deviation causes a loss: then it is the agent's loss, not the principal's.

### Heter Iska

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – כָּאן לַאֲכִילָה, כָּאן לִסְחוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Elazar said:** Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are written in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, and** it is **not difficult. Here,** in the first *baraita*, it is discussing grain purchased **for eating,** and since the one who appointed the agent desired wheat, he does not acquire the barley. **There,** in the second *baraita*, it is discussing grain purchased **for** subsequent sale as **merchandise,** and the transaction takes effect as agreed upon initially, with the agent held responsible for any loss incurred.

This structure — in which the principal can only gain and cannot lose — prompted *Tosafos* to observe that such an arrangement closely resembles the mechanics of a *heter iska*, the permitted business arrangement constructed as a workaround for the prohibition of *ribbit*. The background: the *Torah*'s prohibition of lending at interest was rooted in an agricultural society where borrowing was associated with poverty and hardship, and charging interest on such loans was tantamount to exploitation. It is essentially a form of *tzedakah* that the *Torah* mandated. In a commercial context where money is borrowed for investment and profit, the prohibition remains technically in force — *ein dorshin ta'ama dikra* — but the *heter iska* provides a legal restructuring of the arrangement.

The basic mechanics of *heter iska* are as follows. Rather than a lender giving the borrower a thousand dollars at five percent interest, the parties reframe their relationship as a business partnership. The lender "invests" the thousand dollars in the borrower's business, becoming entitled in principle to all gains and losses. The parties then agree that the borrower will pay the investor a fixed fee — say, five percent — in exchange for the investor trusting the borrower's accounting without verification. The fixed payment is thus characterized not as interest on a loan but as a fee within a business arrangement.

*Tosafos* identifies a parallel mechanism arising directly from the *gemara*'s discussion. If one can structure a situation in which all losses fall on the agent and all gains are shared, the same functional result is achieved. *Tosafos* describes a scenario where the principal gives the agent instructions that are practically impossible to fulfill — requiring gold-vessel collateral, maximum-security storage, and so forth — so that the agent is always technically operating outside the instructions. This guarantees that any loss is the agent's, while any profit is shared, effectively recreating the interest relationship within a formally permissible framework.

## Field Purchase

### No Condition Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה בְּשֵׁם חֲבֵירוֹ – אֵין כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לִמְכּוֹר. וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״עַל מְנָת״ – כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לִמְכּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara presents another situation in which one makes a purchase involving a third party. **The Sages taught:** In the case of **one who purchases a field in the name of another, he is not compelled to sell** it. **But if he said to him** at the time of the sale: I will purchase the field **on the condition** that he will sell it to me, **he is compelled to sell** it.

We turn now to a new *baraita*. The *gemara* cites: *tanu rabbanan* — if someone buys a field in the name of his friend, you do not force him to sell it; but if he stipulated a condition, you do force him to sell. As stated, this is opaque. What precisely is the case, and what is being forced upon whom?

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ בְּשֵׁם רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא – אֵין כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא לִמְכּוֹר. וְאִם אָמַר ״עַל מְנָת״ – כּוֹפִין אֶת רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא לִמְכּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** Noting the ambiguity of this statement, the Gemara asks: **What is it saying? Rav Sheshet said** that **this** is what it **is saying:** With regard to **one who purchases a field from another,** claiming that he is acting **in the name of the Exilarch** or another dominant figure, so that others will be discouraged from contesting the sale, **the Exilarch is not compelled to sell** it to him, i.e., the Exilarch is not required to provide the purchaser with documentation that the field belongs to the purchaser. **But if** the purchaser **said** to the seller: I will purchase the field **on the condition** that the Exilarch will provide me with that documentation, **the Exilarch is compelled to sell** it to him, i.e., to provide him with the documentation.

Rav Sheshet explains: the case is one who purchases a field from another person and writes the purchase document in the name of the *Reish Galuta*. The reason for doing so is practical protection against nuisance suits. Once a private individual acquires land, any number of people may come forward claiming prior rights to it and initiating litigation. By writing the document in the name of the *Reish Galuta* — the Exilarch, the head of the Jewish community under the Persian government — potential claimants would be deterred, since challenging a *Reish Galuta* document carried far greater risk and difficulty than challenging a document in a private person's name. The practice is acknowledged as a kind of legal fiction: the buyer was not genuinely acting as the *Reish Galuta*'s agent, and everyone understood this, yet the document served its deterrent function.

The ruling of the first clause is then: if I have purchased the field in the *Reish Galuta*'s name without any prior stipulation, I cannot force the *Reish Galuta* to transfer it to me. The implication is that the *Reish Galuta* has actually acquired it — *michlal demikna kanya lei* — since I bought it nominally on his behalf.

### Bnei Maarava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה בְּשֵׁם רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא – אֵין כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא לִמְכּוֹר. מִכְּלָל דְּמִקְנָא קַנְיָא לֵיהּ; לֵימָא פְּלִיגָא דִּבְנֵי מַעְרְבָא, דְּאָמְרִי: וְכִי מִי הוֹדִיעוֹ לְבַעַל חִטִּין – שֶׁיַּקְנֶה חִטִּין לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav Sheshet's interpretation. **The Master said:** With regard to **one who purchases a field in the name of the Exilarch, the Exilarch is not compelled to sell** it to him. Since the *baraita* teaches only that the Exilarch is not required to provide the purchaser with documentation that the field is his, it can be understood **by inference that** the purchaser **has** nevertheless **acquired** the field **for himself. Let us say that** this interpretation of the *baraita* **disagrees with** the statement of **the residents of the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **who say: And who informed the owner of the wheat that he should transfer the wheat to the customer?** Since the seller assumed he was selling the field to the Exilarch, the Sages of Eretz Yisrael would hold that the sale does not take effect.

This leads to a challenge from the position of the *Bnei Maarava*, the Western Rabbis, who held: who informed the seller of the wheat that he was transferring ownership to the owner of the money? Their position, raised in the previous day's discussion, is that for a purchase made on another's behalf to take effect for that other person, the seller must be informed for whom the purchase is being made. If the seller was not so informed, the sale does not vest in the intended beneficiary.

Seemingly, if the *Bnei Maarava* are correct, then merely writing the *Reish Galuta*'s name in the document would not suffice to make him the owner, because the seller — the original owner of the field — was not told that the sale was for the *Reish Galuta*'s benefit.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא – לָא קַשְׁיָא; כְּגוֹן דְּאוֹדְעֵיהּ לְבַעַל שָׂדֶה, וְאוֹדְעִינְהוּ לְסָהֲדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **If** it is **due to this** inference that it is surmised that the *baraita* is in conflict with the opinion of the Sages of Eretz Yisrael, it is **not difficult,** as it can be said that the *baraita* is discussing a case **where** the purchaser **informed the owner of the field and informed the witnesses** that he intends to purchase the field for himself.

The *gemara* resolves this: *im mishum ha lo kashya* — the challenge is no difficulty, because the case is one where the buyer did in fact inform the seller (*baal hasadeh*) that he was purchasing on behalf of the *Reish Galuta*. And he also informed the witnesses to that effect. The *gemara* acknowledges that one might have imagined the buyer told only the witnesses — so that the document would be written in the *Reish Galuta*'s name for protective purposes — without informing the seller himself. The answer is that the seller was informed, and therefore even according to the *Bnei Maarava* the acquisition takes effect.

### Abaye's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה בְּשֵׁם חֲבֵירוֹ, רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא – אֵין כּוֹפִין אֶת הַמּוֹכֵר לִמְכּוֹר זִימְנָא אַחֲרִיתִי. וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״עַל מְנָת״ – כּוֹפִין אֶת הַמּוֹכֵר לִמְכּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said: This** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** With regard to **one who purchases a field in the name of his friend, the Exilarch,** the seller is not compelled to sell** the field **another time,** i.e., to provide the purchaser with a document denoting him as the owner of the field. **But if he said to him:** I will purchase the field **on the condition** that you will provide a deed in my name, **the seller is compelled to sell** the field again, i.e., to provide the purchaser with a document denoting him as the owner.

The *gemara* then turns to the second clause: if I stipulated a condition, you force the *Reish Galuta* to transfer the field to me. The *gemara* objects: why would any court force the *Reish Galuta* to do anything? The *Reish Galuta* could simply say he wants no part of this transaction — neither the honor of having property written in his name nor the imposition of being pressed into executing a transfer document for a private individual's convenience.

Abaye therefore offers an altogether different reading of the *baraita*, one that eliminates the *Reish Galuta* from the central operative role (though Rav Ashi retains him). Abaye's reading: the case is simply one where I purchase a field and wish to have the document written not in my own name — perhaps for tax purposes, to avoid creditors, or for any other reason — but in the name of someone else: a spouse, a business associate, or indeed the *Reish Galuta*. I want two documents: the publicly-facing document in the other person's name, to deter challenges, and a real, operative document in my own name confirming my actual ownership.

### Stipulated Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה בְּשֵׁם חֲבֵירוֹ – אֵין כּוֹפִין אֶת הַמּוֹכֵר לִמְכּוֹר זִימְנָא אַחֲרִיתִי. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ: מִידָּע יָדְעַתְּ דַּאֲנָא לְנַפְשַׁאי שָׁקֵילְנָא – וּפַנְחַיָּא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּקָבָעֵינָא, וְזוּזִי בִּכְדִי לָא שָׁדֵינָא, אֶלָּא אַדַּעְתָּא דְּכָתֵב לֵיהּ שְׁטָרָא אַחֲרִינָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: עִנְיָינָא עֲבַדִי לָךְ בַּהֲדֵי הָהוּא דִּזְבַנְתְּ לֵיהּ בִּשְׁמֵיהּ, וְיִכְתּוֹב לָךְ שְׁטָרָא אַחֲרִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the *baraita*: **The Master said:** In the case of **one who purchases a field in the name of another, the seller is not compelled to sell** the field **another time.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that the purchaser **can say to** the seller: **You knew that I took,** i.e., purchased, the field **for myself, and it was merely** for **protection [*ufanaḥya*] that I desired** to obtain ownership by claiming to purchase it in the name of the other person, **and I do not throw** out **money for naught. Rather,** it was **with the intention that you will write another document for him,** i.e., the seller, stating that I purchased the field. The *baraita* therefore **teaches us that** the seller can **say to him: I have performed a matter for you together with the one in whose name you purchased** the field, **and let him write you another document.**

The first clause — that you do not force the seller to write a second document — reflects the following situation: I purchased the field without having explicitly conditioned the sale on receiving a document in my name. In such a case the seller can reasonably say: I sold it, I wrote a document as you requested, and I have discharged my obligation. Why should I write you a second document in your own name?

The *gemara* asks: is that not obvious? Why would anyone think I could compel the seller to write a second document if I never asked for one as a condition?

The answer is that I could have argued: you knew, seller, that I was buying this for myself — *midea yadat d'ana l'nafshei shekilna* — and that the other document was purely protective in character — *panchaya b'alma*, a form of strengthening or securing the transaction. *Rashi* explains *panchaya* as a term for reinforcement or protection. You knew I wasn't going to flush my money buying a field for the *Reish Galuta* — I'm not his agent. You knew the real purchaser was me, and therefore it should be implicit that you owe me a document in my name.

The ruling teaches that the seller can reject this reasoning. He can say: I did not know your arrangement with the person in whose name I sold. Perhaps you had some deal with him. Perhaps there was a reason the property needed to go to him first. It was not my place to inquire. I wrote what you asked me to write; if you want a document in your name, that is your business with the person whose name is on the *shtar*. Therefore you cannot compel me to write a second document.

### Witness Notification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ ״עַל מְנָת״ – כּוֹפִין אֶת הַמּוֹכֵר לִמְכּוֹר. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ לְסָהֲדִי קַמֵּיהּ דִּידֵיהּ: חֲזוֹ דִּשְׁטָרָא אַחֲרִינָא קָא בָעֵינָא. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָמֵינָא, שְׁטָרָא מֵהַיְאךְ דְּקָא שָׁקְלַתְּ בִּשְׁמֵיהּ קָאָמְרַתְּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְהָכִי טְרַחִי וַאֲמַרִי לְהוּ לְעֵדִים קַמָּךְ, דְּמִינָּךְ הוּא דְּקָא בָעֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses Abaye's interpretation of the latter clause of the *baraita*, which states: **But if he said to him:** I will purchase the field **on the condition** that you will provide a deed in my name, **the seller is compelled to sell** the field again. Isn't that **obvious? No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where** the purchaser **said to the witnesses in front of** the seller: You can **see that I require another document. Lest you say** that the seller **can say to him: I thought** that it was **a document from the one in whose name you purchased** the field that **you were saying** you required, and not from me. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us that** the purchaser can **say to him:** It was **for this** that **I took the trouble and said to the witnesses, in front of you,** that I require another document, **as it is from you that I require** the document.

The *gemara* adds one further refinement. Even if I did not explicitly stipulate with the seller that he must write me a second document, I did make an announcement in the seller's presence — addressing the witnesses — saying: take note, I will be asking for another document in my name. The *gemara* asks: does this count as a valid stipulation with the seller?

The *chiddush* is that it does. The seller can still attempt to argue that when I said "I will want another document from him," the word "him" referred to the person in whose name I was buying — meaning I was announcing my intention to obtain a document from that third party, not from the seller. The ruling is that this defense fails: the explicit reason I made the announcement in the seller's presence was precisely so that he should understand it was directed at him. The phrase *l'hachi tarchi v'amri lehu l'kamach* — I took the trouble to say it in your presence for exactly this reason — establishes the stipulation, and the seller can be compelled to write the second document.

One might note, as the lecturer does, that the *gemara* works through all of these details without raising any explicit concern about the legal or ethical status of the fictitious documents themselves. The practice of writing property in another's name for protective purposes appears to have been a recognized convention, even if its precise legal grounding goes unexamined here.

## Rav Kahana Flax

### Flax Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא יְהַב זוּזָא אַכִּיתָּנָא. לְסוֹף אִיַּיקַּר כִּיתָּנָא, זַבְּנֵיהּ מָרָווֹתֵיהּ דְּכִיתָּנָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָה אֶעְבֵּיד, אֵיזִיל אֶישְׁקֹיל זוּזַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי כִּי קָא [מְ]זַבְּנִי אָמְרִי: ״הַאי כִּיתָּנָא דְכָהֲנָא הוּא״ – זִיל שְׁקוֹל; וְאִי לָא – לָא תִּשְׁקוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara mentions a related incident: **Rav Kahana gave money** to a salesman in exchange **for flax,** and temporarily left the flax in the possession of the seller. **Ultimately,** the **flax appreciated** in value and the **owner of the flax sold it** for a greater profit on behalf of Rav Kahana, intending to give him all of the money. Rav Kahana then **came before Rav,** and **said to him: What should I do? Should I go and take my money,** or would doing so have the appearance of collecting interest for a loan? Rav **said to** Rav Kahana: **If, when they sold** the flax, **they said: This flax is Kahana's, go take** the money, as in such a case it is as though the flax was purchased directly from you. **But if** they did **not** say this, **do not take** the money, since you would be receiving a greater sum of money than you provided, and this would appear to be a loan repaid with interest.

The *gemara* now presents the case of Rav Kahana, which connects loosely to the preceding discussion through the question of agency and in whose name a transaction is conducted. Rav Kahana gave money to a flax merchant — prepaying for flax at the then-current price. The flax market subsequently rose significantly in value. The merchant sold the flax and came to Rav Kahana with proceeds reflecting the higher price. Rav Kahana then came before Rav with the question: may he accept the additional money, or does the transaction carry the appearance of *ribbit*? After all, he gave four and now he would receive eight.

### Agent Naming

Rav's answer turns on a crucial factual question: when the merchant sold the flax, did he announce that he was selling Rav Kahana's flax — that is, did he identify the principal explicitly? *Amar lei*: if when he sold it he declared *hai kitna deKahana hu* — this is Rav Kahana's flax — then go ahead and collect. It is clear that Rav Kahana owned the flax, the merchant was merely his agent in selling it, and the profit is the natural appreciation of Rav Kahana's own commodity. But if the merchant did not sell it in Rav Kahana's name, then the arrangement looks like a straight monetary transaction: Rav Kahana gave money, and now money comes back at a higher amount — the appearance of *ribbit*.

### Ownership Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן? כִּבְנֵי מַעְרְבָא, דְּאָמְרִי: מִי הוֹדִיעוֹ לְבַעַל חִטִּין – שֶׁיַּקְנֶה חִטִּין לְבַעַל מָעוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion was Rav's ruling? Was it only **in accordance with** the opinion of **the people of the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **who say: Who informed the owner of the wheat that he should transfer the wheat to the owner of the money?** As the Gemara taught earlier, the Sages of Eretz Yisrael hold that the sale can be performed on another's behalf only if such an arrangement is explicitly stated at the time of the transaction. In this case as well, if the seller did not explicitly state that the money would belong to Rav Kahana, all the money from the sale would be acquired by the seller, who would then be prohibited to give it to Rav Kahana due to the appearance of paying interest.

The *gemara* challenges this distinction. Why should it matter whether the flax was sold in Rav Kahana's name or not? Either way, the flax was Rav Kahana's property in the interim — *kitna mimila hu deyakar minefshia*, the flax appreciated on its own. If the merchant sold it in Rav Kahana's name, he was simply Rav Kahana's agent and the profit is legitimate. If the merchant sold it without identifying Rav Kahana, then he effectively stole Rav Kahana's flax and must pay its market value at the time of the theft — and *detnan*, as we have learned, *mikal gazlan meshalem kesha'at hagezeilah*, a thief pays according to the value at the time of the theft. Either as agent or as thief, Rav Kahana is entitled to the eight. So what is the *ribbit* concern?

### Rav's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָתָם אֲמָנָה הֲוַאי, וְלָא מַשְׁכֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא לְכִיתָּנָא; וְרַב לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דַּאֲמַר רַב: עוֹשִׂין אֲמָנָה בְּפֵירוֹת, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין אֲמָנָה בְּדָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Having rejected the previous explanation of Rav's ruling, the Gemara presents an alternative understanding of the case. The Sages **say: There, it was** a matter of **trust,** as the seller was paid in advance **and Rav Kahana did not pull the flax** as an act of acquisition, or perform any other formal act of acquisition. Consequently, at the time of the second sale the flax belonged to the seller. **And Rav** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rav says: One may make** an arrangement of **trust with regard to** the delivery of items such as **produce,** i.e., one may pay the money in advance with the agreement that he will receive the produce at a later date, **but one may not make** an arrangement of **trust with regard to money,** i.e., one may pay the money in advance with the agreement that he will receive the value of the produce at a later date, as this has the appearance of collecting interest.

The *gemara* resolves this: the premise of the question was mistaken. Rav Kahana had not in fact taken physical possession of the flax — *velo mashcha Rav Kahana lekitna*. The transaction was an *amana* — a futures prepurchase — in which Rav Kahana paid the price now with delivery to come later. There was no *meshicha*, no act of taking possession, in the interim.

This is directly relevant to Rav's principle: *osin amana b'peiros v'ein osin amana b'damim* — one may make a futures prepurchase of produce, but not of money. In the permissible case: I pay four now and receive a bundle of flax later, even if by the time of delivery that bundle is worth eight. That is not *ribbit*, because the transaction is a sale of produce and I receive the produce. But if in the end no produce changes hands — if he simply hands me eight in money in place of the flax he owed me — then I gave four and received eight, with no intermediate ownership of any object. That looks like pure *ribbit* and is not permitted.

### Name Mechanism

Given that there was no *meshicha*, the question returns: how does the merchant's selling the flax "in Rav Kahana's name" remedy the *ribbit* problem? If Rav Kahana never took possession, how does the merchant's verbal identification of the flax as Rav Kahana's transform the transaction into something permissible?

The suggestion appears to be that the merchant's act of selling in Rav Kahana's name functions as if Rav Kahana himself had taken possession and then the merchant was simply his agent in making the sale. Whether this works within the framework of *davar shelo ba l'olam* and the precise parameters of what constitutes real versus apparent *ribbit* are large *sugyas* in their own right. The *gemara* also notes the relevance of *heter iska* as an alternative mechanism in such contexts, though its applicability to futures contracts specifically is not straightforward.

## Gezel Obligation

### Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹזֵל אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ – יוֹלִיכֶנּוּ אַחֲרָיו לְמָדַי. לֹא יִתֵּן לֹא לִבְנוֹ, וְלֹא לִשְׁלוּחוֹ, אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לִשְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין. וְאִם מֵת – יַחֲזִיר לְיוֹרְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who robs another** of an item having **the value of** at least **one *peruta* and takes** a false **oath to** the robbery victim claiming his innocence, and then later wishes to repent, must **bring** the money, which includes the principal together with an additional one-fifth payment, to the robbery victim, even if this necessitates following **after him to** a distant place like **Media.** The robber **may not give** the payment **to** the robbery victim's **son** to return it to the robbery victim, **and neither** may he give it **to his agent, but he may give** the payment **to an agent of the court. And if** the robbery victim **dies, he returns it to his heirs.**

Having worked through the variations on *shinui koneh* — from the craftsman who transforms an object, to the agent who invests money outside his instructions, to the document written in another's name — the *gemara* now pivots to an entirely different dimension of *gezeilah*: the specific *parsha* in *Vayikra* (perek 5) concerning one who takes possession of another's property, swears falsely that he does not have it, and then confesses. The *pesukim* are: *v'heshiv et hagezeilah asher gazal o et ha'oshek asher ashak o et hapikadon asher hufkad ito* — and he shall return the stolen item, or the withheld wages, or the deposit — *vachamishiso yosef alav* — and add a fifth — *v'ashamo yavi l'Hashem* — and bring his *asham* offering.

Notably, the phrase *v'heshiv et hagezeilah asher gazal* has been cited throughout this *perek* as the foundational source for the general obligation to return a stolen object, and from it the *gemara* derives a great deal, including the principle of *asher gazal k'ein shegazal* — return it as it was when stolen — which underlies much of the *shinui koneh* discussion. However, the context of this *pasuk* is specifically the case of a *shevuat sheker*, a false oath. The implication drawn here is that the heightened *obligation* the *pasuk* expresses — how far one must go to ensure the object reaches its owner — may apply specifically in the oath context, not to every case of *gezeilah*.

### Mishna Gezel

The *mishna* states: if one stole from another an item worth even a single *perutah* — *hagozel et chaveiro shaveh peruta* — and swore falsely about it — *venishba lo* — then: *yolichenu acharav leMadai* — one must follow the victim all the way to Madai (Media/Persia) to return it. The obligation to return the stolen item is so severe, in the context of a false oath, that no distance is too great.

### Maddai Obligation

The practical implication is striking: even if the item is worth a single *perutah* — a coin of minimal value, worth less than a cent in today's reckoning — and the aggrieved party has traveled to Persia, the thief is obligated to travel there personally and return it. Presumably, the travel expense may vastly exceed the value of what is being returned. The obligation is absolute; the economic disproportion is irrelevant.

### To Whom Give

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַקֶּרֶן, וְלֹא נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ; מָחַל לוֹ עַל הַקֶּרֶן, וְלֹא מָחַל לוֹ עַל הַחוֹמֶשׁ; מָחַל לוֹ עַל זֶה וְעַל זֶה, חוּץ מִפָּחוּת שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה בַּקֶּרֶן – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לֵילֵךְ אַחֲרָיו. נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ, וְלֹא נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַקֶּרֶן; מָחַל לוֹ עַל הַחוֹמֶשׁ, וְלֹא מָחַל לוֹ עַל הַקֶּרֶן; מָחַל לוֹ עַל זֶה וְעַל זֶה, חוּץ מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה בַּקֶּרֶן – צָרִיךְ לֵילֵךְ אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If **he gave** the robbery victim **the principal** value of the stolen item **but did not give him** the additional **one-fifth** payment, or if the owner **forgave him concerning the principal but did not forgive him concerning** the additional **one-fifth** payment, or if **he forgave him concerning this and concerning that, with the exception of the value of less than one *peruta* of the principal, he need not pursue him** to repay the remaining debt. By contrast, if **he gave** the robbery victim the additional **one-fifth** payment **but did not give him the principal,** or if the robbery victim **forgave him concerning** the additional **one-fifth** payment **but did not forgive him concerning the principal,** or if **he forgave him concerning this and concerning that, with the exception of the value of one *peruta* of the principal, he must pursue him** to repay the remaining debt.

The *mishna* continues: *lo yiten lo livno velo lishlucho* — one may not give it to the victim's son or to a messenger. *Aval noten leshaliach Beit Din* — but one may give it to a court-appointed messenger.

*Rashi* explains: the prohibition on giving to the victim's son or the victim's own messenger means not that doing so is technically prohibited, but rather that the obligation is not thereby discharged. If the money is lost in transit before reaching the victim's hands, the thief remains liable — *delo havei hashava ad demati leidei*, it is not considered a return until it arrives in the victim's possession. Handing money to the victim's messenger does not sever the thief's responsibility if something goes wrong.

This is initially counterintuitive: if the victim sent a messenger, has he not implicitly authorized receipt on his behalf? The *gemara* will address this. The exception — that giving to a *shaliach Beit Din* does discharge the obligation — is explained by *Rashi* as a rabbinic enactment, *takana*, made *mipnei takanat hashavim*, to facilitate repentance. Without such accommodation, the crushing obligation to pursue the victim to the ends of the earth would deter people from even attempting to make amends. By allowing the thief to deliver to the court's agent, the Rabbis created a practical off-ramp.

*Tosafos* takes issue with *Rashi*'s characterization, arguing that the *shaliach Beit Din* mechanism is distinct from the *takana* that will appear later in the *gemara*. According to *Tosafos*, the *shaliach Beit Din* arrangement means the money must still travel to the victim — the court's agent will convey it — but the thief is released from personal liability if the money is subsequently lost. The later *takana*, by contrast, allows the money to be deposited at the *Beit Din* for the victim to retrieve, without any further obligation to ensure delivery.

### Victim Dies

If the victim (*nigzal*) died before the money was returned, the obligation is to return it to his heirs. The *chiddush* here operates specifically in the realm of *kapparah*: one might have thought that since the obligation arose from an interpersonal wrong, returning the money to the heirs rather than to the person wronged would be insufficient to achieve atonement. The *mishna* rules otherwise — returning to the heirs satisfies the obligation, and the *korban* may then be brought.

### Principal Fifth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַקֶּרֶן, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ עַל הַחוֹמֶשׁ –
>
> **Translation:** If the robber **gave** the robbery victim **the principal and took** a false **oath to him concerning** the additional **one-fifth** payment, asserting that he had already paid it,

The *mishna* details several scenarios concerning the *keren* (principal) and the *chomesh* (the additional fifth, which in rabbinic arithmetic equals one-quarter of the principal, since adding a fifth to four yields five, making the added amount a fifth of the total). If the victim forgave the principal but not the *chomesh*, or forgave the *chomesh* but not the principal, these are treated differently. The rule is that the obligation to travel to extraordinary lengths — to *Maddai* — applies specifically to the *keren*, not to the *chomesh*. If what remains outstanding is only the *chomesh*, or only an amount less than a *perutah* of the principal, the thief need not pursue the victim to the ends of the earth, even though the monetary obligation remains.

If, however, even a *perutah*'s worth of the principal remains outstanding — *chutz mishaveh prutah bakeren* — then *tzarich lalechet acharav*, the thief must go after him. It is the nature and character of the remaining obligation, not its dollar amount, that determines the extent of the pursuit required.

### Fifth on Fifth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלֵּם חוֹמֶשׁ עַל חוֹמֶשׁ, עַד שֶׁיִּתְמַעֵט הַקֶּרֶן מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** then the additional one-fifth is considered a new principal obligation. The robber **pays** an additional **one-fifth** payment **apart from** the additional **one-fifth** payment about which he had taken a false oath. If he then takes a false oath concerning the second one-fifth payment, he is assessed an additional one-fifth payment for that oath, **until the principal,** i.e., the additional one-fifth payment about which he has most recently taken the false oath, **is reduced to less than the value of one *peruta*.**

A further scenario: the thief returns the *keren* but swears falsely regarding the *chomesh*. Now the *chomesh* itself is the subject of a *shevuat sheker*. The *mishna* rules: *harei zeh mosif chomesh al chomesh* — one now adds a fifth upon the fifth. This process can in principle repeat itself, compounding: every false oath generates a new *chomesh* on whatever was falsely sworn, with the obligation continuing *ad sheyismaat hakeren mishaveh prutah* — until the outstanding principal falls below the value of a *perutah*. Each iteration of swearing falsely also requires a separate *asham* offering — a point the *gemara* finds remarkable, since even small amounts trigger the full apparatus of a major *korban*.

### Various Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּפִקָּדוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אוֹ בְּפִקָּדוֹן אוֹ בִתְשׂוּמֶת יָד אוֹ בְגָזֵל, אוֹ עָשַׁק אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ אוֹ מָצָא אֲבֵדָה; וְכִחֶשׁ בָּהּ וְנִשְׁבַּע עַל שָׁקֶר״ – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **And such** is the *halakha* **with regard to a deposit, as it is stated:** "If anyone sins, and commits a trespass against the Lord, and he defrauds his counterpart **with regard to a deposit, or with regard to a pledge, or with regard to a robbery, or if he exploited his counterpart; or he has found that which was lost, and deals falsely with it, and swears to a lie**…he shall restore it in full, and shall add the fifth part more to it" (Leviticus 5:21–24). **This** one must **pay** the **principal and** an additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** bring **a guilt-offering.**

The *mishna* concludes by extending the oath consequence beyond the case of straightforward *gezel*. Citing the *pesukim*: *o pikadon o tishumas yad o gazel o ashak et amito o matza aveidah v'kichash bah* — whether it was a deposit (*pikadon*), a loan (*tishumas yad*, which *Rashi* understands as a denied loan or item taken improperly), an outright theft, withheld wages, or a lost object that was found and denied — *venishba al shaker, harei zeh meshalem keren v'chomesh v'asham*. In all cases, swearing falsely about it triggers the same triad of obligations: return the principal, add the fifth, and bring the *asham*.

The broader principle emerging from this *mishna* is the following. As a baseline rule of *gezeilah*, the obligation is to return what was taken, but the normal rules of *dinin* do not require the thief to exhaust himself in pursuit of the victim. It is specifically in the case of a false oath — where the thief compounded his wrong by swearing falsely before God — that the *pesukim* impose a dramatically heightened standard of return.

## Mishna Attribution

### Oath Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ נִשְׁבַּע לוֹ – אִין, לֹא נִשְׁבַּע לוֹ – לָא; מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וְלָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא! דְּתַנְיָא: גָּזַל אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן, וְכׇל אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״אוֹתִי גָּזַל״ – מַנִּיחַ גְּזֵילָה בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא זוֹ דֶּרֶךְ מוֹצִיאָתוֹ מִידֵי עֲבֵירָה, עַד שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם גְּזֵילָה לְכׇל אֶחָד [וְאֶחָד].
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if a robber took a false oath that he did not rob, he must travel even as far as Media in order to repay his obligation. This indicates that if **he takes an oath to** the robbery victim, **yes,** he is required to go to any length to repay his obligation, but if he **did not take an oath to him, no,** he does not have to do so. **Whose** opinion is this? It is **not** the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon and not** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, as it is taught** in a mishna (*Yevamot* 118b): If one **robbed one of five** people **and he does not know which of them** he robbed, **and each one** of the five **says: He robbed me,** the robber **places** the **stolen item between them and withdraws** from them; this is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Akiva says: This is not** the **way to spare him from transgression.** He is not considered to have returned the stolen item **until he pays** the value of the **stolen item to each and every one** of the five.

The *gemara* opens its analysis with a question arising directly from the *mishna*'s structure: the extraordinary obligation to pursue the victim to *Maddai* is stated in a context that presupposes a false oath — *nishba lo in*, it applies when he swore; *lo nishba lo lo*, it does not apply when he did not swear. This raises the question of which Tanna's opinion the *mishna* represents.

The *gemara* states that the *mishna* cannot straightforwardly represent either Rabbi Tarfon or Rabbi Akiva, as both appear in a *baraita* that does not distinguish between oath and non-oath cases.

The *baraita*: if someone stole from one of five people and does not know which one, and each of them claims to be the one who was robbed — *mani'ach gezeilah beineihem umistalek*, Rabbi Tarfon says: place the stolen amount among them and walk away. This satisfies the obligation. Rabbi Akiva says: *lo zu haderech motzi'ah miyedei aveira* — this path does not extricate one from sin. One must pay each person individually the full amount.

### Analysis of Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן – אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִישְׁתְּבַע, אָמַר: מַנִּיחַ גְּזֵילָה בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק! אִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּבַע, אָמַר: עַד שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם גְּזֵילָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: In accordance with **whose** opinion is the mishna written? **If** one suggests that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon,** it is not so, because **even though** the robber **took** a false **oath** that he did not rob, Rabbi Tarfon **says: He places** the **stolen item between them and withdraws;** it is not his responsibility to ensure that it reaches the robbery victim. **If** one suggests that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** it is also not so, because **even though** the robber **did not** necessarily **take** a false **oath,** Rabbi Akiva **says:** He is not considered to have returned the stolen item **until he pays** the value of the **stolen item to each and every one** of the five, while the mishna rules that his obligation is contingent upon his having taken the false oath.

*Tosafos* raises a sharp question here. Generally, when one person says "you owe me" (*bari*) and another says "I don't know" (*shema*), the *bari* claim may prevail. Why then must the thief pay all five, rather than waiting for each to prove his individual claim? *Tosafos* answers: the normal rule that *bari adif* applies when there is no contradicting claim. Here, four out of five claimants are lying, and their contradictory claims cancel each other's credibility. Conversely, *Tosafos* notes, even the opinion that normally *shema* prevails over *bari* might concede that here the thief must pay all five — because the thief himself admits he owes someone the money; the uncertainty is only as to whom. These two scenarios are thus analytically distinct on both ends of the usual *bari v'shema* framework.

### Oath Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, וְכִי קָאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא עַד שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם גְּזֵילָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד – הֵיכָא דְּאִישְׁתְּבַע הוּא דְּקָאָמַר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לַאֲשֶׁר הוּא לוֹ, יִתְּנֶנּוּ בְּיוֹם אַשְׁמָתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, and when Rabbi Akiva says** that the robber is not considered to have returned the stolen item **until he pays** the value of the **stolen item to each and every one** of the five, it is only in a case **where** the robber **took** a false **oath that** Rabbi Akiva **says** this. **What is the reason? As the verse states** with regard to one who takes a false oath concerning a financial obligation: **"Unto him to whom it appertains shall he give it, on the day of his being guilty"** (Leviticus 5:24). The *halakha* that the guilty party must make a rigorous effort to return what he owes is stated in the case of one who took a false oath, and Rabbi Akiva would state his ruling only in that case.

The *gemara*'s initial attempt to reconcile the *mishna* with the positions of Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva is to propose that Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva are themselves debating only the case of a *shevuat sheker*. On this reading: Rabbi Tarfon holds that even after a false oath, placing the money among the five and walking away is sufficient. Rabbi Akiva holds that a false oath imposes a heightened obligation — one must ensure the money reaches the actual victim — and therefore pays each person individually and, in our *mishna*, pursues the victim to *Maddai*.

According to this reading, the *mishna* aligns with Rabbi Akiva when the condition of false oath is met, and Rabbi Akiva's position is specifically conditioned on the oath: without it, perhaps even Rabbi Akiva would not require such extraordinary measures.

The underlying *pasuk* cited in support: *l'asher hu lo yitnenu b'yom ashmato* — "he shall give it to the one to whom it belongs on the day of his guilt." The word *ashmato* — his guilt — emphasizes that it is specifically in the context of realized guilt, of *shevuat sheker*, that the obligation to ensure the money reaches its rightful owner is at its most absolute.

### R. Elazar Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן – אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִישְׁתְּבַע, עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר: תַּקָּנָה גְּדוֹלָה הִתְקִינוּ, שֶׁאִם הָיְתָה הוֹצָאָה יְתֵירָה עַל הַקֶּרֶן – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ לְבֵית דִּין, וּמֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ וּמִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Tarfon** rule that a robber who took a false oath is not required to pay all claimants, being that the verse indicates otherwise? The Gemara answers: **Even though he took** a false **oath** and by Torah law is obligated to return the stolen item to the robbery victim, **the Sages instituted an ordinance** allowing him to place it between the five possible victims, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says:** The Sages **instituted a great ordinance** stating **that if the expense** required to return a stolen item to the victim **is greater than the principal,** the robber may **pay the principal and** the additional **one-fifth** payment **to the court, and** he then **brings his guilt-offering and achieves atonement for himself.** This ordinance would apply here as well, as the expense required to pay all five claimants is greater than the principal.

But what of Rabbi Tarfon, who holds that even after a false oath one need only leave the money among the five claimants? The *gemara* explains this through the lens of a *takana* cited by Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Tzadok: *takana gedola hitkinu* — a major enactment was enacted — *she'im hayta hotza'ah yeteirah al hakeren* — if the expenses of retrieval exceed the principal — *meshalem keren v'chomesh l'Beit Din* — the thief pays the principal and the *chomesh* to the court — *umeivi ashamo umitkaper lo* — brings his *asham* and achieves atonement.

*Rashi* holds that this is the same *takana* as the *mishna*'s provision for a *shaliach Beit Din*. *Tosafos* disagrees, distinguishing them: the *shaliach Beit Din* arrangement requires that the money still reach the victim — it merely releases the thief from liability if the money is lost in transit — whereas Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Tzadok's *takana* leaves the money at the *Beit Din* for the victim to collect, with no further obligation of conveyance.

On this reading, Rabbi Tarfon effectively incorporates this *takana*. Even in principle, one should not have to spend more than the amount stolen in order to return what was stolen. Therefore, when the cost of ensuring return to any specific individual would exceed the principal — as in the case of five potential victims, where paying all five would cost four times the original theft — one deposits the money with the *Beit Din* and discharges the obligation.

### R. Akiva Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – כִּי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא, הֵיכָא דְּיָדַע לְמַאן גַּזְלֵיהּ, דְּקָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ מָמוֹנָא לְמָרֵיהּ; גָּזַל אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה, דְּלָא יָדַע לְמַאן גַּזְלֵיהּ, דְּלָא הָדַר מָמוֹנָא לְמָרֵיהּ – לָא עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** doesn't **Rabbi Akiva** agree that the Sages instituted this ordinance? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Akiva holds that **when the Sages instituted the ordinance,** they did so only for cases **where** the robber **knows whom he robbed, as** in such cases **he is** definitely **returning the money to its owner** by depositing it with the court, who will convey it to the robbery victim. But in the case of one who **robbed one of five** people, **where he does not know whom he robbed,** and **where,** by merely placing the stolen item between the five of them **the money is not returned to its owner, the Sages did not institute an ordinance.**

Rabbi Akiva rejects the application of this *takana* to the five-person case, and his reasoning is pointed. Rabbi Akiva might in principle accept a *takana* when it is known who the victim is — *heicha deyada l'man gazlei*. In such cases, depositing the money with the *Beit Din* still results in the money ultimately reaching the victim, *d'kamahadar mamona l'mareih* — the money finds its way back to the owner. But when one stole from an unknown member of a group of five and gives the money to the *Beit Din*, there is no mechanism guaranteeing the actual victim will ever receive it — *d'lo ahadurei mamona l'mareih*, the money may never be returned to its true owner. Rabbi Akiva holds that no rabbinic *takana* would be enacted in circumstances where the victim is not certain to receive restitution. Therefore, the full obligation stands: pay each person individually.

### R. Shimon Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא עַל שֶׁלָּקַח אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מֵאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן לָקַח – שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ דְּמֵי מִקָּח בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק. עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ? שֶׁגָּזַל אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ מֵאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן גָּזַל; שֶׁרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: מַנִּיחַ דְּמֵי גְּזֵילָה בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה, עַד שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם גְּזֵילָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Yehuda raises an objection** to the explanation that Rabbi Akiva stated his opinion specifically in a case where the robber took an oath. The *baraita* in the *Tosefta* (*Yevamot* 14:2) states that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva did not disagree with regard to one who purchased** an item **from** one of **five** people **and does not know from which of them he purchased** it, as both agree **that** in this case **he places the money of the purchase between them and withdraws.** Since the purchaser has not transgressed, he is not penalized by being required to suffer the loss of paying each of them. **With regard to what did they disagree?** They disagree with regard to one **who robbed one of five people, and he does not know from which of them he robbed** the item, **as Rabbi Tarfon says: He places the stolen item between them and withdraws, and Rabbi Akiva says: He has no remedy,** i.e., he has not fulfilled his obligation to return the stolen item, **until he pays** the value of **the stolen item to each and every one** of them.

At this point Rav Huna bar Yehuda raises an objection from a further *baraita* in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva did not disagree in the case of an honest *purchaser* who bought from one of five merchants and cannot recall which. In that case — *shelakach echad mechamesh bnei adam veein yodea mimi lakach* — all agree: place the payment among them and walk away. The debate applies specifically to *gezel* — *shegazal echad michamesh bnei adam veein yodea mimi gazal*. Rabbi Tarfon says place the stolen amount and walk away; Rabbi Akiva says there is no remedy but to pay each one.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּאִישְׁתְּבַע, מָה לִי לָקַח מָה לִי גָּזַל?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna bar Yehuda states his objection: **And if it enters your mind that** Rabbi Akiva stated his ruling specifically **with regard to** one who **took** a false **oath, what** is the difference **to me** if he **purchased** an item from another, and **what** is the difference **to me** if he **robbed** him? In either case he has transgressed the prohibition against taking a false oath denying his obligation, and he should be penalized by being required to ensure that the one to whom he owes the money receives it.

This *baraita* of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar presents a challenge to the preceding analysis. If the debate between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva is limited to cases of false oath, why would Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar distinguish between an honest purchase and *gezel*? If the critical variable is the oath and not the initial wrong, then once a person swears falsely about either transaction — the honest purchase gone bad or the outright theft — the same heightened obligations should apply, and there should be no reason to distinguish based on how the obligation originally arose. The challenge is then: *mah li lakach mah li gazal* — if it is the false oath that matters, what difference does it make whether the underlying wrong was a legitimate purchase or a theft?

### Chasid Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד מֵתִיב רָבָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָסִיד אֶחָד שֶׁלָּקַח מִשְּׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם וְלֹא הָיָה יוֹדֵעַ מֵאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן לָקַח, וּבָא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, אָמַר לוֹ: הַנַּח דְּמֵי מִקָּחֲךָ בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְהִסְתַּלֵּק. בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אָמַר לוֹ: אֵין לְךָ תַּקָּנָה, עַד שֶׁתְּשַׁלֵּם לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּמִישְׁתְּבַע, חָסִיד מִי מִישְׁתְּבַע בְּשִׁיקְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava raises another objection** to the explanation that Rabbi Akiva stated his opinion specifically in a case where the robber took an oath. There was **an incident involving a certain pious** man **who purchased** an item **from** one of **two people, and he did not know from which of them he purchased** the item, **and he came before Rabbi Tarfon** for a ruling. Rabbi Tarfon **said to him: Place the money of your purchase between them and withdraw. He** then **came before Rabbi Akiva,** who **said to him: You have no remedy until you pay each and every one,** i.e., both of them. **And if it enters your mind** that Rabbi Akiva stated his ruling specifically **where** the purchaser **took** a false **oath, does a pious** man **take a false oath?** It seems from this incident that Rabbi Akiva rules he must pay all potential owners regardless of whether or not he took a false oath.

Furthermore, Rava raises a story: *maaseh bechasid echad* — there was an incident involving a *chasid* who had purchased from one of two people and did not know which. He came before Rabbi Tarfon, who ruled: place the money among them and walk away. He then came before Rabbi Akiva, who ruled: you have no remedy but to pay each one. This *chasid* story, cited to illustrate the debate, involves an honest purchase — not a theft at all — directly contradicting Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's claim that the debate does not apply to purchases.

This generates a further problem for the oath-based reading. If the debate between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva applies only when a false oath was taken, then a *chasid* — a person of established righteousness — should never be the protagonist of such a case. *Chasid mi mishtaba beshikra?* Would a righteous person swear falsely?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּמִישְׁתְּבַע וַהֲדַר הָוֵי חָסִיד, וְהָא כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּאָמְרִינַן ״מַעֲשֶׂה בְּחָסִיד אֶחָד״ – אוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא, אוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי אִילְעַאי; וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי אִילְעַאי חֲסִידִים דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא הֲווֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say that** perhaps **he took** a false **oath and later became a pious** man, **but** isn't there a tradition that **anywhere that we say:** There was **an incident involving a certain pious** man, the pious man is **either Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava or Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Elai, and Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava and Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Elai, were** both **pious** men **from the beginning.**

One might respond: perhaps he swore falsely and subsequently became a *chasid* — it is, after all, precisely the kind of story that fits the spirit of *teshuvah*. However, the *gemara* objects: whenever the phrase *maaseh bechasid echad* appears in the tradition, it invariably refers to either Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava or Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai — *chasidim meikara havu*, people who were righteous from the outset of their careers, not individuals who began with a false oath and later repented.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן הִיא, וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן הֵיכָא דְּאִישְׁתְּבַע. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לַאֲשֶׁר הוּא לוֹ, יִתְּנֶנּוּ בְּיוֹם אַשְׁמָתוֹ״. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מִישְׁתְּבַע – קָנֵיס.
>
> **Translation:** Due to these objections, the Gemara offers an alternative explanation. **Rather,** the mishna here **is actually** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon, and Rabbi Tarfon concedes** that a robber must pay every potential owner in a case **where he took** a false **oath,** as is the case in the mishna here. **What is the reason? As the verse states: "Unto him to whom it appertains shall he give it, in the day of his being guilty"** (Leviticus 5:24). This verse, which requires one to go to any length to return money owed, is referring specifically to one who takes a false oath concerning his financial obligation. **And Rabbi Akiva, who said** in the mishna in tractate *Yevamot* that a robber must pay all potential owners **even though he did not take** a false **oath,** agrees that the verse is referring specifically to one who takes a false oath, but holds that the Sages **penalize** the robber by obligating him to pay them all in any event.

The conclusion that emerges is that the debate of Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva cannot be confined to the case of a false oath. They disagree even in the absence of a *shevuat sheker* — and the *chasid* story makes this plain. This, however, now reintroduces the original problem: if Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva debate even without an oath, how does our *mishna* — which limits the extraordinary obligation to the oath context — align with either of them? The *gemara* leaves this question open for the following day's discussion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, מִכְּדֵי הֵיכָא דְּמִישְׁתְּבַע – לָא סַגִּיא דְּלָא הוֹדָה; מַאי אִירְיָא ״וְנִשְׁבַּע״? אֲפִילּוּ בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה נָמֵי! דְּתַנְיָא, מוֹדֶה רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן בְּאוֹמֵר לִשְׁנַיִם: ״גָּזַלְתִּי אֶחָד מִכֶּם מָנֶה וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה מִכֶּם״ – נוֹתֵן לָזֶה מָנֶה וְלָזֶה מָנֶה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation of the mishna: **But** if the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon, after all,** the mere fact that it is a case **where he took** a false **oath is not sufficient** to obligate him to pay the additional one-fifth and to bring a guilt-offering if it is a case **where he did not** also **admit** that he took a false oath and owes the money. Therefore, **why** would the mishna here **specifically** state: **And he took** a false **oath? Even without** taking the false **oath,** the robber should **also** be obligated to pursue the owner as a result of his having admitted his obligation. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Tarfon concedes** that **in** a case where a robber **says to two** people: **I robbed one of you** of **one hundred dinars and I do not know which of you** it was, **he gives one hundred dinars to this** person **and one hundred dinars to that** person,

Having established that the Tannaic debate operates independently of the oath, the task for tomorrow is to identify which opinion the *mishna* reflects and how the oath condition of the *mishna* can be reconciled with the broader scope of the Tannaic disagreement.
