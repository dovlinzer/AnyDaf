# Shevuot 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shachir & Witnesses

### Rami & Rava Debate

The daf opens at the bottom of 45b with the statement of Rami bar Chama. The mishna had established that the *shvuat sachir* — the oath by which a hired worker swears he was not paid and thereby collects from the *baal habayit* — applies only when the hiring itself was witnessed. If there are no witnesses to the fact that he was hired at all, the *baal habayit* can simply deny that any hiring took place, and since the *sachir* cannot even establish that a relationship existed, he is believed to deny it, and the *sachir* cannot make the oath.

Confronted with this qualification, Rami bar Chama exclaims: *kama ma'alya hai shma'atata* — how wonderful is this teaching! The logic is so satisfying. Rava, however, immediately pushes back: *mai maylusa* — what exactly is so great about it? There are serious difficulties with this reasoning. If we accept the underlying principle — that one who could have denied the entire transaction is believed to admit it partially — then consider the *shvuat shomrim*, the oath of bailees that the Torah itself prescribes. When a *shomer* swears that the deposited item was damaged or lost through circumstances beyond his control, why does he even need to swear? The Torah says he is believed, but under this logic, he could have said *lo hayu devarim me'olam* — "nothing of the sort ever happened, I never accepted the object" — and since he could have made that stronger denial, he should be believed on the lesser claim without any oath at all. *Heichi mishkachat la* — in what case does the *shvuat shomrim* even apply?

### Migo & Shtar

The obvious answer is *de'afkedei be'edim*: all the cases in the Torah where a bailee takes an oath presuppose that there were witnesses to the deposit. Just as in our mishna we require witnesses to the hiring, so too the *shvuat shomrim* applies only when there were witnesses to the entrustment — paralleling the *sachir* case exactly.

But this answer generates a further difficulty. Once you apply the *migo* principle this way — allowing someone to be believed because of a stronger denial he could have made — then even where there are witnesses to the deposit, the bailee should still be exempt from an oath. Why? Because even when witnesses saw the item handed over, the bailee is believed to say he returned it. A person who received money or a *pikadon* with witnesses present is nonetheless believed to say he returned it without bringing witnesses to the return. So the *migo* — "I could have said I never took it" — should still work even with witnesses to the deposit.

The Gemara therefore concludes: *de'afkedei bishtara* — the Torah's cases of *shvuat shomrim* presuppose that the deposit was made with a *shtar*, a document. With a *shtar* in hand, the bailee cannot claim he never received the object, and he cannot claim he returned it either, because *shtarcha beyad baal habayit mai ba'ei* — why would the owner still be holding the document if the item had been returned?

### Return Without Witnesses

From this exchange the Gemara infers a general principle upon which both sides implicitly agree: if one entrusts an item to another *with witnesses*, he does not need witnesses to the return — the bailee is believed to say he returned it even without bringing proof. This is why the Gemara had to push all the way to a *shtar* to resolve the problem: mere witnesses to the deposit do not bar the *migo* of "I returned it." Only a *shtar* does, since the document's continued presence in the owner's hands serves as prima facie evidence that return never occurred.

### Rav Sheshet's Inquiry

Rami bar Chama then applied the verse *va'yyassem David et ha'devarim ha'eleh bilvavo* to Rav Sheshet — meaning that just as David took a matter deeply to heart, so Rav Sheshet genuinely sought to understand whether this requirement of witnesses for the hiring is actually the accepted *halacha*, and what support exists for it. He found Rabbah bar Shmuel and asked: *tani mar midi be'sachir* — have you taught anything relating to the case of the *sachir*? Rabbah bar Shmuel answered yes, and cited a *braita*: the *sachir bizmano nishba venotel* — during the time when he would normally be collecting his wages, the *sachir* can swear and collect when they dispute whether he was paid. How so? When the *sachir* says "you hired me and did not pay me" and the *baal habayit* says "I hired you and I did pay you."

But the *braita* continues with a contrasting case: if the *sachir* says "you agreed to pay me two" and the *baal habayit* says "I only agreed to one" — then *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*, the one who seeks to extract from his fellow bears the burden of proof. This is a classic case of *modeh bemiktzat*, partial admission. The *baal habayit* admits he owes something but disputes the higher amount, so he would have to take the standard oath of *modeh bemiktzat* — but the *sachir* here does not have the independent power of the *shvuat sachir* to collect the disputed amount; he needs witnesses.

### Hamotzi Analysis

From the structure of the *braita* — the *seifa* explicitly requiring *ra'aya* in the disputed-wages case — one might infer that the *reisha*, the classic case where the *sachir* simply swears and collects, requires no witnesses at all. This would be evidence against the position of Rav and Shmuel that witnesses to the hiring are necessary.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, at the top of 46a, rejects this inference. He reads both the *reisha* and the *seifa* as involving witnesses. The *braita*'s use of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* refers specifically to cases where witnesses are needed to *win* — that is, to be paid. In the *seifa*, the *sachir* cannot collect the disputed extra amount unless he produces witnesses; hence the phrase. In the *reisha*, it may well be that witnesses are also required — but there, witnesses establish the *context* for the oath rather than being the direct vehicle of collection. The *braita* reserves *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* for situations where witnesses are the only means of prevailing. One therefore cannot prove from the *braita's* structure that the *reisha* requires no witnesses at all.

## Katzatz & Shvua

### Katzatz Question

Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba reports that the yeshiva of Rav sent a question to Shmuel: *uman omer shtayim katzatzta li* — a craftsman says you agreed to pay me two hundred, and the *baal habayit* says I only agreed to one hundred. They are not disputing whether payment was made; they are disputing what the agreed-upon wage was — the *katzatz*. *Mi nishba* — can the *sachir* take an oath? The reasoning behind the question is that perhaps the same concern that motivates the *shvuat sachir* — that the *baal habayit* is busy and may not clearly remember whether he paid — applies equally to his memory of what wages he originally agreed to.

Shmuel responded: *bezo yishava ba'al habayit veyafsid uman* — here, the *baal habayit* takes the oath of *modeh bemiktzat* and the *uman* loses unless he produces actual witnesses. The rationale is *katzitza vadai midkar dachir inshi* — people definitely remember what wages they agreed to. Setting wages is a focused, deliberate transaction, not the sort of hurried, distracted moment of payment where the *baal habayit* might forget. There is more *yishuv hada'as* when the agreement is made, and there is no proliferation of simultaneous transactions to cause confusion.

### Baraita Contradiction

One might challenge Shmuel's position from the *braita* of Rabbah bar Shmuel quoted earlier, which stated: *katzatz, hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — in the case of a disputed wage rate, the burden of proof falls on the one who seeks to extract. The implication is even stronger than Shmuel's ruling: not only does the *sachir* lack the power of *shvuat sachir*, but the *baal habayit* need not even take an oath — the *sachir* gets nothing unless he brings witnesses. *Ilo lo maysi raya pattur* — if he brings no proof, the *baal habayit* is entirely exempt. This seems to contradict Shmuel, who held that the *baal habayit* must at least take the oath of *modeh bemiktzat*.

### Rav Nachman's Reading

Rav Nachman resolves this by reading the *braita* as presenting two alternatives rather than a single outcome: *litzdadin katanei* — the teaching is two-sided. Either the *uman* produces witnesses and collects the full amount he claims, *o yishava ba'al habayit veyafsid* — or the *baal habayit* takes an oath of *modeh bemiktzat* and the *uman* loses the disputed amount. The *braita* is not saying the *baal habayit* walks free without any oath; it is saying the *sachir* cannot use *shvuat sachir* to collect. This reading requires some forcing of the *braita*'s language, but it reconciles the sources.

### Tailor Case

A further *braita* is then introduced to test this position. Someone gives his *tallis* to a craftsman — say, a tailor — for repairs. The *uman* says "you agreed to pay me two" and the *baal habayit* says "I only agreed to one." This is the identical *katzatz* scenario. The *braita* states: *kol zman she'tallis beyad uman al haba'al habayit lehavei raya* — as long as the *tallis* remains in the *uman*'s hands, the *baal habayit* must bring proof. Here the situation differs from the earlier case because the *uman* is *muchzak* — he is holding the *baal habayit*'s garment. The *baal habayit* is the one seeking to reclaim his property, making him the *hamotzi*, and he must prove the lower amount if he wants his *tallis* returned for less than what the *uman* claims.

The *braita* continues: *nasna lo bizmano nishba venotel* — if the *uman* gave back the *tallis* during the normal time of payment, he swears and collects. *Avar zmano hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — but once that period passes, there is no longer *shvuat sachir* and we revert to the standard rule of burden of proof. The middle clause is the most striking: it appears to say that even in a *katzatz* case — where they are disputing not whether he was paid but what the agreed amount was — *shvuat sachir* applies during *bizmano*. This seems to directly contradict Shmuel's ruling that *katzatz* is not covered by *shvuat sachir*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Attribution

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: *ha mani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this *braita* follows the view of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that whenever the *shvua* would normally fall on the *baal habayit*, the Rabbis transferred it to the *sachir* so that he swears and collects. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the structural transfer of the oath governs — so even in *katzatz*, where ordinarily a *modeh bemiktzat* oath would fall on the *baal habayit*, Rabbi Yehuda says *shvuat sachir* applies. The *Chachamim* disagree.

### Which Yehuda?

But this raises an immediate question: which Rabbi Yehuda is being invoked? If it is Rabbi Yehuda of our mishna, that identification creates a problem, because Rabbi Yehuda of the mishna is actually *stricter* on the *sachir*, not more lenient. The mishna records Rabbi Yehuda as requiring *miktzat hoda'ah* — a partial admission — before *shvuat sachir* can apply. Rabbi Yehuda limits the *sachir*'s ability to swear; he does not expand it. So how can it be Rabbi Yehuda of the mishna who is broadening *shvuat sachir* to include *katzatz* cases?

### Yehuda Permits Katzatz

The Gemara therefore clarifies: this is Rabbi Yehuda *of the braita*, not the mishna. And the *braita* is explicit. It reads: *sachir kol zman shelo avar alav zmano harei zeh nishba venotel* — during *bizmano* he swears and collects. Rabbi Yehuda in that *braita* then specifies when this applies: when the *sachir* says "give me my fifty *dinar* wages that you owe me" and the *baal habayit* says "you already received a gold *dinar* from them" — that is a *modeh bemiktzat* scenario, and there Rabbi Yehuda says *shvuat sachir* applies. But then the *braita* explicitly adds: *o she'amar lo shtayim katzatzta li vehala omer lo katzatzti lecha ela achas* — or if he says "you agreed to two" and the other says "I only agreed to one." This is the *katzatz* case stated directly. And Rabbi Yehuda says *shvuat sachir* applies even there.

The *braita* then draws the limit: if the *baal habayit* says "I never hired you" or "I hired you and paid you in full," then *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — no *shvuat sachir* operates in those cases.

### Paradox Explained

This generates an apparent paradox. Rabbi Yehuda of the *braita* is *stricter* than the *Chachamim* in one direction — he requires *modeh bemiktzat* as a precondition — yet he is *more lenient* in another direction, extending *shvuat sachir* even to *katzatz*. The *Chachamim* require no *modeh bemiktzat*, yet they do not extend *shvuat sachir* to *katzatz*. So where Rabbi Yehuda is strict, the *Chachamim* are lenient, and where Rabbi Yehuda is lenient, the *Chachamim* are strict. Rav Sheshet bar Rav Idi finds this deeply counterintuitive: *heichi demachmir Rabbi Yehuda mikalei Rabbanan, heicha demeikal Rabbi Yehuda machmir Rabbanan* — how can the positions simply flip?

### Rava's Framework

Rava provides the conceptual resolution: *beha kamifligi* — this is the true nature of their debate. It is not a question of leniency or stringency at all. The two positions reflect two entirely different frameworks for understanding what *shvuat sachir* is.

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *bedi'oraita avdu lei takanta l'sachir* — the *takana* for the *sachir* was built specifically on the structure of a *shvuah di'oraita*, a Torah-level oath. The Torah imposes *shvuat modeh bemiktzat* on the *baal habayit*; the Rabbis took that same oath and transferred it to the *sachir*, enabling him to swear and collect. This means that wherever there would have been a *shvuat modeh bemiktzat* falling on the *baal habayit*, the *takana* applies and the *sachir* gets to swear instead — including in a *katzatz* case where the *baal habayit* admitted to one amount while the *sachir* claims more. But where the *baal habayit* is a *kofer hakol* — a complete denier — there is no *shvuat modeh bemiktzat* from the Torah; at most he would take a *shvuat hesset*, which is rabbinic. And *takanta letakanta lo avdinan* — you do not build a *takana* on top of another *takana*. The transfer of the oath to the *sachir* works only when the underlying oath is Torah-mandated.

The *Chachamim*, by contrast, hold: *bedrabbanan nami avdinan takanta l'sachir* — even where the underlying oath would be only rabbinic, the Rabbis can still enact the *takana* of *shvuat sachir*. Their framework is not structural at all; it is functional. The *takana* was created because the *baal habayit* is distracted, busy, and likely to forget whether he paid. Given that concern, the Rabbis empowered the *sachir* to swear wherever that forgetfulness is plausible. But *katzitzah vadai midkar dachir* — the agreed wage rate is something people definitely remember. A *baal habayit* does not forget what wages he committed to; that conversation happens in a focused moment. Therefore, the *takana* simply does not apply to *katzatz* — not because of any structural limitation, but because the underlying concern that motivated the *takana* is absent.

The result is elegant: Rabbi Yehuda's position is formally narrower (only *modeh bemiktzat*) yet substantively broader (includes *katzatz*); the *Chachamim*'s position is formally broader (includes cases of *kofer hakol*) yet substantively narrower (excludes *katzatz*). They are not reversing each other arbitrarily — each is following a consistent internal logic about what *shvuat sachir* fundamentally is.

## Nigzal Oath

### Nigzal Setup

The Gemara now turns to the next case in the mishna: the *nigzal*, the person whose property was taken from him. The mishna describes the scenario: *hayu edim she'nichnas leveyso lemashkeno* — witnesses saw someone, say Reuven, entering Shimon's house with the intent to take property as *mashkon* — collateral — without Shimon's permission and without authorization from a court. This constitutes unlawful seizure. In such a case, the mishna permits the *nigzal* — Shimon — to take an oath specifying what was taken and thereby recover it from the *gazlan*.

### Intent Problem

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty. If all that happened is that witnesses saw Reuven *entering* with the stated intention, why is that sufficient basis to empower Shimon to take an oath and collect? Rav Nachman had already taught the principle: if someone picks up an axe and declares "I am going to chop down so-and-so's palm tree," and the palm tree is subsequently found chopped down, we do not assume the person with the axe did it. As Abaye explains, *inshi gezimi velo avdi* — people make exaggerated threats and declarations without carrying them through. A person's announced intent, even with the subsequent appearance of harm, is not legally conclusive. The classic observation applies: in any investigation, someone declaring "I will do X" is not evidence that he actually did X. So if Reuven merely said he was going in to take something as collateral, that is hardly grounds to let Shimon make an oath.

### Hidden Items Proof

The Gemara answers: we must be dealing with a case where witnesses actually saw him take something. But then a new question arises: if witnesses saw what he took, why is the oath needed at all? The witnesses can simply testify to what was removed.

The answer, attributed to Rabbah bar bar Chanah in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is: *betono keilim hanitalim tachat knafav* — the witnesses saw that items were being secreted under his garment, but they could not see what the items were. They saw the act of concealment and removal but not the contents. This creates the precise situation where the *nigzal*'s oath is needed: the fact of taking is established, but the identity of what was taken is not. The homeowner therefore swears to the contents and collects.

### Purchase Claim

Rav Yehuda further specifies: *ra'u shehitmin keilim tachat knafav veyatza* — witnesses saw the person conceal items under his garment and walk out. Normally one would expect this to be a case of *kofer hakol* — complete denial — upon which the *nigzal* takes his oath. But what if the person admits he took something and claims he purchased it? He says *lekuchin hen beyadi* — these are legitimately purchased items in my possession. The Gemara rules: *eino ne'eman* — he is not believed.

### Owner Doesn't Sell

This ruling requires qualification. Is it not a general principle that *chazaka ma she'tachat yad adam harei hu shelo* — whatever is in a person's possession is presumed to be his? The Gemara clarifies: this case involves a *baal habayit she'eino asuy limkor keilav* — a homeowner who does not normally sell his household items. In such a context, the claim of purchase is inherently implausible. Unlike a store or a dealer, an ordinary homeowner's personal property is not ordinarily available for sale to a visitor who walks through his door. If I enter your home and walk out with your vase, and you claim I stole it, I cannot simply invoke *chazaka* and say I bought it — not when homeowners do not regularly sell their belongings. Conversely, if the *baal habayit* is someone who does sell items from his home, then the claim of purchase *is* believed.

### Hidden Items Factor

The Gemara adds further limiting criteria. Even when the *baal habayit* does not normally sell his items — and thus the baseline is that the taker is not believed to claim purchase — that disbelief applies only to *devarim she'ein darkan lehatmin* — items that are not normally concealed. The logic is: if there is no normal reason to hide something, then the act of hiding it under one's garment is itself suspicious and undermines the purchase claim. Why would you hide something you legitimately bought?

### Confession Exception

However, if the items are the type that *are* normally concealed — perhaps because they are private, or carrying them openly would be embarrassing — then the fact of concealment is not incriminating, and the taker may well be believed to say he purchased them. And even in a case where hiding is suspicious and the homeowner does not sell — *ela inish delo tzniei* — this only applies to someone who is not a generally private, modest type of person. If the person habitually keeps things tucked away and does not carry items openly as a matter of personal modesty and discretion, then hiding something under his garment is simply his normal behavior and cannot be used as evidence against him.

The full picture thus requires a combination of factors to disbelieve the purchase claim: the *baal habayit* does not normally sell his items, the items are not the type normally hidden, and the person is not someone who normally conceals things.

Yet there is an exception in the opposite direction as well. If the *baal habayit* himself claims the items were *stolen* — not merely borrowed — then even with all the above factors present, we are more prepared to believe the taker's claim that he purchased them. The principle is *achzukei inish bignivei lo machzikinnan* — we do not readily presume a person to be a thief. The disrepute of the theft accusation is so serious that we lean toward the less incriminating explanation of an unlikely purchase.

### Lent/Rented Items

The Gemara now introduces another critical case: *ela bidvarim ha'asuyin lehash'il ulehaskir* — the rules discussed above about believing or not believing the taker's purchase claim apply specifically to things that are normally lent out or rented. The Gemara states: if the *baal habayit* says "I lent those to you" or "I rented them to you," and the person in possession says "I bought them" — *eino ne'eman*, the buyer's claim is not believed.

The underlying principle is striking: for items that are ordinarily lent or rented, the general *chazakah* that "what is in a person's possession is his" does not operate. Since such items routinely pass between people as loans or rentals, the *original* owner retains presumptive standing even after the item leaves his hands. The person in possession cannot simply invoke *chazaka* against a known original owner who says the item was lent.

Conversely, for items that are *not* ordinarily lent or rented, the person in possession *is* believed to say they were purchased — the standard *chazakah* of possession reasserts itself when borrowing is not a plausible alternative explanation.

### Rava's Scissors Case

Rav Huna bar Avin then sends a ruling: for things normally lent or rented, if the person in possession claims they are purchased, *eino ne'eman* — not believed. The Gemara illustrates this with an incident involving Rava himself: *afik zuga de'sarsa vesafra de'agadata miyatmi* — Rava extracted a pair of scissors used in tailoring and a book of *agadata* from orphans. Someone claimed he had lent these items to the orphans' father, and it was known that the original ownership had been his. Rava accepted the claim and returned the items, even though the orphans were in possession of them. The implication is clear: the father — and now the orphans — could not claim purchase against the established original owner when the items were the type normally lent or rented.

This last point clarifies the *Tosafot*'s reading of the entire sugya. The earlier discussion, which required multiple converging factors to disbelieve a purchase claim, was dealing with items *not* normally lent or rented. There, in the absence of the lending alternative, you need the full combination of factors — the *baal habayit* not normally selling, the hiding being unexplained, etc. — to overcome the *chazakah* of possession. But for items *that are* normally lent or rented, none of those additional factors are needed. The category itself is sufficient to override *chazakah*: the original owner is simply believed to say it was lent, and the possessor's purchase claim fails. This reading integrates the Rava incident seamlessly — the scissors and *agadata* book belong to the category of normally-lent items, and no further factors were required for Rava to return them.

The Rashi on this passage adds a pointed observation about the *agadata* book: *sifrei de'agadata she'ein adam asuy lilmod bahem tadir* — books of *agadata* are the type people do not regularly study, so they lend them out. But other books — serious tractates — *einam asuyim lehashil lehaskir mishum d'mitkalkelim* — are not normally lent because one fears that the borrower will damage them through intensive use. This is, perhaps, an ironic observation given how many people find themselves drawn precisely to *aggadic* material.

### Who Can Swear

Returning to the mishna's framework, Rava rules: *afilu shomer nishba, afilu ishto shel shomer nishbat* — even the guard of the house can take the oath on behalf of the *nigzal*, and even the guard's wife can take the oath. Anyone who was present in the household and would have observed what was taken is empowered to swear and thereby enable recovery. Rav Pappa then raises a further question: *sachir velakit mai* — what about the *sachir* or a seasonal laborer who happens to be around the house — can they take the oath? The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

### Reasonableness Test

Finally, Rav Yeimar asks Rav Ashi: *ta'an bekasa de'chaspa mai* — what if the *nigzal* takes the oath and claims that a valuable silver goblet was taken? Once the *nigzal* is empowered to swear, is there any limit on what he can claim was stolen? The answer is: *chazina* — we assess the situation. *Ee inish amid hu* — if he is a wealthy person, or *inish demeheman hu demafkid inish gabei* — if he is the type of trustworthy person with whom others would deposit valuables, then *mishtava veshakil* — he may swear and collect. *Ve'ee lo lo* — but if there is no plausible reason why he would have a silver goblet in his home, we do not allow him to make that claim under oath. The *takana derabbanan* empowering the *nigzal* to swear operates within the bounds of reasonable credibility; it does not grant a blank check to claim whatever one wishes.
