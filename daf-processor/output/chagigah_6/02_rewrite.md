# Chagigah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Olat Re'iyah vs. Shalmei…

### Minimum Comparison

The daf opens by reviewing the debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* concerning which of two obligatory festival offerings carries the higher minimum: the *olat re'iyah* or the *shalmei chagigah*. Both offerings are required on *yom tov*, and the positions are mirror images of one another: *Beit Shammai* holds that the *olat re'iyah* carries a higher minimum of two silver coins, while the *chagigah* requires only one *ma'ah*; *Beit Hillel* reverses this. Together, both schools require a total outlay of three silver coins — only the allocation between the two *korbanot* differs.

### Divine vs. Human Benefit

What is striking is that underlying this technical dispute lies a deeper philosophical orientation that characterizes many of the debates between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* — not merely a question of leniency versus stringency, but a question of emphasis. *Beit Shammai* directs more expenditure toward the *olah*, which is given entirely to God, while *Beit Hillel* directs more toward the *shlamim*, which is shared between God and the worshipper. The degree to which the human being factors into religious experience appears to be at the core of this disagreement.

### Shlamim Before Sinai

One of the proofs marshaled in support of *Beit Hillel* is drawn from the closing verses of *Mishpatim*, which *Chazal* understand to have taken place before *matan Torah*. There it says: *vayizbachu olot vayizbachu zvachim shlamim l'Hashem parim* — they brought *olot* and they brought *shlamim* to God, bulls. *Beit Hillel* argues from this verse that *shlamim* existed as a form of personal offering before *matan Torah*, whereas the *olah* present at that moment did not represent a personal offering in the same sense. This gives precedential weight to the *shalmei chagigah*.

### Both Existed Before Sinai

*Beit Shammai* counters that the verse in fact mentions both an *olah* and a *shlamim*, and that both were private offerings — so no special weight can be granted to the *shlamim* on the basis of this precedent alone.

### Type of Olah Before Sinai

The *gemara* then clarifies what precisely is being disputed: the question is whether those *olot* mentioned at the foot of Har Sinai were an *olat re'iyah* — a personal, volitional offering — or an *olat tamid*, the regular communal burnt offering. If the *olah* brought was merely the *olat tamid*, then it carries no weight as a model for a personal *korban*, and *Beit Hillel*'s argument stands: the only personal precedent we have is the *shlamim*. *Beit Shammai*, by contrast, maintains that those *olot* were indeed personal offerings — an *olat re'iyah* — and therefore the personal *olah* also has a pre-Sinaitic precedent, undermining *Beit Hillel*'s reasoning.

It is worth noting how strange the argument structure is in either direction. Generally, what occurred before *matan Torah* is considered legally irrelevant. Yet here, both sides treat the pre-Sinaitic sacrifice as an authoritative model. The argument implicitly restricts its view to the era of *B'nei Yisrael* and yet grants special weight to what occurred before the giving of the Torah — a genuinely unusual move.

## Three vs. Three Dispute

### Olat Re'iyah Position

*Abaye* now broadens the discussion considerably. He observes that the dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* about the nature of that pre-Sinaitic *olah* is not isolated — it aligns with positions held by other *tanna'im* on related questions. *Beit Shammai*, Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Yishmael all share the view that the *olah* brought by *Yisrael* in the wilderness was an *olat re'iyah* — a personal offering.

### Olat Tamid Position

On the other side, *Beit Hillel*, Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Yose HaGlili all hold that the *olah* brought was an *olat tamid* — the communal offering — and not a personal one.

### Rabbi Yishmael on Torah…

The *baraita* cited to establish Rabbi Yishmael's position concerns his famous theory of how the Torah was transmitted. Rabbi Yishmael holds: *klalot ne'emru b'Sinai, u'pratot b'Ohel Moed* — the general principles were stated at Har Sinai, but the specific details were only spelled out later, in the *Ohel Moed*, during the ongoing revelation described throughout *Vayikra* and the subsequent books. This continued revelation — *v'no'aditi lecha sham*, I will meet you there, *v'dibarti itcha me'al hakapores* — is what filled in the legal particulars after Sinai.

### Rabbi Akiva on Torah Tra…

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *klalot u'pratot ne'emru b'Sinai* — both the general principles and the specific details were given at Har Sinai; they were then repeated in the *Ohel Moed*, and repeated a third time in *Arvot Moav*. The repetitions were pedagogical, not revelatory — the content was already fully given at Sinai.

### Hermeneutic Philosophies

This debate inverts what one might expect from these two *tanna'im* in their broader hermeneutic orientations. Rabbi Akiva is generally associated with the method of deriving detailed laws from the smallest textual indicators — every extra letter, every tittle — while Rabbi Yishmael tends toward *pshat* and cautions against over-interpretation, holding that *dibra Torah kilshon bnei adam*. Yet here, it is Rabbi Akiva who says all details were given at Sinai, and Rabbi Yishmael who says they were only given as they appear explicitly in the text, later.

One way to resolve this apparent inversion is to read Rabbi Akiva's position as consistent with his broader hermeneutics: for Rabbi Akiva, "giving the details at Sinai" does not mean spelling them out explicitly, but encoding them in the text through all those minute hints and extra letters — which the *tanna'im* are then licensed to derive. The giving of the details *is* the giving of the text, and the human interpreter then draws out the particulars. For Rabbi Yishmael, by contrast, "giving the details" means actually stating them — and that did not happen until the explicit legislation of the *Ohel Moed*. This reading aligns elegantly with each authority's general interpretive stance.

## Transmission Theory Appl…

### The Logical Problem

The alignment becomes significant when applied to the practical question: if the *olah* brought at Har Sinai was an *olat tamid*, and if the details of how to bring an *olah* were only given when they appear explicitly in the Torah — as Rabbi Yishmael holds — then the *olat tamid* was brought at Sinai without adherence to the full procedural requirements. The *gemara* asks: *mi ka midi d'me'ikara lo b'hefshet v'nituach u'levasof baei hefshet v'nituach* — can there be something that was originally performed without flaying and dismembering, and only later was it required to be done that way? The flaying and dismembering of the *olah* are cardinal elements of how an *olah* is brought, and it is inconceivable that the *olat tamid* was brought without them initially and then subsequently modified.

This presents a problem specifically for Rabbi Yishmael: if he also held that the *olah* was an *olat tamid*, he would have to concede that it was brought without its essential procedural details, which seems untenable. This is why the *gemara* initially places Rabbi Yishmael in the camp of *Beit Shammai* — by saying the *olah* was an *olat re'iyah*, a personal offering, he avoids the problem of the *olat tamid* being brought without its full specifications.

### Tosafot's Solution

*Tosafot* sharpens the logic here. They note that the procedural requirement of flaying and dismembering (*hefshet v'nituach*) is not a general rule stated abstractly about all *olot*, but is spelled out specifically in the context of the *olat tamid* itself. It is therefore particularly inconceivable that the *olat tamid* — the very *korban* about which the Torah later explicitly specifies these steps — could have been brought earlier without following them. This would not be as troubling for a private *olah* such as the *olat re'iyah*, since although all *olot* technically require *hefshet v'nituach*, the explicit textual connection is to the *tamid*. The *gemara* finds this reasoning persuasive.

### Rabbi Elazar's Distincti…

A *baraita* is now cited concerning Rabbi Elazar. It invokes the phrase *olat tamid ha'asuyah b'Har Sinai* — "the *olat tamid* performed at Har Sinai" — a phrase that occurs in the liturgy and might seem on its face to be excellent evidence that a *tamid* was indeed offered at the foot of Sinai. Yet Rabbi Elazar reads this in a striking way: *ma'aseha ne'emru b'Sinai v'atzma lo karvah* — the procedure of the *olah* was commanded at Sinai, but the offering itself was not brought there. The phrase *asuyah b'Har Sinai* refers to the commandment being given at Sinai, not to the sacrifice being performed there. For Rabbi Elazar, then, there was no actual *olat tamid* at Sinai — only the command — and therefore the *olah* that was brought, alongside the *shlamim* mentioned in *Mishpatim*, was a personal offering, an *olat re'iyah*.

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

Rabbi Akiva takes the opposite view: *karvah v'shuv lo paskah* — it was offered at Har Sinai and continued without interruption thereafter. For Rabbi Akiva, both the commandment and the act of bringing the *olat tamid* occurred at Sinai.

### Forty-Year Question

This raises the question of the verse in *Amos* that reads rhetorically: did you bring *zevachim u'minchah* to Me for forty years in the wilderness, *Beit Yisrael*? The implication of the rhetorical question is that they did not. But if the *tamid* was brought starting at Sinai, how can the verse suggest otherwise? The *gemara* resolves this by saying the verse refers to the nation as a whole: only the *shevet Levi*, who had not served idolatry, brought these offerings; the rest of *Yisrael* were disqualified. The forty-year question therefore does not undermine Rabbi Akiva's position.

## Rabbi Yose HaGlili's Evi…

### Three Regalim Mitzvot

The *baraita* of Rabbi Yose HaGlili articulates three distinct *mitzvot* incumbent upon *Yisrael* when ascending for *aliyah la'regel*: *re'iyah*, appearing before God in the *mikdash*; *chagigah*, celebrating through the *korban chagigah*; and *simchah*, rejoicing through the consumption of the *korban simchah*. Each of these three *mitzvot* possesses a distinctive characteristic not shared by the other two. The *re'iyah* is unique in that its offering is an *olah* — given entirely to God — while the other two are *shlamim*. The *simchah* is unique in that it applies to both men and women, unlike the other two.

### Chagigah Pre-Sinai

What the *chagigah* alone possesses — what neither *re'iyah* nor *simchah* can claim — is that *chagigah yeshnam lifnei ha'dibbur*: the *chagigah* existed before *matan Torah*. The *re'iyah* and the *simchah* did not. This is the same logical move as *Beit Hillel*'s argument: only the *shalmei chagigah* has a pre-Sinaitic precedent, which is why it is accorded special weight.

### Logical Inference

The implicit conclusion from Rabbi Yose HaGlili's *baraita* is that if only the *chagigah* — a *shlamim* — existed before the *dibbur*, then the *olah* mentioned alongside the *shlamim* in *Mishpatim* was not a personal *olat re'iyah* but rather the communal *olat tamid*. Rabbi Yose HaGlili thus aligns with *Beit Hillel*'s reading without stating it explicitly. One might wonder why the *shlamim* at the foot of Sinai should be identified specifically as a *shalmei chagigah* rather than a *shalmei simchah* — the text gives no clear indication — but the *gemara* proceeds on this understanding without offering a resolution.

## Rabbi Yishmael Realigned

### Challenge to Yishmael

With the three-against-three alignment established, the *gemara* now scrutinizes the placement of Rabbi Yishmael among the *Beit Shammai* camp. The argument was that because Rabbi Yishmael holds the details were only given as they appear explicitly in the text, he could not have maintained that the *olat tamid* was brought before those details were given. The problem with bringing the *tamid* without *hefshet v'nituach* seemed to force him to conclude that no *olat tamid* was brought at Sinai, and hence the *olah* must have been a personal *olat re'iyah*.

### Precedent from Yose HaGl…

The *gemara* challenges this inference. Rabbi Yose HaGlili — who explicitly holds that the *olah* brought in the wilderness was the *olat tamid* — also holds, in a separate *baraita*, that the *olah* they brought in the wilderness did not require *hefshet v'nituach*: *lefi she'ein hefshet v'nituach ela me'Ohel Moed v'eilach* — those procedural requirements only became operative from the *Ohel Moed* onward. Here is explicit evidence that one can consistently hold both that the *olah* was an *olat tamid* and that it was brought without the later-specified procedural requirements. The manner in which the *tamid* was brought evolved over time as its details were revealed. This is entirely compatible with Rabbi Yishmael's theory of Torah transmission.

### Gemara's Conclusion

Once the *gemara* establishes this precedent from Rabbi Yose HaGlili's *baraita*, it concludes that the concern which seemed to force Rabbi Yishmael into the *Beit Shammai* camp is no longer compelling. Rabbi Yishmael's theory — that details are given as they appear — permits the *olat tamid* to have been brought differently before those details were given. He can therefore hold, like *Beit Hillel*, that the *olah* at Sinai was an *olat tamid*, not a personal *olat re'iyah*. The *gemara* removes Rabbi Yishmael from the *Beit Shammai* column and reassigns him to *Beit Hillel*.

The broader debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva about how the Torah was transmitted — whether details were implicit at Sinai or only given explicitly later — emerges here as something more than an abstract theory. It has direct bearing on how we understand the history of the *korbanot* and on the legal weight we give to pre-Sinaitic precedents. Viewed against the backdrop of the *Beit Shammai*–*Beit Hillel* dispute, there is a certain power to *Beit Shammai*'s reading: identifying the *olah* at the foot of Sinai as an *olat re'iyah* brings a vivid, experiential imagery to what *re'iyah* means — appearing before God in a moment of direct encounter, as captured in the verse *vayichezu et ha'Elohim*. According to *Beit Hillel*, by contrast, what was enacted at Sinai was the *olat tamid* — the steady, daily service — which suggests a different kind of religious weight: not the intense singular encounter but the continuity and regularity of daily devotion.

## Parsing the Verse

### Textual Punctuation

Rav Chisda raises a question about how to read the verse at the end of *Mishpatim* that describes the sacrifices at the foot of Sinai: *vayishlach et na'arei b'nei Yisrael vaya'alu olot vayizbechu zvachim shlamim l'Hashem parim*. The question is whether there is a syntactic break — effectively a period — after the word *olot*, such that the word *parim* (bulls) applies only to the *shlamim* that follow, with the *olah* remaining unspecified as to species. Alternatively, perhaps *parim* applies to the entire verse, and both the *olot* and the *shlamim* were bulls.

### Tropp Analysis

Mar Zutra answers that the resolution depends on the *trop* — the cantillation marks. Whether an *etnachtah* or a lesser disjunctive accent appears after *olot* determines whether the word *parim* falls back over the entire verse or only on the latter clause.

### Vow Formula Test

Rav Acha the son of Rava proposes a practical *nafka mina*: if someone declares a vow to bring an *olah* "like the *olah* brought in the wilderness," would that vow be fulfilled with a bull (*par*) or a sheep (*keves*)? The answer depends on what species the wilderness *olah* actually was. If *parim* covers the *olot* as well, the vow requires a bull; if the *olah* is left unspecified, it might be a sheep.

### Unresolved Answer

The *gemara* concludes with *teiku* — the question remains unresolved. Even granting the reading that the *olah* and *shlamim* are syntactically separated, the species of the *olah* cannot be determined from the verse alone.

## Re'iyah: No Biblical Lim…

### Mishnaic Teaching

The *gemara* now turns to a new dimension of the *korban re'iyah* — the question of whether it has a *shiur*, a prescribed limit. The *mishnah* in the first chapter of *Peah*, recited daily in *davening*, lists certain *mitzvot* that have no *shiur*: *ha'peah, ha'bikkurim, v'hara'ayon, u'gemilut chasadim* — *peah*, *bikkurim*, *ra'ayon* (i.e., *re'iyah*), and acts of loving-kindness. The unusual term *ra'ayon* rather than *re'iyah* will be addressed shortly.

### Initial Interpretation

Rabbi Yochanan's initial reading of this *mishnah* is that *ein lo shiur* means there is no upper limit — one may spend as much as one wishes on these *mitzvot* — but there is a lower limit, a minimum below which one cannot go. Applied to *re'iyah*, one can bring as lavish a *korban* as one desires, but there is a floor.

### Torah vs. Rabbinic Limits

Rabbi Oshaia then teaches a more expansive position: *hara'ayon ein lo shiur lo lema'alah v'lo lemata* — neither an upper limit nor a lower limit. At the biblical level, one may spend as little or as much as one wishes on the *re'iyah*. The apparent lower limits familiar from the *mishnah* — the *ma'ah kesef* for *re'iyah* and the *shtei kesef* for *chagigah* — are not of biblical origin. Rather, *aval Chachamim omrim*, the Sages established a minimum. Rabbi Oshaia's teaching thus reframes the *mishnah*: what it denies is any biblical *shiur*; the rabbinic minimum is a separate layer. This reading presumably applies in parallel fashion to *peah* and *bikkurim* as well — the biblical obligation has no minimum, while the Sages prescribed a floor.

## Defining Re'iyah

### Re'ayon Meaning

The *mishnah*'s unusual term *ra'ayon* prompts a dispute. What precisely does the obligation of *re'iyah* require?

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

Rabbi Yochanan explains: *re'iyat panim ba'azarah* — the *mitzvah* is fulfilled by appearing in the *azarah*, the Temple courtyard. On this reading, the appearance itself is the essence of the *mitzvah*, and bringing a *korban* is not required each and every time one enters the *azarah*.

### Reish Lakish's View

Reish Lakish disagrees: *re'iyat panim be'korban* — appearing before God means appearing with a *korban*. The verse *velo yeira'u panai reikam* — do not appear before Me empty-handed — generates a requirement that every act of appearing be accompanied by an offering. On Reish Lakish's reading, appearing without a *korban* is itself a violation of *lo yeira'u panai reikam*.

## Dispute Limited to Other…

### First Day Consensus

The *gemara* now clarifies the precise scope of this dispute. On the first day of *yom tov* — the *ikar ha'regel* — everyone agrees that *re'iyat panim be'korban* applies: whenever one appears in the *azarah*, one must bring a *korban*. On that day, multiple appearances each require their own offering.

### Chol HaMoed Question

The actual dispute concerns the other days of *yom tov* — *chol ha'mo'ed*. Is it permissible on those days to enter the *azarah* without bringing a *korban*?

### Yochanan on Other Days

Rabbi Yochanan holds that on the other days of *yom tov* there is no obligation to bring a *korban* each time one appears. One may enter the *azarah* freely, without a gift, and this does not violate *lo yeira'u panai reikam*.

### Lakish on Other Days

Reish Lakish holds that *every* appearance, on any day of *yom tov*, requires a *korban*. The obligation of *lo yeira'u panai reikam* is not confined to the first day; it attaches to every act of appearing before God throughout the festival.

This dispute is significant for the broader question of how *re'iyah* is conceptualized. The *mitzvah* has long been understood as consisting of two intertwined components: appearing in the *mikdash* and bringing a *korban*. The discussion here probes what happens when the two are separated — specifically, whether appearance without a *korban* is legally meaningful or constitutes a deficient fulfillment. Conversely, one might ask whether sending a *korban* without personally appearing would constitute anything at all. These two components are deeply bound together, and the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish maps onto the question of which component is primary.

---

## Reish Lakish's Proofs

### Cannot Come Empty-Handed

Reish Lakish challenges Rabbi Yochanan directly: *velo yeira'u panai reikam* — how can you permit someone to appear before God on *chol ha'mo'ed* without bringing a *korban*? The plain meaning of the verse prohibits precisely this.

### Parallel Derivation

Reish Lakish then constructs a logical derivation — a *dinah v'omeir* — from the parallel structure of the festival obligations. The *chagigah* is brought for the person; the *re'iyah* is brought before God. The verse references *zevachim* in relation to both. Just as the *chagigah* offering must be a *zevach*, so too must the *re'iyah* offering be a *zevach* — a proper animal sacrifice, not merely a bird or grain offering.

### Mammal Requirement

How do we know that the *chagigah* must be a mammal? From the verse *velo yalin chelev chagig ad boker* — the fat of the *chagigah* offering shall not remain until morning. Since the *chagigah* has fat, it cannot be a bird; it must be a sheep or a cow. By the same derivation, the *re'iyah* offering must also be a proper mammal.

### Chagigah vs. Olah Type

The derivation continues: the *chagigah* is a *shlamim* — the one who brings it eats from it and benefits. The *re'iyah* is offered before God. By parallel logic — *ma chagigah ha'amurah l'hedyot ra'uy lo, af re'iyah ha'amurah la'gavoah ra'uy lo* — the *re'iyah* must be something entirely for God, which means it must be an *olah*, not a *shlamim*.

### Anthropomorphic Reasoning

The *gemara* invokes an explicitly anthropomorphic justification: *shelo yehei shulchancha malei v'shulchan rabacha reikan* — your table should not be full while the table of your Master is empty. You consume the *chagigah*; God should receive the *olah* in its entirety. Whatever its figurative character, the reasoning is clear: the *re'iyah* offering must be an *olah*, and it must accompany every appearance.

---

## Reish Lakish Limited

### Chol HaMoed Challenge

Rabbi Yochanan presses back: if Reish Lakish holds that *velo yeira'u panai reikam* applies to every appearance, then even on *chol ha'mo'ed* one could never enter the *azarah* without a *korban*. Is that really his position?

### First Day Limitation

Reish Lakish responds that the prohibition of *lo yeira'u panai reikam* applies specifically on the *ikar ha'regel* — the first day of *yom tov*. That is the primary moment of obligatory appearing; it is then that the prohibition against appearing empty-handed is in full force. On the subsequent days of the festival the obligation does not extend in the same way. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish thus resolves into a narrower question than it first appeared: both agree that on the first day every appearance requires a *korban*, and both agree that one may *voluntarily* bring additional *korbanot* on subsequent days. The live question is whether appearing on *chol ha'mo'ed* without a *korban* constitutes a violation — and here Reish Lakish, having anchored the prohibition to the *ikar ha'regel*, concedes that it does not.
