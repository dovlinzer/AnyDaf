# Sotah 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Sotah Tests

### Torah vs. Zot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַקְּנָאֹת״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The verse states: **"This is the law of jealousy"** (Numbers 5:29), indicating that the same law is to be carried out in all cases of jealousy. This **teaches that the woman drinks and repeats,** i.e., she must drink a second time if she becomes a *sota* again.

The daf opens by revisiting a *braita* that was quoted at the close of 18b. The *braita* begins with the phrase *zot torat ha-kena'ot* — "this is the *torah* of jealousies" — and the interpretive tension is immediately apparent: the word *torah* is an expansive, inclusive term, while *zot* is a narrowing, limiting one. These two words pull in opposite directions, and it is precisely this tension that generates the three-way dispute among the *tanna'im*.

The *tanna kamma* fastens onto the word *torah* to allow for multiple occurrences of the *sotah* procedure. That is, a woman who has been suspected, warned before witnesses, and made to drink the bitter waters could, if later suspected again under fresh circumstances, be subjected to the entire process a second time. *Torah* signals multiplicity.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״זֹאת״, שֶׁאֵין הָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה וְהֵעִיד לְפָנֵינוּ נְחוּנְיָא חוֹפֵר שִׁיחִין שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה, וְקִיבַּלְנוּ עֵדוּתוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים, אֲבָל לֹא בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The word **"this"** in the verse is a restricting term, indicating **that the woman does not drink and repeat. Rabbi Yehuda said:** There was **an incident** in which **Neḥunya the ditch digger testified before us** in the name of his teachers **that the woman drinks and repeats, and we accepted his testimony with regard to two men, but not with regard to one man.** Even if she drinks the water of a *sota* while married to her first husband, she must drink again after violating a warning by her second husband. However, one husband cannot have his wife drink twice.

*Rabbi Yehuda* counters by emphasizing the exclusive force of *zot*: *she-en isha shota ve-shona* — a woman cannot be made to undergo the procedure twice. There is no double jeopardy. He does, however, qualify this position based on testimony. The *braita* records that *Nechunya Chofre Shichin* — Nechunya the ditch-digger — testified before the sages that a woman could in fact be made to drink a second time, and this testimony was accepted, but only in a limited context: *bi-shne anashim* — if it involves two different husbands. *Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning is that when the same husband suspects his wife a second time, the problem may lie more with the husband than with the woman. But if an entirely different husband brings the accusation, that represents a genuinely different set of circumstances, and the procedure may be repeated.

### Chakhamim Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה, בֵּין בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד בֵּין בִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: **And the Rabbis say: The woman does not drink and repeat, whether with regard to one man or with regard to two men.**

The *chakhamim* — identified in the *braita* as *Rabbanan Batrai*, the later sages — take a more sweeping position: *en isha shota ve-shona ben be-ish echad ben bi-shne anashim* — a woman can never be made to drink again, whether the suspicion comes from the same husband or from two different ones. This represents a rejection even of the testimony of *Nechunya*, and applies across the board.

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״זֹאת״! וְרַבָּנַן בָּתְרָאֵי נָמֵי, הָא כְתִיב ״תּוֹרַת״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **the first *tanna*** of the *baraita* **as well, isn't it written** in the verse: **"This,"** restricting the number of times a woman must drink? **And** according to **the Rabbis** mentioned **later** in the *baraita* **as well, isn't it written: "The law of** jealousy," amplifying the number of times a woman must drink to include all cases of jealousy?

The *gemara* is not content to leave the dispute as a simple three-way disagreement, because the positions must account for both textual signals — the inclusive *torat* and the exclusive *zot*. The *gemara* therefore presses: does the *tanna kamma*, who emphasizes *torat*, have no use for *zot*? And conversely, does the *Rabbanan Batrai*, who effectively reads the law as prohibiting a second procedure in virtually every case, have no use for *torat*? These questions prompt *Rava* to offer a comprehensive analytical framework.

## Rava Framework

### Identical Circumstances

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד וּבוֹעֵל אֶחָד — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּאֵין הָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה,
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Different *halakhot* apply to different cases: **With regard to one husband** who accused his wife twice about **one paramour, everyone agrees that the woman does not drink and repeat,** having been proven innocent once,

*Rava* maps out the full logical space of possibilities. At one extreme: *be-ish echad u-vo'el echad* — the same husband, and the same individual suspected as the adulterer. Here everything is perfectly identical. The husband was unsatisfied with the first result and wishes to repeat the process against the same person. This is pure double jeopardy in the strictest sense, and *de-khule alma lo pligi* — all opinions agree that this is prohibited. The word *zot* performs its exclusionary function precisely here: this case of absolute identity is excluded by all.

### All Different

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״זֹאת״. בִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים וּשְׁנֵי בּוֹעֲלִין — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּהָאִשָּׁה שׁוֹתָה וְשׁוֹנָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״תּוֹרַת״.
>
> **Translation:** **as it is written: "This** is the law of jealousy." The word "this" is a restricting term and excludes that possibility. **With regard to two** different **husbands and two** different **paramours,** where her first husband suspected her with regard to one paramour during her first marriage and the second husband suspected her with regard to a different man during the second marriage, **everyone agrees that the woman drinks and repeats, as it is written:** "This is **the law of** jealousy," in all cases of jealousy.

At the other extreme: *bi-shne anashim u-shne bo'alim* — two different husbands, and a different suspected adulterer in each instance. Here every relevant circumstance has changed. The woman has never been tested with respect to this husband or this suspected paramour; nothing about the prior proceeding is being relitigated. Here too all opinions agree — but in the opposite direction: *de-isha shota ve-shona* — she may undergo the procedure again. The word *torat* achieves its inclusive purpose precisely in this case.

### Middle Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד וּשְׁנֵי בּוֹעֲלִין, בִּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים וּבוֹעֵל אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree when** there is **one husband and two paramours,** i.e., where one husband warned her with regard to a second paramour after she survived her first ordeal. They also disagree **in** a case of **two husbands and one paramour,** i.e., if her second husband accused her with regard to the same paramour on account of whom she was compelled to drink by her first husband.

The dispute among the three *tanna'im* therefore concerns only the two intermediate cases: one husband who suspects his wife of having conducted herself inappropriately with two different men (*ish echad u-shne bo'alim*), and two different husbands who each suspect their wife of involvement with the same individual (*shne anashim u-vo'el echad*).

### Tanna Kamma Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: ״תּוֹרַת״ — לְרַבּוֹיֵי כּוּלְּהִי, ״זֹאת״ — לְמַעוֹטֵי אִישׁ אֶחָד וּבוֹעֵל אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The opinions are justified as follows: **The first *tanna* holds** that the phrase **"the law of** jealousy" serves **to include all of these** cases. In almost all cases the woman drinks and repeats. The word **"this"** serves **to exclude** only the case of **one husband and one paramour,** in which she does not drink and repeat.

The *tanna kamma* adopts the most permissive position. He reads *torat* as broadly inclusive — *lerabbuye kulhu* — encompassing virtually all cases. The word *zot* serves only to exclude the one scenario on which everyone agrees: identical husband, identical suspected adulterer. Everything else — both middle cases included — is permitted. The *tanna kamma* is the maximalist here.

### Rabbanan Batrai Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן בָּתְרָאֵי סָבְרִי: ״זֹאת״ — לְמַעוֹטֵי כּוּלְּהִי, ״תּוֹרַת״ — לְרַבּוֹיֵי שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים וּשְׁנֵי בּוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** mentioned **later** in the *baraita* **hold** that the word **"this"** serves **to exclude all of these** cases. The woman almost never drinks and repeats. The phrase **"the law of** jealousy" serves **to include** only the case of **two husbands and two paramours,** in which she does drink and repeat.

The *Rabbanan Batrai* adopt the inverse approach. *Zot limute kulhu* — the exclusive term excludes virtually everything — both middle cases are prohibited. The word *torat* serves only to include the one scenario on which all agree: different husband, different suspected adulterer. Everything short of total difference in circumstances is excluded. The *Rabbanan Batrai* are the minimalists.

### Rabbi Yehuda Balance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: ״זֹאת״ — לְמַעוֹטֵי תַּרְתֵּי, ״תּוֹרַת״ — לְרַבּוֹת תַּרְתֵּי. ״זֹאת״ לְמַעוֹטֵי תַּרְתֵּי — אִישׁ אֶחָד וּבוֹעֵל אֶחָד, אִישׁ אֶחָד וּשְׁנֵי בּוֹעֲלִין, ״תּוֹרַת״ — לְרַבּוֹיֵי תַּרְתֵּי, שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים וּבוֹעֵל אֶחָד, שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים וּשְׁנֵי בּוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda** holds: The word **"this"** serves **to exclude two** of the cases, and the phrase **"the law of** jealousy" serves **to include two.** The word **"this"** serves **to exclude** the **two** cases of **one husband and one paramour** and **one husband and two paramours.** In neither of these cases does the woman drink and repeat. The phrase **"the law of** jealousy" serves **to include two** cases, i.e., **two husbands and one paramour,** and all the more so **two husbands and two paramours.** In both of these cases, the woman must drink and repeat.

*Rabbi Yehuda* stakes out the middle position with elegant symmetry: *zot limutei tartei, torah lirboyei tartei* — *zot* excludes two cases, and *torat* includes two cases. The two excluded cases share a single variable: the same husband. Whether that husband is suspicious of the same man or a different man, if it is the same husband a second time, the procedure cannot be repeated. The two included cases share the opposite variable: different husbands. Whether those two husbands suspect her of involvement with the same man or different men, the fact of a different husband constitutes a genuinely different set of circumstances, and the procedure may be repeated. This makes intuitive sense as well: since the *sotah* procedure is fundamentally about the wife's relationship with her husband, it follows that cases involving the same husband form a natural category of exclusion, while cases involving a different husband form a natural category of inclusion — even if the suspected adulterer happens to be the same person.

## Menachah Process

### Who Takes It

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה נוֹטֵל אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ מִתּוֹךְ כְּפִיפָה מִצְרִית, וְנוֹתְנָהּ לְתוֹךְ כְּלִי שָׁרֵת, וְנוֹתְנָהּ עַל יָדָהּ, וְכֹהֵן מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ מִתַּחְתֶּיהָ וּמְנִיפָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **He would take her meal-offering out of** the **Egyptian wicker basket** made of palm leaves in which it was lying **and would put it into a service vessel and** then **place it on her hand. And** the **priest** would then **place his hand underneath hers and wave it** together with her.

The third *perek* opens with the *mishnah* describing the ritual steps of the *sotah*'s *mincha* offering. The *mishnah* states that "he" would take the *mincha* from a wicker basket (*kefifah mitzrit*) and place it into a *kli shareis* — a sanctified temple vessel — and then place it upon her hand. The identity of the "he" is disputed. *Rashi* understands this to refer to the husband, reasoning that until the offering is placed into a *kli shareis* it has not yet entered the domain of the *kohen*'s responsibility. *Tosafot*, by contrast, argues that this must refer to the *kohen* himself, since the *pasuk* speaks of the *kohen* taking it from her hand.

### Sanctification

Once the *mincha* is placed into the *kli shareis*, it becomes sanctified. It is then returned to the woman's hands, so that she holds the now-sanctified offering. At this point, the *kohen* places his hands underneath hers — *maniach yado tachat yadah* — preparing for the waving.

### Five Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵנִיף וְהִגִּישׁ, קָמַץ וְהִקְטִיר, וְהַשְּׁאָר נֶאֱכָל לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The priest **waved** it **and brought** it **near** to the southwest corner of the altar, **removed a handful** from it, **and burned** the handful; **and the remainder was eaten by the priests.**

The *mishnah* enumerates the steps of the *mincha* procedure. First, the *kohen* waves the offering (*tenufah*), with his hands underneath and her hands on top, as a single joint act. Second, he brings it close to the *mizbe'ach* (*hagashah*). Third, he takes the *kometz* — a handful of the offering. Fourth, he burns the *kometz* on the *mizbe'ach* (*haktarah*). Fifth, the remainder (*shirayim*) is eaten by the *kohanim* in the *Beis Hamikdash*. These five steps — *waving, touching the altar, taking the handful, burning, and the remainder being eaten* — constitute the full procedure for every *mincha*, and the *sotah*'s offering is no exception.

## Order Dispute

### Tanna Kamma Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מַשְׁקָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The priest **would force** the woman **to drink** the bitter water of a *sota*, **and afterward he would sacrifice her meal-offering.**

Having described the steps of the *mincha*, the *mishnah* now turns to the question of order: does the drinking precede the *mincha*, or does the *mincha* precede the drinking? The *tanna kamma* holds that first she drinks and only then is the *mincha* brought. The understanding implicit in this position is that once the *mincha* is subsequently brought, it triggers or activates the effect of the bitter waters that she has already consumed.

### Rabbi Shimon Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מַקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ הָיָה מַשְׁקָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַחַר יַשְׁקֶה אֶת הָאִשָּׁה אֶת הַמָּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** The priest would **sacrifice her meal-offering and afterward he would force her to drink, as it is stated:** "And the priest shall take a handful of the meal-offering, as the memorial part of it, and burn it upon the altar, **and afterward he shall make the woman drink the water"** (Numbers 5:26).

*Rabbi Shimon* reverses the order: *makriv et minchatah ve-achar kach mashkeh* — first the *mincha*, then the drinking. And *Rabbi Shimon* does not rest on inference alone; he cites an explicit *pasuk*: *ve-achar yashkeh et ha-ishah et ha-mayim* — "and afterwards he shall cause the woman to drink the waters." The word *ve-achar*, "afterwards," appears after the verses describing the *mincha* procedure, making the sequence clear — or so it would seem. The *pasuk* appears to place the drinking explicitly after the *mincha* offering.

### Rabbi Shimon Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הִשְׁקָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ — כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** But Rabbi Shimon concedes that **if** the priest first **forced her to drink and afterward sacrificed her meal-offering,** it is still **valid.**

Despite his clear preference, *Rabbi Shimon* concedes that if the order was reversed — if she drank first and the *mincha* was brought afterward — the *mincha* is nonetheless *kesherah* (*be-di'avad*, after the fact). The procedure is valid retroactively. This concession, as we will see, becomes important when the *gemara* tries to understand what *Rabbi Shimon* does with the earlier *pasuk* that seems to place the drinking first.

## Waving Process

### Owner Waving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה דְּדָרֵיהּ: לָא תֵּיתֵב אַכַּרְעָךְ עַד דִּמְפָרְשַׁתְּ לָהּ לְהָא מִילְּתָא: מִנַּיִן לְמִנְחַת סוֹטָה שֶׁטְּעוּנָה תְּנוּפָה? מְנָא לַן?! ״וְהֵנִיף״ כְּתִיב בַּהּ! בִּבְעָלִים, מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Elazar said to Rabbi Yoshiya of his generation,** i.e., his contemporary: **You shall not sit on your feet until you explain this matter** to me: **From where** is it derived that **the meal-offering of a *sota* requires waving?** The Gemara expresses surprise at the question: **From where do we** derive this? It is explicitly **written with regard to** the meal-offering of a *sota*: "And the priest shall take the meal-offering of jealousy out of the woman's hand, **and shall wave** the meal-offering before the Lord, and bring it unto the altar" (Numbers 5:25). Rather, the question is as follows: **From where do we** derive that the waving is performed **by the owner,** i.e., the woman, and not only by the priest?

Before turning to the textual analysis of the order dispute, the *gemara* records a separate exchange. *Rabbi Elazar* said to *Rabbi Yoshiya* of his generation — notably a colleague rather than an elder, which is why the pointed manner of the remark was appropriate — that he should not sit down until he could explain: *minayin le-minchat sotah she-te'unah tenufah* — whence do we know that the *sotah*'s *mincha* requires *tenufah*, waving? At first glance this question is surprising, since the *pasuk* explicitly states that the *kohen* shall wave it. *Rabbi Elazar* clarifies: that the *kohen* waves it is explicit. The question is, whence do we know that the *owner* — the woman herself — must also participate in the waving?

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָתְיָא ״יָד״ ״יָד״ מִשְּׁלָמִים. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן מִיַּד הָאִשָּׁה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״יָדָיו תְּבִיאֶינָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoshiya answered: This is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the term **"hand"** written here and **"hand" from** the **peace-offering:** It **is written here,** with regard to the meal-offering of a *sota*: **"And the priest shall take** the meal-offering of jealousy **out of the woman's hand"** (Numbers 5:25), **and** it **is written there,** with regard to the peace-offering: "He that offers his peace-offerings unto the Lord…**His own hands shall bring** the offerings…that the breast may be waved before the Lord" (Leviticus 7:29–30).

The answer is derived through a *gezeirah shavah* — a verbal analogy between two passages. By the *sotah*'s *mincha*, the verse states *ve-lakach ha-kohen mi-yad ha-isha* — the *kohen* shall take from the hand of the woman. By the *shelamim*, the peace-offering, the verse states *yadav tevi'enah* — his hands shall bring it — and this verse speaks about the owner's hands in the context of waving the breast and leg of the *shelamim*. The shared term "hand" links the two passages and allows us to apply the rules of each to the other.

### Joint Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה כָּאן כֹּהֵן — אַף לְהַלָּן כֹּהֵן, וּמָה לְהַלָּן בְּעָלִים — אַף כָּאן בְּעָלִים. הָא כֵּיצַד? מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ תַּחַת יְדֵי הַבְּעָלִים וּמֵנִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as here,** in the case of the *sota*, the **priest** waves the offering, **so too there,** in the case of the peace-offering, the **priest** waves the offering. **And just as there,** in the case of the peace-offering, the **owner** waves the offering, **so too here,** in the case of the *sota*, the **owner** waves the offering. **How** is **this** accomplished? The priest **places his hand beneath the hands of the owner and** then **waves** the offering with the owner.

The *gezeirah shavah* runs in both directions: just as by the *sotah* we have a *kohen* performing the waving, so too by the *shelamim*; and just as by the *shelamim* the owner participates in the waving, so too by the *sotah*. The practical upshot is that this is not accomplished in two separate acts. *Maniach yado tachat yad be'alim u-menif* — the *kohen* places his hands underneath the hands of the owner and they wave together as one. The owner is in direct contact with the offering because it flows from her and is connected to her; the *kohen*'s hands underneath represent the transition from personal ownership toward the *mizbe'ach*.

## Mishnah Structure

### Gemara Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵנִיף וְהִגִּישׁ קָמַץ וְכוּ׳. הָיָה מַשְׁקָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ. הָא אַקְרְבַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: The priest **waved** it **and brought** it **near** to the southwest corner of the altar, **removed a handful** from it, and burned the handful. Yet the continuation of the mishna states: The priest **would force** the woman **to drink, and afterward he would sacrifice her meal-offering.** The Gemara asks: **Didn't** the mishna state in the previous phrase that the offering **was** already **sacrificed?**

When the *mishnah* lists the steps — *henif, higish, kamatz, hiktir* — and then says "he would have her drink and then bring the *mincha*," a difficulty arises: the *mishnah* has already said *hiktir*, that he burned the *kometz*. How can it then say he would first have her drink and only then bring the *mincha*, if the *kometz* has already been described as burned?

### Mishnah Structure Continued

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: סֵדֶר מְנָחוֹת כֵּיצַד? הֵנִיף וְהִגִּישׁ, קָמַץ וְהִקְטִיר, וְהַשְּׁאָר נֶאֱכָל לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This is what** the mishna **is saying: What was the** sacrificial **order of meal-offerings** in general? The priest **waved** the meal-offering **and brought** it **near** to the southwest corner of the altar, **removed a handful** from it, **and burned** the handful, **and the remainder was eaten by the priests.**

The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between two separate discussions embedded in the *mishnah*. The first part of the *mishnah* — *henif ve-higish kamatz ve-hiktir, ve-ha-sha'ar ne'echal la-kohanim* — is answering a structural question: *seder mincha keitzad?* What is the procedure of the *mincha*? What are its steps? The *mishnah* is describing the internal sequence of the *mincha* offering itself, not yet addressing when it occurs relative to the drinking. Only after completing this description does the *mishnah* turn to the separate question of the relationship between the drinking and the *mincha*.

### Sequence Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהַשְׁקָאָה גּוּפַהּ פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן. דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מַשְׁקָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: מַקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַשְׁקָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַחַר יַשְׁקֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** as **for** the correct order for sacrificing the meal-offering of the *sota* and **forcing** her **to drink,** this **itself** is a matter about which **Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis disagree, as the Rabbis hold** that the priest would **force** the woman **to drink and afterward he** would **sacrifice her meal-offering; and Rabbi Shimon holds** that the priest would **sacrifice her meal-offering and afterward he** would **force her to drink, as** it **is stated:** "And the priest shall take a handful of the meal-offering, as the memorial part of it, and burn it upon the altar, **and afterward he shall make** the woman **drink** the water" (Numbers 5:26).

With that structural clarification established, the *gemara* proceeds to the substantive debate. *Rabbanan* hold: *mashkeh ve-achar kach makriv* — first she drinks, then the *mincha* is brought. *Rabbi Shimon* holds the opposite: *makriv et minchatah ve-achar kach mashkeh* — first the *mincha*, then she drinks — as the *pasuk* explicitly states with the word *ve-achar*. And *Rabbi Shimon* concedes *be-di'avad*: if she drank first and the *mincha* was brought afterward, it is *kesherah*. The challenge, then, is to explain how the *chakhamim* can argue against *Rabbi Shimon* given the apparent clarity of the *pasuk*. This requires a careful analysis of all three drinking references in the relevant verses.

## Three Pesukim Analysis

### First V'Hishkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? תְּלָתָא קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי: ״וְהִשְׁקָה״ קַמָּא, ״וְאַחַר יַשְׁקֶה״, ״וְהִשְׁקָהּ״ בָּתְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what do** the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon **disagree** in the mishna? The Gemara answers: **Three verses are written** which pertain to drinking the bitter water: **The first** occurrence of the term is in the verse: **"And he shall make** the woman **drink"** (Numbers 5:24); the second: **"And afterward he shall make** the woman **drink** the water" (Numbers 5:26); and **the last** occurrence of the term is in the verse: **"And he shall make her drink"** (Numbers 5:27).

The *gemara* identifies three distinct references to drinking in the passage in *Bamidbar* 5. The first — *ve-hishkah* — appears immediately after the verse describing the scraping of the *megillah* into the water (*ve-chatav et ha-alot ha-eleh ha-kohen ba-sefer u-macha el mei ha-marim*), and states that he shall cause the woman to drink the bitter, cursing waters. This occurs before any mention of the *mincha* procedure in the *pesukim*.

### V'Achar Yashkeh

The second reference — *ve-achar yashkeh et ha-ishah et ha-mayim* — appears explicitly after the *pesukim* describing the *kohen* taking the *mincha* from her hand, waving it, performing *hagashah*, taking the *kometz*, and burning it on the *mizbe'ach*. It then says "and afterwards he shall cause the woman to drink the waters." *Rabbi Shimon* reads this as the operative instruction for timing: the word *ve-achar*, "afterwards," places the drinking definitively after the *mincha*.

### Third V'Hishkah

The third reference — *ve-hishkah et ha-mayim* — appears yet again following the second reference, in the context of what happens once she has drunk. This repetition creates the exegetical problem: if the second *ve-hishkah* already addressed the drinking after the *mincha*, what work does the third do? And if the first *ve-hishkah* already addressed the drinking before the *mincha*, how does *Rabbi Shimon*, who privileges the second reference, account for the first? These three *pesukim* become the terrain on which *Rabbanan* and *Rabbi Shimon* stake out their respective positions.

## Rabbi Shimon Counter

### Three Prerequisites

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִשְׁקָה״ מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״וְהִשְׁקָהּ״! שֶׁאִם נִמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה וְאוֹמֶרֶת ״אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה״ — מְעַרְעֲרִין אוֹתָהּ וּמַשְׁקִין אוֹתָהּ בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: What** is the meaning when **the verse states** after the sacrifice of the meal-offering: **"And he shall make her drink** the water" (Numbers 5:27)? **But isn't it already stated: "And he shall make** the woman **drink** the water of bitterness that causes the curse" (Numbers 5:24)? The *baraita* answers: The repetition teaches **that if the scroll was** already **erased and** then the woman **says: I will not drink, she is forced [*me'arerin*] to drink against her will.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The *gemara* quotes a *braita* that draws on *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Akiva* jointly: *ve-achar yashkeh* — the word *ve-achar* teaches that the drinking comes *l'achar kol ha-ma'asim kulan ha-amurim le-ma'alah* — after everything enumerated above. And specifically, *maggid she-shloshah devarim me'akvin bah* — three things must precede the drinking for it to be valid: the *kometz* must have been offered, the *megillah* must have been fully erased, and the woman must have already accepted the *shvuah*.

## Prerequisites Analysis

### Kometz Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרַב הַקּוֹמֶץ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: מַקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַשְׁקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: She does not drink **until the handful is sacrificed. Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning** stated earlier, **as he says** that the priest **sacrifices her meal-offering and afterward forces her to drink.**

The first prerequisite — *ad she-lo karav ha-kometz* — is precisely the point at issue between *Rabbi Shimon* and the *rabbanan* in the *mishnah*. The *braita* records *Rabbi Shimon le-ta'ameih*: he is consistent with his own position — first the *mincha*, then the drinking, exactly as the *pasuk* indicates.

### Full Erasure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה. אֶלָּא מַאי מַשְׁקֶה לַהּ? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לֹא נִצְרְכָה לְשֶׁרִישּׁוּמוֹ נִיכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the second condition: She does not drink **until the scroll is erased.** Why does the *baraita* need to state this? **But what** could **he give her to drink** if the scroll was not yet erased into the water? **Rav Ashi says: No,** this *halakha* **is necessary for** an instance **where** the scroll was erased, but **the impression** of the ink **is** still **discernible** on the parchment. The woman does not drink until the scroll is totally erased.

The second prerequisite — *ve-ad she-lo nimchakah megillah* — seems puzzling at first. Of course she cannot drink if the ink has not yet been scraped into the water; there would be nothing to drink. The *gemara* therefore asks: *ela mai mashkeh lah?* — What exactly would she be drinking if it hasn't been erased? *Rav Ashi* resolves this: *lo nitzrecha ela le-shirumo nikkar* — the requirement is not simply that some erasure has occurred, but that the erasure must be total and complete. If words were scraped into the water but faint traces of the writing remained visible on the *megillah* — *rishumo nikkar* — the water cannot be drunk. The *pasuk*'s requirement of erasure demands that the writing be entirely undetectable; a partially erased *megillah* renders the water invalid.

### Oath First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁלֹּא תְּקַבֵּל עָלֶיהָ שְׁבוּעָה. מִישְׁתָּא הוּא דְּלָא שָׁתְיָא, הָא מִיכְתָּב כָּתְבִי לַהּ, וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: מְגִילַּת סוֹטָה שֶׁכְּתָבָהּ קוֹדֶם שֶׁתְּקַבֵּל עָלֶיהָ שְׁבוּעָה — לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם! כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the third condition: She does not drink **until she accepts the oath upon herself.** One might infer from this statement that **it is** only **that** she **does not drink** before she accepts the oath; **however,** the scroll **is written for her** before she accepts the oath. **But didn't Rava say:** With regard to **a scroll of a *sota* that was written before she accepted the oath upon herself,** whoever wrote it **did nothing,** and the scroll is rendered invalid. The Gemara responds: **This was cited for no reason,** as in fact the scroll is not even written before she accepts the oath upon herself, and nothing should be inferred.

The third prerequisite — *ve-ad she-lo tekabbel aleha ha-shvuah* — teaches that the woman must have already accepted the oath before she can drink. The *gemara* raises a subtle point here: this sounds as though the restriction is only that she cannot drink before the oath, but that the *megillah* itself could have been written beforehand. Yet *Rava* elsewhere states clearly that a *megillat sotah* written before the woman accepted the *shvuah* is entirely invalid — *lo asah ve-lo klum*. The order of the *pesukim* must be followed: first the oath, then the writing of the *megillah*, then the erasing, and then the drinking. The *gemara* acknowledges that the inclusion of the oath requirement as a distinct point here was perhaps to round out the list to three, since the underlying rule — that the oath must precede the writing — is already established elsewhere.

## Rabbanan Interpretation

### First Mention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: ״וְהִשְׁקָה״ קַמָּא — לְגוּפוֹ, שֶׁמַּשְׁקֶה וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ. ״וְאַחַר יַשְׁקֶה״ — מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְשֶׁרִישּׁוּמוֹ נִיכָּר. ״וְהִשְׁקָהּ״ בָּתְרָא — שֶׁאִם נִמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה וְאוֹמֶרֶת ״אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה״, מְעַרְעֲרִין אוֹתָהּ וּמַשְׁקֶה אוֹתָהּ בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis hold** that **the first** occurrence of the term: **"And he shall make** the woman **drink,"** is written **to** teach the *halakha* **itself,** i.e., **that** the priest first **forces her to drink and afterward sacrifices her meal-offering.** The second instance: **"And afterward he shall make** the woman **drink," is necessary** to teach **that** as long as **the impression** of the writing **is** still **discernible,** the *sota* is not given the bitter water to drink. The third verse, **the last** occurrence of the term: **"And he shall make her drink,"** teaches **that if the scroll was erased and** then the woman **says: I will not drink, she is forced to drink against her will.**

Having established *Rabbi Shimon*'s reading of *ve-achar yashkeh* as the primary timing instruction, the *gemara* now explains how *Rabbanan* — who hold that the drinking precedes the *mincha* — account for all three *pesukim*. Their logic rests on the principle that the first mention establishes the operative timing. *Rabbanan savri ve-hishkah kama legufo de-mashkeh ve-achar kach makriv* — the *Rabbanan* treat the first *ve-hishkah*, which appears before the *mincha* steps, as the authoritative statement of sequence: she drinks first, and only then is the *mincha* brought.

### Rishumo Nikkar

If the first *ve-hishkah* establishes timing, then what does the second reference — *ve-achar yashkeh* — teach? *Rabbanan* say it is not about timing at all, but about a separate *halakhah*: *mi-ba'ei lei she-rishumo nikkar* — it teaches the *din* of *rishumo nikkar*. The word *ve-achar*, "afterwards," is read not as "after the *mincha*" but as "after everything that preceded the drinking has been properly completed" — including, most critically, the requirement that the *megillah* be fully erased with no faint writing remaining. The verse is a parenthetical reminder: before the actual drinking takes place, ensure that all prior steps — particularly the erasure — were properly performed.

### Forcing Compliance

The third *ve-hishkah* — *ve-hishkah batra* — teaches the position of *Rabbi Akiva*: *she-im nimchakah megillah ve-amrah eini shotah, me'arin otah u-mashkin otah be-al karchah* — if after the *megillah* was erased the woman declares that she will not drink, we press her and cause her to drink against her will. The final mention of drinking is thus not about timing, but about the obligation to ensure compliance: once the *megillah* has been scraped into the water, the process must be seen through, even coercively. In this way, *Rabbanan* derive three distinct teachings from the three *pesukim*, while maintaining that the first *ve-hishkah* governs the actual sequence: drinking first, *mincha* second.

## Rabbi Shimon Counter…

### Afterwards Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: ״וְאַחַר יַשְׁקֶה״ — לְגוּפוֹ, שֶׁמַּקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַשְׁקָהּ. ״וְהִשְׁקָה״ קַמָּא — שֶׁאִם הִשְׁקָהּ וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְרִיב אֶת מִנְחָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה. ״וְהִשְׁקָהּ״ בָּתְרָא — שֶׁאִם נִמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה וְאָמְרָה ״אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה״, מְעַרְעֲרִין אוֹתָהּ וּמַשְׁקִין אוֹתָהּ בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon holds** that the second verse: **"And afterward he shall make** the woman **drink** the water" (Numbers 5:26), is written **to** teach the *halakha* **itself,** i.e., **that** the priest first **sacrifices her meal-offering and afterward forces her to drink. The first** occurrence of the term: **"And he shall make** the woman **drink,"** teaches **that if he forced her to drink and** only **afterward sacrificed her meal-offering,** the offering **is** nevertheless **valid. The last** occurrence of the term: **"And he shall make her drink,"** teaches **that if** the **scroll was erased and** then **she said: I will not drink, she is forced to drink against her will.**

*Rabbi Shimon* takes the opposite approach. *Ve-achar yashkeh legufo* — the explicit *ve-achar yashkeh* is the primary timing instruction, and it places the drinking definitively after the *mincha*. This is not a parenthetical point about proper procedure; it is the statement of the correct sequence: first the *mincha*, then the drinking.

How then does *Rabbi Shimon* handle the first *ve-hishkah*, which appears before the *mincha* steps? He reads it as establishing the *be-di'avad* ruling: *she-im hishkah ve-achar kach hikriv et minchatah, kesherah* — if the order was reversed and she drank before the *mincha* was brought, the procedure is nonetheless valid after the fact. The earlier *ve-hishkah* is not saying "now have her drink"; it is saying that even if you were to have her drink at this earlier point, you could still go forward with the *mincha* and the *sotah* procedure would remain intact. It establishes retroactive validity for an out-of-order sequence, while the true *lechatchila* sequence is governed by *ve-achar yashkeh*.

The third *ve-hishkah batra* — like *Rabbi Akiva*'s reading — teaches that if the *megillah* was erased and the woman refuses to drink, she is compelled.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן — בְּדִיעֲבַד לָא פָּתַח קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the Rabbis' opinion: **And the Rabbis** would respond to Rabbi Shimon that the **verse does not begin** the discussion **with** a *halakha* that is applicable only **after the fact,** and therefore the initial mention of the drinking is referring to the proper time for the ritual.

*Rabbanan*, for their part, refuse to accept that the *pasuk* would open with a *be-di'avad* scenario. Since the *pasuk* describes drinking before the *mincha*, that must be the *lechatchila* order, not a concession to a substandard procedure. This is the crux of why *Rabbanan* resist *Rabbi Shimon*'s reading: the *pshat* of the *pesukim* — however much *Rabbi Shimon*'s overall reading may commend itself — militates against treating the earlier mention of drinking as a mere *be-di'avad* allowance.

## Rabbi Akiva Force

### Rabbi Yehuda Force

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַשְׁקִין אוֹתָהּ בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כַּלְבּוֹס שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל מְטִילִין לְתוֹךְ פִּיהָ, שֶׁאִם נִמְחֲקָה מְגִילָּה וְאָמְרָה ״אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה״ — מְעַרְעֲרִין אוֹתָהּ וּמַשְׁקִין אוֹתָהּ בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כְּלוּם אָנוּ צְרִיכִין אֶלָּא לְבוֹדְקָהּ, וַהֲלֹא בְּדוּקָה וְעוֹמֶדֶת! אֶלָּא: עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרַב הַקּוֹמֶץ — יְכוֹלָה לַחֲזוֹר בָּהּ, מִשֶּׁקָּרַב הַקּוֹמֶץ — אֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לַחֲזוֹר בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does Rabbi Akiva** in fact **hold** that the woman is **forced to drink against her will? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:3) that **Rabbi Yehuda says: A hook [*kelabus*]** made **of iron is** forcibly **placed into her mouth,** so **that if the scroll was erased and she said: I will not drink, she is forced to drink against her will. Rabbi Akiva said:** It is not necessary to force her to drink. **Don't we need** to force her to drink the water **only** in order **to evaluate her** fidelity? **And isn't she established as** having been **evaluated** when she refuses to drink, as she is essentially admitting her guilt? **Rather,** Rabbi Akiva's statement should be understood as follows: **Until the handful is sacrificed she can retract** her decision to drink the bitter water; however, **once the handful is sacrificed she cannot retract** her decision to drink.

The *gemara* now raises a challenge against the attribution of forced drinking to *Rabbi Akiva*. A separate *braita* records a dispute about what happens when the *megillah* has been erased and the woman refuses to drink. *Rabbi Yehuda* states that an iron jaw-spreader — *kalbos shel barzel* — would be placed into her mouth to force it open, and she would be made to drink against her will in any such case.

*Rabbi Akiva* rejects this. The purpose of the entire procedure, he argues, is *le-vodkah* — to test her, to determine whether she committed adultery. The bitter waters are an instrument of investigation, not punishment in their own right. If the very prospect of drinking has caused her to admit guilt — *ve-halo bedukah ve-omedet*, she is already as good as proven — then the waters have achieved their purpose. The name of God has been erased and this has produced the desired result: a confession or its equivalent. There is no basis to compound this by forcing the water upon her as well.

### Akiva Distinction

*Rabbi Akiva* is not categorically opposed to forced drinking. He draws a temporal line: *ad she-lo karav ha-kometz, yekholah la-chazor bah* — before the *kometz* has been offered, the woman can retract. *Mishe-karav ha-kometz, einah yekholah la-chazor bah* — once the *kometz* has been offered, she cannot retract, and she is made to drink.

### Akiva Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, תִּיקְשֵׁי לָךְ הִיא גּוּפַהּ: מִשֶּׁקָּרַב הַקּוֹמֶץ אֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לַחֲזוֹר בָּהּ? וַהֲלֹא בְּדוּקָה וְעוֹמֶדֶת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to your reasoning** in explanation of Rabbi Akiva's statement, **this** explanation **itself should pose a difficulty for you.** Why **can't she retract** her decision **once the handful is sacrificed? And isn't she established as** having been **evaluated** when she refuses to drink?

This distinction seems initially puzzling. If the entire basis for *Rabbi Akiva*'s leniency is that her refusal constitutes an implicit confession — rendering forced drinking unnecessary because the waters have already served their purpose — then why does it matter whether the *kometz* has been offered or not? An implicit confession is an implicit confession regardless of where in the procedure one stands. The *gemara* presses this internal tension within *Rabbi Akiva*'s position.

## Trembling Analysis

### Fear vs. Guilt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דְּקָהָדְרָא בַּהּ מֵחֲמַת רְתִיתָא, וְהָא — דְּקָהָדְרָא בָּהּ מֵחֲמַת בְּרִיּוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult; this** case, where she is forced to drink, is referring to a situation **where she retracts** her decision to drink **due to fear,** as her refusal is not viewed as an admission of guilt, and it is possible that if she drinks she will be found undefiled. **And that** case, where she does not drink, is referring to a situation **where she retracts** her decision **in a state of** good **health.** Since she does not appear to be afraid, her refusal is viewed as an admission of guilt.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction within *Rabbi Akiva*'s position by introducing a crucial distinction between two types of refusal. The fundamental question underlying *Rabbi Akiva*'s entire framework is: can we reliably read her refusal to drink as an implicit admission of adultery? The answer is: not always. There is a real concern that the ritual's terrifying nature — the solemn oath, the divine name being erased, the entire theatre of the *Beis Hamikdash* — may have so overwhelmed an innocent woman that she refuses out of sheer panic, not out of guilt. If that is the case, her refusal is not a confession at all; it is a symptom of fear.

### Calm Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל מֵחֲמַת בְּרִיּוּתָא — כְּלָל כְּלָל לָא שָׁתְיָא. מֵחֲמַת רְתִיתָא עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרַב הַקּוֹמֶץ, דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא אִמְּחוּק מְגִילָּה, אִי נָמֵי אִמְּחוּק מְגִילָּה, דְּשֶׁלֹּא כְּדִין עֲבוּד כֹּהֲנִים דְּמַחֲקִי — מָצְיָ[א] הָדְרָא בָּהּ. מִשֶּׁקָּרַב הַקּוֹמֶץ, דִּבְדִין עֲבוּד כֹּהֲנִים דְּמָחֲקִי — לָא מָצֵי הָדְרָא בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what** Rabbi Akiva **is saying:** In **any** case where she retracts her decision to drink **in a state of** good **health, she does not drink at all.** With regard to a *sota* who retracts her decision **due to fear,** if she retracts her decision **before the handful is sacrificed, when the scroll has not yet been erased; or** even if the **scroll was** already **erased,** since **the priests acted incorrectly when they erased** it beforehand; **she can retract** her decision. **Once the handful is sacrificed, in which** case the **priests acted correctly when they erased** the scroll, **she cannot retract** her decision, and she is forced to drink against her will.

*Rabbi Akiva*'s categorical position against forced drinking applies specifically to *be-riyuta* — a calm, collected demeanor. If a woman is composed and deliberately says she will not drink, that is unmistakably an implicit admission. She understands precisely what is at stake; she is not overwhelmed by terror; and she is choosing not to proceed. *Rabbi Akiva* therefore holds: *kol machamat briyuta klal klal lo shatya* — whenever the refusal comes from a calm, composed state, we never force her under any circumstances. That refusal is treated as a confession, and the waters have served their purpose.

The more complex case is *retita* — a woman who is trembling and visibly terrified. Here *Rabbi Akiva* cannot confidently read her refusal as a confession; the trembling suggests that fear, not guilt, may be driving her behavior. In this case, *Rabbi Akiva* draws his distinction based on when in the procedure the refusal occurs.

Before the *kometz* is offered, *Rabbi Akiva* holds that she may retract — she cannot be forced to drink. *Rav Ashi* explains: either the *megillah* has not yet been erased, or — following *Rashi*'s interpretation of *Rabbi Akiva*'s position — the *megillah* was erased, but the *kohanim* did so prematurely, before the *kometz* was brought. Since the proper order, according to *Rabbi Akiva*, should be *mincha* first and then erasure, a *kohen* who erased the *megillah* before the *kometz* was offered acted improperly — *she-lo ke-din avud kohanim de-machki*. In that case, the woman had more time rightfully available to her before being confronted with the erased water, and the situation was precipitated by the *kohanim*'s jumping ahead. Given both the trembling and the procedural irregularity, *Rabbi Akiva* does not treat her refusal as a confession, and she may back out — *matzi hadra bah*.

Once the *kometz* has been offered and the erasure was performed in the proper order — *ke-din avud kohanim de-machki* — the situation changes. Everything has been done correctly; all proper steps have been completed. Now, even though she is trembling and her refusal might stem from fear, *Rabbi Akiva* holds that we cannot grant her an exit. The trembling means we cannot confidently interpret her refusal as an admission of guilt, and therefore the logic that "the waters have already served their purpose" does not apply. Paradoxically, it is precisely because she may be innocent but simply terrified that *Rabbi Akiva* insists she must drink — *lo matzi hadra bah* — and if necessary, she is compelled.

This elegant resolution reconciles the apparently contradictory positions. *Rabbi Akiva* never forces a calm woman, because her composure makes her refusal a clear implicit admission. He never forces before the *kometz* when a trembling woman refuses, because procedural considerations allow her to withdraw. But he does force after the *kometz* when a trembling woman refuses, precisely because her trembling renders the refusal ambiguous rather than confessional, and since the procedure has been properly completed, it must be seen through. Two *tanna'im* debate exactly where *Rabbi Akiva* placed this line — the earlier *braita* suggests the critical factor is the erasure of the *megillah*, while this *braita* places it at the offering of the *kometz*. The *gemara* resolves this as *trei tanna'ei ve-aliba de-Rabbi Akiva* — two *tanna'im* each transmitting a version of *Rabbi Akiva*'s position.

## Retraction Question

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אָמְרָה אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה מֵחֲמַת בְּרִיּוּתָא, וְחָזְרָה וְאָמְרָה שׁוֹתָה אֲנִי, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If **she** initially **said: I will not drink, while in a state of** good **health, and** then **she retracted** her statement **and said: I will drink, what is** the *halakha*?

The *gemara* now poses a further question. The case of the calm woman whose refusal is read as an implicit admission has been established. But what happens when that woman subsequently changes her mind? She said *eini shotah* — calmly and clearly, without trembling — and *Rabbi Akiva* has read this as a tacit confession of adultery. But then she reverses course and says *shotah ani* — "I will drink." Does she get to retract?

### Cannot Retract

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאָמְרָה אֵינִי שׁוֹתָה — טְמֵאָה אֲנִי קָאָמְרָה, וְכֵיוָן דְּאַחְזִיק נַפְשַׁהּ בְּטוּמְאָה, לָא מָצְיָא הָדְרָה בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Does one say that **when she said: I will not drink,** it is as if **she** confessed and **said: I am defiled, and since she established herself as defiled she cannot retract** her statement?

One view holds that she cannot retract. *Keivan de-amrah eini shotah, teme'ah ani ka-amrah* — since she said she would not drink, she implicitly declared herself to have committed adultery. And *keivan de-achzik nafshah be-tum'ah, lo matzi hadra bah* — once she established her status as one who committed adultery, she cannot undo it. The *gemara* notes the suggestive force of the word *de-achzik* — "established" — which invokes a quasi-judicial framework: she has fixed a *halakhic* status for herself, and one cannot simply reverse such an establishment.

### Fear-Based Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִילְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאָמְרָה שׁוֹתָה אֲנִי — גַּלִּיָא דַּעְתַּהּ דְּמֵחֲמַת בִּיעֲתוּתָא הוּא דְּאָמְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps when she said: I will drink, she revealed her thoughts that it** was only **due to fear that she said** she will not drink?

The opposing view argues that we should not be so quick to finalize the admission. *Keivan de-amrah shotah ani, galya da'ata de-machamat biyututa hu de-amrah* — now that she is willing to drink, it becomes clear that even what appeared to be a calm, collected refusal may have been rooted in fear rather than guilt. The external appearance of composure may not perfectly track inner states. Since she never explicitly confessed — she only implicitly did so through refusal — and since she is now reversing that refusal, perhaps we should not press the implicit admission further and should allow her to drink.

### Teiku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*. The tension between treating an implicit admission as irrevocable and allowing the woman's demonstrated willingness to drink to recast the prior refusal as fear-based cannot be adjudicated here.

## Water Bitterness

### Bitter Additive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: צָרִיךְ שֶׁיִּתֵּן מַר לְתוֹךְ הַמַּיִם. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״מֵי הַמָּרִים״, שֶׁמָּרִים כְּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel's father says:** It is **necessary** for one **to put a bitter** substance **into the water** that the *sota* drinks. **What is the reason** for this? It is **as the verse states:** "And he shall blot them out into **the water of bitterness"** (Numbers 5:23), indicating **that** they **are already bitter** before the scroll is erased.

The daf concludes with a ruling of *Avuyei di-Shmuel* — the father of *Shmuel*. He holds that the *sota*h's water must contain an actual bitter additive: *tzarich she-yitein mar le-tokh ha-mayim* — something physically bitter must be placed into the water, independent of any effect produced by the erasing of the curses.

### Textual Proof

The source for this requirement is the redundant language of the verse itself: *mei ha-marim ha-marim* — the bitter, bitter waters. The word for bitterness appears twice, and *Avuyei di-Shmuel* reads this as indicating two independent sources of bitterness. The *meararim* — the cursing quality — is produced by the erasing of God's name and the curses into the water. But the *marim* — the physical bitterness — must be present independently. *Marim ke-var* — the waters must already be bitter before the spiritual dimension is added. The physical bitterness of the water is thus a freestanding requirement, not a byproduct of the spiritual process.
