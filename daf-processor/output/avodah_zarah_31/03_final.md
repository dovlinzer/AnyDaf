# Avodah Zarah 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wine Categories

### Rav Assi's Classes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה יֵינוֹת הֵן — יֵין נֶסֶךְ אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וּמְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה בִּכְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira: There are three** kinds of prohibited **wines:** It is **prohibited to** derive **benefit from wine** used for **a libation, and** the wine **imparts severe** ritual **impurity when** it has the volume of **an olive-bulk.**

The *daf* opens with a tidy classificatory teaching that sets the stage for much of what follows. Rav Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, announces: *shloshah yeinos hein* — there are three types of wine, each carrying a distinct *halakhic* status. The *gemara* had been immersed in discussions of *stam yeinam* and, on the preceding *amud*, of *gilluy* — the concern about liquids left exposed and the danger of serpents — but now it steps back to offer a structured overview of these three categories and their divergent consequences.

### Yayin Nesech

The first and most severe category is *yayin nesech*: wine that was actually poured in the service of *avodah zarah*. Such wine is *assur behana'ah* — one is forbidden to derive any benefit whatsoever from it. Beyond the prohibition of benefit, the *gemara* imports the full apparatus of ritual impurity: *mesamei tumah chamurah kichazayis*. Wine actively offered to *avodah zarah* is treated like a corpse, as the verse states *zivchei meisim*. Even a single *kezayis* transmits a severe *tumah*, and — like a corpse — it conveys *tumah* even through carrying, without direct contact. This is discussed in the *mishnah* in *Shabbat*, and the intersection of forbidden foods and *tumah ve'taharah* here is striking: the Torah uses the language of *tumah* when describing forbidden species (*al titame'u bechol eileh*), and the *gemara* gives that conceptual link its sharpest legal expression precisely in the domain of *avodah zarah*, where the taboo is total.

### Stam Yeinam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְתָם יֵינָם — אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וּמְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין בִּרְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** It is **prohibited to** derive **benefit from nondescript wine** of a gentile, **and** the wine **imparts the ritual impurity of liquids when** it has the volume of **one-quarter** of a *log*.

The second category, appearing here in what may be the *gemara*'s first use of the precise term *stam yeinam*, is the general, non-specific wine produced or handled by non-Jews — wine whose connection to *avodah zarah* is presumed rather than proven. This too is *assur behana'ah*. The rationale, as the *gemara* frames it, is the concern of *avodah zarah* itself: even though we have no direct evidence this wine was poured in idolatrous service, the *issur* of benefit is maintained because the prohibition is structured around *avodah zarah* concerns, not merely around intermarriage. An important corollary of this two-track structure is that wine belonging to a *ger toshav*, or — according to the *Rambam* — wine of Muslims, who have no association with *avodah zarah* whatsoever, is forbidden to drink because of intermarriage concerns (*chatnut*), yet remains *mutar behana'ah*. This distinction reveals the two independent foundations upon which the prohibition rests.

The *tumah* associated with *stam yeinam* is lighter than that of *yayin nesech*: *mesamei tumas mashkin birevi'is* — it carries the impurity of liquids that have become *tamei* through contact with, say, a dead rodent, rendering it a *rishon letumah* that can defile food but not people. And this impurity applies only if there is a *revi'is* — roughly three and a half ounces — present; anything less does not carry even this lower level of *tumah*.

It bears noting that throughout this discussion, the *gemara* takes for granted what was not made explicit in the *mishnah* itself: that even the mere touch of a non-Jew renders wine *stam yeinam*. One might have imagined from the *mishnah*'s language of "their wine" that the prohibition applied only to wine produced by non-Jews. The *gemara* here presupposes the broader rule — that contact alone is sufficient — and the ensuing discussions about seals and tampering are built on that premise.

### Entrusted Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּפְקִיד יֵינוֹ אֵצֶל גּוֹי — אָסוּר בִּשְׁתִיָּה, וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the wine of **one who deposits his wine with a gentile,** one is **prohibited from drinking** it, **but** one is **permitted to** derive **benefit** from it.

The third and most nuanced category is wine that a Jew produced and then entrusted to a non-Jew for safekeeping — *hamafkid yeino etzel oved kochavim*. This wine occupies a middle halakhic position: *assur bishtiyah umutar behana'ah* — forbidden to drink, yet permitted for benefit. The difficulty is immediately apparent. If one genuinely fears that the non-Jew tampered with the wine and touched it, rendering it *stam yeinam*, then it should be fully *assur behana'ah*. If one has no such fear, it should be entirely *mutar bishtiyah*. What produces this curious middle ground?

## Fruit Entrusted Case

### Swapping Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: הַמַּפְקִיד פֵּירוֹתָיו אֵצֶל גּוֹי, הֲרֵי הֵן כְּפֵירוֹתָיו שֶׁל גּוֹי לִשְׁבִיעִית וּלְמַעֲשֵׂר! כְּגוֹן שֶׁיִּיחֵד לוֹ קֶרֶן זָוִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 11b): With regard to **one who deposits his produce with a gentile,** it has the status **of the produce of a gentile** with regard **to** the *halakhot* of the **Sabbatical** Year **and** with regard **to tithe,** as the gentile might have exchanged the Jew's produce with untithed produce or produce of the Sabbatical Year. According to this logic, wine deposited with a gentile should be entirely prohibited, due to the concern that the gentile exchanged it with his own. The Gemara answers: It is permitted for one to derive benefit from the wine in a case **where** the gentile **designated a corner for** the Jew's wine, i.e., if the wine was locked away in a specific place to which only a Jew has access.

The *gemara* roots the problem in a *mishnah* that teaches: *hamafkid peirotav etzel oved kochavim* — one who leaves his fruit with a non-Jew — must treat the returned fruit as if it were the non-Jew's own, because of the presumption that the non-Jew swapped his lower-quality produce for the Jew's higher-quality produce. The practical consequence falls in the domain of *demai*: the returned fruit must be treated stringently for *shevi'it* and *ma'aser* purposes, as though it belonged to the non-Jew all along. The same logic might apply to wine: you fear he swapped your wine for his, meaning you're getting back his wine, i.e., *stam yeinam*.

The *gemara* then qualifies this: *k'gon she'yiched lo keren zavit* — for instance, if you designated a corner for it. *Rashi* explains this to mean that the wine was locked in a room and you retained the key, so that the non-Jew had no meaningful access. If so, however, there is no reason to suspect swapping — and therefore the middle position still makes no sense. Why should wine be forbidden to drink if we're confident the corner was secure?

### Bar Kappara Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אִקְּלַע לְפָרוֹד, אָמַר: כְּלוּם יֵשׁ מִשְׁנַת בַּר קַפָּרָא? תְּנָא לֵיהּ רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם דְּמִן פָּרוֹד: הַמַּפְקִיד יֵינוֹ אֵצֶל גּוֹי — מוּתָּר בִּשְׁתִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Yoḥanan** once **happened** to come **to Parod,** where the deceased *tanna* bar Kappara had lived. When he arrived, **he said: Is there any Mishna of bar Kappara** here? In response, **Rabbi Tanḥum of Parod taught him** the following *baraita*, citing bar Kappara: With regard to **one who deposits his wine with a gentile, drinking** from the wine **is permitted.**

To address this, the *gemara* invokes a teaching recovered under dramatic circumstances. Rav Yochanan traveled to *Parhod*, the town where Bar Kappara had lived and died. Seeking to recover lost teachings of that great master, he asked the locals: *klum yeish mishnat Bar Kappara* — does anyone here know the teachings of Bar Kappara? Rav Tanchum of *Parhod* responded with the following: *hamafkid yeino etzel oved kochavim mutar bishtiyah* — if you entrust your wine to a non-Jew's care, you may even drink it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ ״מְקוֹם שֶׁיִּפּוֹל הָעֵץ שָׁם יְהוּ״, ״שָׁם יְהוּ״ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא שָׁם יְהוּ פֵּירוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this, Rabbi Yoḥanan **read** the following verse **about him: "Where the tree falls, there it shall be"** (Ecclesiastes 11:3). Does it **enter your mind** that this means that the tree itself **will be there?** It is obvious that a fallen tree lands where it falls. **Rather,** the verse is saying: **There its fruits shall be.** The verse is a metaphor for a Sage, and its fruits represent his disciples. Rabbi Yoḥanan was intimating that although bar Kappara may have died, Rabbi Tanḥum, his disciple, perpetuates his wisdom.

Rav Yochanan then recited over Rav Tanchum the verse *makom she'yipol ha'eitz sham yehu* — where the tree falls, there it remains. *Rashi* explains this poetically: Bar Kappara was the great tree; his teachings — his fruit — remained rooted in the very place of his death. The implication is practical as well: if you want to recover the teachings of a great scholar, you return to where he lived, for that is where his fruit endures.

The upshot of Bar Kappara's ruling is that in a case of genuine protection — a locked room, a designated corner — the wine is completely permissible. This leaves the middle position still unaccounted for: Bar Kappara says *mutar*, while if we were genuinely afraid, it should be fully forbidden. Where does the intermediate status — *assur bishtiyah umutar behana'ah* — come from?

### Eliezer vs Sages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא — הָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** In any event, it is apparent from the *baraita* quoted by Rabbi Tanḥum that there is a case in which it is permitted to drink wine deposited with a gentile. This contradicts the statement of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira. **Rabbi Zeira said** that it is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which permits drinking wine deposited with a gentile, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** whereas **that** statement of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד הַלּוֹקֵחַ וְאֶחָד הַשּׂוֹכֵר בַּיִת בַּחֲצֵירוֹ שֶׁל גּוֹי, וּמִלְּאוּהוּ יַיִן, וּמַפְתֵּחַ אוֹ חוֹתָם בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מַתִּיר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira elaborates: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **both one who purchases or one who rents a house** located **in a gentile's courtyard, and they filled** the house with containers of **wine, and** the **key** to the house **or a seal is in the possession of a Jew,** so that the gentile cannot access the wine, in which case it is even more secure than when a corner is designated for the Jew's wine, **Rabbi Eliezer permits** the wine, **and the Rabbis prohibit** it.

Rav Zeira resolves the tension: *lo kashya, ha Rabbi Eliezer ha rabanan* — the middle position and Bar Kappara's leniency are not contradictory; they reflect a *tannaic* dispute. A *baraita* teaches: if a Jew purchases or rents a house in a courtyard owned by a non-Jew, fills it with wine, and the key and seal are in the Jew's possession — *Rabbi Eliezer matir v'chachamim osrin*. Rabbi Eliezer rules the wine entirely permissible; the Sages forbid it. Bar Kappara's ruling aligns with Rabbi Eliezer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר נַחְמָנִי, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר זְעֵירִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲמַר לִי אַבָּא בַּר חָמָא, הָכִי אָמַר זְעֵירִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Naḥmani, says** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav says, and some say** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Ze'eiri says, and some say** that **Rav Ḥisda says: Abba bar Ḥama said to me** that **this** is what **Ze'eiri said:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer.**

Rav Assi's middle category of *assur bishtiyah umutar behana'ah*, then, represents a kind of in-between position — though the precise mechanism by which it emerges from this dispute is left to be worked through as the *gemara* continues. The ruling ultimately follows Rabbi Eliezer — *hilkheta k'Rabbi Eliezer* — as transmitted through multiple chains: Rav Chiya son of Rabbi Chiya bar Nachmani citing Rav Chisda in Rav's name, with alternative versions citing Rav Chisda in Ze'iri's name, and still others citing Rav Chisda reporting from Abba bar Chama in Ze'iri's name. The multiplicity of transmission chains underscores how important this ruling was in practice — *Tosafot* takes it up at length in the context of Jews transporting and shipping wine and the type of seal required to protect it from non-Jewish interference.

## Seals & Tampering

### One Seal Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַכֹּל מִשְׁתַּמֵּר בְּחוֹתָם אֶחָד, חוּץ מִן הַיַּיִן שֶׁאֵין מִשְׁתַּמֵּר בְּחוֹתָם אֶחָד. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן מִשְׁתַּמֵּר בְּחוֹתָם אֶחָד. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי — הָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָא כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: All** substances are sufficiently **secured by one seal, except wine, which is not** sufficiently **secured by one seal. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even wine is secured by one seal. And they do not disagree** in their reasoning. Rather, **this** opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** and **that** opinion of Rabbi Elazar is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

Rav Elazar states: *hakol mishtamer b'chotam echad chutz min hayayin she'ein mishtamer b'chotam echad* — everything is protected by one seal except for wine, which is not protected by a single seal. For most goods, one seal suffices to give reasonable assurance that the contents have not been tampered with. Wine is different: whether because of its value, the desire non-Jews may have for it for their own use or for *avodah zarah*, or simply the magnitude of the temptation it presents — one seal is not enough to establish confidence that it has not been interfered with.

This position, on its face, aligns with the Sages who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer: whereas Rabbi Eliezer holds that a single seal suffices to render the wine *mutar*, Rav Elazar is saying that even one seal cannot protect wine. Rabbi Yochanan, however, counters: *afilu yayin mishtamer b'chotam echad* — even wine is sufficiently protected by a single seal, no differently from anything else.

The *gemara* then resolves the apparent contradiction: *v'lo pligi* — they are not in fact arguing within the same framework. Rather, *chad k'Rabbi Eliezer v'chad k'rabbanan* — one is operating within the position of Rabbi Eliezer and the other within that of the Sages.

There is, however, an alternative formulation (*ika d'amri*) in which both Rav Elazar and Rabbi Yochanan are understood to operate within the Sages' framework.

### Double Seal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַכֹּל מִשְׁתַּמֵּר בְּחוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם, חוּץ מִן הַיַּיִן שֶׁאֵין מִשְׁתַּמֵּר בְּחוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ יַיִן מִשְׁתַּמֵּר בְּחוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם. וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ כְּרַבָּנַן, מָר סָבַר: כִּי פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּחוֹתָם אֶחָד, אֲבָל בְּחוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם שָׁרוּ. וּמָר סָבַר: אֲפִילּוּ חוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם אָסְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that **Rabbi Elazar says: All** substances are **secured by a seal within a seal,** i.e., two seals, **except wine, which is not secured by a seal within a seal. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even wine is secured by a seal within a seal. And both** hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **when the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Eliezer,** it is **with regard to one seal, but** in the case of **a seal within a seal,** they concede that there is no concern that the gentile might have opened it, and therefore **they permit** the wine. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, **holds** that **they prohibited even** wine that was secured by **a seal within a seal.**

According to this version: Rav Elazar holds that not even a double seal — *chotam betoch chotam* — is adequate for wine (*she'ein mishtamer b'chotam betoch chotam*), while Rabbi Yochanan holds that even within the Sages' position, a double seal does suffice (*afilu yayin mishtamer b'chotam betoch chotam*). The internal debate within the Sages thus concerns whether two seals is adequate — one view says yes, one says nothing is adequate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי חוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם? אָמַר רָבָא: אַגָּנָא דְּפוּמָּא דְּחָבִיתָא שְׁרִיקָא וַחֲתִימָא — הָוֵי חוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם, וְאִי לָא — לָא. דִּיקּוּלָא וּמִיהַדַּק — הָוֵי חוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם, לָא מִיהַדַּק — לָא הָוֵי חוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם. נוֹד בְּדִיסַקַּיָּא, חֲתִימַת פִּיו לְמַטָּה — הָוֵי חוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם, פִּיו לְמַעְלָה — לָא הָוֵי חוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם, וְכִי כַּיִיף פּוּמֵּיהּ לְגָיו וְצַיִיר וַחֲתִים — הָוֵי חוֹתָם בְּתוֹךְ חוֹתָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is a seal within a seal like? Rava says: A basin** placed **over the mouth of a barrel** that is **smeared** with clay **and** stamped with **a seal is** considered **a seal within a seal. And if not,** it is **not** considered a seal within a seal. If **a basket** is placed over a barrel **and** is **fastened** to it, this **is a seal within a seal;** if it is **not fastened** to the barrel, **it is not a seal within a seal.** With regard to **a wineskin** that is placed **in a sack [*disakaya*],** if the wineskin's **stopper is** facing **downward,** this **is a seal within a seal;** if **its stopper is** facing **upward,** this **is not a seal within a seal. And if he bends the** wineskin's **bottleneck inward and ties** the sack **and seals** it, this **is** also considered **a seal within a seal.**

What constitutes a *chotam betoch chotam*? The *gemara* provides Rava's definition: *agna depuma dechavita serika vechatima* — a bowl placed over the mouth of a barrel, plastered and then sealed with a stamp or impression. The bowl plastered in place is one seal; the imprinted stamp is the second. This first example does seem to satisfy an intuitive notion of tamper-proofing: if someone removes the bowl, replaces the plaster, and tries to restore the stamp, it would likely be detectably different from the original.

The subsequent examples, however, complicate that intuition considerably.

### Seal Examples

Three further cases illustrate what qualifies as a *chotam betoch chotam*:

First: a *dikula* — a wicker basket — placed tightly over the mouth of a barrel. *Rashi* explains that *chotam betoch chotam* here means the barrel's ordinary cover (its standard stopper, which one normally applies) together with the tightly-affixed basket placed over the barrel's mouth. The combination of the two constitutes a double seal: *sala pi chavit vechavit megufa, dehaynu bet chotamot megufat hachavit*. If the basket is not tightly affixed (*lo mehadek*), it does not qualify.

Second: a leather flask (*nod*) placed inside a larger bag (*bediskeya*), with the sealed mouth of the flask pointing downward. This is a double seal: one must extract the flask, right it, and then open it — two operations. If the mouth faces upward, the inner flask is immediately accessible once the outer bag is opened, and it does not qualify as *chotam betoch chotam*.

Third: the flask's mouth is folded back inside the flask itself and then bound and sealed — *vechi chayef pumei legav, vetziyar vechasim*. This too constitutes a double seal.

What is striking about the second and third cases is that the "double seal" appears to be nothing more than two obstacles — two things one must undo sequentially — rather than anything that would be visibly detectably disturbed if tampered with. *Rashi*'s language in the basket case makes this explicit: *velo tarach leziyufei kulei hai* — he won't bother to go through all that effort. The protection afforded by a *chotam betoch chotam* seems, in at least these cases, to be primarily a matter of deterrence through inconvenience rather than forensic evidence of interference. This is, as one might say, somewhat surprising — logically, one would have expected "seal" to imply something tamper-evident rather than merely double-barriered. Perhaps the deeper assumption is that we are not genuinely expecting the scenario to occur; the formal structure of a double seal functions as a kind of halakhic threshold, signaling that adequate precaution has been taken, even without a fully traceable record of tampering.

## Samaritans & Seals

### Sealed Barrels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: יַיִן שֶׁל עֵין כּוּשִׁי אָסוּר מִפְּנֵי בִּירַת סְרִיקָא, וְשֶׁל בַּרְקָתָא אָסוּר מִפְּנֵי כְּפַר פַּרְשַׁאי, וְשֶׁל זַגְדוֹר אָסוּר מִפְּנֵי כְּפַר שָׁלֵים. חָזְרוּ לוֹמַר: חָבִיּוֹת פְּתוּחוֹת אֲסוּרוֹת, סְתוּמוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: At first,** the Sages **would say** that **wine from** the Samaritan city of **Ein Kushi is prohibited, due to** the concern that it might have come into contact with the idolatrous inhabitants of **Birat Serika, and** similarly the wine **of Barkata is prohibited due to** the idolatrous inhabitants of **the village of Parshai, and** the wine **of Zagdor is prohibited due to the village of Shaleim.** Subsequently, **they retracted** and began **saying: Open barrels are prohibited** but **sealed** barrels **are permitted.**

A *baraita* records the evolution of *halakhic* thinking about wine stored in Samaritan villages near non-Jewish ones. Originally, wine from *Ein Kushi* was forbidden because of the neighboring *Birat Srika* where non-Jews lived; similarly, wine from *Birkasa* was forbidden because of *Kfar Parshai*, and wine from *Zagdur* because of *Kfar Shalim*. In each case, the concern was not the Samaritans themselves — at this stage of the tradition, Samaritans did not render wine forbidden in the way non-Jews did — but rather the proximity of non-Jewish villages and the fear that those non-Jews would come into contact with the wine and that the Samaritans would not prevent it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי סְבוּר, וּלְבַסּוֹף מַאי סְבוּר? מֵעִיקָּרָא סְבוּר: אֵין כּוּתִי מַקְפִּיד עַל מַגַּע גּוֹי, לָא שְׁנָא פְּתוּחוֹת וְלָא שְׁנָא סְתוּמוֹת. וּלְבַסּוֹף סְבוּר: כִּי לָא קָפֵיד אַפְּתוּחוֹת, אַסְּתוּמוֹת מִקְפָּיד קָפֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** did they **reason initially, and what** did they **reason ultimately?** The Gemara answers: **Initially** they **reasoned: A Samaritan is not particular about the touch of** an idolatrous **gentile,** and **there is no difference** in this regard between **open** barrels **and sealed** barrels. **And ultimately** they **reasoned: When** Samaritans **are not particular** about a gentile's touch, this is only **with regard to open** barrels, but **with regard to sealed** barrels, **they are particular.** Since Samaritans ensure that gentiles do not handle sealed barrels of wine, this wine is permitted.

Subsequently, the Sages revised their position: *chaviyos pesuchos asuros, stumos mutaros* — open barrels are forbidden, sealed ones are permissible. The reasoning behind the original prohibition was *ein kusi makpid al maga ovdei kochavim* — the Samaritan is not careful about non-Jewish contact with the wine, and since we cannot distinguish between open and sealed, all wine in those areas was forbidden. The revised position reflects a more nuanced understanding: while a Samaritan may indeed be indifferent to whether non-Jews touch an open barrel — where casual tasting and handling is expected — he is careful about closed barrels. A customer who asks to buy a sealed barrel of wine expects it to have remained untouched; the Samaritan knows that if a non-Jew opens it, the Jewish buyer will refuse it. The practical concern, therefore, applies only to open barrels.

### Transport & Seals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן בְּיַד כּוּתִי, וְשֶׁל צִיר וְשֶׁל מוּרְיָיס בְּיַד גּוֹי, אִם מַכִּיר חוֹתָמוֹ וּסְתָמוֹ — מוּתָּר, אִם לָאו — אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** from the following *baraita*: With regard to **one who sends a barrel of wine in the hands of a Samaritan, or** a barrel **of** fish **brine or** a barrel **of fish stew in the hands of a gentile, if he recognizes his seal and his** manner of **closing** the barrel, it **is permitted; if** he does **not** recognize them, it **is prohibited.** Apparently, a sealed barrel is permitted only when it is recognizable.

This revision, however, immediately generates a difficulty. The *gemara* introduces the case of *hasholeach chavit shel yayin beyad Kuti* — one who sends a barrel of wine in the hands of a Samaritan — and notes that even here, a recognizable seal is required: *im makir chotamo usetimato mutar*, but if the seal is not recognizable as one's own, it is forbidden. The same requirement applies when fish brine (*tzir*) or *muryes* — a preparation of fish mixed with wine — is sent via a non-Jew. In those latter cases the concern is swapping, not mere contact: the non-Jew may substitute lower-quality, non-kosher brine or adulterated hash for the original. (The concern about *muryes* is specifically that the wine component makes the mixture valuable enough to adulterate by removing some of the hash and replacing it with cheaper wine.)

So we have an apparent inconsistency: the revised ruling says that sealed barrels in Samaritan territory are permissible without any special seal — merely being *satum* (ordinarily closed) is enough — yet when wine is being transported, a recognizable *chotam* is required. If ordinary closure suffices in storage, why does transport demand a formally recognizable seal?

### City vs Road

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא — כָּאן בָּעִיר, כָּאן בַּדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that this is **not difficult. Here,** the first *baraita* is referring to barrels located **in a city; there,** the second *baraita* is referring to barrels that the Samaritan carries **on the road.** Sealed barrels are permitted in a city because the Samaritan is careful to ensure that gentiles do not touch them in front of anyone, so that he does not forfeit the business of Jews. While traveling he is not concerned, as he assumes that no one will discover that the gentile came into contact with the wine.

Rabbi Zeira resolves the difficulty: *kan be'ir, kan baderech* — one case concerns wine in the city, the other concerns wine on the road.

*Tosafot*'s explanation is the more straightforward: in the city, the Samaritan is afraid that someone will see him allow a non-Jew to touch the wine, which would ruin his ability to sell it to Jews. On the road, no one is watching, and that deterrent disappears. *Rashi* presumably implies a similar logic. The care the Samaritan exercises, in other words, is not intrinsic piety but commercial self-interest — and that interest operates only when there are eyes on him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִידֵּי הָנָךְ דְּעִיר לָא בְּדֶרֶךְ אָתוּ? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בֵּין הַגִּיתּוֹת שָׁנִינוּ, כֵּיוָן דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אָפְכִי, מִירְתַת, אֲמַר: הַשְׁתָּא אִי חָזוּ לִי מַפְסְדוּ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya objects to this: Didn't these** barrels located **in the city come by the road** as well? **Rather, Rabbi Yirmeya says: We learned** the *baraita* that permits sealed barrels only in reference to those that are located **between the winepresses. Since everyone is found** there, the Samaritan **is apprehensive,** as he **says** to himself: **Now, if** someone **sees me** allowing a gentile to handle the wine **they will cause me to lose** my profit, as Jews will not purchase it.

Rabbi Yirmiya, however, immediately presses the point: *midi hanech de'ir, lav bederech atu?* — those barrels now sitting in the city — did they not travel on the road to get there? If the Samaritan is untrustworthy on the road, how can sealed barrels in the city be treated as uncompromised, given that they passed through a road journey to arrive there?

Rabbi Yirmiya therefore proposes a different resolution — not geographical but seasonal. *Beina gito shaninu* — the ruling about ordinary closure sufficing applies *between* the pressings, during the busy market season, when there is high foot-traffic and the town is full of people coming and going. *Keivan dechulei alma ahadri*, since everyone is bustling around and present, the Samaritan is careful — *mirtat* — afraid that someone will see him or that non-Jews will get near the wine and he'll lose his Jewish customers. But when the market is quiet and customers come one at a time, he is far less vigilant, and in those circumstances ordinary closure does not suffice; a formally recognizable seal is required.

One might also ask — and this was raised during the discussion — about the nature of the contact being feared here. The concern is that a non-Jew's finger actually touches the wine itself, not merely the outside of a container. This raises the question of how *stam yeinam* applies in contemporary contexts — for instance, whether pouring wine creates a halakhic problem for the wine remaining in the bottle. The logic by which pouring transmits *issur* involves the concept of *kocho* — one's force or energy causing the wine to move. Some authorities would argue this applies only to the wine that actually left the bottle, not to what remains inside; others disagree. When combined with the view that Christians today may not qualify as *ovdei avodah zarah* in the halakhic sense — in which case the *Rama* suggests leniency in cases of indirect contact — there is halakhic room for leniency in *bedi'avad* situations, particularly regarding wine remaining in the bottle. This is a matter to discuss with one's *posek*.

## Beer Prohibition

### Beer Rationales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָסְרוּ שֵׁכָר שֶׁל גּוֹיִם? רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִשּׁוּם חַתְנוּת, רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated: For what** reason did the Sages **prohibit the beer of gentiles? Rami bar Ḥama says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** It is **due to** the concern that Jews will befriend gentiles while drinking with them, which might lead to **marriage** with gentiles. **Rav Naḥman said:** It is **due to** the concern of **exposure.**

The *gemara* now turns to an entirely different prohibition: *sheikhar* — beer — of non-Jews. The question it opens with is itself revealing: *mipnei mah asru sheikhar shel ovdei kokhavim?* — why did they say beer is forbidden? Not "what is the source?" but "what is the rationale?" This is because, as *Tosafot* makes explicit (*issur sheikhar lo matzinu lo bamishnah velo babraita*), there is no Tannaic source for this prohibition at all. It seems to have arisen somewhere in the Amoraic period, without a formal textual anchor. From the outset, then, this *issur* comes out of thin air — and, as we will see, it was almost immediately qualified out of existence.

Two rationales are offered. Rami bar Chama, citing Rabbi Yitzchak, says *mishum chatnut* — intermarriage concerns. Going out to drink beer with non-Jews creates the kind of social conviviality that leads to intermarriage. This rationale is intuitively compelling; drinking together has always been understood as a social bonding activity, and a prohibition grounded in *chatnut* frames beer as a gateway to dangerous intimacy. Rav Nachman counters: *mishum gilluy* — the concern is exposure. Beer left uncovered may have been approached by snakes, and consuming exposed liquids carries the danger of ingested venom.

### Old Beer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַגִּילּוּי דְּמַאי? אִילֵּימָא גִּילּוּי דְּנַזְיָיתָא — אֲנַן נָמֵי מְגַלֵּינַן! וְאֶלָּא דְּחָבִיתָא — אֲנַן נָמֵי מְגַלֵּינַן! לָא צְרִיכָא, בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְצַלּוּ מַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** form **of exposure** is there a concern? **If we say** that the concern is with regard to **exposure of the vat, we too expose** the vat, and there is no reason to prohibit gentiles' beer more than that of Jews. **And** if you say: **Rather,** the concern is for exposure **of** the **barrel, we also expose** barrels. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to prohibit the beer **in a place where the water** used to brew it is allowed to **settle.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה יָשָׁן תִּשְׁתְּרֵי! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי: יָשָׁן מוּתָּר — אֵין מַנִּיחוֹ לְיַישֵּׁן, הֶחְמִיץ מוּתָּר — אֵין מַנִּיחוֹ לְהַחְמִיץ. גְּזֵירָה יָשָׁן אַטּוּ חָדָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, aged** beer should **be permitted, as Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** A substance that might contain exposed water but has **aged** is **permitted,** since the poison **does not allow it to age,** as it goes bad before it grows old. Similarly, if it **soured** it is **permitted,** because the poison impairs the taste but **does not allow it to sour.** Why, then, is all beer prohibited? The Gemara answers: The Sages issued a rabbinic **decree** with regard to **aged** beer **due to** the concern with regard to **new** beer.

The *gemara* tests the *gilluy* rationale. If that were the concern, one would expect old beer to be permissible — as Rabbi [Yehuda ha-Nasi] rules: *miyashan mutar*, old exposed liquid is permitted, because any venom in it would have risen to the surface and become visible over time. Similarly, if exposed wine turned to vinegar, it is permissible: *ein manicho lehakhmitz* — the poison would have prevented fermentation, so the very fact that it became vinegar proves no snake drank from it. Yet old beer, it seems, is also covered by the prohibition. The *gemara* answers: *gezeirah yashan atu chadash* — the rabbinic prohibition extends even to old beer lest one come to drink new beer as well. This answer is somewhat unusual: the standard rule about *gilluy* does not require such an extension, and the invocation of it here suggests that maybe the *gilluy* rationale alone does not fully account for the scope of the prohibition.

### Beer Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא מַפְּיקִין לֵיהּ (לאבבא) [אַבָּבָא] דְּחָנוּתָא, וְשָׁתֵי. רַב אַחַאי מַיְיתוּ לֵיהּ לְבֵיתֵיהּ, וְשָׁתֵי. וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ מִשּׁוּם חַתְנוּת, רַב אַחַאי עָבֵיד הַרְחָקָה יַתִּירְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites the opinions of various Sages with regard to beer. **Rav Pappa** had them **bring out** the **beer** belonging to gentiles from the store **to the entrance of the store, and he** would **drink** it outside the store. **Rav Aḥai** had them **bring** the beer **to his house, and he** would **drink** it there. **And both** of them drank the beer away from the presence of gentiles **due to** concern about **marriage** with gentiles. The Gemara notes that **Rav Aḥai established an extreme preventive measure** for himself beyond what is required by *halakha*.

Rav Pappa's practice: he would buy beer from a non-Jewish establishment and step outside to drink it — *mafkin leh ababa de-chanuta ve-shatei*. Rav Acha went further: he would take the beer home before drinking it — *meitu leh leveiteih ve-shatei*. Both, the *gemara* explains, understood the prohibition as rooted in *chatnut*. And here lies a crucial analytical point. An *issur* grounded in intermarriage concern does not render the substance itself *treif* in the full halakhic sense. Just as there are *poskim* who argue that food cooked by a non-Jew (*bishul akum*) does not render one's pots non-kosher since the prohibition is social rather than ontological, here the beer is not *treif* in the sense that eating or drinking it constitutes consuming a forbidden substance. The prohibition is contextual — don't drink it *there*, in that social setting. Step outside the store or take it home, and the beer is entirely fine. Rav Acha observed an extra *chumra* — technically stepping outside would have sufficed, but he wanted the additional distance of drinking only at home.

Of course, taken at face value, this *issur* would forbid the classic scene of Jews sitting in a non-Jewish bar drinking with non-Jewish friends — exactly the kind of social integration it was designed to prevent. And as the *gemara* itself notes, this prohibition effectively emerged without a formal Tannaic source, and throughout the generations it was observed more in the breach than in practice, with authorities finding ample grounds to limit its application.

### Rav & Chiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הַאי שִׁיכְרָא דַּאֲרַמָּאָה שְׁרֵי, וְחִיָּיא בְּרִי לָא נִישְׁתֵּי מִינֵּיהּ. מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי שְׁרֵי — לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׁרֵי, אִי אֲסִיר — לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֲסִיר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: This Aramean beer is permitted, but my son Ḥiyya does not drink from it.** The Gemara asks: **Whichever** way **you** look at this matter, Rav's statement is difficult: **If** the beer is **permitted,** then it is **permitted to everyone,** and there is no reason for his son to refrain from drinking it. And **if** it is **prohibited,** it is **prohibited to everyone,** and why would Rav say it is permitted?

Rav rules: *hai shichra de-arma'ah shari* — non-Jewish beer is permissible. Yet in the same breath he adds: *Chiya b'ri lo nishti mineih* — but my son Chiya should not drink from it. The *gemara* challenges: *mima nafshach* — make up your mind. If it is permissible, it should be permissible for everyone including your son; if it is forbidden, it should be forbidden for everyone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רַב סָבַר מִשּׁוּם גִּילּוּיָא, וְאָזֵיל מְרוֹרָא דִּכְשׁוּתָא וְקָלֵי לֵיהּ זִיהֲרֵיהּ, וְדִלְקֵי מַלְקֵי לֵיהּ טְפֵי, וְחִיָּיא בְּרִי הוֹאִיל וּלְקֵי לָא נִישְׁתֵּי מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rather, Rav holds** that the prohibition is **due to exposure, but the bitterness of the hops** in the beer **goes and impairs the** snake's **venom,** so that it is safe for an average person to drink. **But** a person of **weak** constitution **is weakened further** by the impaired venom, **and** Rav was saying: In the case of **my son Ḥiyya, since he is weak, he does not drink from it.**

The resolution reveals that Rav's concern is neither *chatnut* nor any social prohibition, but *gilluy* — physical danger from exposure to poison. Rav holds that *gilluy* does not in fact apply to non-Jewish beer, because *azil merora de-kashuta ve-kali leh* — the bitterness of the *kashuta* (hops) destroys and neutralizes any venom. So why should Chiya not drink it? *Hu dalaki* — Chiya is not of robust constitution. The bitterness of the hops, while neutralizing the venom, is itself harsh on a delicate system: *malki tfei* — it further injures someone already unwell. Therefore, for someone healthy, the beer is perfectly fine; for someone frail like Chiya, it is not advisable. This is a medical caution, not a *halakhic* prohibition.

### Non-Jewish Immunity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַשְּׁרָצִים יֵשׁ לָהֶן אֶרֶס, שֶׁל נָחָשׁ מֵמִית, שֶׁל שְׁרָצִים אֵינוֹ מֵמִית. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב: בַּר אַרְיָא, תָּא וְאֵימָא לָךְ מִילְּתָא מְעַלְּיָיתָא דַּהֲוָה אָמַר רַב אֲבוּךְ, הָכִי אָמַר אֲבוּךְ: הָנֵי אַרַמָּאֵי זוּקָאנֵי — (דַּהֲווֹ שָׁתוּ) [מִשּׁוּם דְּשָׁתוּ] גִּילּוּיָא, (וְלָא מִתוּ) [וְהַאי דְּלָא מָיְיתִי] — אַיְּידֵי דְּאָכְלִי שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים חֲבִיל גּוּפַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says: All creeping animals possess venom;** that **of a snake kills,** whereas the venom **of** other **creeping animals does not kill. Shmuel said to Ḥiyya bar Rav: Son of a lion! Come and I will say to you a superior matter that your father, Rav, said. This** is what **your father said: These Arameans** are **swollen [*zukanei*] because they drink exposed** liquids, **but they did not die** from doing so **since they eat repugnant creatures and creeping animals,** which **heat their bodies** and thereby render them less susceptible to the venom.

The *gemara* concludes with a teaching of Shmuel. First, a brief natural observation: *kol hashratzim yesh lahem eres* — all creeping creatures carry venom, but *shel nachash memis* — only the snake's venom is lethal; *shel shratzim eino memis* — other creatures' venom is not fatal. (The inclusion or exclusion of scorpions in this taxonomy is left unresolved.)

Shmuel then turns to Chiya bar Rav and addresses him: *bar arya* — son of lions — let me share with you something excellent that your father Rav said. Here is what Rav taught: *hanei armaei zukani, dehavu shatu gilluya velo meisu* — those non-Jews who are well-fed and robust, they drink from exposed liquids and they do not die. Why? *Aidi de'achli shkutzim u'remashim chavil gufeihu* — because they eat creeping things and other *shekutzim* [abominations], their bodies have already been damaged and desensitized, and they have consequently developed a tolerance for the poison that would kill a Jew. This, Shmuel reports, is what Rav himself taught — which is what makes it a prized, recovered teaching to share with Rav's own son.

The *daf* thus concludes with three interlocking themes: the typology of wine and its seals, which has practical consequences for the transport and storage of wine handled under non-Jewish supervision; the prohibition of non-Jewish beer and its rapid erosion in practice once its rationale is examined; and the background physiology — as the *gemara* understood it — of why non-Jews appear immune to dangers that would be lethal for Jews. The middle position in the wine typology — *assur bishtiyah umutar behana'ah* — remains somewhat underexplained; as noted, it represents a kind of halakhic compromise position between full prohibition and full permission, reflecting partial confidence rather than certainty about tampering, though the *gemara* never fully articulates the mechanism.
