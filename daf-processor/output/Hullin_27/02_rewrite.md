# Chullin 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Basic Rules

### Perek Bet Overview

The second perek of Masechet Chullin opens on 27a and takes up where many students might have expected the first perek to begin: with the act of shechita itself — what is required, where it must be performed, and how much must be cut. The first perek, as we know, focused on the kasherim and psulim, addressing who may perform shechita, with what instrument, and under what conditions. Here, by contrast, we finally turn to the mechanics of the act itself. That said, the Gemara does not remain purely technical; it uses the occasion as an opportunity for a range of abstract and conceptual inquiries, as we shall see.

### Mishnah: Simanim

The Mishnah opens with the rule: ha-shochet echad ba-of u-shnayim ba-beheimah — one who slaughters one siman, either the trachea or the esophagus, in birds, or two simanim in animals (more precisely, mammals), shechitato kesherah — the shechita is valid. The Mishnah then adds: v'rubo shel echad kamo-hu — the majority of one siman counts. One need not cut the full trachea or full esophagus; a majority suffices, as has been discussed previously and will be revisited again. The Mishnah continues with the ruling on half: if one cut exactly half — not a majority — of a siman in a bird, or one full siman and half of another in a mammal, shechitato pesulah — the shechita is invalid. This second case is notable: even though cutting one full siman plus half of another amounts to one and a half simanim cut in total, which exceeds the combined majority of each (roughly 1.2), since one did not cut a majority of the second siman it remains invalid. The Mishnah then restates the affirmative: rov echad ba-of v'rov shnayim ba-beheimah — a majority of one in a bird, a majority of two in a mammal — the shechita is valid.

### Rabbi Yehuda & Vridim

Rabbi Yehuda adds: ad she-yishchot et ha-vridim — one must also shecht the blood vessels in the neck, which are presumably the major vessels responsible for draining the blood, either the jugular vein or the carotid arteries. The precise identity of the vridim merits clarification: the word vridim is suggestive of veins, Rashi speaks of there being one on each side of the neck, and various anatomical illustrations identify them as both the jugular veins and the carotid arteries — a contradiction that requires further investigation. What is clear is that they are a major blood vessel in the neck, and it is worth noting that we actually pasken like Rabbi Yehuda, making this not merely an academic question.

The interesting question already in the Mishnah is whether Rabbi Yehuda considers the cutting of the vridim to be part of the act of shechita itself — implied by his use of the verb yishchot and by the apparent consequence that the shechita is invalid without it — or whether he is addressing a separate concern about removing blood from the meat before consumption. The language of the Mishnah strongly suggests that it is part of shechita, but the Gemara will rule otherwise: for Rabbi Yehuda, the cutting of the vridim is not part of the act of shechita but addresses a distinct problem of ensuring adequate removal of blood. The Gemara acknowledges that this reading is not intuitive from the Mishnah's formulation, and it grapples with that tension directly.

Additionally, the Gemara will limit Rabbi Yehuda's requirement to birds, not to mammals — which is somewhat counterintuitive, since one might have supposed that mammals, with their greater volume of blood, would require more rigorous blood removal. The Gemara's discussion of this follows.

### Majority Requirement

The Gemara opens by noting that the verb ha-shochet — one who slaughters — implies a b'di-eved context: post facto the shechita is valid, but le-chatechila something was lacking. This raises an immediate question: what could be le-chatechila deficient when one has cut the full two simanim in a mammal? One might understand a le-chatechila concern regarding birds — perhaps ideally one should cut both simanim even in a bird. But what more could one have been expected to do when one has cut both the kaneh and the weshet in their entirety?

The Gemara offers two resolutions. The first: eiba'it eima echad ba-of — the implication of b'di-eved is directed at the bird, meaning that le-chatechila one should cut both simanim in a bird as well, even though one suffices. The second resolution, which appears to be the Gemara's primary focus: eiba'it eima a'rubo shel echad kamo-hu — the le-chatechila concern is not with the first clause of the Mishnah at all but with the second, which permits a majority. That is, le-chatechila one should cut the entire siman; b'di-eved a majority suffices.

Rashi explains this le-chatechila requirement as a rabbinic concern: since the boundary between a majority and exactly half can be difficult to gauge with precision, one is asked le-chatechila to cut the entire siman so as to avoid any doubt. There is, however, a deeper conceptual question lurking here that has bearing not only on this halacha but on broader questions in shechita. How do we understand the fundamental obligation: is one's basic duty to cut fifty-one percent, with the majority being the baseline requirement? Or is the baseline obligation to cut the entire siman, with a majority counting as halachically equivalent to the whole — rubo shel echad kamo-hu? If the latter framing is correct, then even the act of cutting more than fifty percent is conceptually a stand-in for cutting the full siman. This in turn bears on the question of le-chatechila, because if conceptually the obligation is to cut the entire siman and the majority merely fulfills it in a diminished form, then of course le-chatechila one should do the full thing. It would also be relevant to cases where a psul — such as shehiya or derasah — occurs in the final forty-nine percent after the majority has already been cut: does that psul affect the shechita, or was the shechita effectively completed at the fifty-one percent mark?

## Location: The Neck

### Rav Kahana: Shach

Having established the basic rules, the Gemara turns to an entirely different question: how do we know that shechita must be performed at the neck or throat? This question has two dimensions: first, why not some other part of the animal entirely — why not stab it in the heart or the back? Second, within the neck, how do we know it must be the front of the throat rather than the back of the neck? Tosafot's reading of the entire subsequent sugya is largely organized around this second question.

Rav Kahana opens: minayin lishchita shehi min hatzavar — from where do we know that shechita is at the neck or throat? He cites the verse: ve-shachat et ben ha-bakar — he shall slaughter the calf, which appears in the context of a korban. From this he derives through a play on words: mi-makom she-shach chata'ehu — from where it bows, there you shall cleanse or validate it. The word shach evokes bowing or inclining, and if one observes a cow standing normally, its neck is angled downward — that is the part of its body that inclines. The word chata'ehu is read as meaning to cleanse or purify, with an analogy to other usages of the root chet-tet-alef in the sense of purification — as in ve-chite et ha-bayit from the purification of a house, or techate'eni be-ezov ve-etaher from the sprinkling of ashes of the Parah Adumah. The reading is therefore: from the place where the animal inclines, there you shall make it kasher or cleanse it.

### Shach Challenged

The Gemara challenges this derivation on multiple grounds. First: perhaps it refers to the tail, which also hangs downward perpetually. The Gemara responds: sach mi-klal she-zakuf — the word sach, which suggests bending, implies that this is a body part that is sometimes upright and sometimes bent. The tail is always hanging down and therefore cannot be the referent. The Gemara then asks: perhaps the ear? Animals can apparently bend their ears downward. The Gemara responds: be'inan dam ha-nefesh ve-leika — in the context of korbanot, the Torah emphasizes that shechita produces the blood of the soul, and cutting the ear would not produce that effect. The Gemara presses further: perhaps one starts at the ear and continues cutting until one reaches the blood of the soul? Here the Gemara shifts register. Tosafot reads the text as continuing with the word ve-le-ta'ameich — according to your own reasoning — rather than the standard ve-tu. The point becomes: if you challenge this derivation, you will have to concede that it fails. And if it fails, how do you know any of the laws of shechita from the Torah? How do you know the prohibitions of derasah, chaladah, hagramah, and ikkur? The answer: ela Gemara — we know them from tradition. And therefore: shechita min ha-tzavar nami Gemara — the requirement that shechita takes place at the neck is likewise a matter of tradition and cannot be derived from a verse.

### Chateihu & Gistra

If the verse cannot teach us the requirement of the neck from first principles, the Gemara asks: ve-kra le-mai ata — what, then, does the verse teach us? The answer: de-lo le-shaveih gistra — not to sever it completely in two, meaning not to chop through the backbone and divide the animal into two parts. Rashi explains this as a warning not to continue cutting after the simanim until the entire backbone is severed. Tosafot reads this differently and more consistently throughout the sugya: according to Tosafot, once we know from tradition that the shechita takes place at the neck, the verse comes to specify that it must be at the throat — the front of the neck — and not from the back of the neck, i.e., not gistra. Since the verse emphasizes where the animal inclines and connects this to the cleansing or removal of blood, it directs us to the throat, from which blood will flow most directly, rather than to the back of the neck.

It is worth pausing to note a striking irony that runs through all of these drashot: the deriving of the location of shechita is consistently oriented around blood — where the blood is, where it flows, how it is cleansed — and yet shechita in its technical halakhic form is about the trachea and esophagus, not about blood. One could in theory perform a perfectly valid shechita that produces very little blood, if the animal were to die of asphyxiation following the severing of the trachea. The Rabbanan do not make blood a formal requirement of shechita at all. Rabbi Yehuda introduces blood as an additional concern, but the Gemara will even bracket his position as relating not to the act of shechita itself but to a separate concern about preparing the meat. And yet the drashot that locate shechita at the throat are all built around the premise of blood flow. This tension is notable and unresolved.

### Rav Yeimar: Flow

Rav Yeimar offers a second derivation. The verse says ve-zavachta — you shall slaughter. He derives from a different play on words: mi-makom she-zav — from where it flows — chateihu — there cut it. Here the word chateihu is spelled with a tav rather than a tet, and the Gemara establishes that this root means to break or sever — as in the verse al tira ve-al techas, do not fear and do not be broken, where the root is read as connoting trembling or collapsing. So: from where the blood flows, there cut it, i.e., the throat. Once again the emphasis is on blood.

The Gemara challenges this as it challenged the previous derivation. Perhaps the nose — mucus flows from the nose? The Gemara responds: zav al yedei chitui ba'inan — we need something that flows as a result of being cut, not something that flows spontaneously. Perhaps the heart — if you want to ensure blood flows, cut at the heart? Again the Gemara invokes ve-le-ta'ameich and returns to the same conclusion: you cannot derive it from a verse, and we know it from tradition. The verse then teaches de-lo le-shaveih gistra — not to sever the animal from the back, but to proceed from the throat. Tosafot's consistent reading holds here as well: tradition gives us the neck; the verse refines this to the front of the throat, based on the emphasis on blood flow.

### Rabbi Yishmael: Sachat

A third derivation is offered from the school of Rabbi Yishmael. The verse says ve-shachat — do not read it as ve-shachat, slaughter, but as ve-sachat. The word sachat can mean squeeze, again evoking the release of blood. Alternatively, the drasha reads: mi-makom she-sach — from where the animal speaks, i.e., its throat — chateihu — there cut it. The Gemara challenges this as before: perhaps cut the tongue, since that is even more directly the organ of speech? The Gemara responds: be'inan dam ha-nefesh ve-leika — the context of korbanot demands blood of the soul, and cutting only the tongue will not produce that. The Gemara presses further — begin at the tongue and continue until you reach the blood of the soul? The same conclusion follows: we cannot derive the throat from a verse, and we know it from tradition. The verse teaches only that one must cut from the throat and not from the back of the neck.

What distinguishes this drasha from the previous two, however, is that it does not rely solely on the theme of blood. The trachea is the organ centrally associated with speech and with the act of shechita — the trachea being one of the two simanim. So at least this drasha, unlike the others, points toward the actual anatomical focus of shechita rather than blood removal, even if it arrives at the same conclusion about the front of the throat.

### Rabbi Chiya: Olah

Rabbi Chiya offers yet another derivation, this time from a ברייתא: minayin lishchita min ha-tzavar — how do we know shechita is from the neck? He cites a verse about the olah: ve-archu bnei Aharon ha-kohanim et ha-netachim et ha-rosh ve-et ha-pader — the kohanim shall arrange the pieces and the head and the fat. The question arises: why does the verse single out the head? The head is merely one of the pieces of the animal — it should be included in the general category of netachim. The Torah describes the process: flay the olah, cut it into pieces, place the pieces on the fire. But the head, as Rabbi Chiya argues, was not part of the same process of flaying and dismemberment. The head was already separated in the act of shechita, and therefore it conceptually falls outside the category of netachim produced by the subsequent flaying and severing. Hence the Torah must single it out explicitly to tell us that it too goes on the fire.

The Gemara then draws the inference: mi-klal de-shechita min ha-tzavar — evidently shechita is from the neck, since otherwise the head would not have been treated as already separately processed. The reasoning is not circular, despite initial appearances. The question is: why would the Torah ever need to single out the head from the other pieces? The only compelling explanation is that the head was already in some sense separated prior to the dismemberment process. And the only way that could be true is if shechita occurred at the neck, creating a significant degree of separation between the head and the body. Rashi acknowledges that the head is not fully severed — the backbone is not cut through — but argues that the degree of detachment is sufficient to render it conceptually separate.

The Gemara then resolves an internal tension in the ברייתא: the opening cites one verse about the head and fat, while the conclusion cites a slightly different verse using the possessive form its head and its fat. The resolution is that the two verses each teach something distinct. The first teaches that the head, despite not being dismembered together with the rest of the animal, is nonetheless placed on the fire. The second teaches that the head and fat are placed on the fire first, based on the juxtaposition of et rosho ve-et pidro ve-arach. And why does each verse mention fat alongside the head? A third ברייתא teaches: chofeh et ha-pader al beit ha-shechita uma'alehu — one covers the place of the severing with the fat before placing the head on the fire. The exposed site of the cut is not aesthetically fitting to be offered to God as is, and so the fat is used to cover it. Ve-zeh derech kavod shel ma'alah — this is a respectful manner of presenting it.

This drasha from the olah is more satisfying than the preceding word-plays. It requires no forced etymology; it reasons directly from the structure of the sacrificial procedure. One limitation Tosafot notes is that this drasha does not distinguish between the throat and the back of the neck — it tells us the neck, but not which side. The earlier drashot, for all their awkwardness, at least attempted to address that further specification through the emphasis on blood flow.

## Spectrum Model

### Torah Links Birds

The Gemara now presents a ברייתא concerning the verse zot torat ha-beheimah ve-ha-of — this is the law of the mammal and the bird — which appears in the context of tumah. The ברייתא asks: in what respect are mammals and birds ever comparable in terms of how they impart tumah? In fact, they are not comparable at all. A dead mammal — a nevela — conveys tumah through maga and masa, touch and carrying. A dead bird does not convey tumah in that way. Conversely, a dead bird that has not been slaughtered properly conveys tumah be-vet ha-bli'a — when it is swallowed and passing through the esophagus — and this tumah extends even to the garments one is wearing at the moment of swallowing, making them a rishon. A dead mammal does not convey tumah in this manner.

So the ברייתא asks: since the tumah laws of mammals and birds are so different, why does the Torah use a single heading — this is the law of the mammal and the bird — as if they share a common law? Lomar lach: mah beheimah bi-shechita af of bi-shechita — just as mammals require shechita, so too birds require shechita. And this is where the verse connecting them becomes relevant: in the context of tumah, a corpse is no longer a nevela if it has been properly slaughtered. The same holds for both mammals and birds. Shechita is the link.

One might then ask: should the equation be complete? Perhaps birds too require two simanim, exactly as mammals? Talmud lomar: zot — this, the limiting word, indicates that the equation is not total. Shechita is required for birds, but not in the identical manner as for mammals. This is the first of two readings of the verse; it establishes that birds require shechita but does not specify what shechita means for birds and does not provide a new source for the idea of shechita at the neck.

Rabbi Eliezer reads the same verse differently. He too asks: in what way are mammals and birds similar? And he answers: mah of hechshero min ha-tzavar af beheimah hechsheirah min ha-tzavar — just as the validation of a bird is from the neck, so too for a mammal. Where do we know that shechita for a bird is from the neck? From melikah. The Torah explicitly describes an act of ritual slaughter in only one place — the melikah of bird offerings, where it says u-malak oto mi-mul orpo — you shall perform melikah from the back of its neck. This is the single instance in which the Torah overtly describes a killing procedure for a korban. Rabbi Eliezer uses it as a model: since melikah is from the neck, and birds and mammals are linked in the verse, the same applies to mammalian shechita — it too is from the neck. Might we then say it must be from the back of the neck, as in melikah? The Torah distinguishes: the verse about melikah singles out that one — the bird korban — as being from the back of its neck, implying that other forms of slaughter are not from the back of the neck, but from the throat. Rabbi Eliezer's drasha is considered the most compelling of all those presented, because it is the one case in which the Torah does explicitly describe a slaughter procedure, and the derivation by analogy has real force.

The Gemara then asks: what does Rabbi Eliezer do with the limiting word zot in the verse? He uses it to prevent the complete equation from running in the other direction: without zot, one might have said — since we are equating birds and mammals — that just as a bird requires only one siman, so too a mammal requires only one siman. The word zot prevents this: the equation is only for the requirement of shechita at the neck, not for the number of simanim. The mammal remains at two.

### Bar Kappara: Spectrum

Bar Kappara taught a sweeping conceptual framework grounded in the same verse: zot torat ha-beheimah ve-ha-of, and the end of the pasuk adds u-ve-chol nefesh ha-chayah ha-romeset ba-mayim — and all living creatures in the water. The verse thus lists three categories: mammals, birds, and fish. Bar Kappara derives from this ordering a spectrum of shechita requirements. The Torah placed birds between mammals and fish. To require two simanim of a bird, as with a mammal, is impossible — shekevar hukash le-dagim — because on one side birds are linked to fish, and fish require no shechita at all. To exempt birds entirely from any shechita is equally impossible — shekevar hukash le-beheimah — because on the other side birds are linked to mammals. Ha keitzad — how does one resolve this? Hechshiro be-siman echad — one falls in the middle of the spectrum, and thus one siman suffices for a bird.

This is an elegant and systematic piece of reasoning, but it rests on two premises that still need to be established: that fish require zero shechita, and that mammals require two simanim. These endpoints define the spectrum. The Gemara will address the first; the second, as we shall see, remains grounded ultimately in tradition.

### Fish & Asifa

How do we know fish require no formal shechita? The Gemara cites the verse in which Moshe speaks to God about feeding Bnei Yisrael: ha-tzon u-vakar yishachet lahem ve-im et kol degei ha-yam ye'asef lahem u-matza lahem — if you slaughter all the cattle and flock for them, and if you gather all the fish of the sea for them, will it be enough? The parallel structure is telling: cattle and flock are slaughtered; fish are merely gathered. Ba-asifa be-alma sagi lehu — gathering alone suffices for fish.

The Gemara challenges this: the word gather is also used in connection with the slav — the quail — as in va-ya'asfu et ha-slav. Would we then say quail requires no shechita? The Gemara distinguishes: in that case, the word gather does not appear in parallel to slaughter performed on other creatures. Here, by contrast, the gathering of fish is explicitly set against the slaughtering of cattle and flock. The parallel is the point; gathering in the verse about fish is the functional equivalent of what slaughter is for mammals. Therefore, ba-asifa be-alma sagi lehu — for fish, mere gathering from the water is sufficient, and no formal shechita is required.

Some have playfully entertained the question of whether the act of asifa — gathering from the water — is itself the required act for fish, raising the theoretical puzzle of what one's status would be if one were to eat a fish directly in the water without ever removing it. The simple reading of the Gemara, however, is unambiguous: lav bnei shechita ninhu — fish are simply not subject to the concept of shechita at all.

### Birds: One Siman

With fish established at zero and mammals understood to require two simanim — a point that, as the Gemara will acknowledge, is itself ultimately rooted in tradition — Bar Kappara's framework is complete. Birds, positioned between these two endpoints by the Torah's own formulation in the verse, require one siman. The framework is clean and appealing, though the Gemara will note tomorrow that the mammals-require-two end of the spectrum is likewise not derivable from a verse and must be learned from tradition.

## Birds from Mud

### Galilean Drasha

An itinerant Galilean preacher — an over Gelila'ah, a transient darshan whose name is not even recorded — offers a theological grounding for the same spectrum. His framework is not hermeneutical but cosmological: the number of simanim required reflects the substance from which each creature was created. Behemah shenivret min ha-yabashah — mammals were created from solid earth — and therefore require two simanim; they possess the most substantial and robust form of life. Dagim shenivru min ha-mayim — fish were created from water — and are the least substantial, and therefore hechshiran be-lo klum — require nothing. Of shenivra min ha-rakak — birds were created from mud, a mixture of earth and water — and therefore occupy a middle position, requiring one siman. The shechita requirement tracks the ontological weight of the creature.

### Scales on Legs

Rav Shmuel Kaputka adds an empirical observation in support of the claim that birds were created from mud: teida she-harei ofot yesh bahem kaskeset be-ragleihem ke-dagim — you can verify this because birds have a type of scales on their legs, similar to fish scales. The legs of a chicken, for instance, exhibit a scaly texture reminiscent of fish scales — a visible remnant of the birds' dual origin from earth and water, the same mud that was their source of creation.

It is worth pausing at this juncture to note another conceptual puzzle raised in passing. The continuum framework — more substantial life requires more simanim — raises a question about the esophagus specifically. Cutting the trachea produces rapid death through asphyxiation. But cutting the esophagus, in isolation, does not directly kill the animal; death would follow only after a prolonged period of starvation. The connection between shechita and immediate killing is therefore not fully symmetrical across both simanim. The Gemara appears to assume that greater vitality demands more cutting, but the mechanism by which the esophagus contributes to death as part of the shechita act is not self-evident from a biological standpoint. This is not a question the Gemara resolves here, but it is worth holding in mind.

### Two Narratives

The Gemara now presents the origin of the claim that birds were created from mud, through a story about Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai. A certain Roman official — referred to as a hēgemōn — posed to Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai a contradiction between two verses in Bereishit. Perek 1 states: va-yomer Elokim yishretzu ha-mayim sheretz nefesh chaya ve-of ye'ofef — God said let the waters teem with living creatures, and birds flying above. This verse clearly implies that birds were created from water. Perek 2, however, states: va-yitzer Hashem Elokim min ha-adama kol chayat ha-sadeh ve-et kol of ha-shamayim — God formed from the ground all the animals of the field and all the birds of the sky. This verse attributes the creation of birds to the ground.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai's initial answer was: min ha-rakak nivre — they were created from mud, which is a mixture of water and earth, thereby harmonizing both verses. But when his students looked at one another in astonishment — apparently finding this answer dismissive — Rabbi Yochanan explained himself. He told them: kasha be-eineichem she-dachiti et oyvi be-kaneh — is it troubling to you that I dismissed my antagonist with a reed, a straw? The implication is that he gave the hēgemōn a deflecting answer rather than his real one.

### Mud Resolution

The real answer, which Rabbi Yochanan gave his students, is that the two narratives in Bereishit are serving different purposes. Perek 1 describes the process of creation and is specific: birds emerged from the water. Perek 2, by contrast, is a narrative centered on man — ve-lama hevi'an el ha-adam? likro lahem shem — it describes how God brought the animals and birds to man so that man could name them. Perek 2 is not offering a precise account of how each creature came into being; it is describing the relationship between man and the created world. Therefore, the phrase min ha-adama in perek 2 need not be taken as a precise statement of how birds were formed, and there is no contradiction.

However, the Gemara then records a minority view that reverses the attribution of the two answers: vi-yesh omrim, some say that Rabbi Yochanan gave the mud answer to his students as the genuine resolution, not as a dismissal. According to this view, since perek 2 does say that God formed from the ground — and this cannot simply be ignored with respect to birds — the truth is that both texts are correct in their own way: perek 1 says water, perek 2 says ground. The resolution is mud. Birds were created from the rakak, the muddy swamp, that mixture of earth and water in which the two elements combine. And it is precisely this hybrid origin that places birds in the middle of the ontological spectrum, between the earth-rooted mammals and the water-born fish, and that grounds the requirement of one siman — the middle point between two and zero.
