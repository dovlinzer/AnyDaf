# Kiddushin 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hebrew Slaves Framework

### Amah vs. Eved Overview

The eighteenth daf of Kiddushin picks up at the bottom of 17b, in the middle of a discussion concerning the *amah ivriyah* — the Hebrew maidservant — and specifically how a Hebrew slave goes free, as well as the differences between a male and a female slave. The *mitzvah* of *hanakah*, the obligation to laden a departing slave with gifts at the time of release, has already been treated; now the Gemara turns to a new clause.

### No Service to Non-Heirs

The Mishnah states: *amah ivriyah einah ovedet lo et haben velo et habat* — a Hebrew maidservant does not serve either the son or the daughter of the master. To situate this properly, it helps to draw out the full comparative framework that emerges from the prior discussion. An *eved ivri* — a male Hebrew slave — who has not undergone the ear-piercing of *nirtza* continues to serve out his six-year term for the master's son, since the son is considered a direct continuation of the father. The six-year arrangement is understood as something like a contract, and a son is its natural continuation. However, the *eved ivri* does not serve other heirs — a daughter or more distant relatives — because a contract is between persons, not a heritable commodity; you finish a contract for the son as a direct continuation of the father, but not for some other heir who stands in a more attenuated relationship.

A *nirtza*, by contrast — the slave who has chosen to have his ear pierced and to remain in perpetual service — serves neither the son nor any other heir. His service, which is framed around a bond of love to his master's household rather than a commercial transaction, terminates at the master's death. And an *amah ivriyah*, the Mishnah now tells us, is likewise governed by this rule: she serves neither the son nor the daughter.

The case of the daughter is worth pausing over. A daughter can, in certain circumstances, inherit the estate when there is no son. And yet she is not considered a "continuation" of the father in the relevant legal sense. Inheritance and legal continuation are distinct categories here: the law of inheritance does not translate automatically into the relational standing required for the slave's contractual obligation to transfer.

### Deriving from Nirtza

The Gemara asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the textual source for the rule that an *amah ivriyah* serves neither the son nor the daughter? Rav Pappa citing Rav Adda bar Ahavah points to the verse *ve'af la'amatcha ta'aseh ken* — "and so shall you do for your maidservant as well." This phrase appears in the passage in Devarim dealing with the release of Hebrew slaves and the obligations of *hanakah*, and it is immediately adjacent to — indeed embedded within — the context of *nirtza*, the ear-piercing ceremony.

The Gemara establishes the derivation through a *hatkasha*, a juxtaposition: *hekisho hakatuv lenirtza* — the Torah has linked the *amah ivriyah* to the laws of *nirtza*. Just as a *nirtza* does not serve the son or the daughter, so too an *amah ivriyah* does not serve the son or the daughter: *af amah ivriyah einah ovedet lo et haben velo et habat*.

But the Gemara immediately presses: is this really what *ve'af la'amatcha ta'aseh ken* is coming to teach? There is a *baraita* which reads this same phrase as the source for the obligation of *hanakah* — the gift-loading at release — as it applies to an *amah ivriyah*. Could it truly be teaching both things at once?

The Gemara works through this carefully. According to the plain sense of the verses (*pshat*), one might have thought that *ve'af la'amatcha ta'aseh ken* means she too undergoes *retzia*, the ear-piercing. But a verse in Shemot that introduces the ear-piercing ritual uses the expression *ha'eved* — "the slave" — with a specific masculine article, from which the rabbis derive *v'lo amah ivriyah*: a female Hebrew slave does not undergo *retzia*. Once the possibility of *retzia* is excluded by the verse in Shemot, the phrase in Devarim must be serving a different purpose. The Gemara states: *hamani mikayem v'af la'amatcha ta'aseh ken — l'haanik* — I read this as the source for the obligation of *hanakah* for an *amah ivriyah*.

### Double Derivation Issue

This raises what the Gemara treats as a problem of double derivation. If *ve'af la'amatcha ta'aseh ken* is already employed to teach *hanakah*, how can it simultaneously be used as the *hatkasha* to *nirtza* to teach the rule about not serving the son or the daughter?

The Gemara's resolution is elegant: *im kein, nichtov kra l'amascha kein* — if it only needed to convey one lesson, the verse could simply have said "and for your maidservant thus." The presence of the word *ta'aseh* — "you shall do" — is textually superfluous and therefore carries independent meaning. From this extra word, *shma mina tarti* — you may infer two separate legal conclusions: the obligation of *hanakah*, and the equation of the *amah ivriyah* with the *nirtza* regarding service to heirs.

It is also worth noting that even without this derivation via the extra word, the proximity of the phrase to the *nirtza* passage could itself serve as the basis for the *hatkasha*, as adjacency in the text (*smuchot*) is itself sometimes sufficient to support a derivation. But the Gemara here takes the path of finding the dual teaching in the single word.

## Nirtza & Non-Jewish Sale

### Nirtza Non-Service

With the rule for the *amah ivriyah* established through the *hatkasha* to *nirtza*, the Gemara now must prove the underlying premise: that a *nirtza* himself does not serve the son or the daughter. This had been assumed in the derivation above, and now the Gemara supplies the textual support.

The source is the verse *v'ratza adonav et ozno bamartze'a v'avado l'olam* — "his master shall pierce his ear with an awl, and he shall serve him forever." The word *l'olam* appears to create unlimited service, but the Gemara limits it in two ways: first by *yovel*, the Jubilee year, which terminates even *nirtza* service, and second by reading *l'olam* as bound to the master personally — not to the master's heirs. The term *avado*, "his serving," refers to the master himself. Because the verse driving perpetual service is specific to the person of the master, there is no basis for extending it to a son or daughter. The reason that the regular *eved ivri* does serve the son is the verse *sheish shanim ya'avod* — "he shall serve six years" — which speaks without qualification and therefore extends through the son as a continuation of the father. That verse does not govern the *nirtza*, and so the *nirtza*'s service is tethered solely to the living master.

### Non-Jew Sale Rule

The Gemara next addresses a related case: a Hebrew slave sold to a non-Jew. The rule is stated: *hanirtza v'hanimkar l'oved kochavim eino oved lo et haben velo et habat* — neither the *nirtza* nor one sold to a non-Jew serves the son or the daughter. The source for the non-Jew case is derived by Chizkiya from the verse *v'chishav im konehu* — "he shall reckon with his purchaser" regarding the right and obligation to redeem such a slave. Crucially, the verse says *im konehu* — with his purchaser — and not with his purchaser's heirs. From this exclusion, Chizkiya derives that once the non-Jewish master dies, the slave goes free; there is no ongoing service owed to heirs.

### Rava's Inference

Rava draws a broader legal principle from this exclusion. The very fact that the Torah had to specify that the slave does not serve the heirs of a non-Jewish master implies that, as a baseline, non-Jews do have laws of inheritance — because otherwise there would have been nothing to exclude. If non-Jews had no heritable estate, the slave's status with heirs would never have been a question in the first place. As Rava states: *devar torah oved kochavim yoresh et aviv* — it is a biblical principle that a non-Jew inherits from his non-Jewish father. The necessity of the exclusion proves the existence of the rule from which the exclusion departs.

## Non-Jew Inheritance Laws

### Non-Jew Inherits Father

From Rava's inference, the Gemara pivots into a broader discussion of inheritance law as it applies to non-Jews and converts. Rava's conclusion — *oved kochavim yoresh et aviv* — is presented as a biblical proposition. The textual proof he adduces is indirect, derived from the logical structure of the exclusion in the verse about the purchased slave. But the Gemara will also present a more direct scriptural basis.

### Convert Rabbinical Inher…

A convert presents a distinct case. The halakhic principle is *kekatan shenolad dami* — a convert is legally like a newborn child. This means that all prior legal family relationships are severed. When a man converts, his non-Jewish father is no longer legally his father; consequently, when the non-Jewish father dies, the convert does not inherit him as a matter of biblical law.

However, the rabbis enacted that a convert does inherit his non-Jewish father, and this is categorized as *midevrei sofrim* — rabbinic legislation rather than biblical law. The Mishnah provides the case: a convert and his brother who did not convert are jointly inheriting from their non-Jewish father. The convert can say to his non-Jewish brother: you take the idols (*avodah zarah*) from the estate and I will take the money — you take the wine used for idolatrous libation (*yayin nesech*, which is *assur b'hana'ah*, forbidden even to benefit from) and I will take the fruit.

### Forbidden Items Division

This division is permitted because it is understood as a carving out of one's share rather than a transaction involving forbidden items. However — and this is a critical distinction — this is only permitted *before* they take physical possession of the estate. Once the convert has taken possession of his share, he can no longer arrange a swap in which he gives up his portion of forbidden items in exchange for permitted items, because at that point he would effectively be selling *avodah zarah* for money, which is itself forbidden.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinical

The Gemara draws a striking inference from this halakhic distinction between before and after physical possession. One might ask: if the convert inherits his non-Jewish father as a matter of biblical law, then the moment the father dies, the inheritance vests immediately and automatically. There would be no meaningful legal gap between the moment of death and the moment of possession — the convert would be considered to own his share of the estate the instant his father dies. In that case, any arrangement dividing the forbidden items from the permitted ones would already constitute a transaction in *avodah zarah*, and the distinction between "before possession" and "after possession" would be legally vacuous.

The fact that the Gemara does treat this as a meaningful distinction — the fact that there is legal space between death and possession — proves that the convert's inheritance is *only* rabbinic. Rabbinically, the sages could structure the inheritance in a way that gives the convert the latitude to ensure he does not end up taking possession of forbidden items. They could, in effect, hold the vesting of the estate in abeyance until physical possession, so that the convert's reckoning of his share happens at that moment rather than at the moment of the father's death. This is only possible at a rabbinic level; a biblical inheritance vests automatically at death and admits of no such flexibility.

### Tosafot on Breira

Tosafot raises an important question here. Why doesn't the arrangement work even after physical possession, on the principle of *yesh breira* — the retroactive clarification doctrine according to which a later determination can retrospectively define what was always the case? If *yesh breira* applied, one could say that when the two brothers divide the estate, it becomes retroactively clarified that each person's portion was always his — so the convert never "owned" the idols even briefly.

Tosafot rejects this reasoning with precision: *d'im ken afilu bau l'reshuso nami eima mehai ta'ama* — if *breira* were the operative principle, it should work even *after* physical possession, since the retrospective clarification would apply regardless of when the division occurs. The fact that the Gemara distinguishes between before and after possession proves that *breira* is not the mechanism at work. What matters is specifically the gap between death and possession, and that gap only exists in a rabbinic framework.

### Non-Jew Inherits Convert

The Gemara then surveys several related permutations. First: can a non-Jew inherit from a convert father? This scenario involves a father who converts while his child does not. When the convert father subsequently dies, does his non-Jewish child inherit? The answer is no, because after the father's conversion, he is legally like a newborn, severing his prior paternal relationship. The non-Jewish child has no legal claim on the estate.

### Convert Inherits Convert

Second: can a convert inherit from a convert father — a case where both father and son converted together? Even here, because both underwent conversion and became legally as newborns at that moment, their prior familial relationship is extinguished. Neither biblical nor rabbinic inheritance applies, because there is no *gezeirah d'avdu rabanan* — no rabbinic enactment — motivated by any practical concern requiring the creation of inheritance in this case. As the Gemara notes, the rabbis generally do not create inheritance; the one exception was the case of the convert inheriting his non-Jewish father, and that was motivated by a specific concern: *shema yachzor lesuro* — lest the convert, upon learning he will receive no share of his father's estate, be tempted to abandon his conversion and return to his prior religion. No comparable concern drives the creation of inheritance between two converts.

Moreover, creating inheritance between legally unrelated individuals — even those who are biologically related — would muddy the legal waters in ways that could generate serious errors. Biological relationship and legal relationship must be kept clearly distinct, because confusing them could produce errors in areas such as *yibbum* (levirate marriage) or other domains where precise legal parentage is critical.

### Debt Repayment Ethics

The Mishnah illustrates this with a practical case: if one borrowed money from a convert who then converts together with his sons — and the convert subsequently dies — the borrower is not obligated to repay the sons, since they are not the deceased's legal heirs. The Mishnah adds: *ve'im hechzir ein ruach chachamim nocha heimenu* — if one did repay them, the sages are not pleased with him.

Rashi's reading of this phrase is that it merely withholds approval rather than leveling a prohibition — no *yasher koach* is forthcoming, but it is not necessarily forbidden. However, the plain language suggests active disapproval. The reason for the disapproval, as the Gemara makes clear, is not that the money was wasted but that such conduct muddies the legal relationship. Making voluntary payment to someone in the position of an heir reinforces — incorrectly — the impression that a legal inheritance relationship exists.

A *baraita* appears to contradict this, stating that *ruach chachamim nocha heimenu* — the sages *are* pleased. The Gemara resolves the contradiction: the two sources are speaking of different cases.

### Conversion & Parentage

The resolution depends on a fundamental distinction in the laws of parentage following conversion: *horato velidato shelo bikhdusha* versus *horato shelo bikhdusha velidato bikhdusha*. In the first case, both conception and birth occurred before anyone was Jewish — the father and son converted after the son was already born. Here the severance of legal relationship is total and unambiguous. In the second case, a man and his pregnant wife convert together, and the child is subsequently born Jewish. The child's conception was outside of holiness, but the birth occurred within it.

The halakhic consequence of the second scenario is nuanced. After the mother immerses in the *mikvah*, the child continues to gestate within her womb. This ongoing physical relationship with the mother, which continues post-conversion, is sufficient to establish a legal maternal bond. The child is therefore legally his mother's child. However, the father's biological connection to the child was completed at the moment of conception — a moment that preceded the conversion. When the parents converted and became as newborns, the father's legal relationship to the child was not renewed, because no new biological act of fathering occurred post-conversion. The child is thus legally related to his Jewish mother but not to his Jewish father.

This case — *horato shelo bikhdusha velidato bikhdusha* — looks much more like an ordinary Jewish family. The child was born Jewish, the mother is the legal mother, and a casual observer might assume the father is also the legal father. For precisely this reason, the question of whether to repay the debt to the child carries different weight in each scenario. 

Tosafot cites an early version of Rashi's commentary — no longer extant — according to which the case where repayment is praised (*ruach chachamim nocha heimenu*) is the case of *horato shelo bikhdusha velidato bikhdusha*, since the child appears closer to a natural Jewish heir. Tosafot disagrees, arguing the opposite: *adrabba*, that is precisely the case where repayment should *not* be praised, because it most easily causes confusion. Since that scenario looks indistinguishable from a normal Jewish father-son relationship, voluntary payment to the child in the role of heir would most powerfully reinforce the mistaken impression of a legal inheritance. The case where the child was also born before conversion — *horato velidato shelo bikhdusha* — is so clearly not a legal inheritance relationship that no confusion results from an act of generosity, and there the sages can look favorably upon it.

### Rav Chiya's Sources

Rav Chiya bar Avin citing Rabbi Yochanan takes up the question of the biblical basis for non-Jewish inheritance, which Rava had derived indirectly from the exclusion in the verse about the slave. Rav Chiya bar Avin prefers to derive it from explicit scriptural testimony: *ki yerushah l'Eisav nasati et Har Seir* — "for I have given Mount Seir as an inheritance to Esav." The term *yerushah* — inheritance — appears explicitly in connection with Esav, who is a non-Jew.

The Gemara challenges this: perhaps Esav is a special case, since he is a *Yisrael mumar* of sorts, being the son of Yitzchak and thus descended from the patriarchal line, even though he personally did not maintain that covenant identity. Perhaps the rules of inheritance were extended to him out of that particular ancestral dignity rather than as a general principle applicable to all non-Jews.

To avoid this objection, the Gemara cites a second verse: *ki livnei Lot nasati et Ar yerushah* — "for I have given Ar as an inheritance to the children of Lot." Lot was not a descendant of Yitzchak or Yaakov, and there is no comparable ancestral dignity to invoke. The explicit use of *yerushah* here establishes that non-Jewish inheritance is a general biblical principle.

The Gemara then examines why each Amora did not adopt the other's approach. Rav Chiya bar Avin did not employ Rava's inference from the slave verse because, he argues, the exclusion of *yorshei konahu* (heirs of the purchaser) is not explicit in the verse — it requires inference — and he would rather anchor the principle of non-Jewish inheritance in an explicit *pasuk* using the term *yerushah*. Rava, for his part, did not rely on the verses about Esav and Lot because he considers them inconclusive: in the case of Esav, *mishum kevodo d'Avraham shani* — one might argue that it was out of honor for Avraham that his grandson received this bequest, and that does not prove a universal principle. Ultimately both authorities arrive at the same substantive conclusion, each finding it supported by their preferred textual strategy.

## Eved-Amah Comparison

### Freedom Methods Listed

The Gemara now presents a *baraita* that compares the laws of the *eved ivri* and the *amah ivriyah* systematically: *yesh b'Ivri she'ein b'Ivriyah v'yesh b'Ivriyah she'ein b'Ivri* — there are rules that apply to the male Hebrew slave that do not apply to the female, and vice versa.

The *baraita* states: *yesh b'Ivri shehu yotzei b'shanim u'v'yovel u'v'misas ha'adon, ma she'ein kein b'Ivriyah* — the male slave goes free through the completion of six years (*shanim*), through the Jubilee (*yovel*), and through the death of the master (*misas ha'adon*), none of which applies to the *amah ivriyah*. Conversely, the *amah ivriyah* goes free through the appearance of physical signs of maturity (*simanim*), which the male slave does not. Furthermore, the *amah ivriyah* cannot be resold (*einah nimkeres l'shneis*), and she can be redeemed against the will of a relevant party (*mifdin otah al karcho*).

This *baraita* is striking on multiple counts. Everything it says seems to conflict with what has been assumed throughout the preceding discussion. We have been operating on the assumption that *shanim*, *yovel*, and *misas ha'adon* apply equally to both the *eved ivri* and the *amah ivriyah*, and the verse *ve'af la'amatcha ta'aseh ken* was the textual basis for that equation. Yet this *baraita* appears to deny it entirely. And the claim that an *amah ivriyah* can be redeemed against someone's will seems puzzling — the entire concept of *giron kesef* (proportional buyback) already permits redemption of a male slave against his master's wishes.

### Yiudah Interpretation

The Gemara challenges the *baraita* immediately: does the *amah ivriyah* really not go free through *shanim* and *yovel*? Does our Mishnah not teach *yeseirah alav amah ivriyah* — that the *amah ivriyah* has *more* paths to freedom than the *eved ivri*, not fewer — since she has all the methods available to him *plus* the additional path of *simanim*?

Rav Sheshet resolves the tension by positing that the *baraita* is discussing a case where the master has already performed *yiud* — formal designation of the *amah ivriyah* as a wife. Once *yiud* has occurred, she transitions from the status of a slave to the status of a married woman. As a married woman, the ordinary mechanisms of slave release — *shanim*, *yovel*, *misas ha'adon* — no longer apply. She can only be released through a *get*, a bill of divorce.

The Gemara presses: is this not obvious? If she has become an *eshet ish* through *yiud*, of course she requires a *get* rather than the slave-release mechanisms. The Gemara answers that the *chidush* is not as self-evident as it seems. One might have thought that *yiud* creates a form of marriage that remains embedded within the framework of servitude — analogous, perhaps, to the relationship between an *eved ivri* and the *shifcha kena'anit* given to him by his master, which is a union that exists within and by virtue of the context of slavery. If *yiud* were such a relationship — one that belongs to the category of servitude rather than constituting a full-fledged *kiddushin* — then the mechanisms of slave release might still apply, since what would need to be dissolved is, in essence, a slave status rather than a marriage. The Torah's ruling that *she'era k'suta v'onata lo yigra* — that a wife acquired through *yiud* retains all the rights of a full wife — is precisely what refutes this *hava amina* and establishes that *yiud* creates genuine marriage.

The implication then follows: *im lo yi'ada yotza af b'simanim* — if the master did not perform *yiud*, then all the ordinary paths of release apply, including the additional path of *simanim*.

It must be acknowledged that this resolution is a forced reading of the *baraita*. The *baraita* explicitly contrasts *shanim* and *yovel* with *simanim* — it says she does not go free through the former but does go free through the latter. Reconciling this by saying the *baraita* is only discussing the post-*yiud* case, in which she goes free with *simanim* but not with *shanim* and *yovel*, strains the plain reading considerably. More naturally, the *baraita* reflects a distinct *tannaic* tradition that conceived of the *amah ivriyah* as standing in a fundamentally different legal category from the *eved ivri*, one with minimal overlap in their respective paths to freedom. Given that the *pesukim* in Shemot dealing with the *amah ivriyah* never explicitly mention *shanim* or *yovel*, and that the apparent equation comes only through the verse *ve'af la'amatcha ta'aseh ken* applied within the context of *hanakah*, there is something textually coherent and even attractive about this *baraita*'s approach. If one thinks about the actual circumstance of an *amah ivriyah* — a girl sold into servitude before the age of twelve — the fewer the overlapping laws of slavery between her situation and that of an adult male slave, the more her legal status reflects something categorically distinct from classic *avdut*. The Gemara, however, reconciles the *baraita* with the predominant position, even at some cost to the plain reading.

### No Resale of Amah

The *baraita* also states that the *amah ivriyah* cannot be resold — *einah nimkeres l'shneis* — while an *eved ivri* can be resold. This is a new *halakhah* that has not yet been addressed. The Gemara signals that the broader treatment of this rule is coming, and the subsequent analysis will spell out the details.

### Eved Resale Limits

Before arriving at the full treatment of the *amah ivriyah*'s resale prohibition, the Gemara addresses an apparent contradiction: does it not also teach that even an *eved ivri* faces limits on resale? A *baraita* states: *begneivato v'lo b'khiflo* — he is sold for his theft, but not for the fine. If a thief stole something worth a thousand and has sufficient assets to pay back the principal (*keren*) but not the double payment (*kefel*), he is not sold into slavery to cover the outstanding penalty. Further: *begneivato v'lo b'zimamo* — he is sold for his own theft, but not if he is an *ed zomem*, a conspiring witness who testified that someone else owed a penalty and was shown to have lied. Even though the *ed zomem* must pay *ka'asher zamam la'asot l'achiv* — whatever he plotted to do to his fellow — being sold into slavery does not apply to him. And crucially: *keivan shenimkar pa'am achat shuv i ata rashai l'mokhro* — once he has been sold one time for a particular theft, he cannot be sold again. The singular *begneivato* implies a single act of being sold.

So the *eved ivri* is also subject to a form of non-resale restriction, at least in the context of *genevah*. How does this square with the *baraita*'s implication that an *eved ivri* can be resold while an *amah ivriyah* cannot?

Rava resolves this: *lo kashya, kan b'gneivah achat, kan b'shtei gneivot* — there is no contradiction; the two cases are different. The distinction concerns whether we are speaking of one *gneivah* or two. Here Tosafot and Rashi read the resolution in opposite directions, and the difference tracks a textual variant in the Gemara.

According to Tosafot's reading: for a *single* act of theft (*gneivah achat*), if the value of the thief's service does not cover the full amount owed, he can be sold multiple times — sold, completes his term, is sold again — until the debt is paid off. This is consistent with the singular *begneivato*: it is one *gneivah*, and he keeps being sold for that one act. However, for a *second* act of theft — a separate *gneivah* — he cannot be sold. He has already entered the condition of *eved ivri* once, and that experience and its lessons are held to be sufficient; a new theft does not reset his eligibility for a second enslavement.

Rashi reads it in precisely the reverse: for one *gneivah*, even a large one, he can only be sold once — one sale per act of theft. But for each separate *gneivah* from a separate party, he can be sold once per offense.

Abaye responds to Rava's formulation by noting that the word *begneivato* in the verse actually suggests multiple possible readings and that the plain language implies a broad scope. Abaye therefore reformulates the resolution differently: *kan b'adam echad, kan b'shnei bnei adam* — the distinction is not between one *gneivah* and two, but between whether the theft was from one person or from two different people. According to Tosafot's reading of Abaye's position, if someone steals once or multiple times from a single person, it is all treated as a single category of *gneivah* from that person, and the person may be sold — and resold — for that entire category. But if he has stolen from a second, different person, he cannot be sold for that second offense. Rashi reads Abaye's resolution in the opposite direction.

A further *baraita* reinforces the analysis with concrete cases. If one stole something worth a thousand and is himself worth five hundred, he can be sold and sold again (*nimkar vechozer venimkar*) until the debt is covered — since for one *gneivah* multiple sales are permitted. If one stole something worth five hundred and is himself worth a thousand, *eino nimkar klal* — he is not sold at all. It would be unjust to compel someone to serve six years of slavery when the market value of that service exceeds the debt; there is no mechanism for partial terms of servitude. One is either sold for the full six years or not sold at all.

The Gra's variant text (*girsa*) reads *nimkar v'eino chozer venimkar* — sold but not resold — which is the foundation for Rashi's contrary reading. The analysis presented here follows the standard text as Tosafot reads it.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

Rabbi Eliezer takes an even more restrictive position, one that Rava acknowledges as logically compelling even if it leads to a near-total elimination of the practice. Rabbi Eliezer says: *im haya gneivo keneged mimkaro nimkar* — the thief is only sold into slavery if the value of his six-year service is *exactly* equal to the value of what he stole. If the *gneivah* is worth more than his service, he is not sold (everyone agrees on this). But if his service is worth more than the *gneivah*, Rabbi Eliezer also says he is not sold.

The logical basis for Rabbi Eliezer's position, as Rava articulates it, is entirely parallel to the reasoning underlying the universally agreed-upon case. Everyone agrees that if the thief is worth a thousand and the theft was worth five hundred, he is not sold — because the verse says *v'nimkar bignevato*, "he is sold *for* his theft," and the Gemara reads this as requiring that the entirety of the sale value goes toward paying off the theft. If only half his sale value goes to the theft, that is not selling him *for* his theft in the required sense. By precisely the same logic, Rabbi Eliezer argues, if the theft is worth a thousand but his service is only worth five hundred, he is not sold — because again, he would be sold for only half his theft. Selling him would not pay off the *gneivah* in full, and the verse requires that being sold suffice to discharge the debt. Rava comments: *beha zachinhu Rabbi Eliezer lerabbanan* — Rabbi Eliezer's logic outshines the rabbis here, in the sense that the textual reasoning is fully consistent and compelling, even though in practice it means that an *eved ivri* sold for *genevah* becomes an almost theoretical category.

### Forced Redemption

Returning to the *baraita*'s final clause — *umifdin otah al karcho*, that the *amah ivriyah* is redeemed against someone's will — Rava initially understands this as meaning against the will of the *master*: one can compel the master to accept the return of the purchase price and release her. But Abaye rejects this reading. Forcing back a redemption against the master's will — returning his money and recovering the slave — is already applicable to an *eved ivri* through the mechanism of *giron kesef*. There is nothing distinctive about the *amah ivriyah* in that respect. Furthermore, Abaye notes, if it means forcing the master to accept an IOU rather than actual payment, that too is untenable: *nakit marganita beyadei, nehavi lei chaspa?* — the master is holding a pearl in his hand; are we going to force him to accept a pottery shard? The father who sold his daughter presumably had no money to begin with; any IOU he could offer would be essentially worthless.

Abaye therefore reinterprets: *be'al korcho de'av* — the forced redemption is against the will of the *father*. If the father has available funds, he can be compelled to use them to redeem his daughter from slavery. The motivation is *mishum pgam mishpacha* — the concern for family honor. Having a daughter in servitude reflects badly on the entire family, and the relatives — or the father himself, per Rashi — can be legally coerced to undo it.

The Gemara then raises an objection: if this is the reasoning, why not similarly coerce the family to redeem a male *eved ivri* who sold himself? The answer: *hadar azil umazvin nafshieh* — he would simply turn around and sell himself again. Forcing a buyback accomplishes nothing if the person can immediately repeat the act.

The same objection seems to apply to the *amah ivriyah*: if the father is forced to redeem her, could he not immediately resell her? This is precisely where the earlier ruling becomes operative. The Gemara answers: *katani einah nimkeres ushenit* — we have already established that an *amah ivriyah* cannot be resold. Therefore, compelling the father to redeem his daughter is a meaningful and lasting act: once he buys her back, he cannot sell her again, and the family honor is permanently restored. By contrast, there is no corresponding restriction preventing an *eved ivri* from reselling himself, so forcing a redemption of a male slave would accomplish nothing permanent.

Tosafot sharpens this by disagreeing with Rashi about *who* is being compelled. Tosafot understands the compulsion as being directed not at the father but at the other family members — along the lines of the biblical provision that a kinsman may redeem a sold relative (*ben dodo o dodo yigalenu*). The family members are compelled to act for the sake of communal honor. The concern about the father simply pocketing the proceeds and reselling his daughter does not arise, because the resale is prohibited.

This position, the Gemara notes, is the view of Rabbi Shimon — which will be elaborated in the discussion of who may be resold and under what conditions.

## Selling Daughter Rules

### Father's Resale Rights

A *baraita* now addresses the father's authority to sell his daughter and the limits on that authority. The baseline rule is: *mocher adam et bito le'ishut veshoneh* — a man may betroth his daughter (sell her for purposes of *ishut*, marriage) and repeat the act. How is this possible? As long as the daughter remains a minor (*ketanah*) and has not yet entered *nisuin* (full marriage), she remains under her father's authority. If she was betrothed (*kiddushin* was performed) but the betrothal was dissolved through divorce or death of the betrothed before *nisuin*, she returns entirely to her father's control and he may betroth her again. Thus multiple betrothals are possible while she remains a minor.

The *baraita* further states: *leshifchuto veshoneh* — he may also sell her as a slave and repeat this. If she was sold and then went free — for example, through the completion of six years, by *yovel*, or through *misas ha'adon* — but she is still a minor (*ketanah*), the father may sell her again. Similarly: *le'ishut achar shifchut* — he may also arrange her marriage after she has been a slave. All of these are permitted.

The one explicit prohibition in this *baraita* is: *lo leshifchut achar ishut* — he may not sell her as a slave after she has already been in a state of marriage (*ishut*). Going from the status of a wife to the status of a slave represents a profound diminution of standing, and the Torah prohibits this path.

### First Dispute Source

This is the position of the *tanna kamma*. The Gemara will derive it from the verse *lo yimshol l'mochrah b'vigdo bah* — "he shall not have authority to sell her in her betrayal" (or "in his garment over her") — a phrase whose precise reading lies at the heart of the tannaic dispute.

According to the *tanna kamma* (identified as Rabbi Akiva in the subsequent analysis), the word *bigdo* is read as *beged* — garment — which, in the idiomatic language of marriage (as in the passage in Megillat Rut where spreading one's cloak symbolizes marriage), refers to the act of marriage itself. The verse is thus saying: once he has spread his cloak over her — once she has been in a state of *ishut* — he may no longer sell her. This produces the prohibition of *shifchut achar ishut*.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon extends the prohibition: *keshem she'ein adam mocher et bito l'shifchah achar ishus, kach ein mocher et bito l'shifchah achar shifchah* — just as one may not sell a daughter as a slave after she has been in a state of marriage, so too one may not sell her as a slave after she has already been in a prior state of slavery. For Rabbi Shimon, once the daughter has undergone either *ishut* or *shifchut*, a subsequent sale into slavery is forbidden. The only path that remains available is selling her into *ishut* again, since the prohibition is specifically against entering *shifchut* a second time after any prior "sale" — whether that prior sale was *ishut* or *shifchut*.

This is the position underlying the earlier ruling that the father cannot resell the *amah ivriyah* after she has been redeemed, and it explains why forcing the family to redeem her is an effective and permanent remedy.

### Bigdo Textual Debate

The disagreement among the *tannaim* turns on how to read the word in the verse. The text as written (the *masorah*, or *kesiv*) is *bagdo*, without a *yud*, which in rabbinic reading yields *bagad* — betrayal, treachery. This textual reading (*yesh em l'masores*) produces the prohibition of *shifchut achar shifchut*: once the father has betrayed his daughter by selling her into slavery once, he may not do so again. The verse is warning against a second betrayal following the first.

The word as read aloud (the *mikra*, or *keri*) is *bigdo*, with a *yud*, which, as Rashi explains, corresponds to *beged* — garment — the image of marriage. This oral reading (*yesh em l'mikra*) yields the prohibition of *shifchut achar ishut*: once he has wrapped her in the garment of marriage, she may not be sold into slavery.

### Three Positions

The three positions thus map onto distinct approaches to the verse. Rabbi Akiva rules *yesh em l'mikra* and derives only the prohibition of *shifchut achar ishut*. Rabbi Eliezer rules *yesh em l'masores* and derives only the prohibition of *shifchut achar shifchut*. Rabbi Shimon holds *yesh em l'mikra v'gam l'masores* — both readings are legally operative simultaneously — and therefore derives both prohibitions: neither *shifchut achar ishut* nor *shifchut achar shifchut* is permitted. For Rabbi Shimon, the daughter who has once experienced either marriage or servitude may never again be placed into slavery by her father.

This synthesis accounts for the position attributed to Rabbi Shimon throughout the preceding discussion, including the ruling that an *amah ivriyah* cannot be resold and that it is therefore effective — and appropriate — to compel the family to redeem her.
