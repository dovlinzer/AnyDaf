# Kiddushin 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lineage & Safek Mamzer

### Opening & Overview

The daf under discussion is 75b, and we pick up at the bottom of 74b. Before diving into the new material, it is worth pausing to survey the broad sweep of what the Gemara has accomplished in these pages. The Mishnah famously lists ten distinct gradations of *yichus*, but much of what the Gemara has done is to narrow and minimize the scope of the concerns they generate. First, the Gemara asserted that no one truly knows their lineage with certainty — we may all be descendants of *mamzerim* for all we know, and once a family's identity has become lost and intermixed, we leave it alone. Second, when the discussion turned to the *asufi* and the *shtuki* — individuals whose father, or both parents, are unknown, making them *safek mamzerim* — the Gemara proceeded to restrict even those categories dramatically.

### Scope Minimization

The Gemara established that the category of *asufi* applies only to a child left to die, and that any sign of care having been extended to an abandoned child removes it from that classification entirely. Similarly, the category of *shtuki* was subjected to careful delimitation. The overall trajectory of the Gemara, then, has been to reassure us that the population of individuals genuinely subject to these concerns is quite small.

### Rava's Position

Into this context came Rava's pivotal ruling: *mamzer vaday amra rachmana velo mamzer safek* — the Torah forbids a definite *mamzer* from entering the congregation, but a doubtful *mamzer* is not Biblically prohibited at all. One might have expected the Gemara to immediately add that rabbinic legislation nonetheless restricts the *safek mamzer*. Instead, the Gemara frames the stringency differently.

### Ma'alah Asu Beyuchsin

Rather than framing the concern as a rabbinic prohibition, the Gemara describes it as *ma'alah asu beyuchsin* — a higher standard that was adopted specifically in matters of marriage and lineage. This framing is significant: it suggests that the weight of the *mamzer* prohibition is not what is driving the concern, neither Biblically nor even rabbinically in the usual sense. It is a separate category of elevated standard. This has practical implications for questions about how vigorously one must investigate questionable lineage, and how much leniency remains available.

### Mishnah: Asurin Bazeh

The most recent Mishnah — found at the top of 74a — states: *kol ha'asurin lavo bakahal mutarin lavo zeh bazeh*, that all those who cannot marry into the *kahal* — the paradigmatic case being the *mamzer*, the *nasin*, and the *safek mamzer* — may intermarry with one another. There followed a seemingly contradictory line from Rabbi Yehuda forbidding this. The Gemara spent much of yesterday's discussion untangling that debate, ultimately concluding that Rabbi Yehuda was not broadly forbidding intermarriage among those of disqualified status. Rather, his statement concerned converts of Ammonite, Moabite, or Edomite origin who, though barred from marrying into general *Klal Yisrael*, are permitted to marry a *mamzer*. Even Rabbi Yehuda, who forbids a *ger* from marrying a *mamzer* in the ordinary case, concedes that such a *ger* — who cannot marry into the general Jewish community — may marry a *mamzer*. Thus the principle holds: *mamzerim* and *safek mamzerim* may marry one another, and the *halacha* is that a *ger* may marry a *mamzer*.

### Rabbi Eliezer's View

With that backdrop, the Gemara arrives at the position of Rabbi Eliezer, which presents a genuine and severe challenge for the *safek mamzer*. Rabbi Eliezer holds: *vaday bevaday mutar* — a definite *mamzer* may marry another definite *mamzer* — but a definite *mamzer* may not marry a *safek mamzer*, and a *safek mamzer* may not marry another *safek mamzer*. The logic follows rigorously from treating the doubt as a genuine *safek*: a *safek mamzer* might not be a *mamzer* at all, so marrying a definite *mamzer* is problematic; and two *safek mamzerim* cannot marry one another because one might be a *mamzer* while the other is not. One is immediately compelled to ask: whom, then, can a *safek mamzer* marry? The answer would be a *ger*. And according to the position that a *mamzer* cannot marry a *ger*, the answer becomes essentially no one. This is a stark and troubling conclusion when treated as a genuine *safek* and the normal rules of doubt are applied without relief. The approach of *ma'alah asu beyuchsin*, by contrast, offers more flexibility — the *safek* is not treated as a real *safek* in the ordinary legal sense, but rather as a separate elevated standard, allowing for greater leniency in practice.

## Forbidden Relations

### Baraita: Forbidden Partn…

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita*. The text, five lines from the bottom of 74b, three lines before the line becomes wide, begins: *tanu rabbanan*. The *baraita* enumerates a list of men — a *ger Amoni u-Moavi, Mitzri v'Edomi*, a *Kuti*, a *nasin*, a *challal*, and a *mamzer* — and states that any of these, being at least nine years old such that their act of intercourse is legally consequential, who had intercourse with a *kohenet*, a *leviyah*, or a *bat Yisrael*, have invalidated that woman — *pasluha*. This is because any such forbidden act of intercourse effectively transforms the woman into a *challalah* and renders her forbidden to a *kohen*.

The converts listed — Ammonite, Moabite, Egyptian, Edomite — cannot marry into *Yisrael* and their intercourse with a Jewish woman is therefore forbidden. The *Kuti*, or Samaritan, represents a category we will return to shortly, whose status as a genuine convert is in question. The *nasin* also cannot marry into the general Jewish population. The *challal* is included because even though intercourse with him is not itself forbidden to a Jewish woman, it transforms her into a *challalah*. The *mamzer* rounds out the list — whether the *mamzerut* stems from *arayot* or from *eshet ish*.

### Tanna Kamma

The *Tanna Kamma* holds that any forbidden act of intercourse — *bi'ato ba'aveira* — invalidates the woman and renders her a *challalah*.

### Rabbi Yosi

Rabbi Yosi qualifies this position: *kol she-zaro pasul posel, v'chol she-ein zaro pasul eino posel*. Not every forbidden act of intercourse transforms the woman into a *challalah* — only an act where the resulting child would be *pasul*, disqualified. If the intercourse is forbidden but the child's status is unaffected, the woman is not transformed.

### Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel frames his position differently: *kol she-ata nosei bito, ata nosei almanato* — whoever's daughter a *kohen* may marry, that *kohen* may also marry the widow of the man in question. If you cannot marry his daughter, you cannot marry his widow. The logic, of course, is that the marital scenario in which a widowed woman becomes available to a *kohen* would occur if her husband died. The statement thus sets up a criterion: the daughter's status governs the mother's.

### Mitzri Sheini Case

What then is the practical difference between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yosi? On the surface, every category listed — *Amoni*, *Moavi*, *Mitzri*, *Adomi*, *Kuti*, *nasin*, *challal*, *mamzer* — seems to produce children whose status is equally disqualified. The Gemara asks, *mai ika beinayhu*, and answers: Rabbi Yochanan identifies the case of a *Mitzri sheini*, a second-generation Egyptian convert. According to the Torah, *dor shelishi yavo lahem bikhal Hashem* — the third generation of Egyptian converts may marry into the congregation. So a second-generation Egyptian who has intercourse with a Jewish woman produces a third-generation child, who is entirely permitted. According to the *Tanna Kamma*, the act of intercourse was forbidden and so the woman becomes a *challalah* regardless. According to Rabbi Yosi, since the child is permitted — he is a *Mitzri shelishi* — the woman does not become a *challalah*.

### Kohen Gadol Ba'Almanah

Both Rabbi Yosi and the *Tanna Kamma* derive their respective positions from the same source: the verse concerning the *Kohen Gadol* who has intercourse with a widow, regarding which the Torah states *velo yechalel zaro be'amav*. The Gemara notes that the word *yechalel* — with its double *lamed* — implies two acts of *chillul*: the *Kohen Gadol* renders the woman a *challalah*, and the child becomes a *challal*. The child becomes a *challal* as a derivative consequence of the mother having been transformed into a *challalah*.

### Three Position Analysis

What is the *nafka mina* between these three positions? Schematically: the *Tanna Kamma* requires only that the intercourse was forbidden — *bi'ato ba'aveira* — to render the woman a *challalah*. Rabbi Yosi adds a second criterion: not only must the intercourse be forbidden, but the *other* status involved — be it *Mitzri*, *Adomi*, or *mamzer* — must pass to the child. If the status does not pass to the child, the woman is not a *challalah*, even if the act itself was forbidden. The *Mitzri sheini* case illustrates this: since the child emerges as a *Mitzri shelishi* and is permitted to marry into the congregation, there is no status passing to the child, and according to Rabbi Yosi the woman is not transformed.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel introduces a third criterion: the disqualifying status must pass not merely to a child, but specifically to a daughter. The *nafka mina* between Rabbi Yosi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is identified by Ulla: the case of a *ger Ammoni* or *Moavi* who has intercourse with a Jewish woman, producing a daughter. The verse teaches *Ammoni v'lo Ammonit, Moavi v'lo Moavit* — only the male Ammonites and Moabites are excluded from the congregation; female descendants are fully permitted to marry. Therefore: according to the *Tanna Kamma*, the act was forbidden, so the woman becomes a *challalah*. According to Rabbi Yosi, the status passes to the son, so the woman becomes a *challalah*. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, since the daughter is not disqualified, the woman is not a *challalah*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires that the paradigm from *Kohen Gadol be'almanah* be matched fully — and in that paradigm, *all* children, sons and daughters alike, become *chalalim*. Only when that full correspondence holds does the woman's status shift.

The three-way comparison may be stated thus: the *Tanna Kamma* requires forbidden intercourse alone. Rabbi Yosi adds that the disqualifying status must pass to any child. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds further that it must pass to daughters as well as sons, requiring that the *kohen gadol be'almanah* paradigm be matched at every point — forbidden intercourse and universal transmission of status.

## Derivative Safek & Kutim

### Almanas Issa

Rav Chisda now introduces a point of consensus across all three positions: *hakol modim be'Almanas Issa she'psula le'kehuna* — everyone agrees that a widow of uncertain lineage, the *Almanas Issa*, is forbidden to *kehunah*. The case is that a woman had intercourse with a *safek challal* — a man whose status as a *challal* is doubtful. Even though the intercourse itself was not forbidden (a *challal* may marry any Jewish woman), the *challal* status, when it is definite, passes from the man to the woman. If the man is a *safek challal*, the woman becomes a *safek challalah*, and she is forbidden to a *kohen*. Rav Chisda observes that even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel — the most lenient of the three *tannaim* — would agree to this ruling. His statement excluding certain cases from the prohibition comes to exclude specific categories, not the *Almanas Issa* case. That case, with its *safek challalah* status extending to the daughter as a *safek challalah*, falls squarely within the principle that if you cannot marry the daughter, you cannot marry the widow.

### Sefek Sefeka

The Gemara then cites a *Mishnah*: *he'id Rabbi Yehoshua ve'Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira al Almanas Issa she'kesheira le'kehuna* — Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira testified that the widow of an *issa* is permitted to marry a *kohen*. The case, as the Gemara explains, is that a woman was married to a *safek challal*, and upon his death she is a widow who is now a *safek challalah*. Why would these authorities permit her to a *kohen*? The Gemara's answer is *havai sefek sefeka ve'sefek sefeka le'kula* — it is a double doubt, and a compound doubt is resolved leniently.

As Rashi notes, this is not technically a *sefek sefeka* in the classic sense — there is really only one underlying question, namely whether her husband was a *challal*. If he was, she becomes a *challalah* and her daughter becomes a *challalah*. But because the second-order status — her own *challalah* status — is a derivative of the first-order doubt, and because applying that first-order doubt one degree removed does produce what has the character of a compound or diluted doubt, the authorities here are lenient. One might call it not a doubled doubt but a *derivative* doubt — a doubtful status transmitted from a doubtful status. According to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, that derivativeness is sufficient to permit her.

This brief excursus into the laws of *chalalut* represents something of a detour — the focus is on *kehunah* concerns rather than the broader *yichus* questions — but both belong to the larger fabric of lineage *halacha*.

Returning now to Rabbi Eliezer's position, which creates a genuine difficulty for the *safek mamzer*: unlike the broader Gemara which uses the framework of *ma'alah asu beyuchsin* to soften the concern, Rabbi Eliezer treats the *safek mamzer* as a genuine *safek* with full legal consequences. As noted, Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Rav: *halacha k'Rabbi Eliezer*. When this ruling was brought before Shmuel, Shmuel responded by citing Hillel's tradition: *asarah yuchsin alu miBavel ve-chulam mutarin lavo zeh bazeh* — ten categories of *yichus* came up from Babylonia, and they are all permitted to intermarry with one another. Rashi clarifies that this means the *pesulim* among them may intermarry. The point is that ruling like Rabbi Eliezer creates a new stringency where a *safek mamzer* suddenly cannot marry a *vaday mamzer*, undercutting this established tradition.

The Gemara then notes an internal contradiction — *rami deRav aDeRav ve-rami deShmuel aDeShmuel* — both Rav and Shmuel appear to contradict themselves. The contradiction arises from a separate case: a woman who was betrothed (*arusah*) — living in her father's home, with no marital cohabitation yet having taken place — who became pregnant. The question is the status of the child. On one reading, Rav says the child is a *mamzer* and Shmuel says the child is a *shtuki*. But if that is their positions, they conflict with what each said previously about whether a *safek mamzer* may marry another *mamzer*.

The Gemara resolves this by reversing their positions: *ipoch* — Rav says the child is a *shtuki* (a *safek mamzer* who therefore cannot marry a *mamzeret*), and Shmuel says the child is a *mamzer* (meaning, by that term, a definite enough status that we permit him to marry a *mamzeret*). Now both authorities are internally consistent: Rav holds that a *safek mamzer* cannot marry a *vaday mamzer*, and Shmuel holds that even a *safek mamzer* may. The oral nature of this tradition — *amar*, he said — makes a reversal of attribution entirely plausible.

The Gemara asks: *tarti lamah li* — why do we need both cases? Could one case not have taught the other? The answer is *tzricha*: each case contains a unique argument that might have led us to think the authority in question would rule differently in that scenario. Regarding the case of the *safek mamzer* generally — a child born to a single woman whose father is unknown — Rav would say the child cannot marry a *mamzeret*, because most men around such a woman are *kesherim*, making it quite likely the child is also *kasher*, and thus a genuine *safek* as to the child's *mamzer* status prevents marriage to a *mamzeret*. One might have thought Rav would be lenient in the case of the betrothed woman, since there is a particular man — her betrothed — to whom we can attribute paternity, and the possibility that it is his legitimate child makes the *safek* significant. But the Gemara teaches that even there, Rav insists on the stringency. Conversely, Shmuel's leniency in permitting the *safek mamzer* to marry a *mamzeret* might seem inapplicable in the case of the *arusah*, since the vast majority of men in the world — *rov pesulim atzlah* — would make the child a *mamzer*; only the betrothed husband would not. One might have thought Shmuel would be stringent there. But the Gemara teaches he is lenient even in that case.

The Gemara then offers an alternative reading — *ve-iba'it eima lo teipoch* — in which the positions are not reversed at all. On this reading, both Rav and Shmuel agree the child of the *arusah* is a *safek mamzer* who cannot marry a *Yisrael*. The debate between them concerns a different question entirely. Rav says *mamzer* simply to convey that the child is forbidden to marry a *bat Yisrael*. Shmuel calls him a *shtuki*, not meaning something different about his *mamzer* status, but adding a separate point: *she'meshatkin oto mi'nichsei aviv* — the child is silenced from claiming any portion in his putative father's estate. Even if the child seized some of the property before the matter was adjudicated, he would have no rightful claim, because we do not know that the betrothed husband is his father at all.

A third possibility: Shmuel calls him a *shtuki* because the case calls for investigation — *beduki*. The mother should be asked, and if she says *le'kasher nivalti* — that the intercourse was with a permitted man, her betrothed — she is believed, in accordance with the ruling of Rabban Gamliel in *Ketubot* that such a woman's testimony about herself is credible. Shmuel had already ruled *halacha k'Rabban Gamliel* in a parallel case involving a single pregnant woman who identified the father. The Gemara notes that this ruling needs to be stated in both contexts, since in the case of the betrothed woman most men who could have been the father would produce a *mamzer* child, and one might have thought the mother's testimony would not be believed there.

Regarding the *halacha*: Tosafot states that we rule like Rabbi Eliezer, since Rav rules accordingly and we follow Rav over Shmuel in ritual prohibitions. The Rambam and the *Shulchan Aruch* concur. The ruling is therefore harsh: a *safek mamzer* may not marry a *vaday Yisrael*, may not marry a *vaday mamzer*, and may not marry another *safek mamzer* — since in the last case, one might be a *mamzer* and the other might not. The only remaining permissible partner for a *safek mamzer* is a *ger*. This is a severe outcome for anyone who actually falls within that category. The practical impact is mitigated, however, by the narrowing of the categories themselves: the trust given to a mother's testimony removes many cases from uncertainty, and the restricted definition of *asufi* removes many others. So while the ruling for a genuine *safek mamzer* is indeed austere, the scope of who actually falls into that category has been substantially diminished.

### Kutim Lo Yisa Kutit

The Gemara now introduces a new matter. The text states: *v'chein Rabbi Eliezer v'chein Rabbi Elazar omer Kuti lo yisa Kutit* — a Samaritan may not marry a Samaritan woman. This is striking: not only is a *Kuti* restricted from marrying into the general Jewish population, but apparently *Kutim* cannot marry one another either. The immediate question, of course, is whom a *Kuti* is supposed to marry. We will need to understand the status of the *Kutim* before we can understand why that restriction applies.

### Ger Achar Asara Dorot

Rav Yosef explains: *asa'uha k'ger l'achar asara dorot* — the Rabbis treated the *Kutim* as having the status of a *ger* after ten generations. This is based on a *baraita*: *ger ad asara dorot mutar b'mamzeret mikan ve'eilach assur b'mamzeret* — a *ger* within ten generations of his conversion may marry a *mamzeret*, but beyond that he may not. Some say the criterion is not a precise count of ten generations but rather until the name of idolatry — the non-Jewish identity — is forgotten from him. The Rambam understands this to mean until the fact that he descends from converts is forgotten.

This ruling contains several important *halachot*. First, it establishes that not only the *ger* himself but descendants of *gerim* retain the status of *ger* for the purposes of marriage to a *mamzer*. Second — and this is the critical implication for the *Kutim* case — once enough generations have passed and the *ger* identity is effectively forgotten, the individual can no longer benefit from the *kula* of *ger* status. At that point he must be treated as a *Yisrael le'chumra*, and a *Yisrael* may not marry a *mamzer*. One might therefore expect that if the *Kutim* have been around for so many generations, their *ger* status is effectively forgotten, and they should be treated as *Yisrael* — which would explain why they cannot marry *mamzerim*. But this still does not explain why they cannot marry *one another*.

One line of reasoning runs as follows: if we assume that *Kutim* are *safek mamzerim* — because of concerns about their adherence to the laws of *gittin v'kiddushin* — then Rabbi Eliezer's rule that a *safek mamzer* cannot marry another *safek mamzer* would explain the prohibition. The problem with this reasoning, as the Gemara points out, is that even *safek mamzerim* may marry a *ger*, and if all *Kutim* are descendants of *gerim*, then any *Kuti* marrying another *Kuti* is marrying a *ger* — which should be perfectly permissible for a *safek mamzer*. Rav Yosef's answer is that because of the passage of so many generations, the *kula* of *ger* status no longer applies, and so they cannot rely on the *ger* dispensation. Their *gerus* is old enough that it is no longer operative for leniency, but their *kuti* identity is not forgotten enough to treat them as pure *Yisrael*. This asymmetric situation is precisely what prohibits *Kuti* from marrying *Kutit*.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef: *mi dami* — how can you compare these cases? In the case of the *ger achar asara dorot*, the problem is clear: *ger yashan u'mamzeret chadasha* — you have an old *ger* whose *ger* identity has faded into the past, and a *mamzeret* whose *mamzer* identity is current and well-known. People will see what appears to be a *bar Yisrael* marrying a *mamzeret*, which is a visible violation that cannot be tolerated.

But by the *Kutim*, argues Abaye, the situation is symmetrical: *idi v'idi ke'haddi hanu* — both elements travel together. The *kuti* label carries with it both the *ger* identity and whatever *mamzer* concern attaches to that group. If you say so many generations have passed that the *ger* label is forgotten and therefore we cannot apply the *kula*, then for the same reason the *mamzer* concern associated with being a *kuti* should also be forgotten. You cannot selectively apply the stringency of the forgotten *ger* status while retaining the stringency of an unresolved *mamzer* concern. If the identity is old enough to lose the *kula*, it should be old enough to lose the *chumra* as well. This challenge leaves the Gemara without a satisfying resolution to the question of why *Kutim* cannot marry one another under Rav Yosef's framework.

## Kutim Status Debate

### Gerei Arayot

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported a different tradition: Rabbi Eliezer holds that *Kutim gerei arayot hein* — the Samaritans are converts motivated by lions, meaning they converted not out of sincere conviction but out of fear of the lions that were ravaging the land (as described in the Book of Kings). Such a conversion is not genuine, and therefore *Kutim* are to be treated as non-Jews. This position is attributed to Rabbi Yishmael. With this framing, the prohibition on *Kutim* marrying one another begins to take on a different character: if they are non-Jews, the concern is not about *mamzer* status in the internal sense, but about the intermingling of Jews and non-Jews among the *Kuti* population.

### Rabbi Akiva's View

The Gemara now introduces a second principle that Rabbi Eliezer combines with the above: *Rabbi Yishmael savar lah k'Rabbi Akiva de'amar oved kochavim ve'eved ha'ba al bat Yisrael havlad mamzer*. Rabbi Akiva holds that when a non-Jew or slave has intercourse with a Jewish woman, the child is a *mamzer*. If we accept both that (a) *Kutim* are non-Jews, and (b) Jewish women intermarried with *Kutim*, then the children born of those unions are *mamzerim* according to Rabbi Akiva. This explains the concern: the *Kuti* population consists of non-Jews intermixed with Jewish *mamzerim*, and because of that mixture, *Kutim* cannot marry one another — a Jew among them would be marrying a non-Jew, and a Jewish *mamzer* would be marrying a non-*mamzer* or non-Jew.

### Matrilineal Descent

At this point the Gemara opens a parenthetical of startling significance. If the child of a Jewish woman and a non-Jewish man would be a *mamzer* according to Rabbi Akiva — rather than the *halacha* as we know it, which says the child follows the mother and is simply a Jew — then one might ask: what does our mainstream tradition say about such a child? The Gemara says the child is *kasher*. But one reading among the *Ba'alei HaTosafot* interprets *kasher* not to mean "Jewish" but rather "non-*mamzer*." On this reading, the child of a non-Jewish father and a Jewish mother is a non-Jew who is free of *mamzer* status, meaning he has the capacity to convert and thereby become *kasher* — better than a *mamzer* who is permanently disqualified, but not a Jew by birth.

This reading, if accepted, would imply that matrilineal descent — the principle that any child of a Jewish mother is Jewish regardless of the father's origin — is not universally accepted in the Talmudic discussion. Rather, on this one reading, Jewish status requires both parents to be Jewish. The child of a non-Jewish father would be either a *mamzer* (Rabbi Akiva's view) or a non-Jew (the mainstream view on this reading). The importance of mentioning this reading is that it becomes relevant to the *Kuti* question: if we say *Kutim* are non-Jews and the children of Jewish mothers with *Kutim* are not *mamzerim* but simply non-Jews, then the *Kuti* population is Jews mixed with non-Jews — which is still a problem, but not because of *mamzerut*. This is the direction that explains why we need Rabbi Akiva's position: the only way the children of *Kutim* with Jewish women are *mamzerim* — as opposed to merely non-Jews — is to follow Rabbi Akiva. And the only way invoking *mamzerut* matters to the *Kutim* problem is if you also think the alternative is Jewish status via matrilineal descent.

Needless to say, this reading of a *Rishon* is not normative, and we do not rule that way. The concept of matrilineal descent is bedrock *halacha*. But the discussion surfaces here in the Gemara's attempt to account for Rabbi Eliezer's position on *Kutim*.

### Position Alignment

The Gemara now asks: does Rabbi Yishmael really hold like Rabbi Akiva on this point? The challenge comes from a ruling of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: *minaayin l'oved kochavim ve'eved sheba al hakohenet ve'al haleviyah ve'al bat Yisrael she'pasluha* — how do we know that a non-Jew or slave who had intercourse with a Jewish woman invalidated her for marriage to a *kohen*? The answer is derived from a verse: *uvas kohen ki tihyeh almanah ugerusha ve'zera ein lah* — the daughter of a *kohen* who is widowed or divorced with no children returns to her father's house. The Gemara derives: she returns to her father's house only if she was widowed or divorced from a man with whom *almanut* and *gerushin* — widowhood and divorce — are halachically possible. *Yatza oved kochavim ve'eved* — excluded is a non-Jew or slave, with whom no valid *kiddushin* can occur and hence no *almanut* or *gerushin*. Therefore, intercourse with such a man invalidates her.

The Gemara points out the contradiction: if Rabbi Yishmael held like Rabbi Akiva that the child of a non-Jewish man and a Jewish woman is a *mamzer*, no *drasha* would be needed to derive that the mother is invalidated. Of course a mother whose intercourse produced a *mamzer* is invalidated. The very fact that Rabbi Yishmael needs the *pasuk* to derive her invalidation demonstrates that he does *not* hold the child is a *mamzer*. Clearly Rabbi Yishmael did not accept Rabbi Akiva's position that the child is a *mamzer*.

### Ervah Requirement

The Gemara therefore reframes the claim: it is not Rabbi Yishmael but Rabbi Eliezer himself who holds both positions simultaneously — *savar lah k'Rabbi Yishmael de'amar Kutim gerei arayot* and *savar k'Rabbi Akiva de'amar oved kochavim ve'eved ha'ba al bat Yisrael ha'velad mamzer*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that *Kutim* are non-Jews and also that children of non-Jewish men and Jewish women are *mamzerim*.

But the Gemara immediately challenges this: how can Rabbi Eliezer hold like Rabbi Akiva on *mamzerut* from a non-Jewish father? The Gemara cites Rabbi Eliezer's own ruling elsewhere: *af al pi she-necheleku Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel be'tzarot* — even in the famous disputes between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding co-wives and the obligation of *yibbum* or *chalitzah* — despite all those disputes which might theoretically put some children's status in question — *modim be'elu she-ein mamzer ela mi she-issuro issur ervah ve'anush karet*. A child is not a *mamzer* unless the prohibition violated was an *issur ervah* punishable by *karet*. A mere *lav* — a simple prohibition without *karet* — does not produce a *mamzer*.

This is the crucial point. The prohibition on a non-Jewish man having intercourse with a Jewish woman is not an *issur karet*. It is not an *ervah* in the Talmudic sense. Therefore, according to Rabbi Eliezer's own principle, the child of such a union would *not* be a *mamzer*. Rabbi Eliezer cannot hold both that *mamzerut* requires an *issur karet* and that the child of a non-Jewish father and Jewish mother is a *mamzer* — these positions are incompatible.

### Unresolved Question

This leaves the Gemara — and us — without a satisfying account of why, according to Rabbi Eliezer, *Kutim* cannot marry one another. The first explanation — that they are *safek mamzerim* who cannot benefit from *ger* status after so many generations — was challenged by Abaye's objection that you cannot selectively discard the *ger kula* while retaining the *mamzer chumra*. The second explanation — that they are non-Jews mixed with Jewish *mamzerim* — collapses because Rabbi Eliezer himself holds that the child of a non-Jewish father and Jewish mother is not a *mamzer*, since that intercourse is not an *issur karet*. So if *Kutim* are non-Jews and their children with Jewish women are not *mamzerim*, the concern is simply one of Jews intermingled with non-Jews — a serious concern, but one that would explain a prohibition on *Kutim* marrying Jews, not necessarily on *Kutim* marrying one another.

The Gemara ends here with the question unresolved: what exactly is Rabbi Eliezer's reason for prohibiting a *Kuti* from marrying a *Kutit*? We will return to find the answer tomorrow.
