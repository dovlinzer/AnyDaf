# Makkot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kofer & Edim Zomemim

### Rav Chisda's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתַן פִּדְיֹן נַפְשׁוֹ״ – דְּמֵי נִיזָּק. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: דְּמֵי מַזִּיק. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? דְּמָר סָבַר: כּוּפְרָא מָמוֹנָא, וּמָר סָבַר: כּוּפְרָא כַּפָּרָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: It is Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse concerning a case where the ox of one person gored another: "If ransom is imposed upon him, **and he shall give the redemption of his soul"** (Exodus 21:30); the term "his soul" means **the value of the victim,** i.e., the owner of the ox must pay the heirs of the deceased his value as it would be assessed were he sold as a slave in the market. **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says:** The term "his soul" means **the value of the one liable for the damage. What, is it not** that **they disagree with regard to this** principle: **That** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **the ransom** is **monetary restitution** for the damage that his ox caused the victim; **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yishmael, **holds** that **the ransom** is **atonement,** as he thereby redeems his own soul from death at the hand of Heaven?

The Gemara on 2b opens in the middle of a discussion about *edim zomemim* — conspiratorial witnesses — and their exemption from paying *kofer*. The question driving the analysis is: who is the *tanna* who holds that *kufra kapara*, that the *kofer* payment is an atonement rather than a restitutive monetary obligation?

*Amar* Rav Chisda: it is the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka. Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka is himself a notable *tanna*, and his son Rabbi Yishmael carries forward a distinctive legal position.

### Kofer Exception Intro

To understand this attribution, it is necessary to recall the basic structure of the exception. When *edim zomemim* testify in a way that would force someone to pay *kofer* — a payment that arises, for example, when someone assaults a pregnant woman causing a miscarriage, or in certain cases of *shor tam* as discussed in Bava Kamma — and those witnesses are subsequently exposed as *zomemim*, we do not impose *kofer* on them as the reciprocal punishment. This stands as an exception to the general rule that *edim zomemim* receive whatever punishment their testimony would have caused the accused to suffer.

The reason given is *kufra kapara*: *kofer* functions as atonement for the crime, not as ordinary monetary restitution. Since the *edim zomemim* did not actually commit the underlying act for which *kofer* effects *kapara*, there is no atonement for them to make — *hanei lav b'nei kapara ninhu*.

### Demei Nizak vs. Mazik

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כּוּפְרָא כַּפָּרָה, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: בִּדְנִיזָּק שָׁיְימִינַן, וּמָר סָבַר: בִּדְמַזִּיק שָׁיְימִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: No,** perhaps **everyone agrees** that **the ransom** is **atonement, and here,** it is **with regard to this** matter that **they disagree:** One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **we assess** the payment **in** terms of the value **of** the **victim; and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yishmael, **holds** that **we assess** the payment **in** terms of the value **of** the **one liable for the damage.**

The Gemara now cites the *baraita* that serves as the basis for Rabbi Yishmael's position. The verse in Bava Kamma states *ve-natan pidyon nafsho* — he shall pay a redemption for his life. The *tanna kamma* holds that this payment is assessed according to *demei nizak*, the value of the injured party. Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka disagrees and says the assessment follows *demei mazik*, the value of the one who caused the damage. These valuations are *damim* — a calculation of how much a person would fetch on the slave market, serving as a proxy for personal worth.

The initial reading of this *machloket* is that it directly mirrors the question of *kufra kapara*: if *kofer* is a *kapara* for the person who committed the act, it stands to reason that the assessment would go by his own worth — *demei mazik*. If it is a monetary payment tied to the damage caused, the assessment logically follows the injured party — *demei nizak*. Accordingly, the Gemara suggests: *mai lav behai kamiflegi?* — is this not the very dispute underlying the question? One *amora* holds *kufra mamona*, that *kofer* is a monetary payment, and the other holds *kufra kapara*, that it is atonement.

### Rava's Reinterpretation

Rava disagrees with Rav Chisda's framing. He says *lo dekuli alma kufra kapara* — in fact, everyone agrees that *kofer* is a *kapara* payment. The dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yishmael is not about the nature of *kofer* at all; it concerns only the method of calculating the payment.

### Gezera Shava Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? נֶאֱמַר הֲשָׁתָה לְמַטָּה, וְנֶאֱמַר הֲשָׁתָה לְמַעְלָה. מָה לְהַלָּן – בִּדְנִיזָּק, אַף כָּאן – בִּדְנִיזָּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who abandon the straightforward meaning of the verse? The Rabbis derive it in the following manner: **Imposition is stated below** with regard to one whose ox killed another: "If ransom is imposed upon him" (Exodus 21:30), **and imposition is stated above,** in the case of one who struck a pregnant woman, causing her to miscarry: "He shall be punished, as the husband of the woman imposes upon him" (Exodus 21:22). **Just as there,** with regard to the miscarriage, it is **in** terms of the value **of** the **victim,** the fetus, that we assess the payment, **here too,** in the case of the ransom, it is **in** terms of the value **of** the **victim** that we assess the payment.

According to Rava, the *tanna kamma* — the *rabbanan* — holds *bedemei nizak shaminan*: we assess based on the *nizak*. Their proof comes from a *gezera shava*. The case of *kofer* for an ox goring is analogized to the case of one who strikes a pregnant woman and causes a miscarriage, where the verse states explicitly *ka'asher yashit alav ba'al ha'ishah* — the payment is set by the husband of the woman, i.e., by the side that was injured. *Mah lehalan b'nizak, af kan b'nizak*: just as there the assessment tracks the *nizak*, so too in the analogous case.

### Pidyon Nafsho Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְנָתַן פִּדְיֹן נַפְשׁוֹ״ כְּתִיב. וְרַבָּנַן? אֵין, ״פִּדְיוֹן נַפְשׁוֹ״ כְּתִיב, מִיהוּ כִּי שָׁיְימִינַן, בִּדְנִיזָּק שָׁיְימִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yishmael,** son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: **"And he shall give the redemption of his soul" is written,** indicating that the sum is based on the value of the one seeking atonement. **And the Rabbis** explain: **Yes,** the phrase **"redemption of his soul" is written,** and the payment functions to redeem his soul. **Nevertheless, when we assess** the sum of the redemption payment, it is **in** terms of the value **of** the **victim** that **we assess** the payment.

Rabbi Yishmael's position is grounded in a close reading of *ve-natan pidyon nafsho* — the word *nafsho*, "his life," points to the value of the *mazik* himself, making his *damim* the operative figure. The *rabbanan* respond that *nafsho katuv* — yes, the verse says "his life" — but *mihu k'deshayminan b'nizak shayminan*: nevertheless, the assessment is still carried out according to the *nizak*. The ambiguity of the verse, they argue, permits this reading, and the *gezera shava* clinches it.

A question naturally arises: if according to Rava everyone agrees that *kofer* is *kapara*, then in what sense is the *mazik* paying through the valuation of the *nizak*? The answer Rava provides is that even under the *tanna kamma*'s framework, the *mazik* is the one paying and achieving *kapara* — the *kapara* is still his — but the *amount* of the payment is calculated based on the *nizak*. The *kapara* nature of the payment and the *demei nizak* valuation are not contradictory.

## Eved Ivri Exception

### Eved Ivri Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין נִמְכָּרִין בְּעֶבֶד עִבְרִי. סָבַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא לְמֵימַר: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ, דְּמִיגּוֹ דְּאִיהוּ לָא נִזְדַּבַּן – אִינְהוּ נָמֵי לָא מִיזְדַּבְּנוּ. אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית לְהוּ לְדִידְהוּ – מִיזְדַּבְּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* teaches: Conspiring witnesses **are not sold as a Hebrew slave** in a case where they testified that one stole property. **Rav Hamnuna thought to say: This statement** applies only in a case where the falsely accused **has** means **of his own** to repay the sum of the alleged theft, **as since,** had the testimony been true, **he would not have been sold** for his transgression, the witnesses too are **not sold** when they are rendered conspiring witnesses. **But** in a case **where** the falsely accused **does not have** means **of his own,** and would have been sold into slavery had their testimony been accepted, **even if** the witnesses **have** means **of their own, they are sold,** as they sought to have slavery inflicted upon him.

The Gemara moves to the next exception from the general rule of reciprocal punishment: *ein nimkarim b'eved ivri* — *edim zomemim* are not sold as *eved ivri*. Two overlapping scenarios drive this question. In the first, witnesses testify that someone stole and has no money to repay, such that he should be sold as a Hebrew slave; they are then shown to be *zomemim*. Should they be sold as *eved ivri* in his place? In the second scenario, *edim zomemim* testify that someone stole but he could pay back the money directly; they are shown to be *zomemim* but themselves have no money — should they be sold as slaves to cover the value of their conspiracy?

At the heart of the question is whether *eved ivri* is a mechanism for payment or a distinct legal institution. If it is purely a payment mechanism, then however the monetary obligation is discharged — whether by *eved ivri* or direct payment — is irrelevant to the question of punishment.

### Rav Hamnuna's Logic

Rav Hamnuna initially suggests that *ein nimkarim b'eved ivri* applies specifically when the defendant *had* money — i.e., when he himself would not have been sold as a slave. In that case, the *edim zomemim* also would not be sold. But if the defendant had no money and *would* have been sold as a slave, then even if the *edim zomemim* possess the funds to pay the monetary equivalent, they should be sold as slaves — because they knowingly conspired against someone they knew had no money, and therefore what they intended to have done to him was precisely sale as *eved ivri*.

### Hamnuna Revised

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְלֵימְרוּ לֵיהּ: אִי אַנְתְּ הֲוָה לָךְ מִי הֲוָה מִיזְדַּבְּנַתְּ? אֲנַן נָמֵי לָא מִיזְדַּבְּנִינַן. אֶלָּא סָבַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא לְמֵימַר: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ אוֹ לְדִידֵיהּ אוֹ לְדִידְהוּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ לָא לְדִידֵיהּ וְלָא לְדִידְהוּ – מִזְדַּבְּנִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: ״וְנִמְכַּר בִּגְנֵבָתוֹ״, אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – בִּגְנֵבָתוֹ, וְלֹא בִּזְמָמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: And let** the witnesses **say to** the person against whom they testified: **If you had** money, **would you have been sold? We too will not be sold.** Since they have the means, they can pay the sum that they owe and not be sold as slaves. **Rather,** the Gemara proposes an alternative formulation of the statement of Rav Hamnuna. **Rav Hamnuna thought to say: This statement** applies only in a case **where either he,** the falsely accused, **or they,** the witnesses, **have** the means to pay the sum; **but** in a case **where neither he nor they have** the means, the witnesses **are sold.** In that case, had their testimony stood, the alleged thief would have been sold into slavery, and they too lack the means to pay the sum that they are liable to pay as conspiring witnesses. Therefore, they are sold. **Rava said to him:** That is not so, as **the Merciful One states: "And he shall be sold for his theft"** (Exodus 21:30), from which it is inferred: **For his theft, but not for his conspiring** testimony.

This initial position, however, is open to a pointed objection. The *edim zomemim* could respond: *i ant hava lach, mi hava mizdaban* — if you, the defendant, had had money, would *you* have been sold? No. *Anan nami lo mizdabninan* — so we should not be sold either, since we have the money to pay. Since *eved ivri* is ultimately a payment mechanism, the fact that we can pay cash should suffice.

Rav Hamnuna then revises his position. He says the exception of *ein nimkarim b'eved ivri* applies only when at least one of the parties — either the defendant or the witnesses — has money. If neither side has money, then the witnesses would indeed be sold as slaves, since the defendant would certainly have been sold and the witnesses have no cash alternative.

### Rava's Exception

Rava rejects even this revised formulation. He argues that the very purpose of the *mishna* stating *ein nimkarim b'eved ivri* is precisely to teach this rule as an absolute exception — *davka* in the case where one would have thought otherwise, namely where the defendant had no money. The verse states *venimkar bignivato* — he is sold for his *theft*. *Amra Rachmana bignivato velo bizmamo*: the Torah specifies sale for theft, not for conspiracy. The exemption from *eved ivri* is not a qualification based on the financial circumstances of either party; it is a categorical carve-out from the principle of reciprocal punishment.

### Rabbi Akiva Kenas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אָמְרוּ: אַף אֵין מְשַׁלְּמִין עַל פִּי עַצְמָן. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? קָסָבַר קְנָסָא הוּא, וּקְנָס אֵין מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. אָמַר רַבָּה: תֵּדַע, שֶׁהֲרֵי לֹא עָשׂוּ מַעֲשֶׂה, וּמְשַׁלְּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the *baraita*: The Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Akiva:** They also do not pay based on their own admission. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva?** He **holds** that money paid by a convicted conspiring witnesses **is a fine [*kenasa*], and** the principle is: **One does not pay a fine based on his own** admission; one pays a fine only on the basis of testimony. **Rabba says: Know** that it is a fine, **as** these witnesses **did not perform an action** and caused no actual damage, **and** yet **they pay** depending on the nature of their testimony, indicating that it is a fine rather than a monetary restitution.

A *baraita* is now introduced citing a position in the name of Rabbi Akiva: *af einam meshalmim al pi atzman* — *edim zomemim* also do not pay on the basis of their own admission. Rabbi Akiva's reasoning is *kenasa hu* — he understands the punishment imposed on *edim zomemim* to be a *kenas*, a punitive fine. And the standing rule is *modeh bekenas patur*: one who admits liability for a *kenas* is exempt from paying it.

This is a striking position. *Kenas* in *halacha* is ordinarily understood as a fixed, precisely defined Torah penalty — and its fixedness is part of what distinguishes it from *mamon*, ordinary monetary obligation. Yet here Rabbi Akiva categorizes the sliding-scale, variable punishment of *edim zomemim* as a *kenas*, which is itself a conceptually arresting move.

### Rava vs. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תֵּדַע, שֶׁהֲרֵי מָמוֹן בְּיַד בְּעָלִים, וּמְשַׁלְּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: Know** that it is a fine rather than a monetary restitution for damages, **as the money** they sought to compel him to pay ultimately remains **in the possession of the owner** against whom they testified, **and** yet **they pay** him.

Rava challenges the coherence of Rabbi Akiva's position. *Teda sheharei lo asu ma'aseh venahargim* — consider that *edim zomemim* who did nothing actually criminal are nonetheless executed. If we are to treat the punishment of *edim zomemim* as a *kenas*, then by Rabbi Akiva's own logic one could argue that an admission of guilt would exempt from execution as well. Yet we certainly impose execution regardless — *venahargim umeshalmim*. This suggests that the punishment framework of *edim zomemim* is not reducible to the category of *kenas* in any straightforward way. The monetary component, moreover, is paid even though no actual theft occurred — which itself highlights the sui generis nature of this entire institution.

## Meshalem Al Pi Atzmo

### Multiple Witnesses Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: עֵד זוֹמֵם מְשַׁלֵּם לְפִי חֶלְקוֹ. מַאי ״מְשַׁלֵּם לְפִי חֶלְקוֹ״? אִילֵּימָא דְּהַאי מְשַׁלֵּם פַּלְגָא וְהַאי מְשַׁלֵּם פַּלְגָא, תְּנֵינָא: מְשַׁלְּשִׁין בְּמָמוֹן, וְאֵין מְשַׁלְּשִׁין בְּמַלְקוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the statement of Rabbi Akiva in the *baraita*, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A conspiring witness pays according to his share.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **Pays according to his share? If we say** that it means **that this** witness **pays half** of the sum that the set conspired to render another liable to pay with their testimony, **and that** witness **pays** the other **half, we** already **learned** this in a mishna (5a): When punishing conspiring witnesses, **one divides** the punishment of **money** among them, **but one does not divide** the punishment of **lashes** among them; each receives the full thirty-nine lashes.

The Gemara at the top of 3a turns to the teaching of Rav Nachman quoting Rav Yehuda quoting Rav: *ed zomem meshalem lefi chelko* — a single *ed zomem* pays according to his proportional share of the conspiratorial testimony.

### Punishment Division Rule

The Gemara asks how to understand this rule. Perhaps it means that among two witnesses each pays half? But this is already taught in the upcoming *mishna*: *meshalmim bemamon ve'ein meshalmim bemalkot* — monetary liability in a case of *edim zomemim* may be subdivided among the witnesses, but corporal or capital punishment may not. One does not receive a fraction of lashes or a fractional execution.

### Single Witness Zomem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: כְּגוֹן דְּאִיתַּזּוּם חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ, דִּמְשַׁלֵּם פַּלְגָא דִידֵיהּ, וּמִי מְשַׁלֵּם? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: אֵין עֵד זוֹמֵם מְשַׁלֵּם מָמוֹן עַד שֶׁיִּזּוֹמּוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara explains that the expression: Pays according to his share, means that in a case **where** only **one of** the set **was rendered a conspiring** witness, the *halakha* is **that he pays his half** of the sum. The Gemara asks: **And does** he **pay** at all in that case? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **A conspiring witness pays money only when both were rendered conspiring** witnesses? If only one was rendered a conspiring witness, he does not pay.

Another possibility is that the case involves a group of witnesses of which only one was shown to be *ed zomem* — perhaps he pays only his own share. But this too is complicated by a *baraita*: *ein ed zomem meshalem mamon ad sheyuzmu sheneihem* — no *ed zomem* pays money unless the entire group of witnesses has been established as *zomemim*. The invalidation of testimony requires all witnesses to have been exposed.

### Chozer U-Maggid Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּאוֹמֵר ״עֵדוּת שֶׁקֶר הֵעַדְתִּי״. כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ? כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיד שׁוּב אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וּמַגִּיד!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The statement is not referring to a case of conspiring witnesses; rather, Rav's statement applies **to** the case of one who **says:** It was **false testimony** that **I testified.** The Gemara asks: **Is it in his** power to have this statement accepted by the court? That runs counter to the principle: **Once** a witness **stated** his testimony **he may not then state** a revision of that testimony.

Rava now introduces a crucial limiting principle. What if one of the witnesses comes forward and says "my testimony was false"? *Kol kaminieh?* Can we simply accept such a statement? The answer is no: *keivan shehigid shuv eino chozer umagid* — once testimony has been registered before the court, the witness cannot return and amend or retract it. This is not merely a rule against self-incrimination; it is a broader rule that testimony, once given, is fixed.

### From Another Court

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּאוֹמֵר: ״הֵעַדְנוּ וְהוּזַמְנוּ בְּבֵית דִּין פְּלוֹנִי״. כְּמַאן – דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָא אָמַר: אַף אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** Rav's statement applies **to** the case of one who **says: We testified and were rendered conspiring** witnesses **in such and such a court.** Since he was already convicted as a conspiring witness, it is as though he is admitting an obligation to give monetary restitution, and he is therefore liable to pay his share. The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this *halakha*? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, since if** it were **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, doesn't he say:** A conspiring witness **also does not pay based on his own** admission?

The Gemara resolves the apparent difficulty by positing a specific scenario: *ela be'omer he-adnu vehuzmanu beveit din ploni* — the witnesses come forward and say that they testified and were established as *edim zomemim* in a different court, which obligated them to pay a certain sum. In this case, showing up to pay is not *meshalem al pi atzmo* — it is not paying on the basis of one's own unilateral admission. Rather, it is fulfilling an obligation already adjudicated and registered in another court. This is the narrow pathway through which a *zomem* can voluntarily pay without running into the problem of *modeh bekenas patur*.

### Ed Echad Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּאוֹמֵר ״הֵעַדְנוּ וְהוּזַמְנוּ בְּבֵית דִּין פְּלוֹנִי וְחוּיַּיבְנוּ מָמוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather,** Rav's statement applies **to** the case of one who **says: We testified and were rendered conspiring** witnesses **in such and such a court and we were rendered liable** to pay a specific sum of **money.** In that scenario, Rabbi Akiva would concede that one pays on the basis of his own admission. Once the court rendered him liable to pay a specific sum, his admission is not that he is liable to pay a fine, in which case he would be exempt. Once the court actually instructed them to pay, the debt has the status of any other monetary restitution, and one does pay monetary restitution based on his own admission.

One might ask: why is this even necessary to teach? The Gemara explains with a logical inference. If one of the two witnesses shows up at a new court to pay while the other does not, the one who comes cannot obligate his fellow through his own declaration — that would be *ed echad bemamon*, a single witness in a monetary case, which is insufficient to compel payment. *Salka da'atakh amina*: one might therefore think that since he cannot force *his friend* to pay, he also cannot force *himself* to pay — *ihu nami lo mechayev*. *Ka mashma lan*: the teaching comes to inform us otherwise. Even though a single witness cannot compel his fellow, he *can* obligate himself by coming forward to fulfill the adjudicated debt. This possibility is not *meshalem al pi atzmo* in the problematic sense, because the obligation was already established by the other court.

## Ketubah Valuation

### Divorce & Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״מְעִידִין אָנוּ אֶת אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁגֵּירַשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְלֹא נָתַן לָהּ כְּתוּבָתָהּ״, וַהֲלֹא בֵּין הַיּוֹם וּבֵין לְמָחָר, סוֹפוֹ לִיתֵּן לָהּ כְּתוּבָתָהּ. אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ שֶׁל זוֹ, שֶׁאִם נִתְאַלְמְנָה אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשָׁה, וְאִם מֵתָה, יִירָשֶׁנָּה בַּעֲלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of witnesses who said: **We testify** with regard to **a man** called **so-and-so that he divorced his wife and did not give her** payment of **her marriage contract,** and they were then rendered conspiring witnesses, the question arises with regard to the manner in which the sum of their payment is calculated. It is not possible to render the witnesses liable to pay the entire sum of the marriage contract, as they can claim: **But isn't** it so that **either today or tomorrow,** i.e., at some point in the future, he may divorce his wife or die and **ultimately he** will be liable **to give her** payment of **her marriage contract?** That being the case, the witnesses did not conspire to render him liable to pay a sum that he would otherwise not be liable to pay. The sum of their payment is calculated as follows: The court **assesses how much** money another **person** would be **willing to give in** order to purchase the rights to **this** woman's **marriage contract,** cognizant of the uncertainty **that if she was widowed or divorced** the purchaser will receive payment of the marriage contract **but if she dies, her husband will inherit** from **her,** and the one who purchased her marriage contract will receive nothing.

The next *mishna* addresses the case of witnesses who testify: *me'idim anu et ish ploni shegeresh et ishto velo natan lah ketubatah* — we testify that so-and-so divorced his wife and did not pay her *ketubah*. The witnesses are then shown to be *zomemim*. The question is: how much must they pay? The case is distinctive because the divorce itself is uncontested — what is disputed is only the timing of the *ketubah* payment. This means the punishment for the *edim zomemim* is not the entire *ketubah* value, but the differential value between receiving it now versus having received it at some other point.

### Risk Analysis

The *mishna* states: *omdim kama adam rotze liten beketubah shel zu she'im nitalmena o nitgarsha ve'im met yirashena ba'alah* — we assess how much someone would be willing to pay for this *ketubah*, factoring in that if the wife predeceases the husband, her estate reverts to him, and against the benefit that if the husband dies or divorces her, she collects. This is a market-based risk-benefit analysis: the *ketubah* is treated as a financial instrument with liabilities and dividends, and we ask what an investor would pay for it on the open market. The actual face value of the *ketubah* may be two hundred *zuz*, but after discounting for risk, the true present value may be considerably less.

### Baal Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כֵּיצַד שָׁמִין? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּבַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna establishes that the payment of the conspiring witnesses is calculated based on the sum that one would be willing to pay for rights to payment of the marriage contract, taking into consideration the uncertainty whether he will ultimately receive that payment. The Gemara asks: **How does** the court **assess** that sum? **Rav Ḥisda says:** One calculates the sum **in** terms of **the husband:** How much would one be willing to pay for the husband's rights to the marriage contract based on the likelihood that his wife will die first and he will inherit from her?

The Gemara then asks *keitzad shamin* — how precisely is this assessment performed? Rav Chisda says: *bava'al* — based on the husband. That is, we look at the particulars of this specific husband: his health, his age, his likelihood of dying soon or divorcing her, his wealth and ability to pay.

### Assessment Expansion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נָתָן בַּר אוֹשַׁעְיָא אוֹמֵר: בָּאִשָּׁה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בָּאִשָּׁה וּבִכְתוּבָּתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Natan bar Oshaya says:** One calculates the sum **in** terms of **the wife:** How much would one be willing to pay for the wife's rights to the marriage contract based on the likelihood that the husband will die first or divorce her? **Rav Pappa says:** One calculates the sum **in** terms of **the wife,** as Rav Natan bar Oshaya said, **and** the assessment is done **in** terms of the sum of **her marriage contract.** One does not include in the calculation any usufruct property that the wife brought with her into the marriage, the profits of which belong to the husband, despite the fact that as a result of the testimony of the conspiring witnesses the husband would lose his rights to those profits. One calculates the sum only in terms of her marriage contract.

Rav Natan bar Oshaya says the assessment is *be'ishah* — based on the wife: we factor in her estate, her liabilities, her dowry and the claims against it. Rav Papa extends the analysis further, saying *be'ishah uveketubatah* — we account for the woman and the *ketubah* itself, including, it seems, her ability to sell the *ketubah* as a financial instrument in the marketplace before it comes due. Each position thus expands the scope of the risk assessment outward from the simplest reading to the most comprehensive accounting of all the variables that affect the *ketubah*'s real-world value.

## Loan Duration Valuation

### Loan Timeline Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״מְעִידִין אָנוּ בְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב לַחֲבֵירוֹ אֶלֶף זוּז, עַל מְנָת לִיתְּנָן לוֹ מִכָּאן וְעַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר, ״מִכָּאן וְעַד עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים״. אוֹמְדִים כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן וְיִהְיוּ בְּיָדוֹ אֶלֶף זוּז, בֵּין נוֹתְנָן מִכָּאן וְעַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, בֵּין נוֹתְנָן מִכָּאן וְעַד עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of witnesses who said: **We testify with regard to a man** called **so-and-so that he owes another** person **one thousand dinars** that he borrowed **on the condition** that he is **to give** the money back **to him from now until thirty days** have passed, **and** the borrower **says** that he borrowed that sum but it was on the condition that he is to give the money back to him **from now until ten years** have passed, and they were rendered conspiring witnesses, here too, it is not possible to render the witnesses liable to pay the entire sum. Rather, the court **estimates how much** money **a person** would be **willing to give** so that he **would** keep a loan of **one thousand dinars in his possession,** and one calculates the difference **between** that sum in a situation where he would be required to **give** the money back **from now until thirty days** have passed, **and** that same sum in a situation where he would be required to **give** the money back **from now until ten years** have passed. That difference is the sum that the testimony of the conspiring witnesses sought to have the borrower lose; therefore, it is the sum that they must pay.

The next *mishna* case: witnesses testify that a certain person owes a thousand *zuz* to his friend, repayable within thirty days. The debtor agrees he owes the thousand *zuz* but says the repayment deadline is ten years from now, not thirty days. The *mishna* says we assess *kama adam rotze liten v'yihyu elef zuz b'yado bein notnam mikan v'ad shloshim yom u'vein notnam mikan v'ad eser shanim* — how much would someone be willing to pay to have a thousand *zuz* in hand, given the difference between a loan repayable in thirty days versus ten years?

### Value Calculation

The logic is straightforward: having access to a thousand *zuz* for ten years rather than one month is enormously more valuable. A borrower can put that capital to productive use over a decade. The proportional difference is substantial — perhaps roughly a hundredfold. This makes the *edim zomemim*'s potential liability in this case quite significant, since the difference in value between the two terms is large.

### Shemita Cancellation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לְעֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים – שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** In the case of **one who lends** money to **another for** a period of **ten years, the Sabbatical** Year **abrogates** the debt (see Deuteronomy 15:1–11) and absolves the borrower of the obligation to repay it.

At this point Rav Yehuda quotes Shmuel: *hamalveh et chavero l'eser shanim, shevi'it mashmatato* — if one lends money to a friend for ten years, the *shemita* year will cancel the debt through *shmitat kesafim*. This creates an immediate tension with the *mishna*. If a ten-year loan is simply cancelled by *shemita*, what meaningful difference in value is the *mishna* actually assessing? A loan that gets wiped out is worth nothing to the lender.

### Lo Yigosh After Shemita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּהַשְׁתָּא לָא קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״לֹא יִגֹּשׂ״, סוֹף אָתֵי לִידֵי ״לֹא יִגֹּשׂ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And even though now,** during the Sabbatical Year, **we do not** yet **read concerning his** loan the prohibition: **"He shall not exact it** of his neighbor and his brother, because the Lord's release has been proclaimed" (Deuteronomy 15:2), as the time for repayment has not yet arrived, **ultimately,** when the time for repayment arrives, he will **come to** a situation where the prohibition **"He shall not exact it"** applies. Therefore, like all other debts it is abrogated by the Sabbatical Year.

The Gemara answers on behalf of the first version of the *sugya*: *af al gav d'hashta lo karinan beih lo yigosh, sof ati lidei lo yigosh* — even though at the time of *shemita* it is not yet called *lo yigosh* (since the loan is not yet collectible during the *shemita* year), in the end, when the loan becomes collectible after the loan term expires, collecting it will constitute a violation of *lo yigosh*. This is a significant extension of the prohibition: one might have thought *lo yigosh* applies only when the lender actually presses the borrower *during* the *shemita* year. Here the Gemara holds that even a loan whose collection period will simply *overlap* with *shemita* — or more precisely, whose eventual collection will lead to a *lo yigosh* situation — already poses a halakhic problem.

### Alternative Shemita View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב כָּהֲנָא: אוֹמְדִים כַּמָּה אָדָם רוֹצֶה לִיתֵּן וְיִהְיוּ אֶלֶף זוּז בְּיָדוֹ בֵּין לִיתֵּן מִכָּאן וְעַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּבֵין לִיתֵּן מִכָּאן וְעַד עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתּוֹ – כּוּלְּהוּ נָמֵי בָּעִי שַׁלּוֹמֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raises an objection** from the mishna: Rather, the court **estimates how much** money **a person** would be **willing to give** so that he **would** keep a loan of **one thousand dinars in his possession,** and one calculates the difference **between** that sum in a situation where he would be required **to give** the money back **from now until thirty days** have passed, **and** that same sum in a situation where he would be required **to give** the money back **from now until ten years** have passed. **And if you say** that **the Sabbatical** Year **abrogates** a ten-year loan, the conspiring witnesses **need** to **pay** the borrower **the entire** sum of the loan **as well,** as their testimony sought to render the borrower liable to pay a debt that by right should be entirely forgiven.

The Gemara then presents an alternative version of the *sugya* — *ika de'amri* — in which Rav Yehuda quotes Shmuel in the opposite direction: *hamalveh et chavero l'eser shanim, ein shevi'is mashmatato* — a ten-year loan is *not* cancelled by *shemita*. In this version, the proof is derived from the *mishna* itself: the *mishna* presupposes that a ten-year loan has meaningful assessable value, which would be impossible if *shemita* cancelled it entirely. Rav Kahana then explicitly invokes the *mishna* as his proof.

### Rava's Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמַלְוֶה עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן, וּבְמוֹסֵר שְׁטָרוֹתָיו לְבֵית דִּין. דִּתְנַן: הַמַּלְוֶה עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן, וְהַמּוֹסֵר שְׁטָרוֹתָיו לְבֵית דִין – אֵין מַשְׁמִיטִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What are we dealing with here** in our mishna? We are dealing with debts that are not abrogated with the passing of the Sabbatical Year, e.g., **in** the case of one who **lends** money **on** the basis of **collateral, or in** the case of one who **transfers his** promissory **notes to the court** for collection. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 10:2): In the cases of **one who lends** money **on** the basis of **collateral and one who transfers his** promissory **notes to the court** for collection, the Sabbatical Year **does not abrogate** those loans. In those cases, the debt would not have been forgiven during the Sabbatical Year. Therefore, the conspiring witnesses are not liable to pay the entire sum.

In this version, the ruling on *lo yigosh* is inverted: *ve'af al gav de'asi lidei lo yigosh, hashta miha lo karina bei lo yigosh* — even though eventually collecting the loan will implicate *lo yigosh*, at the time of *shemita* itself the loan is not yet collectable, so *shemita* has no present cancelling effect. The violation of *lo yigosh* occurs at the future moment of collection, not during the *shemita* year, and that is not sufficient to cancel the loan preemptively.

Rava resolves the tension in both versions by pointing to a specific category of loans that fall outside *shmitat kesafim* entirely. *Hacha b'mai askinan* — what cases is the *mishna* discussing? Either *malveh al hamashkon* — a loan secured by collateral — or *hamoser shtarosav l'veit din* — a case where the lender has deposited his loan deed with the court for collection. Whether this latter mechanism is identical to *pruzbul* or something distinct is itself a matter of *machloket rishonim*. The *mishna* in Masechet Shevi'it teaches: *hamalveh al hamashkon vehamoser shtarosav l'veit din ein mashmitin* — these loans are not cancelled by *shemita*. The *mishna* in Makkot, Rava argues, is discussing precisely such cases.

### Conditions on Torah Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא תְּשַׁמְּטֵנִי שְׁבִיעִית״ – שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמֶּטֶת. לֵימָא קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַתְנֶה עַל מָה שֶׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה הוּא, וְכׇל הַמַּתְנֶה עַל מָה שֶׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה – תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל?
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says** with regard to abrogation of debts: In the case of **one who says to another** the stipulation: I am lending you money **on the condition that the Sabbatical** Year **will not abrogate my** debt, even if the borrower agrees to that stipulation, **the Sabbatical** Year **abrogates** the debt. The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **Shmuel holds** that the lender who proposed that stipulation **is** one who **stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah, and** in the case of **anyone who stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah, his stipulation is voided.**

The Gemara then raises a related question: what if someone lends money and stipulates *al mnat shelo tishmeteni shevi'it* — on the condition that the *shemita* year will not cancel this loan? Rav Yehuda quotes Shmuel: *shevi'is mashmatato* — *shemita* cancels it nonetheless. The principle at stake is whether one can make a *tenai* that overrides a *mitzvah* written in the Torah — *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah tena'o vatel*.

### Shmuel's Exploitation Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיתְּמַר, הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לְךָ עָלַי אוֹנָאָה״, רַב אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ לוֹ עָלָיו אוֹנָאָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵין לוֹ עָלָיו אוֹנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it stated** that there is a dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning this matter? In the case of **one who says to another** the stipulation: I am selling you this item **on the condition that you have no** claim of **exploitation against me** if I charge you more than the item is worth, **Rav says:** The buyer **has** a claim of **exploitation against him,** as one cannot stipulate to waive the *halakha* of exploitation. **And Shmuel says: He has no** claim of **exploitation against him.** Apparently, according to Shmuel, one may stipulate to waive a Torah law in monetary matters, as it is tantamount to waiving his rights to money due him and it is not in effect counter to that which is written in the Torah.

The Gemara tests whether this is actually Shmuel's position by noting that elsewhere Shmuel appears to hold that such a condition *can* work, in the context of *ona'ah* — overcharging. A seller who says *al mnat she-ein lecha alai ona'ah* — on condition that you have no *ona'ah* claim against me — according to Shmuel, there is no *ona'ah*. Rava disagrees and says *ona'ah* still applies regardless.

### Rav Anan's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב עָנָן: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לְךָ עָלַי אוֹנָאָה״ – אֵין לוֹ עָלָיו אוֹנָאָה. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אוֹנָאָה״ – הֲרֵי יֵשׁ בּוֹ אוֹנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Wasn't it stated with regard to that** *halakha* that **Rav Anan said: It was explained to me** personally **by Shmuel** himself that the matter depends on the formulation of the stipulation. If the seller stipulates: **On the condition that you have no** claim of **exploitation against me,** then the buyer **has no** claim of **exploitation against him,** as it is as though he is waiving his right to money due him. But if the seller stipulates: **On the condition that there is no** prohibition of **exploitation in** this transaction, **there is** a prohibition of **exploitation in** that transaction.

Rav Anan clarifies the apparent contradiction in Shmuel's positions. The distinction is: if one says *al mnat she-ein lecha alai ona'ah* — on condition that *you* will not hold *me* liable for *ona'ah*, meaning a personal waiver of the right to sue — that is valid, because one may waive one's own right. But if one says *al mnat she-ein bo ona'ah* — on condition that the *law* of *ona'ah* simply does not apply — that is an attempt to nullify a Torah law, which is invalid.

### Shemita Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא תְּשַׁמְּטֵנִי בַּשְּׁבִיעִית״ – אֵין שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתּוֹ. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא תְּשַׁמְּטֵנִי שְׁבִיעִית״ – שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Here too,** if the lender stipulated to the borrower: I am lending you money **on the condition that you will not abrogate** the debt **during the Sabbatical** Year, **the Sabbatical** Year **does not abrogate** his debt, as the borrower is merely waiving money due him. But if he stipulated: **On the condition that the Sabbatical** Year **will not abrogate my** debt, **the Sabbatical** Year **abrogates** the debt, as that is a stipulation to nullify the *halakha* of the Sabbatical Year concerning that loan, and one may not stipulate counter to that which is written in the Torah.

The same distinction applies to *shemita*. If someone stipulates *al mnat shelo tishmeteni b'hashevi'it* — asking the borrower to agree not to invoke *shemita* cancellation against him — *ein shevi'is mashmatato*: that condition works, because the borrower is waiving his own invocation of the law. But if someone stipulates *al mnat shelo tishmeteni shevi'it* in the sense of trying to nullify *shemita*'s legal force as such — *shevi'is mashmatato*: *shemita* cancels it anyway.

## Default Loan Terms

### Thirty-Day Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ סְתָם – אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְתוֹבְעוֹ פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. סְבַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב לְמֵימַר: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּמִלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר, דְּלָא עֲבִד אִינִישׁ דְּטָרַח דְּכָתֵב שְׁטָר בְּצִיר מִתְּלָתִין יוֹמִין, אֲבָל מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה – לָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב: הָכִי אָמַר חֲבִיבִי: אֶחָד הַמַּלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר וְאֶחָד הַמַּלְוֶה עַל פֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the loan discussed in the mishna, the Gemara cites a Sage who **taught: One who lends** money to **another** for **an unspecified** period **is not permitted to demand** repayment **from him less than thirty days** after the loan took place. The Gemara comments: **Rabba bar bar Ḥana,** who was seated **before Rav, thought to say: This statement** applies only **with regard to a loan with** a promissory **note, as a person does not typically exert** himself **to write** a promissory **note** for a loan whose duration is **less than thirty days. But** with regard to **a loan by oral** agreement, **no,** it may be for less than thirty days. **Rav said to him: This** is what **my uncle,** Rabbi Ḥiyya, **said:** This *halakha* applies to **both one who lends with** a promissory **note and one who lends by oral** agreement. If one fails to establish a time for repayment of the loan, he may not demand repayment until thirty days have passed since the loan was given.

A *baraita* is cited: *hamalveh et chavero stam, eino rashai letov'o pachot mishloshim yom* — if one lends to a friend without specifying a repayment date, one may not demand repayment before thirty days. The assumption built into an unspecified loan is that the minimum term is thirty days.

This is interesting to set alongside a parallel from Nazir, where the Gemara derives *stam nezirut shloshim yom* — that an unspecified term of *nezirut* is thirty days — from a *gezera* based on the word *yihiyeh* in the verse. There, it is presented as a matter intrinsic to the ritual law of *nezirut* and derived from *psukim*. Here, in a purely financial context, the same timescale emerges. It is striking that *Chazal* anchor both a ritual institution and a commercial norm to the same thirty-day baseline, and in the commercial context they feel the need to derive it from a verse rather than simply asserting it as a market convention.

### Written vs. Oral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ סְתָם – אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְתוֹבְעוֹ פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אֶחָד הַמַּלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר וְאֶחָד הַמַּלְוֶה עַל פֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **That is also taught** in a *baraita*: **One who lends** money to **another** for **an unspecified** period **is not permitted to demand** repayment **from him less than thirty days** after the loan took place, and that is the case with regard to **both one who lends with** a promissory **note and one who lends by oral** agreement.

Rabba bar bar Chana suggests before Rav that this thirty-day minimum applies only to a loan documented in a *shtar*. His reasoning: *de-la avid inish de-tarach de-katav shtar batzir mi-tlatin yomi* — no one goes to the trouble of drafting and executing a written contract for a loan intended to be repaid in less than thirty days. The very fact that a *shtar* was written implies a minimum term of thirty days. But for a verbal loan — *malveh al peh* — perhaps no such assumption attaches, and the lender could demand repayment much sooner.

### Shmuel's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב מַתְנָה: לָא תִּיתֵּיב אַכַּרְעָיךָ עַד דִּמְפָרְשַׁתְּ לָהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא: מְנַָא הָא מִילְּתָא דַאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ סְתָם אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְתוֹבְעוֹ פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אֶחָד הַמַּלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר וְאֶחָד הַמַּלְוֶה עַל פֶּה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Shmuel said to Rav Mattana: You shall not sit on your feet until you have explained this *halakha*;** explain it immediately. **From where is this matter that the Sages stated** derived: **One who lends** money to **another** for **an unspecified** period **is not permitted to demand** repayment **from him less than thirty days** after the loan took place, and that is the case with regard to **both one who lends with** a promissory **note and one who lends by oral** agreement?

A *baraita* is then cited that explicitly contradicts Rabba bar bar Chana's distinction: *echad ha-malveh bi-shtar ve-echad ha-malveh al peh* — whether the loan was written or oral, the thirty-day minimum applies equally. Shmuel reportedly told Rav Matna: *lo titeiv akareicha ad de-parshaht lah l'ha shmaita* — I will not let you leave your seat until you explain this teaching. How can the thirty-day minimum apply identically to both written and oral loans, given that the reasoning based on the effort of drafting a *shtar* would seem to distinguish them?

### Gezera from Shemita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּכְתִיב ״קָרְבָה שְׁנַת הַשֶּׁבַע שְׁנַת הַשְּׁמִטָּה״, מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״קָרְבָה שְׁנַת הַשֶּׁבַע״, אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהִיא שְׁנַת שְׁמִטָּה? אֶלָּא מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שְׁנַת הַשְּׁמִטָּה״? לוֹמַר לָךְ: יֵשׁ שְׁמִטָּה אַחֶרֶת שֶׁהִיא כָּזוֹ, וְאֵיזוֹ – זוֹ הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ סְתָם, שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְתוֹבְעוֹ בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם. דְּאָמַר מָר: שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם בַּשָּׁנָה חָשׁוּב שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Mattana **said to him** that it is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "The seventh year, the Sabbatical Year, approaches"** (Deuteronomy 15:9). The question arises: **From** the fact **that it is stated: "The seventh year…approaches," don't I know that it is the Sabbatical Year? Rather, why** must **the verse state: "The Sabbatical Year"?** It is **to tell you** that **there is another** period **that is like the Sabbatical** Year; **and which** period **is it? It is** the period in the case of **one who lends** money to **another** for **an unspecified** period, **who is not permitted to demand** repayment **from him less than thirty days** after the loan took place. During that period, as during the Sabbatical Year, one may not exact payment for his loan. In what sense can that period be characterized as a year? It is **as the Master said: Thirty days in a year are considered a year.**

The resolution comes from the verse in Devarim: *karvah shnat hasheva shnat hashmitah* — the seventh year, the year of release, is approaching. The apparent redundancy — having said "the seventh year," why add "the year of *shemita*"? — generates a *drasha*: *yesh shmitah acheret she-hi kazot* — there is another *shemita* that resembles this one. *Ve'eizu zu? Hamalveh et chavero stam she'eino rashai letov'o pachot mishloshim yom* — which *shemita*? The rule that an unspecified loan may not be collected before thirty days. Just as the *shemita* year creates a protected window during which debts cannot be pressed, the unspecified loan creates a protected window of thirty days during which the lender cannot demand repayment. This derivation applies equally to written and oral loans, since it is grounded in the verse's analogy rather than in any inference from the act of drafting a contract.

### Fiscal Year Analogy

The Gemara then connects this to a broader principle: *shloshim yom beshanah chashuv shanah* — thirty days within a year counts as a full year for certain purposes. This principle surfaces across a range of legal contexts. In the laws of *chazaka*, as discussed in the third *perek* of Bava Batra, questions arise about how to count the years of undisturbed possession that establish legal tenure. In the laws of *metzarin* — neighbors' rights — and in questions of fiscal-year calculations for leases and agreements, the same thirty-day threshold functions as a minimum unit that can stand in for a full year. The discussion of this principle at the end of the amud connects the loan minimum back to a fundamental and recurring *halakhic* construct about how time is measured and how partial periods are treated in Jewish law.
