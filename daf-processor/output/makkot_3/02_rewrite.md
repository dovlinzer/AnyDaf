# Makkot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kofer & Edim Zomemim

### Rav Chisda's Position

The Gemara on 2b opens in the middle of a discussion about *edim zomemim* — conspiratorial witnesses — and their exemption from paying *kofer*. The question driving the analysis is: who is the *tanna* who holds that *kufra kapara*, that the *kofer* payment is an atonement rather than a restitutive monetary obligation?

*Amar* Rav Chisda: it is the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka. Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka is himself a notable *tanna*, and his son Rabbi Yishmael carries forward a distinctive legal position.

### Kofer Exception Intro

To understand this attribution, it is necessary to recall the basic structure of the exception. When *edim zomemim* testify in a way that would force someone to pay *kofer* — a payment that arises, for example, when someone assaults a pregnant woman causing a miscarriage, or in certain cases of *shor tam* as discussed in Bava Kamma — and those witnesses are subsequently exposed as *zomemim*, we do not impose *kofer* on them as the reciprocal punishment. This stands as an exception to the general rule that *edim zomemim* receive whatever punishment their testimony would have caused the accused to suffer.

The reason given is *kufra kapara*: *kofer* functions as atonement for the crime, not as ordinary monetary restitution. Since the *edim zomemim* did not actually commit the underlying act for which *kofer* effects *kapara*, there is no atonement for them to make — *hanei lav b'nei kapara ninhu*.

### Demei Nizak vs. Mazik

The Gemara now cites the *baraita* that serves as the basis for Rabbi Yishmael's position. The verse in Bava Kamma states *ve-natan pidyon nafsho* — he shall pay a redemption for his life. The *tanna kamma* holds that this payment is assessed according to *demei nizak*, the value of the injured party. Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka disagrees and says the assessment follows *demei mazik*, the value of the one who caused the damage. These valuations are *damim* — a calculation of how much a person would fetch on the slave market, serving as a proxy for personal worth.

The initial reading of this *machloket* is that it directly mirrors the question of *kufra kapara*: if *kofer* is a *kapara* for the person who committed the act, it stands to reason that the assessment would go by his own worth — *demei mazik*. If it is a monetary payment tied to the damage caused, the assessment logically follows the injured party — *demei nizak*. Accordingly, the Gemara suggests: *mai lav behai kamiflegi?* — is this not the very dispute underlying the question? One *amora* holds *kufra mamona*, that *kofer* is a monetary payment, and the other holds *kufra kapara*, that it is atonement.

### Rava's Reinterpretation

Rava disagrees with Rav Chisda's framing. He says *lo dekuli alma kufra kapara* — in fact, everyone agrees that *kofer* is a *kapara* payment. The dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yishmael is not about the nature of *kofer* at all; it concerns only the method of calculating the payment.

### Gezera Shava Analysis

According to Rava, the *tanna kamma* — the *rabbanan* — holds *bedemei nizak shaminan*: we assess based on the *nizak*. Their proof comes from a *gezera shava*. The case of *kofer* for an ox goring is analogized to the case of one who strikes a pregnant woman and causes a miscarriage, where the verse states explicitly *ka'asher yashit alav ba'al ha'ishah* — the payment is set by the husband of the woman, i.e., by the side that was injured. *Mah lehalan b'nizak, af kan b'nizak*: just as there the assessment tracks the *nizak*, so too in the analogous case.

### Pidyon Nafsho Dispute

Rabbi Yishmael's position is grounded in a close reading of *ve-natan pidyon nafsho* — the word *nafsho*, "his life," points to the value of the *mazik* himself, making his *damim* the operative figure. The *rabbanan* respond that *nafsho katuv* — yes, the verse says "his life" — but *mihu k'deshayminan b'nizak shayminan*: nevertheless, the assessment is still carried out according to the *nizak*. The ambiguity of the verse, they argue, permits this reading, and the *gezera shava* clinches it.

A question naturally arises: if according to Rava everyone agrees that *kofer* is *kapara*, then in what sense is the *mazik* paying through the valuation of the *nizak*? The answer Rava provides is that even under the *tanna kamma*'s framework, the *mazik* is the one paying and achieving *kapara* — the *kapara* is still his — but the *amount* of the payment is calculated based on the *nizak*. The *kapara* nature of the payment and the *demei nizak* valuation are not contradictory.

## Eved Ivri Exception

### Eved Ivri Question

The Gemara moves to the next exception from the general rule of reciprocal punishment: *ein nimkarim b'eved ivri* — *edim zomemim* are not sold as *eved ivri*. Two overlapping scenarios drive this question. In the first, witnesses testify that someone stole and has no money to repay, such that he should be sold as a Hebrew slave; they are then shown to be *zomemim*. Should they be sold as *eved ivri* in his place? In the second scenario, *edim zomemim* testify that someone stole but he could pay back the money directly; they are shown to be *zomemim* but themselves have no money — should they be sold as slaves to cover the value of their conspiracy?

At the heart of the question is whether *eved ivri* is a mechanism for payment or a distinct legal institution. If it is purely a payment mechanism, then however the monetary obligation is discharged — whether by *eved ivri* or direct payment — is irrelevant to the question of punishment.

### Rav Hamnuna's Logic

Rav Hamnuna initially suggests that *ein nimkarim b'eved ivri* applies specifically when the defendant *had* money — i.e., when he himself would not have been sold as a slave. In that case, the *edim zomemim* also would not be sold. But if the defendant had no money and *would* have been sold as a slave, then even if the *edim zomemim* possess the funds to pay the monetary equivalent, they should be sold as slaves — because they knowingly conspired against someone they knew had no money, and therefore what they intended to have done to him was precisely sale as *eved ivri*.

### Hamnuna Revised

This initial position, however, is open to a pointed objection. The *edim zomemim* could respond: *i ant hava lach, mi hava mizdaban* — if you, the defendant, had had money, would *you* have been sold? No. *Anan nami lo mizdabninan* — so we should not be sold either, since we have the money to pay. Since *eved ivri* is ultimately a payment mechanism, the fact that we can pay cash should suffice.

Rav Hamnuna then revises his position. He says the exception of *ein nimkarim b'eved ivri* applies only when at least one of the parties — either the defendant or the witnesses — has money. If neither side has money, then the witnesses would indeed be sold as slaves, since the defendant would certainly have been sold and the witnesses have no cash alternative.

### Rava's Exception

Rava rejects even this revised formulation. He argues that the very purpose of the *mishna* stating *ein nimkarim b'eved ivri* is precisely to teach this rule as an absolute exception — *davka* in the case where one would have thought otherwise, namely where the defendant had no money. The verse states *venimkar bignivato* — he is sold for his *theft*. *Amra Rachmana bignivato velo bizmamo*: the Torah specifies sale for theft, not for conspiracy. The exemption from *eved ivri* is not a qualification based on the financial circumstances of either party; it is a categorical carve-out from the principle of reciprocal punishment.

### Rabbi Akiva Kenas

A *baraita* is now introduced citing a position in the name of Rabbi Akiva: *af einam meshalmim al pi atzman* — *edim zomemim* also do not pay on the basis of their own admission. Rabbi Akiva's reasoning is *kenasa hu* — he understands the punishment imposed on *edim zomemim* to be a *kenas*, a punitive fine. And the standing rule is *modeh bekenas patur*: one who admits liability for a *kenas* is exempt from paying it.

This is a striking position. *Kenas* in *halacha* is ordinarily understood as a fixed, precisely defined Torah penalty — and its fixedness is part of what distinguishes it from *mamon*, ordinary monetary obligation. Yet here Rabbi Akiva categorizes the sliding-scale, variable punishment of *edim zomemim* as a *kenas*, which is itself a conceptually arresting move.

### Rava vs. Akiva

Rava challenges the coherence of Rabbi Akiva's position. *Teda sheharei lo asu ma'aseh venahargim* — consider that *edim zomemim* who did nothing actually criminal are nonetheless executed. If we are to treat the punishment of *edim zomemim* as a *kenas*, then by Rabbi Akiva's own logic one could argue that an admission of guilt would exempt from execution as well. Yet we certainly impose execution regardless — *venahargim umeshalmim*. This suggests that the punishment framework of *edim zomemim* is not reducible to the category of *kenas* in any straightforward way. The monetary component, moreover, is paid even though no actual theft occurred — which itself highlights the sui generis nature of this entire institution.

## Meshalem Al Pi Atzmo

### Multiple Witnesses Payme…

The Gemara at the top of 3a turns to the teaching of Rav Nachman quoting Rav Yehuda quoting Rav: *ed zomem meshalem lefi chelko* — a single *ed zomem* pays according to his proportional share of the conspiratorial testimony.

### Punishment Division Rule

The Gemara asks how to understand this rule. Perhaps it means that among two witnesses each pays half? But this is already taught in the upcoming *mishna*: *meshalmim bemamon ve'ein meshalmim bemalkot* — monetary liability in a case of *edim zomemim* may be subdivided among the witnesses, but corporal or capital punishment may not. One does not receive a fraction of lashes or a fractional execution.

### Single Witness Zomem

Another possibility is that the case involves a group of witnesses of which only one was shown to be *ed zomem* — perhaps he pays only his own share. But this too is complicated by a *baraita*: *ein ed zomem meshalem mamon ad sheyuzmu sheneihem* — no *ed zomem* pays money unless the entire group of witnesses has been established as *zomemim*. The invalidation of testimony requires all witnesses to have been exposed.

### Chozer U-Maggid Rule

Rava now introduces a crucial limiting principle. What if one of the witnesses comes forward and says "my testimony was false"? *Kol kaminieh?* Can we simply accept such a statement? The answer is no: *keivan shehigid shuv eino chozer umagid* — once testimony has been registered before the court, the witness cannot return and amend or retract it. This is not merely a rule against self-incrimination; it is a broader rule that testimony, once given, is fixed.

### From Another Court

The Gemara resolves the apparent difficulty by positing a specific scenario: *ela be'omer he-adnu vehuzmanu beveit din ploni* — the witnesses come forward and say that they testified and were established as *edim zomemim* in a different court, which obligated them to pay a certain sum. In this case, showing up to pay is not *meshalem al pi atzmo* — it is not paying on the basis of one's own unilateral admission. Rather, it is fulfilling an obligation already adjudicated and registered in another court. This is the narrow pathway through which a *zomem* can voluntarily pay without running into the problem of *modeh bekenas patur*.

### Ed Echad Logic

One might ask: why is this even necessary to teach? The Gemara explains with a logical inference. If one of the two witnesses shows up at a new court to pay while the other does not, the one who comes cannot obligate his fellow through his own declaration — that would be *ed echad bemamon*, a single witness in a monetary case, which is insufficient to compel payment. *Salka da'atakh amina*: one might therefore think that since he cannot force *his friend* to pay, he also cannot force *himself* to pay — *ihu nami lo mechayev*. *Ka mashma lan*: the teaching comes to inform us otherwise. Even though a single witness cannot compel his fellow, he *can* obligate himself by coming forward to fulfill the adjudicated debt. This possibility is not *meshalem al pi atzmo* in the problematic sense, because the obligation was already established by the other court.

## Ketubah Valuation

### Divorce & Ketubah

The next *mishna* addresses the case of witnesses who testify: *me'idim anu et ish ploni shegeresh et ishto velo natan lah ketubatah* — we testify that so-and-so divorced his wife and did not pay her *ketubah*. The witnesses are then shown to be *zomemim*. The question is: how much must they pay? The case is distinctive because the divorce itself is uncontested — what is disputed is only the timing of the *ketubah* payment. This means the punishment for the *edim zomemim* is not the entire *ketubah* value, but the differential value between receiving it now versus having received it at some other point.

### Risk Analysis

The *mishna* states: *omdim kama adam rotze liten beketubah shel zu she'im nitalmena o nitgarsha ve'im met yirashena ba'alah* — we assess how much someone would be willing to pay for this *ketubah*, factoring in that if the wife predeceases the husband, her estate reverts to him, and against the benefit that if the husband dies or divorces her, she collects. This is a market-based risk-benefit analysis: the *ketubah* is treated as a financial instrument with liabilities and dividends, and we ask what an investor would pay for it on the open market. The actual face value of the *ketubah* may be two hundred *zuz*, but after discounting for risk, the true present value may be considerably less.

### Baal Assessment

The Gemara then asks *keitzad shamin* — how precisely is this assessment performed? Rav Chisda says: *beva'al* — based on the husband. That is, we look at the particulars of this specific husband: his health, his age, his likelihood of dying soon or divorcing her, his wealth and ability to pay.

### Assessment Expansion

Rav Natan bar Oshaya says the assessment is *be'ishah* — based on the wife: we factor in her estate, her liabilities, her dowry and the claims against it. Rav Papa extends the analysis further, saying *be'ishah uveketubatah* — we account for the woman and the *ketubah* itself, including, it seems, her ability to sell the *ketubah* as a financial instrument in the marketplace before it comes due. Each position thus expands the scope of the risk assessment outward from the simplest reading to the most comprehensive accounting of all the variables that affect the *ketubah*'s real-world value.

## Loan Duration Valuation

### Loan Timeline Dispute

The next *mishna* case: witnesses testify that a certain person owes a thousand *zuz* to his friend, repayable within thirty days. The debtor agrees he owes the thousand *zuz* but says the repayment deadline is ten years from now, not thirty days. The *mishna* says we assess *kama adam rotze liten v'yihyu elef zuz b'yado bein notnam mikan v'ad shloshim yom u'vein notnam mikan v'ad eser shanim* — how much would someone be willing to pay to have a thousand *zuz* in hand, given the difference between a loan repayable in thirty days versus ten years?

### Value Calculation

The logic is straightforward: having access to a thousand *zuz* for ten years rather than one month is enormously more valuable. A borrower can put that capital to productive use over a decade. The proportional difference is substantial — perhaps roughly a hundredfold. This makes the *edim zomemim*'s potential liability in this case quite significant, since the difference in value between the two terms is large.

### Shemita Cancellation

At this point Rav Yehuda quotes Shmuel: *hamalveh et chavero l'eser shanim, shevi'it mashmatato* — if one lends money to a friend for ten years, the *shemita* year will cancel the debt through *shmitat kesafim*. This creates an immediate tension with the *mishna*. If a ten-year loan is simply cancelled by *shemita*, what meaningful difference in value is the *mishna* actually assessing? A loan that gets wiped out is worth nothing to the lender.

### Lo Yigosh After Shemita

The Gemara answers on behalf of the first version of the *sugya*: *af al gav d'hashta lo karinan beih lo yigosh, sof ati lidei lo yigosh* — even though at the time of *shemita* it is not yet called *lo yigosh* (since the loan is not yet collectible during the *shemita* year), in the end, when the loan becomes collectible after the loan term expires, collecting it will constitute a violation of *lo yigosh*. This is a significant extension of the prohibition: one might have thought *lo yigosh* applies only when the lender actually presses the borrower *during* the *shemita* year. Here the Gemara holds that even a loan whose collection period will simply *overlap* with *shemita* — or more precisely, whose eventual collection will lead to a *lo yigosh* situation — already poses a halakhic problem.

### Alternative Shemita View

The Gemara then presents an alternative version of the *sugya* — *ika de'amri* — in which Rav Yehuda quotes Shmuel in the opposite direction: *hamalveh et chavero l'eser shanim, ein shevi'is mashmatato* — a ten-year loan is *not* cancelled by *shemita*. In this version, the proof is derived from the *mishna* itself: the *mishna* presupposes that a ten-year loan has meaningful assessable value, which would be impossible if *shemita* cancelled it entirely. Rav Kahana then explicitly invokes the *mishna* as his proof.

In this version, the ruling on *lo yigosh* is inverted: *ve'af al gav de'asi lidei lo yigosh, hashta miha lo karina bei lo yigosh* — even though eventually collecting the loan will implicate *lo yigosh*, at the time of *shemita* itself the loan is not yet collectable, so *shemita* has no present cancelling effect. The violation of *lo yigosh* occurs at the future moment of collection, not during the *shemita* year, and that is not sufficient to cancel the loan preemptively.

### Rava's Exclusion

Rava resolves the tension in both versions by pointing to a specific category of loans that fall outside *shmitat kesafim* entirely. *Hacha b'mai askinan* — what cases is the *mishna* discussing? Either *malveh al hamashkon* — a loan secured by collateral — or *hamoser shtarosav l'veit din* — a case where the lender has deposited his loan deed with the court for collection. Whether this latter mechanism is identical to *pruzbul* or something distinct is itself a matter of *machloket rishonim*. The *mishna* in Masechet Shevi'it teaches: *hamalveh al hamashkon vehamoser shtarosav l'veit din ein mashmitin* — these loans are not cancelled by *shemita*. The *mishna* in Makkot, Rava argues, is discussing precisely such cases.

### Conditions on Torah Law

The Gemara then raises a related question: what if someone lends money and stipulates *al mnat shelo tishmeteni shevi'it* — on the condition that the *shemita* year will not cancel this loan? Rav Yehuda quotes Shmuel: *shevi'is mashmatato* — *shemita* cancels it nonetheless. The principle at stake is whether one can make a *tenai* that overrides a *mitzvah* written in the Torah — *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah tena'o vatel*.

The Gemara tests whether this is actually Shmuel's position by noting that elsewhere Shmuel appears to hold that such a condition *can* work, in the context of *ona'ah* — overcharging. A seller who says *al mnat she-ein lecha alai ona'ah* — on condition that you have no *ona'ah* claim against me — according to Shmuel, there is no *ona'ah*. Rava disagrees and says *ona'ah* still applies regardless.

Rav Anan clarifies the apparent contradiction in Shmuel's positions. The distinction is: if one says *al mnat she-ein lecha alai ona'ah* — on condition that *you* will not hold *me* liable for *ona'ah*, meaning a personal waiver of the right to sue — that is valid, because one may waive one's own right. But if one says *al mnat she-ein bo ona'ah* — on condition that the *law* of *ona'ah* simply does not apply — that is an attempt to nullify a Torah law, which is invalid.

The same distinction applies to *shemita*. If someone stipulates *al mnat shelo tishmeteni shevi'it* — asking the borrower to agree not to invoke *shemita* cancellation against him — *ein shevi'is mashmatato*: that condition works, because the borrower is waiving his own personal invocation of the law. But if someone stipulates *al mnat shelo tishmeteni shevi'it* in the sense of trying to nullify *shemita*'s legal force as such — *shevi'is mashmatato*: *shemita* cancels it anyway.

## Default Loan Terms

### Thirty-Day Minimum

A *baraita* is cited: *hamalveh et chavero stam, eino rashai letov'o pachot mishloshim yom* — if one lends to a friend without specifying a repayment date, one may not demand repayment before thirty days. The assumption built into an unspecified loan is that the minimum term is thirty days.

This is interesting to set alongside a parallel from Nazir, where the Gemara derives *stam nezirut shloshim yom* — that an unspecified term of *nezirut* is thirty days — from a *gezera* based on the word *yihiyeh* in the verse. There, it is presented as a matter intrinsic to the ritual law of *nezirut* and derived from *psukim*. Here, in a purely financial context, the same timescale emerges. It is striking that *Chazal* anchor both a ritual institution and a commercial norm to the same thirty-day baseline, and in the commercial context they feel the need to derive it from a verse rather than simply asserting it as a market convention.

### Written vs. Oral

Rabba bar bar Chana suggests before Rav that this thirty-day minimum applies only to a loan documented in a *shtar*. His reasoning: *de-la avid inish de-tarach de-katav shtar batzir mi-tlatin yomi* — no one goes to the trouble of drafting and executing a written contract for a loan intended to be repaid in less than thirty days. The very fact that a *shtar* was written implies a minimum term of thirty days. But for a verbal loan — *malveh al peh* — perhaps no such assumption attaches, and the lender could demand repayment much sooner.

### Shmuel's Challenge

A *baraita* is then cited that explicitly contradicts Rabba bar bar Chana's distinction: *echad ha-malveh bi-shtar ve-echad ha-malveh al peh* — whether the loan was written or oral, the thirty-day minimum applies equally. Shmuel reportedly told Rav Matna: *lo titeiv akareicha ad de-parshaht lah l'ha shmaita* — I will not let you leave your seat until you explain this teaching. How can the thirty-day minimum apply identically to both written and oral loans, given that the reasoning based on the effort of drafting a *shtar* would seem to distinguish them?

### Gezera from Shemita

The resolution comes from the verse in Devarim: *karvah shnat hasheva shnat hashmitah* — the seventh year, the year of release, is approaching. The apparent redundancy — having said "the seventh year," why add "the year of *shemita*"? — generates a *drasha*: *yesh shmitah acheret she-hi kazot* — there is another *shemita* that resembles this one. *Ve'eizu zu? Hamalveh et chavero stam she'eino rashai letov'o pachot mishloshim yom* — which *shemita*? The rule that an unspecified loan may not be collected before thirty days. Just as the *shemita* year creates a protected window during which debts cannot be pressed, the unspecified loan creates a protected window of thirty days during which the lender cannot demand repayment. This derivation applies equally to written and oral loans, since it is grounded in the verse's analogy rather than in any inference from the act of drafting a contract.

### Fiscal Year Analogy

The Gemara then connects this to a broader principle: *shloshim yom beshanah chashuv shanah* — thirty days within a year counts as a full year for certain purposes. This principle surfaces across a range of legal contexts. In the laws of *chazaka*, as discussed in the third *perek* of Bava Batra, questions arise about how to count the years of undisturbed possession that establish legal tenure. In the laws of *metzarin* — neighbors' rights — and in questions of fiscal-year calculations for leases and agreements, the same thirty-day threshold functions as a minimum unit that can stand in for a full year. The discussion of this principle at the end of the amud connects the loan minimum back to a fundamental and recurring *halakhic* construct about how time is measured and how partial periods are treated in Jewish law.
