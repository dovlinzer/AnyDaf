# Bava Kamma 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Positions

### Rabbi Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּזַל פָּרָה, וְנִתְעַבְּרָה אֶצְלוֹ וְיָלְדָה; רָחֵל, וְנִטְעֲנָה אֶצְלוֹ וּגְזָזָהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** If one **robbed** another of **a cow, and it became pregnant in his** possession, **and it** then **gave birth;** or if one robbed another of **a ewe, and it became laden** with wool **in his** possession, **and he** then **sheared it,** the robber **pays according to** the value of the animal at **the time of the robbery.**

The daf opens at the top third of 95b, where we are in the middle of a *baraita* presenting three opinions on a case in which someone steals a pregnant *parah* that subsequently gives birth, or a *rachel* laden with wool that is subsequently sheared. Rabbi Meir's position, discussed at the outset, is that the *gazlan* must return both the animal and its offspring, along with the *gizot* and *veladot*. The *gemara* explains that even though Rabbi Meir holds *shinuy koneh* — that a modification in the stolen object effects a legal acquisition — he nevertheless imposes this obligation as a *kenas*: one should not profit from an object stolen from another.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: גְּזֵילָה חוֹזֶרֶת בְּעֵינֶיהָ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא שׁוּמָא אֶצְלוֹ בְּכֶסֶף. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion of the *baraita*. **Rabbi Yehuda says: A stolen item is returned as is. Rabbi Shimon says:** The stolen item **is viewed as though it had been monetarily appraised** at the time of the robbery. The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between them,** since ostensibly they agree that the robber does not return the value of the enhancement to the robbery victim?

The *gemara* then turns to the positions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Yehuda states: *gezelah chozeret be'einya* — the stolen object returns in its current state. The *gemara* interprets this to mean that the stolen object is returned as it was at the time of the theft, with the *gazlan* paying the difference if the object is now worth less. Notably, neither Rabbi Yehuda's nor Rabbi Shimon's position makes entirely clear whether the *gazlan* acquires the *gizot* — whether the shearing itself constitutes a sufficient *shinuy* in the sheep — but both positions converge on the principle that the *gazlan* pays the value of the object as it stood at the time of the theft.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon's formulation is: *ro'in otah ke'ilu hi shumah etzlo bekesef* — we assess the value of the stolen object as if it were assessed with him at its monetary value. This similarly points toward payment based on the value at the time of theft (*keshat hag'zeilah*). At first glance, the two positions appear identical, and the *gemara* therefore asks: *mai beinayhu* — what is the practical difference between them?

## Yehuda vs. Shimon

### Zevid Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: בְּשֶׁבַח שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי גְּזֵילָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: דְּנִגְזָל הָוֵי, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: דְּגַזְלָן הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid says: They disagree with regard to enhancement that is** still **upon the stolen item.** The disagreement concerns a case where the enhancement occurred while the stolen item was in the possession of the robber, and when he returned the item it was still enhanced. Therefore, he did not keep any of the enhancement. **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that the enhancement **belongs to the one who was robbed, and Rabbi Shimon holds that it belongs to the robber.**

Zevid answers that the dispute concerns *shevach she'al gabei gezelah* — the improvement accrued to the stolen object while in the *gazlan*'s possession. In the case where the animal has not yet been sheared but has grown additional wool during the period of theft, who is entitled to that increased value? According to Zevid's reading, Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *shevach* belongs to the *nigzal* — the original owner — since the phrase *chozeret be'einya* implies returning the object along with whatever increase has accrued. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, holds that the *shevach* belongs to the *gazlan*.

This is a striking position. If the wool has simply grown incrementally during the theft, what is the basis for the *gazlan* to retain it? It is not an obvious *shinuy koneh* — the change is gradual and natural, not a deliberate modification. The *gemara* does not immediately resolve this tension.

### Rav Pappa Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, שֶׁבַח שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי גְּזֵילָה – דְּגַזְלָן הָוֵי; וְהָכָא לְמֶחֱצָה, לִשְׁלִישׁ וְלִרְבִיעַ קָמִיפַּלְגִי – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: שֶׁבַח שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי גְּזֵילָה – כּוּלֵּיהּ דְּגַזְלָן הָוֵי, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: לְמֶחֱצָה, לִשְׁלִישׁ וְלִרְבִיעַ הוּא דְּשָׁקֵיל גַּזְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** that the disagreement between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon can be explained differently: **Everyone** agrees that the **enhancement that is** still **upon the stolen item belongs to the robber, and here they disagree** with regard to whether the robber can keep **one-half,** or **one-third, or one-fourth** of the value of the enhancement, and the remainder is kept by the owner of the animal. **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that the **enhancement that is** still **upon the stolen item belongs entirely to the robber, and Rabbi Shimon holds** that **the robber takes one-half,** or **one-third, or one-fourth** of the value of the enhancement. In other words, he is treated as a shepherd or rancher and receives the share of the enhancement that local custom dictates be paid to that type of laborer.

Rav Pappa offers an alternative reading: *dechulei alma shevach she'al gabei gezelah degazlan havei* — all opinions agree that the improvement to the stolen object belongs to the *gazlan*. This is itself a striking assertion, and it begs the question of why the *gazlan* should have any claim to the natural increase of an animal he wrongfully possesses.

## Takkanat Hashavim

### Tosafot Explanation

Tosafot addresses this directly, explaining that the *gazlan*'s entitlement to the *shevach* is not grounded in *shinuy koneh* at all. It makes little conceptual sense to say that because an animal grew a bit more wool while in the possession of a thief, that incremental growth constitutes a *shinuy* sufficient to transfer ownership. Rather, Tosafot explains, this is a *takkanat hashavim* — a rabbinic enactment designed to facilitate the return of stolen property.

### Purpose & Incentive

The purpose of the enactment is to lower the barrier to repentance and return. If a *gazlan* who has increased the value of a stolen object is required to return not only the principal but also every bit of the increase, he may be discouraged from returning it at all. By allowing him to retain the *shevach*, the Rabbis created an incentive to return the *keren* — the principal — even at the cost of the increase. The fact that the *gemara* here describes this benefit as belonging to the *gazlan* without explicit reference to *shinuy* lends support to Tosafot's reading that the underlying principle is institutional rather than legal-theoretical.

## Yehuda vs. Shimon…

### Portion Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: גָּזַל פָּרָה, וְנִתְעַבְּרָה אֶצְלוֹ וְיָלְדָה; רָחֵל, וְנִטְעֲנָה אֶצְלוֹ וּגְזָזָהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵילָה. יָלְדָה – אִין, לֹא יָלְדָה – הָדְרָא בְּעֵינָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof for Rav Zevid's explanation: **We learned** in the mishna (93b): If one **robbed** another of **a cow, and it became pregnant in his** possession, **and it** then **gave birth;** or if one robbed another of **a ewe, and it became laden** with wool **in his** possession, **and he** then **sheared it,** the robber **pays according to** the value of the animal at **the time of the robbery.** The Gemara infers from the mishna: If the cow **gave birth, yes,** he pays the value of the animal at the time of the theft, but if the cow **did not give birth** and is still pregnant, it is **returned as is.**

Given that all agree the *gazlan* retains the *shevach*, the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon according to Rav Pappa concerns the *degree* to which he retains it. The *gemara* states: *de-kulei alma shevach gabei de-Gazlan havei, ve-hakha le-machtze le-shlish u-le-revia kamiflegi* — here the dispute is whether the *gazlan* keeps all the increase, a half, a third, or a quarter.

Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *gazlan* keeps the entire *shevach*. His phrase *chozeret be'einya* is now read to mean: you return the object exactly as you took it, and you retain all of the increase over and above that baseline. Rabbi Shimon, whose language is *ro'in otah ke'ilu hi shumah* — we assess its value — implies that the *gazlan* receives only a proportional share of the *shevach*, analogous to the arrangement with a sharecropper who tends animals in exchange for a percentage of their increased value. The precise fraction — half, a third, or a quarter — depends on the local custom for such arrangements.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא שׁוּמָא אֶצְלוֹ בְּכֶסֶף – לְמֶחֱצָה, לִשְׁלִישׁ וְלִרְבִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the explanation **of Rav Pappa.** The *baraita* states: **Rabbi Shimon says:** The stolen item **is viewed as though it had been monetarily appraised** at the time of the theft, **for** the purpose of keeping **one-half,** or **one-third, or one-fourth** of the value of the enhancement.

A *baraita* subsequently confirms this reading of Rabbi Shimon's position: *Rabbi Shimon omer ro'in otah ke'ilu hi shumah etzlo bekesef, ve-lemechtza ve-lishlish ve-lirvia* — the assessment is made, and the *gazlan* takes a portion accordingly. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי רַב כָּהֲנָא, אִיבַּעְיָא לַן: לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר לְמֶחֱצָה, לִשְׁלִישׁ וְלִרְבִיעַ הוּא דְּשָׁקֵיל גַּזְלָן, כִּי מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ – בִּדְמֵי מְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא מִבִּשְׂרָא שָׁקֵיל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: When we were** studying **in the study hall of Rav Kahana, we were asked** a question: **According** to the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says that the robber takes one-half,** or **one-third, or one-fourth** of the value of the enhancement; **when we remove** the robber, taking the stolen animal from him and paying him a portion of the enhanced value, do **we remove him** by paying him his share **with money? Or, perhaps he takes** his portion **from the meat** of the animal.

Rav Ashi adds that when he was in the academy of Rav Kahana, a further question was raised: when Rabbi Shimon grants the *gazlan* a share of the *shevach*, does this mean he remains a partner in the animal going forward — entitled to, say, a portion of the calf when it is eventually born — or can the *nigzal* pay him off in cash and thereby dissolve any ongoing partnership? Is the *gazlan* entitled to demand his proportional share of the physical animal itself, or only to receive a monetary payment?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְשַׁטְנָא מֵהָא דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שְׁלֹשָׁה שָׁמִין לָהֶן הַשֶּׁבַח, וּמַעֲלִין אוֹתָן בְּדָמִים; וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: בְּכוֹר לְפָשׁוּט,
>
> **Translation:** **And we resolved the** dilemma **from that which Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** In **three** cases, the court **appraises the enhanced** value **for** the parties involved in enhancing a field, **and they are paid in money** rather than by being given a portion of the property, **and these are they: A firstborn** son who makes payment **to a regular** son. This is a case where two sons, one firstborn and the other not, inherit a field from their father. Before it is divided, they both work and enhance the field. When the time comes to divide the field, the firstborn son, who receives a double portion, must pay his brother for the enhancement that the latter contributed to the former's portion. This payment is given in money rather than land.

The question is resolved from a ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *shelosha shamin lahen hashevach u-mesalkinan lehu bi-damim* — in three cases, one assesses the *shevach* and pays off the relevant party in cash, without that party remaining a partner. The *gazlan* is presumed to fall into this category as well, meaning he can be paid off and does not retain an ongoing stake in the stolen property.

## Three Separation Cases

### Bechor Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעַל חוֹב לְלוֹקֵחַ, וּבַעַל חוֹב לִיתוֹמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the second case is that of **a creditor** who is obligated **to a purchaser,** i.e., a creditor who collects the debt from lands that were sold by the debtor. He pays money to the purchaser for the enhancements generated by the purchaser but does not pay him in land. **And** the third case is that of **a creditor** who is obligated **to orphans,** i.e., a creditor who collects land from the orphans of his debtor. He must pay them for any enhancements done by the orphans after their father's death. This payment is also given in money rather than land.

The three cases enumerated by Shmuel are as follows. The first is *bechor le-pashut* — the firstborn son versus the non-firstborn. When brothers inherit an estate, the *bechor* is entitled to a double share (*pi shnayim*). If, prior to the division of the estate, the *bechor* improves the land through his labor and increases its value, that increase is shared equally among all heirs — since it accrued while the estate remained undivided, it is treated as a collective gain. As a result, when the land is ultimately divided two-thirds and one-third, the *bechor* ends up retaining more of the increase than he is proportionally entitled to, having taken a double share of land that happened to include the full value of his equally-distributed improvement. The siblings cannot demand to remain partners in the land; rather, the *bechor* is entitled to pay them off for the excess and retain his full allotted portion.

### Creditor Case

The second case is *ba'al chov le-lokeach* — a creditor collecting from a purchaser. The scenario is as follows: a debtor sold a field to a purchaser, and that purchaser subsequently improved the field. The creditor now comes to seize the field in satisfaction of the debt, and the field — including the purchaser's improvements — is worth more than the original debt. The creditor is entitled to seize the whole field and pay the purchaser for the value he added, rather than accepting the purchaser as a continuing partner in the land.

### Orphans Case

The third case, *ba'al chov de-yatmei*, follows the same principle: a creditor collecting from orphans who have improved the estate. The improvements made by the orphans are assessed, and the creditor seizes the full property while paying them off for their contribution — they cannot insist on remaining partners.

All three cases share the same structure: someone other than the original owner has increased the value of property to which another party has a prior claim. The party with the prior claim is entitled to seize the whole and pay off the improver, rather than entering into a partnership with them.

## Shmuel Contradiction

### Reimburse Purchaser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, מִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּעַל חוֹב לְלוֹקֵחַ יָהֵיב לֵיהּ שֶׁבַח? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּשֶׁבַח הַמַּגִּיעַ לִכְתֵפַיִם, וְכָאן בְּשֶׁבַח שֶׁאֵין מַגִּיעַ לִכְתֵפַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses Shmuel's statement. **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Did Shmuel** actually **say** that **a creditor gives the purchaser** any of the **enhanced** value at all? **But doesn't Shmuel say: A creditor collects** all of **the enhanced** value? Rav Ashi **said to him:** This is **not difficult. Here,** where Shmuel stated that a creditor does not collect the enhanced value, it was **with regard to** a case where there was **enhancement that reaches shoulders [*shevaḥ hammaggia likhtefayim*],** i.e., the produce that grew due to the improvements made by the purchaser is fully grown and ripened and can now be harvested and carried upon one's shoulders. **But there,** where Shmuel stated that a creditor does collect the enhanced value, it was **with regard to** a case where there was **enhancement that does not reach shoulders,** i.e., its growth is not complete.

Rav Pappa raises a difficulty against this ruling. He asks Rav Ashi: does Shmuel truly hold that a *ba'al chov* must pay the *lokeach* for the *shevach* the *lokeach* created?

### Creditor Collects

There is a second statement attributed to Shmuel: *ba'al chov goveh et ha-shevach* — the creditor collects the *shevach* along with the principal. This appears to say that the creditor takes the full improved value of the land without any obligation to reimburse the purchaser at all. The two statements of Shmuel are directly contradictory.

### Resolution: Ready vs. Na…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא מַעֲשִׂים בְּכׇל יוֹם, וְקָא מַגְבֵּי שְׁמוּאֵל אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁבַח הַמַּגִּיעַ לִכְתֵפַיִם! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא קַשְׁיָא;
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him: But** there were **daily incidents** of this type, **and Shmuel would collect** from purchasers **even enhancement that reached shoulders.** Rav Ashi **said to him: This** is **not difficult.**

The resolution given is: *lo kashya, kan be-shevach ha-magiya li-ktefayim, kan be-shevach she-eino magiya li-ktefayim* — it is not difficult; one ruling applies to *shevach ha-magiya li-ktefayim* and the other to *shevach she-eino magiya li-ktefayim*. Rashi explains *magiya li-ktefayim* as referring to wheat that has grown tall enough to be harvested — crops that are effectively ready to be reaped and are therefore conceptually distinct from the land itself. Since such produce is on the verge of being separable from the land, the creditor must reimburse the purchaser for it. By contrast, if the *shevach* has not yet reached that stage, it is treated as simply an enhancement to the land's value, which the creditor is entitled to seize along with the land.

The *gemara* then raises a further complication: it is known that in practice Shmuel permitted creditors to collect the *shevach* even in cases where crops had reached shoulder height. This seems to undercut the resolution.

## Labor vs. Natural Growth

### Tosafot Alternative

Tosafot offers an alternative reading of *shevach ha-magiya li-ktefayim*. Rather than referring to the physical height of the crops, Tosafot understands the phrase to denote improvement that came about through labor and effort — literally, improvement that required someone's shoulders, their physical exertion. *Shevach she-eino magiya li-ktefayim* is improvement that occurred naturally, without the purchaser's input — for example, an orchard that simply bore fruit over the course of the year as a result of rain alone. For such naturally occurring increase, the creditor need not reimburse the purchaser at all; it is simply an appreciation in the land's value, which the creditor's lien entitles him to take.

For improvement that required the purchaser's labor and effort, however, the purchaser has a legitimate claim to reimbursement. The *gemara* then asks how this squares with Shmuel's known practice of collecting even labor-induced *shevach* without reimbursement.

## Loan Amount Resolution

### Loan Less Than Value

The resolution distinguishes based on the size of the loan relative to the value of the land. If the outstanding loan is smaller than the total current value of the land — including the purchaser's improvements — the creditor is not entitled to take more than the loan amount, and must therefore reimburse the purchaser for the excess created by the purchaser's effort.

### Loan Exceeds Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא דְּמַסֵּיק בֵּיהּ כְּשִׁיעוּר אַרְעָא וּשְׁבָחָא, הָא דְּלָא מַסֵּיק בֵּיהּ אֶלָּא כְּשִׁיעוּר אַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **This** ruling, that Shmuel would collect the enhanced value, is for a case **where** the creditor **is owed by** the debtor **the amount of** the value of **the land and the enhanced value. That** ruling, that Shmuel would not collect the value of the enhancement, is for a case where the creditor **is owed by** the debtor **only the amount of** the value of **the land.**

If, however, the loan equals or exceeds the total current value of the land, the creditor is entitled to seize the entire property. In such a case, even if the purchaser's labor contributed to bringing the land up to the value of the loan, the creditor takes everything. The purchaser invested in land that he knew was encumbered; and since the debtor has guaranteed the purchaser against loss, the purchaser retains his claim against the original debtor. Tosafot notes explicitly that one reason the creditor can act this way without being considered unfair to the purchaser is precisely because the purchaser has that guarantee of indemnification from the seller.

## Purchaser's Rights

### Purchaser Can Force Paym…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִי אִית לֵיהּ זוּזֵי לְלוֹקֵחַ – לָא מָצֵי מְסַלֵּק לֵיהּ לְבַעַל חוֹב, שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִי אִית לֵיהּ זוּזֵי לְלוֹקֵחַ – מָצֵי מְסַלֵּק לֵיהּ לְבַעַל חוֹב, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: אִי הֲווֹ לִי זוּזֵי – הֲוָה מְסַלֵּיקְנָא לָךְ מִכּוּלֵּיהּ אַרְעָא, הַשְׁתָּא – הַב לִי גְּרִיוָא דְאַרְעָא שִׁיעוּר שְׁבָחַאי!
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him: This works out well according to the one who says: If the purchaser** of the field **has money, he** still **cannot remove the creditor** from the land, i.e., the creditor has the right to collect the land. This is **well. But according to the one who says: If the purchaser** of the field **has money, he can remove the creditor** from the land, i.e., the purchaser can choose to pay him money instead, **let** the purchaser **say: If I had money, I would remove you from all of the land; now** that I do not have sufficient money to pay what you are owed, **give me** at least **a *se'a* [*griva*] of land,** which is **the amount of my enhancement.**

This resolution, however, creates a tension with a separate debate. The *gemara* raises the question: according to the view that if the purchaser has cash on hand, he may pay the creditor off and thereby prevent the creditor from seizing the land — if that is true, then consider the following. The creditor comes to seize the land and pay the purchaser the value of his improvement. The purchaser, who has invested $500 in the land, might argue: had I had the funds, I could have paid you off entirely and kept the whole land. Now that you are seizing the land, you should at least leave me a plot of land corresponding to my $500 share of the improvement.

### Purchaser Cannot Force

The *gemara* addresses the opposing view as well: if the purchaser cannot force the creditor to accept payment and walk away — if the creditor has the right to insist on taking the land — then by the same logic, the creditor can take the full improved land and pay the purchaser the cash value of the improvement. Since the purchaser would not in any case have had the right to force the creditor away, the creditor's right to seize and pay off is unimpeded.

### Apotiki Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּשַׁוְּיַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא יְהֵא לְךָ פֵּרָעוֹן אֶלָּא מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the debtor **set aside** this field as **designated repayment for him, as he said to him: You shall not be repaid from anything but this,** in which case he clearly has a lien on this field and nothing else.

The *gemara* resolves the tension by invoking a special case: *de-shaviyah nihaleh apotiki* — the debtor designated this specific plot of land as *apotiki*, dedicated collateral. In an *apotiki* arrangement, the creditor's rights are concentrated specifically on that designated property. While this weakens his claims against the debtor's other assets, it correspondingly strengthens his rights to this particular land. Because of this designation, the purchaser has no capacity to pay the creditor off and make him go away — the *apotiki* creates a focused lien that overrides the purchaser's ordinary rights. Therefore, the creditor may seize the land and pay the purchaser off, and the purchaser cannot insist on retaining a share.

The broader point of this entire digression is that when a *gazlan* has increased the stolen object's value, and the time comes for the *nigzal* to reclaim it, the *gazlan* — or his *lokeach* — can be paid off rather than remaining a partner. This principle is consistent across the *bechor*, creditor, and *gazlan* cases.

## Transfer of Shevach Righ…

### Rights Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: גָּזַל וְהִשְׁבִּיחַ וּמָכַר, וְגָזַל וְהִשְׁבִּיחַ וְהוֹרִישׁ – מַה שֶּׁהִשְׁבִּיחַ מָכַר, מַה שֶּׁהִשְׁבִּיחַ הוֹרִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues the discussion of a stolen item that has been enhanced. **Rava says:** If one **robbed** another of an item **and enhanced** it **and sold** it to another, **and** similarly, if one **robbed** another of an item **and enhanced** it and then died **and bequeathed** it, **he sold that which he enhanced and bequeathed that which he enhanced.** And the robbery victim, should he wish to recover the stolen item, must pay the purchaser or heir for the enhanced value.

Returning to the rights of the *gazlan* and subsequent purchasers in stolen property, Rava rules: *gazal ve-hishbiach u-machar* — if one stole, improved the stolen object, and sold it — the *nigzal* comes to reclaim the animal, and the purchaser says: pay me for the increase, since the *gazlan* was permitted to retain the *shevach*, and that right was transferred to me. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: הִשְׁבִּיחַ לוֹקֵחַ, מַהוּ? בָּתַר דְּבַעְיַהּ, הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: מָה מָכַר רִאשׁוֹן לַשֵּׁנִי? כׇּל זְכוּת שֶׁתָּבֹא לְיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma** related to the aforementioned *halakha*: **What is** the *halakha* if **a purchaser enhanced** the stolen item? Must the robbery victim pay the purchaser for the enhanced value or not? **After** Rava **raised the dilemma, he then resolved it: What has the first** person **sold to the second** in any sale? He has sold **any rights that will come into his possession.** Since the robber had the rights to the value of his enhancements to the stolen item, he sold those rights to the purchaser as well.

Rava then raises the question of whether the same applies when the *lokeach* himself was responsible for the improvement. He resolves it: *mah machar rishon le-sheni kol zechut she-ba le-yado* — whatever the first seller transferred to the second buyer encompasses all rights that had accrued to the first seller. Just as the *gazlan* had rights to retain the *shevach*, so too the *lokeach* who purchased from him inherits those rights.

### Non-Jewish Purchaser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: הִשְׁבִּיחַ גּוֹי, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָה מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: תַּקַּנְתָּא לְגוֹי נֵיקוּ וְנַעְבֵּיד?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if **a gentile** robber **enhanced** the value of a stolen item? **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: Shall we arise and institute an ordinance for** the benefit of **a gentile?**

Rava then asks: *hishbiach oved kochavim mahu* — what if it is a non-Jew who improved the stolen object? Does a non-Jew benefit from the same entitlement to the *shevach*? Rav Achai of Difti challenges this sharply: *takanta le-ovdei kochavim meiku ve-nabdi* — are we going to institute a rabbinic enactment for the benefit of non-Jews? This formulation is significant, because it is the first time the *gemara* uses the language of *takanta* directly in this context, lending explicit support to Tosafot's position that the entire framework of the *gazlan*'s rights to the *shevach* is grounded in *takkanat hashavim* rather than in any formal *shinuy koneh*.

### Complex Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, כְּגוֹן דְּגַזְלַ[הּ] יִשְׂרָאֵל וְזַבְּנַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ, וְהִשְׁבִּיחָהּ גּוֹי, וַהֲדַר גּוֹי וְזַבְּנַהּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל. מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא יִשְׂרָאֵל וַהֲדַר יִשְׂרָאֵל – עָבְדִי רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא גּוֹי בָּאֶמְצַע – לָא עֲבַדוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This question **is not necessary** except **where** it was the case **that a Jew robbed** another of the item **and sold it to** a gentile, **and the gentile enhanced it, and** then the **gentile went back and sold it to** another **Jew. What** is the *halakha* in this case? **Do we say: Since it was initially** stolen by **a Jew and** was **then** acquired by **a Jew, the Sages did institute an ordinance? Or, perhaps since there is** the ownership of **a gentile intervening, the Sages did not institute an ordinance.** The Gemara comments: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* explores the more complex case: a Jewish *gazlan* — Reuven — steals the animal, sells it to a non-Jew (Christopher), who improves it, and then sells it to a Jewish purchaser (Shimon). The question is: *mahu*? Do we say that since the chain began with a Jew and ends with a Jew, the Rabbis' enactment applies and Shimon may retain the *shevach*? Or do we say that since a non-Jew is in the middle — and no enactment was made for non-Jews — the *takanta* is severed and Shimon cannot claim the *shevach*? The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teku*.

## Shinuy Koneh Palm Tree

### Chopped Palm Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי מַאן דִּגְזַל דִּיקְלָא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ וְקַטְלֵיהּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּשַׁדְיֵאּ מֵאַרְעָא לְאַרְעָא דִּידֵיהּ, לָא קְנֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? מֵעִיקָּרָא ״דִּיקְלָא״ מִיקְּרֵי, וְהַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי ״דִּיקְלָא״ מִיקְּרֵי. דִּיקְלָא (וַעֲבֵיד) [וְעַבְדֵיהּ] גּוּבֵּי, לָא קָנֵי. הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת ״גּוּבֵּי דְּדִיקְלָא״ מִיקְּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one robbed another of wood and fashioned it into vessels, he acquired the wood due to the change. The Gemara discusses what qualifies as a change. **Rav Pappa said:** With regard to **this one who robbed another of a palm tree and cut it** down, **although he threw it from the land** of the robbery victim **to his own land,** he **did not acquire** it. **What is the reason** for this? The tree was **initially called a palm tree, and now,** although it has been cut down, **it is also called a palm tree,** so the change to the item is not sufficiently significant for the robber to acquire it. Moreover, if one robbed another of **a palm tree and made** it into **logs, he did not acquire it,** since **now, in any event, they are called palm tree logs.**

Rav Pappa now opens a systematic analysis of *shinuy koneh* through a series of cases involving progressive transformation of a stolen object. The first: *hai man de-gazal dikla me-chavrei ve-katlei* — one who steals a palm tree from his neighbor by chopping it down and causing it to fall into his own field. Even so, *lo kani* — the thief does not acquire it. The reason: *mei-ikara dikla mikrei ve-hashta nami dikla mikrei* — before, it was called a palm tree; now, it is still called a palm tree — a chopped-down palm tree, but a palm tree nonetheless.

This is a striking ruling on multiple grounds. First, compared to earlier discussions in which even slight physical changes — a cow becoming fatter, a ewe giving birth — were treated as potentially significant, here a dramatic physical act — the felling of an entire tree — is deemed insufficient because it does not produce a *shinuy hashem*. Second, there is an interesting structural issue: normally we assume the theft precedes the *shinuy*. Here, however, since *karka einah nigzelet* — land cannot be the object of *gezelah* — the act of chopping the tree down is simultaneously the act of *gezelah* and whatever potential *shinuy* is involved. The *gemara* implies that had the chopping constituted a true *shinuy*, this would be a case of *gezelah ve-shinuy ba'im ke-echad* — the theft and the modification occurring simultaneously — and the *gazlan* would acquire. But since it does not constitute a *shinuy*, the question does not arise.

### Palm Logs

If one then takes the chopped palm tree and turns it into logs: *hashta mihat guvei de-dikla mikrei* — they are still called "palm logs." Again, *lo kani* — no acquisition.

### Logs to Beams

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּבֵּי וְעַבְדִינְהוּ כְּשׁוּרֵי – קָנֵי. כְּשׁוּרֵי רַבְרְבֵי וְעַבְדִינְהוּ כְּשׁוּרֵי זוּטְרֵי – לָא קָנֵי. עַבְדִינְהוּ קְצוּצְיָיתָא – קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if one robbed another of **logs and fashioned them** into **beams, he has acquired** them, since this change is significant. If one robbed another of **large beams and fashioned them** into **small beams, he did not acquire them,** since they are still called beams. If he **fashioned them** into **boards, he did acquire** them.

If, however, one takes those logs and fashions them into beams — *guvei ve-avdinhu kishurei* — then *kani*: the thief acquires. The transformation from raw logs to worked beams is sufficient to constitute *shinuy koneh*. The intuition tracks the lumber industry: felled trees and rough logs retain a natural character; fashioned beams represent something qualitatively different.

### Beam to Smaller Beam

If one stole a beam and reduced it into a smaller beam — *kishurei ve-avdinhu kishurei zutrei* — *lo kani*. Cutting a large beam into smaller ones does not change the category of the object in any meaningful way; they are simply different beams.

### Beams to Planks

If, however, one fashioned beams into planks — *avdinhu kitzutzita* — then *kani*. Planks represent a structurally distinct category from beams, and the transformation is sufficient to effect *shinuy koneh*.

## Shinuy Koneh Lulab

### Lulab to Broom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דִּגְזַל לוּלִיבָּא וְעַבְדִינְהוּ הוּצֵי – קָנֵי. דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא ״לוּלִיבָּא״ מִיקְּרֵי, וְהַשְׁתָּא ״הוּצֵי״. הוּצֵי וְעַבְדִינְהוּ חוּפְיָא – קָנֵי. מֵעִיקָּרָא ״הוּצֵי״, וְהַשְׁתָּא ״חוּפְיָא״. חוּפְיָא וְעַבְדֵיהּ שַׁרְשׁוּרָא – לָא קָנֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּהֲדַר סָתַר לֵיהּ וְהָוֵי חוּפְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: This one who robbed** another of **a palm frond and fashioned it** into **leaves [*hutzei*],** i.e., he removed the leaves from the spine, **has acquired** it, **since initially it was called a palm frond and now** it is called **leaves.** One who robbed another of **leaves and fashioned them** into **a broom has acquired them,** since **initially** they were called **leaves, and now** they are called **a broom.** One who robbed another of **a broom** and **fashioned it** into **a rope has not acquired it. What is the reason** for this? The reason is **that** he can **go back** and **unravel it, and it will** once again **be a broom.**

Rava contributes a parallel series of cases involving a stolen *lulav*. If one stole a *lulav* and fashioned it into a broom (*hutzi*) — detaching the leaves from the spine and binding them to create a sweeping implement — *kani*. *De-mei-ikara lulava mikrei ve-hashta hutzi* — originally it was called a *lulav*, now it is called a broom. The change in name reflects a genuine change in category.

### Broom to Chufya

If one then takes the broom leaves and splits them — *hutzi ve-avdinhu chufya* — again *kani*. A *chufya* is a more worked form of broom, produced by splitting each leaf along its fold to create finer strands for binding. *Me-ikara hutzi ve-hashta chufya* — the name has changed again, and the modification is an irreversible one.

### Reversible Rope

If, however, one takes the *chufya* and weaves the strands into a rope (*sharshura*): *lo kani* — no acquisition. The reason: *dehadar satrah lah* — one could unravel the rope and recover the broom. Since the change is reversible — the material could be restored to its prior form — the *gemara* classifies this as *chozer livriyato* (reversible modification), which does not constitute *shinuy koneh*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: נֶחְלְקָה הַתְּיוֹמֶת, מַהוּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי מָתוּן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: נִיטְּלָה הַתְּיוֹמֶת – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* if **the** central **twin-leaf became split,** i.e., is this considered a change through which a stolen *lulav* would be acquired? The Gemara cites a proof: **Come** and **hear that** which **Rabbi Matun says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** If **the** central **twin-leaf was removed,** the *lulav* **is unfit.**

Rav Pappa then raises a related question about the *tiyomet* — the central spine of the *lulav* — asking whether its splitting constitutes a *shinuy*. The *gemara* explores this through a ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that a *lulav* whose *tiyomet* has been removed is *pasul*, and reasons by analogy that if it has been split, it should be treated equivalently. Two versions of the ruling are cited; ultimately both point toward the split *tiyomet* being treated as *pasul* and therefore as a *shinuy* that would effect acquisition.

## Reversible Changes

### Dust Reversible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי מַאן דִּגְזַל עַפְרָא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ, וְעַבְדֵיהּ לְבֵינְתָּא – לָא קָנֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָדַר מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ עַפְרָא. לְבֵינְתָּא וְעַבְדַיהּ עַפְרָא – קָנֵי. מַאי אָמְרַתְּ – דִּלְמָא הָדַר וְעָבֵיד לֵיהּ לְבֵינְתָּא? הַאי לְבֵינְתָּא אַחֲרִיתִי הוּא, וּפָנִים חֲדָשׁוֹת בָּאוּ לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa said: This one who robbed another** of **earth and fashioned it** into **a brick has not acquired** it due to the change. **What is the reason** for this? It is **that he** can **return** it **and convert it** back into **earth.** By contrast, if he robbed another of **a brick, and** by crushing it **turned it** into **earth, he has acquired it** due to the change. **If you say: Perhaps** he will **return** it **and fashion it** into **a brick? This is a different brick, and a new entity has arrived,** i.e., entered into existence, **here.**

The *gemara* now articulates the general principle of *shinuy hachozer livriyato* through further paired examples. If one stole dirt and fashioned it into a brick — *gazal afra me-chavreih ve-avdeih levinta* — *lo kani*. Despite the apparent radicalness of the transformation, the brick could be crushed and restored to dust, and so it is considered *chozer livriyato*.

### Brick Irreversible

Conversely, if one stole a brick and reduced it to dust — *levinta ve-avdeih afra* — *kani*. One might think: I could simply remold the dust back into a brick. But the *gemara* responds: *hai levinta achriti hi* — that would be a new brick. *Panim chadashos bau lechan* — new form has emerged. The brick one would produce from that dust would not be the same brick that was stolen; it would be a distinct object. The dust itself is treated as continuous with the original material, but the new brick would constitute a transformation into something genuinely novel.

### Metal Reversible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי מַאן דִּגְזַל נְסָכָא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ, (וְעָבֵיד) [וְעַבְדֵיהּ] זוּזֵי – לָא קָנֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָדַר עָבֵיד לְהוּ נְסָכָא. זוּזֵי וְעַבְדִינְהוּ נְסָכָא – קָנֵי. מַאי אָמְרַתְּ – הָדַר עָבֵיד לְהוּ זוּזֵי? פָּנִים חֲדָשׁוֹת בָּאוּ לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Pappa** also **said: This one who robbed another** of **a bar of silver [*naskha*] and fashioned** it into **coins has not acquired it** due to the change. **What is the reason** for this? He can **return** it and by melting the coins **turn them** into **a bar of silver.** By contrast, if he robbed another of **coins and fashioned them** into **a bar of silver, he has acquired them** due to the change. **What do you say** in response to this, that perhaps he will **return and fashion them** into **coins?** These are new coins, and **a new entity has arrived here.**

Similarly: if one stole a piece of metal (*naska*) and minted it into a coin, *lo kani* — no acquisition. The reason: one could melt it back down into formless metal, and so the change is reversible.

### Coin Irreversible

If one stole coins and melted them into metal, *kani* — the thief acquires. One might object: I can simply re-mint coins from this metal. But the *gemara* responds: *panim chadashos bau lechan* — those would be new coins, not the same coins that were stolen.

### Polished Reversible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחִימֵי וְעַבְדִינְהוּ חַדְתֵי – לָא קָנֵי. חַדְתֵי וְעַבְדִינְהוּ שְׁחִימֵי – קָנֵי. מַאי אָמְרַתְּ – הָדַר עָבֵיד לְהוּ חַדְתֵי? מִידָּע יְדִיעַ שִׁיחְמַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa continues: If the stolen coins were **black [*sheḥimei*],** i.e., old and used, **and he made them** as **new** by cleaning them thoroughly, **he has not acquired** them. By contrast, if however, they were **new, and he made them black, he has acquired** them. **What do you say** in response to this, that perhaps he will **return** and **make them new** by cleaning them? **Their blackness is already known,** and therefore the coins have been changed irreversibly.

If one stole blackened coins and polished them — *shichimei ve-avdinhu charatei* — *lo kani*. The blackening will return naturally over time, and polishing is therefore *chozer livriyato*. Moreover, the *shinuy hashem* here is questionable — a polished coin and a blackened coin are arguably the same object in different states.

Conversely, if one stole polished coins and blackened them — *charatei ve-avdinhu shichimei* — *kani*. One might think this too is reversible, since one can simply polish the coins again. But the *gemara* notes that polishing removes a thin layer of silver each time — the coin that emerges after polishing is not fully restored to its original state. Since it is not entirely *chozer livriyato*, the blackening constitutes an irreversible modification and effects *shinuy koneh*.

## Payment Principle

### Universal Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַגַּזְלָנִין מְשַׁלְּמִין כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵלָה. ״זֶה הַכְּלָל״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעָא: גָּנַב טָלֶה וְנַעֲשָׂה אַיִל, עֵגֶל וְנַעֲשָׂה שׁוֹר – נַעֲשָׂה שִׁינּוּי בְּיָדוֹ, וּקְנָאוֹ. טָבַח וּמָכַר – שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא טוֹבֵחַ, שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא מוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **This is the principle: All robbers pay according to** the value of the stolen item at **the time of the robbery.** The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the phrase: **This is the principle?** The Gemara replies: It serves **to add that which Rabbi Ela says:** If one **stole a lamb and** during the time that it was in the thief's possession it **became a ram,** or if one stole **a calf and** it **became an ox,** then **a change occurred** while the animal was **in his possession, and he has acquired it** due to the change. If he then **slaughtered or sold** the animal, **he slaughters his own** animal **and he sells his own** animal, and he does not become liable to pay the penalty of four or five times the value of the animal.

The *gemara* closes this section with the general rule: *zeh ha-klal kol ha-gazlanin meshalmin ke-sha'at ha-gezeilah* — all *gazlanim* pay based on the value of the stolen object at the time of the theft. If the object has since decreased in value, the *gazlan* pays the original value; if it has increased, he pays the original value and retains the increase.

### Natural Development

The *klal* comes to include the case cited by Rav Ila: if one stole a *taleh* (lamb) and it grew into an *ayil* (ram), or a calf that became an ox — *na'aseh shinuy be-yado u-kana'o* — a *shinuy* has occurred in his hands and he acquires it. If he then slaughtered or sold it, it is his — he does not pay four and five (*arba ve-chamisha*). This is a *shinuy* that is *lo bi-yadayim* — it was not actively imposed by the thief but occurred naturally. Yet the *gemara* treats it as *shinuy koneh*. The contrast with the felled palm tree — a far more dramatic physical intervention that nonetheless failed to constitute *shinuy* — is notable and the *gemara* does not directly reconcile the two.

## Stolen Oxen Plowing

### Return Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּגְזַל פַּדָּנָא דְּתוֹרֵי מֵחַבְרֵיהּ, אֲזַל כְּרַב בְּהוּ כְּרָבָא, זְרַע בְּהוּ זַרְעָא. לְסוֹף אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ לְמָרֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ שׁוּמוּ שְׁבָחָא דְּאַשְׁבַּח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who robbed another** of **a pair [*padna*] of oxen.** He then **went** and **plowed** his field **with them,** and **sowed seeds with them, and eventually returned them to their owner.** The robbery victim **came before Rav Naḥman** to claim payment from the robber. Rav Naḥman **said to** the robbery victim and the robber: **Go estimate** the amount by **which** the value of the land **was enhanced** during the time that the pair of oxen was in the possession of the robber, and the robber must pay that amount.

The *gemara* presents the following scenario. A certain person stole a pair of oxen (*padna de-torei*) from his neighbor, plowed his field with them, planted crops, and then returned the oxen to their owner. He claimed he owed nothing further — after all, he had returned the *gezeilah* in its original form.

### Rav Nachman Ruling

The case came before Rav Nachman. This situation is the inverse of what has been discussed: rather than the stolen object itself increasing in value, the *gazlan* used the stolen object as a tool to increase the value of his own land. Rav Nachman ruled: *zil shum shivcha de-ashbach* — go assess how much the land increased in value, and have the *gazlan* reimburse the owner for that increase. This is a striking ruling, essentially requiring the *gazlan* to disgorge the benefit he derived from using the stolen oxen.

## Rava Challenge

### Partial Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: תּוֹרֵי אַשְׁבֻּח, אַרְעָא לָא אַשְׁבַּח?! אָמַר: מִי קָאָמֵינָא נְשַׁיְּימוּ כּוּלֵּיהּ? פַּלְגָא קָאָמֵינָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Naḥman: Did **the oxen** alone **enhance** the value of the land? **Did the land not** become **enhanced** in and of itself? Perhaps not all of the enhanced value of the land was due to the labor performed by the oxen. Rav Naḥman **said: Did I say** that **they should estimate** and give him **all of** the enhanced value? **I said** only **half.**

Rava challenged Rav Nachman on two grounds. First: *torei ashbach, ara lo ashbach?* — the oxen contributed to the increase in the land's value, but the land itself also contributed. If the land is now worth a hundred dollars more, it is not that the oxen alone generated that full increase; the land's own properties contributed to the outcome. Why should the *gazlan* be liable for the full improvement?

Rav Nachman clarified: *mi kamina neshumei kulei? ela palga kamina* — I did not mean he pays the full increase; I meant he pays half the increase. This accounts for the joint contribution of the oxen and the land.

### Original Form Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סוֹף סוֹף, גְּזֵילָה הוּא – וְקָא הָדְרָה בְּעֵינַאּ, דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַגַּזְלָנִין מְשַׁלְּמִין כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵלָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Ultimately, it is a stolen item and is returned as it was** at the time of the robbery, **as we learned** in a mishna: **All robbers pay according to** the value of the stolen item at **the time of the robbery.** Why should the robber also pay the owner half the value of the enhancement?

Rava's second objection was more fundamental: *sof sof gezeilah hi ve-ka hadra be-einah* — ultimately this is a case of *gezeilah*, and the stolen object is being returned in its original form. The *mishnah* itself states: *kol ha-gazlanin meshalmin ke-sha'at ha-gezeilah* — all *gazlanim* pay the value at the time of the theft. If the oxen are returned intact, the *gazlan* has discharged his obligation. The principle of *keshat hag'zeilah* should mean he owes no more. Why should he be required to pay for the increase his land derived from their use?

## Habitual Gazlan Punishme…

### Judicial Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא אָמֵינָא לָךְ, כִּי יָתֵיבְנָא בְּדִינָא לָא תֵּימָא לִי מִידֵּי, דְּאָמַר הוּנָא חַבְרִין עֲלַאי: ״אֲנָא וְשַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא – אַחֵי בְּדִינָא״? הַאי אִינָשׁ גַּזְלָנָא עַתִּיקָא הוּא, וּבָעֵינָא דְּאֶיקְנְסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to** Rava: **Didn't I tell you** that **when I am sitting in judgment, do not say anything to me,** i.e., do not question or comment upon my rulings. An indication that my rulings should not be questioned is **as our friend Huna has said about me,** that **King Shapur and I are brothers with regard to** monetary **laws,** i.e., with regard to monetary laws, my opinion is equal to that of Shmuel. **This man is an experienced robber, and I wish to penalize him.** Therefore, I compelled him to pay the enhanced value, although by right he is not obligated to do so.

Rav Nachman replied with a statement that cuts off further argument: *lav amina lach ki yativna be-dina lo teima li midi?* — have I not told you that when I am sitting in judgment, do not second-guess me? He invoked the testimony of his colleague Huna, who said of Rav Nachman that he and *Shvor Malka* — a name used for Shmuel, reflecting his close relationship with the Persian ruler — are colleagues in *din*. The implication is that Rav Nachman's authority in monetary law is of the highest order.

### Deterrent Punishment

The substantive reason Rav Nachman provided was: *hai inish gazlana atika hu, u-be'ina de-iknesay* — this man is a habitual, seasoned *gazlan*, and I wish to impose a fine upon him. In other words, Rav Nachman acknowledged that strictly speaking, Rava's analysis was correct — the *gazlan* who returns the oxen intact should owe no more than the value at the time of the theft. But for a recidivist *gazlan*, extra-legal judicial discretion — grounded in *hefker beit din hefker* — permits a court to go beyond the standard rules and impose an additional monetary sanction as a deterrent. This stands in pointed contrast to the many leniencies extended earlier in the *sugya* to encourage repentance; here, at the end, a measure of common sense and proportionality reasserts itself in the opposite direction.
