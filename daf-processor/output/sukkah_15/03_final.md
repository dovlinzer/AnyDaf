# Sukkah 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Planks & Gezeirat Tikrah

### Planks Overview

Yesterday's discussion centered on the use of wooden planks as *schach* and whether doing so raises a concern that the *sukkah* would come to resemble an ordinary house. *Rabbi Meir* clearly holds that such a concern exists. The question left open was whether *Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees categorically — rejecting any notion of a rabbinic prohibition on planks — or whether the two *Tannaim* in fact agree in principle but dispute only the threshold: *Rabbi Meir* considering planks of three *tefachim* problematic, while *Rabbi Yehuda* would only raise an objection once they reach four *tefachim* in width. We also established the important principle that even if a plank reaches the problematic width of four *tefachim*, it does not invalidate the *sukkah* as a whole; it merely means one may not sit or sleep beneath that particular plank. This distinction — between what renders a *sukkah* invalid entirely and what merely creates a restricted zone within an otherwise valid *sukkah* — will recur throughout our study.

### Mishnah: Three Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ תִּקְרָה שֶׁאֵין עָלֶיהָ מַעֲזִיבָה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְפַקְפֵּק, וְנוֹטֵל אַחַת מִבֵּינְתַיִם. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מְפַקְפֵּק, אוֹ נוֹטֵל אַחַת מִבֵּינְתַיִם. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: נוֹטֵל אַחַת מִבֵּינְתַיִם, וְאֵינוֹ מְפַקְפֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a roof** made of boards that are four handbreadths wide **upon which there is no** coat of **plaster, Rabbi Yehuda says** that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel disagree with regard to the manner in which to render it fit. **Beit Shammai say: One moves** each board, and then it is considered as though he placed the board there for the sake of the mitzva of *sukka*, **and one** then **removes one** board **from among** the boards and replaces it with fit roofing. **Beit Hillel say:** One need not perform both actions; rather, **one** must either **move** the boards **or remove one from among** them. **Rabbi Meir says: One** only **removes one from among** them **and does not move** the others.

The *mishnah* before us appears to be a continuation of that earlier discussion. It addresses the case of a *tikrah she-ein aleha ma'aziva* — a roof made of wooden planks that lacks plaster, and which therefore consists of material that is technically valid *schach* but raises the rabbinic concern discussed previously. *Rabbi Yehuda* frames this as a dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*, and then *Rabbi Meir* offers his own independent ruling.

### Beit Shammai: Both Acts

*Beit Shammai* holds that two acts are required: *mefakpek* — one must lift up and set back down each individual board — and *notel achat mibeintayim* — one must also remove every alternate board and replace it with valid *schach*. The rationale for requiring both actions simultaneously is not immediately obvious, and the *Gemara* will address this.

### Beit Hillel: One Act

*Beit Hillel* holds that either action alone is sufficient: either *mefakpek* — lifting up and setting back down — or *notel achat mibeintayim* — replacing every alternate board with valid *schach*. One of the two remedies will suffice.

### Rabbi Meir: Replace Only

*Rabbi Meir* rules that the only effective remedy is *notel achat mibeintayim* — replacing alternate boards. Lifting up and setting back down, he holds, accomplishes nothing.

### Two Problem Types

The *mishnah* thus presents two possible underlying problems. The first is *gezeirat tikrah* — the rabbinic concern that wide planks of wood resemble a permanent rooftop and are therefore intrinsically invalid as *schach*. The second is *taasah v'lo min ha'asuy* — the requirement that *schach* be placed actively for the purpose of the *sukkah*, which is violated when material was originally installed as an ordinary roof. Even if there is no intrinsic problem with the planks themselves, the fact that they were installed to serve as a year-round roof may mean they cannot automatically become *schach* without some new deliberate act.

The *taasah v'lo min ha'asuy* explanation seems especially apt for *Beit Hillel* as read by *Rabbi Yehuda*. Since *Rabbi Yehuda* includes *mefakpek* — lifting up and setting back down — as a valid remedy, and since that act does nothing to change the physical character of a plank, the obvious implication is that the plank itself is not problematic. The only issue is one of intent and designation: the roof was installed for the house; one must now perform a new act to dedicate it as *schach* for the *sukkah*.

## Gemara: Problem Type

### Beit Hillel via Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּית הִלֵּל, טַעְמַיְיהוּ מִשּׁוּם "תַּעֲשֶׂה" וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי. אִי מְפַקְפֵּק, עָבֵיד לֵיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה. אִי נוֹטֵל אַחַת מִבֵּינְתַיִם, עָבֵד בַּהּ מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Granted,** according to **Beit Hillel, their reason** for initially prohibiting this roof is **due to** the principle: **Prepare** it, **and not from that which has already been prepared. If one moves** the boards, **he performs an action.** Likewise, **if he removes one** of the boards **from among** them, **he** also **performs an action.** Therefore, in both cases, he prepared the roofing and the *sukka* is fit.

The *Gemara* opens its analysis by observing that *Beit Hillel*'s position is straightforwardly understood through *taasah v'lo min ha'asuy*. The planks are intrinsically valid *schach*; the only deficiency is that they were placed to serve as a house roof rather than as a *sukkah*. If one performs *mefakpek* — lifting up and setting back down — one has performed a new act, and the planks are now designated for the *sukkah*. Equally, if one replaces every alternate board, one has likewise performed an act with the majority of the material, which may suffice to redefine the entire covering as having been newly placed for the *sukkah*. Either way, the problem is remedied by a demonstrative act of rededication.

### Beit Shammai via Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ? אִי מִשּׁוּם "תַּעֲשֶׂה" וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי — בַּחֲדָא סַגִּי! אִי מִשּׁוּם גְּזֵרַת תִּקְרָה — בְּנוֹטֵל אַחַת מִבֵּינְתַיִם סַגִּי!
>
> **Translation:** **However,** with regard to the opinion of **Beit Shammai, what is the rationale** for **their** prohibition against using the original ceiling for a *sukka*? **If** the rationale is also **due to** the principle: **Prepare** it, **and not from that which has already been prepared, one** action **should suffice.** Or **if** the rationale is **due to the decree of the roof,** lest one come to reside beneath a regular plastered ceiling inside a house, **removing one** board **from among** them should **suffice.**

The difficulty lies with *Beit Shammai*. If the only problem is *taasah v'lo min ha'asuy*, then a single act — either *mefakpek* or alternating — should be sufficient. Why does *Beit Shammai* require both? Conversely, if the problem is *gezeirat tikrah* — if the planks are intrinsically rabbinically invalid — then *mefakpek* accomplishes nothing at all. Lifting an invalid plank and setting it back down does not transform it into valid *schach*. In that case, the only operative remedy would be replacing alternate boards and relying on some form of majority. If so, why does *Beit Shammai* include *mefakpek* as a required component?

### Gemara: Shammai Reread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם מִשּׁוּם גְּזֵרַת תִּקְרָה, וְהָכִי קָאָמְרִי: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמְּפַקְפֵּק, אִי נוֹטֵל אַחַת מִבֵּינְתַיִם — אִין, אִי לָא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the rationale is **due to the decree of the roof, and this is** what **they are saying: Although one moves** the boards, **if he removes one** board **from among** them, **yes,** it is fit; **if not, no,** it is unfit. Moving the boards is inconsequential. Removing one board from among them is all that is necessary.

The *Gemara* resolves this by re-reading *Beit Shammai*'s formulation. The correct understanding, the *Gemara* explains, is not that *Beit Shammai* requires both acts as joint conditions; rather, *Beit Shammai* is making the following statement: even if one performs *mefakpek*, it is of no avail — it does nothing — because the planks are intrinsically invalid due to *gezeirat tikrah*. The only thing that is effective is *notel achat mibeintayim*, replacing the alternate boards. *Mefakpek* is mentioned only to be dismissed. *Beit Hillel*, by contrast, holds that the planks are fundamentally valid *schach*, and therefore lifting up and setting back down is sufficient to address the intent problem.

The crux of the debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* is thus whether the planks are intrinsically invalid *schach* or intrinsically valid *schach*: *Beit Shammai* holds they are rabbinically invalid, and the only remedy is alternating; *Beit Hillel* holds they are valid, and a new act of dedication suffices.

### Rabbi Meir: No Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: נוֹטֵל אַחַת מִבֵּינְתַיִם, אֲבָל לֹא יְפַקְפֵּק — רַבִּי מֵאִיר הַיְינוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, say the latter clause** of the mishna: **Rabbi Meir says: One removes one from among** them **but does not move** the others. This indicates that the opinion of **Rabbi Meir is** identical to the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** as according to the above explanation, Beit Shammai also hold that removing one of the boards and replacing it with fit roofing can render the *sukka* fit. It is unreasonable to say that Rabbi Meir would hold in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai, which is rejected.

The *Gemara* then turns to *Rabbi Meir*'s ruling — that the only effective remedy is replacing the alternate boards and that *mefakpek* does not help — and observes that this sounds precisely like *Beit Shammai* as now understood. How could *Rabbi Meir* be adopting a *Beit Shammai* position?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּדָבָר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This is what** Rabbi Meir **is saying: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel do not disagree on this matter.** They agree that the boards are prohibited due to the decree of the roof and that only by removing one of the boards is the *sukka* rendered fit. Rabbi Meir disagrees with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda that there is a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel.

The *Gemara* explains that *Rabbi Meir* is responding to *Rabbi Yehuda*'s framing. *Rabbi Yehuda* had presented the question as a debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*, implying that *Rabbi Meir*'s insistence on the invalidity of planks aligns him with *Beit Shammai* against the ruling of *Beit Hillel*. *Rabbi Meir* pushes back: *lo necheleku Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel ba-davar zeh* — *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* did not dispute this matter. What he is presenting is not the opinion of *Beit Shammai* against *Beit Hillel*, but rather the consensus of all — *divrei hakol*. There is no *Tannaic* authority who validates planks. The apparent debate attributed by *Rabbi Yehuda* to *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* simply did not occur, in *Rabbi Meir*'s view. The positions of *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Meir* thus come into sharp relief: *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that planks are valid *schach* and the only concern is the intent under which they were placed, while *Rabbi Meir* holds that planks are intrinsically rabbinically invalid and must be interspersed with valid *schach*.

## Repetition Question

### Repetition Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אִית לֵיהּ גְּזֵרַת תִּקְרָה, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לֵית לֵיהּ גְּזֵרַת תִּקְרָה — וְהָא אִפְּלִיגוּ בַּהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא! דִּתְנַן: מְסַכְּכִין בִּנְסָרִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *tanna* of the mishna **teaching us?** Is it **that Rabbi Meir is of** the opinion that the Sages issued **the decree of the roof and Rabbi Yehuda is of** the opinion that the Sages did not issue **the decree of the roof? But didn't they disagree about this once, as we learned** in the mishna above: **One** may **roof** the *sukka* **with boards;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir prohibits** their use. The Gemara explained that the dispute is whether or not the Sages issued the decree of the roof.

The *Gemara* then raises a pointed question: if the underlying issue of this *mishnah* is simply whether *gezeirat tikrah* applies to planks, that is precisely the debate already recorded in yesterday's *mishnah* — where *Rabbi Yehuda* permits the use of planks as *schach* and *Rabbi Meir* prohibits it. What novel teaching does the present *mishnah* add? Why repeat the same dispute?

### Chiya: Different Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רֵישָׁא — בִּנְסָרִים מְשׁוּפִּין עָסְקִינַן, וּמִשּׁוּם גְּזֵרַת כֵּלִים נָגְעוּ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** That is not the dispute, as **in the first clause,** i.e., in the earlier mishna, **we are dealing with** the case of **planed boards.** The rationale for their disagreement is not due to the decree of the roof; **but it is due to the decree of the vessels** that they **touched upon it.** The dispute is whether or not the Sages issued a decree prohibiting the use of planed beams in roofing the *sukka*, although as flat wooden vessels they are not susceptible to ritual impurity, lest one come to roof the *sukka* with vessels that are susceptible to ritual impurity.

*Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* offers a radical re-reading: the earlier *mishnah* did not concern wide planks and *gezeirat tikrah* at all. Rather, the earlier debate involved thin, finely smoothed planks — planks that are not wide enough to suggest a permanent rooftop but are so well-crafted and polished that they begin to resemble finished wooden objects. The concern in the earlier *mishnah*, on this reading, was not that the planks look like a roof but that they approach the appearance of a *kli* — a vessel. If thin boards are sanded and refined to the point where they look like manufactured items, perhaps there is a concern that they carry a vessel-like quality that disqualifies them from serving as *schach*, since *schach* must be material that does not *mekabel tumah*. The present *mishnah*, by contrast, deals with genuinely wide planks and the concern that they look like a permanent rooftop — *gezeirat tikrah* in the classic sense. Under this reading, the two *mishnayot* address entirely different problems, and the repetition is fully justified.

### Gemara Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר: סִכְּכָהּ בְּחִיצִּין זְכָרִים — כְּשֵׁרָה. בִּנְקֵבוֹת — פְּסוּלָה, וְלָא גָּזַר זְכָרִים אַטּוּ נְקֵבוֹת. הָכָא נָמֵי לָא נִגְזַר נְסָרִים מְשׁוּפִּין אַטּוּ כֵּלִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to Rav Yehuda, who said** that **Rav said:** If **one roofed** a *sukka* **with convex arrow** shafts, the *sukka* is **fit,** but if he roofed his *sukka* **with concave** arrow shafts, the *sukka* is **unfit; and he did not issue a decree** and prohibit roofing with **convex** shafts **due to** the prohibition against roofing with **concave** shafts, **here too, let us not issue a decree** and prohibit roofing with **planed boards, due to** the prohibition against roofing with actual **vessels.**

The *Gemara* rejects this alternative reading. It invokes a ruling cited earlier: *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* stated that if one used male wooden pegs (*chitzin zachar*) as *schach*, the *sukkah* is valid, but if one used female wooden pegs (*chitzin nekeiva*) — which have a receptacle — the *sukkah* is invalid. What is significant here is that the male pegs are entirely usable, smooth, and functional as objects; they simply lack a *beit kibul*, a cavity that would constitute them as a vessel in the halachic sense. And yet their use as *schach* is permitted without any concern that they too closely resemble a vessel or that allowing them will lead people to use the female pegs. The *Gemara* draws the explicit conclusion: *lo gazar zachar atu nekeiva* — we do not extend the prohibition of vessel-like objects to non-vessels merely because they look similar to vessels. If so, there is no basis for the proposed reading that the earlier *mishnah* was concerned with smoothed planks resembling vessels. That simply is not how the *halacha* works. A plank that is not a *kli* is not a *kli*, regardless of how polished it appears.

### Both Are Tikrah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא עַל כׇּרְחָךְ רֵישָׁא פְּלִיגִי בִּגְזֵרַת תִּקְרָה, וְסֵיפָא פְּלִיגִי בִּגְזֵרַת תִּקְרָה. וְאִפְּלוֹגֵי בְּתַרְתֵּי זִימְנֵי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** according to Rav, **perforce you** must **say** that in **the first clause** of the mishna **they disagree with regard to the decree of the roof, and in the latter clause,** i.e., this mishna as well, **they disagree with regard to the decree of the roof.** Once again, the question arises: **Why do I** need them to **disagree** about the same issue **twice?**

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *al korchacha*, inescapably, both the earlier *mishnah* and the present one concern the same category of wide planks and the same question of *gezeirat tikrah*. Both debates are about whether planks wide enough to evoke a permanent rooftop are rabbinically disqualified as *schach*. If so, the repetition requires explanation on different grounds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה (הִיא), דְּקָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: אַמַּאי קָא אָסְרַתְּ בִּנְסָרִים — מִשּׁוּם גְּזֵרַת תִּקְרָה? הַאי סְבָרָא לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי הוּא דְּאִית לְהוּ, וּבֵית הִלֵּל לָא גָּזְרִי! וְאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל בְּדָבָר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rather, **the latter clause** is the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who is saying to Rabbi Meir: Why do you prohibit** roofing **with boards?** Is it **due to the decree of the roof? That** is the **reason according to Beit Shammai, who are of** the opinion that the Sages issued the decree. **But,** contrary to your opinion, **Beit Hillel do not issue the decree. And Rabbi Meir said** to Rabbi Yehuda: **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel did not disagree with regard to this matter** at all. These are not two separate disputes; rather, it is one extended dispute.

The *Gemara* resolves this by explaining that the current *mishnah* is not simply a restatement but a substantive rhetorical move by *Rabbi Yehuda* addressed directly to *Rabbi Meir*. *Rabbi Yehuda* is saying: you, *Rabbi Meir*, object to planks because of *gezeirat tikrah* — because they look like a permanent roof. But know that this concern belongs to *Beit Shammai* alone. There exists a *machloket* between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* in which *Beit Hillel* does not hold of *gezeirat tikrah* at all: in *Beit Hillel*'s view, the planks are perfectly valid, and all that is required is a new act of dedication. So by insisting on the invalidity of planks, *Rabbi Meir* is adopting a *Beit Shammai* position against *Beit Hillel*. *Rabbi Meir* responds that there was no such dispute — *lo necheleku Beit Shammai u-Veit Hillel* — and that his ruling reflects universal consensus.

The *chiddush* of the present *mishnah* thus lies not in introducing a new category of law but in the context it provides: the application of the plank dispute to a case where boards were actually functioning as a house rooftop throughout the year. This raises the additional dimension of *taasah v'lo min ha'asuy* — material designated and used for a permanent dwelling must be actively rededicated for *schach*. *Beit Hillel*'s allowance of *mefakpek* captures this: since the planks are valid in themselves, lifting up and setting back down constitutes the necessary act of rededication. According to *Rabbi Meir*, who holds the planks rabbinically invalid regardless, no such act of rededication can rehabilitate them; only alternating with valid *schach* helps.

One might ask why alternating should suffice at all, given that one is left with fifty percent invalid and fifty percent valid *schach* that are not physically intermingled but arranged side by side. This question — about how *rov* operates in non-intermingled arrangements — will be taken up more fully in the subsequent *mishnah*, but even at this stage it is in many ways the more structurally interesting question that the *Gemara* does not fully draw out in this context. The simple reading of this *mishnah* appears to be that four *tefachim* of invalid *schach* does not invalidate the *sukkah* as a whole; rather, the *sukkah* remains valid and one is simply obligated to position oneself beneath the valid *schach*. Alternating thus ensures that a fully valid *sukkah* exists with accessible valid *schach* sections, even if other sections remain problematic. This reading — though we do not rule in accordance with it ultimately — is the *pshat* of the *mishnah* as it stands.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Rav: Categorical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַב, דְּאָמַר מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן אַרְבָּעָה, דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אִית לֵיהּ גְּזֵרַת תִּקְרָה וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לֵית לֵיהּ גְּזֵרַת תִּקְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: **This** works out **well according to Rav, who said** that **the dispute is** specifically **in** a case where the boards **have four** handbreadths **in their** width. He says **that Rabbi Meir is of** the opinion that the Sages issued **the decree of the roof, and Rabbi Yehuda is not of** the opinion that the Sages issued **the decree of the roof.**

With the structure of the *mishnah* established, the *Gemara* turns to the interpretive dispute between *Rav* and *Shmuel* regarding the earlier *mishnah* about planks, and shows how each reading plays out in relation to the present *mishnah*. *Rav* reads the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Meir* as categorical: *Rabbi Yehuda* holds no position of *gezeirat tikrah* whatsoever, while *Rabbi Meir* accepts it fully. On *Rav*'s reading, the current *mishnah* works elegantly: a rooftop functioning during the year would by assumption be constructed of planks that are four *tefachim* wide, and regarding such planks *Rabbi Yehuda* categorically rejects the problem while *Rabbi Meir* affirms it. The present *mishnah* is thus a clean application of the earlier categorical debate to a practically important case: the plank-roofed structure that was used as an ordinary dwelling.

### Shmuel: Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר בְּשֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן אַרְבָּעָה מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל יֵשׁ בָּהֶן אַרְבָּעָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פְּסוּלָה, סֵיפָא בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to Shmuel, who said** that **the dispute is** specifically **in** a case where the boards **do not have four** handbreadths **in their** width, **but where they have four** handbreadths **in their** width, **everyone agrees that** the *sukka* **is unfit,** and both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree that the Sages issued the decree of the roof; if so, **in the latter clause** of the mishna, **with regard to what** matter **do they disagree?**

*Shmuel* reads the earlier dispute differently: *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Meir* debate only in cases where the planks are less than four *tefachim* wide. Once planks reach four *tefachim*, all authorities — *divrei hakol* — agree they are invalid. On *Shmuel*'s reading, a functional rooftop would obviously be constructed of four-*tefach* planks, meaning that on the present *mishnah*'s facts, both *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Meir* should agree the planks are rabbinically invalid.

### Shmuel's Problem

This creates a significant difficulty for *Shmuel*: if four-*tefach* planks are universally invalid, how does *Rabbi Yehuda* permit the remedy of *mefakpek* — lifting up and setting back down? If the planks are categorically invalid *schach*, *mefakpek* should accomplish nothing, just as *Rabbi Meir* insists.

### Bitul Tikrah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבַיטּוֹלֵי תִּקְרָה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר בָּטְלָה בְּהָכִי, וּמַר סָבַר בְּהָכִי לָא בָּטְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: One may not use boards of this sort for roofing his *sukka*. Even according to Rabbi Yehuda, a *sukka* roofed in that manner is unfit, due to the decree of the roof. However, here, in the latter clause, it is **with regard to negating** an existing **roof** that consists of boards of this sort, in order to render the *sukka* fit that **they disagree.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds:** The ceiling **is thereby negated,** by moving the boards, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that the ceiling **is not thereby negated** unless he also removes one beam from among them.

The *Gemara*'s answer on behalf of *Shmuel* is *bitul tikrah* — the negation of the roof's status. The thrust of the explanation is as follows: the reason wide planks are rabbinically invalid as *schach* is the concern that they resemble a permanent rooftop. When the very same planks that have been functioning as one's actual roof throughout the year are now lifted up and placed back down intentionally for the purpose of *schach*, the act constitutes a dramatic public declaration that these are no longer understood as a permanent roof but are now being consciously repurposed as *schach*. This act of *mefakpek* thus has the effect of negating the *tikrah* status — *batla* — and therefore the rabbinic invalidity that stemmed from the roof-like appearance is dissolved.

This is a striking explanation, and the *Gemara* itself seems to sense its difficulty. Throughout the earlier analysis, the principle had been stated that the rabbinic invalidity of planks is unconditional — so unconditional that even turning a plank ninety degrees does not help, because once the rabbis declared it invalid they treated it as categorically disqualified, like a metal pipe. It would seem inconsistent to then say that *mefakpek* — which is nothing more than lifting and setting down — can dissolve that same rabbinic disqualification. Moreover, the general principle of *lo plug* resists this kind of individualized remedy: when the rabbis issued a broad prohibition, we do not allow individuals to perform workarounds that demonstrate their personal awareness of the concern. The *gemara* is acknowledging, in effect, that *Shmuel*'s reading requires an explanation that is difficult to sustain on first principles, and that *Rav*'s reading — under which *Rabbi Yehuda* simply rejects *gezeirat tikrah* entirely — provides a far cleaner account of the entire *sugyah*.

Indeed, the *sugyot* consistently work more smoothly according to *Rav*, and the categorical nature of the debate is the reading that fits the *mishnaiot* most naturally. This will be true elsewhere as well. Nonetheless, as is often the case in areas of *issur*, where we would ordinarily rule like *Rav*, we ultimately rule like *Shmuel* in most of these *sugyot* — even though the explanatory burden falls more heavily on *Shmuel*'s reading.

## Alternating Schach

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקָרֶה סוּכָּתוֹ בְּשַׁפּוּדִין אוֹ בַּאֲרוּכּוֹת הַמִּטָּה, אִם יֵשׁ רֶיוַח בֵּינֵיהֶן כְּמוֹתָן — כְּשֵׁרָה. הַחוֹטֵט בְּגָדִישׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ סוּכָּה — אֵינָהּ סוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who roofs his *sukka* with** metal **skewers or with the long boards of the bed,** which compose its frame, **if there is space between** each one of **them equal to** the width of the skewers or the boards, and if he places fit roofing in those spaces, the *sukka* is **fit.** In the case of **one who hollows out** and creates a space inside **a stack of grain to establish a sukka for him,** it is **not a *sukka*.**

The next *mishnah* returns to the theme of alternating valid and invalid *schach*, this time in a more sharply defined form. *Ha-mekareh sukato bi-shfudin o be-aruchot ha-mittah* — if one roofs one's *sukkah* with metal bars or with bed legs. Metal bars are clearly invalid *schach*, as metal objects are *mekabel tumah* regardless of whether they possess a *beit kibul*; for metal, the absence of a receptacle is irrelevant. Bed legs are wood, and wooden objects without a *beit kibul* are not ordinarily *mekabel tumah*; however, a bed is *tamei*, and when the legs are attached to the bed they participate in the bed's *tum'ah* status. Why the legs remain problematic when detached is a question the *Gemara* will take up — but the *mishnah* proceeds by assuming they are invalid.

*Im yesh revach beneihem ke-motem, kesherah* — if there is a gap between the invalid elements that is equivalent in width to the invalid elements themselves, and if one fills that gap with valid *schach*, the *sukkah* is valid.

### 50% Threshold

This immediately raises the question of why fifty percent suffices. The principle of *rov* — majority — ordinarily requires more than half, something closer to fifty-one percent. And even when *rov* is invoked, it applies where the elements are intermingled to form a single undifferentiated mass; here, the valid and invalid *schach* are arranged in alternating strips, each clearly distinct from the other. Why should such an arrangement render the whole *sukkah* valid?

One possible reading, suggested by the earlier discussion, is that the *mishnah* does not in fact assert that the entire *sukkah* is uniformly valid. Rather, the *sukkah* as a unit is not invalidated, but one must position oneself beneath the valid sections. This is the *pshat* reading — aligned with the position that four *tefachim* of invalid *schach* does not invalidate a *sukkah* entirely. However, this is not the *halacha* as we rule.

### Wall Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. דְּאִתְּמַר: פָּרוּץ כְּעוֹמֵד, רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: מוּתָּר. וְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר: אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Let us say,** based on the mishna, **that this will be a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, as it is stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed concerning the following matter: With regard to the domains of Shabbat, if **the breached** segment **is equal to the standing** segment, is it deemed a partition or not? **Rav Pappa said: It is permitted** to carry within the partition; as long as the breached segment is not greater, it is considered a solid partition. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: It is prohibited** to carry within the partition, unless the standing portion is greater. Apparently, from the mishna, even if the fit roofing is equal to the unfit skewers and boards, the *sukka* is fit, contrary to the opinion of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua.

The *Gemara* observes that the question of whether fifty percent is sufficient parallels a debate regarding walls. For *Shabbat* purposes, a valid enclosure must have walls sufficient to constitute a *reshut ha-yachid*. If the wall consists of alternating solid sections and open gaps — *parutz ke-omeid*, where the gap equals the standing portion — is it a valid partition or not?

*Rav Papa* holds it is permitted, since the majority is not broken through. *Rav Huna bei de-Rav Yehoshua* holds it is forbidden. The *Gemara* notes that this debate turns on how one frames the question. If one defines the requirement of a wall as "the majority must be sealed," then fifty percent sealed is not a majority and fails. If one instead frames the requirement as "the wall is invalid only if the majority is breached," then fifty percent breached is not a majority breach, and the wall is valid. The *Gemara* suggests that the *sukkah* case may be analogous, and applies the same structural question to the roofing scenario.

### Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מַאי כְּמוֹתָן — בְּנִכְנָס וְיוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** could have **said to you: What** is the meaning of: **Like** the skewers and the boards? It does not mean that the space between the skewers and boards equals the width of the skewers and boards themselves. It is referring to a case **where** the space is large enough so that the fit roofing can **enter and emerge** easily, i.e., it is wider than the unfit roofing. According to this interpretation, the mishna can be explained according to Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, as well. The mishna agrees that even if the standing and breached areas are equal, the *sukka* is unfit.

To explain how the *mishnah* could rule the *sukkah* valid even on the stricter view that majority is required, the *Gemara* offers two solutions.

The first, attributed to *Rav Ami*, is *bema'atif* — that the valid *schach* is added in slightly greater quantity than the invalid material, so the strict threshold of majority is met even on *Rav Huna bei de-Rav Yehoshua*'s position. When the *mishnah* says the gap equals the invalid material, it means the gap is large enough to allow the insertion of material of that size with room to spare — so the valid *schach* that is inserted is actually slightly more than the invalid material beside it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֶפְשָׁר לְצַמְצֵם! אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: בְּמַעֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it possible to be precise?** The mishna need not be understood in that manner, since it is possible to calibrate the width of the spaces to equal the width of the unfit roofing, as the mishna required no more than that. **Rabbi Ami said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where one extends** the width of the spaces beyond the width of the unfit roofing. The mishna deems the *sukka* fit only if the width of the spaces is greater than the width of the unfit roofing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּשֶׁאֵין מַעֲדִיף, אִם הָיוּ נְתוּנִים שְׁתִי — נוֹתְנָן עֵרֶב, עֵרֶב — נוֹתְנָן שְׁתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is referring to a case **where one does not extend** the width of the spaces, and nevertheless, the fit roofing is greater than the unfit roofing, **if** the skewers **were placed lengthwise** across the *sukka*, **one places** the fit roofing **widthwise,** and if the skewers were placed **widthwise, one places the** fit roofing **lengthwise.** By doing so, the fit roofing overlaps the skewers at least somewhat; otherwise it would fall between the unfit roofing. Consequently, even if the space equals the unfit roofing, the fit roofing is greater than the unfit roofing.

The second solution, attributed to *Rava*, is more structurally elegant and more broadly significant. Even if the gap and the invalid sections are exactly equal in dimension, the arrangement the *mishnah* envisions involves *sheti* and *erev* — the valid *schach* runs perpendicular to the invalid material. In other words, the invalid material are like beams running in one direction, and the valid *schach* is laid across them in the other direction. Given this crossing configuration, the valid *schach* must extend beyond the beams on either side in order to be supported, so it naturally covers somewhat more than its nominal width. There is therefore inherently more valid *schach* than the measurement would suggest, entirely apart from intentional additions.

*Tosafot* reads the *Gemara* as also addressing a second underlying question: even granted sufficient quantity, how can the valid *schach* and the invalid material be treated as a single unified covering when they are not genuinely intermingled? *Tosafot* understands that for the purposes of *rov* in *schach*, genuine physical intermingling is not required. It is sufficient that the valid *schach* overlaps or rests upon the invalid material at the point of support. That minimal physical contact — where the valid *schach* lies across the invalid beam — constitutes sufficient intermingling to treat the covering as a single unit and apply the principle of *rov* to it as a whole. This is a significant *chiddush*: *Tosafot*'s threshold for what constitutes intermingling is notably low, requiring only that the materials make contact at a crossing point rather than being truly mixed together. Crucially, once this intermingling standard is met and *rov* applies, the entire covering is judged as valid *schach* — meaning one may sit anywhere beneath it and need not restrict oneself to the valid sections alone.

## Bed Legs Issue

### Leg Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ בַּאֲרוּכּוֹת הַמִּטָּה. לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי בַּר טַבְיוֹמֵי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי בַּר טַבְיוֹמֵי: סִכְּכָהּ בִּבְלָאֵי כֵלִים — פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **Or with the long boards of the bed,** which compose its frame, the *sukka* is unfit. The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the mishna **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami bar Tavyomei, as Rabbi Ami bar Tavyomei said:** If **one roofed** the *sukka* **with worn,** incomplete, **vessels,** the *sukka* is **unfit.** Although these incomplete vessels are no longer susceptible to ritual impurity, they remain unfit because they were initially unfit for roofing. Proof can be adduced from the mishna: The long boards of the bed are no longer vessels but rather pieces from broken vessels; still, they may not be used for roofing the *sukka*.

The *Gemara* now takes up the question it had bracketed earlier: why are bed legs invalid *schach*? *Rabbi Ami bar Tuvyumi* states that if one used *blaei kelim* — worn-out garments or worn-out vessels — as *schach*, it is invalid. This ruling addresses a practically important question: if an object was once a *kli* and hence *mekabel tumah*, but has since worn out or broken and no longer technically qualifies as a vessel, does its former status as a *kli* leave a residual disqualification? The answer *Rabbi Ami bar Tuvyumi* offers is yes — *sikacha bi-vla'ei kelim pesula*. Even if the object no longer presently constitutes a vessel, its prior history as one renders it invalid as *schach*. Applied to bed legs: when attached to a bed, they partook of the bed's *tum'ah*; once detached, they may no longer be independently *mekabel tumah*, but the prior vessel status leaves them disqualified as *schach*.

### Segmented Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי חָנָן אָמַר רַבִּי: בַּאֲרוּכָּה וּשְׁתֵּי כְרָעַיִם, בִּקְצָרָה וּשְׁתֵּי כְרָעַיִם. הָכָא נָמֵי: בַּאֲרוּכָּה וּשְׁתֵּי כְרָעַיִם, בִּקְצָרָה וּשְׁתֵּי כְרָעַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: The mishna is referring to a case **similar to** that **which Rabbi Ḥanan said** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said** in another context: It is not referring to the long boards alone. Rather, it is referring to a case **with the long board** of the bed **and two** of the **legs** attached to it **or** to a case **with the short board** of the bed **with two legs** attached to it. In this case, the structure could be propped up against a wall and used as a bed. **Here too,** the mishna is referring to roofing **with the long board and two legs or with the short board and two legs,** which are still considered complete vessels.

The *Gemara*, however, does not accept this as the explanation for the *mishnah*'s case. It invokes a ruling by *Rav Chanan* in the name of *Rebbi* from *Mishnah Kelim* 18, *mishnah* 4. That *mishnah* states that a bed becomes *tamei* and becomes *tahor* as a complete unit — the position of *Rabbi Eliezer*. The *Chachamim* disagree, holding that individual segments of a bed can become *tamei* and can be immersed in a *mikveh* as segments. The *Gemara* uses this *mishnah* as a springboard: *Rav Chanan* in the name of *Rebbi* specifies which segments of a bed retain *kli* status even in their segmented form — namely, the long half of the bed with two legs, or the short half of the bed with two legs.

### Half-Bed Status

The ruling of *Rav Chanan* in the name of *Rebbi* thus identifies the specific configuration of bed segments that retain independent *kli* status: *arucha u-shnei krayim* — the long section with two legs — or *ktzara u-shnei krayim* — the short section with two legs. In the current *mishnah*, the *Gemara* suggests, we are dealing not with bare detached legs but with precisely such a segment — a portion of the bed including two legs that retains its *kli* identity because it remains functional in its current form.

### Function Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי חַזְיָא? לְמִסְמְכִינְהוּ אַגּוּדָּא וּלְמֵיתַב עֲלַיְיהוּ וּמִשְׁדֵּא אַשְׁלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And for what** purpose **are** these parts **suited;** what function qualifies their status as vessels? The Gemara answers: It is possible for one **to lean them against the wall and to sit on them,** after placing boards across the top **and placing ropes** across their length and width. The boards of the bed can thereby be used for the purpose of sitting or lying upon them; consequently, they are considered vessels.

What function does such a segment serve? *Rav Chanan* explains: *le-mismichinhu aguda* — one can lean it against a wall; *u-le-meitav alaiyu* — and sit upon it; *u-mashdi ashlei* — and secure it with ropes as needed. In other words, a half-bed standing against a wall serves as a functional seat or resting surface. Because it can still be used in this way, it retains the status of a *kli* and remains *mekabel tumah* — not merely by virtue of its history but because it is presently functional. *Tosafot* notes that this functional use alone might not be entirely sufficient for *kli* status, and that the fact that one intends to reassemble the bed contributes as well: the combination of present usability and anticipated reassembly together sustain the *kli* identity for the *Chachamim*.

The practical significance for our *mishnah* is thus: bed legs are invalid *schach* not because they once participated in a *kli* and carry residual historical disqualification — the position of *Rabbi Ami bar Tuvyumi* — but because they presently constitute part of a *kli* in their segmented form. The question of whether historical *kli* status alone suffices to disqualify material from serving as *schach* remains unresolved and will continue to be addressed as the *sugyah* develops.
