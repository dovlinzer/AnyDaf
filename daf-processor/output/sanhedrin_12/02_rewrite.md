# Sanhedrin 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chaverim & Ibur

### Chaverim & Festivals

Today's *daf* in the Bavli is 12. We pick up in the middle of 11b and continue with the issue of *ibur hashana*. A point worth noting — raised in connection with the Yerushalmi — is the question of whether a *chaver* is permitted to be present in the *beit din* when they perform *kiddush hachodesh* or *ibur hashana*.

### Chaverim Phrase

This connects naturally to something familiar from the synagogue liturgy. When we make the announcement of *rosh chodesh* in *shul*, we say *chaverim kol Yisrael v'nomar amen* — "all Israel are *chaverim*, and let us say amen." The instinct is to read *chaverim* colloquially, as simply meaning "friends" or "companions." But there may be a more precise technical meaning here: the term *chaver* as a distinct status in matters of *tumah v'tahara*, referring to individuals trusted in ritual matters. The Yerushalmi's conclusion was that a *chaver* could be *min haminyan* — counted among those participating in the proceedings of calendar-making — though this was debated.

The Gemara elsewhere invokes a related idea: during the *regel*, many concerns about *tumah v'tahara* were waived, based on a *pasuk* indicating that when all Israel gathers for the festival, everyone is treated as a *chaver* in matters of ritual purity. This is a *mishnah* in Chagigah. So when we say *chaverim kol Yisrael*, it may be evoking this same concept — that we all come together as one people. There is also a broader question here about *kiddush hachodesh* in our own time: we have no *beit din*, no witnesses appearing before a court, and yet Hillel the Second established the fixed calendar, and somehow it is by communal recognition — by acclamation — that the *kiddush hachodesh* takes effect.

## Three Criteria

### Reasons & Requirements

The *Tnu Rabbanan* at the bottom of 11b, approximately ten lines above the wider section, states: *al shlosha devarim me'avrin et hashana* — for three reasons one intercalates the year: *al ha'aviv*, *v'al peirot ha'ilan*, *v'al hatkufa*. The *aviv* refers to whether the grain has sufficiently ripened; the *tkufa* refers to whether the seasons are properly aligned — whether spring is arriving at the right time, which logically is often connected to *aviv*; and the third factor is whether the fruit will be ready in time for *shavuot* so that people can bring their *bikkurim*.

The *Tnu Rabbanan* then states: *al shnayim me'avrin* — two of these factors are required to intercalate the year; *v'al echad mehem ein me'avrin* — one factor alone is insufficient. Unlike what was said in the Yerushalmi — that *bedieved* one factor might be enough — this *braita* makes no such qualification.

### Aviv & Happiness

*Ubizman she'aviv echad mehem hakol smechim* — when *aviv* is one of the two factors driving the intercalation, everyone is very happy. Rashi explains: *she'ein choshim bit'chilat Pesach* — they are not anxious about Pesach being pushed off, *lefi she'ein tevuah reuyah* — because the grain is not yet ready. If the grain has not ripened, there is no hardship in waiting another month. The new grain cannot be eaten anyway before the *omer* is brought, and since it is not yet ripe, there is no grain sitting ready that people are forbidden from eating.

But if *aviv* is not one of the factors — meaning the grain *has* ripened — and the year is still being intercalated for the other two reasons, then everyone is unhappy: *atzeivim*, grieved, because the *omer* is being delayed a month despite the grain being ready, and the prohibition of *chadash* weighs upon them.

### Tkufa Debate

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: *al hatkufa* — regarding the *tkufa*. The Gemara raises an ambiguity: *al hatkufa smechim* — does he mean that the *tkufa* factor makes everyone happy? Or does he mean *o al hatkufa me'avrin* — that the *tkufa* alone is a sufficient reason to intercalate? The Gemara concludes *teyku* — the question is unresolved.

Rashi's explanation of why the *tkufa* would generate happiness is as follows: if the *tkufa* is properly aligned and the seasons are in the right time — even though it is not one of the factors driving intercalation — then it means the weather will be ideal for the *chagim*. Everyone who goes to Israel for *Sukkot*, for example, loves the perfect autumn weather. If the *tkufa* is synced, the *olei regel* travel to Israel at a beautiful time. But if the *tkufa* is not a factor, it means the seasons are misaligned — it will be too cold or too hot — and the *aliya l'regel* becomes uncomfortable.

## Geographic Regions

### Lands & Requirements

The next *Tnu Rabbanan* addresses the geographic dimension of *ibur hashana*: *b'shalosh aratzot ma'avrin et hashana* — on the basis of three different regions one assesses whether the conditions for intercalation exist: Yehuda, Ever HaYarden, and the Galil. *Al shtayim hen ma'avrin* — if the conditions exist in two of the regions, one intercalates; *v'al achas meihen ein ma'avrin* — but if only one region shows the signs, one does not.

### Yehuda Priority

*Ubizman she'Yehuda achas meihen hakol smechim* — when Yehuda is one of the two regions confirming the readiness, everyone is very happy, because *she'ein Omer ba ela miYehuda* — the *omer* comes only from Yehuda. Rashi clarifies that this does not mean literally that the *omer* cannot come from elsewhere, but rather that the proper practice is to bring it from Yehuda. So when the conditions in Yehuda allow us to bring the *omer* from its ideal source, there is general rejoicing.

### Authority in Yehuda

The next *Tnu Rabbanan* addresses where the act itself must take place: *ein ma'avrin et hashana ela b'Yehuda* — one may only intercalate the year in Yehuda. *V'im ivruha baGalil me'uberet* — but if they did it in the Galil, it is nonetheless valid.

### Chananya's Testimony

Against this, *He'id Chananya Ish Ono*: if they intercalated in the Galil, *einah me'uberet* — it does not take effect. This is framed as testimony, not merely as his opinion — presumably he had a tradition, or witnessed a case in which an intercalation performed in the Galil was voided.

Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, then asks: *mai ta'ama d'Chananya Ish Ono* — what is the reasoning behind Chananya's position? Interestingly, he frames the question not as "why should Yehuda be required in the first place?" but rather "why does he say it doesn't even work *bedieved*?"

## Authority: EY vs Bavel

### Eretz Yisrael Req.

The question is: what is the reason that even *bedieved*, intercalating outside of Yehuda fails? Rabbi Yehuda ben Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi's answer draws on the verse: *l'shichno tidreshu uvasa shamah* — "to His dwelling place you shall seek, and there you shall come."

### Lechono Tidreshu

*Kol drisha she'atah doresh lo yehei ela b'shichno shel makom* — any act of religious seeking must be performed in the proximity of God's dwelling, meaning in Yehuda, in the orbit of Jerusalem and the *Beit HaMikdash*. The question arises: in what sense is *ibur hashana* an act of *derisha* — of seeking? The connection becomes clearer through a second verse: *eretz asher Hashem Elohecha doresh otah* — "a land which the Lord your God seeks out," *mereishit hashana v'ad achrit shana* — "from the beginning of the year to its end." The idea is that assessing the year — determining its seasons, its agricultural conditions, its calendar — is itself an act of *derisha* of the land, a seeking out of its condition from beginning to end. This is an elegant reading.

### Tosafot Exceptions

Tosafot raises an immediate challenge: *v'hadar Rabbi Akiva iver shana b'Nehardea* — Rabbi Akiva performed an intercalation in Nehardea, which is not only outside Yehuda but outside Eretz Yisrael entirely. Tosafot, citing the beginning of the chapter *HaIsha Shalom* and the end of Berachot, answers: *shelo hiniach kamoso b'Eretz Yisrael* — Rabbi Akiva left no equal to himself in the Land of Israel. The principle is that if the greatest *talmid chacham* is situated outside Eretz Yisrael, the requirement to perform the intercalation specifically in Eretz Yisrael does not apply — because religious authority follows the greatest Torah scholar.

Tosafot further notes, citing the Yerushalmi explicitly: *d'cheshe'ein yecholim l'avrah b'Eretz Yisrael ma'avrin osah b'chutz la'aretz* — when it is impossible to intercalate in Eretz Yisrael, one may do so outside the Land. The case differs from Rabbi Akiva's: there it was a question of where the greater authority resided; here it is a question of political impossibility. The Yerushalmi cites Baruch ben Neriah and Yechezkel as examples of intercalations performed outside the Land, and the passage leads into the famous story of Chananya.

### Geonic Authority Shift

This entire discussion bears directly on one of the most significant questions in the history of Jewish law: why do we follow the Bavli rather than the Yerushalmi? Some wish to say it is because the Bavli was redacted a century later and therefore assessed all that the Yerushalmi had said — though whether the Bavli's editors actually knew the Yerushalmi as a text is itself quite questionable. The real reason is historical and sociological: the center of Torah had been shifting progressively to Bavel, and as the *Geonei Bavel* became the preeminent Torah authorities, communities throughout the Jewish world turned to them with their *halachic* questions. The *Geonei Bavel* used the Bavli; hence the Bavli became the determinative *Talmud* for all of Jewry.

But the picture was not so clean. Communities in Eretz Yisrael continued to turn to the *Geonei Eretz Yisrael*. There was not one undisputed center of authority. The decisive moment — what one might call the *maka b'patish*, the final hammer blow that settled the question — was a dispute in the Geonic period (eighth or ninth century) over the calendar itself, likely involving *rosh chodesh Tishrei*, when Bavel and Eretz Yisrael were determining different dates. One could live with disputes about the permissibility of various actions on *Shabbos*, but one cannot have Bavel observing one calendar and Eretz Yisrael another — that would fracture the people entirely. When the *Geonei Bavel* won that dispute by invoking the principle of *lo hiniach kamoso* — that greater Torah authority may relocate the seat of religious power — they definitively established Bavel as the locus of *halachic* authority, and with it, the Bavli as the authoritative *Talmud*.

Even so, the picture remained nuanced. Many of the Rambam's *psakim* that seem puzzling are explicable as reflecting the Yerushalmi. And Ashkenazic *minhagim* often diverge from the Bavli, a point Tosafot itself makes: Ashkenazic Jewry derived from Eretz Yisrael and carried with them the *minhagei Eretz Yisrael*, so that *minhagei Ashkenaz* sometimes track the Yerushalmi rather than the Bavli. Tosafot's own methodology is to work through the texts of the Bavli and render them consistent — not to systematically reconcile the Bavli with the Yerushalmi. When Tosafot encounters an Ashkenazic practice out of sync with the Bavli, it sometimes offers a reading of the Bavli that accommodates it, and sometimes simply acknowledges that the practice follows *minhag Eretz Yisrael* and diverges from the Bavli.

The entire question of *kiddush hachodesh* and *ibur hashana* — where it must be performed, who has authority to perform it — is in essence a question about where the seat of religious authority lies. That is what makes these *sugyot* so historically consequential.

## Time Restrictions

### Daytime Req.

The next *Tnu Rabbanan* introduces a temporal requirement: *ein ma'avrin et hashana ela bayom* — intercalation may only be performed during the daytime. *V'im ivruha balaila, eina me'uberet* — if they did it at night, it has no effect. Similarly, *v'ein mikadshin et hachodesh ela bayom* — *kiddush hachodesh* must also occur during the day. *V'im kidshuhu balaila, eino mekudash* — if they sanctified it at night, it is not sanctified. This is striking, given that the new moon is actually visible at night — and yet the active *kiddush* must occur in daylight.

### Daytime Proof

Rabbi Abba asks: *mai kra* — what is the scriptural basis? *Tiku bachodesh shofar bakeseh l'yom chageinu* — "Blow the shofar in the new month, at the covering, on our festival day." The Gemara asks: *eizehu chag shehachodesh mitkasseh bo* — which is the only festival on which the moon is concealed? The answer is *Rosh Hashanah*, since it falls on the first of the month when the moon is not yet visible. And the verse identifies this as *mishpat l'Elohei Yaakov* — a "judgment" belonging to the God of Yaakov. *Mah mishpat bayom* — just as judicial proceedings of *beit din* occur during the day, *af kiddush hachodesh bayom* — so too *kiddush hachodesh* must occur during the day.

## Famine Restrictions

### Famine Ban

We now arrive at 12a. *Ein me'avrin et hashana bishnei ra'avon* — one does not intercalate the year during a period of famine. The reason is that however compelling the other factors may be, one does not wish to delay the bringing of the *omer*, because people who are suffering from famine need access to grain as early as possible. Delaying *Pesach* by a month means delaying the *omer* and, with it, the permission to eat the new grain.

### Elisha Story

A *braita* is cited in which Rabbi offers a proof from a narrative about Elisha. A man came from Baal Shalisha and brought to the man of God — Elisha — *lechem bikkurim esrim lechem se'orim*, ripened bread of twenty barley loaves. The *braita* notes: *v'ein l'cha kala bechol Eretz Yisrael l'vashel peiroseha yoter miBa'al Shalisha* — there is no region in all of Eretz Yisrael where produce ripens more quickly than Ba'al Shalisha. And yet, *v'af al pi chein lo bikra ela min echad* — only one type, the barley, had ripened. The wheat had not yet ripened.

One might suggest that this was because the episode occurred before the *omer* — early in the spring season, when only barley ripens. But the *braita* counters: *talmud lomar ten l'am veyocheilu* — Elisha said "give to the people and let them eat," which indicates that this was *after* the *omer*, when eating was already permitted. So even after the *omer*, in the fastest-ripening region of Eretz Yisrael, only the barley had ripened and the wheat had not yet begun. *Haya omer me'atah re'uya haysa zo shana shetistabeir* — one would have said that this year truly should have been intercalated, that *Pesach* should have been pushed off so that more grain — including wheat — would ripen before the festival.

*U'mipnei mah lo ibra* — so why did Elisha not intercalate? *Sheshnas batzores haysa* — because it was a famine year. *V'hayu hakol ratzin l'veit hagranos* — and everyone was running to the granaries, desperately needing grain. One could not delay the bringing of the *omer* and thereby delay people's access to the new grain, even though from a purely agricultural standpoint the conditions might have warranted intercalation.

This passage also yields an important subsidiary observation. One might have assumed that *aviv* as a criterion for *Pesach* means only that the barley has ripened, since the *omer* brought on *Pesach* is from barley, while the *shtei halechem* of *Shavuot* is from wheat. But the *braita*, and Rashi explicitly, indicate that the ideal *aviv* condition for *Pesach* includes the beginning of the wheat's ripening as well — at least in some regions.

## Time Restrictions…

### Before RH Ban

The next *Tnu Rabbanan*: *ein me'avrin et hashana lifnei Rosh Hashanah* — one may not intercalate the year before *Rosh Hashanah*. A clarification is needed here: *Rosh Hashanah* in this context means the midpoint of the year, not the *Rosh Hashanah* twelve months prior. Since *ibur hashana* concerns *Adar* and *Nissan*, the relevant *Rosh Hashanah* falls mid-year. The intercalation must be done in the second half of the year, not the first. *V'im ivruha, eina me'uberet* — even if one did intercalate before *Rosh Hashanah*, it is not valid.

*Aval mipnei hadochak* — however, if there is pressing political constraint (Rashi suggests Roman governmental pressure, since *ibur hashana* was an exercise of religious authority and therefore sometimes subject to political interference), then *ma'avrin osa achar Rosh Hashanah miyad* — one may intercalate immediately after *Rosh Hashanah*. The preference is to do it closer to the relevant time both because the data about conditions will be more accurate and because people will take greater notice of it. But if compelled by circumstance, one may do it right after *Rosh Hashanah*.

### Adar Only

Even so: *v'af al pi chein ein me'avrin ela Adar* — regardless of when the decision is made, the only month one may add is *Adar*. The Gemara itself never explains why this is so. The intuitive answer would be that the Torah commands *Pesach* to fall in *chodesh ha'aviv*, and therefore the only way to properly situate *Pesach* is to fix the month immediately preceding it — which is *Adar*.

Tosafot, however, offers a different reason entirely. The *pasuk* in Esther states *b'chodesh shenayim asar hu chodesh Adar* — "in the twelfth month, which is *Adar*." The Torah similarly fixes *Nissan* as the first month and *Tishrei* as the seventh. These numerical designations are anchored. If one were to add a month other than *Adar*, all those fixed numberings would shift, and *Adar* could no longer be the twelfth month. The only way to preserve the numbering is to add a thirteenth month at the end — a second *Adar* — without displacing any of the others. This is a fascinating reason, and it is noteworthy that the Gemara itself does not state it.

## Political Constraints

### Coded Message

The Gemara then raises a challenge: *v'ha shalchu lei l'Rava* — but they sent to Rava the following message. The message is cryptic, evidently designed so that Roman authorities would not understand it. *Zug ba mi-Rakat* — a pair has come from *Rakat*, which Rashi identifies as Tiberias, meaning two *talmidei chachamim*. *V'tafso nesher* — and the eagle has seized them. The eagle, of course, is Rome, whose legions bore the eagle as their standard. *U'vi'yadam devarim ha-na'asim b'Luz* — and in their hands were things produced in Luz. The Gemara interjects: *u'mai nihu techelet* — what is this? *Techelet*. *Techelet* was produced in Luz. Rome had a vested interest in *techelet* as a controlled substance — it was the royal blue — and so these scholars may have been seized for violating some edict or for evading duties on it. *U'vizchut ha-rachamim u'vizchutam yatz'u b'shalom* — through divine mercy and their own merit, they came out unharmed.

### Rome's Prevention

*V'amusei yarchei Nachshon* — and those who carried the lineage of the loins of Nachshon, meaning *Nachshon ben Aminadav*, i.e. the house of Yehuda and ultimately the *nasi*'s household. *Bikshu likvo'a natziv* — they sought to establish a *natziv*, which we will shortly see is a term for an intercalated month. *V'lo hinicha'an adomi ha-laz* — and this Edomite — Rome — did not allow them. The Roman government prevented them from performing the *ibur hashana*.

### Decision in Av

*Aval ba'alei asupot ne'esfu v'kav'u lo natziv echad b'yerach she-met bo Aharon haKohen* — but those who gather — the *talmidei chachamim* — gathered and established an extra month in the month in which Aaron the *Kohen* died, which is Av. Now this seems problematic: how could they do *ibur hashana* in Av, which is before *Rosh Hashanah*? The Gemara resolves this: *chashuvei machshvei, galuyei lo me-galu* — they performed the calculations, and they determined that an intercalation would be required. But they did not make the formal announcement, or alternatively they did not perform the official act creating the intercalation — they merely resolved upon it and completed the calculation. The public proclamation was reserved for the proper time.

### Natziv Terminology

The Gemara then asks: *mai mashma d'hai natziv lishna d'yarcha hu* — how do we know that the word *natziv* in this coded message means a month? The source is the verse: *u'li-Shlomo shenayim asar netzivim al kol Yisrael v'chalkelu et ha-melech v'et beito chodesh ba-shanah* — Solomon appointed twelve officers over all Israel, and each one provided sustenance for the king's household during one month of the year. The association of a *natziv* — an officer — with a monthly appointment is what gives the term its meaning as a month in the coded communication.

The Gemara then cites a verse indicating that there was *v'natziv echad asher ba'aretz* — one *natziv* over the land. Two opinions are offered: one says this was a superintendent over the twelve; the other says *keneged chodesh ibur* — this was the officer corresponding to the intercalated month, the second *Adar*, whose role only arose in a leap year.

## Shemita & Consecutive

### No Consecutive Years

The next *Tnu Rabbanan* addresses a different set of restrictions: *ein me'avrin et hashana lo mishanah l'chaverta* — one does not intercalate from one year to the next. The precise meaning of this is debated between Rashi and Tosafot. Rashi understands it to mean that one may not intercalate this year for the sake of the *next* year's seasons — intercalation must address a problem of the *current* year. Tosafot understands it differently: one may not even make the calculation more than a year in advance. This would explain the apparent redundancy with the rule that one cannot intercalate before *Rosh Hashanah* — Tosafot's reading extends the prohibition to the calculation itself, not merely the act, since so many relevant factors are unknown that far in advance.

*V'lo shalosh shanim zo achar zo* — and one may not intercalate three years in a row, as that would distort the calendar entirely.

Rabbi Shimon then introduces a story: *ma'aseh b'Rabbi Akiva shehayah chavush b'veit ha'asurim* — there was an episode involving Rabbi Akiva while he was imprisoned, in the period leading up to his martyrdom. *V'iber shalosh shanim zo achar zo* — and they effectively calculated three consecutive leap years, sending a delegation to consult with Rabbi Akiva in prison. This seems to directly contradict the prohibition.

The Gemara responds: *amru lo misham ra'ayah?* — you want to bring a proof from there? *Beit din yashvu v'kav'u achat achat bizmanah* — the court sat and established each one in its proper time. The resolution is not entirely clear. One reading is that the three leap years Rabbi Akiva calculated were not actually consecutive — they were the next three occasions on which intercalation would be required, but spread out over a longer period. Another reading is that even if three consecutive leap years were warranted, what is prohibited is the advance determination that they will all be intercalated, without reassessing each one in its proper time — whereas here, each year was independently assessed when it arrived. The precise parameters of the prohibition and the precise force of the resolution thus remain somewhat open.

### Shemita Bans

*Ein me'avrin et hashana lo bishvi'it v'lo b'motza'ei shvi'it* — one does not intercalate during *shemita* and does not intercalate in the year immediately following *shemita*. The reason for the *shemita* year is obvious: intercalating extends the period in which one cannot plant. The reason for the year after *shemita* is that people have been waiting anxiously for the *omer* to be brought as soon as possible so that they may begin eating grain from the fields they have just resumed planting. Delaying *Pesach* by an extra month makes that wait even longer.

### Preferred Timing

*Eimatai regilin l'aber* — when was the preferred time to intercalate? *Erev shvi'it* — the year before *shemita*. This makes sense: giving people an extra month of planting and harvesting before *shemita* arrives is a genuine benefit.

### Vegetable Imports

*Shebeveit Raban Gamliel hayu me'avrin b'motza'ei shvi'it* — in the house of Raban Gamliel they would intercalate in the year after *shemita*. How could they do this if it would delay people's access to new grain? The Gemara explains: *uv'plugta d'hani tanna'ei* — this reflects a tannaitic dispute. The underlying debate is: *ein mevi'in yerak michutz la'aretz* — one may not import vegetables from outside Eretz Yisrael, while *v'raboteinu hitiru* — the sages permitted it. The question between them, as Rabbi Yirmiyah explains, is whether one is concerned about the soil that adheres to the vegetables, since the soil of *chutz la'aretz* is *tamei* and importing it could create *tumah* problems.

The house of Raban Gamliel held that vegetables may be imported. If so, food could be brought in from *chutz la'aretz* to compensate for the delay, making intercalation after *shemita* tolerable. It is worth noting that this logic — intercalating a leap year and relying on imports to supply food — reflects a trade-off between *halachic* concerns and economic priorities, specifically whether it is appropriate to make calendar decisions on the assumption that agricultural self-sufficiency can be supplemented by imports.

## Tumah Exception

### Tumah as Reason

The next *Tnu Rabbanan*: *ein me'avrin et hashana mipnei hatumah* — one does not intercalate the year on account of *tumah*. The case would be: there is concern that people are *tamei* and unable to bring the *korban Pesach*; if we add a month, people will have time to become *tahor* and be able to participate. Nevertheless, this is not a valid reason. *Rabbi Yehuda omer me'avrin* — Rabbi Yehuda says it is a valid reason.

### Chizkiyahu Story

Rabbi Yehuda then adds: *ma'aseh b'Chizkiyahu melech Yehuda she'eibur et hashana mipnei hatumah* — there was an episode in which Chizkiyahu, king of Yehuda, intercalated the year because of *tumah*. *U'vikeish rachamim al atzmo* — and he then sought divine forgiveness for himself.

This is immediately puzzling. Rabbi Yehuda first says intercalating for *tumah* is permitted, then cites Chizkiyahu as support, but then reports that Chizkiyahu sought forgiveness — which would imply it was wrong. The Gemara will need to unravel what Rabbi Yehuda is actually saying.

The scriptural basis for the episode is the verse: *ki marbit ha'am rabat me'Ephraim u'Menashe Yissachar u'Zevulun lo hitaharu* — the vast majority of the people from those tribes were not ritually pure when Chizkiyahu convened the great *korban Pesach* revival. A subsequent verse states: *ki achlu et haPesach b'lo kakatuv* — they ate the *Pesach* offering contrary to what is written. The plain reading of these verses is that they ate the *korban Pesach* in a state of *tumah*. But the Gemara does not read it that way.

First, if the majority of the people were *tamei*, the principle of *tumah hutra b'tzibbur* would apply, and eating the *korban Pesach b'tumah* in that case would be permitted. So that cannot be what "contrary to what is written" refers to. Second, the Gemara chooses to understand the violation differently: Chizkiyahu, seeing that the people were *tamei*, decided to solve the problem by intercalating the year — adding a month to give people time to become *tahor*. It was this act of intercalation that was the violation. The people's eating of the *korban Pesach* at the wrong time was the consequence — they observed *Pesach* at what should not have been the correct time, because the *ibur hashana* had been improperly performed. *Ki hitpalel Chizkiyahu aleihem leimor Hashem hatov yichaper b'ad* — Chizkiyahu then prayed for forgiveness on their behalf.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees entirely: *im mipnei hatumah ibrunah me'uberet* — if they intercalated because of *tumah*, the intercalation is valid. So what was Chizkiyahu praying for forgiveness for? *She'ein me'avrin ela Adar v'hu iber Nissan b'Nissan* — because one may only add *Adar*, and Chizkiyahu made his decision when *Nissan* had already arrived. He effectively added a second *Nissan* and pushed *Pesach* to the second *Nissan* rather than the first.

Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda then offers yet another reading in the name of Rabbi Shimon: Chizkiyahu's sin was that he directed all the *tamei* people to go and bring a *Pesach sheini* — the second *Pesach* observed a month later for those who could not bring the offering in *Nissan*. This was wrong because since the majority of the people were *tamei*, *tumah hutra b'tzibbur* should have applied, and they should have been permitted to bring the *Pesach rishon* together with everyone else.

### Tumah Hutra/Dechuya

The Gemara now works through the conceptual structure underlying this dispute. *Amar mar Rabbi Yehuda omer me'avrin — alma sevira lei l'Rabbi Yehuda tumah dechuya hi b'tzibbur* — the Gemara infers that Rabbi Yehuda, by saying intercalation is warranted, must hold that *tumah* is merely *dechuya* — overridden but still a violation — in the case of a *tzibbur*. If *tumah* is only overridden and not fully permitted, then we should prefer to avoid the conflict altogether by adding a month, rather than having people eat the *korban* in a state of *tumah* even under permitted circumstances.

But a challenge arises: there is a *braita* concerning the *tzitz* — the golden headplate worn by the *Kohen Gadol* — which atones for *korbanot* brought in *tumah*. The dispute there concerns whether the *tzitz* operates to make such a *korban* acceptable only while the *Kohen Gadol* is actively wearing it on his forehead (*divrei Rabbi Shimon*) or whether its presence in the *mishkan* is sufficient even if he is not wearing it (*Rabbi Yehuda*). Rabbi Shimon challenges Rabbi Yehuda: what about the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur*, when he wears white garments and is not wearing the *tzitz*, and yet we know that *korbanot* brought in *tumah* on *Yom Kippur* are acceptable? Rabbi Yehuda responds: *henach l'Yom Hakippurim she-tumah hutra b'tzibur* — do not cite *Yom Kippur* to me, because on *Yom Kippur* the *tumah* is *hutra* — fully permitted — for the *tzibbur*, and that is why those *korbanot* are acceptable, not because of the *tzitz*.

This is decisive: Rabbi Yehuda himself says *tumah hutra b'tzibbur*, not *dechuya*. But if *tumah* is fully permitted for the *tzibbur*, there is no basis for intercalating the year in order to avoid the situation. Why, then, did Rabbi Yehuda say intercalation is warranted?

### Emendation of View

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *ela chisuri michsera v'hachi katani* — the *braita* must be emended, and this is how it should be read: *ein me'avrin et hashana mipnei hatumah v'im ibru me'uberet* — one does not intercalate because of *tumah*, but if one did, *bedieved* it takes effect. *Rabbi Yehuda omer eina me'uberet* — Rabbi Yehuda says it does not take effect even *bedieved*.

This is a striking reversal. Rabbi Yehuda has been moved from one extreme to the other: from being the one who says *lechatchila* one may intercalate for *tumah*, to being the one who says even *bedieved* it does not work. But now the positions are internally consistent. Rabbi Yehuda holds *tumah hutra b'tzibbur* — there is no violation, hence no motivation to intercalate, and an intercalation performed for that reason is nullified even after the fact. And now Rabbi Yehuda's citation of Chizkiyahu also makes sense: Chizkiyahu added a month because of *tumah* and asked for forgiveness precisely because it was invalid — consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's position that such intercalation is ineffective.

The Gemara then asks: *ei hachi Rabbi Shimon omer im mipnei hatumah ibrunah me'uberet — hainu tanna kamma* — if this is how we read the positions, then Rabbi Shimon, who says *bedieved* the intercalation works, is identical to the first *tanna*, who also says *bedieved* it works. *Amar Rava lechatchila ika beinaihu* — Rava resolves this: the debate between them is about *lechatchila*. The first *tanna* holds that *lechatchila* one does not intercalate for *tumah*, but *bedieved* it takes effect. Rabbi Yehuda holds it does not take effect even *bedieved*. And Rabbi Shimon holds that one may even do so *lechatchila*. A supporting *braita* confirms this three-way structure.

### Fixed-Time Principle

Before proceeding, Tosafot makes an important clarifying observation about the phrase *tumah hutra b'tzibbur*. The formulation implies that the leniency is a function of the *tzibbur* — communal — nature of the offering. But Tosafot insists this is not precise. *Ein ha-davar talui b'tzibbur* — the matter does not depend on whether the offering is a *korban tzibbur*.

Tosafot cites the *mishnah* in the chapter *Hotzi'u*: the *par* of *Yom Kippur*, the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, and *Pesach* — all of which are technically *korbanot yachid*, individual offerings — override both *Shabbos* and *tumah*. On the other hand, the *par he'elam davar shel tzibbur*, the *se'irei avoda zara*, and *chagiga* — all *korbanot tzibbur* — do *not* override *Shabbos* and *tumah*. So the distinction between *tzibbur* and *yachid* does not map onto the question of whether *tumah* is permitted.

Tosafot then states the true principle: *kol she-zmano kavu'a docheh* — any *korban* whose time is fixed overrides *Shabbos* and *tumah*, even if it is a private offering; *v'chol she-ein zmano kavu'a eino docheh v'afilu de-tzibur* — and any *korban* whose time is not fixed does not override, even if it is a communal offering. The phrase *tumah hutra b'tzibbur*, which is so commonly invoked, is thus a shorthand that is, strictly speaking, imprecise — it is really about fixed-time *korbanot*, not about the communal versus individual distinction.

## Chizkiyahu's Error

### 30th Adar Error

The Gemara now returns to Chizkiyahu's specific error. *Amar mar she-ein me'avrin ela Adar v'hu iber Nissan b'Nissan* — the claim is that Chizkiyahu did not add an extra *Nissan* in the straightforward sense. Rather, he declared a second *Adar* — but he did so on the 30th day of *Adar* itself. The violation was thus not adding *Nissan* per se, but declaring the intercalation when it was already the 30th of *Adar*.

### Shmuel's Ruling

This is based on Shmuel's ruling: *de-amar Shmuel ein me'avrin et hashana b'yom shloshim shel Adar* — even if *Adar* is a 30-day month, one may not perform the intercalation on *Adar* 30. The reason is that *Adar* 30 *hu ra'uy l'kovo Nissan* — that day is potentially *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*. Once a day is capable of being *Nissan*, the window for declaring an intercalation has closed.

Chizkiyahu's mistake was that he reasoned: *v'hu savar chov v'ra'uy lo amrinan* — he thought that the rule *ra'uy*, "potentially," does not close the window. He believed that as long as *Nissan* had not been formally declared, there was still time to add a second *Adar*. That was his error, and it is what required his seeking of forgiveness.

### Tannai Debate

The Gemara confirms Shmuel's ruling with a supporting *braita*: *ein me'avrin et hashana b'yom shloshim shel Adar hu ra'uy l'kovo Nissan* — one may not intercalate on *Adar* 30 since that day is eligible to become *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*. With this, the Gemara has assembled all the opinions regarding Chizkiyahu's sin: according to Rabbi Yehuda (as re-read), his sin was adding a month because of *tumah* when that is not a valid basis; according to Rabbi Shimon, his sin was that he did so on *Nissan* itself — specifically on *Adar* 30, which can already be the beginning of *Nissan*; and according to Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon, his sin was directing *tamei* individuals to bring *Pesach sheini* when they should have been permitted to bring *Pesach rishon* along with the majority.
