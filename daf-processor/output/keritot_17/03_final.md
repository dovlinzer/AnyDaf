# Keritot 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Shabbatot Debate

### Abaye's Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ זְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּשַׁבָּתוֹת לָאו כְּגוּפִין דָּמְיָין, וְשִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת הוּא דְּבָעֵי מִינֵּיהּ, דְּיָמִים שֶׁבֵּינְתַיִם מִי הָוַיִין יְדִיעָה לְחַלֵּק אוֹ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabba: You cannot prove your claim from the mishna. **Actually, I** could **say to you** that in a case where one acts **intentionally** in that he knows it is **Shabbat and unwittingly** in that he does not know that his actions are prohibited **labors,** it is **obvious to Rabbi Akiva that *Shabbatot* are not comparable** to separate **entities,** as it can be inferred from the latter clause of this mishna that one is not liable to bring a separate sin offering for each primary category of labor performed on each individual Shabbat. **And it is** in reference to a case where one acted **unwittingly** in that he did not know it was **Shabbat and intentionally** in that he knew his actions are prohibited **labors, that** Rabbi Akiva **raises his dilemma to** Rabbi Eliezer. The dilemma is whether the **intervening days are** considered **awareness to differentiate** between the *Shabbatot* **or not.**

The discussion on 17b opens with Abaye's response to Rava, which offers a subtly different framing of the debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer — yet arrives at the same substantive conclusions. *Amar lei Abaye*: Abaye contends that the question of *zadon Shabbos ve-shiggas melachos* — knowing it was Shabbat but forgetting the forbidden labors — was never even in dispute between the two Tannaim. Rabbi Akiva took it entirely for granted that different *Shabbosos* constitute *gufin mechulakim*, categorically distinct entities. That was his unquestioned premise, not the subject of his inquiry. What Rabbi Akiva actually posed to Rabbi Eliezer was the case of *shiggas Shabbos ve-zadon melachos* — forgetting that it was Shabbat altogether — and whether, when a person did so, the days intervening between one Shabbat and the next constitute a *yedi'ah lechalek*, a sufficient intermediate awareness to divide the liability. Rabbi Eliezer answered yes, and — crucially — Rabbi Akiva accepted this answer. Conversely, when Rabbi Eliezer maintained that different *toldos* of the same *av melacha* count as separate *chata'os*, that Rabbi Akiva did not accept.

According to this reading, the two opinions in the *Mishnah* were not arguing over *gufin mechulakim* at all. The debate was about whether the passing of days between one Shabbat and the next, in a case where the person forgot it was Shabbat, functions as an intervening *yedi'ah*. The structurally parallel version attributed to Rava had the positions reversed: the question taken for granted was the *yedi'ah* function of the intervening days, while *gufin mechulakim* by multiple *Shabbosos* was what was actually debated. Yet the bottom line under both readings is identical. Rabbi Akiva holds that the same *melacha* performed across multiple *Shabbosos* is not *gufin mechulakim* — all such repetitions are treated as a single act — while forgetting that it was Shabbat, with days passing between one Shabbat and the next, does constitute *yedi'ah lechalek* and divides the liability.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְשַׁיט לֵיהּ דִּיָמִים שֶׁבֵּינְתַיִם [הָוַיִין יְדִיעָה] לְחַלֵּק וְקַבֵּיל מִינֵּיהּ, וּפְשַׁט לֵיהּ וַלְדֵי מְלָאכוֹת כִּמְלָאכוֹת דָּמַיִין וְלָא קַבֵּיל מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Eliezer **resolved** the dilemma **for him** by stating **that** the **intervening days** are considered awareness **to differentiate** between the *Shabbatot*. **And** Rabbi Akiva **accepted** this resolution **from him,** due the ruling of the mishna that one who knows the essence of the prohibition against labor on Shabbat and performs multiple prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot* is liable to bring a sin offering for each and every Shabbat. **And** Rabbi Eliezer also **resolved** his other dilemma **for him** by stating that **subcategories of labors are comparable** to primary categories of **labor** and one is liable for each act, **but** Rabbi Akiva **did not accept** this resolution **from him.**

Rava's contribution, preceding Abaye's reformulation, had established the same ultimate positions for Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer from a different angle. The key point shared by both Abaye and Rava is that multiple *Shabbosos* are considered *guf echad* — a single unified entity — according to Rabbi Akiva, because the analogy to *arayos* that Rabbi Eliezer urged does not hold. *Gufin mechulakim* by *arayos* is compelling because different women are genuinely distinct persons. Days on a calendar are not analogous objects; the same *melacha* repeated on different *Shabbosos* is simply the same act done again, not a structurally different transgression. What does divide, for Rabbi Akiva, is the passage of days when the person had forgotten it was Shabbat — there, the days themselves impose a kind of constructive remembrance, *yedi'ah lechalek*.

### Rav Chisda's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: זְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת – אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ דִּכְגוּפִין דָּמְיָין, וְכִי בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ, שִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת הוּא דְּבָעֵי מִינֵּיהּ, דְּיָמִים שֶׁבֵּינְתַיִם אִי הָוְיָין יְדִיעָה לְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says:** In a case where one acts **intentionally** in that he knows it is **Shabbat and unwittingly** in that he does not know that his actions are prohibited **labors, even Rabbi Akiva holds that** the *Shabbatot* **are comparable** to separate **entities; and when he raises the dilemma** to Rabbi Eliezer, **it is** in reference to a case where one acted **unwittingly** with regard to **Shabbat and intentionally** with regard to prohibited **labor. And he raises the dilemma** in order to clarify whether or not the **intervening days are** considered **awareness to differentiate.**

Rav Chisda advances a dramatically different reading, one that diverges not only in the interpretation of the debate but in the ultimate position it assigns to Rabbi Akiva. According to Rav Chisda, *zadon Shabbos ve-shiggas melachos* — knowing it was Shabbat while forgetting the *melachos* — is a case where even Rabbi Akiva concedes that different *Shabbosos* are *gufin mechulakim*. Far from resisting this conclusion, Rabbi Akiva endorsed it without controversy. The question Rabbi Akiva actually raised was the *shiggas Shabbos* case: when a person forgot it was Shabbat, are the intervening days between one Shabbat and the next treated as a *yedi'ah lechalek*? Rabbi Eliezer answered affirmatively, and Rabbi Akiva accepted this as well. Under Rav Chisda's reading, then, Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer ultimately agree on both principles: multiple *Shabbosos* are *gufin mechulakim*, and the intervening days function as *yedi'ah lechalek*. The sole disagreement that remained was over whether multiple *toldos* of the same *av* count as separate *chata'os* — and there Rabbi Akiva refused to accept Rabbi Eliezer's position.

The implication of Rav Chisda's view is maximal liability. Both the distinction between *Shabbosos* and the passage of days multiply one's *chata'os*. If a person forgot three *melachos* and performed them on four consecutive *Shabbosos*, the resulting number of sin-offerings could be very large indeed. Rabbi Eliezer, of course, always arrives at maximum multiplicity; what Rav Chisda asserts is that Rabbi Akiva, properly understood, does too — at least on these two axes.

The obvious tension with the plain reading of the *Mishnah* is noted. The *Mishnah* had framed the debate in terms of *gufin mechulakim*, comparing multiple *Shabbosos* to multiple women, animals, and minors. If Rabbi Akiva had accepted *gufin mechulakim* for *Shabbos* without dispute, what was the comparison to *niddah* and *behemah* about? Rav Chisda will need to reread those comparisons as bearing on *yedi'ah lechalek* rather than *gufin mechulakim*. That reinterpretation will be taken up later in the *Gemara*.

### Comparison to Niddah

To summarize the three positions before the *Gemara* moves to its proofs: according to both Abaye and Rava, Rabbi Akiva says multiple *Shabbosos* are *not gufin mechulakim* — all repetitions of the same *melacha* across different *Shabbosos* count as one — while the days between *Shabbosos* do constitute *yedi'ah lechalek*. According to Rav Chisda, Rabbi Akiva says that multiple *Shabbosos* are *gufin mechulakim*, and the days in between are also *yedi'ah lechalek* — so both mechanisms of division apply. The *Mishnah*'s comparisons to *niddah*, minors, and animals, which appear on their face to be a *gufin mechulakim* debate, will require alternative explanation under Rav Chisda's reading, and the *Gemara* will return to this problem shortly.

## Letters on Shabbat

### Two Letters Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַכּוֹתֵב שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד – חַיָּיב. בִּשְׁתֵּי הֶעְלֵמוֹת – רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין. וּמוֹדֶה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, שֶׁאִם כָּתַב אוֹת אַחַת בְּשַׁבָּת זוֹ וְאוֹת אַחַת בְּשַׁבָּת אַחֶרֶת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One who writes two letters** on Shabbat **during one lapse of awareness** is **liable,** as writing two letters renders one liable for performing the prohibited labor of writing. If he does so **in two** separate **lapses of awareness,** e.g., he wrote one letter without realizing it was Shabbat, then became aware that it was Shabbat, and then again became unaware it was Shabbat and wrote a second letter, **Rabban Gamliel deems** him **liable** to bring a sin offering **and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt. And Rabban Gamliel concedes that if he wrote one letter on this Shabbat and one letter on another Shabbat** he is **exempt.**

The *Gemara* now turns to Rav Chisda's evidentiary basis for his position. He cites a *baraita* concerning one who writes two letters on Shabbat. If both letters are written within a single period of forgetfulness, the person is liable — that is uncontroversial. The *baraita* then addresses the case of *btei helamot*, two separate states of forgetfulness separated by an intermediate moment of awareness: one letter was written, the person remembered (either that it was Shabbat or that writing was forbidden), forgot again, and then wrote the second letter. The question is whether that intervening awareness divides the two half-measures so as to prevent liability. A *chata'os* for Shabbat writing requires completing the full *shiur* of two letters; if one letter followed by remembering followed by another letter is treated as two separate incomplete acts, no liability attaches.

### Rabban Gamliel's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא אַחֲרִיתִי: הַכּוֹתֵב שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת בִּשְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת, אֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת זוֹ וְאֶחָד בְּשַׁבָּת זוֹ – רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין. קָא סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ, דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in **a different** *baraita*: In the case of **one who writes two letters on two *Shabbatot*, one on this Shabbat and one on that Shabbat, Rabban Gamliel deems** him **liable and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.** Rav Ḥisda explains: It **enters your mind that Rabban Gamliel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.**

On this question, the *baraita* records a dispute: Rabban Gamliel holds the person *chayav*, while the *Chachamim* exempt. Rabban Gamliel's position is that while remembering in between can divide in a direction that multiplies *korbanot*, it cannot divide in a direction that exempts a person from liability for completing a *shiur*. This principle — *ein yedi'ah lechatzi shiur* — means that awareness gained after completing only half a required measure does not retroactively split the act into two inadequate halves. The *Chachamim* disagree, holding that any genuine intermediate awareness divides the act regardless of the direction of its consequence.

A related *baraita* is then cited: one who writes one letter on this Shabbat and one letter on the following Shabbat — here too Rabban Gamliel holds the person *chayav*, and the *Chachamim* exempt. At first glance this seems contradictory to the earlier ruling, since in the first case Rabban Gamliel was holding people *chayav* over the *Chachamim*'s *petur*, while here he also holds *chayav* — yet the reason must be different. In the earlier case the logic was *ein yedi'ah lechatzi shiur*; in this case, involving two distinct *Shabbosos*, the relevant principle must be something else.

### Rav Chisda's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמֵינָא זְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר דְּשַׁבָּתוֹת כְּגוּפִין דָּמְיָין, הָא דְּתַנְיָא פָּטוּר – בִּזְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת, דְּשַׁבָּתוֹת כְּגוּפִין דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to my** opinion, **as I say** that in a case where one acts **intentionally** in that he knows it is **Shabbat and unwittingly** in that he does not know his actions constitute prohibited **labors, even Rabbi Akiva says that** the ***Shabbatot* are comparable** to separate **entities.** Consequently, **that which is taught** in the first *baraita*, that Rabban Gamliel concedes that if one wrote one letter on Shabbat and another letter the following Shabbat he is **exempt,** can be interpreted as referring **to** a case where one acts **intentionally** in that he knows it is **Shabbat but unwittingly** in that he does not know his actions constitute prohibited **labors. Since** separate ***Shabbatot* are comparable** to separate **entities,** the two acts do not combine to form the minimal act of writing that would render one liable to bring a sin offering.

Rav Chisda now explains both rulings of Rabban Gamliel in a unified fashion, and this is the crux of his argument. He proceeds on the assumption that Rabban Gamliel holds like Rabbi Akiva as interpreted by Rav Chisda himself — namely, that Rabbi Akiva accepts *gufin mechulakim* for *Shabbos* in the case of *zadon Shabbos ve-shiggas melachos*, and accepts *yedi'ah lechalek* for the case of *shiggas Shabbos ve-zadon melachos*.

With this framework, the two *baraisos* are resolved as follows. The first case — where Rabban Gamliel holds *chayav* when a person writes one letter, remembers, and then writes another — involves a case of *shiggas Shabbos ve-zadon melachos*: the person forgot it was Shabbat. Since the person forgot it was Shabbat, going from one Shabbat to the next is like having remembered in between — *yedi'ah lechalek*. One might think that this constructive *yedi'ah* would exempt the person by dividing the two letters. But Rabban Gamliel holds *ein yedi'ah lechatzi shiur*: even when an intermediate remembrance is acknowledged, it does not divide a half-*shiur* so as to prevent liability. Hence the person remains *chayav*.

The second case — where Rabban Gamliel holds *patur* when one letter is written on one Shabbat and one on the next — involves *zadon Shabbos ve-shiggas melachos*: the person remembered it was Shabbat both times. Since the person knew it was Shabbat, the principle of *gufin mechulakim* applies, according to Rav Chisda's reading of Rabbi Akiva. Different *Shabbosos* are categorically distinct, and the two letters do not combine. Therefore the person is *patur*.

This elegant resolution — *gufin mechulakim* explains the *petur*, *ein yedi'ah lechatzi shiur* explains the *chiyuv* — works perfectly under Rav Chisda's framework. The *Gemara* now presses the question of whether the same resolution is available to Abaye and Rava.

### Contradiction Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָדְתַנְיָא פָּטוּר, בְּמַאי מוֹקְמַתְּ לַהּ? לָא בַּחֲדָא וְלָא בַּחֲדָא!
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **that which is taught** in the first *baraita*, that one who writes a letter on each of two *Shabbatot* is **exempt, in what** case **can you interpret it,** i.e., in what circumstances would this ever apply? This ruling does **not** apply **in this** case, where he is aware that it is Shabbat but unaware that his action constitutes a prohibited labor, because Rabbi Akiva, and presumably Rabban Gamliel, maintains that each Shabbat is considered a single entity and therefore it is considered as though he wrote two letters on one Shabbat, and he is liable. **And** this ruling also does **not** apply **in that** case, where one is unaware that it is Shabbat and aware that his action constitutes a prohibited labor, because Rabban Gamliel maintains there is no awareness for a half-measure. Consequently, the two letters are considered to have been written in one lapse of awareness.

According to Abaye and Rava, Rabbi Akiva holds that multiple *Shabbosos* are *not gufin mechulakim* — they are *guf echad*, a single entity. On that view, writing one letter on one Shabbat and one on the next should *never* divide, because the different *Shabbosos* simply do not constitute distinct *gufim*. That means Rabban Gamliel should always hold *chayav* when writing letters across different *Shabbosos*, regardless of whether the person knew it was Shabbat or forgot. But we also have a ruling where Rabban Gamliel holds *patur*. If *Shabbos* is *guf echad*, how can any combination of circumstances ever yield a *petur* from Rabban Gamliel when it comes to letters written on different *Shabbosos*?

The *Gemara* spells out the difficulty precisely. The *chiyuv* ruling is easy to account for: whether the person remembered it was Shabbat (in which case all *Shabbosos* are *guf echad* anyway) or forgot it was Shabbat (in which case the days in between are *yedi'ah lechalek* and should divide — but *ein yedi'ah lechatzi shiur* prevents that division), the person is always *chayav*. The *petur* ruling, however, has no explanation under the Abaye-Rava framework. Neither *gufin mechulakim* (which Abaye and Rava deny for *Shabbos*) nor any other principle available to them can generate a *petur* here.

### Attribution Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר: שַׁבָּתוֹת כְּגוּפִין דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rava** could **say to you** that **Rabban Gamliel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says: *Shabbatot* are comparable** to separate **entities,** and therefore an individual who wrote one letter on one Shabbat and another letter on a different Shabbat is exempt.

The *Gemara*'s response is to question whether Rabban Gamliel must be aligned with Rabbi Akiva at all. Perhaps Rabban Gamliel holds like Rabbi Eliezer, who consistently maintains that *Shabbos* is *gufin mechulakim*. If so, the *petur* ruling is immediately explained: one letter on one Shabbat and one on the next, when the person knew it was Shabbat both times, yields a *petur* because different *Shabbosos* are *gufin mechulakim*, and the two letters do not combine.

The *Gemara*, however, notes a complication. The *baraita* uses the language *u-modeh Rabban Gamliel* — "Rabban Gamliel agrees" — which implies that there is a broader opinion with which he is in some respect disagreeing. The implicit structure of agreement-and-disagreement suggests that wherever Rabban Gamliel "agrees," he is departing from his usual position, meaning there must be some case where he disagrees with whichever *Tanna* is his *patron* — the source of his *gufin mechulakim* principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי: וּמוֹדֶה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי אַאַחְרָנְיָיתָא. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ – הַיְינוּ דִּפְלִיגִי בְּשִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת, דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: אֵין יְדִיעָה לַחֲצִי שִׁיעוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But from** the fact **that** the *baraita* **teaches: And Rabban Gamliel concedes** to the Rabbis that if he wrote one letter on one Shabbat and one more letter on a different Shabbat he is exempt, it can be derived **by inference that they disagree about another** *halakha*. **Granted, if you say** Rabban Gamliel **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** and the opinion of the Rabbis is that of Rabbi Akiva, **this is** referring to the fact **that they disagree** in the other *baraita* **with regard to** the case where one acts **unwittingly** in that he does not know it is **Shabbat and intentionally** in that he knows his actions constitute prohibited **labors, as Rabban Gamliel holds** that although the intervening days are considered awareness to differentiate between the *Shabbatot*, **there is no awareness for a half-measure,** and therefore he is liable.

If Rabban Gamliel is aligned with Rabbi Akiva (under Rav Chisda's reading), then the case where he disagrees with Rabbi Akiva is the *shiggas Shabbos* case: Rabbi Akiva would say that *yedi'ah lechalek* applies and divides the two letters even across *Shabbosos*, while Rabban Gamliel says *ein yedi'ah lechatzi shiur* and holds *chayav*. That disagreement is coherent and identifiable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בִּזְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר, אַלְמָא שַׁבָּתוֹת כְּגוּפִין דָּמְיָין. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי. בְּמַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabban Gamliel concedes** to Rabbi Akiva **in** a case where one acts **intentionally** in that he knows it is **Shabbat and unwittingly** in that he does not know his actions constitute prohibited **labors, that he is exempt. Evidently,** Rabbi Akiva holds that ***Shabbatot* are comparable** to separate **entities,** and therefore the letters written on different *Shabbatot* do not combine, which is why he is exempt. **But if you say Rabban Gamliel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** and from the fact that one *baraita* states that Rabban Gamliel concedes that he is exempt it can be derived **by inference that they disagree** about another similar *halakha*, this raises the question: **About what** case do they disagree?

If, however, Rabban Gamliel is aligned with Rabbi Eliezer, then one must find a case where Rabban Gamliel disagrees with Rabbi Eliezer on an analogous point. Can the *shiggas Shabbos* case serve here? No — because we know from a *baraita* that Rabbi Eliezer himself holds *ein yedi'ah lechatzi shiur*, just as Rabban Gamliel does. They agree on that point. So where would their disagreement lie?

### String on Loom Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּשִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת – אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לֵיהּ דְּאֵין יְדִיעָה לַחֲצִי שִׁיעוּר, דְּתַנְיָא: הַכּוֹתֵב שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת בִּשְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת, אוֹת אַחַת בְּשַׁבָּת זוֹ וְאוֹת אַחַת בְּשַׁבָּת זוֹ – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **If** you suggest that they disagree **with regard to** a case where one acts **unwittingly** in that he does not know it is **Shabbat and intentionally** in that he knows his actions constitute prohibited **labors,** then that cannot be correct. After all, **even Rabbi Eliezer holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel that there is no awareness for a half-measure, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who writes two letters on two** separate ***Shabbatot*, one letter on this Shabbat and one letter on that Shabbat, Rabbi Eliezer deems** him **liable.**

The *Gemara* proposes the case of *achas al ha-arig* — adding a single thread to an already-woven fabric. Normally, the *shiur* for weaving on Shabbat requires two threads. But if there is already a partly completed weave in place and one adds a single thread, perhaps that thread is significant enough in context to render the person liable. This case is structurally analogous to completing a half-*shiur* — similar to the two-letter scenario — and perhaps Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer would disagree here. This proposal is rejected, however, because a *Mishnah* explicitly records that Rabbi Eliezer himself holds *chayav* in this case: *ha-oreg sheloshah chutin bitchilah* — one is liable for weaving three threads at the outset, or one thread onto an existing weave. Since Rabbi Eliezer also obligates in this analogous case, it cannot be the point of dispute between him and Rabban Gamliel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, בְּאַחַת עַל הָאָרִיג – חַיּוֹבֵי מְחַיֵּיב, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הָאוֹרֵג שְׁלֹשָׁה חוּטִין בַּתְּחִלָּה, וְאֶחָד עַל הָאָרִיג – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **But rather,** you might suggest that Rabban Gamliel's concession that he is exempt is referring to a disagreement **with regard to** adding **one** thread **to** a preexisting **woven fabric.** If one weaves three threads together he has violated the prohibited labor of weaving on Shabbat. It might be suggested that if one adds a single thread to a preexisting woven fabric, Rabban Gamliel would deem him liable whereas Rabbi Eliezer would deem him exempt. And yet Rabban Gamliel concedes that if one writes one letter next to a letter written on the previous Shabbat he is exempt. This suggestion is also difficult, as Rabbi Eliezer **deems** one **liable** in the case of adding a single thread, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 105a) that **Rabbi Eliezer says: One who weaves three threads at the beginning** of a new fabric, **or** adds **one** thread **to** a preexisting **woven fabric,** is **liable.**

### Half-Measures Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיג בַּחֲדָא, דְּתַנְיָא: הוֹצִיא חֲצִי גְרוֹגֶרֶת וְחָזַר וְהוֹצִיא חֲצִי גְרוֹגֶרֶת בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] – חַיָּיב. בִּשְׁתֵּי הֶעְלֵמוֹת – פָּטוּר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד בִּרְשׁוּת (אֶחָד) [אַחַת] – חַיָּיב, בִּשְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** When it was derived **by inference that** Rabban Gamliel **disagrees about one** other *halakha*, that disagreement is referring to the following case, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One who **carried out half of a dried fig** into the public domain on Shabbat **and then carried out** another **half of a dried fig, in one lapse of awareness,** is **liable;** if he carried them out **in two lapses of awareness** he is **exempt. Rabbi Yosei says:** Even in a case where this occurred **in one lapse of awareness,** if they were both carried **to one** public **domain** he is **liable;** but if they were carried **to two** public **domains** that are separated from each other he is **exempt.**

Rava resolves the difficulty by identifying a different case as the locus of disagreement. The *baraita* records a dispute about one who carries half a *grogeres* (the requisite measure for carrying on Shabbat), then remembers, then carries another half-*grogeres*. If done in a single state of forgetfulness, the person is *chayav*; if there is an intervening awareness, the person is *patur* — this is the principle of *yesh yedi'ah lechatzi shiur* held by the *tanna kamma* there. Rabbi Yosi adds a further case: if the two halves were carried into two different domains — say, into one *reshus ha-rabbim* and another *reshus ha-rabbim* — even within a single state of forgetfulness, Rabbi Yosi says the person is *patur*, because the two halves went to separate locations and do not combine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **As** in such a case, **Rabban Gamliel** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** who says that the separate domains do not cause the two acts to be considered separate; therefore, as he carried out a full dried fig, which is the minimum measure for the prohibited labor of carrying from one domain to another, he is liable. **And Rabbi Eliezer** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who says the separate domains cause the acts to be considered separate, and he is exempt. Another *baraita* then states that although Rabban Gamliel maintains that the separate domains do not cause the two acts to be considered separate, he concedes that individual *Shabbatot* are considered separate entities, and therefore writing two letters on two different *Shabbatot* is considered two separate acts and the individual is exempt.

Rava suggests that this is the point of disagreement between Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Eliezer. Rabban Gamliel would hold that two half-*shiurim* carried into two separate domains do not combine — *patur* — while Rabbi Eliezer would hold, in line with Rabbi Yosi's dispensation, that they do combine — *chayav*. This makes Rabbi Eliezer even more stringent than Rabban Gamliel in this respect. With this case identified as their point of disagreement, the structure of *u-modeh Rabban Gamliel* is coherent: Rabban Gamliel agrees with Rabbi Eliezer that different *Shabbosos* divide (under *gufin mechulakim*) and yields a *petur*, but disagrees in the case of two half-measures into two domains.

The long chain of reasoning in this section has thus served a single purpose: to test whether the two contradictory rulings attributed to Rabban Gamliel can be resolved consistently with either Rabbi Akiva's position (as read by Rav Chisda) or Rabbi Eliezer's position. The conclusion is that the two rulings cohere under either alignment, but with different cases serving as the point of disagreement between Rabban Gamliel and his respective *patron*.

## Mishnah Analysis

### Niddah Singular

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר לוֹ: חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה נִדָּה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ תּוֹצָאוֹת הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof for Rav Ḥisda's opinion from the mishna: Rabbi Eliezer **said to** Rabbi Akiva: He is **liable** to bring a sin offering **for** violation of **each and every one** of the labors, and this is derived **from an *a fortiori*** inference. **Just as** in the case of **a menstruating woman, with regard to whom there are not multiple** actions that **result** in multiple sin offerings, one is liable to bring a separate sin offering for each act of unwitting intercourse; in the case of Shabbat, with regard to which there are multiple primary categories and subcategories of labor, is it not right that one will be liable to bring a sin offering for each prohibited labor?

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Mishnah* and scrutinizes a grammatical detail that turns out to carry significant interpretive weight. In the *Mishnah*, Rabbi Eliezer's *kal va-chomer* argument invokes *niddah* in the singular: *u-mah niddah she-ein bah totza'os harbeh* — "just as *niddah*, which does not have many subcategories, nevertheless obligates a separate *chata'os* for each act, how much more so Shabbat." The *Gemara* observes that the use of the singular *niddah* fits naturally with Rav Chisda's reading. According to Rav Chisda, the *Mishnah*'s debate is not about *gufin mechulakim* — not about multiple women — but about whether the passage of time constitutes *yedi'ah lechalek*. That question can be illustrated with a single *niddah*, as we shall see momentarily. But according to Rava, who holds that the debate concerns *gufin mechulakim* — whether multiple *Shabbosos* are analogous to multiple distinct entities — the comparison should be to *nidot* in the plural, multiple women, since that is the parallel structure. Why then does the *Mishnah* use the singular?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: תְּנִי ״נִדּוֹת״. שְׁמוּאֵל תָּנֵי ״נִדָּה״, רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה תָּנֵי ״נִדָּה״. רַב נָתָן בַּר אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: תָּנֵי ״נִדּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds that **Rava** could **say to you:** Indeed, **teach** the mishna with the plural term **menstruating women.** The Gemara notes that **Shmuel teaches** the mishna with the singular term **menstruating woman,** and **Rav Adda bar Ahava teaches** the mishna with the term **menstruating woman;** but **Rav Natan bar Oshaya says: Teach** the mishna with the plural term **menstruating women.**

Rava's response is pragmatic: simply emend the text and read *nidot*. Indeed, there is a direct *machlokes girsa'os* — a dispute over the correct textual reading — among the Amoraim. Shmuel's version of the text had *niddah* in the singular, and he learned in accordance with Rav Chisda's reading. Rav Adda bar Ahava also read *niddah* in the singular. Rav Nasan bar Oshaya, by contrast, read *nidot* in the plural — consistent with Rava's position that the debate is about *gufin mechulakim*.

### Mikva'ot Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב חִסְדָּא דְּאָמַר: שִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת הוּא דְּבָעֵי מִינֵּיהּ, דְּיָמִים שֶׁבֵּינְתַיִם מִי הָוַיִין יְדִיעָה לְחַלֵּק וְאִי לָא, נִדָּה – מַאי יָמִים שֶׁבֵּינְתַיִם הָוַיִין יְדִיעָה לְחַלֵּק אִית בַּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rav Ḥisda, who says: It was** in reference to a case where he was **unwitting** with regard to **Shabbat and intentional** with regard to prohibited **labors that** Rabbi Akiva **raises** his **dilemma,** and the question was **whether** the **intervening days are** considered **awareness to differentiate** between the *Shabbatot* **or not,** how could Rabbi Eliezer attempt to resolve the dilemma by a comparison to a menstruating woman? In the case of **a menstruating woman, what intervening days apply to her** that can **be** considered **awareness** of his sin, which serves **to differentiate** the acts of intercourse into distinct transgressions?

But the *Gemara* presses further on Rav Chisda's behalf. If the debate in the *Mishnah* is about *yedi'ah lechalek* — whether the passage of time between one Shabbat and the next constitutes a constructive remembrance — what does a comparison to *niddah* add? What analog to *yamim she-beintayim* exists in the context of *niddah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּא עָלֶיהָ וְטָבְלָה, וְרָאֲתָה וְחָזַר וּבָא עָלֶיהָ, וְטָבְלָה, וְחָזַר וּבָא עָלֶיהָ, דִּטְבִילוֹת הָוְיָין כְּיָמִים שֶׁבֵּינְתַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This is possible in a case **where he** unwittingly **engaged in intercourse with her** while she was menstruating, **and** then **she immersed** in a ritual bath and became pure, **and** then **she saw** menstrual blood **and he engaged in intercourse with her again, and** then **she immersed** and then saw menstrual blood **and he engaged in intercourse with her again. As** here **the immersions** which purify her **are** considered **like intervening days.**

Rava, though himself not aligned with Rav Chisda's reading, supplies the answer. He constructs the following scenario: a man forgot that his wife was a *niddah* (just as one might forget it was Shabbat) and had relations with her. Then she went to the *mikvah* and became pure. Then she became a *niddah* again. Then he had relations with her again — still forgetting, or perhaps forgetting again. Then she went to the *mikvah* again. Then she became a *niddah* again, and he had relations with her again. In this sequence, the *tevilos* — the ritual immersions — function as the structural equivalent of the days between one Shabbat and the next. Rabbi Eliezer maintained that these immersions constitute *yedi'ah lechalek*: just as the days between *Shabbosos* should have prompted awareness, the *tevilos* of the woman — her returning to purity — should have prompted awareness that her subsequent *niddah* status was a new and distinct prohibition. Each cycle of *niddah-tevilah-niddah* is treated as a separate episode, analogous to one *Shabbos* and the next. And Rabbi Akiva, according to Rav Chisda's reading, ultimately accepted this reasoning from Rabbi Eliezer.

This is a substantive parallel, not merely a formal one. The structural logic of *yedi'ah lechalek* — constructive awareness imposed by an intervening event — operates in both domains. The *tevilah* is to *niddah* what the intervening days are to *Shabbos*.

### Baraita Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּלָא כְּהָדֵין תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר לֹא כָּךְ שְׁאֵלוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אֶלָּא כָּךְ שְׁאֵלוֹ: הַבָּא עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ נִדָּה בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד, מַהוּ? חַיָּיב אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן, אוֹ חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara notes that **the mishna,** which states that Rabbi Akiva raised a dilemma with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat and Rabbi Eliezer offered a resolution based on the *halakhot* of a menstruating woman, **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **this** following ***tanna*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: That is not what Rabbi Akiva asked Rabbi Eliezer. Rather, this is what he asked him:** In the case of **one who** unwittingly **engaged in intercourse with his wife** while she was **menstruating, and then again engaged in intercourse with his wife** while she was **menstruating, during a single lapse of awareness, what is** the *halakha*? Is he **liable** to bring only **one** sin offering **for all of the** acts of intercourse, **or** is he **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one?**

The *Gemara* notes that our *Mishnah* does not conform to a certain *tanna*'s position, as expressed in a *baraita*. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar there states that Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer did not actually debate the *Shabbos* case by using *niddah* as the starting point; rather, the framing was the reverse. The established datum was *Shabbos*, and Rabbi Eliezer sought to apply it to *niddah*. Specifically, Rabbi Eliezer raised the question: a man has relations with his wife who is a *niddah*, separates, and then — while still in a single state of forgetfulness — has relations with her again. Presumably, everyone would have assumed this was a single *chata'os*. Rabbi Eliezer contended otherwise: even this is multiple *chata'os*, one for each act. He derived this from a *kal va-chomer* from *Shabbos*: Shabbat, which has only a single prohibition addressed to a single person, nonetheless generates multiple *chata'os* for multiple acts (in Rabbi Eliezer's own view involving multiple *toldos*). *Niddah*, which carries two prohibitions — he is forbidden to her and she to him — should certainly yield multiple *chata'os* for multiple acts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֹיה: חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה שַׁבָּת שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אֶלָּא אַזְהָרָה אַחַת, שֶׁהוּא מוּזְהָר עַל הַשַּׁבָּת וְהַשַּׁבָּת אֵינָהּ מוּזְהֶרֶת עָלָיו – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, נִדָּה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ שְׁתֵּי אַזְהָרוֹת, שֶׁהוּא מוּזְהָר עַל הַנִּדָּה וְנִדָּה מוּזְהֶרֶת עָלָיו – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to him:** He is **liable for each and every one,** and this is derived **from an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as** in the case of **Shabbat, with regard to which there is only one prohibition because he is prohibited** to perform labor **on Shabbat but Shabbat is not prohibited with regard to him,** he is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every** prohibited labor; in the case of **a menstruating woman, with regard to whom there are two prohibitions, as** the man **is prohibited** to engage in intercourse **with the menstruating woman and the menstruating woman is prohibited** to engage in intercourse **with him, is it not right that he** should **be liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every** act of intercourse?

Rabbi Akiva responded that *Shabbat* has many *toldos* and subcategories, which is why multiple acts yield multiple *chata'os*. *Niddah* has no such subcategories; there is only one act, and no internal differentiation can generate multiple obligations. Rabbi Eliezer then shifted to *ketanos* — multiple young women — since there the different bodies are clearly *gufin mechulakim*. Rabbi Akiva accepted that reasoning but pointed out that different bodies are different people; a man with his wife multiple times involves only one body. Rabbi Eliezer then shifted to *behemah*: a man who has relations with one *behemah* and then the same *behemah* multiple times — Rabbi Eliezer claimed this generates multiple *chata'os*, even though it is the same *behemah*, on the basis of his tradition. Rabbi Akiva replied: *behemah ke-niddah* — a *behemah* is like a *niddah*, I do not accept your tradition, and just as I say one *chata'os* for a *niddah*, so too for a *behemah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בַּשַּׁבָּת, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ תּוֹצָאוֹת הַרְבֵּה לְחַטָּאוֹת הַרְבֵּה, תֹּאמַר בַּנִּדָּה, שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ תּוֹצָאוֹת הַרְבֵּה לְחַטָּאוֹת הַרְבֵּה!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: No, if you said** that one is liable to bring multiple sin offerings **in** the case of **Shabbat, with regard to which there are multiple** categories of labor that **result in** liability to bring **multiple sin offerings, shall you** also **say** the same **in** the case of **a menstruating woman, with regard to whom there are not multiple** actions that **result** in **multiple** transgressions and **multiple sin offerings,** as there is only the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with her?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הַבָּא עַל הַקְּטַנּוֹת יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ תּוֹצָאוֹת הַרְבֵּה לְחַטָּאוֹת הַרְבֵּה, וְחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to him:** The case of **one who engages in intercourse with** five **minor** menstruating **girls will prove** it, **as with regard to it there are not multiple** actions that **result in multiple sin offerings, and** yet one is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for** having engaged in intercourse with **each and every one** of the girls.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בַּקְּטַנּוֹת שֶׁכֵּן גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֵין גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין וְחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הַבְּהֵמָה כַּנִּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: No, if you said** that one is liable for each one **with regard to minors, who** are **separate entities,** shall you also say that he should be liable for each act with regard to one who engages in intercourse five times with his wife while she is menstruating? Rabbi Eliezer **said to him:** The case of **one who copulates with an animal** multiple times during a single lapse of awareness **will prove** that one should be held separately liable for each act, **as there are no separate entities, and** yet one is **liable for each and every** act. Rabbi Akiva **said to him:** The case of **the animal is like** the case of **the menstruating woman,** and one is liable to bring only one sin offering.

According to this *baraita*, then, the debate is far more radical than the *Mishnah* suggests. Rabbi Eliezer is claiming that the same prohibited act performed multiple times with the same person — or even the same animal — yields a separate *chata'os* for each repetition. This is a genuinely startling position. Tosafot notes the internal difficulty: if Rabbi Eliezer already had a tradition that *behemah* generates multiple *chata'os* for repeated acts, why did he need to bring a proof from *behemah* to *niddah*? The answer, Tosafot suggests, is that Rabbi Eliezer was proceeding on the basis of tradition throughout. He had a tradition about *behemah* but not about *niddah*, and therefore he attempted to apply his *behemah* tradition to the *niddah* case through analogy. Rabbi Akiva, for his part, simply rejected the *behemah* tradition and maintained that a single *behemah* is *guf echad* — and so is a single *niddah*.

### Minors & Animals

The easier and more straightforward reading of the *Mishnah* — endorsed by Abaye and Rava — is that the entire debate is about *gufin mechulakim*. The comparisons to *niddah*, to minors, and to *behemah* are all asking the same question: are these different entities, such that repeated acts with each one constitute distinct transgressions? Rabbi Akiva's responses — that a *niddah* is a subject who participates, not a passive object; that *ketanos* have separate bodies; that a *behemah*, in the context of *arayos*, is not merely a passive implement — all address the *gufin mechulakim* question directly. The *Mishnah*'s structure coheres perfectly under this reading.

Rav Chisda's reading, by contrast, requires the *Mishnah*'s comparisons to bear on *yedi'ah lechalek*, which creates difficulties — most acutely with the *behemah* case, where the *Gemara* does not even attempt to explain how *yedi'ah lechalek* is relevant to relations with an animal.

### Rambam's Psak

How does the *Rambam* rule on these questions? His ruling is notable and aligns with Rav Chisda's reading, which in practice coincides with Rabbi Eliezer's maximally multiplying position. For *Shabbos*, the *Rambam* holds that multiple *Shabbosos* are *gufin mechulakim*, so the same *melacha* performed on two different *Shabbosos* generates two *chata'os*. Similarly for *arayos*: different women, or even different animals in the context of *arayot*, are *gufin mechulakim* and each generates a separate *chata'os*.

The *Rambam* draws a sharp distinction, however, for other prohibitions — such as *nosar* (eating from sacrificial meat left beyond its permitted time) or *shechutei chutz* (slaughtering offerings outside the Temple). There, different animals are considered *guf echad*. Performing the same prohibited act on multiple animals in a single state of forgetfulness yields only one *chata'os*. The underlying logic, not articulated explicitly but inferrable from the *Mishnah*'s reasoning, seems to be this: in *arayos*, even when one party is an animal, the transgression is defined by the relational act between two parties. The animal, however passive, participates in defining the act — it is not merely an inert object. This gives *arayos* a structural parallel to *gufin mechulakim* even where one of the parties is not human. By contrast, in cases of eating or *shechitah*, the prohibited object is purely passive, and one's repeated engagement with different passive objects of the same category is simply the same prohibited act repeated — a single *aveirah* multiplied, not multiple distinct *aveiros*.

Thus the *Rambam* rules: for *Shabbos* and *arayos*, *gufin mechulakim*; for everything else, *guf echad*. Multiple *toldos* of the same *av*, by contrast, is a dispute where the *halacha* follows the majority against Rabbi Eliezer — a single *chata'os* for multiple *toldos* of one *av melacha*.

## Asham Talui Intro

### Chelev Uncertainty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סָפֵק אָכַל חֵלֶב, סָפֵק לֹא אָכַל, וַאֲפִילּוּ אָכַל, סָפֵק יֵשׁ בּוֹ כַּשִּׁיעוּר וְסָפֵק אֵין בּוֹ, שׁוּמָּן וָחֵלֶב לְפָנָיו, וְאָכַל אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן אָכַל, אִשְׁתּוֹ וַאֲחוֹתוֹ עִמּוֹ בַּבַּיִת, שָׁגַג בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן שָׁגַג, שַׁבָּת וְיוֹם חוֹל, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכָה בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן עָשָׂה – מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If there is **uncertainty** whether **one ate** forbidden **fat** and **uncertainty** whether **one did not eat** forbidden **fat, or even** if **one ate** forbidden fat and there is **uncertainty** whether **there is the measure** that determines liability **in** the piece he ate **and uncertainty** whether **there is not** the measure that determines liability **in** the piece he ate, if one has a piece of permitted **fat and** a piece of forbidden **fat before him and he ate one of them and he does not know which of them he ate;** or if **his wife and his sister** were **with him in the house** and **he unwittingly** engaged in intercourse **with one of them and he does not know with which of them he unwittingly** engaged in intercourse; or if he confused **Shabbat and a weekday and he performed labor** prohibited on Shabbat **on one of** the days **and he does not know on which of them he performed** the labor, in all of those cases he is liable to **bring a provisional guilt offering.**

With the conclusion of the *Gemara*'s extended treatment of *gufin mechulakim*, a new *perek* begins — one devoted entirely to *asham talui*, the suspended guilt-offering brought when a person is uncertain whether they have committed a sin that would normally require a *chata'os*. The *Mishnah* opens with a series of cases. A person does not know whether they ate *chelev* (forbidden fat) or not. Or: they know they ate something, but are uncertain whether it contained a *kezayis* — the minimum measure that triggers liability for a *chata'os*. Or: there were two pieces before the person, one of *shuman* (permitted fat) and one of *chelev*, and the person ate one without knowing which.

In all these scenarios, if the person had known with certainty that they had eaten *chelev* in sufficient measure, they would have been obligated in a *chata'os*. But because the matter remains uncertain, they bring an *asham talui* instead.

### Thought Permissible

Both Rashi and Tosafot emphasize a critical precondition for *asham talui*: the person must have believed, at the time of the act, that what they were doing was entirely permitted. *Asham talui*, like *chata'os*, is predicated on a *shegaga* — an unwitting transgression committed under the genuine belief that one was acting permissibly. If a person knew at the time that they were eating something that might be *chelev*, and proceeded anyway — treating the doubt as insufficient reason to abstain — that is an entirely different category. Such a person has not acted unwittingly; they knowingly took the risk of committing a prohibited act. They would not bring a *chatas* had they certainly sinned in this fashion (since *chata'os* requires genuine *shegaga*), and correspondingly they do not bring an *asham talui* for the uncertainty. They must seek atonement through *teshuvah*.

### Extended Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאִם אָכַל חֵלֶב וָחֵלֶב בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא חַטָּאת אַחַת, כֵּן עַל לֹא הוֹדַע שֶׁלָּהֶן אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶלָּא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as** in a case **where one** unknowingly **ate** a piece of forbidden **fat and** then another piece of forbidden **fat in a single lapse of awareness** he is **liable** to bring **only one sin offering, so too, in** a case where their status is **unknown** to him and he ate them both unwittingly during a single lapse in awareness, he is **liable** to bring **only one provisional guilt offering.**

The *Mishnah* extends the principle to cases involving *arayos* and Shabbat. A man whose sister and wife were both in his house, and who entered a room under conditions of uncertainty and does not know with whom he had relations — this creates a doubt about whether he committed a capital *aveirah*. Similarly, a person who performed *melacha* without knowing whether it was Shabbat or a weekday — perhaps it was *bein ha-shmashos*, the transitional period — and does not know on which day the act occurred. In all such cases of doubt, where the underlying act would have generated a *chata'os* had it certainly been a sin, an *asham talui* is brought.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיְתָה יְדִיעָה בֵּינָתַיִם, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהוּא מֵבִיא חַטָּאת עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, כָּךְ מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת.
>
> **Translation:** But **if** he **had** gained **knowledge between** the first and second instance of eating that there is a possibility the fat might be prohibited, then the *halakha* is different: **Just as he** would be liable to **bring a sin offering for each and every** piece when he gained knowledge of their prohibited status in between each act of consumption, **so too,** he must **bring a provisional guilt offering for each and every** instance in which he consumed food that might be forbidden after learning of their uncertain status in between each unwitting act of consumption.

### Parallel Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאָכַל חֵלֶב וָדָם וּפִיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר בְּהֶעְלֵם אַחַת חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, כָּךְ עַל לֹא הוֹדַע שֶׁלָּהֶן מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as** in a case **where one ate** forbidden **fat, and blood, and *piggul*, and *notar* in one lapse of awareness he is liable** to bring a sin offering **for each and every one, so too, with regard** to a case where **their** status is **unknown** to him and he ate them unwittingly during one lapse of awareness, he must **bring a provisional guilt offering for each and every** item.

The *Mishnah* articulates a general principle: the rules governing *asham talui* parallel those governing *chata'os*. However many *chata'os* a person would be obligated to bring had the sin been certain, that many *ashmot teluyot* they bring in the case of doubt. If two forbidden acts were performed in a single state of forgetfulness — say, *chelev* and blood and *pigul* and *nosar* all consumed in one episode — a person would bring a *chata'os* for each distinct prohibition. Correspondingly, if one is in doubt about all of them, one brings a separate *asham talui* for each. If there was an intervening *yedi'ah* between two acts of the same type, such that the definitely-sinning person would have brought two *chata'os*, then the doubting person brings two *ashmot teluyot*. The entire structure of liability for *asham talui* is derived directly from the structure of liability for *chata'os*.

## One vs. Two Pieces

### Pasuk Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר, רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: חֲתִיכָה אַחַת שָׁנִינוּ, סָפֵק שֶׁל חֵלֶב סָפֵק שֶׁל שׁוּמָּן. חִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: חֲתִיכָה מִשְׁתֵּי חֲתִיכוֹת שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's mention of one who ate a piece of fat whose status was uncertain, it **was stated** that **Rav Asi says: We learned** that one is liable to bring a provisional guilt offering in the case of **one** single **piece** that is **uncertain** if it is **of** forbidden **fat** and **uncertain** if it is **of** permitted **fat. Ḥiyya bar Rav** disagreed and **said: We learned** this *halakha* in the case of an uncertainty involving one **piece out of two pieces,** one of which is forbidden fat and the other permitted fat, but in the case of a single piece, one who consumes it is not liable to bring an offering.

The *Gemara* immediately focuses on the central interpretive question of this *perek*: what level of concreteness in the doubt is required to trigger the obligation of *asham talui*? The *Mishnah*'s first case was generic — a person simply does not know if they ate *chelev*. The third case was specific — two pieces were present, one definitively *chelev* and one definitively permitted, and the person ate one without knowing which. Does the obligation of *asham talui* apply only in the more concrete scenario, or even in the abstract case of simple uncertainty?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? רַב אַסִּי סָבַר: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמָּסוֹרֶת, ״מִצְוַת״ כְּתִיב. וְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמִּקְרָא, ״מִצְווֹת״ קָרֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **With regard to what** matter do Rav Asi and Ḥiyya bar Rav **disagree?** The Gemara answers: It is stated in a verse discussing the obligation to bring a provisional guilt offering: "And if anyone sin, and do any of the commandments [*mitzvot*] of the Lord that are not to be done, though does not know it; yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 5:17). **Rav Asi holds:** The **tradition** of the manner in which the verses in the Torah are written **is authoritative,** and one derives *halakhot* based on the spelling of the words. Therefore, the obligation to bring a provisional guilt offering applies only when there is uncertainty with regard to a single piece, because the word **is written** as "***mitzvat*,"** in the singular form. **And Ḥiyya bar Rav says:** The **vocalization** of the Torah **is authoritative,** meaning that one derives *halakhot* based on the pronunciation of the words, even if it diverges from the spelling. Therefore, since **we read** the word in the plural, as ***mitzvot*,** the obligation to bring a provisional guilt offering applies only when two pieces are present.

Rav Assi and Chiya bar Rav dispute this question, and the *Gemara* frames their dispute as turning on *yesh em la-masores* vs. *yesh em la-mikra* — whether one follows the written consonantal text or the traditional vocalization/reading in interpreting a *pasuk*. The relevant verse, in the context of the *asham talui*, includes the phrase *achas mi-kol mitzvos* — "one of all the *mitzvos*" (Leviticus 4:2 and related passages). The word *mitzvos* can be read as *mitzvot* (plural) if one follows the *keri* (the traditional reading), or as *mitzvas* (construct singular) if one follows the *kesiv* (the written form).

Rav Assi holds *yesh em la-masores*: we follow the *kesiv*, and the word is *mitzvas* — singular. Therefore, a single piece of uncertain status is sufficient to generate an *asham talui* obligation. Chiya bar Rav holds *yesh em la-mikra*: we follow the *keri*, and the word is *mitzvot* — plural. The verse is thus requiring that two things be involved, meaning there must have been two pieces present — one definitively prohibited and one definitively permitted — for the obligation to arise.

### Rav's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו שְׁתֵּי חֲתִיכוֹת, אַחַת שֶׁל שׁוּמָּן וְאַחַת שֶׁל חֵלֶב, אָכַל אַחַת מֵהֶן וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזוֹ מֵהֶן אָכַל – חַיָּיב חֲתִיכָה אַחַת, סָפֵק שֶׁל חֵלֶב סָפֵק שֶׁל שׁוּמָּן וַאֲכָלָהּ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a series of virtually identical statements attributed to Rav, followed by different rationales for his opinion, after each of which it raises several objections from a *baraita* or a mishna. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** In a case where **one had two pieces** of fat **before him, one of** permitted **fat and one of** forbidden **fat,** if **he ate one of them and he does not know which of them he ate,** he is **obligated** to bring a provisional guilt offering. But if there was only **one piece** before him and there was **uncertainty** whether it was **of** forbidden **fat** and **uncertainty** whether it was **of** permitted **fat, and he ate it,** he is **exempt.**

Rav Yehuda transmits a ruling in Rav's name that gives concrete form to this dispute. Two pieces before a person, one of *shuman* and one of *chelev*, and the person ate one without knowing which — this person is *chayav* an *asham talui*. But one piece alone, whose status is uncertain — *safek shel chelev safek shel shuman* — and the person ate it without knowing what it was: *patur*. No *asham talui* is brought. This ruling follows the *mitzvot* reading: two pieces are required, because the verse implies that the transgression occurs within a field of two distinct options, one of which was definitively prohibited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָשָׂה אַחַת מִכׇּל מִצְוֹת ה׳ בִּשְׁגָגָה״ – עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁגּוֹג בִּשְׁתַּיִם, ״מִצְוַת״ כְּתִיב, ״מִצְווֹת״ קָרֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rav?** He derives it from **that** which **the verse states:** "And if anyone sin, **and do any of the commandments [*mitzvot*] of the Lord** that are not to be done, **though he does not know it;** yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity" (Leviticus 4:22). Rav derives from the plural *mitzvot* that one is not liable to bring a provisional guilt offering **until he is unwitting with regard to** one of **two** items, which in this case is two pieces of fat. The Gemara asks: But isn't it **written "*mitzvat*,"** in the singular form? The Gemara answers: **We read** it as ***mitzvot*,** in the plural. In other words, according to Rava, Rav maintains that the vocalization of the Torah is authoritative.

Rava explains Rav's reasoning through the verse: *asher lo se'aseinah* — "which he did in error" — referring to *mitzvot* in the plural. One must have transgressed against one of two things, meaning there must have been two items present, one definitely forbidden, creating a concrete fifty-fifty possibility of sin.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כּוֹי – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו אָשָׁם תָּלוּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rava from a *baraita*. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to **a *koy*,** a kosher animal with characteristics of both a domesticated animal and a non-domesticated animal, **one is obligated to** bring **a provisional guilt offering for** eating **its** fat. The prohibition of forbidden fats applies only to those of a domesticated animal; the corresponding fats are permitted in the case of a non-domesticated animal. Since a *koy* is of uncertain status, one must bring a provisional guilt offering for eating its fat. Evidently, one is liable to bring a provisional guilt offering even in a case where there was uncertainty involving a single item.

Abaye challenges Rav's ruling with a series of cases that appear to generate *asham talui* obligations without meeting the criterion of two pieces, one of which was definitively prohibited.

### Koy Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: יֵשׁ אֵם לַמָּסוֹרֶת, ״מִצְוַת״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Abaye: **Rabbi Eliezer holds** that the **tradition** of the manner in which the verses in the Torah are written **is authoritative.** Consequently, as the singular form **"*mitzvat*" is written,** he maintains that a provisional guilt offering must be brought in a case of uncertainty involving a single item, such as the fat of a *koy*.

The first challenge involves the *koy* — that taxonomically ambiguous creature, possibly a crossbreed, whose classification as *chayah* or *behemah* is unresolved. The fat of a *chayah* is permitted; the fat of a *behemah* is *chelev* and forbidden. If a person ate the fat of a *koy*, Rabbi Eliezer holds they are *chayav* an *asham talui*. Yet there are not two pieces present — there is one piece of *koy* fat, and its very identity is the source of the uncertainty. The *Gemara* deflects this challenge by noting that Rabbi Eliezer is the authority behind this ruling, and Rabbi Eliezer holds *yesh em la-masores* — the *kesiv*, which reads *mitzvas* singular, allowing *asham talui* from a single ambiguous piece. The challenge does not apply to Rav, who follows the *mitzvot* reading.

### Tefach Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: סָפֵק בֶּן תִּשְׁעָה לָרִאשׁוֹן אוֹ בֶּן שִׁבְעָה לָאַחֲרוֹן – יוֹצִיא, וְהַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר, וְחַיָּיב בְּאָשָׁם תָּלוּי. הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raised an objection to** Rav from a *baraita*: If one consummates levirate marriage with his *yevama* and seven months later she gives birth, there is **uncertainty whether** the child **is nine** months **old,** i.e., counting from conception, and is the offspring **of the first** husband, which would mean the levirate bond did not take effect, or **whether** the child is only **seven** months **old** and is the offspring **of the latter** husband, i.e., the *yavam*, not the deceased, in which case the levirate bond did take effect. In such a situation, due to the possibility that she is forbidden to him as his brother's wife, **he must divorce her. But the offspring is** of **unflawed** lineage, as whether it was born from the first or the second husband, its conception involved no transgression. **And to atone for the possibility that they engaged in forbidden intercourse they are each** **obligated** to bring **a provisional guilt offering.** This indicates that one is obligated to bring a provisional guilt offering even when the uncertainty involves a single item. The Gemara answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who, as stated above, maintains that one is obligated to bring a provisional guilt offering for uncertainty involving a single item.

A second case is raised involving *tefach bekeil tefach* — a case where one half-*shiur* was combined with another in certain contexts. The *Gemara* similarly deflects this as consistent with Rabbi Eliezer's more expansive position, not a challenge to Rav.

### Paternity Case

The third challenge: the case of a woman whose husband died, who performed *yibum* with the deceased brother's kin, and who then gave birth after seven months, creating uncertainty about which man fathered the child. As noted above: if the child was from the first husband, then she was not a *yevamah* and the *yibum* constituted an *issur arayos*; if the child was from the second husband (the *yavam*), then the *yibum* was valid. The court rules that she must be divorced (due to the doubt of *issur arayos*) and that the child is *kasher* (because *mamzer vadai* is what the Torah prohibits, not *mamzer safek*). And she and he are obligated in an *asham talui*. Yet in this case, there is no second piece that was definitively *assur* — no other woman who was definitively his *erva* was standing there. The doubt arises entirely from the uncertain paternity of the child. Once again the *Gemara* attributes this ruling to Rabbi Eliezer and his more permissive standard for *asham talui*, not imposing the two-pieces criterion.

### Blood Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלָּהּ לְאַחַר זְמַן – טְמֵאִין מִסָּפֵק וּפְטוּרִין מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן, וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: חַיָּיב מִשּׁוּם אָשָׁם תָּלוּי! הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raised** another **objection to** Rav from a mishna (*Nidda* 14a): If blood **was found on her** cloth **after time** passed, they are both **impure due to uncertainty, and they are exempt from** bringing **the** sin **offering. And it is taught with regard to** this last case in the mishna: They are each nevertheless **liable to** bring **a provisional guilt offering.** The Gemara again answers: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer.**

A closely related case involves a husband and wife who have relations, after which blood is found. If blood is found on him (*al shelo*), or on something he wiped with, she is definitively a *niddah* at the time of relations — blood got onto him, and this means she was already bleeding during the act. Both are *tamei* and obligated in a *chata'os*. If blood is found on her (*al shelah*) immediately after they separate — *os yom*, within moments — the same ruling applies; the presumption is that she was bleeding during the act. But if blood is found on her *le-achar zman*, some time later, then it is uncertain whether she was bleeding during the act. In that case they are *tamei mi-safek* and exempt from a *korban* — no *chata'os*, because there is no certainty of sin. A *baraita*, however, says they are *chayav* an *asham talui*. This case also lacks two distinct pieces, one definitively prohibited — the woman is a *niddah* now, certainly, but whether she was a *niddah* at the moment of relations is what is in doubt. The *Gemara* again attributes this case to Rabbi Eliezer.

### Concrete vs. Definite

The resolution of Abaye's various challenges consolidates into a conceptual distinction. Rav's ruling — two pieces required, one definitely forbidden — demands that there be a *definite* prohibited object present at the time of the act: not just a possible sin, but a certain *issur* that the person may have encountered. All the cases raised by Abaye — the *koy*, the uncertain paternity, the blood evidence — share a common feature: there is no definitively forbidden object present at the time of the act. The uncertainty is intrinsic to the situation, not a function of which of two clearly identifiable options the person encountered.

Rabbi Eliezer's position, by contrast, requires only a *concrete reason* for the doubt — a specific factual basis to think the person may have sinned, as opposed to a vague or entirely abstract worry. All of Abaye's cases have such a concrete basis: the ambiguous taxonomy of the *koy*, the uncertain timing of conception, the uncertain timing of bleeding. These are not idle or speculative worries; they are real factual uncertainties. But for Rav, concreteness of doubt is not sufficient — what is required is that there was, at the moment of the act, a definitively prohibited possibility confronting the person. That standard is met by two pieces, one certainly *chelev*, not by a single piece of ambiguous identity.
