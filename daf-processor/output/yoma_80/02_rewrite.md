# Yoma 80 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kotevet & Shiurim

### Tanya: Shiurim Kezayis

The daf opens with a *baraita* cited in the standard *tanya* formula. The *baraita* records the view of Rebbi: *kol hashiurim bekuleitan kkezayis* — the vast majority of *shiurim* in *halacha* are measured by a *kezayis*. The single exception is *tumat ochalin*, the impurity of foods, regarding which the *baraita* states *sheshina kasuv bemasma'an veshinu chachamim bishiuran* — the Torah employs a slightly different term when speaking of foods becoming *tamei*, and therefore the Sages defined a different minimum amount, not a *kezayis*.

### Yom Kippur Precedent

The *baraita* then invokes *Yom Kippur* as a proof and paradigm for this principle: *vera'aya ledavar Yom Hakippurim*. The Torah does not say "do not eat" on *Yom Kippur*; it says *milo te'uneh* — any soul that does not afflict itself. Because the Torah frames the prohibition in terms of affliction rather than eating, it is conceptually distinct, and consequently the *shiur* is not a *kezayis* but a *kotevet* — the large dried date. The Gemara then asks: what is the purpose of citing *Yom Kippur* as a proof for the principle? Why not simply say that by *tumat ochalin* the Torah also uses a different phrase, and therefore a different size follows, without needing the analogy from *Yom Kippur*?

The answer is illuminating. *De'ee mei'hasam* — had you only the case of *tumat ochalin*, one might have dismissed the variation in language as stylistic rather than substantive: *hava amina dichrahu*, it's just the way the verse speaks, nothing more. *Yom Kippur* provides an incontrovertible demonstration that when the Torah frames a prohibition differently — affliction instead of eating — it genuinely signals a different conceptual reality, and that in turn produces a different *shiur*. Once this is established as an unambiguous precedent, it is all the more compelling to apply the same logic to subtler shifts in language elsewhere.

### Tumat Ochalin

The Gemara now examines the derivation for *tumat ochalin* specifically. The verse states: *mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel asher yavo elav mayim yitma* — from all the food that shall be eaten, that which water comes upon shall become *tamei*. Rabbi Avahu citing Rabbi Elazar derives: *ochel haba meichamas ochel* — the verse teaches us about food that comes from food. The phrase *asher ye'achel*, food that shall be eaten, signals food that emerges from something that is itself eaten. What fits this description? *Zeh beitzas tarnigoles* — the egg of a chicken. The chicken is food; the egg comes from it; the egg is therefore food from food, and the *shiur* for *tumat ochalin* is *kibeitzah*, an egg-sized amount.

One might immediately ask: there are other foods that come from food — why specifically an egg? The Gemara raises a goat kid: it comes from a goat, which is food. The response is *mechusar shechita* — the kid still requires *shechita* to become permitted for consumption; it is not food the moment it emerges. An egg, by contrast, is ready to eat immediately. The Gemara then raises the *ben pakua* — a fetus found in a slaughtered animal, which halakhically does not require its own *shechita* since the mother's *shechita* takes effect upon it. Even so, the *ben pakua* requires *keria*, being torn open to release its blood; it is not immediately ready as food. Thus the egg stands as the clearest case of food that comes directly and immediately from food.

### Egg Size Debates

Having established that the reference is to an egg, the Gemara asks: *ve'eima beitzat bar yokhni* — perhaps it refers to an ostrich egg, which is enormous? The Gemara's response is the principle *tafasta meruba lo tafasta*: if you try to grasp too much, you grasp nothing. You cannot assume the most extreme case; if both a large and a small interpretation are available, the expansive one cannot simply be asserted. One must settle for the more moderate measure.

Yet this invites a counter-question: *ve'eima beitzat tziporta* — perhaps the verse means the egg of a small bird, something like a sparrow, *dizuta tuvei*, which is very small? This produces a genuine difficulty. The principle of *tafasta meruba lo tafasta* pushes against the largest option, but does not automatically establish the smallest either. The Gemara is effectively left with a question on this derivation — why specifically a hen's egg and not a smaller bird's egg? The derivation of Rabbi Avahu quoting Rabbi Elazar, while establishing the logic of *ochel haba meichamas ochel*, has an unresolved vulnerability at the level of specifying the exact size.

### Abbahu: Beit Hablia

Rabbi Avahu therefore offers his own independent derivation. From the same phrase, *mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel*, he reads: *ochel she'atah ochlo be'vat achat* — food that you eat in one gulp, food that can be swallowed whole in a single act of swallowing. And the rabbis estimated: *ein beis hablia machzik yoter mi'beitzas tarnigoles* — the esophagus, the gullet, cannot hold more than a hen's egg. This derivation has the advantage of anchoring the *kibeitzah* measure directly to the size of a hen's egg without relying on the chain of reasoning about *ochel haba meichamas ochel* and the difficulties it generated. It also reflects a physiological observation about the capacity of the human *beis hablia*, though the lecture notes that ancient hen's eggs were likely smaller than their modern counterparts and that, in any case, the Gemara never explicitly resolves whether we always operate by reference to a typical, average specimen.

The broader framework is now clear: we have established, first from *Yom Kippur* and then from *tumat ochalin*, that a change in the Torah's terminology signals a change in *shiur*, and that precedent carries over to other contexts where the language similarly departs from the standard formulation.

## Record-Keeping & Change

### Cheilev Recording

The Gemara transitions to a new but closely related topic. Rav Elazar states: *ha'ocheil cheilev bazman hazeh* — one who eats *cheilev*, forbidden fat, in the present day, *tzarich sheyichtov lo shiur*, must write down the precise amount consumed. The reason for focusing on *cheilev* is that among the common prohibitions, eating forbidden fat is one of the *issurei kares* for which an unintentional violation (*beshogeg*) obligates a *korban chatat*. By contrast, many other non-*kosher* foods — such as *chazir* or improperly slaughtered meat — carry only a *lav*, a negative prohibition, without *kares*, and therefore do not generate a *korban* obligation. Similarly, *basar bechalav* is only a *lav*. Thus *cheilev* and blood are the paradigmatic everyday prohibitions whose violation generates *korban* liability, along with *chametz bePesach*.

### Future Courts Shift

The requirement to record the amount, continues the Gemara, is *shema yavo beis din acher veyarbe bishiurin* — lest a later *beis din* come and modify the minimum *shiurim*. The intent is practical: when the *Beis Hamikdash* is rebuilt, one will need to know exactly how much *cheilev* was consumed in order to determine whether the appropriate *korban* must be brought. One should not merely record that a *korban* may be owed, but should note the actual quantity — even down to the specific volume — because whether that quantity crosses the threshold for obligation may depend on how a future *beis din* defines the minimum.

There is something deeply striking about this passage. The Gemara takes entirely for granted the practice of maintaining a personal record of *korbanot* one may be liable for — a kind of spiritual ledger. This sits in productive tension with the broader themes of our *masechet*, where *korban* as a vehicle for atonement coexists with *teshuva* as the primary response to sin. And as one participant in the shiur observed, the very act of recording the precise quantity in the notebook simultaneously anticipates the future obligation to bring a *korban* and is itself embedded in a process of *teshuva* and self-accounting — the two realities meeting on the page of the *pinkas*.

### Grandfathering Issue

The Gemara then examines what it means for a future *beis din* to "increase the *shiurim*." One reading is that the *beis din* would make the minimum *smaller* — that is, would require a *korban* even for an amount previously considered sub-threshold, a smaller *kezayis*. The Gemara challenges this immediately from a *baraita*: *asher lo te'asina bishgagah* — the verse on which the *korban chatat* obligation rests describes a sin that the person would have refrained from had they known. *Hashav miyediato meivi korban al shigagato* — one who would have held back brings a *korban* for the inadvertent violation. *Lo shav miyediato* — one who would not have refrained, *ein meivi korban*. If at the time of eating the amount was sub-threshold under the then-prevailing *shiur*, the act was not halakhically defined as an act of *achila* at all. The person is therefore grandfathered in: since the act did not constitute a *chiyuv* at the time it was performed, a later court's retroactive reclassification cannot impose liability. No retroactive shrinkage of the *shiur* can obligate someone for what was, at the time of the act, a permissible or at most sub-threshold quantity.

This point, as noted in the discussion, connects to the foundational question raised at the beginning of *Masechet Horayot*: when *beis din* reinterprets a biblical criterion, is it uncovering an objective truth that was always there, or is it establishing an interpretive truth that takes effect from that point forward? The Gemara here seems to operate on the premise that halakhic obligation is defined by the standard in force at the time of the act — though the framing through *shav miyediato* and psychological culpability is somewhat unusual. As the lecturer suggests, the Gemara may be saying that even if objectively one was liable, the *halacha* of *korban* is anchored in the actor's state of mind and awareness at the moment of the transgression.

### Larger Shiur Relief

Having foreclosed the possibility of retroactive tightening, the Gemara accepts that *yarbe bishiurin* must mean the *beis din* will make the minimum *larger*, thereby relieving someone of an obligation they previously believed they had incurred. This is actually the more intuitive application: one thought a *korban* was owed because the amount eaten met the old threshold; the new *beis din* sets a higher minimum, and that amount now falls short of the new standard. It would be incoherent to bring a *korban* for an act that, according to the authoritative ruling of the *beis din*, does not reach the threshold. Hence the *korban* cannot be brought. This is why the precise amount must be recorded — because depending on how the *shiurim* develop, one may discover that no *korban* is in fact owed at all.

## Halacha LeMoshe

### Yochanan Statement

Moving from the practical question of record-keeping, the Gemara takes up a more fundamental issue. Rabbi Yochanan declares: *shiurin v'onshin halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — both the minimum amounts and the punishments for transgressions are *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, transmitted to Moses at Sinai and not derived through textual interpretation. The Gemara immediately presses: punishments are written explicitly in the verses — *kares*, *chayiv misa*, and so forth. What is the novelty in calling them *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*?

One might add: there are aspects of punishment that are not so clearly spelled out. The generalized category of *malkot*, for instance — the very assumption that most negative prohibitions entailing a *maaseh* carry *malkot* as their penalty — is not transparently derived from any single verse. Still, the Gemara resists a broad reading. It concludes: *ela haki ka'amar* — what Rabbi Yochanan means is more precisely *shiurin shel onshin halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — the minimum amounts that must be reached for punishments to apply are *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*. The *shiurim* themselves, not the existence of the punishments, are the content of the Sinaitic tradition. A *baraita* is then cited in support: *tanya nami hachi* — similarly it was taught, *shiurin shel onshin halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*.

It is worth pausing here. The claim that all *shiurim* are *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* is in one sense axiomatic in rabbinic literature — not just the *shiurim* for punishment but those for *mikveh*, *esrog*, *lulav*, and so on. And yet even within a *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* there can be uncertainty about the precise parameters. As the lecturer notes, even according to the Rambam, who insists there was never *machloket* about *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, this does not mean there cannot be interpretive questions about its exact scope and application.

### Othniel Tradition

Against this firmly Sinaitic framing, the Gemara introduces a startling dissent: *acherim omrim, Beis Dino shel Yavetz tiknum* — others say that the *beis din* of Yavetz established them. As Rashi identifies, "Yavetz" here refers to Othniel ben Kenaz, the first of the *shoftim*, whose name is connected to this tradition through a *midrashic* identification. The implication is radical: the *shiurim* were not transmitted from Sinai at all, but were established by this early post-Mosaic *beis din*. This would mean that the fixed minimum amounts are not ancient Sinaitic tradition but represent a human, judicial determination — significant, authoritative, but not part of the revelation at Sinai.

### Not New Creation

The Gemara cannot accept this implication as stated. It invokes the verse *elleh hamitzvot* — these are the commandments — from which it derives that no prophet may introduce *chiddush*, a genuinely new law, once the Torah has been given: *she'ein navi rasha'i lechadesh davar mei'attah*. If the *shiurim* were genuinely instituted by Othniel ben Kenaz as a new creation, that would constitute precisely such a *chiddush* by post-Mosaic human authority, which is impermissible. The Gemara therefore resolves: *ella she-shachachum vechazaru veyisadum* — the *shiurim* were always there as part of the Sinaitic tradition, but they had been forgotten in the upheaval of the conquest and settlement of the land, and Othniel ben Kenaz rediscovered and re-established them. He was not creating but recovering.

This resolution is, as the lecturer observes, quite fascinating in its own right — particularly in the context of earlier discussions on this *daf* about the *inuyim* of *Yom Kippur*. There too, the *Rishonim* debate whether the additional afflictions beyond eating and drinking are *d'oraita* or *d'rabbanan*, with some holding that the Torah effectively says *lemosrah hakatuv ela lechakhamim* — the Torah transferred the definition of its own terms to the Sages, granting them unusually broad authority to set the parameters. A parallel question arises by *Chol HaMoed*: the verses hint at the concept but leave the contours of prohibited *melacha* largely to rabbinic determination, which is why exceptions like *hefsed merubah* are possible. The Gemara's resistance to saying that Othniel *instituted* the *shiurim* — as opposed to recovering them — sits in interesting tension with the more expansive model of rabbinic authority invoked in those other contexts.

## Melo Lugmav

### One Cheek Full

The *sugya* turns to the *shiur* for drinking on *Yom Kippur*. The minimum amount of liquid whose consumption constitutes a violation is *melo lugmav* — the fill of one's cheeks. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel clarifies: *lo melo lugmav mamash* — not literally the fill of both cheeks, but *kol she'illu yesalkeno letzad echad veyeira'eh k'melo lugmav* — whatever amount, if one were to shift it entirely to one side, would appear to fill that one cheek. The *mishna* itself says simply *melo lugmav*, and the Gemara notes: read it as *k'melo lugmav* — like the fill of the cheeks, not to be taken with absolute literalness.

### Baraita Positions

A *baraita* is then introduced that sets out competing positions. How much must one drink to be liable on *Yom Kippur*? *Beit Shammai omrim revi'it* — Beit Shammai say a *revi'it*, approximately three to three and a half ounces. *Beit Hillel omrim melo lugmav* — Beit Hillel say the fill of one's cheeks. Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *k'melo lugmav* — like the fill of one's cheeks, the formulation we have been discussing. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: *kedei gmi'ah* — enough for one swallow.

The Gemara asks: is this *baraita* a stronger proof than the *mishna* for the interpretation we have been working with? No — we can read the *baraita*'s *melo lugmav* the same way we read the *mishna*'s, as meaning *k'melo lugmav*, moving the liquid to one side so it appears to fill the cheek. If so, what distinguishes *melo lugmav* from Rabbi Elazar's *k'melo lugmav*? The Gemara answers: *ika beinaihu melo lugmav dachuk* — the difference lies in whether the cheek is firmly packed and fully puffed, or merely noticeably protruding on one side. Both positions refer to one side; they disagree about the degree of fullness required.

### Hoshaia Challenge

Rav Hoshaia then raises a sharp challenge. If *melo lugmav* means one cheek, then Beit Hillel's position should be *stricter* than Beit Shammai's, not more lenient — because one cheek is *less* than a *revi'it*. The standard expectation is that Beit Shammai is more stringent and Beit Hillel more lenient. Yet here, if one cheek is smaller than a *revi'it*, Beit Hillel is setting a lower threshold for liability, making it *easier* to transgress. That is the reverse of the expected pattern.

The response is a memorable line: *pitschil be-Og Melech HaBashan pitschil* — it depends on whom you are talking about. The implicit assumption of *melo lugmav* is that the measure is *personal* — it is the fill of *your* cheeks. For a very large person, Og Melech HaBashan being the paradigm of extraordinary size, the fill of one's cheeks could easily exceed a *revi'it*. When Beit Hillel stated *melo lugmav* and Beit Shammai was understood to be the more stringent position, they were imagining a large individual — for whom the *revi'it* is a smaller amount than the fill of his cheeks. For such a person, Beit Shammai with their *revi'it* is indeed stricter, since his *melo lugmav* is larger.

This point has immediate practical significance. For the *halacha* of *shiurim* on *Yom Kippur* — particularly in cases where one must eat or drink in *shiurim* to avoid transgression — the question of how much constitutes *melo lugmav* matters enormously. The Gemara here implies that the average *melo lugmav* is less than a *revi'it*, since the scenario in which Beit Hillel is more lenient requires a larger-than-average person.

### Zeira Presses

Rav Zeira now presses a deeper conceptual question: *mah yishna achilah dechol chad vechad b'kotevet, u-mah yishna shtiyah dechol chad vechad be-dideih?* — why is eating on *Yom Kippur* governed by a universal standard, the *kotevet*, the same for everyone, while drinking has just been shown to vary by the individual, each person's *melo lugmav* being their own personal measure? This is a striking inconsistency. We are accustomed to *shiurim* being universal — the size of a *mikveh*, the *shiur* for an *esrog*, the size of a *kezayis* — measured against an external, objective standard, not calibrated to each individual's body.

### Abaye: Threshold

Abaye responds: *akimu leih rabanan de-be-kotevet be-hachi mitva dateih* — the rabbis established that a date-sized amount will produce *yishuv hada'at*, an abatement of the sense of affliction, for every person. *Batziar me-hachi lo mitva* — less than that will not achieve this, universally, regardless of the person's size. By contrast, for drinking: *bi-shtiyah be-dideih mitva dateih be-de-chavreih lo* — only the amount proportional to that individual's cheek size produces *yishuv hada'at* for that person; the fill of another person's cheeks would not do so for them. The rabbis *know* this, and they have calibrated the *shiurim* accordingly — universal for food, personal for drink.

### Og Example

Rav Zeira pushes further: very well, but take Og Melech HaBashan himself. You claim a *kotevet*-sized amount achieves *yishuv hada'at* universally — but for a man of Og's dimensions, can a small dried date meaningfully abate the affliction of fasting?

Abaye is unperturbed. *Kim lehu lerabanan de-vehachi mitva dateih, u-vatzir mei-hachi lo mitva dateih* — yes, the rabbis established that that amount achieves some abatement of affliction, and less than that achieves none. The key is a *threshold*: below the threshold, no one — not even a small person — experiences any meaningful relief. Above the threshold, the effect occurs for everyone, but to varying degrees. *Kulei alma tuva* — for most people that minimum amount produces substantial relief. *Og Melech HaBashan purta* — for Og it produces only a little. But even a little is above the threshold; below it, nothing. The structure is thus: the *shiur* marks the point below which there is simply no effect whatsoever, and above which there is some effect, even if the degree varies by individual.

### Food Type Matters

Rav Zeira is still not satisfied. He now shifts from the question of who is eating to what is being eaten: *basar shamen be-kotevet ve-lulavei gefanim be-kotevet?* — rich fatty meat the size of a date and grape leaves the size of a date are supposed to produce the same degree of *yishuv hada'at*? This seems implausible. The question is acute: by the time Abaye has admitted that the effect of the *shiur* varies by person — Og gets less relief than a smaller man — he has already broken the logic of a purely universal standard. And if individual variation is acknowledged, why not variation by food type as well? A satisfying piece of fatty meat surely does more to abate hunger and affliction than a comparable quantity of grape leaves.

### Final Threshold

Abaye's response follows the same structure as before: *kim lehu lerabanan, de-vehachi mitva dateih* — the rabbis established the threshold. Below it, no food, however rich, produces any relief whatsoever. *U-vatzir mei-hachi lo mitva dateih* — less than the threshold amount, neither the fatty meat nor the grape leaves helps at all. *Mihu*, however — once the threshold is crossed: *basar shamen tuva, ve-lulavei gefanim purta* — rich fatty meat produces a great deal of *yishuv hada'at*, and grape leaves produce very little. Above the threshold, the effect varies by food type.

The structure of Abaye's position is thus consistent throughout: the *shiur* is the threshold, not the total measure of the effect. Below it: nothing, universally. Above it: some effect, universally — but the degree varies by person and by food. Rava's subsequent objection moves to a related question that the Gemara takes up in the next *sugya*.

## Achilat Pras

### Rava Challenge

Rava raises what is at first a structural puzzle about the combination of eating over time. The halakhic principle is that multiple small amounts consumed within the time it takes to eat half a loaf — *kedei achilat pras* — may combine to reach the minimum *shiur*. This applies to a *kezayis* for ordinary food prohibitions: eat a *kezayis* of *chazir* or *neveilah* within *achilat pras* and the act counts as a single *achila* warranting punishment. The same time window, *kedei achilat pras*, is used on *Yom Kippur* for the *kotevet* — eat a date's worth of food within that window, and the act is complete. But the *kotevet* is approximately twice the size of a *kezayis*. Why should it take the same amount of time to combine? A larger minimum amount should arguably require a proportionally longer window, and the same short window seems incongruous.

### Rabbinic Knowledge

Abaye responds with his characteristic formula: *kim lehu lerabanan, d'hachi mitva dateih ub'hachi lo mitva dateih* — the rabbis know that eating done within the period of *achilat pras* produces *yishuv hada'at* and abates the affliction; eating spread over a longer period does not have the same effect, even if the total quantity consumed is identical. The act of *achila* that matters, whether for incurring punishment or for the *inuy* of *Yom Kippur*, is defined by that same temporal unit — because that is the window within which the consumption registers as a unified physiological and experiential event.

### Tamei Food

Rava then extends the challenge to a different area: eating *tamei* food. Normally, *tamei* food does not render a *person* *tamei* — it can only make *kodshim* *tamei*. However, the rabbis taught that if one eats a *chatzi pras* — half a loaf's worth — of *tamei* food, one's body becomes *tamei* through the act of ingestion: *tumat geviyah*. The time window for that combination is also *kedei achilat pras*. Yet the amount at stake is *chatzi pras* — half the loaf — which is four or five times the *kezayis* and about twice the *kotevet*. Why is the same five-minute window used for all of them: a *kezayis* for standard prohibitions, a *kotevet* for *Yom Kippur*, and a *chatzi pras* for *tumat geviyah*? It seems that the larger the required amount, the longer the combination window should be.

### Rav Pappa Answer

Rav Pappa responds: *hanach l'tumat geviyah d'lav d'Oraita hi* — set aside the case of *tumat geviyah*, making one's body *tamei* through eating, because that is not a biblical requirement. Being rabbinic, the *shiurim* and time windows can be defined as the rabbis see fit, without the constraint of perfect proportionality.

The Gemara immediately objects: does Rav Pappa himself not cite the verse *ve-lo titamu bahem ve-nitmetem bam* — you shall not make yourselves *tamei* through them and you shall become *tamei* through them — as a source for *tumat geviyah d'Oraita*? The answer: *la mi-derabbanan ve-k'ra asmakhta b'alma* — even when Rav Pappa invokes that verse, he means only that it is *mi-derabbanan*, a rabbinic enactment, and the verse serves merely as an *asmakhta*, a scriptural support. Therefore, the time window for *tumat geviyah* can be set as the rabbis determine, and the apparent inconsistency with larger amounts requiring the same temporal window is resolved by the rabbinic nature of the entire *inyan*.

### D'Oraita vs Rabbanan

The Gemara closes on a note that resonates with the major themes running throughout this entire *sugya*. The interplay between *d'Oraita* and *d'rabbanan* has been a consistent thread from the first discussion of *shiurim* all the way through: the Gemara initially resisted the idea that the rabbis defined the *shiurim* through their own authority (as in the *sugya* of Othniel ben Kenaz), yet at the same time repeatedly invoked the rabbis' knowledge and determinations — *kim lehu lerabanan* — to explain how the measures work in practice. The *beis hablia* derivation showed rabbinic input into the measurement of the *kibeitzah*; Abaye's entire series of responses to Rav Zeira rested on rabbinic establishment of thresholds. And now the daf closes with something declared to be *d'Oraita* revealed to be *d'rabbanan* with a verse serving only as *asmakhta*.

Similarly, the question of how the *shiurim* could ever be changed by a future *beis din* — which opened the *sugya* of record-keeping — touches the same fault line: if *shiurim* are *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai*, how much interpretive latitude does any later court genuinely have? These interlocking questions — how much is fixed from Sinai, how much is defined by rabbinic wisdom and authority, and where the line between the two lies — form the deep conceptual architecture of the *daf*, and the final *machloket* about *tumat geviyah* brings that architecture fully into view.
