# Sotah 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Meraglim & Lashon Hara

### Kalev at Chevron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיַּעֲלוּ בַנֶּגֶב וַיָּבֹא עַד חֶבְרוֹן״ — ״וַיָּבֹאוּ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁפֵּירַשׁ כָּלֵב מֵעֲצַת מְרַגְּלִים, וְהָלַךְ וְנִשְׁתַּטַּח עַל קִבְרֵי אָבוֹת. אָמַר לָהֶן: אֲבוֹתַי! בִּקְּשׁוּ עָלַי רַחֲמִים שֶׁאֶנָּצֵל מֵעֲצַת מְרַגְּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** It is also stated with regard to the spies: **"And they went up into the south, and he came to Hebron"** (Numbers 13:22). Why is the phrase "and he came" written in the singular form? The verse **should have** said: **And they came. Rava says:** This **teaches that Caleb separated himself from the counsel of** the other **spies and went and prostrated himself on the graves of the forefathers** in Hebron. **He said to them: My forefathers, pray for mercy for me so that I will be saved from the counsel of the spies.**

The *aggadeta* on this daf opens in the midst of a long narrative touching on the crossing of the *Yarden* at the time of Yehoshua, with digressions into related topics — most immediately, the story of the *meraglim*. The *gemara* had already noted that the verse describing the spies' arrival at Chevron employs the singular form — *vayavo ad Chevron* — and derives from this that only Kalev went to Chevron, where he prostrated himself at the graves of the *avot* and prayed: *bikshu alai rachamim*, "pray for mercy on my behalf." Rashi observes that this detail provides a satisfying closure to a parallel found later in Yehoshua's narrative: it is Kalev who ultimately receives Chevron as his inheritance — *ha'aretz asher darach bah* — a fitting reward for the very place where he had gone to seek the protection of the ancestors.

### Tosafot: Dead & Prayer

There is a pointed question raised by Tosafot on this passage. The *gemara* in the chapter *Mi She-meto* concludes that the dead are not aware of what transpires in this world — *lo yad'in midi* — and this applies even to the *avot ha-olam*, to Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov themselves. If so, what is the point of praying that the *avot* should intercede on one's behalf, as Kalev did?

Tosafot resolves this by suggesting that the dead do not directly hear prayers addressed to them, but they are informed — *modi'in lahem* — that someone below has prayed that they should in turn pray on his behalf. They receive, as it were, a summary of what has been requested of them. Tosafot adds corroborating sources: the practice cited in the second chapter of Ta'anis of going out to the cemeteries so that the *meitim* should pray on behalf of the living, and the episode in the chapter of the *chasidim* involving Rabbi Mana who prostrated himself at his father's grave. These precedents reflect a clearly encoded *minhag*, observed particularly during the *aseres yemei teshuva*.

### Intermediaries & Worship

The deeper concern with this practice, however, is not primarily whether the dead have awareness — Tosafot's answer addresses that — but whether the practice remains properly directed. The ideal, as understood by Tosafot and by the *gemara*, is that one requests of the deceased that they pray to God on one's behalf; they function as an intermediary in petition, not as the object of worship. The problem is that this line becomes quite blurred in practice. When people go to a *beit ha-kevarot*, it is very likely that they direct their prayers to the *meitim* themselves, not merely asking them to carry prayers forward to God. At that point, it becomes a *tefillah* addressed to a being other than God — something that would deeply disturb the Rambam, and which touches on a fundamental question about the nature of worship.

This concern is distinct from the concept of a *shaliach tzibbur*, who represents the congregation but remains a this-worldly representative praying together with the community, not an intermediary in any theologically charged sense. Similarly, the liturgical passage of *Machnisei Rachamim*, in which we ask angels to bring our prayers before God, occupies its own category.

The contrast with certain Christian practices is instructive here. Many Christian communities pray directly to deceased saints — not "please bring my prayer to God," but "Saint so-and-so, please help me" — where the saint is effectively the one prayed to, not merely the conduit. This is understandable in a theological framework where God has taken human form and where deceased holy individuals are correspondingly relatable objects of petition. But it is precisely this blurring — from intermediary to the thing prayed to — that raises serious concerns in the Jewish framework.

One might raise the concept of *zechut avot* in this context, but that is fundamentally different. *Zechut avot* is not a matter of directing prayer to the ancestors; it is a theological accounting in which one stands before God and invokes the accumulated merit of prior generations. The two concepts are not merely adjacent on a continuum — one is a claim about how God reckons merit, the other is a question about the very object of worship.

### Spies' Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיֵּלְכוּ וַיָּבֹאוּ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מַקֵּישׁ הֲלִיכָה לְבִיאָה, מָה בִּיאָה — בְּעֵצָה רָעָה, אַף הֲלִיכָה — בְּעֵצָה רָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And they went and they came"** (Numbers 13:25–26). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** This verse **likens** their **going to** their **coming. Just as** their **coming** back was **with wicked counsel, so too,** their **going** to Eretz Yisrael was **with wicked counsel.**

After this digression, the *gemara* returns to the narrative of the spies, picking up from *daf* 34b. The verse reads: *vayashuvu mitur ha'aretz miketz arba'im yom* — and they returned from scouting the land. A subsequent verse says *vayelchu vayavo'u* — they went and they came. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai derives from the juxtaposition of the going and the coming — *makish halicha livi'ah* — that just as their return was characterized by *etza ra'ah*, an evil plan or collusion, so too their original departure was already undertaken with that same evil design. They left with bad intentions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״אֶפֶס כִּי עַז הָעָם״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן (סִימָן אֱמֶת לְבַדּוֹ לְוִיָּה) מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: כָּל לָשׁוֹן הָרַע שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ דְּבַר אֱמֶת בִּתְחִילָּתוֹ — אֵין מִתְקַיֵּים בְּסוֹפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **and** then **it is written: "However the people** that dwell in the land **are fierce"** (Numbers 13:28). Why did the spies praise the land and then slander it? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** three statements **in the name of Rabbi Meir,** represented by the **mnemonic** device: **Truth, alone, borrowing.** The first statement answers this question: **Any slander that does not begin with a truthful statement ultimately does not stand,** i.e., it is not accepted by others.

The spies' report itself begins with a remarkable blend of truth and slander. They affirm that the land is indeed flowing with milk and honey and offer its fruit as evidence — *vegam zavat chalav udevash hi* — but then immediately pivot: *efes ki az ha'am*, the people are too strong for us. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Meir derives a principle from this structure.

### Lashon Hara & Truth

The principle is striking: *kol lashon hara she'ein bo devar emet bit'chilato ein mitkayem besofo* — any *lashon hara* that does not contain some element of truth at the outset will not endure. A skilled purveyor of harmful speech knows that proximity to the truth lends credibility and staying power. By contrast, slander that contains no factual core fails to take hold. It is worth noting that the *Chafetz Chaim* draws a careful distinction between *lashon hara* — true but harmful speech — and *motzi shem ra* — false slander. The *gemara* here uses *lashon hara* in its more generic sense of harmful speech, without that technical distinction, but the practical observation is about slander: it is most destructive when anchored in verifiable truth.

### Kalev's Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּהַס כָּלֵב אֶת הָעָם אֶל מֹשֶׁה״, אָמַר רַבָּה: שֶׁהִסִּיתָן בִּדְבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And Caleb stilled [*vayyahas*] the people toward Moses"** (Numbers 13:30). **Rabba says:** This means **that he persuaded them [*hesitan*] with** his **words.** *Vayyahas* and *hesitan* share the same root in Hebrew.

The verse then says *vayahas Kalev et ha'am el Moshe* — Caleb silenced the people before Moses. Rava interprets *vayahas* as *she'hisitam bidvarim* — he incited or seduced them with words, a way of drawing them in before pivoting. The question arises: why was it Kalev who spoke and not Yehoshua?

### Rosh Ketiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּתַח יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דְּקָא מִשְׁתַּעֵי אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: דֵּין רֵאשׁ קְטִיעָה יְמַלֵּל?
>
> **Translation:** How did he do so? **Joshua began** to address the people, and **as he was speaking they said to him: Should this** person, who has **a severed head,** as he has no children, **speak** to the people about entering Eretz Yisrael?

The answer is that Yehoshua did try to speak, but the people dismissed him immediately: *dein rosh keti'ah yemallel* — should this "cut-off head" speak? Rashi explains that Yehoshua had no children, and therefore the people reasoned that he had no personal stake in the outcome — he would not inherit the land himself and thus had no basis to advocate for entering it. His words carried no weight in their eyes.

The *Aruch* offers an alternative interpretation: *rosh keti'ah* alludes to the letter *yud* at the beginning of Yehoshua's name. Moshe had renamed him *Yehoshua* — adding a *yud* to *Hoshea ben Nun* — and a *yud* is a tiny, seemingly "cut-off" letter. The remark was simply a dismissive insult: this shrimp should speak? Either way, Kalev saw that whatever Yehoshua said, the people would find some way to be dismissive of him.

### Kalev's Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: אִי מִשְׁתַּעֵינָא — אָמְרִי בִּי מִילְּתָא וְחָסְמִין לִי. אָמַר לָהֶן: וְכִי זוֹ בִּלְבַד עָשָׂה לָנוּ בֶּן עַמְרָם? סָבְרִי בִּגְנוּתֵיהּ קָא מִשְׁתַּעֵי, אִישְׁתִּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Caleb **said** to himself: **If I speak they will** also **say something about me and stop me** from speaking. He began to speak and **said to them: And** is **this** the **only** thing that **the son of Amram,** Moses, **has done to us? They thought** that he wanted **to relate** something **to the discredit of** Moses, and **they were silent.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: הוֹצִיאָנוּ מִמִּצְרַיִם, וְקָרַע לָנוּ אֶת הַיָּם, וְהֶאֱכִילָנוּ אֶת הַמָּן. אִם יֹאמַר ״עֲשׂוּ סוּלָּמוֹת וַעֲלוּ לָרָקִיעַ״ לֹא נִשְׁמַע לוֹ? ״עָלֹה נַעֲלֶה וְיָרַשְׁנוּ אֹתָהּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **He** then **said to them: He took us out of Egypt, and split the sea for us, and fed us the manna. If he says** to us: **Build ladders and climb to the heavens, should we not listen to him? "We should go up at once,"** even to the heavens, **"and possess it"** (Numbers 13:30).

So Kalev calculated differently. He reasoned: *im ishta'ina, amrei bi milta, vachasmi li* — if I try to speak directly against them, they'll find a way to silence me too. So he said to them: *vachi zo bilvad asa lanu Ben Amram* — is this the only thing the son of Amram did to us? Note that he uses the dismissive epithet *Ben Amram* — a degrading way of referring to Moshe — as a rhetorical device. The people thought he was about to say something against Moshe and fell silent to hear the complaint. He had them. And then he pivoted: *hotzianu miMitzrayim, uvaka lanu et hayam, vehe'echilanu et haman* — he took us out of Egypt, split the sea, fed us the manna. And even if he told us to make ladders and climb to heaven, would we not listen? The structure is a direct parallel — and almost the inverse — of what the *gemara* said about *lashon hara* that must begin with truth. Here, a false premise — the suggestion that Moshe had wronged them — was used to gain the audience's attention, and then the truth was deployed to make the real point. It is, as one student aptly noted, a case of coming to praise Caesar by first seeming to come to bury him.

### Land Consumes Inhabitants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶרֶץ אֹכֶלֶת יוֹשְׁבֶיהָ הִיא״, דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אֲנִי חֲשַׁבְתִּיהָ לְטוֹבָה, וְהֵם חָשְׁבוּ לְרָעָה. אֲנִי חֲשַׁבְתִּיהָ לְטוֹבָה — דְּכׇל הֵיכָא דִּמְטוֹ, מִת חֲשִׁיבָא דִּידְהוּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנִיטַּרְדוּ וְלָא לְשַׁאֲלוּ אַבָּתְרַיְיהוּ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אִיּוֹב נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ, וְאִטְּרִידוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בְּהֶסְפֵּידָא, הֵם חָשְׁבוּ לְרָעָה, ״אֶרֶץ אֹכֶלֶת יוֹשְׁבֶיהָ הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** The spies said: **"It is a land that consumes its inhabitants"** (Numbers 13:32). **Rava taught: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: I intended** the land to appear to consume its inhabitants **for** their own **good, but they considered** this proof that the land was **bad. I intended** it **for** their **good** by causing many people to die there so **that anywhere that** the spies **arrived, the most important of them died, so that** the Canaanites **would be preoccupied** with mourning **and would not inquire about them. And there are** those **who say** that God caused **Job** to **die** at that time, **and everyone** in Canaan **was preoccupied with** his **eulogy,** and did not pay attention to the spies. However, the spies **considered** this proof that the land was **bad** and said: **"It is a land that consumes its inhabitants."**

Returning to the body of the spies' report: *eretz ochelet yoshveha hi* — it is a land that consumes its inhabitants. Rava expounds: God says, *ani chashavti letova* — I arranged this for their benefit. Whenever the spies passed through, a prominent person among the local inhabitants died — *meis chashiva didehu ki heichi denitardu* — so that the locals would be distracted by mourning and would not investigate who these strangers were. There is a second version: it was Iyov who died at that time, and all the world was occupied with his *hespeda*, which additionally removed the *zechut* that had protected the Canaanites — *sar tzilam me'aleihem* — since Iyov's merit had served as a kind of shield for them. But what the spies interpreted as ominous devastation was in fact divine protection in disguise. *Hem chashvu leraa*.

### Liars or Interpreters?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַנְּהִי בְעֵינֵינוּ כַּחֲגָבִים וְכֵן הָיִינוּ וְגוֹ׳״, אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: מְרַגְּלִים שַׁקָּרֵי הֲווֹ, בִּשְׁלָמָא ״וַנְּהִי בְּעֵינֵינוּ כַּחֲגָבִים״ — לְחַיֵּי, אֶלָּא ״וְכֵן הָיִינוּ בְּעֵינֵיהֶם״, מְנָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי?
>
> **Translation:** The spies said: **"And we were like grasshoppers in our own eyes, and so were we** in their eyes" (Numbers 13:33). **Rav Mesharshiyya says: The spies were liars. Granted,** to say: **"We were like grasshoppers in our own eyes," is well, but** to say: **"And so were we in their eyes," from where** could **they** have **known** this?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, כִּי הֲווֹ מַבְרִי אֲבֵילֵי — תּוּתֵי אַרְזֵי הֲווֹ מַבְרִי, וְכִי חֲזִינְהוּ, סְלִקוּ יָתְבִי בְּאִילָנֵי, שָׁמְעִי דְּקָאָמְרִי: קָחָזֵינַן אִינָשֵׁי דְּדָמוּ לְקַמְצֵי בְּאִילָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **But** that **is not so,** as **when** the Canaanites **were having the mourners' meal, they had the meal beneath cedar trees, and when** the spies **saw them they climbed up** the **trees** and **sat in** them. From there **they heard** the Canaanites **saying: We see people who** look **like grasshoppers in the trees.**

Finally, the *gemara* reaches the spies' claim: *vanehi be'eineinu kachagavim vechen hayinu be'eineihem* — we were in our own eyes like grasshoppers and so too in their eyes. Hamasharshiya declares bluntly: *meraglim shikrei havu* — the spies were liars. This is presented as something of a *chidush*, since until this point the spies had not said anything demonstrably false — *eretz ochelet yoshveha* is an interpretation of facts, not a fabrication. But now Hamasharshiya says outright they were lying. 

*Bishlama vanehi be'eineinu kachagavim lachayei* — granted, their self-assessment as grasshoppers is subjective and could be true. *Ela vechen hayinu be'eineihem mna yadei* — but how did they know how they appeared to the Canaanites?

The *gemara* pushes back — *velo hi* — and offers a resolution. Since there were constant deaths among the inhabitants, the spies would observe them providing *havra'ah* — the meal of condolence — to mourners under cedar trees. Bnei Yisrael, needing to conceal themselves, climbed up into those very cedar trees. From there they overheard the locals saying: *kachazinan inshei dedamu lekamzei be'ilanei* — we see people up there that look just like grasshoppers in the trees. The cedar trees appear in the story for a simple reason: they had to be elevated to hear what was being said at the level of giants, and concealment in the trees is what allowed them to overhear the remark at all. So the *gemara* concludes: they were not liars — they had actual evidence for their claim.

## Punishment & Plague

### Crying & Tisha B'Av

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּשָּׂא כׇּל הָעֵדָה וַיִּתְּנוּ אֶת קוֹלָם וַיִּבְכּוּ״, אָמַר רַבָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם עֶרֶב תִּשְׁעָה בְּאָב הָיָה, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: הֵן בָּכוּ בְּכִיָּה שֶׁל חִנָּם וַאֲנִי אֶקְבַּע לָהֶם בְּכִיָּה לְדוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And all the congregation lifted up their voice and cried"** (Numbers 14:1). **Rabba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: That day was the eve of the Ninth of Av,** and **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said:** On that day **they wept a gratuitous weeping, so I will establish** that day **for them** as a day of **weeping for** the future **generations.**

The verse states: *vatissa kol ha'eda vayitnu et kolam vayivku ha'am balayla hahu* — the entire congregation lifted their voice and the people wept on that night. Rabba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan identifies the timing: *oto halayla erev Tisha B'Av haya* — that night was the eve of the 9th of Av. God declared: *hem bachu bechiya shel chinam* — they wept for nothing. So God responded: *ani ekba lahem bechiya l'dorot* — I will establish for them a night of weeping for all generations. The report of the *meraglim* thus marks the very first of the catastrophes associated with Tisha B'Av.

### Stoning & Divine Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ כׇּל הָעֵדָה לִרְגּוֹם אֹתָם בָּאֲבָנִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְכָבוֹד ה׳ נִרְאָה בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנָּטְלוּ אֲבָנִים וּזְרָקוּם כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"But all the congregation bade stone them with stones"** (Numbers 14:10), **and it is written** immediately afterward: **"When the glory of the Lord appeared in the Tent of Meeting"** (Numbers 14:10). **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says:** This **teaches that they took stones and threw them upward** as if to throw them at God.

The verse continues: *vayomru kol ha'eda lirgom otam ba'avanim* — the congregation said to stone them with stones. Immediately the verse adds that *kevod Hashem* appeared in the *Ohel Mo'ed*. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba teaches: this teaches that they actually took stones and threw them upward — *klapei ma'ala* — toward God Himself. This reading connects to the earlier teaching about *mimenu* — greater than Him — in which the spies had implicitly disparaged even God's power. The throwing of stones at the divine presence is of a piece with that blasphemy.

### Spies' Plague

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּמֻתוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים מוֹצִאֵי דִבַּת הָאָרֶץ רָעָה בַּמַּגֵּפָה״, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: שֶׁמֵּתוּ מִיתָה מְשׁוּנָּה. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא, דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי שֵׁילָא אִישׁ כְּפַר תְּמַרְתָּא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּרְבֵּב לְשׁוֹנָם וְנָפַל עַל טִיבּוּרָם, וְהָיוּ תּוֹלָעִים יוֹצְאוֹת מִלְּשׁוֹנָם וְנִכְנָסוֹת בְּטִיבּוּרָם, וּמִטִּיבּוּרָם וְנִכְנָסוֹת בִּלְשׁוֹנָם. וְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: בְּאַסְכָּרָה מֵתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And those men who brought out an evil report of the land, died by the plague before the Lord"** (Numbers 14:37). **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** This means **that they died an unusual death. Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa says** that **Rabbi Sheila Ish Kefar Temarta taught:** This **teaches that their tongues were stretched out** from their mouths **and fell upon their navels, and worms were crawling out of their tongues and entering their navels, and** worms were likewise coming **out of their navels and entering their tongues.** This is the painful death that they suffered. **And Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: They died of diphtheria,** which causes one to choke to death.

The verse then records: *vayamutu ha'anashim motzi'ei dibat ha'aretz ra'ah ba-mageifah* — the men who spread the evil report of the land died in the plague. The word *ra'ah* is read in conjunction with *mageifah* — it was a particularly terrible plague, a *mitah meshunah*, an unusual death.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish teaches, citing the *drasha* of Rav Shila of Kefar Tamarta: their tongues stretched downward and fell to their navels — *nishtarbev lishonam v'nafal al tiburam* — and worms emerged from their tongues and entered their navels, and then cycled back from their navels to their tongues. The punishment was calibrated to the sin: they had misused their speech through *lashon hara*, and so the tongue became the locus of their grotesque death. The *tibur* — the navel — is significant as well, since the *gemara* in Yoma discusses the navel as a marker of the seat of life; the worms thus invaded the very center of their being and cycled between the organ of their sin and the core of their existence.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers an alternative: they died of *askara*, croup — again, a disease of the throat and tongue, fitting punishment for those who destroyed through speech.

## Jordan Crossing & Ar (II)

### Waters Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן שֶׁעָלָה הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מִן הַיַּרְדֵּן חָזְרוּ מַיִם לִמְקוֹמָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בַּעֲלוֹת הַכֹּהֲנִים נֹשְׂאֵי אֲרוֹן בְּרִית ה׳ מִתּוֹךְ הַיַּרְדֵּן נִתְּקוּ כַּפּוֹת רַגְלֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים אֶל הֶחָרָבָה וַיָּשֻׁבוּ מֵי הַיַּרְדֵּן לִמְקוֹמָם וַיֵּלְכוּ כִתְמוֹל שִׁלְשׁוֹם עַל כׇּל גְּדוֹתָיו״,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss the entry of the Jewish people into Eretz Yisrael. **And once the last one of the Jewish people ascended out of the Jordan, the water returned to its place, as it is stated: "And it came to pass, as the priests that bore the Ark of the Covenant of the Lord came up out of the midst of the Jordan, as soon as the soles of the priests' feet were drawn up unto the dry ground, that the waters of the Jordan returned to their place, and went over all its banks, as it had before"** (Joshua 4:18). The Gemara understands that the priests who carried the Ark stood in the water until all of the Jewish people passed through the Jordan. Once all the Jewish people had reached the other side of the Jordan, the priests stepped back from the water and the Jordan returned to its natural state.

The *gemara* returns now from the digression about the *meraglim* to the narrative of Yehoshua. *V'keivan she-alah ha-acharon she-b'Yisrael min ha-Yarden* — when the last Israelite left the Jordan, *chazru mayim limkomam* — the waters returned to their place. The verse quoted is *vayhi ba-alot ha-kohanim nosei aron brit Hashem mi-toch ha-Yarden*, when the *kohanim* emerged from the Jordan, at which point *nitku kapot raglei ha-kohanim el ha-charavah* — the soles of their feet stepped onto dry land — and *vayashuvu mei ha-Yarden limkomam*, the waters returned. They returned *al kol gedotav* — to their full banks — because, as the earlier *psukim* indicate, the Jordan was at peak strength following the snowmelt.

### Ark Lifts Carriers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא אָרוֹן וְנוֹשְׂאָיו וְכֹהֲנִים מִצַּד אֶחָד, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל מִצַּד אֶחָד. נָשָׂא אָרוֹן אֶת נוֹשְׂאָיו וְעָבַר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי כַּאֲשֶׁר תַּם כׇּל הָעָם לַעֲבֹר וַיַּעֲבֹר אֲרוֹן ה׳ וְהַכֹּהֲנִים לִפְנֵי הָעָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **It follows** that **the Ark and its bearers and the priests** were **on one side** of the Jordan, the east side, **and** the rest of **the Jewish people** were **on the other side,** the west side. Subsequently, **the Ark carried its bearers** in the air **and crossed** the Jordan, **as it is stated: "When all the people were completely passed over, the Ark of the Lord passed on, and the priests, before the people"** (Joshua 4:11).

A problem arises when one reads the *psukim* carefully. The Jordan split as soon as the *kohanim* stepped in — *as soon as their feet touched the water, the waters stopped*. That means the *kohanim* themselves never crossed the Jordan; they stepped in from the east bank, the waters held, all of Israel passed through, and then the *kohanim* stepped back out — onto the east bank, not the west. The *gemara* states this explicitly: *nimtza aron v'nos'av v'kohanim mi-tzad echad* — the *aron* and its carriers and the *kohanim* ended up on the east side of the Jordan, while all of Israel was on the west side.

The resolution: *nassa aron et nosav* — the *aron* lifted up those who were carrying it and miraculously transported them across. The *aron* was *nosei et nosav* — it carried those who bore it. The proof-text is the verse *vayhi ka'asher tam kol ha'am la'avor vaya'avor aron Hashem v'ha-kohanim lifnei ha'am* — when all the people had finished crossing, the *aron* passed and the *kohanim* were before the people. The logical reading of that verse is that the *kohanim* caused the *aron* to pass; but the *gemara* inverts this: it was the *aron* that passed and, in doing so, carried the *kohanim* over.

## Uzza's Punishment

### Uzza's Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל דָּבָר זֶה נֶעֱנַשׁ עוּזָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּבֹאוּ עַד גֹּרֶן כִּידֹן וַיִּשְׁלַח עֻזָּא אֶת יָדוֹ לֶאֱחֹז אֶת הָאָרוֹן״, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: עוּזָּא, נוֹשְׂאָיו נָשָׂא, עַצְמוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** **And over this matter Uzzah was punished** for not taking proper care of the Ark, **as it is stated: "And when they came to the threshing floor of Chidon, Uzzah put forth his hand to hold the Ark;** for the oxen stumbled" (I Chronicles 13:9). **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: Uzzah,** the Ark **carried its bearers** when it crossed the Jordan; **all the more so** is it **not** clear that it can carry **itself?**

It was precisely a failure to appreciate this power of the *aron* that brought about the punishment of Uzza. The verse recounts how, during King David's attempt to transport the *aron* to Jerusalem, they arrived at *goren Kidon*. *Vayishlach Uza yado le'echoz et ha'aron* — Uzza extended his hand to grab the *aron*, thinking the oxen had slipped and it was about to fall — *ki shamtu ha-bakar*. He was struck down on the spot.

## Uzza's Punishment (II)

### God's Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּחַר אַף ה׳ בְּעֻזָּה וַיַּכֵּהוּ שָׁם עַל הַשַּׁל וְגוֹ׳״. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, חַד אָמַר: עַל עִסְקֵי שָׁלוּ. וְחַד אָמַר: שֶׁעָשָׂה צְרָכָיו בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The verse states: **"And the anger of the Lord was kindled against Uzzah; and** God **smote him there for his error [*hashal*]"** (II Samuel 6:7). **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** disagreed over the interpretation of this verse. **One says:** God smote him **for his forgetfulness [*shalo*],** because he did not remember that the Ark can carry itself. **And one says:** God smote him **because he** lifted the edges [*shulayyim*] of his garment in front of the Ark and **relieved himself in its presence.**

The *gemara* frames God's rebuke of Uzza as follows: *nosav nasa, atzmo lo kol sheken* — the *aron* lifted those who were carrying it; surely it can take care of itself without your assistance. *Vayichar af Hashem b'Uzza vayakehu sham al ha-shal* — God's anger burned against Uzza and He smote him there on the *shal*, the error. The *gemara* emphasizes the word *shal* — it was not *meizid*, a deliberate sin. Uzza was trying to do something that appeared good. Yet the consequence was fatal — a pattern recognizable from the story of Nadav and Avihu, and from the broader principle that God holds the righteous to a more exacting standard, *medakdek im ha-tzadikim*.

The power-plant analogy is apt here: the presence of God is like a power plant that gives light and energy to the entire world, but proximity without proper protection — approaching incorrectly, doing the wrong thing — carries severe consequences. This is not a negation of the source's beneficence; it is a function of its intensity.

### Uzza's Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּמׇת שָׁם עִם אֲרוֹן הָאֱלֹהִים״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עוּזָּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עִם אֲרוֹן הָאֱלֹהִים״ — מָה אָרוֹן לְעוֹלָם קַיָּים, אַף עוּזָּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"And he died there with the Ark of God"** (II Samuel 6:7). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Uzzah entered the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "With the Ark of God." Just as the Ark exists forever, so too, Uzzah entered the World-to-Come.**

Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar debate the nature of *al ha-shal*. One says it refers to the error itself — the act, though well-intentioned, constituted a transgression of protocol. The other says *shal* means he relieved himself in its presence — a reading that attempts to account for why the consequences were so severe by suggesting a secondary disrespect. Rashi reads *shal* through the lens of *nashal zeitecha*, something that falls away.

Rabbi Yochanan, however, consistent with his framing of Uzza's act as an error rather than deliberate sin, rules that *Uza ba le'Olam Haba* — Uzza enters the world to come. The proof-text is the verse *vayamat sham im Aron Ha'elokim* — he died there *with* the *aron* of God. Just as the *aron* endures forever, so too Uzza will come into the world to come.

### David's Zmirot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה נֶעֱנַשׁ דָּוִד — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקָּרָא לְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה ״זְמִירוֹת״, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זְמִרוֹת הָיוּ לִי חֻקֶּיךָ בְּבֵית מְגוּרָי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: For what** reason **was David punished** with Uzzah's death? He was punished **because he called matters of Torah: Songs, as it is stated: "Your statutes have been my songs in the house of my pilgrimage"** (Psalms 119:54).

The *gemara* then asks: why was David also deserving of punishment in the context of this episode? *Mipnei she-kara l'divrei Torah zmirot* — because he referred to the words of Torah as *zmirot*, as songs. The reference is to the verse in Tehillim: *zmirot hayu li chukecha b'veit megurai* — your statutes were to me like *zmirot* in the house of my dwelling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה שֶׁכָּתוּב בָּהֶן ״הֲתָעִיף עֵינֶיךָ בּוֹ וְאֵינֶנּוּ״, אַתָּה קוֹרֵא אוֹתָן זְמִירוֹת?! הֲרֵינִי מַכְשִׁילְךָ בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן יוֹדְעִין אוֹתוֹ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלִבְנֵי קְהָת לֹא נָתָן כִּי עֲבֹדַת הַקֹּדֶשׁ וְגוֹ׳״, וְאִיהוּ אַתְיֵיהּ בַּעֲגַלְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: Matters of Torah** are so difficult and demanding **that it is written: "Will you set your eyes upon it? It is gone"** (Proverbs 23:5), i.e., one whose eyes stray from the Torah even for a moment will forget it, and **you call** them **songs?** For this reason **I will cause you to stumble in a matter that even schoolchildren know, as it is written** with regard to the wagons brought to the Tabernacle: **"And to the descendants of Kohath he did not give, because the service of the holy things** belongs to them; they carry them upon their shoulders" (Numbers 7:9). **And** although the Ark clearly must be carried on people's shoulders, David erred and **brought it in a wagon.**

This requires some unpacking. David's dancing before the *aron* is not the problem — that was an expression of joy and was regarded as proper honor. The concern is specifically that he characterized the *chukim* — the statutes of Torah — as *zmirot*. *Shira* is elevated poetic composition; *zemer* is a common tune. The distinction matters because one is expressing that Torah is a source of joy, which is legitimate, while the other is recasting the very genre of the words themselves — as if calling Torah a *zemer* places it in a different literary category, something lighter, something comparable to any pleasurable reading.

The *gemara* draws a contrast: if David had said Torah is a *sha'ashua*, a plaything, that remains clearly a metaphor for how one experiences it. But calling it *zmirot* is recasting the text itself as belonging to a different genre of writing, and that is what seems to trouble the *gemara*.

### Treating Torah Lightly

God's response to David is: *divrei Torah she-katuv bahem hata'if einecha bo v'eineno* — the words of Torah are such that if you but flutter your eye away, they disappear. You called them *zmirot*? *Hareini machshilcha b'davar she-afilu tinokot shel beit raban yod'in oto* — I will cause you to stumble in a matter that even schoolchildren know.

The connection between the *zmirot* rebuke and the *hata'if einecha* verse may initially seem opaque. But the logic runs as follows: if you treat Torah only as a source of pleasant entertainment — *zmirot* — and never as something requiring serious, careful attention, then you will not pay sufficient care to its words. That carelessness will cost you. And indeed: *u'vnei Kehos lo nasu ki avodat ha-kodesh aleihem b'katef yisa'u* — the Torah states explicitly that the *aron* was to be carried on the shoulder, not on a wagon. Every schoolchild knows this. Yet David had the *aron* transported on a wagon — *va'atun asinu ba'agalah* — an error that directly precipitated the tragedy of Uzza. The consequence of treating Torah lightly is that one commits errors that children know better than to make.

## Jordan Crossing & Ar (II)

### Ark's Autonomy

The broader principle the *gemara* has been developing is that the *aron* is not merely a sacred object that requires protection — it is itself a source of divine power that protects those who carry it, indeed that carries those who carry it. This is the inverse of what Uzza assumed when he reached out to steady it. The *aron* is *nosei et nosav*: it bears its bearers. Like a power plant, it is the source of energy and protection for those who relate to it correctly; but approached incorrectly, it is lethal. This principle connects the miraculous transport of the *kohanim* across the Jordan, the death of Uzza, the smiting of the men of Beit Shemesh who looked at the *aron* dismissively while continuing to harvest — and, in a different register, the punishment of David for treating the Torah with insufficient gravity.

The *gemara* relates the episode of Beit Shemesh as well: when the *aron* was returned by the Philistines, the people of Beit Shemesh were smitten. One opinion holds that the problem was they kept on harvesting in the field while the *aron* passed through — continuing their mundane work without pausing to acknowledge the moment. A second opinion holds that they went further and spoke dismissively of it: *man amricha de'mrayat, u'man asach de'puyasat* — who got you so angry? And who appeased you? — addressing the *aron* in a personified but contemptuous fashion. Either way — passive impassivity or active dismissal — the *aron* is not an object that tolerates being taken lightly.

## Three Stone Sets

### Moshe's Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצֵאתָ אַתָּה אוֹמֵר שְׁלֹשָׁה מִינֵי אֲבָנִים הָיוּ: אֶחָד שֶׁהֵקִים מֹשֶׁה בְּאֶרֶץ מוֹאָב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן בְּאֶרֶץ מוֹאָב הוֹאִיל מֹשֶׁה בֵּאֵר וְגוֹ׳״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְכָתַבְתָּ עֲלֵיהֶן אֶת כׇּל דִּבְרֵי הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת וְגוֹ׳״, וְאָתְיָא ״בֵּאֵר״ ״בֵּאֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the discussion of how the Ark was brought into Eretz Yisrael. **You are found saying** that **there were three sets of stones. One** is a set **that Moses erected in the land of Moab, as it is stated: "Beyond the Jordan, in the land of Moab, Moses took upon himself to expound [*be'er*]** this law, saying" (Deuteronomy 1:5). **And it states there** with regard to the mitzva to erect the stones on Mount Ebal: **"And you shall write on the stones all the words of this law** clearly elucidated [*ba'er*]" (Deuteronomy 27:8). **It is derived** through a verbal analogy between the word **"*be'er*"** that appears with regard to Moses, and the word **"*ba'er*"** that appears with regard to the mitzva to write the Torah on the stones on Mount Ebal that Moses also wrote down the Torah on stones.

Moving on, the *gemara* establishes that there were three distinct sets of stones erected in connection with the giving and writing of the Torah. The first was erected by Moshe in *Eretz Moav*, before the crossing of the Jordan. The connection is made through the parallel language of *be'er*: the opening of Devarim says *ho'il Moshe be'er et ha-Torah ha-zot* — Moshe began to explicate this Torah — and the commandment later uses *ba'er heiteiv*, explicate well. The *gemara* links the two instances of *be'er* to establish that Moshe himself performed a similar act of inscription.

### Yehoshua's Stones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶחָד שֶׁהֵקִים יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּתוֹךְ הַיַּרְדֵּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה אֲבָנִים הֵקִים יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּתוֹךְ הַיַּרְדֵּן״. וְאֶחָד שֶׁהֵקִים בַּגִּלְגָּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵת שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה הָאֲבָנִים הָאֵלֶּה אֲשֶׁר לָקְחוּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** there is **one** set **that Joshua erected in the Jordan, as it is stated: "Joshua also set up twelve stones in the midst of the Jordan,** in the place where the feet of the priests that bore the Ark of the Covenant stood, and they are there to this day" (Joshua 4:9). **And** there is **one** set **that** Joshua **erected in Gilgal, as it is stated: "And these twelve stones, which they took** out of the Jordan, Joshua set up in Gilgal" (Joshua 4:20).

The second set was erected by Yehoshua within the Jordan itself. The verse states: *ushteim esreh avanim hekim Yehoshua besoch ha-Yarden* — Yehoshua erected twelve stones in the midst of the Jordan.

The third set was the re-erection of those same twelve stones at Gilgal after the crossing. The verse states that they took the stones from the Jordan and erected them at Gilgal.

### Writing Method Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד כָּתְבוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַתּוֹרָה? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַל גַּבֵּי אֲבָנִים כְּתָבוּהָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכָתַבְתָּ עַל הָאֲבָנִים אֶת כׇּל דִּבְרֵי הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת וְגוֹ׳״, וְאַחַר כָּךְ סָדוּ אוֹתָן בְּסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: How did the Jewish people write the Torah? Rabbi Yehuda says: They wrote it on stones, as it is stated: "And you shall write on the stones all the words of this law"** (Deuteronomy 27:8). **And afterward they plastered them** over **with plaster.**

A *baraita* addresses the question of how exactly Israel wrote the Torah on the stones, given that the Torah also commands *vesadta osam ba-sid* — you shall plaster them with plaster. The intersection of carved inscription and plaster covering requires explanation.

### Yehuda's Method

The verse then states *vayahas Kalev et ha'am el Moshe* — Caleb silenced the people before Moses. Rava interprets *vayahas* as *she'hisitam bidvarim* — he incited or seduced them with words, a way of drawing them in before pivoting.

Rabbi Yehuda says: *al gabei avanim kesavuha* — they wrote it directly on the stones, as the verse states, *vechasavta al ha-avanim et kol divrei ha-Torah ha-zot*. And afterward, *sadu osan ba-sid* — they covered the stones with plaster. Presumably the inscription was carved into the stone, so it would remain legible beneath the plaster, though the readability would certainly be compromised.

### Shimon's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לִדְבָרֶיךָ, הֵיאַךְ לָמְדוּ אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם תּוֹרָה? אָמַר לוֹ: בִּינָה יְתֵירָה נָתַן בָּהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וְשִׁיגְּרוּ נוֹטֵירִין שֶׁלָּהֶן, וְקִילְּפוּ אֶת הַסִּיד וְהִשִּׂיאוּהָ. וְעַל דָּבָר זֶה נִתְחַתֵּם גְּזַר דִּינָם לִבְאֵר שַׁחַת, שֶׁהָיָה לָהֶם לִלְמֹד וְלֹא לָמְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said to him: According to your statement** that they plastered over the writing, **how did the nations of the world study Torah? He said to him: The Holy One, Blessed be He, granted them an extra** degree of **understanding, and they sent their scribes [*noteirin*], and they peeled off the plaster and copied it** down. **And on account of this matter their decree** to be sent **to the pit of destruction was sealed, as** once the Torah was in their possession **they should have studied** it, **and they did not study.**

Rabbi Shimon challenges Rabbi Yehuda directly: *lidvarecha, heich lamdu umot she-be'oto ha-zman Torah* — according to your view, how did the nations of that time learn Torah? This is a striking assumption embedded in the question: the purpose of writing the Torah *ba'er heiteiv* in *shivim lashon* was not merely internal — not merely so that Israel would know its own covenant — but so that the nations would have access to it. The Torah of Israel was to serve as a model for the world. If the inscription was then covered with plaster, how could the nations have read it?

Rabbi Yehuda responds: *binah yeseirah nasan bahem ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — God granted the nations particular wisdom and understanding. They sent their scribes, *veshigru notirin shelahem*, and those scribes *vekilfu et ha-sid* — peeled off the plaster — and carried it away. 

This is, on reflection, a brilliant solution. When text is carved into stone and plaster is poured over it, the plaster hardens in relief, with the letters raised on its surface — which is precisely how printing type functions. The peeled plaster becomes a perfect printing surface, a mirror image that can be pressed onto other materials to produce copies. The nations sent their scribes, who removed the plaster and thereby obtained what amounts to a printing press for the Torah in seventy languages. The *ba'er heiteiv* — the clear, detailed exposition — was not destroyed when the plaster was applied; it was preserved in a form ideally suited for distribution.

### Writing on Plaster

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַל גַּבֵּי סִיד כְּתָבוּהָ, וְכָתְבוּ לָהֶן לְמַטָּה ״יְלַמְּדוּ אֶתְכֶם לַעֲשׂוֹת כְּכֹל וְגוֹ׳״. הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁאִם הָיוּ חוֹזְרִין בִּתְשׁוּבָה — הָיוּ מְקַבְּלִין אוֹתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** That is not what happened. Rather, the Jewish people **wrote** the text of the Torah **on top of** the **plaster, and they wrote below for** the gentiles to read that the verse commands the Jewish people to destroy the gentile inhabitants of Eretz Yisrael: **"Lest they teach you to do like all** their abominations" (Deuteronomy 20:18). **You derive** from the fact that they wrote this verse **that if** the gentiles who lived in Eretz Israel **would have repented,** the Jews **would have accepted them,** i.e., allowed them to live in Eretz Yisrael.

Rabbi Shimon's own alternative view is: *al gabei sid ktavuha* — they wrote directly on top of the plaster, not on the stones beneath. And underneath — *vechatav lahem lemata* — was written the verse *leman asher lo yilamdu etchem la'asot kechol ha-to'evot* — so that they should not teach you to do all of their abominations — a verse about why the seven nations must be destroyed. On Rabbi Shimon's view as well, the plaster was pulled off and sent to the nations, but it was written on top of the plaster rather than transferred from the stone beneath. Either way, both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon are answering the same practical question: if the writing was meant for the *umot ha-olam*, how did they receive it when the stones stood in Eretz Yisrael? The answer is that the plaster itself — whether as printing press or as inscribed surface — was sent to them.

## Nations & Repentance

### Nations Could Repent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר שֵׁילָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיוּ עַמִּים מִשְׂרְפוֹת סִיד״ — עַל עִסְקֵי סִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Sheila said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon? As it is written: "And the peoples shall be as the burnings of plaster"** (Isaiah 33:12). This is homiletically interpreted to mean that the nations were punished **on account of matters of plaster,** i.e., they did not study the Torah that was written on plaster.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כִּי סִיד, מָה סִיד אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה אֶלָּא שְׂרֵיפָה — אַף אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם אֵין לָהֶם תַּקָּנָה אֶלָּא שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that the Torah was not written on plaster, interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: He explains that the gentiles are **like plaster; just as plaster has no remedy but burning,** i.e., it is created by burning stone, **so too, the nations of the world have no remedy other than burning** in Gehenna.

Rava bar Shila asks: what is the reasoning behind Rabbi Shimon's view? The verse beneath the inscription — *leman asher lo yilamdu etchem* — contains within it an implication: *ha lamadeta she'im hayu chozrim bi-teshuva, hayu mekabelin otan* — this teaches that had the nations repented, they would have been accepted. The very fact that God provided the Torah to the nations — that it was published for them in seventy languages — meant that repentance and acceptance were genuinely available to them. Rabbi Shimon understands that even the seven nations, had they changed their ways, could have been received rather than destroyed.

Rashi extends this: not only would they not have been destroyed, they could even have converted. The publication of Torah in seventy languages was a genuine invitation.

### Plaster Metaphor

The verse then cited is: *vehayu amim misrephot sid* — the nations will be like the burning of a plaster pit. Rava bar Shila reads this as *al iskei sid* — because of the plaster. The nations had the Torah given to them through the medium of the plaster in seventy languages, and they failed to learn from it. Therefore they will burn like plaster. The plaster that was meant to transmit Torah to them becomes the symbol of their judgment.

### Seven Nations Debate

Rabbi Yehuda reads the same imagery differently: *mah sid ein lo takana ela sreifa* — just as plaster has no remedy other than burning, *af otam ovdei kochavim ein lahem takana ela sreifa* — so too those nations have no remedy other than destruction. On this reading, the *gemara* is establishing a debate specifically about the *shiva amamin*, the seven Canaanite nations. Rabbi Shimon holds that even they, had they repented upon receiving the Torah through the plaster, would have been accepted. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *ein lahem takana* — they were destined for destruction regardless, with no possibility of reprieve.

This debate is focused specifically on the seven nations and not on the *umot ha-olam* generally. Regarding the general nations, neither opinion could hold *ein lahem takana* — otherwise the entire exercise of publishing Torah in seventy languages would have been pointless. The debate is thus a narrow one about whether the Canaanite nations, whom Israel was commanded to destroy upon entering the land, had any theoretical avenue of escape through repentance — and on this, Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda disagree.
