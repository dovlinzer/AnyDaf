# Shabbat 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overview: 39 Melachot

### Daf Context

Today's *daf* is 74, though we begin in the middle of 73b, which we did not finish in the previous session. A few words of introduction are appropriate, because we have been spending considerable time building toward this *mishna*. The *mishna* lists the thirty-nine *melachot*, and the *gemara* had in fact quoted its opening line no fewer than six times before actually arriving at it — building anticipation, as it were — and then once more immediately upon reaching it. The *mishna* on 73a is thus the central location, the canonical listing of the thirty-nine categories of *melacha*.

As we work through the *gemara* examining each category in turn, we should note at the outset something that is, frankly, a little frustrating: the discussion surrounding each *melacha* can be extremely terse. Sometimes this is because a later *perek* will develop that category more fully. Sometimes it is simply terse with very little material anywhere in the *gemara* about the exact parameters. It is genuinely difficult to explain why there is extensive discussion about purely theoretical questions — how many *chata'ot* one brings, or whether one may wear various objects on *Shabbat* — while the thirty-nine *melachot* themselves sometimes receive only a few lines. That said, the rest of the *masechet* does elaborate on many of them, sometimes addressing *de'oraisa* parameters, sometimes *de'rabbanan*, and sometimes leaving the distinction unclear.

### Four Categories

The thirty-nine *melachot* break down into four organizational categories. The process of making bread accounts for eleven *melachot*; making a garment accounts for thirteen; making a *klaf* — a type of parchment with writing upon it — accounts for nine; and the process of building a house accounts for the remaining group. Clothes, food, shelter, and, of course, books: that brings us to thirty-eight, with *hotza'ah* — carrying — standing apart as the thirty-ninth, not falling neatly into any of the preceding categories. The working assumption, which we will see operative throughout today's *gemara*, is that these categories are linked to what was done in the *mishkan*. Even though this is not precisely how the *mishkan*'s activities were categorized at the time, the *mishkan* serves as an organizing principle — a useful framework for thinking about those thirty-nine *melachot*.

### Yereim Position

That said, it is worth recalling the position of the *Yereim*, which holds that according to one approach in the *gemara*, the thirty-nine *melachot* are not based on the *mishkan* at all. Rather, they are grounded in what *Chazal* understood *melacha* to be — the main forms of constructive labor through which people interacted with the world. On this view, the categories are not merely organizational; they are the very reason these activities constitute *melachot*. The *Yereim* further notes: *lama mesarah hakatuv lachachamim* — once it was transmitted to the Sages, it became crystallized. No one can add to it. This is a radically different approach from the one normally taught, but it is the *Yereim*'s reading of one strand within the *gemara*.

### Avot & Toldot

The other essential preliminary concerns the distinction between *avot* and *toldot* — parent categories and their derivatives. A *toldah* is something similar to its *av* either in form or in function. Baking, cooking, and frying, for instance, are all similar in purpose and function — using heat to transform food. Some *toldot* are similar in form but not in function: grinding sawdust resembles grinding wheat into flour in its physical process — taking a whole thing and reducing it to small pieces — but the goal is entirely different. Conversely, some things are similar in function but not in form. As a general rule, one may assume that anything achieving a very similar goal is a *toldah*; things that share physical process but not goal are not always *toldot*.

### Parallel Avot

When something is similar to its parent category both in form and in function, it may not be a *toldah* at all, but rather a parallel *av*. This is the concept that Rashi and the Rambam refer to as *me'ein melacha achat* — "of the same type as one *melacha*" — meaning that an *av* category can encompass a set of parallel activities rather than a single defining act. Planting a seed, planting a tree, and perhaps even grafting are all direct manipulations of the thing one is trying to cause to grow, and so they may constitute parallel *avot*. By contrast, putting down manure, watering, or pruning a tree — where one is working on the surrounding environment to encourage growth rather than directly on the plant itself — would be *toldot*. The closer the resemblance in every dimension, the more likely the activity functions as a parallel *av* rather than as a derivative.

## Choresh

### Choresh Definitions

The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *hachoresh vehachofer vehachoretz* — whether one makes a furrow, digs a ditch for the sake of planting, or carves out a narrow channel — *kulan melacha achat hen*, they are all one *melacha*. Notice that the *gemara* does not call these *toldot*; it calls them one *melacha*, because they are all essentially the same thing: working the ground in some way to make it softer and more appropriate for planting. The physical change in the earth — whatever its precise form — serves the function of making the ground more fit for growth.

### Ground Leveling

Rav Sheshet then teaches: if one has a mound of earth — *gavshushit* — and removes it to level the ground, the *din* depends on context. *Bevayit chayav mishum boneh*: if it was done inside the house, where floors were made of dirt, one is liable for *boneh*, building, since leveling the floor is a significant part of the construction process. *Besadeh chayav mishum choresh*: if done out in the field, it constitutes *choresh*, plowing — even though plowing normally involves making a hole while here one is flattening something. The conceptual point is that one is working the ground, doing something to it that makes it more appropriate for planting. Whether that is a *toldah* or a parallel *av* is not entirely clear, but it is certainly within the *choresh* category when performed in the field.

### Filling a Hole

Rava extends the analysis with a parallel case. If one levels the ground by filling a hole — *siluka d'guma* — the same logic applies: *bevayit chayav mishum boneh*, inside the house it constitutes building a floor; *uvasadeh chayav mishum choresh*, in the field it constitutes plowing. Notice that in neither formulation does one's subjective intention determine the category. Rather, it is the objective character of the action — as determined by its location — that defines the *melacha*. This may be operating according to Rabbi Yehuda's view that *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa chayav*, but even so, we must objectively define what the action accomplished, and that determination depends on context.

### Digging for Dirt

Rabbi Abba's teaching presents the classic case of *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*: *hachofer guma b'Shabbat v'eino tzarich ela l'afrah* — one digs a hole on *Shabbat* but only wants the dirt. Perhaps one needed dirt to cover a mess on a dirt floor, or for some similar purpose. The ruling is *patur*: one is exempt. The *issur* attaches to digging the hole, so why is one exempt? Because this is *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*. The person performed the exact physical act of digging, and an outside observer would see what looks entirely like a *melacha*. But one's goal was entirely different from the stated purpose of the *melacha* — the purpose as defined by how it was performed in the *mishkan*. Digging a hole is a *melacha* because its goal is either to build something in the house or to make something fit for planting. Here the person is wholly indifferent to the hole; the dirt is the object. Performing the physical act for an entirely different purpose renders one *patur*.

Now, this is normally the subject of the famous dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon holds one is exempt for *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*; Rabbi Yehuda holds one is liable even so. The *gemara* therefore clarifies: *v'afilu Rabbi Yehuda* — even according to Rabbi Yehuda, *d'amar melacha she'eina tzricha legufa chayav aleha*, who holds that one is liable even when not acting for the *melacha*'s intended purpose — *hani milei metakken*, that is only when one's action is nonetheless constructive. *Hai mekalekel hu*: here, the hole is not making the floor more level, nor making the field more fit for planting. It is, objectively, making a mess. The hole is a *mekalkel*, not a *metakken*. If the hole happened to be dug in a way that would also serve planting purposes, then according to Rabbi Yehuda one would be liable even if that was not the intent. But if it is simply a disruptive hole that serves no constructive function in its location, even Rabbi Yehuda agrees there is no *chiyuv*.

## Kotzer

### Harvest Terms

Moving to *kotzer* — harvesting. Having addressed planting and plowing, the *gemara* now turns to cutting growing things. *Kotzer* applies when one picks something that is growing specifically because one wants that thing. Pruning for wood, picking fruit off a tree — any act of detaching a growing thing because one desires it — falls under this category.

The *gemara* teaches: *hakotzer, habotzer, v'hagoder, v'hamasik, ha'oreh* — whether one harvests wheat, harvests grapes, picks dates, picks olives, or picks figs — *kulam melacha achas hen*, they are all one *melacha*. Farmers use a different verb for every type of harvesting, but for our purposes these are all parallel forms of the same act. Crucially, the *gemara* does not call these *toldot* of one another; they are all parallel *avot*.

### Throwing at Dates

Rav Pappa introduces a striking case: *hai man d'shada pissa l'dikla v'atar tamrei* — someone throws a clump of earth or a rock at a palm tree and the dates fall. *Chayav shtayim* — this person has incurred liability for two *melachot*, and if done *beshogeg*, one would bring two *korbanot*. What are the two? *Achat mishum tolesh v'achat mishum mefarek*.

The first, *tolesh*, is the abstraction of *kotzer*: where *kotzer* referred specifically to wheat, the *gemara* here uses *tolesh* — removing something growing from where it grows — as the more general term. One is liable for detaching the dates from the tree. The second, *mefarek*, is understood by Rashi as a *toldah* of *dash*. Just as threshing extracts the kernel from the husk by applying pressure, so too *sechita* — squeezing — extracts juice or content from its container, and *mefarek* operates on the same principle: separating something from within which it is contained.

This model explains, for instance, why the *gemara* elsewhere characterizes milking a cow as *mefarek* — one is removing food from its container. Though Tosafot raises the difficulty that *mefarek* in other contexts is limited to *gidulei karka*, things growing from the ground, not from animals, the conceptual structure remains: *mefarek* is an abstraction of *dash*, the removal of food from that which contains it.

Why are dates specifically a case of *mefarek*? Rashi explains that dates grow in large clusters, much like grapes, so that knocking one loose separates it from the larger mass of which it is a part. Tosafot, however, objects: unlike juice within a grape or milk within an udder, a date in a cluster is not really enclosed within a shell. Tosafot therefore reads the case differently — what one does when striking the date is loosen it from its outer skin, *klipa ha-elyona*, the thin upper peel that covers the date. *U-kshemaka b'tamar mefarek et ha-klipa min ha-temarim*: by striking the date, one separates that outer layer from the date itself. Either way, the result is that throwing at dates generates a double liability: *tolesh* and *mefarek*.

This parallels the earlier observation that pruning can generate a double liability under certain conditions. Here too one act produces two *melachot* from two distinct categories, and since they are separate categories, two *chata'ot* would be required.

### Shinui Objection

Rav Ashi responds: *ein derech telisha b'kach v'ein derech prika b'kach* — throwing a rock at a tree is not the normal manner of *telisha*, nor the normal manner of *prika*. This constitutes a *shinui* — a non-standard method — and therefore one is not liable.

This sets up an important and recurring tension between Rav Pappa and Rav Ashi about the definition and scope of *shinui*. How far outside the norm must a method be before it qualifies as *shinui*? If an action is unusual but equally effective — or even more effective — does it still constitute *shinui*? The classic example is writing with one's non-dominant hand: that is *shinui* because it is both unusual and significantly more difficult, and the difficulty itself is part of what makes it a departure from the standard mode of action. But what of something unusual that presents no additional difficulty and produces the same result? These are the contours of a debate that will recur in later sections of the *gemara*.

## Me'amer

### Gathering Definition

The *gemara* continues with *me'amer* — gathering. Having cut the grain, one now gathers it, much as in Yosef's dream. The question of what exactly this *melacha* prohibits is more nuanced than it might initially appear. Does it cover any act of gathering scattered items together? If your children scatter their toys, may you not collect them?

### Salt Gathering

Rava addresses the case: *hai man de-khanif milkha mi-milkhasa* — someone gathers salt from a salt flat, a place where seawater is dried to extract the salt that spreads across a large area, which is then gathered into piles. This is strikingly similar to gathering grain: something has been produced by a drying or separating process, it lies spread out, and one gathers it together. Rava rules: *chayav mishum me'amer* — one is liable for gathering.

### Ground Growth Only

Abaye, however, limits this: *ein imur ela b'gidulei karka* — *me'amer* applies only to things that grow from the ground. This is an important limitation that we sometimes apply and sometimes do not across different *melachot*. We already encountered the question regarding *mefarek* and *dash* — do they apply to milking cows, which does not involve *gidulei karka*? Here, at least for *me'amer*, Abaye explicitly limits the *melacha* to things growing from the ground. Salt, even though its gathering is functionally similar — it is a food substance, it undergoes a processing stage, and then one gathers it — is not *gidulei karka* and therefore does not carry the *issur* of *me'amer*.

### Practical Examples

It follows from Abaye's limitation that gathering toys into a toy box on *Shabbat* is not *me'amer*. What would constitute *me'amer*, then? Twigs that have fallen from trees, scattered across the ground and gathered for firewood — that is a textbook example of *me'amer*, much like the *mekoshish eitzim*.

One useful practical resource here is the illustrated *39 Melachos* set, which presents visual examples with clear distinctions between *de'oraisa* and *de'rabbanan* cases. Among the examples it lists for *me'amer*: piling scattered fruit in an orchard, putting together a bouquet of flowers, and — interestingly — stringing figs, with a note that this applies even at home. The last example is striking, because one might have assumed that *me'amer* applies only where the items lie as they fell, before any prior gathering. One might have expected *ein imur achar imur* — that once something has already been gathered and brought inside, a second act of collecting cannot constitute *me'amer*. Yet the stringing of figs — a process by which figs were pressed tightly together into balls, a distinct stage of processing for sale or storage — raises exactly this question. The details can be found in the relevant *mekorot*, much of it drawn from the *Mishna Berurah*.

## Dash & Combing

### Threshing Types

The *gemara* continues with *dash*, threshing. Animals would be driven over a board fitted with cleats, pressing down on the stalks of grain to extract the kernel from the husk. A *tanna* teaches: *ha-dash, ha-menapeitz, v'ha-menafet* — whether one threshes grain, combs flax to remove its seeds, or combs cotton to remove its seeds — *kuleihu melacha achas hen*, they are all one *melacha*.

At first glance this seems straightforward: they all accomplish the same thing, extracting a kernel from surrounding material, just applied to different objects. But there is a meaningful difference. As far as one can tell, the purpose of working the cotton or the flax is to make the cotton and flax themselves usable — not because one wants the seeds. So the goal of the action is inverted relative to threshing: in threshing, one wants the kernels; in combing cotton or flax, one wants the surrounding fibers and the seeds are the byproduct.

### Wool Combing Question

Rashi raises the question directly: why are *menapeitz* and *menafet* not treated as *toldot* of wool combing rather than as parallel *avot* of *dash*? One might say that the object being worked determines the category — that wool, flax, and cotton are materially distinct enough to warrant different classifications. But that still does not explain why the *gemara* does not at minimum call them *toldot* of something. Perhaps the form — separating something from the material surrounding it — places them alongside *dash*; perhaps they are a type of *borer*, separating what one wants from what one does not. Whatever the precise categorization, the *gemara* calls them *melacha achas*, which suggests they function as parallel *avot* rather than as derivatives — even if the reasoning is difficult to unpack entirely.

## Separation Melachot

### Three Separation Methods

The *gemara* continues with three *melachot* that are all essentially ways of separating good from bad: *hazoreh*, winnowing — throwing threshed grain into the air so the wind carries off the chaff while the denser kernels fall; *haborer*, selecting by hand to remove dirt and debris; and *meraked*, sifting through a sieve. The *gemara* acknowledges the obvious: *hainu zoreh hainu borer hainu meraked* — winnowing, selecting, and sifting are all conceptually identical.

### Mishkan Principle

Abaye and Rava both respond with a unifying principle: *kol milta dehavya bamishkan, af al gav de-ika dedamya leh, chashiv leh* — anything that was a distinct process in the *mishkan*, even if there exist other activities similar to it, receives its own separate category. Each of these three processes — winnowing, selecting, and sifting — was performed as a distinct operation in the *mishkan*, and that is sufficient to grant each its own *melacha*, regardless of their conceptual overlap. Part of what seems to motivate this ruling is the need to account for thirty-nine distinct *melachot* — though the *gemara* has not yet said so explicitly.

### Kutesh Question

If the principle is that anything done in the *mishkan* receives its own category, then *velichtov nami koteish* — why not also include *kotesh*, the pounding that removes the inner layer of bran from the wheat kernel after threshing but before the fine grinding? The process works as follows: threshing removes the outer husk; pounding removes the inner bran layer still attached to the kernel, yielding whiter flour; then fine grinding produces flour. So why not count *kotesh* as its own *melacha*?

Abaye answers: *sheken ani ochel pitto belo ketisha* — a poor person eats his bread without this step. The bran is not always removed; people who eat coarser bread do not pound the grain at this stage. So *kotesh* was not a universal step in the process.

Rashi adds a further observation: there is actually an ambiguity about which category *kotesh* belongs to. One might classify it under *tochen*, since it involves pounding something and breaking it up. Or one might classify it under *borer*, since its purpose is to remove a part one does not want from the part one does want. Rashi's deeper point is that we are constructing a coherent organizational principle around the process of making bread, and we want to count only steps that are always performed. Since *kotesh* is not always performed, it is not listed — not because it lacks the substance of a *melacha*, but because the list aims to describe a standard, universal process. Since it would in any case be subsumed under *tochen*, there is no harm in omitting it.

### Thirty-Nine Limit

Rava offers a more fundamental answer: *hai mani Rebi hi, d'amar avot melachot arbayim chaser achas* — this *mishna* follows the view of Rebi, who holds there are exactly thirty-nine *melachot*. The number is not merely a convenient count — it is a fixed limit. *V'i chashiv kotesh havu lehu arbayim* — if *kotesh* were counted, there would be forty. Therefore it cannot be included.

The *gemara* then presses: but if we have to reach exactly thirty-nine and cannot exceed it, why do we count three separate *melachot* for what is essentially one process of separation — *zoreh*, *borer*, and *meraked* — while including *kotesh* only once, or not at all? Rashi notes the asymmetry: if *kotesh* is a type of *tochen*, we have two types of *borer* and two types of *tochen*, yet the *gemara* assigned three to separation and one to grinding. Why not swap one of the three separation *melachot* for *kotesh*?

The *gemara* concludes: *ela mechvarta ke'Abaye* — Abaye's answer is the better one. The reason *kotesh* is not counted is not primarily the arithmetic of thirty-nine, but the fact that it was not a universal step in the bread-making process. The arithmetic does play a role — the *gemara* acknowledges that we could have organized things differently and arrived at the same number — but the decisive factor is whether a step was universally performed. This does raise the broader question of whether the thirty-nine are an objective list or an organizational scheme that could have been constructed somewhat differently. The *gemara*'s discussion here suggests, perhaps uncomfortably, that both things are somewhat true.

One further note: Rashi comments that the bread-related *melachot* in the *mishkan* were actually performed not for bread at all but in the processing of plants to extract dye. The "bread" framework is an organizing metaphor, not a description of what literally happened in the *mishkan*. This makes the universality argument somewhat ironic — we are assessing which steps were "always" performed in a process that was never actually about bread — but the organizing principle holds regardless.

## Borer Part 1

### Borer Principle

The *gemara* now moves to the *halachot* of *borer* in earnest. A *braita* teaches: if one had before him multiple types of foodstuffs — *borer v'ochel, borer u'maniach*, one may sort and eat, one may sort and set aside. *Velo yavor*: but do not do *borer*. *V'im beirer chayav chatas*: and if one did *borer*, one is liable to bring a sin offering. This is perplexing: it says one may sort and then says do not sort. The *gemara* immediately asks: *mai ka'amar*? What exactly is being said?

What the *braita* is missing is the key criterion: what distinguishes the permitted sorting from the prohibited *borer*? Three different *amoraim* each insert their own criterion.

### Timing Criterion

Ulla says: *borer v'ocheil l'vo bayom* — one may sort if one will eat that day; *u'borer u'maniach l'vo bayom* — one may set aside if it will be eaten that day, which Tosafot explains means setting it aside for someone else who will eat it that day. *U'l'machar lo yivror*: but one may not sort for tomorrow. If one did sort for tomorrow, *chayav chatas* — one is liable.

Rav Chisda challenges this: *v'chi mutar l'afos l'vo bayom? V'chi mutar l'vashel l'vo bayom?* Does one bake for today? Does one cook for today? These are certainly prohibited even if one will eat the product the same day. How does "for today" make any difference?

The implicit logic behind Ulla's criterion — as Tosafot explains — is that selecting food in the context of eating might be classified as part of the eating process, *derech achila*, rather than as food preparation. Baking and cooking, by contrast, are always preparation, never eating. But even granting this framework, selecting for "later today" is plainly not part of the immediate act of eating. The criterion of today versus tomorrow is therefore rejected.

### Quantity Criterion

Rav Chisda proposes instead: *borer v'ocheil pachas m'kishiur, borer u'maniach pachas m'kishiur* — one may sort less than the minimum *shiur*, i.e., less than the volume of a *grogeres* (a dried fig). *V'kishiur lo yivror* — do not sort the minimum amount or more. Rav Yosef challenges with the same question: *v'chi mutar l'afos pachas m'kishiur?* Does one bake less than a *shiur*? The criterion of quantity is likewise rejected. Rashi notes in passing that there is a question of *chatzi shiur* — a biblical prohibition of performing a *melacha* below the minimum — though its application to *melachot* as opposed to prohibited foods is debated.

The underlying idea behind Rav Chisda's criterion — again, as Tosafot explains — was that a small quantity might be considered eating rather than preparation. But this too is rejected: the distinction between preparation and eating is not defined by quantity.

### Method Criterion

Rav Yosef proposes: *borer v'ocheil b'yad, borer u'maniach b'yad* — one may sort using one's hand. *B'kanun u'v'tamchui lo yivror* — one should not use a funnel or a large dish, which facilitates the sorting without being the professional tool; if one did, *patur aval assur* — exempt but prohibited, perhaps because of *shinui* or because it begins to resemble the professional act of *borer*. *U'vinfa u'v'khvara lo yivror* — one certainly should not use a sieve or sifter, the professional vessels for separation. If one uses such a vessel, *chayav chatas* — one is fully liable.

This criterion has considerable logical force: it distinguishes between *derech achila* — the natural manner of eating, which involves using one's hand to pick out what one wants — and the professional activity of *borer*, marked by the use of dedicated tools. The three-tier structure (permitted by hand, exempt but prohibited with a non-standard vessel, fully liable with a professional vessel) also introduces the nuance of *shinui*: using an intermediate vessel is a departure from normal eating but not the full professional mode of *borer*.

Rav Hamnuna objects: *midi kanun v'tamchui tani?* The *braita* never mentioned a funnel or a large dish. Tosafot explains the objection: if there are to be three levels — *mutar*, *patur aval assur*, and *chayav chatas* — based on the specific type of vessel, the *braita* would need to have specified those vessels explicitly. Since it did not, this reading cannot be correct as a *peshat* of the *braita*, even if the underlying legal distinction is sound.

### Immediacy Criterion

Abaye therefore proposes: *borer v'ochel l'alter u'borer u'maniach l'alter* — one may sort if eating immediately; *u'vo bayom lo yivror* — do not sort for later that day; *v'im birer, na'asah k'borer l'otzar* — if one did sort for later, one has done the paradigmatic act of *borer*: selecting into storage, an early stage of preparation. Anything beyond immediate is the biblical *borer*, and one is *chayav chatas*.

Abaye can also claim some support in the *braita* itself: the phrase *borer v'ochel* implies immediate eating, since *ochel* suggests the eating follows directly from the sorting. This is better grounded in the text than the previous criteria, and the *gemara* affirms it: *shapir amar Nachmani* — Rava, hearing the explanation, says that Abaye (whose other name was Nachmani) gave a fine *peshat*.

What exactly constitutes "immediate," *l'alter*? The general accepted definition is the period of last-minute preparation just before a meal — the window during which one prepares things for that same meal, even for the end of the meal. If one's meal is expected to last an hour and one sorts something that will be eaten at the end, that may still qualify as *l'alter* if the sorting occurred within the normal last-minute preparation window. Any *psak halacha* on the precise parameters requires a separate discussion.

## Borer Part 2

### Food vs. Chaff

The *gemara* does not settle on Abaye's criterion alone. Rav Huna now introduces the criterion of direction: *borer v'ochel, ochel mitoch psoles* — one may sort food from chaff, removing the food and leaving the waste; *borer u'maniach, ochel mitoch psoles* — or one may set aside food for someone else, still removing the food from the waste. *Psoles mitoch ochel lo yivror* — however, one may not remove the chaff from the food; *v'im birer chayav chatas* — and if one did, one is liable.

The logic here is that removing food from chaff mimics the natural manner of eating — one focuses on the food and takes it — while removing chaff from food is the professional manner of *borer*, the act of the one who selects and prepares produce for storage. The latter is precisely how grain was sorted in its early processing stages: the chaff was removed from the grain, not the grain from the chaff.

Abaye objects: *midi ochel mitoch psoles katani?* The *braita* does not use the language of food from chaff or chaff from food. Abaye therefore stands by his own criterion of immediacy. Nevertheless, the *gemara* does not abandon Rav Huna's distinction — it resurfaces shortly.

### Two Food Types

The *gemara* introduces a further complexity: what if one has two types of food before one — not food and chaff, but two perfectly edible items, one of which one happens not to want? The *gemara* reports a case: *hayu l'fanav shenei minei ochlin — borer v'achal, borer v'hiniach*. One sorted and ate; one sorted and set aside. Rav Ashi says *patur* — exempt; Rabbi Yirmeya mi-Difti says *chayav* — liable.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya — ha be-kanon ve-tamchuy, ha be-nafa u-kevara*. The case of *patur* involves using a non-standard vessel, while the case of *chayav* involves using a professional sieve or sifter. This resolution reintroduces Rav Yosef's vessel criterion. Notice what has happened: even after the *gemara* affirmed Abaye's answer as the best *peshat* of the *braita*, the other criteria — vessel type and direction (food from chaff versus chaff from food) — have quietly been reinstated through subsequent *sugya* material.

This is the crucial structural question underlying all of *borer*: are these criteria "and's" or "or's"? Must one satisfy all three — immediate, by hand, removing food from chaff — for the act to be *mutar*? Or does satisfying any one of them suffice? The weight of *halachic* tradition is that all three must be satisfied for the act to be *mutar*, and that missing even one renders it a *de'oraisa* violation. This is not stated explicitly in the *gemara* but is where the *halacha* arrives.

### Fruit Basket Story

The *gemara* relates a story involving Rav Bibi. It was his *Shabbat* of attendance — Rashi explains that it was his turn to see to the needs of the rabbis. Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi came to visit, and he placed before them a basket of fruit, *shada kammaihu kalkala de-firi*, pouring it out in front of them and letting them select for themselves. The *gemara* notes: *v'lo yadana* — we do not know the reason. Was it *im mishum de-savar ochel mi-toch psolet assur* — because he thought that even removing food from chaff is forbidden, and therefore he did not want to sort for them in advance? Or was it *mishum ayin yafa* — simply a generous gesture, allowing his guests to take what they liked?

Tosafot offers a clarification: perhaps Rav Bibi thought that when the sorting is not *l'alter* — not immediate — even *ochel mitoch psoles* is forbidden. So he did not sort in advance because it would not have been immediate, and the guests sorted for themselves immediately as they ate. Whatever the precise reason, what is significant is what the story quietly demonstrates: all three criteria are still in play. The story follows directly on Abaye's ruling about immediacy, yet it invokes the *ochel mitoch psoles* distinction. Neither criterion has displaced the other.

### Lupine Exception

Chizkiya teaches a case that seems to contradict the established rules: *ha-borer turmusin mi-toch psolet shelahen chayav* — one who removes lupines from their shells is liable. This is striking, because this appears to be *ochel mitoch psoles* — removing food from waste — which we established is the permitted direction. Why is it *chayav*?

The *gemara* explains: *shani turmusin de-shalkei lehu sheva zimni* — lupines are different because they are boiled seven times. Without continued boiling — or without timely removal from their shells — they will rot. The implication is that because of this boiling process, when one attempts to extract the seeds they become soft and mushy. The shells that have not yet been similarly processed are comparatively solid. In some sense, therefore, what appears to be *ochel mitoch psoles* is actually more akin to *psolet mitoch ochel* — one is removing the degraded, mushy component from the still-intact material. *U-ke-psolet mi-toch ochel dami*: it resembles chaff from food. This is admittedly difficult to understand clearly, and the difficulty is compounded by the fact that Chizkiya says the person is *chayav* rather than merely *assur* — a seemingly strong ruling for what might have seemed like an edge case of *ochel mitoch psoles*. Rashi offers several attempts at explanation, none of which resolves the matter entirely.

## Tachan

### Chopping Beets

Rav Pappa teaches: *hai man defaris silka chayav mishum tochen* — one who chops up a beet is liable for the *melacha* of *tochen*, grinding. This is a jarring statement. Cutting food into small pieces is *tochen*?

### Cutting vs. Grinding

Tosafot limits the ruling: *davka besilka shayach techina, aval shear ochlin sharei* — only with beets does the concept of grinding apply; for other foods it is permitted. The basis for the distinction is not fully spelled out, but perhaps beets, which are normally not eaten raw, undergo a meaningful transformation when minced very finely — something approaching a real change in substance rather than merely a change in size.

The Rosh, however, reads the text differently. He understands the *gemara* not to be discussing beets at all — he reads *silta* rather than *silka* — and understands the case as one in which a plant has been reduced to something almost dust-like, its leaves removed and the material ground to a fine powder. The Rosh's position is that ordinary cutting of food into pieces is not *tochen*. The question is: at what point does cutting stop being cutting and become grinding? The Rosh holds that real *tochen* requires a genuine physical transformation — reducing a solid to something powder-like — not merely producing smaller pieces of the same substance.

This question has immediate practical implications. Can one make egg salad on *Shabbat*? The analysis involves multiple *melachot*: mincing the ingredients (*tochen*), adding mayonnaise (*losh*, kneading), and so on. The *Tzitz Eliezer* addresses this at length, concluding that it is permitted, relying in part on the argument that mincing food does not constitute *tochen*, and in part on the principle that — by analogy to *borer* — if done *l'alter*, immediately before eating, it falls within the context of eating rather than preparation. One should not *pasken* from these remarks alone, but the framework is instructive.

### Sawdust Example

Rav Menashya teaches: *hai man desalis silti chayav mishum tochen* — one who produces sawdust is liable for *tochen*. This is a clearer case. Sawdust represents a genuine physical transformation: one takes solid wood and reduces it to fine powder. The material is not merely smaller; it has undergone a real change. Notably, this applies even though wood is not food — it is not part of the bread-making process — though it does grow from the ground. This demonstrates that *tochen* is not limited to food but extends to any material that undergoes this kind of transformative reduction.

### Kapid Amishchata

Rav Ashi adds a related but distinct ruling: *ei kapid amishchata chayav mishum mechatech* — if one cuts wood and is particular about the exact size of each piece, one is liable for *mechatech*, the *melacha* of cutting.

This introduces an important distinction between two related categories. *Koreia*, tearing, is a *melacha* that applies to cloth: any productive tearing of cloth, without a requirement of precision. *Mechatech*, cutting, applies to other materials — leather, wood, and similar substances — but carries the additional requirement of *kapid amishchata*: one must be particular about cutting to a specific, intended size. Tearing a cloth napkin at random is *koreia*; cutting a plank to an exact measurement is *mechatech*. The criterion of precision — caring about the exact dimension of the resulting piece — is what transforms ordinary cutting into the *melacha* of *mechatech*. This criterion will recur in later discussions.

## Dash & Sechita

### Dash Definition

The *gemara* addresses *dash*, threshing, as the parent *melacha*, and *sechita*, squeezing, as its *toldah*. The *av melacha* of *dash* is the act of threshing grain in the field: an animal walks over a board fitted with cleats, pressing down on stalks to extract the kernel from the husk. The *toldah* is *sechita* — squeezing fruit for its juice — because the act is structurally identical: extracting the desired content from its container. Threshing happens in the field; squeezing happens in the kitchen. The change of venue makes it a *toldah* rather than a parallel *av*.

### Fruit Categories

Not all fruits are equal in this context. *De'oraisa*, the prohibition of *sechita* applies only to olives and grapes. The reason is historical and functional: those were the fruits that were historically processed specifically for their juice — olives for oil, grapes for wine. For all other fruits, squeezing is *miderabanan*, unless it is common practice in a given place to squeeze them for their liquid. In America, for example, it is standard practice to squeeze oranges for juice, and therefore squeezing an orange in America would be *de'oraisa*.

### Lemon Squeezing

The case of lemon is particularly instructive. Unlike oranges, one does not drink lemon juice; one uses it to flavor food. The *halacha* is therefore that one may squeeze a lemon directly onto food on *Shabbat*. The reason is the principle of *mashke haba le'ochel ke'ochel dami* — liquid that goes directly into food takes on the status of food rather than of liquid. Since the juice is being absorbed directly into what one is eating, one is not producing a liquid in any independent sense. However, one may not squeeze a lemon into an empty cup, because there one is creating an independent liquid — *sechita* — even if it is only *miderabanan*. Squeezing onto fish: *mutar*. Squeezing into a cup: *assur*.
