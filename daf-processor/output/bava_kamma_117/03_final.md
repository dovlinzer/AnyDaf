# Bava Kamma 117 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ship & Caravan Loss

### Cargo Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יְשַׁנּוּ מִמִּנְהַג הַסַּפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **and they should not differ from the custom of sailors.**

The *daf* opens in the middle of a discussion about the practices of sailors and caravan-owners when cargo must be jettisoned to save a ship. The principle established is that when weight must be cast off, the loss is distributed proportionally according to each passenger's share of the cargo. This distribution is subject to one overarching constraint: *u'vilvad shelo yeshanu miminhag hassafanim* — one may not deviate from the established custom among sailors.

### Ship Owner Insurance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַשָּׁאִין הַסַּפָּנִים לְהַתְנוֹת, שֶׁכׇּל מִי שֶׁאָבְדָה לוֹ סְפִינָה נַעֲמִיד לוֹ סְפִינָה אַחֶרֶת. אָבְדָה לוֹ בְּכוּסְיָא – אֵין מַעֲמִידִין, שֶׁלֹּא בְּכוּסְיָא – מַעֲמִידִין לוֹ. וְאִי פֵּירַשׁ לְמָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין הַסְּפִינוֹת הוֹלְכִין – אֵין מַעֲמִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if a number of boats are traveling together, the sailors are permitted to stipulate that anyone whose boat was lost will provide him with a different boat. If his boat was lost through negligence, the other sailors do not provide him with another boat, but if it was lost not through negligence, the sailors provide him with another boat. And if the boat sailed to a place where the boats do not travel, the other sailors do not provide him with a different boat.**

The Gemara then addresses a mutual insurance arrangement among ship-owners sailing together as a convoy. The ruling is: *verasha'in hassafanim lehatnot* — the ship-owners are permitted to stipulate among themselves that if any one of them loses his ship, the others will collectively replace it. However, this arrangement carries implicit parameters. If the ship was lost through *kusya* — negligence — it is not replaced. Only when the loss occurred without negligence does the mutual obligation to replace apply.

### Seasonal Routes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דִּבְנִיסָן מְרַחֲקִי חַד אַשְׁלָא, וּבְתִשְׁרִי מְרַחֲקִי תְּרֵי אַשְׁלֵי, וְקָא אָזֵיל בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן לִמְקוֹם תִּשְׁרֵי; מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: דַּוְושֵׁיהּ נָקֵיט וְאָזֵיל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Isn't this **obvious?** No, it is **necessary because in the month of Nisan, the boats distance themselves from the shore by one rope length, and in the month of Tishrei, they distance themselves by two rope lengths. And in this case, the boat was traveling during Nisan and sailed to the place where boats sail during Tishrei. Lest you say that the sailor is not considered negligent because he is taking his usual route and traveling as he is accustomed to, the *baraita* teaches us that his actions are considered negligent.**

The Gemara raises a seemingly obvious point: if a ship owner sailed to a place where ships do not normally travel, he has evidently broken from the group and cannot expect replacement. *Pishita* — this is self-evident, the Gemara objects. The Gemara therefore specifies a more nuanced case: in Nisan, when snowmelt causes river waters to run high, ships sail one unit from the shore; in Tishrei, when water levels are lower, ships sail two units out. The case is one where a ship sailed in Nisan to the position that ships occupy in Tishrei — a normal sailing location, but not for this season. One might have thought that since it is a standard route at other times, the deviation is excusable, perhaps because the ship simply follows its habitual course. The Gemara rules otherwise: in Nisan, the standard is to sail closer to shore, and departing from that constitutes negligence. The ship is therefore not replaced.

### Caravan Rescue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁיָירָא שֶׁהָיְתָה מְהַלֶּכֶת בַּמִּדְבָּר וְעָמַד גַּיִיס וּטְרָפָהּ, וְעָמַד אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְהִצִּיל – הִצִּיל לָאֶמְצַע. וְאִם אָמַר: ״אֲנִי אַצִּיל לְעַצְמִי״ – הִצִּיל לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught in a *baraita*: In a case of a caravan that was traveling in the desert and a troop of bandits stood over it and pillaged it, and one of the travelers arose and rescued the caravan from the bandits, he has rescued the property for the common good, i.e., each member of the caravan receives back his property. And if he stated: I will rescue the property from the bandits for myself, then he has effectively rescued the items for himself, and he is now the legal owner of all the property.**

The Gemara proceeds to a *baraita* concerning caravans. A caravan traveling through the wilderness is attacked by troops who plunder it. One member of the caravan stands up and, through some act of bravery, saves the caravan. The ruling: *hitzil le'emtza* — he saved it for everyone. He does not get to appropriate the rescued property simply because his valor preserved it. It still belongs to all the original owners. However, if before acting he declared *ani atzil le'atzmi* — I am rescuing this for myself — then *hitzil le'atzmo*, he keeps it.

### Salvage Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּיָכוֹל לְהַצִּיל, אֲפִילּוּ סֵיפָא נָמֵי לָאֶמְצַע! וְאִי דְּלֹא יָכוֹל לְהַצִּיל, אֲפִילּוּ רֵישָׁא נָמֵי לְעַצְמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of the case? If each member of the caravan is able to rescue his property, then even in the latter clause, where the rescuer states that he is saving the property for himself, the *halakha* should also be that the possessions are rescued for the common good, as the other members of the caravan did not despair of retrieving their property. And if each member of the caravan is unable to rescue his property, they despair of retrieving their property and relinquish ownership of it. Consequently, even in the first clause, the *halakha* should also be that the possessions are saved for himself, so that he is their legal owner. Why is there a discrepancy between the *halakha* in the first clause and the *halakha* in the latter clause?**

The Gemara asks what the underlying logic of this ruling is. It distinguishes two scenarios based on the concept of *hefker*. If the property was *yachol lehatzil* — salvageable through normal collective effort — then the property never became *hefker* to begin with. In that case, no declaration makes a difference; it belongs to the owners regardless of what the rescuer says. Conversely, if it was *eino yachol lehatzil* — not realistically salvageable — then the property is considered *hefker*, and even without a declaration the rescuer might be entitled to keep what he saves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הָכָא בְּשׁוּתָּפִין עָסְקִינַן, וּכְגוֹן זֶה – שׁוּתָּף חוֹלֵק שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת חֲבֵירוֹ; אֲמַר – פָּלֵיג, לָא אֲמַר – לָא פָּלֵיג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rami bar Ḥama said: Here, in the case of the *baraita*, we are dealing with a caravan whose members are partners and jointly own the confiscated property, and in a case such as this, a partner may divide the property without the other partner's consent. Therefore, if he said that he intends to save the property for himself, he has dissolved the partnership and divided the property so that if he is able to rescue only some of the property, the property he rescues is his share of the joint property. If he did not say that he intends to save the property for himself, he has not divided the property, and whatever he rescues is jointly owned by the partners.**

Rami bar Chama resolves the difficulty by explaining that the *baraita* is dealing with *shutafim* — partners. The logic is that in such circumstances, one partner has the unilateral right to dissolve the partnership — *shutaf cholek shelo l'da'at chaveiro*. If he declares he is acting for himself, he is effectively withdrawing from the partnership, and since the property is *hefker*, whatever he saves is his. If he says nothing, the implicit position is that he continues to act on behalf of the partnership, and what he saves accrues to both partners.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָכָא בְּפוֹעֲלִין עָסְקִינַן, וּכְרַב – דְּאָמַר רַב: פּוֹעֵל יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בַּחֲצִי הַיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava stated a different answer: Here, in the *baraita*, we are dealing with laborers who were hired to protect the caravan, and one of these guards saved the travelers' property. And it is in accordance with the opinion of Rav, as Rav says: A laborer may retract his commitment to his employer even in the middle of the day.**

Rava offers a parallel reading: the case involves workers rather than partners. If a worker says nothing, since the property was *hefker* and he was hired to watch it, he is acting on behalf of his employer and saves it for the employer. If he declares he is acting for himself, that declaration constitutes quitting his position on the spot.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה דְּלָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ – כְּבִרְשׁוּתֵיהּ דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת דָּמֵי, וְכִי הָדַר בֵּיהּ – טַעְמָא אַחֲרִינָא הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי לִי בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל עֲבָדִים״ – וְלֹא עֲבָדִים לַעֲבָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **As long as he did not retract his commitment, he is considered to be under the jurisdiction of the employer, so that anything he saves automatically belongs to the employer. And when he retracts his commitment, it is for a different reason that he is able to do so, as it is written: "For unto Me the children of Israel are servants"** (Leviticus 25:55), indicating that Jews are servants of God, **but not servants of servants, i.e., of other Jews. Consequently, it is permitted for a laborer to conclude his term of employment whenever he wants. Once he does so, if he saves property that the members of the caravan are unable to rescue, the property belongs to him.**

### Worker Quits

Rava grounds this in the principle he articulates elsewhere: *po'el yachol lachazor bo afilu b'chatzi hayom* — a worker may quit his job even in the middle of the workday. Employment is not indentured servitude. As long as he has not yet quit — *k'reshutei d'ba'al habayit* — he is operating within his employer's domain and whatever he does is on the employer's behalf. Once he declares he is acting for himself, he has effectively quit. The Torah basis for this right to quit is the verse *ki li b'nei yisrael avadim* — the children of Israel are slaves to God — *v'lo avadim l'avadim* — and not slaves to slaves. The Gemara notes this as a powerful statement against treating employment as a form of enslavement. There may be contractual penalties for quitting, but one cannot be compelled to continue working.

### Rav Ashi Salvage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּשֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהַצִּיל עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק. גַּלִּי דַּעְתֵּיהּ – לְעַצְמוֹ, לָא גַּלִּי דַּעְתֵּיהּ – לָאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi stated another answer: The *baraita* is discussing a case where the other members of the caravan are able to rescue the property with difficulty. Therefore, if he revealed his intention to rescue the property for himself and the other travelers did not protest, it is clear that they have despaired of recovering their property, and the rescuer has secured the property for himself. If he did not reveal his intention to rescue the property for himself, the status quo remains and the property has been rescued for the common good.**

Rav Ashi offers yet another reading: the case is one where the property is *yachol lehatzil yadei dechaka* — salvageable, but only with effort. Rashi explains this as follows: if the rescuer declares he is saving it for himself and the other owner — Michael in the shiur's illustration — hears this and says nothing, that silence constitutes implicit *ye'ush* and an act of *hefker* on the owner's part, as if to say the property is as good as lost. In that scenario, the rescuer keeps it. If, however, no such declaration is made, the property is still potentially salvageable and is not presumed *hefker*; the owner retains title.

## Field Theft

### Mishnah Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹזֵל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וּנְטָלוּהָ מַסִּיקִין, אִם מַכַּת מְדִינָה הִיא – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״; אִם מֵחֲמַת הַגַּזְלָן – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ שָׂדֶה אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **one who robs a field from another and thugs [*massikin*] subsequently take the field from the robber, the *halakha* is dependent upon the circumstances: If it is a regional disaster in which the thugs seize all the property in the region, the robber says to the owner of the field: That which is yours is before you, i.e., it is your prerogative to try to reclaim it from the thugs. No compensation is required since the thugs would have seized the property in any event. But if the thugs took that field alone due to the robber, the robber is obligated to provide the owner with a different field.**

The Gemara moves to the next *mishnah*. *Ha-gozel sadeh me-chavero* — one who steals a field from his fellow. The *mishnah* then addresses what happens when troops subsequently seize that field. If the seizure is a *makas medinah* — a general regional calamity affecting everyone's fields, not just this one — then the thief says to the original owner: *harei shelcha lefanecha*, here is your property, effectively returning it in its diminished state and discharging his liability. The implicit logic is that even had the theft never occurred, the original owner would have lost the field to the same troops. However, if the seizure was *machamas hagazlan* — specifically on account of the thief — then the thief must *l'amid lo sadeh acher*, provide a replacement field.

This *mishnah* raises a difficulty. The ordinary rule is that *karka* — real property — is not subject to the laws of *gezeilah* in the ordinary sense. One who occupies another's land does not incur the standard liabilities of a thief in the way one who takes movable property does. If so, why does this *mishnah* seem to treat the field as stolen? One possible reading is that the *mishnah* is grounded in a concept resembling *garmi* — that even if the technical laws of *gezeilah* do not apply to land, when damage results directly and foreseeably from one's act, liability may still follow on that basis. The distinction between whether the seizure is general or personal could then be understood as turning on whether the owner's situation was made meaningfully worse by the thief's involvement: if everyone's field was seized, the owner is no worse off; if only this field was targeted because of the thief, real damage has been caused.

### Pointing Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאַחְוַי אַחְווֹיֵי. לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּאַנְסוּהָ לְאַרְעָא דִידֵיהּ וְלָא אֲנַסוּה כּוּלֵּי אַרְעָתָא, הָא מֵרֵישָׁא שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ: אִם מַכַּת מְדִינָה הִיא כּוּ׳, אִי לָא – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** No, it is **necessary for a case where the individual did not actually take control of the property before it was seized by the thugs, but rather showed the field to the thugs and thereby facilitated their occupation of the field. A different version of this answer is as follows: With what are we dealing here? A case where gentiles coerced someone and told him to show them his land for them to seize, and he showed them that field belonging to another, along with his own fields, and the thugs then seized all the land he showed them.**

The Gemara reinterprets the phrase *machamas hagazlan* in an unexpected direction. The case is not, the Gemara suggests, one of straightforward theft at all. Rather, it is a case of *d'achvei achveih* — he pointed it out. Troops or authorities came and demanded to know where a certain person's field was, and the man in question disclosed the location. The Gemara uses the term *gazlan* loosely here; the core liability is not for *gezeilah* but for the act of disclosure itself. The *mishnah* becomes a source for the broader principle that one who points out another's property to hostile authorities and causes it to be seized bears liability for the resulting loss.

This case is the gateway into the extended discussion of *moser* — one who informs against or betrays a fellow Jew to non-Jewish authorities. The concern is not merely the immediate financial loss; once hostile governmental power begins to take interest in a person's property, the danger has a way of escalating into a threat against life itself. The Talmudic image is that once the authorities begin seizing a person's assets, they show no compassion — like a trapped bull that is slaughtered without mercy.

### Wheat Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַחְוִי אַכַּרְיָא דְחִטֵּי דְּבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, חַיְּיבֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי. יָתֵיב רַב יוֹסֵף אֲחוֹרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא, וְיָתֵיב רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא לְרַב נַחְמָן: דִּינָא אוֹ קְנָסָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts a related incident: There was **a certain man who showed piles of wheat, belonging to the Exilarch's household, to thugs, who seized them. The case came before Rav Naḥman for judgment, and Rav Naḥman deemed that man liable to pay the Exilarch for the stolen wheat. Rav Yosef was sitting behind Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya, and Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya was sitting before Rav Naḥman. Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya said to Rav Naḥman: Did you deem him liable to pay because that is the *halakha*, or was it a fine?**

The Gemara grounds this discussion in a concrete case. A certain person pointed out a stockpile of wheat belonging to the *Reish Galuta* — the Exilarch — to non-Jewish authorities. The case came before Rav Nachman, who was himself related to the Exilarch. Rav Nachman ruled that the man who pointed out the wheat must reimburse the owner. Rav Yosef, sitting two rows behind Rav Huna bar Chiya, observed the proceedings as Rav Huna bar Chiya put a question to Rav Nachman.

### Din vs Knas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַתְנִיתִין הִיא, דִּתְנַן: אִם מֵחֲמַת הַגַּזְלָן – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ שָׂדֶה; וְאוֹקִימְנָא דְּאַחְוִי אַחְווֹיֵי. בָּתַר דִּנְפַק, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְרַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא: מַאי נָפְקָא לָךְ מִינֵּיהּ
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: This individual's liability is written explicitly in the mishna, i.e., it is the *halakha*, as we learned in the mishna: If thugs took the field due to the robber, he is obligated to provide the owner with a different field. And it was established that the mishna is referring to a case where an individual showed the field to thugs, who later seized it. So too, in this case, the one who showed the Exilarch's wheat pile to the thugs is obligated to pay for them. After Rav Naḥman left, Rav Yosef said to Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya: What difference does it make to you**

Rav Huna bar Chiya asked Rav Nachman: *dina o knasa* — is your ruling substantive law, a *din*, or is it a punitive fine, a *knas*? Rav Nachman replied that it is a *mishnah* — pointing to the ruling derived from our *mishnah* that one who causes another's field to be seized must reimburse. After Rav Nachman left, Rav Yosef asked Rav Huna bar Chiya why the distinction matters. Rav Huna bar Chiya explained: if this is *din* — substantive law grounded in a legal principle — then we may extrapolate from it and apply it to analogous cases. If it is *knas* — a penalty imposed for pragmatic or deterrent reasons — then each ruling stands on its own and one cannot extend it to other cases by logical inference.

### Fine Extrapolation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דִּינָא אִי קְנָסָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי דִּינָא – גָּמְרִינַן מִינֵּיהּ, אִי קְנָסָא – לָא גָּמְרִינַן מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **if it is the *halakha* or if it is a fine?** Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya **said to him: If it is the *halakha*, we learn from it and apply this ruling to other cases, but if it is a fine, we do not learn from it, as it is possible that Rav Naḥman had a specific reason to impose a fine in this case.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּמִקְּנָסָא לָא גָּמְרִינַן? דְּתַנְיָא, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: הַמְטַמֵּא וְהַמְנַסֵּךְ; חָזְרוּ לוֹמַר: אַף הַמְדַמֵּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do you say that we do not learn from the imposition of a fine in one case and apply the ruling in other cases? The Gemara answers that the source is as it is taught in a *baraita*: Initially, the Sages would say that one who renders another's food ritually impure, thereby rendering it unfit for him to consume, and one who pours another's wine as a libation for idol worship, thereby rendering it an item from which deriving benefit is prohibited, are liable to pay the owner for the financial loss they caused despite the fact that damage is not evident. Subsequently, they added to this list, to say that even one who intermingles *teruma*, the portion of the produce designated for the priest, with another's non-sacred produce, thereby rendering the non-sacred food forbidden to non-priests, is liable to compensate the owner for the loss of value of the produce, as fewer people will be willing to buy it from him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזְרוּ – אִין, לֹא חָזְרוּ – לָא; מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּקְנָסָא הוּא, וּקְנָסָא לָא גָּמְרִינַן מִינֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It may be inferred from the *baraita* that it is only because the Sages **subsequently** added to the list that **yes,** one who intermingles *teruma* with another's non-sacred produce must compensate him. But if **they had not subsequently** added to the list, he would **not** be liable. **What is the reason that we do not learn that he is liable from the cases of one who renders another's food impure or pours wine as a libation for idol worship, as this is also a case in which one causes damage that is not evident? Is it not due to the fact that his payment is a fine, and with regard to a fine, we do not learn from one case that it may be imposed in other circumstances?**

The question of whether one can extrapolate from a *knas* ruling to similar cases is addressed through a *baraita*. Originally the rabbis said: one who renders another's grain *tamei*, or one who pours another's wine as a libation to *avoda zara*, must pay, despite the fact that the damage is *hezek she'eino nikar* — invisible damage, not perceptible to the eye. The ordinary rule is that invisible damage is not actionable. Subsequently, the rabbis added a further ruling: even one who mixes *terumah* grain into *chulin* grain — causing the entire mixture to have *terumah* status and diminishing its market value — must also pay. The fact that a new ruling was needed in order to include this second case demonstrates that from a *knas* one cannot extrapolate; each application requires its own independent ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; מֵעִיקָּרָא סָבְרִי: לְהֶפְסֵד מְרוּבֶּה חָשְׁשׁוּ, לְהֶפְסֵד מוּעָט לֹא חָשְׁשׁוּ; וּלְבַסּוֹף סָבְרִי: לְהֶפְסֵד מוּעָט נָמֵי חָשְׁשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No, this is not the reason. Rather, initially the Sages maintained that they were concerned with regard to a large financial loss, e.g., the cases of one who renders another's food impure or pours his wine as a libation for idol worship, but with regard to a small financial loss, e.g., one who intermingles *teruma* with another's non-sacred produce, they were not concerned. And ultimately the Sages maintained that they were concerned with regard to a small loss as well and imposed liability.**

The Gemara contests this proof. Perhaps the original ruling was in fact *din* — substantive law — and a new ruling was needed not because extrapolation from *knas* is impossible, but because there was a logical reason to distinguish the two cases at the level of *din* itself. Specifically, the original ruling applied to large losses — grain rendered entirely unusable — while the *terumah* case involves a lesser loss, since the grain is not destroyed but merely downgraded in status. Originally the rabbis held that the principle of liability for invisible damage applied only to significant losses, and only later did they extend it to smaller losses as well. In this reading, no extrapolation was made because the cases were genuinely distinguishable on their merits, not merely because extrapolation from *knas* is impermissible.

### Damage Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא תָּנֵי אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: הַמְטַמֵּא וְהַמְדַמֵּעַ; חָזְרוּ לוֹמַר: אַף הַמְנַסֵּךְ. חָזְרוּ – אִין, לֹא חָזְרוּ – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't the father of Rabbi Avin teach the *baraita* as follows: Initially they would say that one who renders another's produce impure and one who intermingles *teruma* with another's non-sacred produce are both liable to pay for the financial loss that they caused, despite the fact that the damage is not evident. Subsequently, they added to this list, to say that even one who pours another's wine as a libation for idol worship is also liable to pay a fine for the loss that he caused. It may be inferred that it is only because the Sages subsequently added to the list, that yes, one who pours the libation is liable. But if they had not subsequently added to the list, he would not be liable.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא גָּמְרִינַן מִקְּנָסָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Since one who offers libations for idol worship causes a large financial loss, the rationale offered previously cannot apply to this version of the *baraita*. Accordingly, **what is the reason that the liability for pouring another's wine as a libation could not be extrapolated from the fine imposed for rendering another's food impure or intermingling it with *teruma*? Is it not due to the fact that we do not learn from the imposition of a fine in one case that a fine may be imposed in other cases?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; מֵעִיקָּרָא סָבְרִי כְּרַבִּי אָבִין, וּלְבַסּוֹף סָבְרִי כְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No, this is not the reason. Rather, the reason is that initially the Sages held in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Avin, and ultimately they held in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yirmeya.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא סָבְרִי כְּרַבִּי אָבִין – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין: זָרַק חֵץ מִתְּחִילַּת אַרְבַּע לְסוֹף אַרְבַּע, וְקָרַע שִׁירָאִין בַּהֲלִיכָתוֹ – פָּטוּר; שֶׁהֲרֵי עֲקִירָה צוֹרֶךְ הַנָּחָה הִיא, וּמִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Initially they held in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Avin, as Rabbi Avin says: If one stood in the public domain on Shabbat and shot an arrow from the beginning of an area measuring four cubits to the end of an area measuring four cubits, and the arrow tore another's silks [*shira'in*] in the course of its travel through the air, the one who threw it is exempt from paying for the cloth. The reason for this is that lifting an item is a necessity for placing it elsewhere, and therefore the entire process, from when one shoots the arrow until it comes to a rest, is considered to be a single act. The one performing it is liable to receive the death penalty for violating Shabbat. One who performs a single act for which he is liable to receive the death penalty and is also liable to pay money receives only the death penalty. Similarly, one who pours another's wine as a libation for idol worship incurs the death penalty, and is therefore exempt from paying for the wine.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְבַסּוֹף סָבְרִי כְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִשְּׁעַת הַגְבָּהָה קַנְיֵיהּ, אִיחַיַּיב לֵיהּ מָמוֹן; מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ לָא הָוֵי עַד שְׁעַת נִיסּוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And ultimately they held that the liabilities are not incurred simultaneously, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yirmeya, as Rabbi Yirmeya says: From the time of the lifting, the thief acquires the wine and is therefore immediately liable to pay money to the owner. But he is not liable to receive the death penalty until the time that he pours the libation.** Once the Sages concluded that the liabilities are not incurred simultaneously, they ruled that one who pours another's wine as a libation for idol worship is liable to reimburse him.

A second *baraita* — attributed to the father of Rabbi Avin — presents the cases in reverse order: first the ruling about *metame* and *medam'ea*, and then the subsequent addition of the case of pouring wine to *avoda zara*. Now the second case, pouring wine, involves a *greater* loss than the first; logically it should have been easier to include by extrapolation. Yet a new ruling was still required. This suggests the reason for requiring a new ruling is precisely the principle that from *knas* one cannot extrapolate.

The Gemara deflects this as well. The case of pouring wine to *avoda zara* involves a question of *kim leih bidrabbah minei* — whether the act, which carries a capital penalty, exempts the actor from financial liability simultaneously incurred. This turns on a dispute between Rabbi Avin and Rabbi Yirmiyah. Rabbi Avin held that the entire act of *chillul Shabbos*, including the moment of *akira* (lifting), is subsumed under the capital liability, applying the principle that the greater penalty absorbs the lesser — *kim leih*. Rabbi Yirmiyah disagrees: financial liability for wine attaches at the moment of lifting, while the capital liability for *avoda zara* attaches only at the moment of pouring. These do not coincide, and thus *kim leih* does not apply. The Gemara's resolution is that originally the ruling followed Rabbi Avin and therefore the wine case was excluded on substantive grounds, not merely because extrapolation from *knas* is forbidden. Only later, once the ruling followed Rabbi Yirmiyah, was the wine case added by a new explicit ruling.

The upshot is that the underlying question — whether the liability of one who points out another's property is *din* or *knas* — remains practically significant. As a *din*, grounded in an expansive understanding of *garmi* liability, it can serve as precedent. As a *knas*, it stands alone and cannot be extended. The Gemara's extended discussion of *hezek she'eino nikar* illuminates the structural question: when the rabbis impose liability for a type of damage that would ordinarily be exempt, are they declaring a *din* — that this really is *hezek* — or are they imposing a *knas* to deter harmful conduct?

## Forced Disclosure

### Rav Nachman Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי אֶבְיוֹנֵי. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּלוּם מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִידָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֲנָסוּהוּ גּוֹיִם וְהֶרְאָה מָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ בָּא לְיָדִי, וְחִיַּיבְתִּיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the matter of one who showed another's field to thugs. **Rav Huna bar Yehuda happened to come to the town of Bei Abiyonei and came before Rava,** who **said to him: Did any legal incident come to you for judgment recently? Rav Huna bar Yehuda said to him: There was a case of a Jew whom gentiles coerced and, as a result he showed them property belonging to another, which the gentiles later seized. He came to me for judgment, and I deemed him liable to compensate the owner for the loss.**

Rav Huna bar Yehuda came to a certain place and subsequently appeared before Rava. Rava told him he had recently adjudicated a case in which a Jew was forced by non-Jews to reveal the location of a fellow Jew's property, and he had ruled the man liable to reimburse.

### Baraita Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַהְדַּר עוֹבָדָא לְמָרֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֲנָסוּהוּ גּוֹיִם וְהֶרְאָה מָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ – פָּטוּר, וְאִם נָטַל וְנָתַן בַּיָּד – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Huna bar Yehuda: **Reverse your decision in this case and return the money to its owner, i.e., the thug, as it is taught in a *baraita*: In the case of a Jew whom gentiles coerced and, as a result he showed them property belonging to another that the gentiles later seized, he is exempt from reimbursing the owner of the property. But if he actively took the property and gave it to the gentiles by his own hand, he is liable to compensate the owner.**

Rav Huna bar Yehuda challenged Rava's ruling from a *baraita*: *Yisrael she'ansuhu ovdei kochavim l'haros mamon chaveiro patur* — a Jew who was compelled by non-Jews to disclose his fellow's property is exempt. The *baraita* continues: *v'im natal v'nasan b'yad chayav* — but if he physically picked it up and handed it over, he is liable.

### Point vs Hand Over

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: אִם הֶרְאָה מֵעַצְמוֹ, כְּנָשָׂא וְנָתַן בַּיָּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that **Rabba says: If he showed the gentiles the property of his own volition, it is as though he actively took the property and gave it to the gentiles by his own hand, and he is liable to compensate the owner.**

The *baraita* thus draws a sharp distinction between pointing out (*achvei*) and actively handing over (*natal v'nasan b'yad*). The *Rishonim* debate what this distinction means in practice. Rashi understands it as follows: handing over is more than was required. The person was only compelled to point out the location; when he went further and physically picked up the object and delivered it, that additional act was not coerced. Nobody was holding a weapon to his head at that precise moment, and so he becomes a *mazik* through his voluntary additional participation.

Other *Rishonim*, including the Rif, read the *baraita* differently: even when the gun is literally at his head and he is commanded to physically hand it over, he remains liable. The reasoning these authorities offer is that while no moral culpability attaches — one is not expected to forfeit one's life rather than cause financial damage to another — the fact that the friend's property was destroyed in the course of saving the actor's life does not shift that loss onto the friend. The actor benefited; the actor must reimburse. This reading, while logically consistent, is admittedly difficult. When a person's participation is entirely compelled and the coercing party is genuinely not interested in the actor but only in the victim's property, the degree to which the actor can be said to be an agent of harm is genuinely attenuated.

One might further observe that when it comes to land — *karka* — there can be no *natal v'nasan b'yad* in a literal sense, since land cannot be physically handed over. This arguably makes the case of pointing out land more lenient: with a gun to one's head, one is always exempt, since no physical transfer is possible.

### Rava Voluntary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּאֲנָסוּהוּ גּוֹיִם, וְאַחְוִי אַחַמְרָא דְּרַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב פִּנְחָס בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: ״דְּרִי וְאַמְטִי בַּהֲדַן״, דְּרָא וְאַמְטִי בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, פַּטְרִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts another incident: There was **a certain man that gentiles had coerced and so he showed them the wine of Rav Mari, son of Rav Pineḥas, son of Rav Ḥisda, and the gentiles said to him: Carry the wine and bring it with us.** Complying with the gentiles, **he carried and brought it with them. The case came before Rav Ashi,** and he **exempted the man from compensating Rav Mari for the wine.**

Rava rules: *im herah me'atzmo kenatfo v'natan b'yad dami* — if the person volunteered the information of his own accord, it is equivalent to handing it over physically. Where there was no compulsion at all, the act of voluntary disclosure is treated as fully active participation, and liability follows.

### Rav Ashi Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְהָתַנְיָא: אִם נָשָׂא וְנָתַן בַּיָּד – חַיָּיב! אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא אוֹקְמֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאוֹקְמֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא – מִיקְלֵי קַלְיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said to Rav Ashi: But isn't it taught in a *baraita*: If he took the property and manually transferred it to the gentiles, he is liable to compensate the owner? Rav Ashi said to them: That statement applies only in a case where the Jew did not bring the gentiles to the property at the outset; but if he brought the gentiles to the property at the outset, it is as though he already burned it, as the gentiles then had access to the property. Since the damage inflicted by the Jew was committed by merely showing the wine to the gentiles, he is exempt from payment even though he later actively carried the wine with his hands.**

A certain person was compelled by non-Jewish authorities and pointed out the location of wine belonging to Rav Mari, the son of Rav Pinchas, the son of Rav Chisda. The parties came before Rav Ashi, who exempted the man who had disclosed the location. The rabbis challenged: does not the *baraita* say that one who physically hands it over is liable? Rav Ashi replied: *hanei milei dela okmei ileiyei me'ikara* — that ruling applies when the authorities were not already standing directly over the property. But here, once the person pointed out the wine and the troops positioned themselves over it, the wine was as good as destroyed at that very moment — *miklei kalyei*, it was already consumed. His subsequent act of physically loading and transferring it therefore occurred after the property was already considered lost. Since he did not cause the destruction through the physical transfer — the destruction had already occurred at the moment of pointing out — he is not liable for the handing over.

### River Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אָמַר לוֹ אַנָּס ״הוֹשֵׁיט לִי פְּקִיעַ עָמִיר זֶה, אוֹ אֶשְׁכּוֹל עֲנָבִים זֶה״, וְהוֹשִׁיט לוֹ – חַיָּיב! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דְּקָאֵי בִּתְרֵי עֶבְרֵי נַהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu raised an objection to the opinion of Rav Ashi from a *baraita*: In a case where a ruffian said to a Jew: Pass me this bundle of grain, or this cluster of grapes, and the Jew passed it to him, the Jew is liable to pay the owner of the grain or the grapes. Since the ruffian was already present, it is evident from this *baraita* that one who hands over another's property to a third party is liable despite the fact that the latter already had access to it. Rav Ashi answered: With what are we dealing here? We are dealing with a case where the Jew and the ruffian were standing on two different sides of a river, so that the ruffian did not have access to the item when the Jew passed it to him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״הוֹשֵׁיט״ וְלָא תָּנֵי ״תֵּן״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out that the language of the *baraita* **is also precise according to this explanation, as it teaches its ruling using the term: Pass, which indicates that the ruffian could not have reached the item himself, and it did not teach using the term: Give, which would indicate that the ruffian was standing next to the other individual. The Gemara concludes: Learn from the language of the *baraita* that Rav Ashi's interpretation is correct.**

Rabbi Abbahu posed a challenge to Rav Ashi from a *baraita*: if a bandit says *hoshit li paki amir zeh* — extend to me this bundle of grain — and the person hands it over, he is liable. This sounds like a case of someone standing directly over the property, yet liability still attaches for the physical transfer. The Gemara resolves this: the case is *kegon d'ka'ei a'avra d'nahara* — the bandit is standing on the opposite bank of a river. He cannot reach the property himself. In that situation, the person who hands it across is genuinely an active agent; the bandit is not in a position to seize it unilaterally. The Gemara notes that the *baraita*'s language is precise: it says *hoshit* — extend to me — not simply *ten* — give. This implies that the bandit is at a distance and needs the item extended toward him, confirming that the case is one where the bandit cannot reach the object himself.

This also helps explain, at a more conceptual level, why active handing-over generates liability even under duress. When the coercing party is not standing directly over the object, the person who physically delivers it retains some degree of agency — he is the one who closes the gap between the victim's property and the thief. Even if his life is threatened, the fact that the transfer would not occur without his active participation lends his role a character distinct from pure passivity.

### Net Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא שׁוּתָא דַּהֲווֹ מִנְּצוּ עֲלַהּ בֵּי תְרֵי. הַאי אָמַר: דִּידִי הוּא, וְהַאי אָמַר: דִּידִי הוּא. אֲזַל חַד מִנַּיְיהוּ וּמַסְרַהּ לְפַרְהַגְ[בָּ]נָא דְמַלְכָּא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, יָכוֹל לוֹמַר: אֲנָא כִּי מְסַרִי – דִּידִי מְסַרִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְכׇל כְּמִינֵּיהּ?! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ עַד דְּמַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ וְקָאֵי בְּדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain fishing net over which two people were quarreling. This one said: It is mine, and that one said: It is mine. One of them went and gave it to an officer [*lefarhagna*] of the king. Abaye said:** He is exempt from payment because **he can say to the court: When I gave it to the official, I gave what is mine. Rava said to Abaye: And is it in his power to do so when the ownership of the net is the subject of dispute? Rather, Rava said: We excommunicate him until he brings the net back and stands in court for adjudication.**

A fishnet was being disputed by two claimants, each asserting *didi hu* — it belongs to me. One of them, unwilling to see the dispute drag on, simply handed the net over to a royal officer — in a gesture reminiscent of the Solomonic principle of preferring the other party's loss to one's own. The other claimant then demanded that the one who surrendered it pay for his share of the net.

### Joint Property

Abaye ruled that the one who surrendered the net could claim: *ana ki mati didi mati* — when I surrendered, I surrendered only my share. He has no claim to have surrendered the other's property. Rava, however, rejected this: why should he simply be believed? The dispute over ownership was unresolved, and his unilateral declaration that he surrendered only his share cannot be accepted on its face. Rava therefore ruled: *meshamtinan lei* — we place him under *cherem* until he retrieves the net from the royal officer and brings the parties back before a proper court. The possibility that the net belongs to the other claimant obligates him to restore it so the case can be properly adjudicated.

### Straw Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה בָּעֵי אַחְווֹיֵי אַתִּיבְנָא דְחַבְרֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״לָא תַּחְוֵי וְלָא תַּחְוֵי!״ אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״מַחְוֵינָא וּמַחְוֵינָא!״ יָתֵיב רַב כָּהֲנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, שַׁמְטֵיהּ לְקוֹעֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain man who desired to show another individual's straw to the gentile authorities, who would seize it. He came before Rav,** who **said to him: Do not show it and do not show it, i.e., you are absolutely prohibited from showing it. The man said to him: I will show it and I will show it, i.e., I will certainly show it. Rav Kahana was sitting before Rav, and, hearing the man's disrespectful response, he dislodged the man's neck from him, i.e., he broke his neck and killed him.**

A certain man was prepared to disclose to authorities the location of his fellow's straw — a relatively minor piece of property. The matter came before Rav. Rav warned him emphatically: *lo tachvei v'lo tachvei* — do not point it out, do not point it out. The man replied defiantly: *machvina u'machvina* — I will point it out, I will point it out. At this point, Rav Kahana, who was sitting before Rav, broke the man's neck, killing him to prevent the disclosure.

## Rav Kahana Story

### Neck Breaking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרֵי רַב עִילָּוֵיהּ: ״בָּנַיִךְ עֻלְּפוּ שָׁכְבוּ בְּרֹאשׁ כׇּל חוּצוֹת כְּתוֹא מִכְמָר״; מָה תּוֹא זֶה, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּפַל בְּמִכְמָר – אֵין מְרַחֲמִין עָלָיו; אַף מָמוֹן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּפַל בְּיַד גּוֹיִם – אֵין מְרַחֲמִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Seeing Rav Kahana's action, **Rav read the following verse about him: "Your sons have fainted, they lie at the head of all the streets, as an antelope in a net"** (Isaiah 51:20). **Just as with regard to this antelope, once it falls into the net, the hunter does not have mercy upon it, so too with regard to the money of a Jew, once it falls into the hand of gentiles, they do not have mercy upon him,** i.e., the Jew. Since gentiles who seek a Jew's money will kill him in order to seize the property, Rav Kahana acted appropriately when he broke the miscreant's neck, as he protected the Jew's property and, by extension, the Jew himself.

Rav responded by reciting a verse: *banecha ulfu shachvu b'rosh kol chutzot k'to michmar* — your children have fainted and lain down at the head of every street like a trapped bull. The Gemara explains: just as a bull that falls into a trap is shown no compassion and is immediately slaughtered, so too once the property of Jews falls into the hands of non-Jewish authorities, *ein merachmin alav* — they show no compassion. Rashi understands this to mean that ultimately the Jew's life itself becomes endangered — which would explain why Rav Kahana viewed the would-be informer as a *rodef*. Tosafot understands the verse as referring to the property, which would make the basis for killing the man more difficult to explain. In any event, the story implies that the man was seen as presenting an existential danger, not merely a financial one, and his act was of sufficient severity that Rav Kahana felt compelled to take his life.

### Persian Greeks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב: ״כָּהֲנָא, עַד הָאִידָּנָא הֲווֹ פָּרְסָאֵי – דְּלָא קָפְדִי אַשְּׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים, וְהַשְׁתָּא אִיכָּא יַוְונָאֵי – דְּקָפְדוּ אַשְּׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים, וְאָמְרִי ׳מַרְדִּין, מַרְדִּין׳. קוּם סַק לְאַרְעָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְקַבֵּיל עֲלָךְ דְּלָא תַּקְשֵׁי לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן שֶׁבַע שְׁנִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav then said to Rav Kahana: Kahana, until now there were Persian rulers who were not particular about bloodshed. But now there are Greeks who are particular about bloodshed, and they will say: Murder [*meradin*], murder,** and they will press charges against you. Therefore, **get up and ascend to Eretz Yisrael to study there under Rabbi Yoḥanan, and accept upon yourself that you will not raise any difficulties to the statements of Rabbi Yoḥanan for seven years.**

Rav turned to Rav Kahana and said: *ad idna havu Parsai d'lo kafdei ashfikhus damin* — until now we were ruled by Persians who were not particular about bloodshed, and you could have gotten away with it. *V'hashta Yavnai d'kafdei ashfikhus damin* — but now it is the Greeks who are particular about murder, and this will not be overlooked. The Koren edition notes that during Rav's lifetime the Parthian-Persian rulers, who had been culturally Hellenized and exercised loose control over internal Jewish affairs, were replaced by the Sassanid emperors, who were less Hellenized and exercised tighter central authority. Under the earlier, more Hellenized rulers, internal Jewish matters — including a Jew killing another Jew — were largely left to the community's own governance. Under the Sassanids, such actions would not be tolerated by the central government. The authorities would declare *mardin mardin* — murder, murder — and Rav Kahana would face criminal jeopardy.

### Exile Terms

Rav therefore instructed Rav Kahana: *kum sa'ak l'Eretz Yisrael* — arise and go up to the Land of Israel. And he imposed a condition: *kabel alach d'lo tiklei l'Rabbi Yochanan sheva shanim* — accept upon yourself not to challenge Rabbi Yochanan for seven years. The reason for this condition is not explicitly stated in the text. It may be understood as a form of *galus* and *atonement* for the killing, or it may have been intended as a protective measure to conceal Rav Kahana's identity from authorities. In any event, the condition itself — seven years of self-imposed silence in learning — would for someone of Rav Kahana's stature represent a genuine and painful form of exile: not merely geographic, but an exile from the full exercise of his Torah capacity.

### Rav Kahana Arrives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַיל, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּיָתֵיב וְקָא מְסַיֵּים מְתִיבְתָּא דְיוֹמָא לְרַבָּנַן. אֲמַר לְהוּ: רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ הֵיכָא? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַאי קוּשְׁיָא וְהַאי קוּשְׁיָא, וְהַאי פֵּירוּקָא וְהַאי פֵּירוּקָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. אֲזַל רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״אֲרִי עָלָה מִבָּבֶל, לְעַיֵּין מָר בִּמְתִיבְתָּא דְּלִמְחַר״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana **went to Eretz Yisrael and found Reish Lakish, who was sitting and reviewing Rabbi Yoḥanan's daily lecture in the academy for the Rabbis, i.e., the students in the academy. When he finished, Rav Kahana said to the students: Where is Reish Lakish? They said to him: Why do you wish to see him? Rav Kahana said to them: I have this difficulty and that difficulty with his review of Rabbi Yoḥanan's lecture, and this resolution and that resolution to the questions he raised. They told this to Reish Lakish. Reish Lakish then went and said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: A lion has ascended from Babylonia, and the Master ought to examine the discourse he will deliver in the academy tomorrow, as Rav Kahana may raise difficult questions about the material.**

Rav Kahana went to the Land of Israel and found Reish Lakish — Rabbi Yochanan's foremost colleague and student — sitting and delivering the review session of the day's lesson to the assembled rabbis. Rav Kahana asked where Reish Lakish was. When they inquired why he sought him, Rav Kahana enumerated a series of difficult questions — *hai kushya v'hai kushya* — along with the corresponding answers he had worked out. His erudition was immediately apparent. Reish Lakish went and told Rabbi Yochanan: *ari ala mi-Bavel* — a lion has come up from Babylonia. He advised Rabbi Yochanan to review carefully whatever *sugya* he planned to teach the next day, because this new arrival would challenge him strenuously.

### Row Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִמְחַר אוֹתְבוּהּ בְּדָרָא קַמָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אָמַר שְׁמַעְתְּתָא – וְלָא אַקְשִׁי, שְׁמַעְתְּתָא – וְלָא אַקְשִׁי; אַנְחֲתֵיהּ אֲחוֹרֵי שְׁבַע דָּרֵי, עַד דְּאוֹתְבֵיהּ בְּדָרָא בָּתְרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: ״אֲרִי שֶׁאָמַרְתָּ – נַעֲשָׂה שׁוּעָל!״
>
> **Translation:** **The next day, they seated Rav Kahana in the first row, in front of Rabbi Yoḥanan. Rabbi Yoḥanan stated a *halakha* and Rav Kahana did not raise a difficulty, in accordance with Rav's instruction. Rabbi Yoḥanan stated another ***halakha* and again, Rav Kahana did not raise a difficulty. As a result, they placed Rav Kahana further back by one row. This occurred until he had been moved back seven rows, until he was seated in the last row. Rabbi Yoḥanan said to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish: The lion you mentioned has become a fox, i.e., he is not knowledgeable.**

The next day, Rav Kahana was placed in the front row of the *beis medrash*, directly before Rabbi Yochanan — the seat of honor reserved for the most distinguished students. Rabbi Yochanan taught a *sugya*, and Rav Kahana said nothing. He taught another, and Rav Kahana remained silent. Honoring his commitment to seven years of silence, he asked no questions. In response to his silence, the academy progressively moved him back — seven rows to the rear — until he sat in the very last row. Rabbi Yochanan said to Reish Lakish: *ari she-amarta na'asa shual* — the lion you spoke of has turned out to be a fox.

### Pillars Removed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: יְהֵא רַעֲוָא דְּהָנֵי שְׁבַע דָּרֵי לִהְווֹ חִילּוּף שְׁבַע שְׁנִין דַּאֲמַר לִי רַב. קָם אַכַּרְעֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״נֶהְדַּר מָר בְּרֵישָׁא״. אֲמַר שְׁמַעְתְּתָא, וְאַקְשִׁי. אוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּדָרָא קַמָּא. אָמַר שְׁמַעְתְּתָא, וְאַקְשִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Kahana **said to himself: May it be God's will that these seven rows I have been moved should replace the seven years that Rav told me to wait before raising difficulties to the statements of Rabbi Yoḥanan. He stood up on his feet and said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: Let the Master go back to the beginning of the discourse and repeat what he said. Rabbi Yoḥanan stated a *halakha* and Rav Kahana raised a difficulty. Therefore, they placed him in the first row, and again, Rav Yoḥanan stated a *halakha*, and he raised a difficulty.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲוָה יָתֵיב אַשְּׁבַע בִּסְתַּרְקֵי, שָׁלְפִי לֵיהּ חֲדָא בִּסְתַּרְקָא מִתּוּתֵיהּ. אֲמַר שְׁמַעְתְּתָא – וְאַקְשִׁי לֵיהּ, עַד דְּשָׁלְפִי לֵיהּ כּוּלְּהוּ בִּסְתַּרְקֵי מִתּוּתֵיהּ, עַד דְּיָתֵיב עַל אַרְעָא. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן גַּבְרָא סָבָא הֲוָה וּמְסָרְחִי גְּבִינֵיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: דַּלּוֹ לִי עֵינַי וְאֶחְזְיֵיהּ. דַּלּוֹ לֵיהּ בְּמַכְחַלְתָּא דְכַסְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan was sitting upon seven cushions [*bistarkei*] so that he could be seen by all the students, and since he could not answer Rav Kahana's questions, he removed one cushion from under himself to demonstrate that he was lowering himself out of respect for Rav Kahana. He then stated another ***halakha* and Rav Kahana raised another difficulty. This happened repeatedly until Rabbi Yoḥanan removed all the cushions from underneath himself until he was sitting on the ground. Rabbi Yoḥanan was an old man and his eyebrows drooped over his eyes. He said to his students: Uncover my eyes for me and I will see Rav Kahana, so they uncovered his eyes for him with a silver eye brush.**

Rav Kahana heard this remark and was deeply offended. He made a declaration: may these seven rows of relegation serve in exchange for the seven years of silence that Rav had imposed upon me. He then rose to his feet and asked Rabbi Yochanan to repeat the first teaching. Rabbi Yochanan obliged, and Rav Kahana challenged it. He challenged every subsequent teaching as well. As the challenges mounted, the academy moved Rav Kahana progressively forward until he was restored to the front row.

Rabbi Yochanan was seated upon seven cushioned pillows — *bastarkei* — a detail that scholars have noted reflects a distinctly Babylonian narrative trope, through which the Babylonian Talmud imagines the great *yeshivos* of the Land of Israel. As Rav Kahana's challenges continued, one cushion was removed from beneath Rabbi Yochanan after each teaching, until all seven were removed and Rabbi Yochanan sat on the ground.

Rabbi Yochanan was very old at this point, and his eyelashes had grown so long that they fell over his eyes. He asked that his eyelids be lifted — they were raised with silver tongs — so that he could see Rav Kahana.

### Death Event

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲזָא דִּפְרִיטָה שִׂפְווֹתֵיהּ, סְבַר אַחוֹכֵי קָמְחַיֵּךְ בֵּיהּ. חֲלַשׁ דַּעְתֵּיהּ, וְנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ. לִמְחַר אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבָּנַן: חָזֵיתוּ לְבַבְלָאָה הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: דַּרְכֵּיהּ הָכִי. עָל לְגַבֵּי מְעָרְתָּא, חֲזָא דַּהֲוָה
>
> **Translation:** Once his eyes were uncovered, Rabbi Yoḥanan **saw that Rav Kahana's lips were split and thought that Rav Kahana was smirking at him. As a result, Rabbi Yoḥanan was offended, and Rav Kahana died as punishment for the fact that he offended Rabbi Yoḥanan. The next day, Rabbi Yoḥanan said to the Rabbis, his students: Did you see how that Babylonian, Rav Kahana, behaved in such a disrespectful manner? They said to him: His usual manner of appearance is such, and he was not mocking you. Hearing this, Rabbi Yoḥanan went up to Rav Kahana's burial cave and saw that it was**

When Rabbi Yochanan's eyelids were lifted, he observed that Rav Kahana's lips were parted — a physical condition Rav Kahana had always had. Rabbi Yochanan misread this as mockery, as if Rav Kahana were laughing at him. *Chalash da'atei* — Rabbi Yochanan was despondent, wounded deeply by what he perceived as contemptuous laughter from his student. As a consequence of Rabbi Yochanan's distress, Rav Kahana died.

The narrative carries a layered tragic irony. Rav Kahana had killed a man and gone into exile as a form of punishment and atonement. The terms of his exile — seven years of silence — were meant to be the instrument of his rehabilitation. By breaking those terms and then challenging Rabbi Yochanan with such force that the master was humiliated, Rav Kahana failed to fulfill the conditions of his exile and suffered its ultimate consequence.

### Revival Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָדְרָא לֵיהּ עַכְנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עַכְנָא, עַכְנָא, פְּתַח פּוּמָּיךְ וְיִכָּנֵס הָרַב אֵצֶל תַּלְמִיד״, וְלָא פְּתַח. ״יִכָּנֵס חָבֵר אֵצֶל חָבֵר״, וְלָא פְּתַח. ״יִכָּנֵס תַּלְמִיד אֵצֶל הָרַב״, פְּתַח לֵיהּ. בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי וְאוֹקְמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **encircled by a serpent [*akhna*], which had placed its tail in its mouth, completely encircling the cave and blocking the entrance. Rabbi Yoḥanan said to it: Serpent, serpent, open your mouth and allow the teacher to enter and be near the disciple, but the serpent did not open its mouth to allow him entry. He then said: Allow a colleague to enter and be near his colleague, but still the serpent did not open its mouth. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Allow the disciple to enter and be near the teacher, referring to Rav Kahana as his own teacher. The snake then opened its mouth for him to allow him entry. Rabbi Yoḥanan requested divine mercy from God and raised Rav Kahana from the dead.**

Rabbi Yochanan went to the cave where Rav Kahana was buried. He found a snake coiled around the entrance. He addressed it: *achna achna, ptach pumach v'yikaness harav eitzel talmid* — snake, snake, open your mouth and let the master enter to the student. The snake did not open. He tried again: let one colleague enter to greet another. The snake still did not open. Finally he said: let the student enter to greet his master. The snake opened. This detail — that the snake responded only when Rabbi Yochanan positioned himself as the lesser party — underscores the Gemara's implicit sense that Rav Kahana's Torah greatness exceeded even that of Rabbi Yochanan.

Rabbi Yochanan prayed for Rav Kahana's revival, and Rav Kahana was restored to life. Rabbi Yochanan said to him: had I known that the parted lips were simply your natural appearance and not a sign of mockery, *lo chalsha da'ati* — I would not have been despondent. He then invited Rav Kahana to return with him to the *yeshiva*.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּיְּירֵיהּ אוֹקְמֵיהּ. שַׁיְילֵיהּ כֹּל סְפֵיקָא דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ, וּפַשְׁטִינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ. הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״דִּילְכוֹן אֲמַרִי; דִּילְהוֹן הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan then completely **awakened him and stood him up. Thereafter, he asked him about every uncertainty that he had, and Rav Kahana resolved each of them for him. And this is the background to that which Rabbi Yoḥanan says** to his students on several occasions: What **I said was yours is** in fact **theirs,** i.e., I thought that the Torah scholars in Eretz Yisrael were the most advanced, but in fact the scholars of Babylonia are the most advanced, as evidenced by Rav Kahana's knowledge.

Rav Kahana replied: if you can pray that I will not die again at your hands, I will come with you; otherwise I will not. *Chalaf sha'ata chalaf sha'ata* — a miracle occurs once; you cannot guarantee it will occur again. On this condition, Rabbi Yochanan continued. He then posed to Rav Kahana all the questions that had remained unresolved for him — *kol sefeika d'hava lei* — and Rav Kahana answered every one of them.

The Gemara concludes with Rabbi Yochanan's own remark: *Yochana amri*, you the people of the Land of Israel think this Torah is yours — *d'lehon hi* — it is really the Torah of Babylonia. This remarkable conclusion — recorded, of course, in the Babylonian Talmud — brings the entire narrative to a close with a striking acknowledgment that the center of gravity of Torah scholarship had shifted. The story of Rav Kahana, beginning with an act of violent prevention of betrayal in Babylonia and ending with his resurrection and vindication in the *yeshiva* of the Land of Israel, is the Bavli's own testimony to the depth and supremacy of Babylonian Torah learning.
