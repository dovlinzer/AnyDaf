# Bava Batra 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trees vs. Saplings

### Rav's Rope Test

The daf opens on the last line of 69b with a ruling from Rav concerning a foundational definitional question that runs through the entire topic of field sales: when a seller says "I am selling you this field except for the trees," which plantings qualify as "trees" subject to the exclusion, and which are merely young saplings that pass to the buyer along with the field?

Rav's answer is formulated in terms of a practical climbing test: *kol she-oleh lo be-chevel hava yishuv* — anything that one must use a rope to climb in order to reach its fruit is considered a tree and is therefore excluded from the sale. *Kol she-eino oleh lo be-chevel lo hava yishuv* — anything that one does not need a rope to climb is not considered a tree in this sense and is not excluded.

### Yoke Bending Test

The *dayanei Gola* — the judges of the exile, identified as Shmuel and Karna — offer a different criterion at the top of 70a. Their test focuses not on the method of climbing but on the tree's structural maturity. When oxen walk through a field bearing a yoke and press up against a tree, if the yoke causes the tree to bend — *kol she-ol kovsho lo hava yishuv* — that tree is still young and pliable and is therefore not a tree subject to exclusion. If, however, the tree does not bend when pressed by the yoke — *kol she-eino ol kovsho hava yishuv* — it is a mature, firm tree and is excluded from the sale.

### No Dispute

The *gemara* resolves any apparent tension between these two positions by noting that *velo pligi* — they do not actually disagree. The two standards apply to different species. The rope test (*Rav's* criterion) applies to palm trees, which are tall and thin, lack climbable branches, and require a rope to ascend; there, the relevant question is whether a rope is needed. The yoke-bending test (the *dayanei Gola's* criterion) applies to other trees, which are not typically climbed by rope, and for those the standard is whether the trunk is firm enough to resist the pressure of a passing yoke. Once the cases are distinguished by species, the two rulings are entirely compatible.

## Carob/Sycamore Logic

### Exclude One → Sell Rest?

The *gemara* now turns to a related but distinct question posed by Rav Acha bar Huna to Rav Sheshet. The background is the principle established earlier in the *masechta* that when one sells a field, older carob trees and sycamore trees (*sadan*) are not included in the sale — they are treated, by virtue of their size and independent economic significance, almost as separate entities within the field rather than as ordinary components of it.

Given this, the question is: *chutz mi-charuv ploni chutz mi-sadon ploni mahu* — what is the law if the seller explicitly says "except for that carob tree" or "except for that sycamore tree"? The seller has named a specific tree he is retaining. Does this explicit exclusion of one tree implicitly convey all the other carob and sycamore trees to the buyer? The argument in favor of such a reading is intuitive: the seller went out of his way to carve out one tree, which seems to imply he is selling everything else. Is it possible, then, that the seller's very attempt to protect his interests actually weakens his position and grants the buyer more than he would otherwise have received?

The *Rashbam* notes that there are reasons a seller might name a particular tree even if he does not intend to surrender the others — for instance, he may simply not have known the governing *halakha* — and perhaps the language would even be read generously to preserve his right of access to the named tree. But the dilemma stands: could the explicit exclusion of one thing be read as an implicit inclusion of others?

### Rav Sheshet: No

Rav Sheshet answers directly: *lo kana* — the buyer does not acquire the other trees. The seller's naming of one carob tree does not weaken his position with respect to the others.

### Braita Ambiguity

A *braita* is then cited that seems to address this very case. The *braita* states that if a seller says "except for this carob tree" or "except for this sycamore tree," the buyer *lo kana* — does not acquire. The language of the *braita*, however, is strikingly ambiguous: does *lo kana* mean the buyer does not acquire that one named tree, implying he does acquire the rest? Or does it mean he does not acquire any of the carob or sycamore trees? The text, taken on its face, would seem to support the former reading — after all, the subject of the *braita* is the one named tree, and it says the buyer does not acquire it, leaving the others unaddressed.

Rav Sheshet, however, reads the *braita* as confirming his position: *afilu shar charuvim nami lo kana* — even the other carob trees the buyer does not acquire. He acknowledges that the textual formulation is awkward, but maintains that his reading is correct — and he turns immediately to logic rather than to the text to substantiate the claim.

### Field Analogy

The proof Rav Sheshet offers is elegant. Suppose I own several contiguous fields — call them A, B, C, D, and E — and I am selling you field A. If I say "I am selling you field A, but not field B," would anyone think that this exclusion of field B somehow conveys to the buyer fields C, D, and E, which were never on the table to begin with? Obviously not. The sale concerns field A alone. The other fields are entirely separate and independent entities; explicitly naming one of them as excluded in no way draws the others into the transaction.

The logic translates directly to carob and sycamore trees. The very reason these trees are not included in a standard field sale is that they are conceptualized as entities unto themselves — functionally equivalent to independent fields embedded within the larger parcel. Once we accept that conceptual framework, it follows necessarily that excluding one carob tree cannot implicitly convey the others: they are each separate and are each independently outside the scope of the sale. *Hachi nami lo kana* — here too the buyer acquires nothing.

### Half-Tree Question

The *gemara* then presents an alternative formulation of the question that was posed to Rav Sheshet — one in which the first version (the exclusion of a whole named tree) is taken as already resolved, and the genuinely contested question involves something more subtle.

What if the seller says *chutz mi-chatzi charuv ploni* — "except for half of this carob tree"? Here the intuitive pull toward the buyer's position is considerably stronger. When a seller explicitly retains half of something, it seems natural to infer that he is selling the other half. Does the half-exclusion language implicitly convey the remaining half of that specific tree to the buyer?

Rav Sheshet's answer from the earlier version already makes clear that *she'ar charuvim vadai lo kana* — the other carob trees are entirely out of the picture, separate fields by definition, and nothing the seller says could draw them in. But *ha mah she-shiyer be'oto charuv kani* — what about the remaining half of the one tree he named? Is the buyer acquiring that half?

Or do we say *afilu mah she-shiyer be'oto charuv nami lo kani* — even that remaining half the buyer does not acquire? This would mean that even within the boundaries of the single named tree, the seller's language of "half-exclusion" does not operate as an implicit conveyance of the other half, because the entire tree — in all its parts — is conceptually exterior to the field sale.

### Half-Tree Answer

Rav Sheshet is entirely consistent. *Lo kani* — the buyer does not acquire even the other half of the named tree. The *braita* cited in support of this version states that when a seller says "except for half of this carob tree or this sycamore," the buyer does not acquire any of the other trees — to which Rav Sheshet adds that he does not acquire the remaining half of the named tree either. Once again the *braita*'s language points toward the other conclusion, since it singles out the *other* trees as the ones not acquired, seemingly implying the remaining half of the named tree is acquired. Rav Sheshet again overrides the textual implication with his logical framework.

The confirmatory proof is identical in structure to the earlier one. If I say "I am selling you field A, but not half of field B," does that mean I am selling you the other half of field B? Plainly not. Field B is extraneous to the sale of field A entirely. Saying something — however peculiar — about half of it does not draw any part of it into the transaction. *Hacha nami lo kani* — the same is true of the half-named carob tree. Rav Sheshet's position is that the carob and sycamore trees are so thoroughly exterior to the field sale that no formulation of exclusionary language, whether of a whole tree or half a tree, can serve as an implicit vehicle for conveying any portion of them to the buyer.

## Deposit vs. Document

### Deposit Claim

A new case is introduced, brought by Rav Amram who poses a question to Rav Chisda. The connection to what precedes it is the upcoming mention of *dayanei Gola*, which appeared earlier in the daf. The case itself is entirely distinct.

Someone deposits an object with a guardian — a *shomer* — and to prevent any future denial, they draw up a *shtar* documenting that Reuven received a pouch of a hundred gold coins from Dov to watch on such-and-such a date, signed by witnesses, held by the depositor. Now Dov returns to collect his deposit. Reuven does not deny receiving it — he cannot, given the *shtar* — but says: *hechzarti lach* — I already returned it to you.

Can Reuven be believed?

The case for believing him is built on *migo*. A *shomer chinam* who claims that an accident (*ones*) befell the object is believed and is exempt — he need only take the standard *shvuat ha-shomrim*, the oath that accompanies a *shomer*'s claim, to be released from liability. A *shomer sachar* is similarly exempt from events of genuine force majeure. So Reuven could have said *ne'ensu* — an accident occurred — and been believed. Since that alternative claim, which would have been equally exculpatory and equally difficult to disprove, was available to him, the *migo* principle would seem to lend credibility to his actual claim of *hechzarti*.

The counterargument is equally forceful: *shtarcha b'yadi mai ba'ei* — if you really returned the deposit, why am I still holding the *shtar*? When a debt or obligation is discharged, the document evidencing that obligation is returned to the obligor or destroyed. The continued presence of the *shtar* in Dov's hands constitutes what the *Rashbam* characterizes as a *migo be-makom edim* — the *migo* argument runs up against something that functions as affirmative evidence that the return never happened. The depositor's retention of the *shtar* is effectively testimony that nothing was returned.

One might press back: perhaps Reuven did return the deposit but Dov did not yet have the *shtar* readily available, and Reuven accepted the arrangement informally. The *Rashbam* acknowledges this possibility, which is precisely why the question is genuinely difficult. Is the *shtar*'s continued presence in Dov's hands truly the equivalent of *edim* — witnesses — or merely a presumption that can be overcome?

An important distinction must be noted. This case concerns a *pikadon*, not a loan. In a loan case, the *migo* from *ne'ensu* does not exist: *ones* provides no exemption to a borrower, who bears absolute liability for the principal regardless of what happens to it. Since the *ne'ensu* claim would be worthless in a loan context, there is no *migo*, and the borrower cannot be believed to say he repaid it against a held *shtar*. The *pikadon* case is different precisely because *ne'ensu* is a viable and credible claim for the *shomer*, which is what generates the *migo* in the first place.

### Rav Chisda: Migo

Rav Chisda rules that Reuven is believed — *meheman*. The *migo* is operative even against the held *shtar*. The counterargument that it is *migo be-makom edim* is apparently not accepted, at least not as a categorical bar to belief.

The *gemara* presses back: but why should he be believed? The depositor is holding the *shtar* — what is it doing in his hand if the deposit was returned? This seems to restate the original objection. Rav Chisda responds: but consider what happens when someone says *ne'ensu*. In that case too, the depositor is still holding the *shtar*, yet we do not say the *shtar*'s presence disproves the *ne'ensu* claim. The claim of *ne'ensu* is fully believed notwithstanding the *shtar*. By the same token — and this is the *migo* — the claim of *hechzarti* should be equally credible.

*Tosafot* notes that this response is technically non-responsive to the *be-makom edim* objection: a claim of *ne'ensu* is entirely consistent with the depositor still holding the *shtar*, since the object was lost or stolen before any occasion to retrieve the *shtar* arose. But *hechzarti* is different — the very act of return creates an occasion to retrieve the *shtar*, and its non-retrieval seems anomalous. The *gemara* is dancing around this tension, and *Tosafot* acknowledges it.

### Oath Requirement

The *gemara* then adds precision. Even granting Rav Chisda's position, a further objection arises: when someone claims *ne'ensu*, he is believed — but only after taking a *shvua*, the *shvuat ha-shomrim*. So if we are extending that same credibility to a claim of *hechzarti* via *migo*, surely the same oath requirement applies. How can he be believed at all, and certainly without an oath?

The *gemara* clarifies: *hachi nami me'eman bi-shvua* — indeed, Reuven is believed only upon taking a *shvua*, just as he would swear in the *ne'ensu* scenario. When Rav Chisda said "he is believed," he meant believed with an oath. The deposit is considered returned, and Reuven is released from liability, but only after swearing that he did in fact return it. *Tosafot* notes that this was the precise concern that the *gemara*'s earlier objection was driving at — not primarily the *be-makom edim* point, but the apparent circumvention of the oath requirement.

## Iska & Orphans

### Iska Mechanics

The *gemara* now transitions to a new case that brings together the themes of *pikadon*, the credibility of the claim of return, and the special evidentiary rules that apply when dealing with orphans. The connecting thread back to the earlier part of the daf is that *dayanei Gola* reappear here in a distinct context.

The case involves a *shtar kis* — literally a "pocket document," a document drawn up in connection with an *iska*, a commercial investment arrangement. The economics of an *iska* require some background.

One party has capital and another has the business acumen or contacts to invest it. They enter an arrangement: the capitalist gives his money to the investor, and they share in the profits. If the capitalist bears all the risk of loss — since the money is his — the arrangement is financially straightforward. But if they wish to share the downside risk as well, a more complex structure is required: half of the money is treated as a loan to the investor, and half is treated as a *pikadon* — a deposit entrusted to the investor to work with. The loan half is the investor's obligation to repay regardless of the investment's performance; the *pikadon* half is invested on behalf of the capitalist, whose money it remains, meaning any loss on that half is borne by the capitalist himself.

To illustrate: the capitalist gives a hundred dollars. Fifty is a loan; fifty is a *pikadon*. If the investment doubles to two hundred dollars, the profit is split: each party gains fifty. If the investment falls to fifty dollars, the loan half has lost twenty-five for the investor (who must still repay the original fifty), and the *pikadon* half has lost twenty-five for the capitalist. The capitalist ends up with seventy-five — recovering the fifty-dollar loan in full from the investor and receiving twenty-five dollars as the residual value of his *pikadon* half.

This structure is the classic *heter iska*, the permissible investment framework. There are also issues of *ribis* — prohibited interest — that arise from this arrangement, since the investor is generating profit from the capitalist's *pikadon* half in exchange, effectively, for having received the loan half. Various technical adjustments are made to address this, as discussed in Bava Metzia, but the essential point here is the split structure: half loan, half *pikadon*. This is the ruling of *Nehardei*, and the *gemara* notes that it is universally accepted: *dikuli alma is lehu di-Nehardei* — everyone accepts the Nehardean position that an *iska* is half loan and half *pikadon*.

### Tannaim Debate

The *shtar kis* case involves this *iska* document being brought against orphans after the father — the investor — has died. The capitalist comes to collect his principal. He holds the *shtar*. The orphans do not know whether their father paid back the *pikadon* half.

A *braita* records a dispute between the *dayanei Gola* — Shmuel and Karna — and the *dayanei Eretz Yisrael*. The *dayanei Gola* say *nishba ve-goveh kulo* — the creditor takes an oath and collects the entire principal, both the loan half and the *pikadon* half. The *dayanei Eretz Yisrael* say *nishba ve-goveh mechtza* — the creditor takes an oath and collects only half, recovering only the loan portion and not the *pikadon* portion.

The *gemara* initially suggests that this dispute might map onto the earlier debate about whether a depositary is believed to say *hechzarti* against a held *shtar*: perhaps *dayanei Gola* hold that a *shomer* would not be believed to claim *hechzarti* even with a *migo*, and therefore the orphans cannot make that claim on their father's behalf; while *dayanei Eretz Yisrael* hold that the father would have been believed, and therefore the claim is made on behalf of the orphans.

### Orphan Exception

The *gemara* rejects this mapping. Everyone — including *dayanei Gola* — actually accepts Rav Chisda's ruling that a *shomer* is believed to claim *hechzarti* with a *migo*. The dispute is not about whether the father, were he alive, would be believed. He would be believed by all. The dispute is specific to the case of orphans.

### Father's Silence

The argument for *dayanei Gola* — that the creditor collects the full amount — rests on the following reasoning: *im ita de-parei meimar hava amar* — if the father had actually returned the *pikadon* half to the creditor, he would certainly have told his children. A father who knows that he returned a substantial sum and that the creditor still holds a *shtar* creating apparent liability would not leave his heirs in ignorance. He would have made a point of informing them so they could defend against any future claim. The fact that the orphans arrive knowing nothing of any such return is therefore itself evidential: the father's silence to them indicates that no return took place. The claim of *hechzarti* cannot be made on the orphans' behalf, because the circumstances that would have prevailed had it truly been returned make the orphans' ignorance essentially impossible.

The *dayanei Eretz Yisrael* — who permit collection of only half — counter with: *amar malach ha-maves hu de-ansei* — perhaps the angel of death is what prevented him. Perhaps the father died suddenly, without opportunity to inform his children. Or perhaps he was preoccupied with his illness and dying and simply did not manage to convey the relevant information in time. In this view, the father's failure to tell the children does not eliminate the possibility that the *pikadon* was returned; we therefore remain willing to make the claim of return on the orphans' behalf, which shields the *pikadon* half from collection.

It is worth noting that the *gemara* does not consider making the alternative claim of *ne'ensu* on behalf of the orphans — arguing that some accident befell the *pikadon* — even though everyone agrees the father would have been believed on such a claim. *Tosafot* explains: we do not make claims on behalf of orphans regarding events that are not *shchiach*, not commonly occurring. An accidental loss of a deposit is sufficiently uncommon that we will not proactively assert it on orphans' behalf as a basis for exempting the creditor.

### Halakha: Half Collection

The *gemara* now draws together the strands and arrives at a halakhic conclusion. Rav Huna bar Avin formulates a summary: in the case of a *pikadon* with a *shtar*, where the depositary claims *hechzarti*, he is believed (*ne'eman*) — understood as believed with a *shvua*, as established above. But in the *iska* case brought against orphans — *shtra ki hai yatmei* — the creditor takes an oath and collects the full amount. The *gemara* challenges this apparent inconsistency: if a depositary is believed against a *shtar* to say he returned it, why can't we make that same claim for the orphans regarding the *pikadon* half, thus blocking the full collection? The *gemara* answers: *shani hasam* — that case is different, precisely for the reason articulated: had the father truly returned the deposit while the creditor held a *shtar*, he would have told his children. Their ignorance defeats the claim.

Rava rules: *hilchesa kidayanei Gola, nishba ve-goveh mechtza* — the *halakha* follows the *dayanei Gola*, and the creditor takes an oath and collects half. Mar Zutra then states: *hilchesa kidayanei Gola* — we rule like the *dayanei Gola*. Ravina challenges Mar Zutra: but Rava explicitly said the creditor collects *mechtza* — half — while the *dayanei Gola* in the *braita* said he collects the full amount! Mar Zutra responds: *anan de-dayanei Gola ipcha masninan lah* — our version of the *braita* has the positions reversed. In our tradition, it is the *dayanei Gola* who say *nishba ve-goveh mechtza*, and the *dayanei Eretz Yisrael* who say he collects the full amount. Everyone is in agreement, then, that the *halakha* is *nishba ve-goveh mechtza* — the creditor takes an oath and collects half.

The *gemara* thus concludes on a harmonious note. The depositary is believed — with a *migo* and a *shvua* — to claim that he returned a *pikadon*, even against a held *shtar*; the continued presence of the *shtar* does not rise to the level of *makom edim*. The orphan case is an exception: because a father who had genuinely returned the *pikadon* would have ensured his children knew of it, their ignorance undercuts the claim of return, and the creditor collects the loan half of the *iska* in full. The *pikadon* half he cannot collect, the claim of return being preempted by the father's silence, and the *halakha* accordingly is that he swears and collects half.
