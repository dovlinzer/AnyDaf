# Zevachim 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pigul & Blood Apps

### Perek 4 Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, שֶׁנָּתַן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת – כִּיפֵּר. וּבְחַטָּאת – שְׁתֵּי מַתָּנוֹת. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אַף חַטָּאת שֶׁנְּתָנָהּ מַתָּנָה אַחַת – כִּיפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say:** With regard to **all** the offerings **whose** blood is to be **placed on the external altar,** even those that require that the blood be sprinkled on two opposite corners of the altar so that it will run down each of its four sides, in a case **where** the priest **placed** the blood on the altar **with** only **one placement, he facilitated atonement. And in** the case of **a sin offering,** which requires four placements, one on each of the four corners of the altar, at least **two placements** are necessary to facilitate atonement. **And Beit Hillel say: Even** with regard to **a sin offering,** in a case **where** the priest **placed** the blood with **one placement, he facilitated atonement** after the fact.

We have just begun the fourth *perek* of Zevachim and pick up in the *gemara* two lines from the bottom of 36b. The discussion remains centered on *pigul*, but in order to address the more nuanced cases that arise in that context, the *gemara* introduced the debate between *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai*.

### Hillel vs Shammai

The question at issue is: what is the minimum number of *zrikot* — applications of blood — required for a *korban* to be valid? For all *korbanot* other than a *chatat*, even though the Torah prescribes *zorek saviv*, throwing the blood all around, which *Chazal* understand to mean two diagonally opposite corners (each corner serving two walls), *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* both agree that *bedi'avad* even a single *zrika* suffices — presumably even one wall. For a *chatat*, where the Torah prescribes placing the blood on the four corners of the altar with the finger — not throwing, but direct application — *Beit Shammai* requires a minimum of two *bedi'avad*, while *Beit Hillel* holds that one suffices there as well. With regard to the inner *chata'ot* applied on the inner *mizbe'ach*, all agree that all four applications are required.

### Kapara Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ אִם נָתַן אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ וְאֶת הַשְּׁנִיָּה חוּץ לִזְמַנָּהּ – כִּיפֵּר. וְאִם נָתַן אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה חוּץ לִזְמַנָּהּ וְאֶת הַשְּׁנִיָּה חוּץ לִמְקוֹמָהּ – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** since the priest facilitates atonement with one placement in all cases other than a sin offering according to Beit Shammai, and even in the case of a sin offering according to Beit Hillel, **if he placed the first** placement **in its proper manner, and the second** with the intent to eat the offering **beyond its** designated **time, he facilitated atonement.** Since the second placement is not indispensable with regard to achieving atonement, improper intent while performing that rite does not invalidate the offering. **And** based on the same reasoning, **if he placed the first** placement with the intent to eat the offering **beyond its** designated **time and** he placed **the second** placement with the intent to eat the offering **outside its** designated **area,** the second of which does not render an offering *piggul*, the offering is ***piggul*,** an offering disqualified by improper intention, **and one is liable** to receive **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*] for its** consumption. This is because the intent that accompanied the second placement does not supersede the *piggul* status of the offering.

This framework has direct consequences for *pigul*. Because *kapara* takes effect at the moment the first valid blood application is performed, the *korban* achieves its *kapara* status immediately. Any subsequent applications are no longer relevant to *pigul*: once the *korban* is valid, its *pigul* status is fixed, even though the remaining applications are still part of the *lechatchila* required *avoda*. Thus the entire *pigul* analysis turns on the first application — or, according to *Beit Shammai* in the case of *chatat*, the first two.

## V'Dam Zvachecha

### One App Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן לַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן, שֶׁנְּתָנָן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת – שֶׁכִּיפֵּר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְדַם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **with regard to** those offerings whose blood is **placed on the external altar, that** if the priest **placed** the blood **with one placement, he facilitated atonement? The verse states: "And the blood of your offerings shall be poured** upon the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 12:27), indicating that a single pouring of blood suffices.

The *gemara* now asks: from where do we derive that a single application on the outer *mizbe'ach* suffices? *Tanu Rabanan*: the rabbis taught — *minayin l'nitanim al hamizbe'ach hachitzon shenetanam b'matana achat shekiper?* — how do we know that if one placed them with a single *matana* it is effective? *Talmud lomar*: *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech al yesod mizbach ha'olah* — "you shall pour the blood of your sacrifices on the base of the *olah* altar," which is the outer *mizbe'ach*. The *pasuk* speaks in general terms — pour the blood of your sacrifices — and does not limit itself to any particular number of applications. It appears to be describing the fundamental act of *kapara* itself. From this we derive that even one application, even one pouring, suffices.

### Shirayim to Yesod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לְכׇל הַדָּמִים שֶׁטְּעוּנִים מַתַּן דָּם לַיְסוֹד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְדַם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״. נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״וְהַנִּשְׁאָר בַּדָּם יִמָּצֵה״ – שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּדָּם״; וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּדָּם״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does this** verse **come to** teach **this** *halakha*? **That** verse **is necessary for that which is taught** in another *baraita* discussing a different matter: **From where** is it derived **that all** the **blood** that is left over from an offering after the requisite placement **requires placement of blood on the base** of the altar? **The verse states: "And the blood of your offerings shall be poured** upon the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 12:27). The Gemara answers: The *tanna* of the first *baraita* **derives** this **from** the verse cited **by Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** with regard to a verse discussing a bird sin offering: **"And the rest of the blood shall be squeezed out** at the base of the altar; it is a sin offering" (Leviticus 5:9). **As** there is **no** need for **the verse to state "of the blood,"** because the entire verse is talking about the blood, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states "of the blood"?**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this reading. *V'hai l'hachi hu d'ata?* — is that really what this *pasuk* comes to teach? Perhaps the *pasuk* is needed for an entirely different *halacha*, as the *gemara* cites from a *baraita*: *minayin l'chol hadamim shete'unin matan dam l'yesod?* — from where do we know that the leftover blood of all *korbanos* must be poured at the *yesod*, the base of the altar?

The Torah explicitly states this only in the context of *chatat*; it is not stated for other *korbanos*. One might note — and this was a suggestion offered by Rabbi Yitzchak Aaron — that the reason the Torah omits this by other *korbanos* is that for those offerings the *p'shat* of the relevant *pesukim* suggests that all the blood is thrown around the *mizbe'ach* (*v'zarku bnei aharon et hadam al hamizbe'ach saviv*), leaving no *shirayim*. Only by *chatat*, where blood is applied to four specific points with the finger, is there obviously going to be blood left over. But the *gemara* still needs to know the source for pouring *shirayim* to the *yesod* for all *korbanos*. *Talmud lomar: v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* — pour the blood of your sacrifices. This would then be read not as describing the act of *kapara* but as referring to the remainder after the primary application: all leftover blood must be poured at the base. If so, the *pasuk* cannot simultaneously be teaching us that one application suffices.

### Chatas Ha'of

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא לָמַדְנוּ אֶלָּא לַנִּיתָּנִין מַתַּן אַרְבַּע – שֶׁטְּעוּנִין מַתַּן דָּמִים לַיְסוֹד; שְׁאָר דָּמִים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַנִּשְׁאָר בַּדָּם יִמָּצֵה״ – שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּדָּם״; וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּדָּם״? לִימֵּד עַל כׇּל הַדָּמִים, שֶׁטְּעוּנִין מַתַּן דָּמִים לַיְסוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Since we** have **learned only about** those offerings whose blood is **placed** on the altar with **four placements,** i.e., outer sin offerings, **that they require placement of the** leftover **blood on the base** of the altar, **from where** is it derived that the same applies to the leftover **blood** of all the **other** offerings? **The verse states: "And the rest of the blood shall be squeezed out** at the base of the altar; it is a sin offering" (Leviticus 5:9). **Since** there is **no** need for **the verse to state "of the blood,"** because the entire verse is talking about the blood, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states "of the blood"?** This **teaches that all the** leftover **blood** from all the offerings **requires placement of blood on the base** of the altar.

The *gemara* responds: the source for the *halacha* that *shirayim* of all *korbanos* go to the *yesod* can be derived instead from the *pasuk* concerning *chatat ha'of* — the bird *chatat* — where the blood is squeezed out against the wall of the *mizbe'ach* and runs down to the base. The *pasuk* there reads: *v'hanishar badam yimatze* — and what remains of the blood shall be squeezed out. The Torah could have written simply *v'hanishar*, "what remains," without the word *badam*, "of the blood." As Rabbi (Rebbi) teaches in a *baraita*: *she'ein talmud lomar badam, u'mah talmud lomar badam?* — the word *badam* was not needed and its presence must therefore be teaching something. Before the additional word is cited, the *baraita* notes that from the explicit text one might only have known that *nitanim matan arba*, those offerings requiring four blood applications and requiring *shirayim* to the *yesod*, are subject to this rule. *Sha'ar damim minayin?* — from where do we know it applies to all other blood offerings? *Talmud lomar: v'hanishar badam yimatze* — the extra word *badam* generalizes the principle beyond the bird offering. Thus *kol hadamim shete'unin matan damim l'yesod* — for all *korbanos*, any remaining blood must be poured at the base. The *halacha* of *shirayim* to the *yesod* can accordingly be derived from *chatat ha'of*, freeing the *pasuk* of *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* to teach that one application suffices.

### Multiple Teachings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי לְהָכִי הוּא דְּאָתָא?! מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לַנִּיתָּנִין בִּזְרִיקָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן בִּשְׁפִיכָה – יָצָא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְדַם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still,** does **this** verse, Deuteronomy 12:27, **come to** teach **this** *halakha*, that if a priest placed the blood with one placement, he facilitated atonement? **That** verse **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **with regard to** those offerings whose blood is supposed to be **placed by** means of **sprinkling** from a certain distance from the altar, **that** if the priest **placed** the blood **by pouring** it from up close **he has fulfilled** his obligation? **The verse states: "And the blood of your offerings shall be poured** upon the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 12:27).

The *gemara*, however, is still not satisfied. *V'akti l'hachi hu d'ata?* — even now, is that all *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* comes to teach? Perhaps it is needed for yet another *halacha*, as a further *baraita* states: *minayin l'nitanim b'zrika shenetanan b'shficha yatza?* — from where do we know that for offerings whose blood is supposed to be thrown (*zrika*), if one instead poured (*shficha*) it at the base, one has nonetheless fulfilled the obligation? *Talmud lomar: v'dam zvacheicha yishafech*. This is a considerable *chiddush*: the blood of a normal *korban* is to be thrown at the wall of the altar, yet if one poured it at the base instead, one is *yotzei*. One might argue that this makes sense given that the lower half of the altar and the base are both areas where blood goes, but nonetheless it is a significant ruling. If this is what *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* is teaching — that *shficha* serves as an alternative to *zrika* — then it is not available to teach that one application suffices.

## Shficha vs Zrika

### Shficha Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: לֹא זְרִיקָה בִּכְלַל שְׁפִיכָה, וְלֹא שְׁפִיכָה בִּכְלַל זְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the ruling in this *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, while the *tanna* of the earlier *baraita* **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says: Sprinkling is not included in pouring, and** similarly **pouring is not included in sprinkling.** Rather, these are separate actions performed under different circumstances, and one does not fulfill his obligation to sprinkle the blood by pouring it. Therefore, the verse is available to him to teach that if a priest placed the blood with one placement, he facilitated atonement.

The *gemara* addresses this by noting that *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* could hold *k'Rabbi Akiva*, who rules *lo zrika bikhlal shficha v'lo shficha bikhlal zrika* — throwing and pouring are mutually exclusive categories; each serves its own type of offering and cannot substitute for the other. According to *Rabbi Akiva*, there is no ruling that *shficha* works as an alternative to *zrika*, and therefore *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* need not be reserved for that teaching. It remains available to derive that one application suffices.

## Bechor Drashos

### Second Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: בֵּרַךְ בִּרְכַּת הַפֶּסַח – פָּטַר אֶת שֶׁל זֶבַח. שֶׁל זֶבַח – לֹא פָּטַר אֶת הַפֶּסַח. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא זוֹ פּוֹטֶרֶת זוֹ, וְלֹא זוֹ פּוֹטֶרֶת זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara demonstrates that this is the opinion of Rabbi Akiva: This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 121a): If **one recited the blessing over the Paschal offering,** which is: Who sanctified us with His mitzvot and commanded us to eat the Paschal offering, **he has** also **exempted** himself from reciting a blessing over the Festival peace **offering,** i.e., the peace offering that is eaten together with the Paschal offering. The blessing for the Festival peace offering of the fourteenth of Nisan is: Who sanctified us with His mitzvot and commanded us to eat the offering. If he recited the blessing over the Festival **offering, he has not exempted** himself from reciting a blessing over **the Paschal offering.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.** Rabbi Yishmael's reasoning is based on the *halakha* that the blood of the Paschal offering is poured, while the blood of the Festival offering is sprinkled. He maintains that sprinkling is included in the more general category of pouring, and therefore the blessing over the Paschal offering includes the Festival offering. Pouring is not included in the more limited category of sprinkling, and therefore reciting the blessing over the Festival offering does not exempt one from reciting a blessing over the Paschal offering. **The mishna continues: Rabbi Akiva says: This** blessing **does not exempt** one from reciting a blessing over **that one, and that** blessing **does not exempt** one from reciting a blessing over **this one.** Sprinkling is not included in pouring, and pouring is not included in sprinkling. Therefore, there is a separate blessing for each offering.

The *gemara*, however, continues to press the question. Even granting that *Rabbi Akiva*'s position removes one usage of the *pasuk*, the *gemara* cites yet another *baraita* that appears to need *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* for a different purpose. *Rabbi Yishmael* teaches: the *pasuk* in Parashat Korach — *ach bechor shor o bechor kesev o bechor ez lo tifdeh kodesh hem* — teaches us about the *bechor*, the firstborn animal, that its blood is thrown on the *mizbe'ach* and its *emurim* are burned, like any normal *korban*. But the Torah does not specify in other contexts what to do with the blood of *ma'aser* (tithe animals) or the *korban Pesach*. For *ma'aser*, the focus elsewhere is on it as a gift arrangement; for *Pesach*, the focus is on eating the meat. So from where do we know what to do with their blood and *emurim*? *Talmud lomar: v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* — "pour the blood of your sacrifices" — teaching that there are *korbanos* whose blood arrangements the Torah did not specify elsewhere, and this *pasuk* now tells us: pour it at the base.

This establishes the premise underlying the earlier discussion — that the *korban Pesach*'s blood is poured rather than thrown — but it also means the *pasuk* is occupied with teaching the *halacha* for *Pesach* and *ma'aser*, and cannot simultaneously teach that one application suffices.

## Shficha vs Zrika (II)

### Rabbi Akiva View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַכַּתִּי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״אַךְ בְּכוֹר שׁוֹר אוֹ בְכוֹר כֶּשֶׂב אוֹ בְכוֹר עֵז וְגוֹ׳״ – לָמַדְנוּ לִבְכוֹר, שֶׁטָּעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִים לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ. מַעֲשֵׂר וּפֶסַח מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְדַם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Still,** does **this** verse, Deuteronomy 12:27, **come to** teach **this** *halakha*, that if a priest placed the blood with one placement he facilitated atonement? That verse **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael says: Since it is stated: "But the firstborn of a bull, or the firstborn of a sheep, or the firstborn of a goat,** you shall not redeem; they are sacred. You shall sprinkle their blood upon the altar and shall burn their fat for an offering made by fire, for an aroma pleasing to the Lord" (Numbers 18:17), **we learned with regard to a firstborn** animal, which must be given to a priest to be offered on the altar in the Temple, **that it requires placement of** its **blood and sacrificial portions on the altar. From where** is it derived that the same applies to an animal **tithe** offering **and a Paschal offering? The verse states: "And the blood of your offerings shall be poured** upon the altar of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 12:27). This verse teaches that all offerings require placement of their blood on the altar.

To resolve this, the *gemara* says: *savar lah k'Rabbi Yosi HaGelili* — the author of our *mishnah* would hold like *Rabbi Yosi HaGelili*, whose position we will now examine. But first, the *gemara* takes up the source for *Rabbi Akiva*'s ruling that *zrika* and *shficha* are mutually exclusive.

### Pesachim Mishnah

The *gemara* cites the final *mishnah* in Pesachim. After the full treatment of *hilchot* *Seder*, *Kiddush*, and related matters, the last *mishnah* addresses the question of whether the *bracha* recited over one offering can exempt the obligation to recite a separate *bracha* for another. The relevant offerings are the *korban Pesach* itself and the *chagiga* of the fourteenth of Nisan — the *chagiga* brought when a group is large and people would otherwise eat the *Pesach* while ravenously hungry, so an accompanying *shelamim*-like offering is brought to be eaten first. When eating the *Pesach* one recites *asher kidshanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'echol hapesach*; when eating the *chagiga* one recites *asher kidshanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'echol et hazevach*.

The first opinion in the *mishnah* rules: *birach birchat hapesach patar et shel zevach; shel zevach lo patar et hapesach* — if one recited the *bracha* for the *Pesach*, one need not make a separate *bracha* for the *chagiga*, but if one recited the *bracha* for the *chagiga*, that does not exempt the *Pesach* and a separate *bracha* is required. *Divrei Rabbi Yishmael* — these are the words of *Rabbi Yishmael*. *Rabbi Akiva* disagrees: *lo zo poteret zo v'lo zo poteret zo* — neither *bracha* exempts the other; each requires its own.

### Pesach/Chagiga

One might naturally understand this dispute as turning on the principle of *ikar* and *tafel*: *Rabbi Yishmael* holds that the *Pesach* is the *ikar* of the evening, and the *chagiga* is subordinate to it, so the *bracha* on the *ikar* covers the *tafel*, while the reverse does not hold. *Rabbi Akiva* would reject this framing, requiring separate *brachot* in any case. *Rashi* indeed entertains this reading. However, the *gemara*'s preferred understanding — and the one adopted in Pesachim — is that the dispute reflects the precise question of *shficha* versus *zrika*.

The assumption is that the Torah never specifies what to do with the blood of the *korban Pesach* because the *Pesach* is fundamentally about eating the meat; accordingly, its blood is poured (*shficha*). The *chagiga*, by contrast, is a classic offering whose blood is thrown (*zrika*). According to *Rabbi Yishmael*, since *shficha* serves as a legitimate alternative to *zrika* for any offering that normally requires *zrika*, the *chagiga* too can be done by *shficha*. Thus the *bracha* over the *Pesach* — which is a *shficha* offering — covers the *chagiga* as well, because the *chagiga* too can be performed by *shficha*. But the *bracha* over the *chagiga*, which is fundamentally a *zrika* offering, does not cover the *Pesach*, because the *Pesach* can only be done by *shficha* and not by *zrika*.

*Rabbi Akiva*, by contrast, holds that *shficha* and *zrika* are mutually exclusive: the *Pesach* is only *shficha* and the *chagiga* is only *zrika*. Neither can substitute for the other, and therefore neither *bracha* exempts the other.

## Bechor Drashos (II)

### Yosi HaGelili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: ״דָּמוֹ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״דָּמָם״; ״חֶלְבּוֹ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״חֶלְבָּם״; לִימֵּד עַל בְּכוֹר, מַעֲשֵׂר וּפֶסַח, שֶׁטָּעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the *tanna* of the earlier *baraita*, who derives from Deuteronomy 12:27 the *halakha* that if a priest placed the blood with one placement he facilitated atonement, **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili maintains that the *halakha* that the blood and sacrificial parts of an animal tithe offering and a Paschal offering must be placed on the altar is derived from the aforementioned verse concerning a firstborn animal: "You shall sprinkle their blood upon the altar and shall burn their fat for an offering made by fire, for an aroma pleasing to the Lord" (Numbers 18:17). This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*, that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: It is not stated** in this verse: **Its blood, but** rather: **"Their blood."** Similarly, **it is not stated: Its fat, but** rather: **"Their fat."** The plural form **teaches with regard to** the **firstborn** animal, which is mentioned explicitly in the verse, as well as with regard to the animal **tithe** offering **and** the **Paschal offering,** which have a level of sanctity similar to that of a firstborn animal, **that** each **requires placement of** the **blood and sacrificial portions on the altar.**

The *gemara* continues. *Rabbi Yishmael*'s use of *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* both to establish that the *Pesach*'s blood is done by *shficha* and to teach that *shficha* serves as an alternative to *zrika* raises a difficulty: both inferences are attributed to the same *pasuk*, yet the *mishnah* in Pesachim seems to assume *Rabbi Yishmael* relies on both simultaneously. The *gemara* resolves this with the comment *trei tanna'ei aliba d'Rabbi Yishmael* — there are two different *tanna'im* who transmit *Rabbi Yishmael*'s position, each using the *pasuk* for a different teaching. *Rashi* recognizes, however, that this resolution is not entirely satisfying, since the *tanna* who speaks about the *bracha* still presupposes both that the *Pesach* is *b'shficha* and that *shficha* can work for *zrika* offerings — both derived from the same *pasuk*. *Rashi* therefore concludes that the debate over the *bracha* in the *mishnah* in Pesachim is really about *ikar* and *tafel* and has nothing to do with *shficha* and *zrika*. This does not fit perfectly with the language of *trei tanna'ei*, but *Rashi* accepts the difficulty in order to resolve the deeper logical problem. The bottom line is that a great many *halachot* are being derived from this one *pasuk*, and while the *gemara* seems to treat them as mutually exclusive candidates, *Tosafot* works through how the various *halachot* might in fact be derived from other *pesukim* as well.

The *gemara* now turns to *Rabbi Yosi HaGelili*'s interpretation, which offers an entirely different reading of the *bechor* passage. *Damo lo ne'emar ela damam* — the *pasuk* in Parashat Korach does not say *et damo tizrok*, "its blood shall be thrown," in the singular, but rather *et damam*, "their blood," in the plural. Similarly, *chelbo lo ne'emar ela chelbam* — it says *chelbam*, "their fat," not *chelbo*, "its fat." The simple reading of the plural is that the three types of *bechor* mentioned — *bechor shor*, *bechor kesev*, and *bechor ez* — are all collectively addressed. But *Rabbi Yosi HaGelili* argues that the Torah could have used the singular throughout, since they are all one category of *korban bechor*, and the plural is therefore a deliberate expansion. *Limed habechor ma'aser u'fasach* — it teaches that not only *bechor*, which the *pasuk* mentions explicitly, but also *ma'aser* and *Pesach* — offerings for which the Torah never specifies what to do with their blood and *emurim* — are governed by the same rule. For all three, the blood is thrown on the *mizbe'ach* and the *emurim* are offered.

Thus, according to *Rabbi Yosi HaGelili*, the *bechor pasuk* in Korach teaches *zrika* for *ma'aser* and *Pesach*, derived from the plural *damam* and *chelbam*. This leaves *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* free to teach its other lessons, and *Beit Hillel* and *Beit Shammai* who need to derive that one application suffices can accordingly use it for that purpose — or, as we will see, they may derive it from *chatat* instead.

### Textual Tensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּמוֹקֵים לֵהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ בִּבְכוֹר – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְשָׂרָם יִהְיֶה לָּךְ״. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּמוֹקֵי לֵהּ נָמֵי בְּמַעֲשֵׂר וָפֶסַח – מַעֲשֵׂר וָפֶסַח בְּעָלִים אָכְלִי לֵיהּ; מַאי ״וּבְשָׂרָם יִהְיֶה לָּךְ״? אֶחָד תָּם וְאֶחָד בַּעַל מוּם; לִימֵּד עַל בְּכוֹר בַּעַל מוּם, שֶׁנִּיתָּן לַכֹּהֵן. שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ לוֹ בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question with regard to the disagreement about the interpretation of Numbers 18:17. **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who interprets the entire** verse as referring **to a firstborn** animal, and according to whom the plural form in the verse is referring to three such firstborns, that of a bull, that of a sheep, and that of a goat, **this** explains that **which is written** immediately afterward: **"And their meat shall be yours"** (Numbers 18:18), in the plural, i.e., the meat of these firstborn animals shall be eaten by the priests. **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who interprets** this verse as referring **also to** an animal **tithe** offering **and a Paschal offering,** there is a difficulty, as an animal **tithe** offering **and a Paschal offering are eaten by their owners,** not by the priests. If so, **what** is the meaning of the phrase **"and their meat shall be yours"?** The Gemara answers that the plural term "their meat," is referring not to an animal tithe offering or a Paschal offering, but to different types of firstborns, i.e., **both an unblemished** animal, whose blood is poured on the altar, **and a blemished** one, which is not brought as an offering on the altar. **This** verse **teaches that a blemished firstborn is given to a priest** as a gift, and that he may eat its meat. This is a novel concept, **as we have not found in the entire Torah** another *halakha* similar **to it,** where a blemished offering is given to the priests for consumption.

Having established *Rabbi Yosi HaGelili*'s reading, the *gemara* examines the rest of the *pasuk*. According to *Rabbi Yishmael*, the entire passage — *et damam tizrok al hamizbe'ach v'et chelbam taktir* — speaks only about *bechor*, with the plural reflecting the three species of *bechor* animal mentioned. The next *pasuk*, *u'vsaram yiyeh lach*, "and their flesh shall be yours," refers to the *kohanim* receiving the meat of the *bechor* — entirely coherent within a single-theme passage about *bechor* in the context of *matnot kehunah*.

According to *Rabbi Yosi HaGelili*, however, the passage addresses *bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *Pesach* together. The following *pasuk*, *u'vsaram yiyeh lach*, then presents a difficulty, since *ma'aser* and *Pesach* are not eaten by *kohanim* — *ma'aser* is eaten by the owners, and *Pesach* similarly. The *gemara* resolves this: *mah u'vsaram yiyeh lach? echad tam v'echad ba'al mum* — the plural of *u'vsaram yiyeh lach* is teaching a different extension. In the first *pasuk*, the plural *damam* and *chelbam* extends the blood-and-*emurim* rules to all three offerings. In the second *pasuk*, the plural *u'vsaram* teaches something about *bechor* itself: the *kohanim* receive the meat both from a *tam* — an unblemished *bechor* brought as a *korban* — and from a *bechor ba'al mum*, a blemished firstborn animal.

This latter point is itself a striking *halacha*. *Shelo matzinu lo bechol haTorah kulah* — as *Rashi* notes, we do not find elsewhere in the Torah what to do with a *bechor ba'al mum*. It cannot be redeemed — *lo tifdeh* is explicit — yet it cannot be offered. *Tosafot* sharpens this further: not only is there no other *pasuk* about it, but there is no other phenomenon in the Torah quite like it — something that is *kadosh* yet may be eaten in its state of *mum* without redemption. You need the plural *u'vsaram yiyeh lach* to tell you that this bizarre category exists and that the *kohen* eats the meat both of a *tam* (brought as a *korban*) and of a *ba'al mum* (eaten directly in its *kedushah* state). Both the *kosher*-for-offering and the blemished *bechor* are included.

### Yishmael vs Yosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״לְּךָ יִהְיֶה״ דְּסֵיפָא. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּמוֹקֵי לֵהּ נָמֵי בְּמַעֲשֵׂר וָפֶסַח; הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תִפְדֶּה כִּי קֹדֶשׁ הֵם״ – הֵם קְרֵיבִין, וְאֵין תְּמוּרָתָן קְרֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Yishmael,** who interprets this verse differently, from where does he derive this *halakha* that a blemished firstborn is given to a priest? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the phrase: "As the breast of waving and as the right thigh, **it shall be yours"** (Numbers 18:18), **which is the latter clause** of that same verse. The repetition of the phrase "shall be yours" serves to teach that even the meat of a blemished firstborn shall be eaten by the priests. § The Gemara raises another question: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who interprets** the verse as referring **also to** an animal **tithe** offering **and a Paschal offering, this is as it is written** in the same verse: **"You shall not redeem; they are sacred"** (Numbers 18:17). The word "they" indicates that only **they are sacrificed** on the altar, **but their substitutes are not sacrificed.**

In general, if one substitutes a non-sacred animal for one designated as an offering, both the original and the substitute are considered to be consecrated, and they are therefore sacrificed. But if one substitutes a non-sacred animal for a firstborn, an animal tithe offering, or a Paschal offering, the substitute is not sacrificed.

### Temura Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: תְּמוּרַת בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר – הֵן וּוְלָדָן וּוְלַד וְלָדָן עַד סוֹף כׇּל הָעוֹלָם, הֲרֵי הֵן כִּבְכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר, וְיֹאכְלוּ בְּמוּמָן לַבְּעָלִים. וּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי שֶׁתְּמוּרַת פֶּסַח קְרֵיבָה, וּתְמוּרַת פֶּסַח אֵינָהּ קְרֵיבָה, וְאֵין לִי לְפָרֵשׁ. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּמוֹקֵי לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ בִּבְכוֹר; מַעֲשֵׂר וָפֶסַח דְּלָא קְרֵיבָה תְּמוּרָתָן, מְנָא לֵיהּ? מַעֲשֵׂר גָּמַר ״עֲבָרָה״–״עֲבָרָה״ מִבְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Temura* 21a): With regard to **the substitute of a firstborn and** of an animal **tithe** offering, both **they,** the substitutes themselves, **and their offspring, and the offspring of their offspring, forever,** i.e., for all future generations, **are as** the **firstborn and** the animal **tithe** offering, respectively, **and** therefore **they are eaten in their blemished** state **by the owners;** but unlike the firstborn and the animal tithe offering themselves, they are not sacrificed on the altar. **And** likewise with regard to the substitute of a Paschal offering, **we learned** in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 96b) that **Rabbi Yehoshua says: I** have **heard** two rulings from my teachers: One ruling was **that the substitute of a Paschal offering is sacrificed** as a peace offering after Passover, **and** another ruling was that **the substitute of a Paschal offering is not offered** as a peace offering after Passover; **and I cannot explain** these apparently contradictory rulings, as I do not remember the circumstances to which each ruling applies. And as will be explained, if the substitution took place after the sacrifice of the Paschal offering, the animal is sacrificed, as it is considered the substitute of a peace offering, whereas if the substitution occurred before the sacrifice of the Paschal offering, it is not sacrificed, as it is the substitute of a Paschal offering. This indicates that the substitute of a Paschal offering is not sacrificed, just like the substitute of a firstborn is not sacrificed. **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who interprets the entire** verse as dealing **with a firstborn, from where does he** derive the *halakha* **that the substitute of** an animal **tithe** offering **and** of **a Paschal offering are not sacrificed?** The Gemara answers: With regard to an animal **tithe** offering, **he derives** this by means of a verbal analogy between the terms **passing,** and **passing** written **with regard to a firstborn.** Concerning a firstborn it is stated: "And you shall pass [*veha'avarta*] to the Lord all that opens the womb" (Exodus 13:12), and concerning an animal tithe it is stated: "Of whatever passes [*ya'avor*] under the rod" (Leviticus 27:32). From this verbal analogy it may be derived that just as the substitute of a firstborn is not sacrificed, so too, the substitute of an animal tithe offering is not sacrificed.

To ground this in explicit *halacha*, the *gemara* cites a *mishnah*: *tmurat bechor u'ma'aser* — if one makes a *temura* for a *bechor* or *ma'aser*, *hein u'valdeihen v'velad valdeihen ad sof kol ha'olam harei hen k'bechor u'ma'aser* — they and their offspring to all generations have the status of *bechor* and *ma'aser*. The consequence: *v'ye'achlu bemumam labe'alim* — they may only be eaten once they develop a *mum*; they cannot be offered as *korbanos*. They carry the *kedushah* of *bechor* and *ma'aser* but are never brought on the altar, only consumed after a blemish appears, like a *bechor ba'al mum*.

For *Pesach*, the *gemara* cites *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s statement: *shamati she'tmurat haPesach kreiva u'tmurat haPesach eina kreiva* — I have heard that some forms of *temurat haPesach* may be offered and others may not, *v'ein li l'faresh* — but I cannot explain the distinction. The *gemara* elsewhere resolves this: a *temura* of a *Pesach* made before Pesach — when the *Pesach* still retains its full *Pesach* identity — cannot be offered; a *temura* of a *Pesach* after Pesach — when the *Pesach* has effectively assumed the status of *shelamim* — may be offered as *shelamim*.

Now, where does *Rabbi Yishmael* derive these same *temura* rules for *ma'aser* and *Pesach*, since he reads *kodesh hem* as applying only to *bechor*? For *ma'aser*, the *gemara* answers: *gmar avara avara m'bechor* — a *gezeira shava*. The Torah uses a form of the root *ayin-beit-reish* (passing over) by *ma'aser* (*kol asher ya'avor tachat hashevet*) and by *bechor* (*v'ha'avarta kol peter rechem*). The *gezeira shava* links them and carries the *temura* rule from one to the other. For *Pesach*, the *pasuk* concerning *shelamim* states *im kesev hu makriv*, and the extra word *im* — whose necessity is not grammatically obvious — extends the rule: the *temura* of a *Pesach* after Pesach may be brought as *shelamim*. This implicitly establishes that the *temura* of a *Pesach* before Pesach — when *pesach hu*, "it is a *Pesach*," and only it may be brought — cannot be offered.

## Chatas Source

### Problem Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הָנָךְ תַּנָּאֵי דְּמַפְּקִי לֵיהּ לְהַאי ״דַּם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ״ לִדְרָשָׁא אַחֲרִינָא – הַאי כׇּל הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן שֶׁנְּתָנָן מַתָּנָה אַחַת שֶׁכִּיפֵּר, מְנָא לְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **all those *tanna'im* who derive a different exposition from this** verse: **"And the blood of your offerings shall be poured"** (Deuteronomy 12:27), **this** *halakha* that is recorded in the mishna, that with regard to **all** the offerings **whose** blood is to be **placed on the external altar,** in a case **where** the priest **placed** the blood on the altar with **one placement, he facilitated atonement, from where do they** derive this?

Having reviewed the full range of *drashot* drawn from *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* and the *bechor* passages, the *gemara* now poses the question in the other direction: *v'chol hanahu tanna'im d'mapkei lei l'hai dam zvacheicha yishapech l'drasha acharina* — all the *tanna'im* who use *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* to derive something else — where do they learn the fundamental *halacha* that a single *netina* suffices for the outer *mizbe'ach*? The *gemara* had raised this as a cliffhanger, and now addresses it directly.

### Chatas Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבְרִי לְהוּ כְּבֵית הִלֵּל, דְּאָמְרִי: אַף חַטָּאת שֶׁנְּתָנָהּ מַתָּנָה אַחַת – כִּיפֵּר; וְיָלְפִינַן כּוּלְּהוּ מֵחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Those *tanna'im* **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Hillel, who say: Even** with regard to **a sin offering,** in a case **where** the priest **placed** the blood with **one placement, he facilitated atonement** after the fact. **And they derive** the *halakha* applying to **all** the other offerings **from** that which applies to **a sin offering.**

The *gemara* answers: *savira lahu k'Beit Hillel d'amri af chatat she'nitna matana achat kiper, v'yalfinan kuleihu m'chatat* — they hold like *Beit Hillel*, who maintains that even for *chatat* a single application suffices, and from *chatat* we derive the same rule for all other *korbanos* by means of a *binyan av* or *kal vachomer*: if a single *netina* suffices for *chatat* — which explicitly requires four — then it certainly suffices for all other offerings. This will of course require us to understand where *Beit Hillel* themselves derive that one application suffices for *chatat*, and the *gemara* now turns to that question.

Before doing so, the *gemara* notes that this approach is not available to *Beit Shammai*, who holds that *chatat* requires two applications *bedi'avad*. *Chatat* cannot serve as the source for the other *korbanos* when it itself demands two. It is therefore specifically for *Beit Shammai* that *v'dam zvacheicha yishafech* must bear the teaching that one application suffices for the outer *mizbe'ach*. *Tosafot* raises a conceptual point here: there may be a principled reason not to derive the rule for other *korbanos* from *chatat* even according to *Beit Hillel*, since by those other *korbanos* the Torah demands *zorek saviv*, "all around," and one might argue that this is not merely a number but a conceptual requirement — the blood must have reached all four walls. Anything less than that is not *saviv* in any sense; it is not one-out-of-four, it is simply zero. By *chatat*, which specifies four points, doing one of the four is at least genuinely one-of-four. The *gemara*, however, does not pursue this reasoning and proceeds on the assumption that the rule transfers from *chatat* to all other offerings.

## Shammai 2 Apps

### Six Corners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? ״קַרְנוֹת״, ״קַרְנוֹת״, ״קַרְנוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן שֵׁשׁ; אַרְבָּעָה לְמִצְוָה וּשְׁתַּיִם לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Beit Shammai maintain that in the case of **a sin offering,** which requires four placements, at least **two placements** are necessary to facilitate atonement, whereas Beit Hillel hold that even in the case of a sin offering one placement suffices. **Rav Huna said: What is the reasoning** for the opinion **of Beit Shammai?** He explains: The verse states: "And the priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger, and put it upon the **corners** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:25). Subsequently, the verse states: "The priest shall take of its blood with his finger, and put it on the **corners** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:30), and an additional verse states: "The priest shall take of the blood of the sin offering with his finger, and put it on the **corners** of the altar" (Leviticus 4:34). As the minimum amount justifying the use of the plural form, i.e., in the word "corners," is two, one may conclude that **there are six** references to the corners of the altar **here. Four** of them are mentioned **for a mitzva,** meaning that the priest should place the blood on all four corners of the altar *ab initio*, **and** the other **two** are mentioned **to invalidate** the offering if he did not present the blood on at least two corners.

*Amar Rav Huna*: *mai ta'ama d'Beit Shammai?* — what is the reasoning of *Beit Shammai* that two applications of blood are required for *chatat bedi'avad*? The answer lies in counting the occurrences of the word *karnos* — corners — in the Torah's discussion of the *chatat* brought by individuals (in Parashat Vayikra, covering the *nasi* and the ordinary individual). The Torah says *karnos hamizbe'ach* in three separate places. Since each usage of *karnos* in the plural yields two, three instances of *karnos* yields six corners altogether. *Harei kan shesh* — here we have six.

### Shammai Math

From this number of six, *Beit Shammai* derives: *arba l'mitzvah u'shtayim l'akev* — four is the *mitzvah*, the standard requirement that is the full and proper performance of the *avoda*, and two is the *akeiv*, the minimum that is *me'akev*, without which the *korban* is invalid.

### Shina Principle

This analysis rests on a general principle in the laws of *kodshim*: *shina lo hakasuv l'akev* — if the Torah repeats an element of a procedure, that repetition signals that the element is *me'akev*. Absent repetition, even mandatory steps in the *avoda* are not necessarily *me'akev bedi'avad*. It would be counterintuitive to apply such logic outside of *kodshim* — one would never suggest that a *mitzvah* need not be fulfilled because the Torah mentioned it only once — but the principle is well-established in *kodshim*, where the elaborate multi-step procedures of the *avoda* lead the *gemara* to presume that not every step is absolutely indispensable unless it is expressly repeated. Given this background, *Beit Shammai* starts from the position that we cannot simply say all four are *me'akev*. With six total mentions, four serve as the *mitzvah* and two as the *akeiv*.

## Hillel 1 App

### Hillel Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל: ״קַרְנַת״, ״קַרְנַת״, ״קַרְנוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע; שָׁלֹשׁ לְמִצְוָה, אַחַת לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what is the reasoning for the opinion of **Beit Hillel?** The Gemara answers: The matter should be understood according to the written consonantal text, i.e., the way in which the words are actually spelled. The word **"corners"** is written plene, i.e., with a *vav*, in one of the verses, which means that it must be read in the plural form. In the other two verses, **"corners"** and **"corners"** are written deficient, i.e., without a *vav*, in a way that can be vocalized in the singular form, i.e., as *karnat*. Therefore, **there are four** references to corners **here. Three** of these references are written **to** indicate the placements of blood are performed only as **a mitzva,** i.e., they are performed *ab initio*, and the remaining **one** is written **to** indicate that its absence **invalidates** the offering, i.e., the offering is not valid if the blood was not placed against at least one corner of the altar.

*Beit Hillel* disagrees with how to count the corner-mentions. Where *Beit Shammai* reads all three occurrences of *karnos* as the full plural form — *karnos*, *karnos*, *karnos* — yielding two, two, two, for six, *Beit Hillel* focuses on the *masores*, the way the words are written in the *sefer Torah*. Two of the three instances are written without a *vav* — *karnat* — which, as a defective spelling, suggests the singular form in *smichus*, carrying a value of one each. Only one instance is written in the full plural *karnos*, giving two. According to *Beit Hillel*'s reading by the *masores*: *karnat*, *karnat*, *karnos* — one, one, two — yielding four altogether.

## Shammai 2 Apps (II)

### Gemara Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא כּוּלְּהוּ לְמִצְוָה! כַּפָּרָה בִּכְדִי לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to this explanation of Beit Hillel, why not **say** that **all of them are** written **for** the **mitzva** and none are to invalidate, i.e., that the blood must be presented on all four corners *ab initio*, but the offering atones after the fact even if the blood was not presented at all? The Gemara rejects this possibility: **We have not found** anywhere in the Torah an example of an offering in which **atonement** can be achieved **with no** placement of the blood.

The *gemara* raises an obvious question against *Beit Hillel*: *v'eima kuleihu l'mitzvah?* — why not say that all four are merely a *mitzvah*, the ideal performance, with nothing being *me'akev*? If the Torah did not repeat the requirement, why say even one is *me'akev*?

### Kapara Min

The *gemara* answers: *kapara b'chidi lo ashkechan* — we never find that *kapara* is achieved without any blood application whatsoever. While the principle of *shina lo hakasuv l'akev* would in theory lead us to say nothing is *me'akev*, we cannot conclude that the *korban* achieves *kapara* without any blood being placed at all. Something must be *me'akev*. Since the Torah did not repeat any specific number of applications as an absolute requirement, we set the floor at the minimum: one application is *me'akev*, and the remaining three are *mitzvah*.

## Hillel 1 App (II)

### Averaging Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּבֵית הִלֵּל: אַהֲנִי מִקְרָא, וְאַהְנִי מָסוֹרֶת; אַהֲנִי מִקְרָא – לְטַפּוֹיֵי חֲדָא, וְאַהֲנַי מָסוֹרֶת – לְבַצּוֹרֵי חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **this is the reason of Beit Hillel: The vocalized text** of the Torah, i.e., the way in which the words of the Torah are pronounced based on the tradition of what vowels the words contain, **is effective** in determining how the verses are to be expounded. **And** likewise, **the consonantal text** of the Torah, i.e., the way in which the words are actually written, **is** also **effective** in determining how the verses are to be expounded. The Gemara elaborates: **The vocalized text is effective in adding one** more corner **and the consonantal text is effective in subtracting one** corner. Consequently, the verse is interpreted as referring to five corners, four of which are necessary for the mitzva *ab initio*, and one of which is indispensable after the fact.

The *gemara* now presents an alternative formulation of *Beit Hillel*'s reasoning that is more elegant and arithmetically explicit. Rather than relying on the logical floor argument, *Beit Hillel* may be understood as follows: *hani mikra v'hani masoret* — we pay attention both to the *mikra*, the way the words are read aloud (*karnos*, *karnos*, *karnos*, giving six), and to the *masores*, the way they are written (*karnat*, *karnat*, *karnos*, giving four). Neither reading is simply discarded. The *mikra* adds one to the four, and the *masores* subtracts one from the six. The result: five. Arithmetically, we take both values (six and four), sum them, and divide by two, yielding five.

### Hillel Math

From the number five, *Beit Hillel* draws the same structural conclusion as *Beit Shammai* did from six: *arba l'mitzvah v'achas l'akev* — four is the *mitzvah*, and one is the *akeiv*. The *gemara* acknowledges that this is a more satisfying way to reach the conclusion, even if the arithmetic is unusual.

## Testing Method

### Tefillin Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״לְטֹטֶפֶת״, ״לְטֹטֶפֶת״, ״לְטוֹטָפוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע; אַהֲנִי קְרָא וְאַהֲנַי מָסוֹרֶת – חַמְשָׁה בָּתֵּי בָּעֵי לְמִיעְבַּד! סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: ״טַט״ בְּכַתְפִּי שְׁתַּיִם, ״פַּת״ בְּאַפְרִיקִי שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if that is so,** that this is the proper manner of expounding the verses, consider the case of the phylacteries of the head, about which it is stated: "And **for frontlets [*totafot*]** between your eyes" (Exodus 13:16), and: "They shall be **for frontlets [*totafot*]** between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 6:8), and subsequently: "They shall be **for frontlets [*totafot*]** between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 11:18). The word *totafot* is written once in plene form, i.e., with a *vav*, indicating the plural form, and twice in a deficient form, i.e., without a *vav*, indicating the singular. Accordingly, **there are four** frontlets **here,** and from here the Sages derive that the phylacteries of the head consist of four compartments, which together form a cube. Why not say that **the vocalized text,** read in the plural, **is effective,** i.e., that it requires six compartments, **and** likewise, **the consonantal text,** read in the singular, **is effective,** i.e., it requires four compartments, and therefore one should be **required to prepare five compartments** for the phylacteries of the head? The Gemara answers that Beit Hillel **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that the requirement to have four compartments in the phylacteries of the head is not derived from the number of times that the word *totafot* is mentioned, but rather from an exposition of the word *totafot* itself: ***Tot* in** the **Katpi** language means **two,** and ***pat* in** the **Afriki** language means **two,** for a total of four.

The *gemara* now stress-tests the averaging methodology. *Ela me'ata* — if we accept this approach, consider the case of *tefillin*. The Torah mentions *l'totafot bein einecha* three times. Two of the three occurrences are written without a *vav* — *l'totafet*, *l'totafet* — and one with a *vav* — *l'totafot*. By the *masores* this yields two, two, two — wait, more carefully: if we read the defective spelling as singular (value: one) and the full spelling as plural (value: two), we get one, one, two — four. If we read all three by the *mikra* as the plural form *l'totafot*, we get two, two, two — six. Adding four and six and dividing by two yields five. *Chamisha batei ba'ei l'me'avad* — should we say five compartments are required for *tefillin*?

### Totafot Etymology

The *gemara* answers: *savar lah k'Rabbi Akiva* — *Beit Hillel* can hold like *Rabbi Akiva*, who teaches *tat b'Katfi shtayim, pat b'Afriki shtayim* — the word *totafet* itself carries the meaning of four, because *tat* in one language means two and *pat* in another language (African) means two, making the word itself encode the number four. Since the word *totafot* inherently means four, the averaging method does not apply here. We do not need to count and average the individual occurrences; the meaning of the word itself settles the number at four.

### Sukkah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״בְּסֻכַּת״, ״בְּסֻכַּת״, ״בַּסּוּכּוֹת״ – אַהֲנִי מִקְרָא וְאַהֲנַי מָסוֹרֶת, חֲמֵשׁ דַּפְנָתָא בָּעֵי לְמִיעְבַּד! הָתָם חַד קְרָא – כּוּלֵּיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ, וְחַד – לִסְכָכָה; אֲתַאי הִלְכְתָא, וּגְרַעְתַּהּ לִשְׁלִישִׁית וְאוֹקֵימְתַּהּ אַטֶּפַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection to this method of exposition: **If that is so,** consider the case of a *sukka*, about which it is stated: **"In *sukkot* [*basukkot*]** shall you reside seven days; all that are homeborn in Israel shall reside **in *sukkot* [*basukkot*].** So that your future generations will know that I caused the children of Israel to reside **in *sukkot* [*basukkot*]** when I took them out of the land of Egypt: I am the Lord your God" (Leviticus 23:42–43). Two instances of the word *basukkot* are written in a deficient manner, i.e., without a *vav*, indicating the singular, and one instance is written in plene form, i.e., with a *vav*, indicating the plural form. The Sages derived from here that a *sukka* must have four walls. Why not say that **the vocalized text,** read in the plural, **is effective,** requiring six walls, **and** likewise, **the consonantal text,** read in the singular, **is effective,** requiring four walls, and therefore one should be **required to build** a *sukka* with **five walls?** The Gemara answers: **There, one** of the five indicated by the **verse is** needed **entirely for** the mitzva **itself,** i.e., to teach the basic *halakha* that one must dwell in a *sukka*. **And** another **one** of the five is needed **to** teach that a *sukka*, as is indicated by its name, must have **a covering [*sekhakha*].** Accordingly, there are three left, alluding to the requirement that a *sukka* must have three walls. The Gemara adds that **the *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **comes and reduces the** size of the **third** wall, teaching that it need not be complete, **and sets** its minimum length **at** only **one handbreadth.**

The *gemara* presses further. *Ela me'ata, basukat, basukat, basukot* — the Torah mentions *sukkot* three times in the relevant passage. By the *masores*, two of the three occurrences are written without a *vav* — *basukat* — suggesting a value of one each; the full spelling *basukot* gives two. That yields one, one, two — four. By the *mikra*, all three are read as the plural *basukot*, yielding two, two, two — six. Adding four and six, dividing by two: five. *Chamisha defanot ba'ei lemivnei* — should we require five walls for a *sukkah*? That is clearly not the *halacha*; three walls suffice.

### Sukkah Resolution

The *gemara* resolves this: *hatam chad kra kuleh legufeh* — one of the three mentions of *sukkot* is needed entirely for its plain meaning, simply to establish the basic *mitzvah* of dwelling in a *sukkah* (*basukkot teshvu shivat yamim*). That mention is consumed by the fundamental *pshat* of the text and does not enter the counting. We are left with *sukat* and *sukot* — the defective and the full spelling — giving four by the *masores* and two by the *mikra*... wait: *sukat* (one) and *sukot* (two): by the *masores*, three; by the *mikra*, *sukot* and *sukot*, four. Adding three and four gives seven, dividing by two gives three and a half. The *gemara* notes that you cannot work with a half. And in a sense this works out neatly: one for *sechach* and two-and-a-half for walls — a non-integer that cannot yield a clean requirement. The *halacha lemoshe misinai* then reduces one full wall to a *tefach*, yielding the familiar rule of two full walls and one *tefach*.

One might ask why the first mention is used up for *pshat* here but not in the cases of *karnos* or *totafot*. *Tosafot* raises precisely this question, acknowledging that it is difficult to determine with precision when the *gemara* regards the first mention as consumed by *pshat* and when it allows counting to begin from the first occurrence.

### Yoledet Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״וְטָמְאָה שְׁבֻעַיִם״ – ״שִׁבְעִים״; אַהֲנִי קְרָא וְאַהֲנַי מָסוֹרֶת – אַרְבְּעִים וּתְרֵין בָּעֲיָא לְמֵיתַב! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב ״כְּנִדָּתָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises yet another objection: **If that is so,** that both the vocalized and the consonantal texts are taken into account, consider the case of a woman who gives birth to a female child. Concerning this woman the verse states: "But if she bear a female child, **then she shall be impure two weeks [*shevu'ayim*],** as in her menstruation" (Leviticus 12:5). The way that the word is written allows it to be read as ***shivim*,** seventy. Why not say that **the vocalized text** of the Torah **is effective,** teaching us that the woman is ritually impure for fourteen days, **and the consonantal text is** also **effective,** teaching us that she is impure for seventy days, and, therefore, **she** should be **required to sit** in a state of ritual impurity for **forty-two** days, which is the halfway point between fourteen and seventy days? The Gemara answers: **There it is different,** and the consonantal text is disregarded entirely, **as it is written: "As in her menstruation,"** and a menstruating woman is impure for one week. Evidently, the verse is speaking in terms of weeks, and is not alluding to the number seventy at all.

The *gemara* raises one more case. *Ela me'ata, vetame'ah shevu'ayim* — by a woman who gives birth to a girl, the Torah states she shall be *tame'ah shevu'ayim*, for two weeks. But the word is written without a *vav* — *shivi'im* — which, taken as written, would mean seventy days. If we apply the averaging method: fourteen (two weeks) and seventy, summing to eighty-four, divided by two gives forty-two. *Ahni kra v'ahni mesoret, arba'im v'tarti ba'ei lemesav* — would this woman have to wait forty-two days?

### Yoledet Resolved

The *gemara* answers: *shani hatam d'chtiv kenidatah* — that case is different because the *pasuk* adds *kenidatah*, "like her *niddah*." Her *niddah* period is a week, and the comparison fixes the unit of counting: *shevu'ayim* means two of those weekly units, i.e., two weeks. The word *kenidatah* anchors the meaning of *shevu'ayim* and prevents any alternative reading from the defective spelling. The averaging method therefore cannot be applied here.

The *gemara* leaves us at this point with two accounts of *Beit Hillel*'s position on *chatat*: either the number four is derived from the *masores* alone and the floor argument (*kapara b'chidi lo ashkechan*) brings us to one *me'akev*; or, more elegantly, the averaging of *mikra* and *masores* yields five, which divides naturally into four for *mitzvah* and one for *akev*. For *Beit Shammai*, the derivation proceeds through three occurrences of the full plural *karnos*, yielding six, which divides into four for *mitzvah* and two for *akev*.
