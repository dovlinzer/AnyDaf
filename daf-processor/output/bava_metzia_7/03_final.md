## Hamotzi/Takfo Kohen

### Hamotzi Framework

Yesterday's discussion concluded with an important question about the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav harayah*: does it mean that in a case of doubt we simply throw up our hands as a *beit din* and decline to act — leaving the matter as an unresolved question mark — or does it mean that we are actually rendering a ruling, at least tentatively, that the object belongs to the person currently in possession? The practical difference is significant. If we are merely declining to intervene, then whoever manages to grab the disputed item next would be in the same position as the first holder, and the object could ping-pong back and forth indefinitely. But if we are actually ruling that the current possessor is the owner, then the other party who grabs it away would be required to return it.

### Takfo Kohen Principle

This is the question of *takfo kohen ein motzi'in miyado*. The *Gemara* concluded that we adopt the second position: we are indeed ruling, as a matter of *dinei mamonos*, that the object belongs to the person in whose hands it currently rests. If the other party were to seize it, he would be compelled to return it. This is distinct from the perspective of *issur*, however, where the matter may remain unresolved — which is why, in the relevant cases, the object would still retain *kedushas bechor*.

### Ma'aser Behemah Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: מַאי סְפֵיקוֹת? אִילֵּימָא סְפֵק בְּכוֹרוֹת, ״יִהְיֶה קֹדֶשׁ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא שֶׁכְּבָר קָדוֹשׁ. אֶלָּא סְפֵק פִּדְיוֹן פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר, וְכִדְרַב נַחְמָן. דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ עֲשָׂרָה סְפֵק פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹר בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – מַפְרִישׁ עֲלֵיהֶן עֲשָׂרָה שֵׂיִין, וּמְעַשְּׂרָן, וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: What are** these animals of **uncertain** status that that are subject to tithe according to the mishna? **If we say** that the reference is to animals whose status as **firstborns** is **uncertain, the Merciful One states:** "The tenth **shall be sacred** to the Lord," indicating that the tithe animal becomes sacred only when it is designated as tithe, from which it is inferred: **But not** an animal **that is already sacred** for a different reason. Therefore, since an animal whose status as firstborn is uncertain is already considered sacred due to the uncertainty, the sanctity of animal tithe would not apply to it. **Rather,** the mishna must be referring to a case of **an uncertain redemption of a firstborn donkey,** i.e., a lamb used as redemption for a donkey whose status as firstborn is uncertain. **And** this is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Naḥman, as Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: An Israelite who has ten donkeys** whose status as **firstborn** is **uncertain in his home separates ten lambs** to redeem them, **and tithes** the lambs, separating one as a tithe, **and they** all **belong to him,** as a priest cannot prove that he is entitled to any of the ten.

With that framework established, the *Gemara* turns to the specific scenario that had been under discussion: what is the case of *sfekos* in which one takes *ma'aser behemah* from an animal of doubtful status? *Rav Achai mi-Difti* asks *Ravina*: what exactly is the scenario in which a *Yisrael* who has a doubtfully-firstborn animal in his possession would bring it together with his other animals and take *ma'aser behemah*?

One might suggest the case involves a *safek bechor* — an animal of doubtful firstborn status. But that presents an immediate problem: the Torah says the tenth animal shall be holy, and not something that is already holy. If the animal might already possess *kedushas bechor*, how could it be included among the others for purposes of *ma'aser*?

The answer is rather that the case involves *safek pidyon peter chamor* — a donkey of uncertain firstborn status that was redeemed with a sheep. In such a case, the sheep used for redemption does not carry *kedushas bechor*, even if the donkey was definitively a *peter chamor*; in that case, the sheep would merely be money owed to the *kohen*, without any intrinsic *kedusha*. Since the *Yisrael* is presumed to be the owner, and since either way the sheep carries no *kedusha*, it can be included among his flock and counted toward *ma'aser behemah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ עֲשָׂרָה סְפֵק פִּטְרֵי חֲמוֹר בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – מַפְרִישׁ עֲלֵיהֶן עֲשָׂרָה שֵׂיִין, וּמְעַשְּׂרָן, וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This is precisely the ruling of **Rav Naḥman** in the name of **Rabba bar Avuha:** **An Israelite who has ten donkeys** whose status as **firstborn** is **uncertain in his home separates ten lambs** to redeem them, **and tithes** the lambs, separating one as a tithe, **and they** all **belong to him,** as a priest cannot prove that he is entitled to any of the ten.

This is precisely the ruling of *Rav Nachman* in the name of *Rabba bar Avuha*: if a *Yisrael* has ten animals that were used to redeem ten doubtful *peter chamor* cases, he separates ten sheep and redeems each doubtful donkey with a sheep. He then takes *ma'aser behemah* from all ten sheep, and they are all his — the nine that remain after *ma'aser* are his, and the tenth that is designated *ma'aser behemah* also belongs to the owner. The *kohen* may have a monetary claim to some of these sheep, but since *hamotzi mechavero alav harayah* leaves the *Yisrael* as the presumed owner, and since there is no *kedusha* on the sheep at all, the *ma'aser* is valid. Crucially, this only works if we maintain that *takfo kohen ein motzi'in miyado* — that we are ruling the *Yisrael* is the presumed owner, not merely suspending judgment.

## Bathhouse Sanctification

### Bathhouse Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ דְּמַסּוּתָא? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין: הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא בֵּי רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַב חִסְדָּא בֵּי רַב הוּנָא, וּפַשְׁטַהּ מֵהָא דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כׇּל מָמוֹן שֶׁאֵין יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ בְּדַיָּינִין, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ – אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter **of the bathhouse? Come** and **hear** a conclusion, **as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin said: There was** a similar **incident** that was brought before **the school of Rav Ḥisda, and Rav Ḥisda** brought the case before **the school of Rav Huna, and** Rav Huna **resolved** the issue **based on that which Rav Naḥman says:** With regard to **any property that** one **cannot recover** from the possession of another party **by legal process,** if **he consecrated it** while it was in the possession of the other party the **consecration is not** valid.

The *Gemara* now returns to a case that had been introduced earlier in the discussion: the *masuta*, the bathhouse, where one party had sanctified the property while the other remained silent. The question had been whether the act of sanctification functions like physically seizing the object, and whether the other party's silence constitutes an admission that the sanctifying party is the true owner. The *Gemara* now asks: *mai hava ala de-masuta* — what was the final outcome of that bathhouse case?

*Rav Chiya bar Avin* relates that a case of this kind came before *Rav Chisda*, who forwarded the question to *Rav Huna*, and *Rav Huna* resolved it.

### Rav Nachman Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ בְּדַיָּינִין, הִקְדִּישׁוֹ – קָדוֹשׁ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אַפְּקֵיהּ. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גָּזַל וְלֹא נִתְיָיאֲשׁוּ הַבְּעָלִים – שְׁנֵיהֶם אֵינָם יְכוֹלִין לְהַקְדִּישׁוֹ. זֶה – לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְזֶה – לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rav Naḥman's statement: By inference, is it so that if **one can recover** the property from the other party **by legal process,** and **he consecrated it,** the **consecration** is valid, **although he has not** yet **recovered it? But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** In a case where one **robbed** another of an item **and the owner did not despair** of retrieving it, **neither** the owner nor the robber **can consecrate it; this** one, the robber, **because it is not his, and that** one, the owner, **because it is not in his possession?** The indication is that one cannot consecrate even his own item if it is not in his possession.

The resolution is based on a principle stated by *Rav Nachman*: *kol mamon she-eino yachol lehotzio be-dayanim, hekdesho eino kadosh* — any property that one cannot extract through the courts, one's act of sanctifying it does not render it holy. Since in the bathhouse case neither party could prove ownership in court, the act of sanctification was legally meaningless. And if the sanctification was meaningless, the other party's silence carries no significance either — there was no reason for him to respond to a legally ineffective act. Therefore, the *kedusha* did not take effect.

This prompts an immediate question from the *Gemara*: are we saying that if one *can* prove ownership in court, then sanctifying property while it is still physically in another's hands *does* take effect — even before actually extracting it? But *Rav Yochanan* ruled explicitly that if someone steals an object and the owners have not yet despaired, neither party can sanctify it: the thief cannot because it is not his, and the owner cannot because *eino birshuto* — it is not presently under his physical control, even though he would eventually be able to recover it through the courts.

### Movables vs. Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ בְּמַסּוּתָא מִטַּלְטְלִין עָסְקִינַן? בְּמַסּוּתָא מְקַרְקְעֵי עָסְקִינַן, דְּכִי יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיאָה בְּדַיָּינִין, בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Did you think** that in the case of the bathhouse **we are dealing with a movable bath?** No, **we are dealing with a bath** that is excavated in the **ground,** in which case **once** its owner **can recover it by legal process** there is no need to take possession of it, as it already **exists in his possession.**

The *Gemara* resolves this tension by clarifying what kind of bathhouse is under discussion. One might have assumed the *masuta* refers to a portable barrel-type vessel used for bathing — a movable object. In that case, *Rav Yochanan*'s principle would apply: even if one could prove ownership in court, since it is physically in another's possession, the sanctification would not work. But the *Gemara* clarifies: *be-masuta de-mekarkei askinan* — we are discussing a bathhouse that is a fixed structure, embedded in the ground. With regard to land, the concept of *eino birshuto* does not apply. Since one cannot physically carry land away, the mere fact that someone else is using or sitting on your property does not remove it from your *reshus*. Once you can prove it is yours, it is considered to be in your domain. Therefore, if one could prove ownership, sanctification of a fixed bathhouse would take effect. But in this case, since ownership could not be established, the sanctification was void.

### Silence as Admission

The upshot for the broader question is clear: the other party's silence in the bathhouse case cannot be read as an admission of the sanctifying party's ownership. The sanctification carried no legal weight, and therefore the silence in response to it carries no legal weight either. Had the sanctifying party been able to establish ownership in court, the calculus might have been different — but absent that, the entire act was, as the *Gemara* puts it, meaningless.

## Garment Division

### Rav Tachlifa Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר מַעְרְבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שְׁנַיִם אֲדוּקִים בְּטַלִּית – זֶה נוֹטֵל עַד מָקוֹם שֶׁיָּדוֹ מַגַּעַת, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל עַד מָקוֹם שֶׁיָּדוֹ מַגַּעַת, וְהַשְּׁאָר חוֹלְקִין בְּשָׁוֶה. מַחְוֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: וּבִשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Taḥalifa from the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **taught** this *baraita* **before Rabbi Abbahu:** If **two** people are **grasping a garment, this** one **takes up to where his hand reaches, and that** one **takes up to where his hand reaches, and they divide the remainder,** the part of the garment that is in the grasp of neither, **equally. Rabbi Abbahu indicated** by means of a hand gesture that Rav Taḥalifa should add: **And** this is **with** the proviso that they take **an oath.**

*Rav Tachlifa bar Maarava* taught a *braita* before *Rabbi Abbahu*: if two people are holding onto a *tallis*, each one takes up to wherever his hand reaches, and the remainder is divided equally. This seems to be a significant departure from the *Mishnah*, which simply states they divide it equally without distinguishing based on how much each person is physically holding.

*Rabbi Abbahu* signals his response — *Rashi* explains this as pointing heavenward — indicating that while indeed one keeps what is in his hand and the division is therefore not necessarily equal, the requirement of an oath (*shvuah*) still applies, lest people simply grab onto others' garments and claim ownership. The *shvuah* is an independent concern that is not resolved merely by the physical distribution of possession.

### Fringes Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין, דְּקָתָנֵי דְּפָלְגִי בַּהֲדָדֵי, וְלָא קָתָנֵי זֶה נוֹטֵל עַד מְקוֹם שֶׁיָּדוֹ מַגַּעַת, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דִּתְפִיסִי בְּכַרְכְּשָׁתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** then **how can you find** a case where the *halakha* in **the mishna** applies? **As** the mishna **teaches that they divide** the garment **between them, and does not teach that this** one **takes up to where his hand reaches** and that one does likewise. **Rav Pappa said:** The mishna is discussing a case **where** neither of them is grasping the garment itself, but rather **they are holding onto the fringes [*bekarkashta*]** of the garment. Therefore, each is required to take an oath and they divide the garment between them.

The *Gemara* asks: how then does one reconcile this *braita* with the *Mishnah*, which prescribes equal division and does not mention unequal possession? *Rav Pappa* answers: the *Mishnah* is speaking of a case where both parties are holding onto the *karkashta* — the fringes or edges — with the body of the *tallis* hanging between them. Neither party has physical possession of a substantive portion of the garment, and therefore the *braita*'s principle of taking proportionally does not apply. Earlier in the *Gemara*, holding onto an edge was described as ineffective when someone else holds the body of the *tallis*; here, where neither party holds the body, their grip on the fringes is similarly insufficient to establish unequal possession.

### Three-by-Three Shiur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַאי סוּדָרָא, כֵּיוָן דְּתָפֵיס בֵּיהּ שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ, קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״וְנָתַן לְרֵעֵהוּ״. דִּכְמַאן דִּפְסִיק דְּמֵי, וְקָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya said: Learn** a *halakha* with regard to the symbolic transfer of **a cloth** as a formal act of acquisition **from** Rav Pappa's statement: The entire cloth need not change hands. Rather, **once** the recipient of the cloth **has grasped three by three** fingerbreadths **of** the cloth, which is the minimum size of a cloth that can be considered a utensil, the transaction takes effect, as **we consider** such an action to be an implementation of the verse upon which acquisition by means of a cloth is based: "Now this was the custom in former times in Israel…to confirm all things: A man drew off his shoe, **and gave it to his neighbor;** and this was the attestation in Israel" (Ruth 4:7). This is **because** such an amount of the cloth is significant enough to be considered **as though it were severed** from the rest of the cloth, **and** therefore when the recipient grasps it, it **effects the acquisition,** even though the rest of the cloth is still in the hand of the other party.

*Rav Mesharshiya* draws a broader principle from the *braita*'s rule: when one is holding part of an object, what he is holding is treated as if it were fully severed and completely in his possession. He applies this to the case of *kinyan chalipin*, where the purchaser gives the seller a handkerchief in exchange for land or other goods. *Rav Mesharshiya* teaches: once the seller takes hold of the handkerchief, and specifically once he grasps a portion of it at least three finger-widths by three finger-widths — which constitutes the minimum *shiur* for a *beged*, a garment — the verse *venasan le-re'ehu* is fulfilled, and the acquisition is complete. The underlying logic, derived from the *braita*, is that once you grasp part of an object, it is treated as if the portion in your hand is fully detached and entirely yours — *dekeman de-pasik dami*. There is no need to hold the entire handkerchief, because what you hold is legally complete in itself.

### Get and Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִדְּרַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: גֵּט בְּיָדָהּ וּמְשִׁיחָה בְּיָדוֹ, אִם יָכוֹל לְנַתְּקוֹ וְלַהֲבִיאוֹ אֶצְלוֹ – אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת! הָתָם כְּרִיתוּת בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא. הָכָא נְתִינָה בָּעֵינַן, וְהָא אִיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** in **what** way **is** this case **different from** the ruling **of Rav Ḥisda? As Rav Ḥisda said:** In a case where a husband placed **a bill of divorce in** his wife's **hand, and a string** attached to the bill of divorce remained **in his hand, if** the husband **can** still **pull** the bill of divorce out of her hand **and bring it to him, she is not divorced; and if** he is **not** able to do so, e.g., if the string is too flimsy, then **she is divorced.** This indicates that as long as part of the bill of divorce remains in his hand, it is not considered as though he gave it to her. The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of a bill of divorce, **we require** that it accomplish a complete **severance** between the husband and wife, **and** as long as the husband continues to have some hold on the bill of divorce **there is no** complete severance. By contrast, **here,** in the case of a transaction by means of a cloth, **we require** an act of **giving, and there is** a valid act of giving even if only part of the cloth was given.

One might ask whether this principle of treating what one holds as fully severed applies universally. *Rav Chisda* rules that if a *get* is in the wife's hand but the husband is holding a string attached to it, the analysis depends on whether he could yank the string and retrieve the *get*. If he could pull it back, she is not divorced; if she has sufficient grip that he cannot, she is divorced.

This seems to contradict the principle just derived — if what she holds is treated as if fully detached from the rest, why should the husband's string matter at all? The *Gemara* answers: *hatam krisus be'inan ve-lika* — there, the Torah requires a full severance between husband and wife, and as long as he retains the ability to retrieve the *get*, that complete severance has not been achieved, even if she has full possession of the document. *Hacha nesinah be'inan ve-hai'ika* — here, by contrast, all that is required is an act of giving, and that giving is accomplished the moment the seller takes hold of the handkerchief. The requirements are different, and so the analysis diverges.

### Golden Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם הָיְתָה טַלִּית מוּזְהֶבֶת – חוֹלְקִין. פְּשִׁיטָא? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָאֵי דַּהֲבָא בֵּי מִצְעֵי. הָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּמִיקְרַב לְגַבֵּי דְּחַד. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלוֹג הָכִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי חָזֵית דְּפָלְגַתְּ הָכִי? פְּלוֹג הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** Even **if** the **garment was** fashioned **with gold** thread, **they divide** it. The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** Why would a gold garment have a different *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where the gold is in the middle** of the garment, neither in one's hand nor in the other's. The Gemara challenges: **This too** is **obvious;** the *halakha* is that they divide the remainder. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where** the gold is **closer to one** of them, though it is not in his grasp. **Lest you say that** the one to whom the gold is closer can **say to** the other: **Divide** it in **this** manner, along the middle line of the garment between us, leaving most of the gold in my possession, Rava therefore **teaches us that** they divide the gold equally. The reason is that the other litigant can **say to him** in response: **What did you see that** led **you** to **divide** it in **that** manner, e.g., lengthwise? **Divide** it in **this** manner, e.g., widthwise, so that the gold will be divided equally between us.

*Rava* raises a case involving a *tallis mu-zehevet* — a *tallis* with gold woven into it. Obviously, the gold is also divided. The novel point emerges when the gold is not equally distributed: what if the golden portion is closer to one side, toward one of the two disputants? One might argue that since the gold is closer to *Reuven*'s end, *Reuven* should be entitled to it — that one should split the *tallis* widthwise at the midpoint, thereby giving *Reuven* all the gold. *Rava* teaches that this argument is rejected: the other party can counter, "What makes you think to split it this way? Split it lengthwise." Since either method of division is equally valid, neither party can compel the other to accept a division that benefits one side. The gold does not automatically go to the party closest to it; the division remains equal.

## Shtar Division

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁנַיִם אֲדוּקִין בִּשְׁטָר, מַלְוֶה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁלִּי הוּא, וְנָפַל מִמֶּנִּי וּמְצָאתִיו, וְלֹוֶה אָמַר: שֶׁלְּךָ הוּא, וּפְרַעְתִּיו לָךְ – יִתְקַיֵּים הַשְּׁטָר בְּחוֹתְמָיו, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 1:8): In a case where **two** people, a creditor and a debtor, **are grasping** a promissory **note,** and the **creditor says:** The promissory note **is mine,** as the debt has not yet been repaid, **and I** merely **dropped it and I** subsequently **found it, and** the **debtor says:** The promissory note **was** once **yours,** i.e., you lent me the money, **but I** already **repaid you,** and you therefore gave me the note, in that case **the** promissory **note must be ratified through its signatories** for the creditor to collect the debt. In other words, the court must first ascertain the validity of the promissory note by verifying that the signatures of the witnesses are authentic. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** The creditor and the debtor **divide** the debt attested to in the promissory note, i.e., the debtor is liable to pay half the amount, due to uncertainty as to who is telling the truth.

The *Gemara* now extends the principles of disputed possession to a more complex object: a *shtar*, a promissory note. The *Tanna'im* taught: if two people are each holding onto a *shtar* — a document recording, say, that *Reuven* owes *Shimon* a thousand dollars — and the *malve* (creditor, *Shimon*) says it is his, that he held it while waiting for repayment and then lost it and found it again, while the *loveh* (debtor, *Reuven*) says yes, it was yours, but I paid the debt, got the document back, and then lost it — what does one do?

*Rebbi* says: *yitkayem ha-shtar be-chotamav* — validate the signatures. The *Gemara* asks: does that mean we then give the entire *shtar* to the creditor and let him collect the full debt? That would seem to ignore the *Mishnah*'s principle that two parties holding an object divide it.

### RSBG Position

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says *yachloku* — treat it like the garment case and divide equally. Since both parties have a claim and both are holding the document, divide either the debt or the market value of the *shtar* equally between them.

### Judge's Hands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל לְיַד דַּיָּין – לֹא יוֹצִיאוֹ עוֹלָמִית. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הוּא בְּחֶזְקָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If a promissory note **fell into the possession of a judge** and the two parties do not agree as to which of them it belongs, either to the creditor, and the debt has yet to have been repaid, or to the debtor, and the debt was repaid, **it may never be removed** from the judge's possession to collect the debt until proof is provided. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The promissory note **retains its presumptive status** of validity and the litigants proceed in accordance with its contents.

A third case in the *braita*: *nafal le-yad dayan*, if it fell into the hands of a judge — *lo yotzi'am le-olam*, never give it back. *Rava* explains that this means a third party found a *shtar* that had already passed through the court system and bears the stamp of notarization (*hanpak*). In that case, even though it is clear the document was previously in the creditor's hands (as evidenced by the court's processing of it), a third-party finder should never return it. The concern is *pira'on* — that perhaps *Reuven* paid the debt, received the document, and then lost it. Since we cannot know, the *shtar* is held and neither party receives it.

### Rabbi Yosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הוּא בְּחֶזְקָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** The promissory note **retains its presumptive status** of validity and the litigants proceed in accordance with its contents.

*Rabbi Yosi* says *harei hu be-chezkaso* — it remains in its presumed status. Since the *shtar* had been in the possession of the *malve* (that is how it came to be notarized), we presume it remained there. *Rabbi Yosi* is not concerned for *pira'on*. *Rashi* adds that there is an additional basis for this: once a debt is paid, the borrower invariably tears up the document. If the document is intact, the presumption is that it has not been paid.

### Modeh B'Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בִּמְקוּיָּם – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יַחְלוֹקוּ, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקוּיָּם. רַבִּי סָבַר: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ – צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ. וְאִי מְקַיֵּים לֵיהּ פָּלֵיג, וְאִי לָא מְקַיֵּים לֵיהּ לָא פָּלֵיג. מַאי טַעְמָא? חַסְפָּא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, מַאן קָא מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ לְהַאי שְׁטָרָא – לֹוֶה, הָא קָאָמַר דִּפְרִיעַ. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ אֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מְקַיֵּים לֵיהּ – יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says: In** a case where the promissory note was **ratified** by the court, **everyone agrees** that the litigants **divide** it, and the debtor repays only half of the debt. **They disagree** with regard to a case **where** it was **not ratified. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that even when a debtor **admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **must ratify it** in court in order for the creditor to collect the debt. **And** therefore, **if he ratifies** the promissory note in court **he divides** it with the debtor, **and if he does not ratify it he does not divide** it with the debtor. If he is unable to ratify the signatures of the witnesses, he receives nothing even if the debtor admits that he borrowed the money. **What is the reason** for Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion? He holds that an unratified promissory note **is merely a shard. Who renders this** document a valid promissory **note?** The **debtor** does. The validity of the note is solely dependent on the corroboration of the debtor, and **doesn't** the debtor **say that** the debt mentioned in the promissory note was **repaid? Therefore,** the note is worthless unless it is ratified by the witnesses in court. **And Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel holds** that if a debtor **admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **is not required to ratify it** in court in order for the creditor to collect the debt. **And** therefore, **even if** the creditor **does not ratify it,** the promissory note is valid, and **they divide** it.

*Rava* in the name of *Rav Nachman* clarifies the dispute between *Rebbi* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. If the *shtar* had already been validated — the signatures notarized and the court stamp affixed — then everyone agrees: divide it equally, since it is already a confirmed thousand-dollar *shtar* and both parties are holding it. The dispute is only where the *shtar* has not yet been validated.

In such a case, the *shtar* is not independently enforceable — normally, an unvalidated *shtar* cannot be used to collect. What gives it force is *Reuven*'s own admission that it was written against him. But *Reuven* simultaneously claims that he paid the debt. This generates the dispute of *modeh be-shtar she-ktavo*: when a debtor admits the *shtar* was written but claims he paid, must the creditor still validate the signatures before collecting?

*Rebbi* holds *tzarich le-kaymo* — validation is still required. Since the *shtar* only has force because of *Reuven*'s admission, and *Reuven* also claims repayment, you cannot use his partial admission against him. This is the principle of *ha-peh she-asar hu ha-peh she-hitir*: the mouth that created the obligation is the same mouth that claims release. The creditor cannot take the admission of the debt while ignoring the claim of payment. Therefore, the *shtar* is essentially worthless — *chaspa be-alma hu* — unless and until the signatures are validated independently. Once validated, then *yachloku*. *Rebbi*'s statement that one must validate is thus not an instruction to give the entire *shtar* to the creditor; it is a prerequisite — only after validation would the division proceed.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds *ein tzarich le-kaymo* — no further validation is required. His position is rooted in a different understanding of the evidentiary framework: fundamentally, witnesses whose signatures appear on a *shtar* are considered as if their testimony has already been examined by the court, as the principle *edim ha-chatumim al ha-shtar ke-mi she-nechkera edutam be-veit din* establishes. The Rabbis imposed a requirement of additional validation, but once the debtor himself has admitted the document was written, that rabbinic requirement falls away. The debtor's admission renders the *shtar* fully operative, and he is therefore no longer believed to say *pariti* — that he paid. The admission of the debt and the claim of payment are not symmetrically credible; the written instrument prevails. Therefore, *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* says: even without independent validation, *yachloku*.

### Ketubah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָתַנְיָא: מָצָא שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה בַּשּׁוּק, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – יַחֲזִיר לָאִשָּׁה, אֵין הַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – לֹא יַחֲזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: עוֹדָהּ תַּחַת בַּעֲלָהּ – יַחֲזִיר לָאִשָּׁה. נִתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשָׁה – לֹא יַחֲזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If one **found a marriage contract in the marketplace,** in a case **when the husband admits** that he has not yet paid the amount written in the contract to his wife, the finder **must return** the document **to the wife.** In a case when **the husband does not admit** this, but instead claims that he has already paid the amount written in the contract, the one who found it **should not return it to this** one, the husband, **or to that** one, the wife. **Rabbi Yosei says** that there is a distinction between different situations: If the wife **is still under** the auspices of **her husband,** i.e., she is still married to him, the one who found the marriage contract **must return** it **to the wife** because presumably the husband did not pay her the amount specified in the marriage contract during their marriage. If the wife **was widowed or divorced, he should not return it to this** party, the husband or his heirs, **or to that** party, the wife, as perhaps she already received payment and the contract was later lost by her husband or his heirs. In this case, Rabbi Yosei suspects that there was payment.

A *braita* is now cited that appears to undermine the position of *Rabbi Yosi*, who is not concerned for *pira'on*. The *braita* discusses a found *ketubbah*: if the husband admits it fell from the wife, return it to her. If he denies this and says he paid, what does one do? The first opinion says: do not return it to either party, because we are concerned for *pira'on*. *Rabbi Yosi* says: if she is still married to him — *odah tachat ba'alah* — return it to the wife, since there is no reason to suppose a *ketubbah* would be paid while they are still married. But if she is widowed or divorced, do not return it to either.

This *braita* seems to show that *Rabbi Yosi* himself is concerned for *pira'on* at least in certain circumstances — in direct tension with his ruling elsewhere that *harei hu be-chezkato* and we are not concerned for *pira'on*.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיפוֹךְ: נָפַל לְיַד דַּיָּין לֹא יוֹצִיאוֹ עוֹלָמִית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הֲרֵי הוּא בְּחֶזְקָתוֹ. אִי הָכִי קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבָּנַן אַדְּרַבָּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Reverse** the order of the *tanna'im* in the *baraita* and teach it as follows: If the promissory note **fell into the possession of a judge, it may never be removed;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei. And the Rabbis say: It retains its presumptive status.** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the contradiction between the statement **of the Rabbis** in this *baraita* **and** the statement **of the Rabbis** with regard to the case of the marriage contract is **difficult.** Whereas according to the emended version of the *baraita* the Rabbis do not take into account the possibility that the debt was repaid, in the case of the marriage contract the Rabbis take this possibility into account.

The *Gemara* explores three approaches to resolving this tension.

The first approach suggests reversing the attributions in the original case about the *shtar* found by a third party: assign *lo yotzi'am le-olam* to *Rabbi Yosi* and *harei hu be-chezkato* to the *Chachamim*. But this creates a new problem — the *braita* about the *ketubbah* shows that both positions are concerned for *pira'on*, and simply reversing the attributions does not resolve the contradiction between the *Chachamim* in that context and the *Chachamim* here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה, כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: אֵין הַבַּעַל מוֹדֶה – לֹא יַחֲזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁנִּתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה, אֲבָל עוֹדָהּ תַּחַת בַּעֲלָהּ – יַחֲזִיר לָאִשָּׁה, שֶׁרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: עוֹדָהּ תַּחַת בַּעֲלָהּ – יַחֲזִיר לָאִשָּׁה, נִתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ שֶׁנִּתְגָּרְשָׁה – לֹא יַחֲזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the problem: The *baraita* that discusses **the marriage contract is entirely** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei;** it contains no dispute. **And** the *baraita* **is incomplete, and this** is what **it is teaching:** In a case where **the husband does not admit** that he did not pay the marriage contract, the one who found it **should not return it to this** party **or to that** party. **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** the woman **was widowed or divorced. But** if **she is still under** the auspices of **her husband,** the finder **must return** it **to the wife. As Rabbi Yosei says:** If **she is still under** the auspices of **her husband,** the finder **must return** it **to the wife.** If she **was widowed or divorced, he should not return** it **to this** party **or to that** party.

The *Gemara* then attempts to read the entire *ketubbah braita* as a single continuous position of *Rabbi Yosi* — *kullah Rabbi Yosi* — by erasing the anonymous *Tanna Kamma* attribution and reconstructing the text: when the husband denies the *ketubbah* fell from the wife, do not return it to either party because we are concerned for *pira'on*, and that is true once they are divorced or he has died. But while she is still married to him, return it to her, since *pira'on* during marriage is implausible. And this entire teaching is *Rabbi Yosi*'s consistent view: I am not concerned for *pira'on* during marriage, but I am after marriage ends. This allows *Rabbi Yosi*'s ruling in the *shtar* case to refer to a case where one gives back to the *malve* — because there, the concern for *pira'on* is simply not applicable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְדִבְרֵיהֶם דְּרַבָּנַן קָאָמַר לְהוּ. לְדִידִי אֲפִילּוּ נִתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשָׁה נָמֵי לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לְפֵירָעוֹן. לְדִידְכוּ אוֹדוֹ לִי מִיהַת בְּעוֹדָהּ תַּחַת בַּעֲלָהּ, דְּיַחֲזִיר לָאִשָּׁה, דְּלָאו בַּת פֵּירָעוֹן הִיא. וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: אֵימוֹר צְרָרֵי אַתְפְּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Actually, do not reverse** the opinions in the *baraita*, but instead resolve the contradiction differently: **Rabbi Yosei was telling the Rabbis** what the *halakha* should be in the case of a marriage contract **according to their statement,** i.e., according to their opinion that one who finds a promissory note needs to take into account that the debt may have been repaid already. His statement should be understood as follows: **In my** opinion, **even if she was widowed or divorced we do not suspect** that there was **payment.** But **according to your** opinion, **concede to me, in any event,** that **while she is still under** the auspices of **her husband** the finder should **return** the document **to the wife, as** the marriage contract **is not** yet **subject to payment.** Since the husband is not yet liable to pay, it is unlikely that he paid. **And the Rabbis said to him** in response: Even if they are still married, **say** that **he gave her bundles** of money, and in exchange she gave him back the marriage contract. If the finder then returns the marriage contract to the wife, that would enable her to collect the sum twice.

*Rav Pappa* offers a second approach: never reverse the first *braita*. *Rabbi Yosi* is genuinely not concerned for *pira'on* and says *harei hu be-chezkato* in the *shtar* case. His statement in the *ketubbah braita* is actually directed at the *Rabbanan* — he is arguing *le-didchu*, according to your view: you, the *Rabbanan*, are normally concerned for *pira'on*. My own view, *le-didi*, is that even if they were divorced I would return it to the wife, since I am never concerned for *pira'on*. But according to you who are concerned, at least concede that while they are still married you should return it to her. The *Rabbanan* respond that even during marriage, a husband might have prepaid the *ketubbah* — *eimar tzararei atpasa* — bundled money given to the wife while they remain married, thereby settling the debt in advance and receiving the document back. Therefore they remain concerned for *pira'on* even during the marriage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אֵיפוֹךְ קַמַּיְיתָא. וְטַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּחָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁתֵּי כְתוּבּוֹת. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לִשְׁתֵּי כְתוּבּוֹת לָא חָיֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Actually, reverse** the order of the *tanna'im* in **the first** *baraita*, which discusses one who finds a promissory note, **and** resolve the contradiction between the different statements of the Rabbis as follows: **The reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis here,** that a marriage contract cannot be returned to the wife, **is that we suspect that** the husband wrote **two marriage contracts;** after the first marriage contract was lost, the husband had to write a second one in its stead. Returning to the wife the marriage contract that was found would enable her to collect twice. **And Rabbi Yosei** holds that it should be returned to the wife because **he does not suspect that** the husband wrote **two marriage contracts;** in his opinion, it is a rare occurrence.

*Ravina* prefers the first approach and restates it with a refinement: reverse the first *braita* so that *Rabbi Yosi* is the one concerned for *pira'on*. The *Rabbanan*'s reason in the *ketubbah* case for declining to return the document to the wife even during marriage is entirely different from *pira'on* — they are concerned for *shtei ketubot*, the possibility that a replacement *ketubbah* was written after the first one was lost. It is standard practice that a couple cannot continue living together without a *ketubbah* in place, so if the original was lost, a new one would be issued. The original, now found, might therefore be superseded. The *Rabbanan* are never concerned for *pira'on*; their reason to withhold the *ketubbah* is the concern for a duplicate. *Rabbi Yosi*, who is concerned for *pira'on*, would therefore still refuse to return the *shtar* to the *malve* — while during marriage, in the *ketubbah* case, he is not concerned for *pira'on* but *is* concerned, at least implicitly, for other reasons. The elegant feature of *Ravina*'s approach is that it sharpens the underlying debate: *Rabbi Yosi* and the *Rabbanan* disagree not merely about *pira'on* but about fundamentally different concerns, and in the *ketubbah* context their respective concerns diverge in a revealing way.

## Tofes and Toref

### Rabbi Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּשֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם אֲדוּקִים בַּטּוֹפֶס, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם בַּתּוֹרֶף. אֲבָל אֶחָד אָדוּק בַּטּוֹפֶס וְאֶחָד אָדוּק בַּתּוֹרֶף – זֶה נוֹטֵל טוֹפֶס, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל תּוֹרֶף. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Elazar says:** With regard to the **dispute** between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi in the case of a creditor and a debtor both grasping a promissory note, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that they divide the promissory note evenly, specifically **in** a case **where both are grasping the standard** part of the promissory note, i.e., the part that contains the standard formulation of the note, **or both** are grasping **the essential** part of the promissory note, where the names of the creditor and debtor are written, as well as the amount owed and the date. **But** if **one** of them **is grasping the standard** part **and** the other **one is grasping the essential** part, they divide the promissory note between them based on the section that each of them is holding; **this** one **takes** the **standard** part **and that** one **takes** the **essential** part. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Actually, they divide** everything equally.

*Rabbi Elazar* rules that the dispute over equal versus unequal division of the *shtar* depends on the physical arrangement of the document. When both parties are holding equally — both grasping the *tofes* or both grasping the *toref* — the division is equal. *Tofes* refers to the boilerplate standard language of the document, while *toref* refers to the specific operative details: who is the *malve*, who is the *loveh*, the amount of the debt, and — critically — the date. But when one party is holding the *tofes* and the other is holding the *toref*, then *zeh notel tofes ve-zeh notel toref* — each takes the portion he is holding, and the division is unequal.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד אָדוּק בַּטּוֹפֶס וְאֶחָד בַּתּוֹרֶף. וְהָתַנְיָא: זֶה נוֹטֵל עַד מָקוֹם שֶׁיָּדוֹ מַגַּעַת! לָא צְרִיכָא דְּקָאֵי תּוֹרֶף בֵּי מִצְעֵי. אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? לָא צְרִיכָא דִּמְקָרַב לְגַבֵּי דְחַד. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אָמַר לֵיהּ פְּלוֹג הָכִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי חָזֵית דְּפָלְגַתְּ הָכִי? פְּלוֹג הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement: **And** does he hold that this is the *halakha* **even** in a case where **one is grasping the standard** part **and one the essential** part? **But isn't it taught** in the *baraita* that was cited above with regard to a garment: **This** one **takes up to where his hand reaches** and that one takes up to where his hand reaches? Here as well, if one is grasping the standard part and the other is grasping the essential part, they should take the parts they are holding. The Gemara answers: **No,** this *halakha* that Rabbi Yoḥanan stated is **necessary** for a case **where** the **essential** part **is located in the middle.** He was not discussing the case where one was grasping the standard part and the other one was grasping the essential part. In that case, he would agree that each takes the part he was grasping. The Gemara asks: **If so, what** is the purpose **of stating** that they divide it equally? That is obvious. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** the essential part of the document **is closer to one** of them. **Lest you say** that the one to whom the essential part is closer can **say to** the other one: **Divide** it in **this** manner, leaving the essential part on my side, Rabbi Yoḥanan **teaches us that** the other one can **say to him** in response: **What did you see that** led **you** to **divide** it in **that** manner? **Divide** it in **this** manner, so that we will both share the essential part.

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees: *le-olam cholkin* — the division is always equal, even when one party holds the *tofes* and the other holds the *toref*. The *Gemara* objects that we have the principle from the garment *braita* that each party receives whatever is in his hand — *zeh notel ad mekom she-yado maga'at*. *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position can be reconciled by positing that the *toref* is in the middle of the document, such that both parties are in fact holding portions of lesser importance, and the *toref* is between them. The *Gemara* then asks: if that is so, what is *Rabbi Yochanan* teaching us — it is obvious that when neither party holds the essential part, the division would be equal? The novel case is where the *toref* is not precisely centered but is closer to one party's side. One might think that party can demand a widthwise division so as to capture the *toref* in his portion. *Rabbi Yochanan* teaches that this cannot be compelled — the other party can say, "What makes you think to divide widthwise? Divide lengthwise." The same argument that appeared in the golden *tallis* case recurs here.

### Liens and Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דְּאָמַר זֶה נוֹטֵל טוֹפֶס וְזֶה נוֹטֵל תּוֹרֶף, לְמָה לֵיהּ? וְכִי לָצוּר עַל פִּי צְלוֹחִיתוֹ הוּא צָרִיךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לִדְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says this** one **takes** the **standard** part **and that** one **takes** the **essential** part, **why do** either of them need it? **Does he need** half of the document **to cover the opening of his flask?** Having half a promissory note is of no legal consequence. Ravina **said to him:** The division in question is not division of the document itself, with each taking half of the paper. It is a division **of** its **monetary** value, as the value of each section of the promissory note is compared to the value of the other.

*Rav Achai mi-Difti* presses *Ravina* with a pointed question: according to *Rabbi Elazar*'s view, what does it even mean to say that one party gets the *tofes* and the other gets the *toref*? Is the point that each walks away with a piece of parchment — to use, as the *Gemara* puts it, as a bottle stopper? The value of the *shtar* lies in its ability to collect a debt; how does holding one portion versus another translate into a practical difference?

*Ravina* answers: *li-dmei* — the distinction is not about physical pieces of paper but about the relative monetary value that each portion of the document represents, which then determines how the overall value of the *shtar* is apportioned.

### Calculation Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר הָכִי: שְׁטָרָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ זְמַן, כַּמָּה שָׁוֵי? וּדְלֵית בֵּיהּ זְמַן, כַּמָּה שָׁוֵי? בִּשְׁטָרָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ זְמַן – גָּבֵי מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדֵי. וְאִידַּךְ – לָא גָּבֵי מִמְּשַׁעְבְּדֵי. יָהֵיב לֵיהּ הֵיאַךְ דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The one grasping the essential part of the promissory note, which contains the date, **can say this:** Consider a promissory **note that has** the **date** written **on it; how much** is it **worth? And** consider a promissory note **that does not have** the **date** written **on it; how much** is it **worth?** The significance of writing the date is that if a creditor is in possession **of** a promissory **note that has** the **date** written **on it,** he **can collect** his debt even **from liened** property that has been sold by the debtor to another individual after taking the loan. **But** if a creditor is in possession of **the other** kind of promissory note, i.e., one that does not have the date written on it, he **cannot collect** his debt **from liened** property. Therefore, **the other** party, who is grasping the standard part of the document, **gives him** the difference between the two values.

The key valuation point is this: a *shtar* that contains a date generates liens (*meshuabdei*). When a loan is documented with a date, the creditor acquires liens on property that the debtor owned from that date forward — if the debtor later sold land, the creditor can collect from those purchasers. A *shtar* without a date has no such liens and therefore cannot collect from sold property. The *toref* is the portion of the document that contains the date. Therefore, a *shtar* with a date is worth significantly more than a *shtar* without one.

The calculation proceeds as follows: a thousand-dollar *shtar* with liens might be worth, say, nine hundred and fifty dollars on the market, while the same *shtar* without liens might be worth only seven hundred dollars. The *toref* — by virtue of containing the date — adds the differential of two hundred and fifty dollars in value. *Yehav lei hai de-beini beini*: the party holding the *toref* receives the incremental value that the date adds. The remaining seven hundred dollars in base value is divided equally between the parties, each receiving three hundred and fifty. The party holding the *toref* therefore walks away with six hundred dollars and the other with three hundred and fifty.

Why does the *toref* specifically represent the date? *Tosafos* offers the following reasoning: every other element of the *shtar* is essential to its validity. Remove any part of the *tofes* — any standard clause — and the *shtar* is invalid entirely. The only element of a *shtar* whose absence does not invalidate the document, but merely diminishes its power, is the date. Without the date, the *shtar* is still a valid instrument for collecting from the debtor directly (*bnei chorin*); it simply lacks the power to collect from encumbered property (*meshuabdei*). Therefore, the date functions as a conceptually separable component — it does not define the *shtar*'s legitimacy but adds its lien-value. The party who holds the portion of the document containing the date is treated as holding that added value.

### Division = Valuation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְיַחְלוֹקוּ״ נָמֵי דַּאֲמַרַן לְדָמֵי. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, שְׁנַיִם אוֹחֲזִין בְּטַלִּית הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפָלְגִי? הָא אַפְסְדוּהּ! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, דְּחַזְיָא לִקְטַנִּים. וְהָא דְּאָמַר רָבָא: אִם הָיְתָה טַלִּית מוּזְהֶבֶת – חוֹלְקִין, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפָלְגִי לַהּ, הָא אַפְסְדוּהָ! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, דְּחַזְיָא לִבְנֵי מְלָכִים. וְהָא דִּתְנַן: הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם רוֹכְבִין עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה וְכוּ׳, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפָלְגִי לַהּ? הָא אַפְסְדוּהָ! בִּשְׁלָמָא טְהוֹרָה – חַזְיָא לְבָשָׂר. אֶלָּא טְמֵאָה, הָא אַפְסְדוּהָ! אֶלָּא לִדְמֵי, הָכָא נָמֵי לִדְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **also** true in general, with regard to cases **where we said** that the two litigants **divide** the disputed item: The reference is **to** the **monetary** value, and not division of the actual item. **As, if you do not say so,** but rather you hold that the item itself is divided, in the case of **two** people who come to court **holding a garment,** do they **also divide** the garment itself into two? **But** by doing so they would **ruin it.** The Gemara rejects this proof: **This** is **not difficult,** as it is possible to explain that they actually cut the garment in two. This is because each party receives an item that has monetary value, **as it is fit** to be made into a garment **for small** children. The Gemara asks: **But** as for that **which Rava said,** that **if** the **garment was** fashioned **with gold** thread **they divide** it, does that **also** mean **that they divide** the garment itself? By doing so they would **ruin it.** The Gemara answers: **That** is **not difficult, as** after the garment is divided **it is fit** to be made into a garment **for the children of kings** or wealthy people. Therefore, it is not ruined. The Gemara asks: **But** with regard to **that which we learned** in the mishna: If **two** people **were sitting on an animal** and each of them claims to own the entire animal, they each take an oath and divide the animal, does that **also** mean **that they divide** the animal itself? By doing so they would **ruin it. Granted,** if it is **a kosher** animal it is **fit** to be slaughtered and divided between them **for** the **meat. But** if it is **a non-kosher** animal, slaughtering it and dividing the carcass would **ruin it** and render it worthless. **Rather,** clearly they divide its **monetary** value. **Here too,** in the other cases where the ruling is to divide the item, it means that the litigants divide its **monetary** value and not the item itself.

The *Gemara* clarifies that in all of these cases — the *tallis*, the animal, the *shtar* — when the *Mishnah* and *braitot* speak of "dividing," they do not mean a literal physical cutting of the object. *V'yachloku nami de-amrinan li-dmei* — division means calculating the value and distributing it accordingly. This is not merely a technicality; it is essential to understanding what *yachloku* means throughout the entire chapter.

The *Gemara* proves this from multiple directions. If you thought the *tallis* could be literally cut in half, one might object that this destroys its value — but the *Gemara* notes that two half-*tallisot* might still be serviceable for children, so perhaps literal cutting is feasible. Then *Rava*'s case of the golden *tallis* presents a further complication: half a golden *tallis* for children seems implausible, since children do not wear gold-threaded garments. But even that might be answered by imagining princes' children. The conclusive proof comes from the case of two people riding an animal — *hayu shnayim rokhvim al gabei behema* — where the *Mishnah* says to divide it. Literally cutting a live kosher animal in half to divide the meat would be conceivable, but a non-kosher animal like a donkey, once dead, is worthless. Therefore, *ela li-dmei* — obviously "dividing" means selling the object or assessing its value and dividing the proceeds. And *hacha nami li-dmei* — the same is true for the *shtar*: the division is entirely a matter of monetary valuation, not of physically apportioning a piece of parchment.
