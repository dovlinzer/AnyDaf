# Bava Kamma 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yom Kippur & Kimlei

### Yom Kippur Slaughter

The *daf* we take up today is 71a, picking up two lines from the top of the page, continuing through a series of fascinating cases in the *mishna*. By way of background: the earlier portions of the *mishna* dealt with the testimony of witnesses to theft and slaughter and the problem of *davar v'chatzi davar* — whether a witness may testify to only part of a legally operative act. The *mishna* then addressed a case of selling on *Shabbat*, where the *Gemara* noted that ordinarily no principle of *kimlei* would apply to the act of selling, but constructed a scenario in which *Shabbat* would in fact be violated in the act of the sale itself, in which case *kimlei* would override the penalty of four-and-five. That discussion culminated in an important conceptual question about whether *kimlei* voids an interpersonal transaction entirely, or merely prevents the court from enforcing it — concluding that while *kimlei* may prevent enforcement, the transaction retains enough halakhic validity that a residual *chiyuv bidei shamayim* attaches, and the sale would still generate liability for four-and-five.

We now turn to the next case in the *mishna*: one who steals an animal and slaughters it on *Yom Kippur*. Slaughtering on *Yom Kippur* is a violation carrying *kares*, yet the *mishna* holds the thief liable for four-and-five without invoking *kimlei*. The *Gemara* opens by pressing the question: why not say *kimlei*?

### Kimlei & Lashes

The *Gemara* poses the problem directly: *he d'katala leka* — true, there is no capital liability for the slaughter itself — *malkus mi ika* — but there is a liability for lashes, since any violation carrying *kares* is subject to lashes when accompanied by warning. And we ordinarily hold *kaim lan d'eino lokeh u'meshalem* — one does not receive lashes and pay simultaneously. Should not the liability for lashes therefore override the monetary payment of four-and-five?

Before tracing the *Gemara*'s path, it is worth pausing on a question the *Gemara* might have raised but did not. Why does the discussion go to lashes at all? One might have thought to ask whether *kares* itself — a divine punishment not administered by the courts — suffices to trigger *kimlei*. This is a genuinely distinct question: where a sin is punishable by death administered by a court but the circumstances preclude court action, at least the sin is of the type that courts would punish. A sin that is weighty but categorically outside the jurisdiction of any earthly court — such as a violation punishable only by *kares* — presents a different question altogether. Similarly, the *Gemara* might have noted that *kimlei* by lashes typically applies only when lashes are actually going to be administered — which requires both witnesses and warning — and could have resolved the case by stipulating that no such warning was given. *Tosafot* in fact makes this point. But the *Gemara* does not take that route.

Instead, the *Gemara* says: *ha mani Rabbi Meir hi* — this *mishna* goes according to the position of Rabbi Meir, who says in Tractate *Makkot* that one can indeed receive lashes and pay simultaneously. The case there involves *eidim zomemin* who are liable both for lashes — for violating *lo sa'aneh b're'echa ed shaker* — and for the monetary penalty of conspiratorial witnesses. Rabbi Meir holds that both may be imposed together. Accordingly, when one slaughters on *Yom Kippur* and is liable for lashes, Rabbi Meir maintains that the four-and-five payment still stands.

### Rav Ashi's Challenge

Rav Ashi presses further: if the *mishna* follows Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Meir rejects *kimlei* even when lashes apply, then perhaps Rabbi Meir would go further still and reject *kimlei* even in a case of capital liability — *afillu tavach b'Shabbos*. If so, the *mishna* ought to hold a person liable for four-and-five even when slaughtering on *Shabbat*, where the violation carries the death penalty. Yet the later *mishna* explicitly exempts one who slaughters on *Shabbat* from paying four-and-five. How does that square with attributing the *Yom Kippur* case to Rabbi Meir?

### Capital vs. Lashes

The *Gemara* entertains the response: *v'chi teima* — one might say there is an obvious distinction between capital liability and lashes. Rabbi Meir says one can receive lashes and pay; that does not mean one can be put to death and pay. The two categories are qualitatively different. Yet the *Gemara* immediately introduces a *braita* that appears to complicate this distinction — and it is to that *braita* that we now turn.

## Braita: 3 Cases

### Rabbi Meir's Position

The *Gemara* says: *v'ha tanya* — we have a *braita* — *ganav v'tavach b'Shabbos, ganav v'tavach l'avoda zara* — one who stole and slaughtered on *Shabbat*, or stole and slaughtered for the purpose of *avoda zara*, both carrying capital liability — *ganav shor haniskal v'tavacho* — or one who stole a *shor haniskal*, a condemned ox, and slaughtered it, which is a puzzling case since that animal would seem to have no monetary value — *meshalem arba v'chamisha, divrei Rabbi Meir* — nevertheless pays four-and-five, in the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

This appears to demonstrate that Rabbi Meir dispenses with *kimlei* entirely, even in the face of capital liability. If so, our *mishna* cannot be attributed to Rabbi Meir, because our *mishna* does invoke *kimlei* to exempt a person who slaughtered on *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* also notes: *vachachamim potrin* — the Sages exempt in these cases — on the first two cases apparently on account of *kimlei*, and on the *shor haniskal* presumably because it is worth nothing. So we have a genuinely difficult *braita* to reckon with.

### Third Party Slaughter

The *Gemara* offers a resolution: *bar minei d'ha* — bracket that *braita* entirely — because it is speaking of an entirely different scenario. *B'toveach al yedei acher* — the *braita* is discussing a case where the slaughter was performed by a third party. That is: Reuven stole the animal and brought it to Shimon the butcher, who slaughtered it without knowing it was stolen — and it happened to be *Shabbat*, or it happened that Shimon was not *shomer Shabbat*. Reuven was not the one who violated *Shabbat* and therefore there is no *kimlei* problem. But this immediately generates a different question: how is Reuven liable for four-and-five at all? The Torah says "and you slaughtered it" — not "and you arranged for someone else to slaughter it."

This brings us to a major *chiddush*: the *Gemara* asks, *v'chi zeh chotei v'zeh mitchayev* — can this one sin and that one become liable?

### Shaliach Doctrine

Rava resolves this: *shani hacha d'amar kra* — here the verse itself provides the answer. The Torah says *u'tvacho u'mechoro* — "and you slaughtered it or sold it." Just as selling necessarily involves another party — a buyer — so too slaughter may be accomplished through another person. The Torah thus recognizes that the *ganav*'s liability for four-and-five is established even when the slaughter is performed through an agent.

This is a significant principle. The underlying logic is worth considering. Why does the theft of another's animal and its subsequent slaughter or sale generate the heightened penalty of four-and-five? The *Gemara* elsewhere explains that the additional payment reflects the degree to which the thief *nishtarish b'cheita* — entrenches himself in his transgression, pushing the animal ever further from its owner's possession. Seen in that light, it is not the personal physical act of slaughter that is decisive — it is the thief's role in causing the animal to be removed still further from its owner. Whether the thief himself slaughters it or arranges for someone else to do so, the act of entrenchment is equally his. Indeed, one might argue this reading is simply the natural one: not every thief is a *shochet*, and it would be strange to suppose the Torah intended the penalty only for the unusual case in which the thief personally performs the slaughter.

It should be noted that the butcher in this scenario is independently liable for *chillul Shabbat* — but that is a separate matter. The liability for four-and-five flows to Reuven as the *ganav*; it is his *cheita*, not the butcher's, that the four-and-five addresses.

Additional support for this principle appears in two further *drashot*: the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives it from the word *o* — "or sell it" — suggesting that "other manners" of disposition are included; the school of Chizkiyah derives it from the word *tachat* — "in place of the ox" — hinting that another person acting *tachat*, in one's stead, is included.

### Zutra's Challenge

Mar Zutra raises a sharp objection: *mi ikha midi* — is there any case where the following would be true — *d'ee avid ihu lo michayav, d'avid shaliach michayav* — that had you performed the act yourself you would be exempt, yet because your agent performed it you are liable? If Reuven had slaughtered the animal on *Shabbat* himself, he would be exempt from four-and-five on account of *kimlei*. How can the butcher's performing the act make Reuven more liable than he himself would have been? The agent cannot enlarge the principal's liability beyond what it would have been had he acted personally.

### Rav Ashi's Response

Rav Ashi's answer is elegant and, one suspects, obvious once stated: *hatam lav mishum d'lo michayav hu* — when Reuven himself would have been exempt upon slaughtering on *Shabbat*, it is not because slaughtering on *Shabbat* fundamentally fails to generate liability for four-and-five. The act of slaughter by the *ganav* is inherently an act that creates the four-and-five obligation. What would have blocked payment is an extrinsic circumstance — *ela de-kam lei b'draba minei* — the principle of *kimlei*, which overrides the monetary obligation. But when a third party performs the slaughter and is not himself violating *Shabbat* at that moment, there is nothing to trigger *kimlei* with respect to Reuven. The underlying liability is fully present; only the override is absent. An override that would have applied to Reuven personally simply does not extend to protect him from the consequences of acts performed by others.

So the picture now comes into focus: the *braita* that attributes liability for four-and-five even in the *Shabbat* and *avoda zara* cases to Rabbi Meir is speaking throughout of *toveach al yedei acher*, slaughter through a third party. There is no *kimlei* issue because Reuven did not violate *Shabbat*. And Rabbi Meir does not hold that one may be put to death and simultaneously pay — that remains a valid distinction. The *mishna*'s *Yom Kippur* case, where Reuven slaughtered the animal himself, follows Rabbi Meir only in the narrower sense that Rabbi Meir permits lashes and payment to coexist.

## Unfit Slaughter

### Focus on Fitness

Having resolved the *kimlei* problem by reframing the *braita* as a *toveach al yedei acher* case, the *Gemara* now takes a step back and asks: if it is agreed by all parties that the liability comes through a third party and there is therefore no *kimlei* difficulty, why do the Sages disagree with Rabbi Meir in the *braita* at all? The *Gemara*'s answer transforms the discussion entirely.

*Amri man chachamim Rabbi Shimon* — the Sages whose position is recorded in the *braita* are in fact Rabbi Shimon. And Rabbi Shimon's principle is: *shechita she'eina re'uya lo shema shechita* — a slaughter that does not render the animal fit for consumption is not halakhically called a *shechita* for purposes of the four-and-five penalty. This reframes all three cases in the *braita* simultaneously. The debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon (the Sages) is not about *kimlei* or about agency — it is about whether an act of slaughter that leaves the meat halakhically unfit counts as the kind of *shechita* that triggers the payment.

This is an elegant, unifying answer. In the case of *avoda zara*, the meat becomes forbidden in benefit. In the case of the *shor haniskal*, the meat cannot be eaten. And in the case of *Shabbat* — as we are about to see — there may be a basis to say the meat is forbidden as well, depending on a dispute among *Tannaim*.

The *Gemara* initially objects: by *Shabbat*, surely the *shechita* is kosher — *shechita kesheira*. The *mishna* in *Chullin* teaches explicitly that one who slaughters on *Shabbat*, even though he is liable with his life, the *shechita* is nevertheless valid and the meat is permitted. So how can the *Shabbat* case be classified as *shechita she'eina re'uya*?

The *Gemara* resolves this by attributing the position to Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar, as derived from a *mishna* concerning one who cooks on *Shabbat*. That *mishna* records a graduated dispute: Rabbi Meir holds that cooking *b'shogeg* renders the food permitted and cooking *b'meizid* renders it forbidden on that day only, for both the violator and others (according to Rashi). Rabbi Yehudah extends the prohibition for the *meizid* violator himself even after *Shabbat* ends — forever — while others may eat it. Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar goes furthest: *b'shogeg*, the food may be eaten after *Shabbat* by others, but not by the violator on the day itself; *b'meizid*, neither the violator nor anyone else may ever eat from it — *lo yochel olamis, lo lo velo l'acherim*.

For Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar, therefore, an intentional act of slaughter on *Shabbat* would produce meat that no one may ever consume — a genuine case of *shechita she'eina re'uya* on a biblical level.

The *Gemara* then traces Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar's reasoning: Rabbi Chiyya expounded before the house of the *Nasi* on the verse *u'shmartem et haShabbat ki kodesh hi lachem* — "You shall observe the *Shabbat*, for it is holy to you." Just as sanctified items (*kodesh*) are forbidden for consumption, so too the products of a *Shabbat* violation are forbidden for consumption. But a further *gezerah shavah* limits this analogy: the verse says *lachem* — "to you" — meaning the object remains yours to some degree, so benefit is not entirely forbidden as with full *kodesh*. And the phrase *mechaleleha mos yumas* — "those who desecrate it shall be put to death" — restricts this prohibition to *b'meizid*, willful desecration, not to *b'shogeg*.

### Idolatry & First Nick

Having resolved the *Shabbat* case, the *Gemara* turns to *avoda zara* — which at first seemed the clearest case of *shechita she'eina re'uya* — and finds it harbors a subtle difficulty. The *Gemara* asks: *amai mechayev shocheit l'avoda zara* — why would Rabbi Meir hold the thief liable even in the *avoda zara* case? The problem is this: *keivan d'shachat bei purta asra* — the moment the slaughterer makes the first nick in the animal's neck, he has already rendered it forbidden. For what makes an animal become a *tikroves avoda zara* — an offering to an idol, forbidden in benefit? It is not specifically the act of *shechita*; it is any act of worship or dedication that physically takes hold of the animal. The moment the first cut is made in the name of the idol, the one making it has exercised sufficient dominion to assign the animal a forbidden status. From that instant, the animal is *assur b'hana'ah* and effectively worthless. Yet the halakhic act of *shechita* — the act that would create liability for four-and-five — is only complete when the process concludes. So by the time the *shechita* is legally consummated, the animal is already worth nothing and, in a sense, no longer belongs to its owner. How then can Rabbi Meir hold the thief liable?

### Rava on Completion

Rava answers: *be'omer begmar zvicha hu ovda* — one should understand the case as one in which the slaughterer declares that the act of worship takes effect only upon the completion of the *shechita*, not at the first nick. The beginning of the process is not itself an act of worship; the dedication to the idol is the completed slaughter. Consequently, the animal's status changes at the very moment the *shechita* is complete — the same moment that liability for four-and-five would attach. This makes sense conceptually: the relevant act of worship in the context of animal sacrifice is the slaughter itself, not the initiation of the cutting.

### Condemned Ox Value

The *Gemara* now turns to the *shor haniskal* — and here the problem is not about the timing of forbidden status but about value altogether. A *shor haniskal* is *assur b'hana'ah* — entirely forbidden in benefit — and moreover is to be stoned. It has no market value whatsoever. How can one be liable for four-and-five in connection with an animal that is literally worth nothing? Five times zero is zero.

### Rava's Solution

Rava introduces a multi-part answer: *hacha b'mai askinan, kegon shemasro lishomer* — the case is one in which the owner had entrusted the ox to a *shomer* before it became a *shor haniskal*. The ox then killed someone while in the *shomer*'s possession, was testified against, and the *beit din* issued its ruling — all while the animal remained with the *shomer*.

The first pillar of Rava's solution invokes the position of Rabbi Yaakov: *af mishenigmar dino hechziro shomer leva'alim muchzar* — even after the animal has been condemned as a *shor haniskal*, the *shomer* can return it to the owner and say *harei shelcha lefanecha* — "here is your animal back" — and thereby discharge his obligation as *shomer*. This is akin to the principle of *hezek she'eino nikar* — a damage that leaves no visible physical trace — where returning the physically unchanged object satisfies the *shomer*'s duty even if the object's value has been destroyed. If the *shomer* can return the condemned ox and thereby extinguish a monetary obligation worth, say, ten thousand dinars, then the living ox — though forbidden in benefit and worthless on the open market — is worth its full market value to the *shomer*. Its continued existence protects the *shomer* from having to pay the owner.

### Shimon's Principle

The second pillar of Rava's solution invokes the position of Rabbi Shimon: *davar ha-goreim le-mamon ke-mamon dami* — something whose loss would cause a monetary loss is treated as if it were money itself. Even though the *shomer* does not own the ox and even though it has no market value, the ox's loss would cause the *shomer* a loss of ten thousand dinars. Rabbi Shimon holds that this suffices to treat the animal as though it were the *shomer*'s ten thousand-dinar asset. Consequently, one who steals the condemned ox from the *shomer* has taken something that functions as money for that *shomer*, and both *kefel* and four-and-five can apply.

The source for Rabbi Shimon's principle is a *mishna*: *Rabbi Shimon omer: kadshei shehayav b'achrayutan* — consecrated animals for which the owner has accepted financial responsibility. If one steals such an animal from the owner's house, the owner is entitled to collect *kefel*, because the animal's loss causes the owner a real monetary loss — he will be obligated to replace the sacrifice. Even though the animal is technically *hekdesh* and the verse exempts theft from a consecrated domain, since the owner bears financial responsibility, it is as though the animal is his property with its full value. *Almasi: davar ha-goreim le-mamon ke-mamon dami.*

The combination is thus: because Rabbi Yaakov holds the *shomer* can return the condemned ox and discharge his obligation, the ox is worth its full value to the *shomer*; and because Rabbi Shimon holds that something whose loss causes a monetary loss is treated as money, the *shomer* is treated as though he owns that value. One who steals the condemned ox from the *shomer* has therefore taken something with real monetary value, and is liable for *kefel* and, upon slaughter, for four-and-five.

The Sages' exemption, on this reading, flows not from any of the *kimlei* considerations but from *shechita she'eina re'uya* — the slaughter does not render the animal fit for consumption. Rabbi Meir, who does not accept *shechita she'eina re'uya* as a bar to liability, holds the thief liable; the Sages, who follow Rabbi Shimon's principle of *shechita she'eina re'uya*, exempt him.

### Tosafot's Question

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question on this entire framework. The passage attributes to Rabbi Meir the position of *davar ha-goreim le-mamon ke-mamon dami*, associating him with Rabbi Shimon's principle. But why invoke that principle at all? *Tosafot* asks: *teima, lama li lemeymar de-savar ke-Rabbi Shimon?* Why not simply say Rabbi Meir follows his own well-known position — *de-dan dinei de-garmi* — that Rabbi Meir holds one liable for *garmi*, for closely caused indirect damages? And if Rabbi Meir holds that causing an indirect monetary loss generates liability, then all the more so he would hold one liable for something whose loss directly causes a financial loss to the *shomer*.

### Garmi vs. Goreim

The resolution lies in recognizing that *garmi* and *davar ha-goreim le-mamon* are fundamentally different legal categories, operating in different domains of halakha. *Garmi* is a principle of *neziqin* — tort law. It governs cases where I have caused damage to another person's property or financial position through a causally proximate but technically indirect act — such as burning another's promissory note. The *garmi* principle holds me liable for the damage I caused, and I pay as one who injured. But *garmi* as a principle of tort law would not make me liable for *kefel* or four-and-five; it would make me pay only compensatory damages for the loss I caused.

*Davar ha-goreim le-mamon*, by contrast, is a principle of ownership and valuation. Rabbi Shimon is not saying that the *shomer* suffered a *neziqin* loss — he is saying that because the loss of this animal would cost the *shomer* money, the animal is treated as though it is the *shomer's* asset worth that amount. It is a principle about how we determine the *value* of an object and to whom it *belongs* for purposes of the laws of theft and their penalties. Only through this principle of ownership-based valuation can one arrive at *kefel* or four-and-five; *garmi* in tort cannot accomplish that.

## Father & Inheritance

### Post-Slaughter Death

The *Gemara* now addresses a case involving theft from one's father and the effect of subsequent inheritance on the liability for four-and-five. The scenario: one steals an ox from his father, slaughters it, and then the father dies. The thief is now an heir and, together with his brothers, inherits a portion of whatever money the estate is owed — including the four-and-five obligation that his father's estate held against him. The question is whether this produces a meaningful complication.

Rava poses a related question to Rav Nachman: if one steals an ox belonging to two partners, slaughters it, and then admits the act to one of the partners — such that *modeh biknas patur* exempts him from liability to that partner — is he still required to pay four-and-five to the other partner for his share of the ox? The *Gemara* frames the question around the verse *chamisha vakar amar rachmana* — the Torah specifies five *cattle*, perhaps implying that only liability for five whole units is operative, not five half-units.

### Mishnah Rules

The *mishna* addresses the father-theft case directly. If one steals from his father, slaughters, and the father subsequently dies — *meshalem tashlumei arba'ah vachamishah* — he pays four-and-five. But if one steals from his father, the father dies first, and then the thief slaughters — *eino meshalem tashlumei arba'ah vachamishah* — he does not pay four-and-five. The distinction turns on the sequence of slaughter and death.

The complication in the first case — where slaughter precedes death — is that upon the father's death, the thief becomes a partial heir to his own obligation. The four-and-five the estate is owed from him now partly flows back to him. He would, in effect, be paying a portion of the penalty to himself. Can he still be liable for the full four-and-five? The *Gemara* here notes that when the father dies, it is as though the thief has implicitly admitted his liability to himself — *ke-mi she-kodam ve-hoda le-echad mehen dami* — since he will not pay his own portion to himself. Yet in spite of this, the *mishna* says he pays.

### Court Judgment Fix

The *Gemara*'s first attempt at explaining why the *mishna* holds him fully liable in the first case is: *kegon she-amad aviv be-din* — the case is one in which the father had already brought the matter to court and the obligation was judicially fixed before the father died. Once the court has ruled, the obligation is crystallized, and subsequent changes — including the thief's inheritance of a share — do not diminish it.

### Timing Objection

The *Gemara* immediately objects: if the real distinction is whether the obligation was fixed by court judgment before the father's death, then the *mishna* should have drawn its line there. Instead, the *mishna* frames the distinction around the timing of the *shechita* relative to the father's death — slaughter before death versus death before slaughter. This framing strongly implies that the timing of the *shechita*, not the timing of any court proceeding, is the operative variable. The proposed answer is therefore inconsistent with the *mishna*'s structure.

### Partial Payment

The revised answer is that partial payment of four-and-five is in fact halakhically effective. In the first case — where the thief slaughtered before his father died — the liability for four-and-five attached in full at the moment of slaughter, when the animal was entirely not his. Even though upon inheriting he will not pay himself his proportional share of the penalty, he is still required to pay his brothers their portions. The *mishna*'s ruling that he pays four-and-five thus means: he pays his brothers their shares, even though he retains his own share. Partial payment in this way discharges the obligation.

The support for this reading comes from the *mishna* itself: *meshalem tashlumei arba'ah vachamishah* — the *mishna* says flatly that he pays four-and-five, not that he pays four-and-five minus his inherited share. This indicates that paying to siblings while retaining one's own portion is nonetheless considered payment of the full penalty.

### Rava's Concession

Rav Nachman had initially answered Rava's question about the two-partner case by saying *chamisha vakar amar rachmana velo chamisha chatzei vakar* — the Torah specified five whole cattle, not five half-cattle. But by the morning — *betzafra* — Rava reported that Rav Nachman reconsidered and held instead that *chamisha vakar rachamana ve'afilu chamisha chatzei bakar* — five units of cattle means five times whatever one stole, even if it was half-cattle. This fits better with the *mishna*, since the *mishna* clearly shows that a thief can be liable for four-and-five even when he will not be paying the full amount. As for why Rav Nachman had not seen this the night before — he offered that *delo achli bisra*, he had not eaten meat. Rashi reads this as a figure of speech: he had not thought the matter through carefully enough. *Tosafot*, characteristically more literal, reads it as a reference to the Nine Days: *shari beta'anis havah* — he had been fasting, and his mind was not fully engaged.

### Liability vs. Payment

With that established, the *Gemara* returns to the fundamental question: if partial payment suffices in the case where the father died after the slaughter, why does the father's death before the slaughter exempt entirely? If the thief slaughtered the animal after inheriting a share of it, why not say he pays four-and-five on the remaining portion belonging to his brothers?

The *Gemara*'s final resolution draws a sharp distinction between the conditions for *liability* and the conditions for *payment*. Liability for four-and-five must attach at a moment when the animal was entirely the property of another — *utvacho kulo be'issura* — the slaughter must be wholly a slaughter of that which is forbidden to him. In the first case, where the father died after the slaughter, the animal was entirely the father's at the moment of slaughter; liability attached in full. What happens afterward — the thief inherits a share, the payment is partial — concerns the mechanics of payment, not the moment of liability. Partial payment is sufficient to discharge a liability that was fully created.

In the second case, by contrast, the father died before the slaughter. At the moment the thief raised the knife, a portion of the animal already belonged to him by right of inheritance. The slaughter was therefore not *utvacho kulo be'issura* — it was not entirely a slaughter of another's property. The act fails to meet the threshold for creating liability in the first place. It is not a question of reduced payment; there is simply no liability. The *mishna*'s framing around the timing of the *shechita* relative to the father's death is thus precisely calibrated: it is the moment of slaughter that determines whether full liability arose, and that moment is either wholly in a state of prohibition or it is not.
