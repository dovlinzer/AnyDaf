# Shabbat 153 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Death & Eulogies

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִשְׁמָתוֹ עוֹלָה, וְשׁוּב אֵינָהּ יוֹרֶדֶת. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שִׁילַת מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: מֵהֶסְפֵּדוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם נִיכָּר אִם בֶּן הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא הוּא, אִם לָאו.
>
> **Translation:** **His soul ascends** to its place beneath the Throne of Glory, **and does not descend anymore.** **Rav Yehuda, son of Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat, said in the name of Rav: From a person's eulogy it is apparent whether or not he has a share in the World-to-Come.**

The *daf* opens with a fascinating *aggadata* unit that wraps up a broader disquisition about old age and death — a thread the *Gemara* has been developing in connection with dying generally and on *Shabbat* specifically. We begin at the top of 153a.

### Eulogy as Indicator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שִׁילַת: אַחֵים בְּהֶסְפֵּידָא, דְּהָתָם קָאֵימְנָא! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דִּמְחַמּוּ לֵיהּ וְחָאֵים. הָא — דִּמְחַמּוּ לֵיהּ וְלָא חָאֵים.
>
> **Translation:** Is it really true? Didn't Rav say to Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat: Stir the hearts of those gathered during my eulogy, for I will be standing there and listening to your words? **This is not difficult,** for **this** statement, which maintains that those who merit a share in the World-to-Come can be identified by their eulogies, is referring to a situation in which **they** attempt to **stir** the listener **and he is stirred;** while **that** statement is referring to a situation in which **they** attempt to **stir** the listener **and he is not stirred.**

*Amar Rav Yehuda brei d'Rav Shmuel bar Shilas mishmei d'Rav* — Rav Yehuda son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilas says in the name of Rav: *me'hespeddo shel adam nikkar im ben olam haba hu im lav* — from the eulogy of a person, one can tell whether he merits the world to come or not. This is, admittedly, a somewhat startling claim, since eulogies are notorious for embellishment. Yet the *Gemara* proceeds to work with this principle, and one might observe — as the Ramban does in a related context — that it is forbidden to exaggerate beyond the accepted norm. Even after subtracting conventional inflation, a eulogy still conveys a meaningful picture of who the person was.

The *Gemara* challenges this immediately: *ini*? Is it really true? It cites the fact that Rav himself said to Rav Shmuel bar Shilas, *achim be'hespeda d'hasam kamina* — warm the people up, get them emotionally stirred, at my own funeral. If a person's righteousness naturally produces the appropriate communal response, why would Rav need to give such instructions?

The *Gemara* answers: *lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. *Ha d'mchammu lei v'achim, v'ha d'mchammu lei v'lo achim* — sometimes you try to arouse the crowd and succeed; sometimes you try and do not succeed. The success or failure of the effort is itself the indicator.

### Rashi's Approach

Rashi clarifies that *me'hespeddo shel adam* does not refer primarily to the content of what is said at the eulogy — how lavishly the deceased is praised — but rather to how the *tzibur* responds: how much they cry, wail, and mourn. The depth of the communal emotional reaction is what signals the person's worthiness.

Rashi adds a further nuance, explaining why effort is needed at all, even for a genuinely righteous person. Four lines down in the narrow script, Rashi writes: *l'hespeda v'achim l'olam chimmuma ba'ei* — it truly requires working people up emotionally, *she'ein bnei adam nichmarim kol kach al zaken* — because people do not typically get so torn apart when someone dies after a full old age. If a person dies at 100, even if the loss is profound, the community has been expecting it, and the person lived a complete life. Hence, even for a genuine *tzaddik* who died in old age, the *maggid hesped* must labor to produce the appropriate response — and whether that labor succeeds tells us something real.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַבָּה: כְּגוֹן מָר דְּסָנוּ לֵיהּ כּוּלְּהוּ פּוּמְבְּדִיתָאֵי, מַאן מַחֵים הֶסְפֵּידָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִיסָּתַאי אַתְּ וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב חָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rabba: In the case of the Master,** i.e., Rabba, **whom all of the inhabitants of** his city, **Pumbedita, hate, who will be stirred during his eulogy?** He **said to him: It is sufficient for me** if **you and Rabba bar Rav Ḥanan** are stirred.

*Amar lei Abaye l'Rabba* — Abaye said to Rabba: what about a case like yours, master — *d'sane lei kulei Pumbedisa* — where the entire population of Pumbedisa hates you? Rabba was known for rebuking the people of Pumbedisa sharply; the city was cosmopolitan and, Rashi notes, prone to dishonesty and corruption, and Rabba would confront them, earning their resentment. *Man achim hespedeyhu* — who is going to get emotionally stirred at your eulogy? The challenge is pointed: a person can be profoundly righteous and still fail the "eulogy test" because his righteousness made him enemies.

### Rabba's Response

*Amar lei* — Rabba replied: *mistaya at v'Rabba bar Rav Chanan* — it would be enough just you and Rabba bar Rav Chanan. If those two are genuinely torn up at my funeral, that is all that matters. The broader masses who despise him have reasons for that reaction that say nothing about his actual greatness. The *tzibur* that matters is the circle that truly knows a person — and when that circle responds with genuine grief, it is a meaningful signal.

This represents a significant qualification of the principle. The *Gemara* acknowledges that public response in general is not always a reliable gauge. Sometimes a person who is not righteous at all attracts widespread mourning; sometimes the reverse. What matters is the response of those who are the appropriate measure of the person — and that is what tells us about *olam ha-ba*.

### Merit Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִנֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מֵרַב: אֵיזֶהוּ בֶּן הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״וְאׇזְנֶיךָ תִּשְׁמַעְנָה דָבָר מֵאַחֲרֶיךָ לֵאמֹר זֶה הַדֶּרֶךְ לְכוּ בוֹ כִּי תַאֲמִינוּ וְכִי תַשְׂמְאִילוּ״. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: כׇּל שֶׁדַּעַת רַבּוֹתֵינוּ נוֹחָה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar raised a dilemma** before **Rav: Which** type of person **has a share in the World-to-Come?** He **said to him: "And your ears shall hear a word behind you, saying: This is the path, walk on it, when you turn to the right or to the left"** (Isaiah 30:21). **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Anyone with whom our Rabbis are pleased** has a share in the World-to-Come.

*Ba'ei minei Rabbi Eliezer mei'Rav* — Rabbi Eliezer asked of Rav: *eizehu ben olam haba* — who is the person who merits the world to come? Rav replied with a verse from Yeshayahu: *ve'oznecha tishmanah davar mei'acharecha leimor zeh haderech lechu vo, ki sa'aminu vechi sasmilu* — your ears will hear something from behind you saying, "This is the path, walk on it," whether you turn right or left.

Rashi understands this literally: when you die you will hear a heavenly voice or an angel directing you on the path that the righteous have taken, and that itself tells you that you merit *olam ha-ba*. This reading, however admittedly limited in practical guidance, is supplemented by the Maharsha's interpretation. The Maharsha connects the verse to the famous *midrash* on the *pasuk* in the portion of Yisro, where Yisro advises Moshe to *vehodata lahem es haderech asher yelchu vah ve'es hama'aseh asher ya'asun* — and *Chazal* understand *haderech asher yelchu vah* to refer to ethical, interpersonal conduct: burying the dead, caring for the poor, *gemilus chasadim*, and acting *lifnim mishuras hadin*. The Maharsha reads Rav's verse as alluding to that same framework: *haderech zu gemilus chasadim, yelchu zu bikur cholim, bah zu kvurah, ki sa'aminu zeh hadin vechi sasmilu lifnim mishuras hadin*. It is the totality of a person's ethical and interpersonal behavior that is the measure of worthiness for *olam ha-ba*.

### Teachers' Approval

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְסָבְבוּ בַּשּׁוּק הַסּוֹפְדִים״, בְּנֵי גָלִילָא אָמְרִי: עֲשֵׂה דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי מִטָּתֶךָ, בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה אָמְרִי: עֲשֵׂה דְּבָרִים לְאַחַר מִטָּתֶךָ. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: מָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ, וּמָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In interpreting the verse: **"And the eulogizers walk about the marketplace"** (Ecclesiastes 12:5), **the people of the Galilee say: Do things** that you will want people to say at your eulogy **in front of your bier. The people of Judea say: Do things** that you want people to say at your eulogy **behind your bier.** The Gemara remarks: **And they do not disagree;** this **Sage** expressed it according to the norm **in his place, and** this **Sage** expressed it differently according to the norm **in his place.**

*Rabbi Chanina omer: kol sheda'as rabosav nochin heimenu* — anyone whose teachers derive *nachas* from him, that person merits *olam ha-ba*. It is the teachers specifically, not the parents, whose satisfaction is the indicator. A person who has genuinely grown in learning and character, such that those who trained him are proud of what he became — that is a sign of genuine spiritual achievement.

### Preparation Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: שׁוּב יוֹם אֶחָד לִפְנֵי מִיתָתֶךָ. שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וְכִי אָדָם יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶהוּ יוֹם יָמוּת? אָמַר לָהֶן: וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן, יָשׁוּב הַיּוֹם, שֶׁמָּא יָמוּת לְמָחָר, וְנִמְצָא כׇּל יָמָיו בִּתְשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned there** in a mishna that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Repent one day before your death. Rabbi Eliezer's students asked him: But does a person know the day** on which **he will die? He said to them: All the more so** this is a good piece of advice, and **one should repent today lest he die tomorrow; and** by following this advice **one will spend his entire life in** a state of **repentance.**

The *Gemara* now cites *Kohelet*: *vesovevu bashuk hasofdim* — the eulogizers will spread out through the marketplace. *Bnei Galila amri: asei devarim lifnei misascha* — the people of the Galil say: do things so that the eulogizers will speak about them in front of your bed, meaning in front of your coffin as it is carried out. *Bnei Yehuda amri: asei devarim le'achar misascha* — the people of Yehuda say: do things so that the eulogizers will speak of them behind your bed. *Velo pligi, mar ki'asrei umar ki'asrei* — they do not disagree; each follows the custom of his locale, since in the Galil eulogizers walked before the coffin while in Yehuda they walked behind. The underlying message, however, is identical: live in such a way that what the eulogizers say about you is what you would want said. Think about what you want written in your obituary — and then direct your life accordingly toward that end.

### Daily Repentance

*Tnan hasam* — we learned in a *Mishnah* elsewhere: *Rabbi Eliezer omer: shuv yom echad lifnei misascha* — do *teshuvah* one day before you die. His students challenged him: *vechi adam yodea eizehu yom yamut* — does a person know which day he will die? Rabbi Eliezer replied: *vechol sheken!* — all the more so! *Yashuv hayom, shema yamut lemachar* — repent today, for perhaps you will die tomorrow.

### Uncertainty Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף שְׁלֹמֹה אָמַר בְּחׇכְמָתוֹ: ״בְּכׇל עֵת יִהְיוּ בְגָדֶיךָ לְבָנִים וְשֶׁמֶן עַל רֹאשְׁךָ אַל יֶחְסָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** King **Solomon also said in his wisdom: "At all times your clothes should be white, and oil shall not be absent from upon your head"** (Ecclesiastes 9:8).

The force of this teaching is not merely about ensuring one dies in a state of *teshuvah*, but about using the uncertainty of death's timing as a strategy for living. *Venimtsa kol yamav bitshuva* — the result is that a person's entire life becomes one lived in *teshuvah*. The concern about being prepared at any moment propels continuous ethical and spiritual attentiveness — it is not just preparation for the end but a framework for the present.

### Purity Metaphors

*Ve'af Shlomo amar bechochmato: bechol eis yihyu vegadecha levanim veshemen al roshcha al yechsar* — and Shlomo too said in his wisdom: at all times your clothes should be white and the oil on your head should not be lacking. Rashi understands "white clothes" as referring to the soul — keep your soul clean, do *teshuvah*. The Maharsha reads "clothes" differently from the soul itself: *begadim* are what dress the soul, meaning your *actions* — your actions should at all times be pure, and where they are not, *teshuvah* must follow immediately.

### King's Feast Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ שֶׁזִּימֵּן אֶת עֲבָדָיו לִסְעוּדָה, וְלֹא קָבַע לָהֶם זְמַן. פִּיקְחִין שֶׁבָּהֶן קִישְּׁטוּ אֶת עַצְמָן וְיָשְׁבוּ עַל פֶּתַח בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ, אָמְרוּ: כְּלוּם חָסֵר לְבֵית הַמֶּלֶךְ? טִיפְּשִׁין שֶׁבָּהֶן הָלְכוּ לִמְלַאכְתָּן, אָמְרוּ: כְּלוּם יֵשׁ סְעוּדָה בְּלֹא טוֹרַח?
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said** the following story as **a parable** to this lesson: The situation is comparable **to a king who invited his servants to a feast and did not set a time for them** to come. **The wise among them adorned themselves and sat at the entrance to the king's house. They said: Is the king's house missing anything** necessary for the feast? Certainly the king could invite them at any moment. **The fools among them went to** attend to **their work** and **said: Is there such thing as a feast without** the **toil** of preparing for it?

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakai: mashal lemelech sheziman es avadav lise'uda, velo kava lahem zman* — a king invited his servants to a feast but did not specify when it would take place. *Pikchin shebahen kishu es atzman veyashvu al pesach beis hamelech* — the wise ones dressed themselves up and stood at the entrance of the king's palace. *Amru: klum chaser leveise hamelech* — they reasoned that the king's house lacks nothing; the feast can happen at any moment without advance preparation, so they must always be ready. *Tipshim shebahen halchu l'mlachtan* — the foolish ones went off to their work. *Amru: klum yesh seuda v'lo torach* — is there a feast without effort? We will have ample warning.

### Punishment Imagery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּתְאוֹם בִּיקֵּשׁ הַמֶּלֶךְ אֶת עֲבָדָיו. פִּיקְחִין שֶׁבָּהֶן נִכְנְסוּ לְפָנָיו כְּשֶׁהֵן מְקוּשָּׁטִין, וְהַטִּיפְּשִׁים נִכְנְסוּ לְפָנָיו כְּשֶׁהֵן מְלוּכְלָכִין. שָׂמַח הַמֶּלֶךְ לִקְרַאת פִּיקְחִים וְכָעַס לִקְרַאת טִיפְּשִׁים. אָמַר: הַלָּלוּ שֶׁקִּישְּׁטוּ אֶת עַצְמָן לַסְּעוּדָה — יֵשְׁבוּ וְיֹאכְלוּ וְיִשְׁתּוּ, הַלָּלוּ שֶׁלֹּא קִישְּׁטוּ עַצְמָן לַסְּעוּדָה — יַעַמְדוּ וְיִרְאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Suddenly, the king requested** that **his servants** come to the feast. **The wise among them entered before him adorned** in their finest clothes, **and the fools entered before him dirty. The king was happy to greet** the **wise ones and angry to greet** the **fools.** The king **said: These** wise servants **who adorned themselves for the feast shall sit and eat and drink,** but **these** fools **who did not adorn themselves for the feast shall stand and watch.**

*Upitom bikesh hamelech es avadav* — and suddenly the king summoned his servants. *Pikchin shebahen nichnasu l'fanev k'shehen mekushatin* — the wise ones entered before him dressed and ready. *V'atipshim nichnasu l'fanev k'shehen meluchlachin* — the foolish ones came before him dirty. The application is stark: people assume they have time to begin living better, to do *teshuvah*, to start taking their obligations seriously — but tragedy and death can come without warning, *chas v'shalom*, and one cannot wait.

*V'kaas likrat tipshim*. The king was pleased with the prepared ones and angry at the foolish. *Halalu shekishru atzman l'seuda, yeshvu v'yochlu v'yishtu* — those who prepared themselves may sit, eat, and drink. *Halalu shelo kishru atzman l'seuda, yaamdu v'yiru* — those who did not prepare must stand and watch.

### Contrast in Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲתָנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי מֵאִיר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר אָמַר: אַף הֵן נִרְאִין כִּמְשַׁמְּשִׁין. אֶלָּא אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ יוֹשְׁבִין: הַלָּלוּ — אוֹכְלִין, וְהַלָּלוּ — רְעֵבִין. הַלָּלוּ — שׁוֹתִין, וְהַלָּלוּ — צְמֵאִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ הִנֵּה עֲבָדַי יֹאכֵלוּ וְאַתֶּם תִּרְעָבוּ הִנֵּה עֲבָדַי יִשְׁתּוּ וְאַתֶּם תִּצְמָאוּ הִנֵּה עֲבָדַי יָרוֹנּוּ מִטּוּב לֵב וְאַתֶּם תִּצְעֲקוּ מִכְּאֵב לֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir's son-in-law said in the name of Rabbi Meir:** If the punishment for those who did not prepare themselves in advance was merely to stand and watch, it would not be severe enough because **they also look like servants** at the feast, which is not such a disgraceful punishment. **Rather, these and these,** both groups of people, **sit** at the feast. **These** wise and righteous people **eat, and these** wicked fools **are hungry; these** righteous people **drink, and these** wicked people **are thirsty, as it is stated:** "Therefore, **thus said the Lord,** God: **Behold, My servants shall eat and you shall be hungry; behold, My servants shall drink and you shall be thirsty;** behold, My servants shall rejoice and you shall be ashamed. **Behold, My servants shall sing from a joyous heart and you shall scream from a pained heart"** (Isaiah 65:13–14).

The son-in-law of Rabbi Meir adds in Rabbi Meir's name: *af hein nirin k'mashmashin* — even they appear as though they are attending upon the diners, since they stand around them like servants. So the final formulation is sharpened: *elu v'elu yoshvin* — both groups are seated. *Halalu ochlin v'halalu re'evin* — these eat and the others go hungry. *Halalu shotin v'halalu tzemayim* — these drink and the others go thirsty. As the verse in Yeshayahu states: *koh amar Hashem hinei avadai yocheilu v'atem tir'avu, hinei avadai yishtu v'atem titzma'u, hinei avadai yaronu mi'tuv lev v'atem titzaku mikh'eiv lev*.

The sitting at the same table while being unable to eat is, in its way, a more acute exclusion than standing: it makes the person's deprivation absolutely explicit. This connects to the *Gemara*'s teaching elsewhere that *Gehenom* for the *reshaim* may consist of the same experience that is *Gan Eden* for the *tzaddikim* — the same sun that warms and illuminates the righteous destroys the wicked. The famous parable of the feast where all arms are rigid and those in *Gan Eden* feed one another while those in *Gehenom* starve captures the same idea: identical circumstances, utterly different experiences.

### Mitzvot as Prep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״בְּכׇל עֵת יִהְיוּ בְגָדֶיךָ לְבָנִים״ — אֵלּוּ צִיצִית, ״וְשֶׁמֶן עַל רֹאשְׁךָ אַל יֶחְסָר״ — אֵלּוּ תְּפִילִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the verse quoted above can be interpreted in the following way: **"At all times let your clothes be white"; this** is clothing that contains **ritual fringes [*tzitzit*],** which are white. **"And oil shall not be absent from upon your head"; these** words hint to **phylacteries,** which are worn on the head.

*Davar acher: b'chol eit yihyu v'gadecha l'vanim* — the *Gemara* offers an alternative reading of the verse from *Kohelet*. Here, "white clothes" refers to *tzitzit* — the white strings of the *tzitzit*. The Maharsha notes that *b'chol eit*, "at all times," comes to include even circumstances where one lacks *techelet* and the strings are white alone; even in that incomplete form one must wear them. *V'shemen al roshcha al yechsar* — "the oil on your head should not be lacking" refers to *tefillin* of the head. Again the Maharsha notes that *b'chol eit* means even if one lacks the *tefillin* of the arm, one must still ensure one wears the *tefillin* of the head. More broadly, the *Gemara* in *Berakhot* notes that a man surrounded by *mitzvot* — wearing *tzitzit* and *tefillin* — is far more likely to lead a virtuous life overall.

### Aggadic Conclusion

With *Hadran Alach Sho'el*, the *perek* concludes. This was a remarkable unit traversing the territory from death and eulogies through repentance, preparation, and the eternal consequences of how one lives. We now begin the final *perek* of *Maseches Shabbat*: *Mi Shehecheshich Bederech* — what does one do when caught away from home as *Shabbat* begins.

## Traveling at Shkiya

### Problem Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהֶחֱשִׁיךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ — נוֹתֵן כִּיסוֹ לְגוֹי, וְאִם אֵין עִמּוֹ גּוֹי — מַנִּיחוֹ עַל הַחֲמוֹר. הִגִּיעַ לֶחָצֵר הַחִיצוֹנָה — נוֹטֵל אֶת הַכֵּלִים הַנִּיטָּלִין בַּשַּׁבָּת. וְשֶׁאֵינָן נִיטָּלִין בַּשַּׁבָּת — מַתִּיר הַחֲבָלִים, וְהַשַּׂקִּין נוֹפְלִין (מֵאֲילֵיהֶם).
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One** who was traveling on Shabbat eve and **night fell,** and Shabbat began while he was still **en route, gives his** money **pouch to a gentile** traveling with him. **And if there is no gentile with him he places it on the donkey.** Once **he reached the outer courtyard** of the city, where belongings can be securely placed, **he takes the vessels that may be moved on Shabbat** off the donkey. With regard to the vessels **that may not be moved on Shabbat, he unties the ropes** that attach his bags to the donkey, **and the bags** of vessels **fall on their own.**

The final *perek* takes up a practical scenario: a person is traveling and, with his wallet and donkey and all his belongings, finds himself caught on the road as *shkiya* arrives. Something like being stuck in one's car as *Shabbat* is about to begin. What does one do?

### Non-Jew Solution

The *Mishnah* presents the first solution: *noten kiso l'nochri* — give your wallet to the non-Jew traveling with you. If there is no non-Jew, or perhaps no non-Jew one trusts, *u'manicho al hachamor* — place it on the donkey. Once one enters the outer courtyard of the city — a space surrounded by the walls of buildings that constitutes *reshut hayachid* — one may remove from the donkey the *kelim* that are not *muktze*. For the *muktze* items, one unties the ropes — assuming they are a *kesher she'eino shel kayama*, a non-permanent knot permitted to untie on *Shabbat* — and the bags fall down on their own.

### Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא שָׁרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְמִיתַּב כִּיסֵיהּ לְגוֹי? קִים לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּאֵין אָדָם מַעֲמִיד עַצְמוֹ עַל מָמוֹנוֹ, אִי לָא שָׁרֵית לֵיהּ — אָתֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna: One who was traveling on Shabbat eve and night fell, and Shabbat began while he was still en route, gives his money pouch to a gentile. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that **the Sages permitted him to give his pouch to a gentile?** Is it not prohibited for a Jew to ask a gentile to perform a prohibited labor on Shabbat? The Gemara answers: **The Sages maintain** that **a person does not restrain himself** when faced with losing **his money. If you do not permit him** to give his pouch to a gentile, **he will come to carry four cubits in a public domain,** thereby violating a Torah prohibition.

*Mai ta'ama sharu lei rabanan l'meitav kiseih l'nochri?* — why did the Rabbis permit giving the wallet to the non-Jew? This is, at minimum, a rabbinic problem of *amira l'akum*, instructing a non-Jew to perform *melacha* on one's behalf. The *Gemara* answers: *kim lehu l'rabanan de'ein adam ma'amid atzmo al mamono* — the Rabbis have established that a person will not stand idly by regarding his money. Even the most observant individual, faced with the prospect of abandoning his wallet, will rationalize keeping it on his person. Therefore, *ei lo sharis lei, asi l'asuyei arba amos b'reshus harabim* — if they do not provide a lesser-prohibited outlet, he will end up carrying it through the public domain in outright violation of a biblical prohibition. The *Gemara* recognizes — as it does in other contexts such as a fire breaking out on *Shabbat* — that sometimes one must find a *muttar* alternative or the person will act in outright transgression regardless.

### Found vs. Owned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דַּוְקָא כִּיסוֹ, אֲבָל מְצִיאָה — לָא. פְּשִׁיטָא, ״כִּיסוֹ״ תְּנַן! מַהוּ דְתֵימָא הוּא הַדִּין אֲפִילּוּ מְצִיאָה, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״כִּיסוֹ״ — אוֹרְחָא דְמִילְּתָא קָתָנֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְלָא אֲמַרַן, אֶלָּא דְּלָא אֲתַי לִידֵיהּ, אֲבָל אֲתַי לִידֵיהּ — כְּכִיסֵיהּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This allowance to give the pouch to a gentile is **specifically** with regard to **his** own **pouch, but** in the case of **a lost object** that he found, **no,** it was not permitted. The Gemara asks: That is **obvious,** as **we learned** in the mishna: **His pouch,** and nothing else. The Gemara answers: Rava specified this **lest you say** that **the same is true even** with regard to **a lost object,** that one may give it to a gentile on Shabbat, and **the** mishna **taught** the case of **his pouch** merely because it is the **manner** in **which the matter** typically occurs. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that the mishna is in fact establishing a *halakha* restricted to his pouch. The Gemara comments: **And we only said** that this allowance does not apply to a lost object when **it did not come into his possession** before Shabbat. **However,** if the object already **came into his possession** before Shabbat, **its** legal status is **like** that of **his pouch.**

*Amar Rabbah: davka kiso* — this dispensation applies only to one's own wallet. *Aval metzia lo* — if one found an object in the street during *Shabbat*, the Rabbis did not permit having a non-Jew carry it. We are not afraid that a person will violate *Shabbat* over someone else's money, since he lacks that visceral attachment.

The *Gemara* pushes back: *pishita, kiso tanya* — of course, the *Mishnah* says "his wallet." One might have thought, however, that the *Mishnah* simply uses the standard case and the same rule would apply even to a found object — *orcha d'milsa kasani*. The *Gemara* establishes: *ka mashma lan* — no, the distinction is substantive. A found object that has not yet been picked up does not trigger the same concern.

### Found Object Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, בָּעֵי רָבָא: מְצִיאָה הַבָּאָה לְיָדוֹ מַהוּ, כֵּיוָן דַּאֲתַי לִידֵיהּ — כְּכִיסֵיהּ דָּמֵי? אוֹ דִילְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּלָא טָרַח בַּהּ — לָאו כְּכִיסֵיהּ דָּמֵי. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Some state** this dilemma in a different manner. **Rava raised a dilemma:** With regard to **a lost object that came into his possession** before Shabbat, **what is** the ruling? Is the *halakha* that **since it** already **came into his possession, its** legal status is **like** that of **his pouch? Or perhaps, since he did not exert** himself to acquire **it, its** legal status is **not like** that of **his pouch.** Since he expended no effort, he would be capable of restraining himself even when faced with losing it. Therefore, there is no need for the Sages to permit him to give it to a gentile. The Gemara concludes: **Let** this dilemma **stand** unresolved.

*V'lo amran ela d'lo asai l'yadei* — that is only when the object has not yet come into his hand. *Aval asai l'yadei, k'kisei dami* — if he has already picked it up, it is treated like his own wallet, and the same concern applies. We are now afraid he will not leave it behind, and so a *muttar* outlet is needed.

*Ika d'amri* — an alternative version presents this as an open question (*baei Rabbah*): if a lost object came into his hand, do we say *keivan d'asai l'yado k'kisei dami* — since it came to his hand it is like his wallet? Or do we say *keivan d'lo tarach bah, lav k'kisei dami* — since he expended no effort, it did not arrive through his labor, and we are not concerned he will violate *Shabbat* for it? The *Gemara* leaves this as *teiku*, unresolved.

### Donkey Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין עִמּוֹ גּוֹי. טַעְמָא דְּאֵין עִמּוֹ גּוֹי, הָא יֵשׁ עִמּוֹ גּוֹי — לְגוֹי יָהֵיב לֵיהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? חֲמוֹר — אַתָּה מְצֻוֶּוה עַל שְׁבִיתָתוֹ, גּוֹי — אִי אַתָּה מְצֻוֶּוה עַל שְׁבִיתָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And if there is no gentile with him** he places it on the donkey. The Gemara infers: **The reason** is specifically because **there is no gentile with him;** if **there is a gentile with him, he gives it to the gentile** and does not place it on a donkey. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: With regard to **a donkey** belonging to a Jew, **you are commanded with regard to its rest** on Shabbat. With regard to **a gentile, you are not commanded with regard to his rest** and no Torah prohibition is being violated.

*Ein imo nochri* — if there is no non-Jew available, the *Mishnah* permits placing the wallet on the donkey. The *Gemara* confirms: *ta'ama d'ein imo nochri* — the reason is specifically the absence of a non-Jew. *Ha yesh imo nochri, nochri adif* — if there is a non-Jew available, it is preferable to give it to the non-Jew rather than place it on the donkey.

### Legal Distinction

*Mai ta'ama?* — why is the non-Jew preferable? *Chamor atah metzuveh al shevisaso* — regarding your donkey, you are commanded to ensure its rest: *Lo taaseh kol melacha* concludes with *shorcha vachamorcha*, your ox and your donkey. You bear a positive biblical obligation of *shevitat behemto*. *Ee ata metzuveh al shevitato* — regarding a non-Jew, however, you bear no such obligation. He is not under your authority in the relevant sense, and having him carry involves no biblical violation — only a rabbinic one. Hence the non-Jew is the preferred option where available.

### Hierarchy of Options

### Ranking People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמוֹר וְחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן — אַחֲמוֹר מַנַּח לֵיהּ, לְחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן לָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָנֵי — אָדָם, הַאי — לָאו אָדָם. חֵרֵשׁ וְשׁוֹטֶה — לְשׁוֹטֶה. שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן — לְשׁוֹטֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara teaches an additional *halakha*: If there was no gentile with him but there was **a donkey, a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor,** meaning someone under thirteen years old, **one places it on the donkey,** but **one neither gives it to the deaf-mute, nor the imbecile, nor the minor. What is the reason** for this? Although they are not obligated to fulfill mitzvot, **these** are **people,** but **this** donkey **is not a person.** It is preferable to place it on the donkey rather than give it to a person. And if **a deaf-mute and an imbecile** were with him, he gives it to **the imbecile.** If **an imbecile and a minor** were with him, he gives it to **the imbecile.**

The *Gemara* then constructs a full hierarchy. Suppose you have with you a donkey and also a *cheresh* (a deaf-mute), a *shoteh* (one of unsound mind), and a *kattan* (a minor). All three of the latter are not fully *bar daat*, not yet obligated adults. Which is the preferred carrier — the donkey or one of those three?

*Chamor manach lay, cheresh shoteh ve-kattan lo yahiv lay* — it is better to load the donkey than to give it to any of the three. This is not obvious: loading the donkey risks transgressing the biblical obligation of *shevitat behemto*. The *Gemara* explains — and this is a striking passage — *hani adam hani lav adam*: these are people, and the donkey is not.

### Chinuch Concerns

Rather than simply resolving which option involves the lesser immediate technical violation, the *Gemara* takes a broader perspective rooted in *chinuch*. The donkey has no understanding of what is happening. But a *kattan* will eventually become an adult; a *shoteh* or *cheresh* may recover. And beyond those individuals, the broader act of habitually using people — even those currently exempt — to carry on *Shabbat* creates patterns that will influence adult Jewish behavior. The long-term implications outweigh the immediate technical question of whether using the donkey might more directly brush against a biblical prohibition.

### Deaf-Mute Priority

*Cheresh ve-shoteh* — if one must choose between a *cheresh* and a *shoteh*, *le-shoteh yahiv lei* — give it to the *shoteh*. The *Gemara* elsewhere recognizes that a *cheresh* possesses a degree of intelligence; some opinions hold him to be a full *bar daat*. A *shoteh*, while in a state of mental illness, is considered entirely lacking in *da'at*. Hence the *cheresh* is closer to full halachic adulthood, and it would be more problematic to use him.

*Shoteh ve-kattan* — between the *shoteh* and the minor, *le-shoteh yahiv lei* again — give it to the *shoteh*. A minor will become an adult, and the *chinuch* concern looms large.

### Deaf-Mute Status

It is worth pausing to note that the treatment of the *cheresh* in halachic literature raises sensitive and evolving questions. Halachically, *cheresh* refers specifically to the deaf-mute — one who is both deaf and unable to speak. A person who is only deaf but can speak bears no halachic disability. Historically, one might have assumed that few deaf people were also mute; but in fact, prior to hearing aids and modern audiological support, the absence of auditory feedback made normal enunciation essentially impossible for a standard deaf person, meaning most deaf people would indeed have fallen into the *cheresh* category.

Since the establishment of schools for the deaf beginning in the 18th century, and particularly with the development of sign language, there has been significant halachic evolution. The question of whether sign language constitutes speech is debated among *poskim*, and many contemporary authorities hold that a deaf-mute today no longer falls into the traditional halachic category of *cheresh* — a position that represents a major development, though not all *poskim* concur.

### Cheresh vs. Kattan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: חֵרֵשׁ וְקָטָן מַאי? אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: תְּרוּמַת חֵרֵשׁ, לֹא תֵּצֵא לְחוּלִּין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised** before the Sages: If **a deaf-mute and a minor** were with him, **what** is the ruling? To whom does he give his pouch? The Gemara answers: **According to** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer you have no dilemma.** Rabbi Eliezer holds that a deaf-mute has a greater degree of halakhic intelligence than does a minor, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yitzḥak says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: The *teruma* of a deaf-mute** that he separated from his produce **does not vacate** its sanctified status and assume **non-sacred** status **because his** status with regard to halakhic intelligence **is uncertain.**

*Iba'ye l'hu: cheresh ve-kattan* — the *Gemara* raises the harder question: what if one must choose between a *cheresh* and a minor? On one hand, the *kattan* will become an adult, making the *chinuch* concern acute. On the other hand, the *cheresh*, while an adult, has some degree of *da'at* and — depending on the view — may be closer to full halachic standing.

*Aliba d'Rabbi Eliezer lo tiba'ye lach* — according to Rabbi Eliezer, there is no question. A *Tosefta* records that Rabbi Yitzchak says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: *trumas cheresh lo tesei l'chullin mipnei shehu safek* — *terumah* separated by a *cheresh* does not lose its status of *terumah* because his halachic capacity is uncertain. He may be a full *bar daat*, and therefore one would certainly prefer to give it to the *kattan* rather than to someone who might be a full halachic adult violating *Shabbat*.

### Rabbis' View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, דִּתְנַן: חֲמִשָּׁה לֹא יִתְרוֹמוּ, וְאִם תָּרְמוּ אֵין תְּרוּמָתָן תְּרוּמָה, אֵלּוּ הֵן: חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, וְהַתּוֹרֵם אֶת שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְגוֹי שֶׁתָּרַם אֶת שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲפִילּוּ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ אֵין תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When you raise this dilemma,** the issue **is in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, as we learned** in a mishna: There are **five** categories of people who **may not separate *teruma*** *ab initio*, **and if they separated *teruma*, their *teruma* is not** considered ***teruma*. They are: A deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor, and one who separates *teruma*** from produce **that is not his; and a gentile who separated *teruma*** from the produce **of a Jew, even with his permission, his *teruma* is not** considered ***teruma***.**

*Ki tiba'ye lach, aliba d'Rabbanan* — the question arises according to the Rabbis, who hold that the *cheresh* definitively lacks full halachic status. The *Mishnah* teaches: *chamishah lo yitrumu v'im tormu ein trumasam trumah* — five categories may not take *terumah* and if they do it is not effective, including *cheresh, shoteh*, and *kattan*. According to this view, the *cheresh* is definitively not a full *bar daat* and his carrying would not constitute a full *chillul Shabbat*.

### Unresolved Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי? לְחֵרֵשׁ יָהֵיב לֵיהּ, דְּקָטָן אָתֵי לִכְלַל דַּעַת, אוֹ דִילְמָא לְקָטָן יָהֵיב לֵיהּ, דְּחֵרֵשׁ אָתֵי לְאִחַלּוֹפֵי בְּגָדוֹל פִּיקֵּחַ. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי לְחֵרֵשׁ יָהֵיב לֵיהּ, אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי לְקָטָן יָהֵיב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** should one do? Should **he give it to the deaf-mute** because **the minor** will eventually **reach the stage of** halakhic **intelligence,** when he will be obligated to observe the mitzvot, and it is preferable that he not become accustomed to desecrating Shabbat? **Or perhaps he** should **give it to the minor,** because if he is allowed to give it to the **deaf-mute,** onlookers **will mistake him for an adult with** halakhic **intelligence** and conclude that it is permitted to give his pouch to an adult? In that case it would be preferable to give the pouch to a minor, as it is clear that he is not obligated in mitzvot. Regarding this *halakha*, **some say** that **he gives it to the deaf-mute** and **some say** that **he gives it to the minor,** and no definitive conclusion was reached.

So the question becomes: is it better to give it to the *cheresh* — since he is definitively not halachically adult and therefore creates no *chillul Shabbat* concern — but risk that onlookers see an apparent adult Jew carrying on *Shabbat*, leading others to do the same? Or is it better to give it to the *kattan* — since he will become an adult and it is therefore worse for his *chinuch* — but a child is obviously a child and onlookers will not draw the wrong conclusion?

*Ika d'amri l'cheresh yahiv lei, ika d'amri l'kattan yahiv lei* — the *Gemara* records opinions on both sides without resolving the matter. What is most striking across this entire passage is the consistent willingness of the *Gemara* to weigh long-term educational and communal consequences against immediate technical halachic considerations — and in certain cases to allow those broader concerns to override what might seem the technically preferable solution in the immediate case.

### Hidden Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין שָׁם לֹא גּוֹי וְלֹא חֲמוֹר וְלֹא חֵרֵשׁ וְלֹא שׁוֹטֶה וְלֹא קָטָן, מַאי? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: עוֹד אַחֶרֶת הָיְתָה, וְלֹא רָצוּ חֲכָמִים לְגַלּוֹתָהּ. מַאי ״עוֹד אַחֶרֶת הָיְתָה״? מוֹלִיכוֹ פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. אַמַּאי לֹא רָצוּ חֲכָמִים לְגַלּוֹתָהּ? — מִשּׁוּם ״כְּבוֹד אֱלֹהִים הַסְתֵּר דָּבָר וּכְבוֹד מְלָכִים חֲקוֹר דָּבָר״. וְהָכָא, מַאי ״כְּבוֹד אֱלֹהִים״ אִיכָּא? דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If **there is neither a gentile nor a donkey nor a deaf-mute nor an imbecile nor a minor there, what** should one do? **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: There was another manner** of dealing with this situation, **and the Sages did not want to reveal it.** The Gemara asks: To **what** was Rabbi Yitzḥak referring when he said: **There was another manner?** The Gemara answers: The alternative is to **move** the pouch in increments, **each less than four cubits,** and thereby carry the object in the public domain without violating a Torah prohibition. The Gemara asks: **Why didn't the Sages want to reveal this** alternative? The Gemara answers that it is **due to** the verse: **"It is the glory of God to conceal a thing; but the glory of kings is to search out a matter"** (Proverbs 25:2). **And here, what glory of God is there** in concealing this option? The Gemara answers: If one were to move the pouch in this manner, there is concern **lest he** come to carry the object **four cubits in the public domain.**

*Ein sham lo nochri v'lo chamor v'lo cheresh v'lo shoteh v'lo kattan* — if none of these are available: no non-Jew, no animal, no one exempt from *Shabbat* obligations. *Amar Rabbi Yitzchak: od acheres haysa, v'lo ratzu chachamim l'galosah* — there was another solution, but the Sages did not want to reveal it. Of course the *Gemara* now proceeds to tell us what it is: *molicho pachas pachas m'arba amos* — carry it in increments of less than four *amos* at a time. Each act of carrying is defined by its start (*akira*) and its end (*hanacha*). Walking less than four *amos* between each stop means no single act of carrying meets the *shiur* of a biblical violation. Done sequentially, it obviously covers the full distance home — but each individual segment remains below the threshold.

### Concealment Rationale

*Amay lo ratzu chachamim l'galoto?* — why did the Sages not wish to reveal this solution? *Mishum kevod Elokim haster davar, u'kevod melachim chakor davar* — honoring God sometimes requires concealing a matter; honoring kings requires investigating it. Rashi explains: protecting *kevod shamayim*, preventing people from sinning, sometimes means not spelling out every available leniency. If this solution were public knowledge, people would come to abuse it. The concern is specific: *de-mati le-asuyei arba amos b'reshus harabim* — revealing it might easily lead to actual biblical violation, since it is all too easy to lose count and carry more than four *amos* in one stretch.

### Disclosure Debate

This raises a genuine tension that recurs in *halachic* practice: there are certain leniencies whose existence ought to be known, but whose broad publication may produce the opposite of the intended effect. The concern is not merely about "control" — it is about the educational dynamics of how leniencies function. When a *b'diavad* option becomes too publicly known, it tends to become a *lechatchila*, and the robust baseline obligation loses its force.

At the same time, the problem with non-disclosure in this specific case is obvious: if a person is stranded outside the city with no one to call and no way to find out the ruling, concealing the solution does him no good whatsoever. There is no opportunity to quietly advise him *be'sha'as hadechak*. This makes the case for non-disclosure harder to sustain here, and the tension is real.

### Rabbinic Concerns

There is an important principle underlying the structure of these discussions: *ein domeh gezeiros Chazal zu l'zu* — one cannot straightforwardly extrapolate from one rabbinic enactment or dispensation to another. Each context has its own considerations about how anxious a person will be, what the public visibility of the act is, and what the downstream consequences of permitting it might be. The scenarios of fire on *Shabbat* and financial loss have different profiles, even though both involve the concern that a person under pressure will violate *Shabbat* if no outlet is provided.

### Honor Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם גָּדְשׁוּ סְאָה. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם מָחֲקוּ סְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: On that day they** measured with **a large *se'a*** measurement. **Rabbi Yehoshua said: On that very day they** measured with **a minimal *se'a*.**

*Kevod Elokim haster davar, u'kevod melachim chakor davar* — the verse from *Mishlei* sets up the tension elegantly. On one side: the honor of God sometimes requires concealment, the withholding of information that would be spiritually dangerous in unqualified hands. On the other side: the honor of kings — and by extension, the ideal of Torah scholarship — requires investigating and uncovering the truth, bringing hidden matters to light.

The tension between these principles is not merely academic. A *posek* is regularly faced with the question of how much to share. When someone asks a *halachic* question, they are not always asking for a philosophically complete answer — they are often implicitly asking to be helped to *keep halacha*. Spelling out every *b'diavad* pathway in the answer to a practical question can undermine the questioner's sense of the primary obligation. Yet withholding information that the person genuinely needs — especially when they have no other way to access it — crosses a different line.

### Measures Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: מָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה — לַקּוּפָּה מְלֵאָה קִישּׁוּאִין וְדִילּוּעִין, אָדָם נוֹתֵן לְתוֹכָהּ חַרְדָּל — וְהִיא מַחְזֶקֶת. מָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה — לַעֲרֵיבָה מְלֵאָה דְּבַשׁ, נוֹתֵן לְתוֹכָהּ רִימּוֹנִים וֶאֱגוֹזִים — וְהִיא מְקִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in another *baraita*: The Sages articulated **a parable** to illustrate the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. To what is** this **matter similar?** It is similar **to a basket full of squash and gourds into which a person places mustard seeds.** Just as the basket also **holds** the mustard, the decree will also endure. They also articulated **a parable** to illustrate the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua: To what is** this **matter similar?** It is similar **to a large bowl full of honey into which** one **places pomegranates and walnuts.** Just as the bowl **expels** the honey from inside it, so too, the decree will cause Torah prohibitions to be violated.

According to Rashi's explanation, this is precisely the debate about whether concealing the *pachos pachos m'arba amos* solution was wise. Rabbi Eliezer holds that it was — like pouring mustard seeds into a basket full of gourds and cucumbers (*le-kuppah mele'ah kishu'in ve-dilu'in, adam noten le-tochah chardal, ve-hi machzeket*): the small additions fill in the gaps and the whole holds together. The concealment creates a protective barrier around the biblical prohibition and the system works.

Rabbi Yehoshua holds the opposite: *domeh le-arivah mele'ah devash, noten le-tochah rimonim ve-egozim, ve-hi mekiah* — it is like a trough full of honey into which you try to force pomegranates and walnuts — it spits them out. If you try to seal off a leniency without actually providing it, people will find the situation untenable and simply violate the prohibition outright. The *chumra* produces its own *kula*: by refusing to provide a legitimate outlet, you drive people to transgress. According to this view, the Sages should have disclosed the *pachos pachos* solution openly.

### Later Implementation

In practice, the *halacha* follows that *pachos pachos m'arba amos* is a valid solution, and the fact that the *Gemara* itself reveals it represents at least a partial embrace of disclosure. The precise balance — teaching the solution where needed while not broadcasting it indiscriminately — captures the middle path between the two positions.

### Rambam's View

The Rambam takes a striking further step. Returning to the unresolved *teiku* about the *metziah* — the found object that came into one's hand — the Rambam rules that while one cannot give it to a non-Jew and cannot place it on a donkey in that case, *pachos pachos m'arba amos* remains available even there. The Rambam's reasoning, as he explains in a *teshuvah*, is that unlike the other solutions — giving to a non-Jew or loading a donkey — which are more visible and public acts, moving an object less than four *amos* at a time is a private, inconspicuous method. Therefore, even for a *metziah*, this lesser-known solution remains accessible. The *Gra* identifies a *Gemara* that may serve as the basis for the Rambam's position, though the Rambam's application extends beyond what the *Gemara* itself explicitly states.

## Animal Work Issues

### Work Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר אֵין עִמּוֹ גּוֹי מַנִּיחוֹ עַל הַחֲמוֹר. וַהֲלֹא מְחַמֵּר, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה כׇל מְלָאכָה״!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **the Master said:** When **there is no gentile with him, one places** the pouch **on the donkey.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't he** thereby **driving a** laden **animal? And the Torah stated:** "And the seventh day is Shabbat for the Lord your God, **you shall not perform any manner of labor,** neither you, nor your son, nor your daughter, nor your slave, nor your maidservant, nor your animal, nor your stranger that is within your gates" (Exodus 20:10). He is causing the prohibited labors of carrying from domain to domain and of carrying in the public domain by placing the pouch on his donkey on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* now returns to the donkey solution and confronts the biblical problem that was set aside earlier. Placing a load on one's donkey on *Shabbat* would seem to transgress the prohibition of *mechamer* — having one's animal perform labor. The verse *lo taaseh kol melacha* concludes with *shorcha vachamorcha*, explicitly including one's animals. The Rambam, it should be noted, emphasizes this not as a *lav* but as the *aseh* of *lema'an yanua'ch shorcha vachamorcha* — a positive obligation to ensure one's animals rest — though the *Gemara* here frames it as a negative prohibition. This distinction will be explored further in subsequent discussion.

### Walking Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מַנִּיחוֹ עָלֶיהָ כְּשֶׁהִיא מְהַלֶּכֶת. וְהָא אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא קָיְימָא לְהַשְׁתִּין מַיִם וּלְהַטִּיל גְּלָלִים, וְאִיכָּא עֲקִירָה וְהַנָּחָה! כְּשֶׁהִיא מְהַלֶּכֶת — מַנִּיחוֹ עָלֶיהָ, כְּשֶׁהִיא עוֹמֶדֶת — נוֹטְלוֹ הֵימֶנָּה. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ חֲבֵרוֹ נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: One places** the pouch **on** the donkey **as it is walking,** because in doing so he does not commit a full-fledged violation of the Torah prohibition against carrying out from domain to domain on Shabbat. A full-fledged violation consists of lifting and placing the object. Because the animal was already walking when the pouch was placed on it, the animal performed no lifting. The Gemara asks: **Isn't it impossible** that the animal **will not stand** in its place at some point after it begins walking, either in order **to urinate or to defecate, and** when it begins walking again **there is** both an act of **lifting and** an act of **placing** performed by the donkey. The Gemara answers: There is a solution to that problem. **When** the donkey **is walking, one places** the pouch **on it,** and **when it stands, he removes it.** The Gemara asks: **If so,** one could **even** place the pouch **on another** Jew **as well,** as there would be neither lifting nor placing.

*De'amar Rav Adda bar Abba: menicha aleha k'she'hi mehalechet* — one waits until the donkey begins walking and then places the load upon it. The halachic significance: *melacha* in the context of carrying is defined by *akira* and *hanacha* — the initial lifting and the final setting down. If the donkey was already in motion when the load was placed upon it, the donkey itself never performed an *akira* — it never lifted the object off the ground. Therefore, no *melacha* was performed by the animal.

### Stopping Problem

The *Gemara* immediately raises the objection: *v'iy efshar d'lo kaima l'hashtin mayim ul'hateil gelalim* — it is impossible that the donkey will not stop at some point to urinate or defecate. When it stops and then starts moving again, that constitutes *akira* and *hanacha*. The donkey will have performed the complete act of carrying.

### Load Management

The solution: *k'she'hi mehalechet menicha aleha* — when the donkey starts walking, place the load on it; *k'she'hi omedet, notleh heimena* — when it stops, remove it. As soon as it starts moving again, replace the load. This requires close, active attention throughout the journey — one must remain constantly aware of the donkey's movements and respond immediately when it stops. The *Gemara* notes this is itself not without purpose: the arrangement makes one acutely conscious of the delicate halachic situation one is navigating.

### Friend as Carrier

*Ee hachi, chaveiro afilu chaveiro* — if this is the approach, the same logic could apply to a friend: have the friend begin walking, then place the load on him while he is already in motion. He never performs an *akira*, and the same technical solution applies.

### Rav Papa's Analysis

### Violation Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כׇּל שֶׁבְּגוּפוֹ חַיָּיב חַטָּאת — בַּחֲבֵרוֹ פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר, כׇּל שֶׁחֲבֵרוֹ פָּטוּר אֲבָל אָסוּר — בַּחֲמוֹרוֹ מוּתָּר לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Any** action **that** if one performs it **by himself, he is liable** to bring **a sin-offering** for it, if he performed it **by means of another,** he is **exempt but** it is still **prohibited** for him to do so. And **any** action **that** if performed by means of **another,** he is **exempt** from bringing a sin-offering **but** it is still **prohibited** for him to do so, performing the action **by means of his donkey is permitted *ab initio*.**

*Amar Rav Papa* — Rav Papa clarifies the full hierarchy of violation levels. *Kol she'begufo chayav chatas* — anything that, if one carried it oneself, stopping and starting, would constitute a full biblical transgression requiring a *chatas* offering — *b'chaveiro patur aval assur* — when done via one's friend (placing the object on him while he walks), one is exempt from biblical liability but it remains rabbinically forbidden. The technical loophole of the missing *akira* reduces it to a rabbinic level, not to full permissibility.

### Donkey Priority

*Kol she'bechaveiro patur aval assur, b'chamoro mutar l'chatchila* — whatever is only rabbinically forbidden when done via one's friend is entirely *muttar lechatchila* via one's donkey. This seems to apply even beyond the emergency case of the wallet: if the technical *melacha* is absent because there was no *akira* by the donkey, the act is fully permitted from the outset. This explains why the hierarchy places the donkey above giving the load to a Jewish adult, even an unrelated one.

### Running Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הָיְתָה חֲבִילָתוֹ מוּנַּחַת לוֹ עַל כְּתֵיפוֹ — רָץ תַּחְתֶּיהָ עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לְבֵיתוֹ. דַּוְקָא רָץ, אֲבָל קַלִּי קַלִּי — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** One who was traveling on Shabbat eve and **his package was resting on his shoulder** as night fell, **he runs beneath it,** i.e., with his package on his shoulder, **until he reaches his home.** The Gemara infers: **Specifically,** one **runs** until he reaches home; **however,** walking **a little bit** at a time, **no,** he may not do so.

*Haisa chavilasah munachas al kteifo* — if one has a bundle already on one's shoulder as *Shabbat* begins, *rutz ad she'magia l'veiso* — run straight home without stopping. If one never stops, one never performs a *hanacha* in *reshut harabim*, and the journey is completed without a complete act of carrying.

### Speed Requirement

*Davka rutz* — this is valid specifically when running. *Aval kali kali, lo* — walking slowly, pausing incrementally, is not permitted. *Keivan d'leika hekeira* — since there is nothing visually distinguishing slow walking with a bundle from ordinary carrying, *asi l'mevad akira v'hanacha* — one will almost certainly come to perform a full *akira* and *hanacha* inadvertently. The running itself serves as the *hekeira*, the visible signal that something unusual is happening — and it also keeps one focused on the goal of completing the act without stopping.

### Final Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? — כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ הֶיכֵּירָא, אָתֵי לְמִיעְבַּד עֲקִירָה וְהַנָּחָה. סוֹף סוֹף, כִּי מָטֵי לְבֵיתֵיהּ אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא קָאֵי פּוּרְתָּא, וְקָמְעַיֵּיל מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד! דְּזָרֵיק לֵיהּ כִּלְאַחַר יָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? **Since,** when walking in the usual manner, **he has no conspicuous** reminder that it is Shabbat, there is concern lest **he come to** perform the acts of **lifting and placing** by stopping to rest on his way home. The Gemara asks: **Ultimately, when he reaches his home, it is impossible that** he will **not** stop and **stand a bit, and** at that point, **he** will have performed the prohibited labor of **carrying** the package **from the public domain into the private domain** of his house. The Gemara answers: This is referring to a case in which he does not place the package in the typical manner when he reaches his home. Rather, **he throws it** down **in an unusual manner.** Since he did not perform the action in the typical manner, it is not prohibited by Torah law.

*Sof sof* — the *Gemara* raises the final difficulty: *ki mati l'veiseh, ee efshar d'lo kai purta* — when one arrives home, it is impossible not to stop, even briefly, before crossing into *reshut hayachid*. At that moment one performs a *hanacha*. How does one avoid that final prohibited act?

This question, as Rashi and *Tosafot* note, could equally have been raised about the donkey or the other cases discussed: in all of them, arriving home requires the animal or carrier to stop, which constitutes a *hanacha*. But perhaps in those cases the *Gemara* was not concerned because there was never an *akira* to begin with — without both endpoints, the act of carrying is incomplete. Here, where the *Gemara* may be assuming that the person had been standing still before *Shabbat* began and then started walking — meaning there was a genuine *akira* — the *hanacha* at the end becomes a real concern that must be addressed.

### Shinuy Method

*Dezarak leh k'le'achar yad* — one lets the bundle drop in an unusual manner, *k'le'achar yad*, slipping it off one's back or setting it down abnormally rather than in the standard way. This constitutes a *shinuy* — an alteration from the normal method of performing the act — which reduces the *hanacha* from a full biblical violation to a lesser category.

### Unresolved Timing

The deeper unresolved question underlying this final exchange is when exactly the act of walking began. If *Shabbat* descended while the person was already in motion, there was no *akira* — and without a starting point, the only issue is the *hanacha* at the end, which the *shinuy* addresses. If the person was standing still when *Shabbat* began and then started walking, there is a genuine *akira*, and the full prohibition of carrying applies — making it necessary to address both endpoints. The *Gemara* leaves this tension implicit, and the discussion of the broader prohibition of having one's animal work on *Shabbat* will continue in the following *daf*.
