# Nedarim 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hefker & Ownership

### Hefker as Speech Act

The previous *perek* closed with a *machlokes* between Rabbi Yosi and the *Rabbanan* concerning how *hefker* operates, along with a dispute among the *Amora'im* about how to apply Rabbi Yosi's position, drawing on various *beraisos* and *mishnayos*. Before turning to the new *perek*, it is worth stepping back to examine the nature of *hefker* more broadly.

The case at the end of the prior *mishna* involved a person who was *mudar hana'ah* from another and placed food on a rock, declaring it *hefker*, so that the other person could freely take and benefit from it. The operative question is: what precisely does *hefker* accomplish? If *hefker* merely grants others permission to use an object without actually extinguishing the original owner's title — so that during the limbo period between the declaration and another person's acquisition the original ownership persists — then the *neder* would remain in effect wherever that *neder* depends on ownership. This would create a real difficulty for understanding how Rabbi Yosi's position functions in the *mishna*.

### Two Hefker Models

There are essentially two ways to understand *hefker*. The first model holds that *hefker* is a grant of permission to others to use or acquire the object, but does not transfer ownership until a second party actually acquires it. Under this view, the original owner's title lingers in that interim period. The second model holds that *hefker* is a complete and immediate severance of ownership — the moment one declares an object *hefker*, it passes out of one's *reshut* entirely.

Which model the *mishna* presupposes is precisely the question. The tension between these two models runs through many of the discussions that follow.

### Hefker & Nedarim

The Rambam observes that *hefker* is, in a certain sense, a kind of *neder*. This is not to say they are identical, but that in both cases one's words change the status of an object. With a *neder*, the words render the object *assur* or *mutar*; with *hefker*, the words alter to whom the object belongs. The Rambam's comparison is illuminated by the *Gemara* on daf 7, which asks whether *yad* functions for *hefker* — that is, whether the rules of partial verbal formulations that govern *nedarim* apply equally to *hefker*. That the *Gemara* even entertains the question demonstrates that these two categories share significant structural overlap as *speech acts* that change the *halachic* status of an object.

One can observe a similar dynamic with the separation of *terumah* and *maaser*: there too one employs words to change the legal standing of produce. In each of these cases — *hefker*, *nedarim*, tithing — it is a verbal act directed at an object, a *cheftza*, that produces a *halachic* transformation. The *masechta* has touched on this underlying pattern repeatedly.

### Hefker After Death

The comparison to *nedarim* carries one further striking implication: what happens if a person declares something *hefker* and then dies before anyone has acquired it? If *hefker* is a genuine *kinyan* — a real transfer of title to no one, with the property suspended in ownerless limbo — then the declaration should remain effective even after the owner's death; anyone who comes along afterward may acquire it. But if *hefker* is more akin to a *neder*, then it is a personal obligation of the declarer, and just as one's heirs are not obligated to fulfill the *nedarim* of their ancestor, they would not be bound to treat the property as ownerless. The property would simply fall back into the estate, having never truly left the owner's *reshut* — it was merely an attempted verbal transformation that lapsed upon death.

This is thus another domain in which the structural similarity between *hefker* and *nedarim* has concrete *halachic* consequences.

### Hefker Beit Din

The principle of *hefker beit din hefker* — that a rabbinic court possesses the authority to render property ownerless — also arose at the end of *amud alef*. Rabbi Shimon ben Hotzadak holds: *kol hamaphkir bifnei shlosha havi hefker* — declaring something *hefker* before three people constitutes valid *hefker*; before fewer than three it does not. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi disagrees and says that *devar Torah*, even one person suffices, and the rabbis required three for a pragmatic reason: *kedai sheyihiyeh echad zocheh ushnayim me'idim* — so that one person may acquire the property and two may serve as witnesses.

These two positions suggest rather different theories of what the three-person requirement accomplishes. On Rabbi Shimon ben Hotzadak's view, one might read the requirement as analogous to a *beit din*, though it is equally possible he is not invoking formal judicial procedure at all. Rather, his concern may be with *publicity*: once three people know something, the matter becomes public — *avsha milta* — and acquires a standing in the communal sphere. Indeed, there is a known principle with *lashon hara* that once three people know of something it changes its status, because a certain *kol*, a communal voice, begins to form around that information. Under this reading, Rabbi Shimon ben Hotzadak's three-person requirement is about making the *hefker* publicly known, not about convening a *beit din*.

The principle of *hefker beit din hefker* itself comes up in several critical contexts. One striking application, to be encountered in *Kiddushin*, is the *sugya* of *afkinu rabanan lekiddushei minei* — the notion that under very circumscribed circumstances the rabbis possess the power to annul a marriage retroactively. At least according to some *Rishonim*, the mechanism is that the *beit din* declares that the money used for *kiddushin* was never truly the groom's — it was *hefker* — and thus the *kiddushin* never took effect. This illustrates the extraordinary reach of *hefker beit din hefker*.

### Goral & Authority

The *Gemara* in *Gittin* identifies two potential sources for *hefker beit din hefker*, and the two sources carry sharply divergent implications. The first source is the division of the land of Israel by *goral* — by lottery — when the Jewish people entered the land. At first glance this raises an obvious question: how did each person actually acquire his portion? The entire land was in some sense the collective inheritance of the Jewish people; no individual owned any of it; and yet through the *goral* each family received a defined tract. The mechanism by which this worked — by which the *beit din* could take something from the collective and vest it in an individual — is *hefker beit din hefker*. The *beit din* rendered the communal property ownerless with respect to all others and simultaneously designated it as belonging to a specific person.

This first source implies something quite far-reaching: *hefker beit din hefker* is not merely the power to make property ownerless, but the power to transfer ownership directly. The *beit din* can take from the collective and give to an individual. This reflects a model of communal or governmental ownership: the land ultimately belongs to God — *l'Hashem ha-aretz umlo'ah* — and the *beit din* functions as its administrator, capable of directing ownership where it chooses.

The second source is from Ezra, who exercised the power to confiscate — to declare *cherem* — the property of those who refused to ascend to the land of Israel. This source tells a different story. Here the *beit din*'s power is punitive and coercive: it is a policing authority, the capacity to fine or confiscate property from those who have violated communal norms. This is not a model of governmental ownership of everything, but a model of governmental enforcement power.

These two models — communal ownership with the *beit din* as administrator versus *beit din* as wielder of police power — represent genuinely different conceptions of what the Torah's governmental structure prescribes and what the principle of *hefker beit din hefker* means at its root.

A related but distinct question is whether the government may seize private land for public use. There are indeed such rules, traceable to Yehoshua's conquest of the land. The *Rambam* and the *Gemara* in *Bava Batra* enumerate approximately eight basic public-property-law rules that Yehoshua established: public easements through fields, specifications for road widths, rights of pasturage, and similar matters. These public-land-use questions constitute their own complex domain.

### Bitul Chametz

One other significant application of the concept of *hefker* is *bitul chametz*. According to Tosafot on Pesachim daf 2, *bitul chametz* — the nullification of one's *chametz* before Passover — operates through a mechanism related to *hefker*. It may not be that the act of *bitul* is identical to *hefker*, but rather that *bitul* causes one's *chametz* to become *hefker*. Under this view, a person who nullifies all his *chametz*, declares it ownerless, and simply closes the door on whatever remains — the Cheerios on the shelf, the residue on the frying pan — has in principle fulfilled his biblical obligation. The rabbinic requirements of *bedikat chametz* (searching), active destruction, and other measures were layered on top because *bitul* alone seems too easy.

### Ramban's Critique

The Ramban offers a sharp critique of Tosafot's position. He argues that *bitul chametz* cannot function through *hefker*, and that it operates through an entirely different mechanism — one closer to *bitul avodah zarah*. Just as a non-Jew who once worshipped an idol and then rejects it — smashing it, discarding it, or declaring it worthless — has performed a *bitul* that removes the idol's prohibited status, so too the person performing *bitul chametz* is not declaring the *chametz* ownerless but rather fundamentally reorienting his relationship to it: *eino ani rotzeh b'kiyumo* — I do not want this to exist for me any longer.

Under the Ramban's model, the decisive factor is a psychological-legal relinquishment of attachment to the *chametz*, not a formal act of ownership transfer. A person who simply does not want his *chametz* — who has inwardly and verbally severed his connection to it — has accomplished something *mide'oraita*, and the rabbis then required additional acts because this inner relinquishment alone seems insufficiently concrete.

One might press: with *avodah zarah*, the act of physical destruction demonstrates that the object is no longer worshipped — the idol's status is defined entirely by its being an object of veneration, and the owner's rejection makes that plain. But with *chametz*, what does a verbal declaration actually accomplish? Why should words alone have the power to change the *halachic* status of the *chametz*?

### Ran on Chametz

The Ran in *Psachim* — on daf 1 amud alef in the pages of the Rif — offers a distinctive answer. *Chametz* is categorically different from other property, the Ran explains, because the Torah itself declared *bal yera'eh u'val yimatzei* — two distinct prohibitions against owning or possessing *chametz* on Passover. These prohibitions have the effect of removing the *chametz* from one's *reshut* in a meaningful sense: the only dimension of "ownership" one retains over *chametz* on Passover is precisely the dimension that makes one liable for violating those two *mitzvot*. The *Gemara* itself uses language suggesting that the Torah places the *chametz* into one's ownership for the purpose of creating the violation. What remains of ordinary ownership is therefore only a residual sliver of connection, and to dissolve even that sliver requires only a much weaker mechanism than would be needed to declare ordinary property *hefker*.

This is the counterintuitive point: because the Torah has rendered the *chametz* so nearly outside one's *reshut* already, the bar for completing the severance is lower than for real property. It is not that *bitul chametz* is a full *hefker*; rather, the ownership that needs to be dissolved is itself attenuated, and so an attenuated act suffices.

The question of how all this fits with Rabbi Yosi's position — who holds that *hefker* does not fully remove ownership until another person acquires the property — is itself a subject the *Rishonim* address primarily from the perspective of the *Rabbanan*. Tosafot might resolve the difficulty by noting that *bitul chametz* is a different and weaker kind of *hefker* than ordinary *hefker*, one appropriate to the already-diminished ownership the Torah leaves one with respect to *chametz*. This would align, in spirit if not in precise mechanism, with the Ran's approach.

### Hefker Witnesses

Returning to the question of how many people are required for *hefker* to take effect: Rabbi Shimon ben Hotzadak holds that three people are required even *mide'oraita*, while Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi holds that one suffices *mide'oraita* and three are required only *miderabbanan* (so that one may acquire while two serve as witnesses). The *Mishna* in the previous *perek* apparently involved a case with only two people — the *mudar hana'ah* and the one being *mafkir* — and the *Ritva* resolves this by invoking *sha'at hadachak*: because the person had nothing to eat, it was a pressing situation, and even if one follows Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and requires three rabbinically, the rabbis relaxed their requirement in a case of genuine need.

Alternatively, the *Machaneh Ephraim* draws a distinction between land (*karka*) and movable property (*metaltelin*). Ownership of movable objects is, in a sense, constituted more immediately by physical possession — you hold it, you own it; you put it down, and someone else picks it up, and it is *hefker*. Land, by contrast, involves a much more formal and layered structure of ownership, requiring public establishment of rights — analogous to filing a deed with a municipal office in modern law. On this view, even according to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, the rabbinic requirement of three applies only to land; for movable property, even *miderabbanan* one person suffices.

### Solo Hefker

A further question is whether *hefker* declared entirely by oneself — *hefker beino levein atzmo*, with no one present at all — takes any effect. The *Rosh* holds that it does work, at least at some level, and the *Kesef Mishnah* notes this position. The Rambam, by contrast, appears to hold that *mide'oraita* at least one person must be present, and *miderabbanan* three are required. The logic behind the Rambam's position is pointed: if you declare something *hefker* with no one around, what have you accomplished? Everyone else will continue to regard the object as yours, and in practical terms it functions as if you still own it.

This observation loops back directly to the question of *bitul chametz*: if the entire act of *bitul* is performed privately, with no one present and no external manifestation, does it accomplish anything? The debate between Tosafot and the Rambam about the nature of *hefker* bears directly on this.

### Shulchan Aruch Ruling

Interestingly, the *Shulchan Aruch* does not rule uniformly. In *Orach Chayim*, the *Shulchan Aruch* rules in accordance with the *Rosh* and accepts *hefker beino levein atzmo* in the context of *shevitat behemto* — the prohibition against allowing one's animal to work on *Shabbat*. If a person has lent his animal to a non-Jew who intends to plow with it on *Shabbat*, the owner may declare the animal *hefker* privately on *Shabbat* morning and thereby remove it from his ownership, dissolving the prohibition. In *Choshen Mishpat*, however, where the *Shulchan Aruch* addresses property law more directly, it rules like the Rambam — requiring at least one person present even *mide'oraita*, and three *miderabbanan*. The *Shulchan Aruch* thus applies different standards depending on the context: a leniency for *shevitat behemto* that it does not extend to ordinary property transactions.

## Perek Hashutfim

### Breira Concept

We now turn to *amud bet* and the opening of *Perek Hashutfim*. The *perek* introduces one of the most far-reaching and technically demanding topics in all of *Shas*: *breira*. The concept of *breira* — *huvar hadavar lemafrea* — asks whether something that happens later can retroactively clarify the status of something that was ambiguous or unresolved at an earlier moment. Can a subsequent event cast its light backward in time and determine what was true at the outset?

This question recurs across six or seven *sugyos* throughout *Shas*, with additional *gemaras* that echo those discussions — touching on *Gittin*, *Eruvin*, and the present *masechta*, among others.

### Terumah Example

The first classic case involves separating *terumah* and *maaser* from a barrel of wine on *Shabbat*, when such separation is rabbinically prohibited. The person wishes to drink from the barrel but cannot perform the separation formally. So he declares: whatever remains in the bottom of the barrel at the end will be the *terumah* and *maaser*, designated accordingly, and the rest is *chullin* and permitted. He makes sure to leave behind the correct proportion.

The difficulty is apparent: throughout the drinking, the wine in the barrel is all mixed together. No wine has actually been separated; one has merely designated *wine that has not yet been identified* as the future *terumah*. When the dregs finally remain at the bottom, the claim is that it has now been *huvar hadavar lemafrea* — retroactively clarified — that those dregs were always the *terumah*. This is *breira*.

### Yesh/Ein Breira

The fundamental *machlokes* is between *yesh breira* — there is *breira*, we can employ this mechanism — and *ein breira* — we cannot. The *halacha* as established based on a principle derived from *Beitzah* is that *yesh breira* applies to rabbinic prohibitions and *ein breira* applies to biblical prohibitions. The *Gemara* in the barrel-of-wine case is therefore not straightforwardly problematic, since separating *terumah* and *maaser* on *Shabbat* is a rabbinic prohibition, and for rabbinic matters we say *yesh breira*. The difficulty comes when trying to reconcile individual *sugyos* with this settled rule, since not every application fits neatly.

### Eruvin Example

The second classic case comes from *eruvei techumin*. On *Shabbat* one may not walk beyond the *techum*, the boundary outside a city. A person who wishes to walk to a *shiur* on *Shabbat* afternoon does not know which *shul* the *rebbe* will be speaking in. He therefore sets up two *eruvin* — one in each direction — so that he can walk in either direction and still reach his house afterward. Then, when the announcement is made on Friday night specifying where the *rebbe* will appear, he retroactively designates which of the two *eruvin* should have taken effect.

In some ways this resembles a *tnai*, a conditional statement: I make this *eruv* on condition that my *rebbe* comes to this *shul*. But the *Gemara* notes that *tnai* and *shaliach* apply only within the same category of act, and that *tnai* may not fit naturally here. The reliance is therefore not on *tnai* but on *breira*: the later event — the announcement of the *rebbe*'s venue — retroactively clarifies which *eruv* was always the intended and operative one.

## Partners Mutual Neder

### Mutual Neder Case

The *mishna* states: *ha-shutafin she-nadru hana'ah zeh mi-zeh, asurin likanes le-chatzer* — two partners who have each taken a *neder* against receiving any *hana'ah* from the other are forbidden from entering their shared *chatzer*. The scenario is two people, call them Reuven and Shimon, who own adjacent apartments and share a courtyard — something like the hallway of a modern apartment building. Each has declared a *neder* of *hana'ah* against the other, meaning each has sworn not to receive any benefit from the other. They intensely dislike one another.

### Tanna Kamma Position

The *Tanna Kamma* holds that since the *chatzer* is jointly owned, neither party may enter it at all. The reasoning: every square inch of the courtyard is co-owned by both partners. If Reuven walks across the courtyard, he is deriving benefit from that portion of the property which belongs to Shimon — and vice versa. Since each has taken a *neder* against benefiting from the other, and since there is no identifiable spot in the courtyard that belongs exclusively to one of them rather than jointly to both, neither may enter.

### Rabbi Elazar View

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov disagrees: *zeh nichnas le-toch shelo ve-zeh nichnas le-toch shelo* — each one may enter into his own portion of the *chatzer*. The *Gemara* understands this disagreement as reflecting the *machlokes* on *breira*. The *Tanna Kamma* holds *ein breira*: one cannot retroactively determine which portion of the jointly owned courtyard was "yours," and therefore the entire space remains problematic. Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov holds *yesh breira*: once you are walking on a particular patch, it becomes retroactively clarified that that patch is the portion you own, and you are therefore walking only on your own property.

### Partnership Models

This forces a deeper question: what does joint ownership of a property actually look like? There are several possible models. One model: the ownership is like alternating stripes — Reuven fully owns patch 1, Shimon fully owns patch 2, Reuven fully owns patch 3, and so on, in a kind of checkerboard or striped pattern, with full independent ownership of each distinct strip. A second model: ownership is a grey blur — every single molecule of the property is owned fractionally by both parties, with each holding a percentage interest in every infinitesimal piece. A third model: the property is divisible into two halves, each partner owning one half outright as a separate and distinct parcel.

The practical stakes of this question are significant. Even according to *ein breira*, if the partnership arrangement can be understood as the third model — each person owns one clearly defined half — then perhaps each could technically tiptoe through his own half without ever setting foot on the other's. But if the ownership is the grey-blur model, there is no square inch that is unambiguously one partner's alone, and the *Tanna Kamma*'s blanket prohibition follows naturally.

### Ran Explanations

The Ran's discussion of this *mishna* is extensive. He offers multiple explanations for Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's position, and these explanations are important because, as one looks more carefully at the *mishna*, the invocation of *breira* seems puzzling. Normal *breira* involves a one-time retroactive clarification — at the end of the barrel, the dregs are designated as *terumah*. But in the case of the *chatzer*, the partners are not passing through once; they live there and use the courtyard continuously. If walking in one direction is retroactively clarified as being on Reuven's portion, but tomorrow Reuven walks the same path, and yesterday Shimon walked it, the "clarification" seems to shift back and forth. It is not obvious that *breira* in the ordinary sense can bear the weight of this case.

Rabbeinu Tam, whose view is cited within the Ran, makes an observation along these lines: this case may not really be about *breira* in the standard sense at all. The Ramban suggests it is a *weaker* form of *breira* than usual because the situation is so anomalous. The Ran, interestingly, takes the opposite position: he argues this is actually a *stronger* form of *breira* than the ordinary case. In a barrel of mixed wine, there is no prior agreement that could have specified which wine would be the *terumah*. Here, by contrast, there was an original partnership agreement between two real people with real claims to defined portions of a real property. The terms of the agreement, however imprecisely articulated, always contained the seeds of a division. Retroactively clarifying who owned which portion, then, is not conjuring a clarification from nothing — it is drawing out what was implicit in the original agreement. That is a stronger and more legally grounded use of *breira* than the wine barrel case.

### Not Real Hana'ah

The first explanation the Ran entertains for why Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov permits entry is that walking across a courtyard may not constitute real *hana'ah* at all. The *neder* forbade *hana'ah* from the other person's property. But is the act of putting one's feet on shared property truly a benefit within the meaning of the *neder*? Traversing a space to get from point A to point B, with no enjoyment of the amenities of the space as such, may simply fall below the threshold of *hana'ah* that the *neder* was intended to prohibit.

If that is the case, then the mechanism that allows entry is not *breira* at all, and the case does not really turn on *yesh breira* versus *ein breira*. Walking is categorically outside the *neder*'s scope. This does not mean *breira* is irrelevant to the case — the *Gemara* still invokes *breira* as a factor in Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov's position — but it would mean that *breira* provides an additional layer of reasoning rather than the primary one. There is tension here that the *Gemara* will need to work through in the coming days.

### Millstones/Ovens

The second clause of the *mishna* states: *u-shneihem assurim le-ha'amid reichayim ve-tannur u-legadel tarnagolim* — both partners are forbidden from placing a millstone (*reichayim*) or an oven (*tannur*) in the *chatzer*, or from raising chickens there.

The *reichayim* referred to is not the great circular millstone but the hand-operated flat stone grinder — a large base stone with a smaller stone moved back and forth across it to grind flour. The Torah itself singles out the upper millstone as something that cannot be taken as collateral (*mashkon*) for a loan, precisely because a household grinds fresh flour regularly and confiscating the millstone would deprive the family of bread.

The *tannur*, the oven, presents a parallel issue. In the world of the *mishna*, ovens were clay tandoor-style structures kept not inside the home but in the *chatzer*, functioning as a communal resource for all tenants of the courtyard. If one of the partners now constructs a *tannur* in the *chatzer*, this becomes a permanent fixture of the shared space — and a fixture that the other partner would ordinarily have the right to use as co-owner of the *chatzer*.

Two distinct rationales could explain the prohibition. First, permanent installation: by placing a *reichayim* or *tannur* in the *chatzer*, the partner is occupying shared space permanently — and that occupancy itself constitutes a use of the *chatzer* in a way that touches on the co-ownership and implicates the *neder*. Second, communal-use introduction: by introducing a communal-use object into the *chatzer*, the partner creates something that the other partner, as co-owner, would naturally be entitled to use — but now cannot, because of the *neder*. This creates a structural conflict between the *neder* and the terms of the ownership agreement.

The third prohibited act, *legadel tarnagolim* — raising chickens — raises questions addressed in both the *Yerushalmi* and the *Bavli* here. One issue is the principle of *zeh neheneh ve-zeh lo chaseir*: if the chickens roam the courtyard but their presence does not actually diminish anyone else's use of the space, perhaps there is no real *hana'ah* problem. Chickens are property of one partner, but they occupy shared space the way any movable object might. The *Yerushalmi*'s treatment of this question will need to be revisited in more detail. A further issue is that the chickens, unlike the millstone or oven, are not stationary — they range across the entire *chatzer*, and one cannot invoke *breira* to argue they were always only on the owner's portion of the space.

### Unidirectional Neder

The *mishna* then shifts to a case where the *neder* is unidirectional: *haya echad mehem mudar hana'ah michaveiro, lo yikanes lachatzer* — if one of the partners has been forbidden from receiving *hana'ah* from the other, he cannot enter the *chatzer*.

This case is, on reflection, more difficult than the mutual-*neder* case. Here the person forbidden from the *chatzer* is a co-owner of that property. He owns fifty percent of it. How can the other partner's *neder* strip him of access to his own property? The principle *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — a person cannot forbid something that does not belong to him — would seem to protect the co-owner's right of access, since the *chatzer* is also his.

Yet the *Tanna Kamma* permits this situation to arise. The *Tanna Kamma*'s position in the first clause — that joint ownership means every fleck of the *chatzer* is co-owned — creates this paradox: because every inch is partially his, every inch is also partially the other partner's, and so the forbidden party can find no spot in the *chatzer* that is uncontaminated by the other's ownership.

### Ran Explanations

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov responds with two points. First: *yachol lomar l'toch sheli ani nichnas, v'eini nichnas l'toch shelcha* — the forbidden party can say, "I am entering into my portion, not yours." Through the mechanism of *breira*, he can treat his movement through the *chatzer* as movement exclusively through his own portion of the property. Second — and this is the *mishna*'s resolution of the impasse in the *Tanna Kamma*'s model:

### Forcing Sale

*V'kofin et hanoder limkor chelko* — we compel the partner who made the *neder* to sell his portion. The *neder* he made has created an impossible situation for the other co-owner: the other co-owner cannot access his own property because the *neder* has rendered every portion of the shared space potentially problematic. Since the situation is untenable, *beit din* forces the *noder* to sell his share to a third party. Once he has done so, the ownership structure changes, the *neder* no longer attaches to that person's property, and the formerly forbidden party may now freely enter the *chatzer*.

One might ask: must the sale be to the person forbidden under the *neder*? No — that would itself constitute *hana'ah*, since the person would be acquiring property from one he is forbidden to benefit from. The sale is to a third party or to some other arrangement that simply removes the *noder*'s ownership from the picture.

A close structural parallel exists in *halacha*: if two partners jointly own a slave and one frees his half, the slave is left in a state of *chatzyo eved chatzyo ben chorin* — half slave, half free — unable to marry a slave woman (as a free person) and unable to marry a free woman (as a slave). The *halacha* there, as here, resolves the impasse by compelling the other master to free him as well, because leaving a person permanently in this legal limbo would *levatel lo mi'periyah v'reviyah* — deny him the ability to fulfill the commandment of procreation. The coercive resolution in our *mishna* follows the same logic: an unworkable *halachic* conflict demands a structural remedy.

### Outsider Neder

The next clause of the *mishna* takes the case outward: *ha'echad min hashuk mudar be'echad mehen hana'ah, lo yikanes lachatzer*. An outsider — someone who neither lives in nor owns any part of the *chatzer* — has taken a *neder* forbidding himself from receiving *hana'ah* from one of the two partners. He, too, cannot enter the *chatzer*.

The reasoning is consistent with the *Tanna Kamma*'s model: since the *chatzer* is jointly owned, and since one of the two owners is the person from whom the outsider is forbidden to benefit, every portion of the *chatzer* contains an ownership stake belonging to that person. There is no spot the outsider can stand on without implicating the *neder*.

Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov again offers a way out: *yachol lomar letoch shel chavercha ani nichnas ve'eini nichnas letoch shelcha* — the outsider can say he is entering into the other partner's portion, not the forbidden partner's. Whether a visitor or outsider may rely on this mechanism — essentially claiming to use only the "half" of the *chatzer* belonging to the person from whom he is *not* forbidden — raises its own questions about trespass and permissible use of jointly owned space.

### Rented Property

The *mishna* then introduces a case involving rental: *hamudar hana'ah mechaveiro, veyesh lo merchatz uveit habad mushcharim ba'ir* — a person who is *mudar hana'ah* from another, where that other person owns a bathhouse and an olive press which are rented out to the public in the city. *Im yesh lo bahen tefisat yad, assur* — if the owner retains a *tefisat yad* in those properties, it is forbidden for the *mudar* to use them.

### Tefisat Yad

The concept of *tefisat yad* — literally "a hand's grasp" or residual grip — is the central analytical tool for this clause and will be the primary topic of the following day's discussion. The question is: once a property has been rented out, does the owner retain any meaningful rights over it? Clearly the owner retains legal title — ownership and rental are distinct — but during the rental period the renter holds exclusive use rights. Does the owner's residual connection to the property during the rental period constitute enough of a *reshut* that the *neder* extends to prohibit use of that property?

The *mishna* offers two positions: if the owner has *tefisat yad* — some form of residual use-right or access during the rental period — then the *mudar* is forbidden from using the property (*assur*). If *ein lo bahen tefisat yad* — the owner has fully transferred operational control and retains no use-rights during the rental — then the *mudar* is permitted (*mutar*), because the property is effectively no longer within the owner's *reshut* in the relevant sense.

The exact nature of *tefisat yad* — what kinds of residual rights during a rental qualify — is left for the *Gemara* to elaborate and will be the subject of the next day's learning.

### House/Field Neder

The *mishna* then introduces a *konam*-formulation: *ha'omer lachaveiro konam leveitcha she'ani nichnas, uvesadecha she'ani loke'ach* — one who says to his fellow: "Your house is forbidden to me as a *konam* that I shall not enter it," or "Your field is forbidden to me as a *konam* that I shall not take from its produce."

### Death/Sale

*Met o shemacharo le'acher, assur* — if the owner dies, or if he sells the property to someone else, the prohibition remains in effect. This clause was already employed as a proof-text approximately two weeks earlier in the discussion of what can dissolve a *neder*: specifically, whether making a *neder* against a person's property lapses when that property is transferred.

The language here is deliberate. The clause that follows the present one uses the formulation *konam bayit ze she'ani nichnas* — "this house is forbidden to me" — rather than "your house." The distinction is precise: saying "your house" implies that the prohibition is tied to your ownership, and if you sell it, the prohibition should lapse. Saying "this house" declares the object itself — the *cheftza* — forbidden, independent of who owns it. Therefore, even after death or sale, the prohibition adheres to the object and the person remains forbidden from entering or taking from it. This *mishna* was a critical proof-text in that earlier *machlokes*.

## Modern Applications

### Corporate Ownership

The foregoing discussion of partnership, *hefker*, *breira*, and ownership raises a question with profound practical ramifications: how does *halacha* treat corporate ownership in the modern sense? Most publicly traded companies conduct business on *Shabbat*. Shareholders own fractional interests in those companies. Does *halachic* ownership attach to those fractional shares in a way that creates *Shabbat* liability or *chametz* problems on Passover?

The *Minchat Yitzchak* addresses questions along these lines. David Zvi Hoffman in his *Melamed LeHo'il* has a *teshuvah* dealing specifically with the question of corporate ownership of *chametz* on Passover, arising from the case of a beer brewery. The underlying analytical question is whether a corporation constitutes a distinct legal entity — in which case shareholders own interests in the corporation, not in its assets directly — or whether shareholders are simply partners in a human partnership arrangement and directly co-own all corporate assets. If corporations can have genuine *halachic* personhood distinct from their shareholders, many of the problems dissolve; if shareholders are merely partners in the classical *shutafim* model, the same questions about joint ownership, *hefker*, and *breira* that animate the *mishna* of *Perek Hashutfim* apply with full force to every publicly traded company.

These are genuinely open and important questions, and one that the classical sources, composed before the advent of corporate law, could not directly anticipate. The *poskim* of the last century and a half have had to work carefully with the conceptual tools the *Gemara* provides to address a commercial world the *Tannaim* and *Amoraim* could not have foreseen.
