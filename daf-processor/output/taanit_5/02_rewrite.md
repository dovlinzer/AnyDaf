# Taanit 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mashiv HaRuach & Shmini…

### Three Solutions Debate

The daf opens at the very top of 5a, picking up directly where the previous session left off. The debate concerned the recitation of *mashiv ha'ruach u'morid ha'gashem* on *Shmini Atzeret* for those living in *chutz la'aretz*, where *Simchat Torah* carries the status of *safek Shmini Atzeret*. The Gemara had entertained several proposed solutions. The first was to recite *mashiv ha'ruach* at *Musaf* of *Shmini Atzeret*, then stop, and resume at *Musaf* of *Simchat Torah*. The Gemara rejected this on the grounds that once one begins reciting *mashiv ha'ruach* at *Musaf* of *Shmini Atzeret*, one has signaled the day's sanctity as *Shmini Atzeret*, and one cannot then reverse that signal for the remainder of the day by ceasing. A second proposal was therefore advanced: recite it at *Musaf* and *Mincha*, then cease at *Ma'ariv* of *Simchat Torah*, so that the entire day of *Shmini Atzeret* is treated consistently, after which one performs a kind of reset and begins *Simchat Torah* as if the recitation had not yet commenced.

### Rava's Position

Rava rejects all of these proposals. The ruling at the top of 5a reads: *Rava amar, kivan she'hitchil shuv ein posek* — once one has begun reciting *mashiv ha'ruach* on *Shmini Atzeret*, there is no basis for stopping and restarting on *Simchat Torah*. It is not merely impractical — it is self-contradictory. Having already signaled that the season of rain has commenced, one continues without interruption.

### Rav & Rav Sheshet Agree

The Gemara records that Rav Sheshet concurred: *ve'chein amar Rav Sheshet, kivan she'hitchil shuv ein posek* — once begun, one does not stop. And even Rav, who had previously maintained the peculiar position that one recites *mashiv ha'ruach* at *Musaf* of *Shmini Atzeret* and then suspends it until *Musaf* of *Simchat Torah*, retracted that view.

### Twenty-One Days Count

The Gemara conveys this retraction in the name of Rav Chananel: *de'amar Rav Chananel amar Rav, moneh esrim ve'chad yom ke'derech she'moneh asarah yamim me'Rosh Hashanah ve'ad Yom Kippurim* — one counts twenty-one days, analogously to the way one counts the ten days from *Rosh Hashanah* to *Yom Kippur*. Counting twenty-one days from *Rosh Hashanah* brings one to *Shevi'i shel Sukkot*, *Hoshana Rabbah*, and on day twenty-two — *Shmini Atzeret* — one begins reciting *mashiv ha'ruach*. And once begun, *kivan she'hitchil shuv ein posek*, the *halacha* follows: one does not stop. This is stated as the operative ruling.

### Rashi's Explanation

The comparison to the ten days from *Rosh Hashanah* to *Yom Kippur* invites scrutiny. On the surface it seems inapt, because the ten days of *teshuvah* are counted inclusively of both endpoints, whereas here one counts twenty-one days and only begins on day twenty-two — the endpoints are not included in the same way. Rashi resolves this by situating the discussion in the era of *kiddush ha'chodesh*, when the precise date of *Rosh Hashanah* might have been uncertain — since *Rosh Hashanah* itself is a two-day holiday, it was possible not to know with certainty which of the two days was the true first day. What Rav is therefore saying, according to Rashi, is: use the same starting point you would use to count ten days to *Yom Kippur* — because by *Yom Kippur* the true date of *Rosh Hashanah* was presumably known — and from that same starting point count twenty-one days forward to arrive at *Shmini Atzeret*. The counting back from *Yom Kippur* to establish *Rosh Hashanah* provides the anchor from which to count forward.

### Yom Tov vs. Season

There is something conceptually striking about the entire thrust of this discussion, and it is worth pausing on it. The reason one recites *mashiv ha'ruach* is, presumably, because the rainy season has arrived and there is no longer any concern of rain interrupting one's dwelling in the *sukkah* — the concern of *siman klalah*. Once *Sukkot* has passed, the rainy season is unproblematic, and one begins invoking it in prayer. If that is the rationale, then the question of whether *Simchat Torah* is a *safek Shmini Atzeret* becomes essentially irrelevant: the rainy season has arrived regardless, and once one is no longer sitting in the *sukkah*, one should simply begin reciting *mashiv ha'ruach* and continue. Why would anyone think to restart the formula on *Simchat Torah*?

The very fact that the Gemara seriously entertained the possibility of stopping and restarting on *Simchat Torah* reflects a different conceptual framework — one in which the recitation of *mashiv ha'ruach* is linked not merely to the season but to the identity of the *yom tov* itself. This connection is reminiscent of Rabbi Eliezer's argument about the *lulav* and the *aravah*, and of the Gemara in *Rosh Hashanah* that teaches *bechag nidonim al ha'mayim* — we are judged concerning water on *Sukkot* — just as *Pesach* is the season of judgment concerning grain. The liturgical shift appears to be tied directly to the identity of the *yom tov*, not merely to the calendar date of the season. Indeed, the Gemara elsewhere notes that *mashiv ha'ruach* is introduced specifically at *Musaf*, arguably because *Musaf* is the *tefillah* that most fully embodies the distinctive identity of the *yom tov*.

Against that backdrop, the concern that *Simchat Torah*, as a *safek Shmini Atzeret*, demands its own fresh commencement of *mashiv ha'ruach* becomes intelligible — one wanted to ensure that the recitation was properly anchored to the *yom tov* rather than carried forward by inertia. When Rav ultimately rules *kivan she'hitchil shuv ein posek*, he need not be entirely abandoning the *yom tov* framework. He may simply be saying: yes, there is an intrinsic link between the recitation and *yom tov* — but once one has begun, one cannot reverse course. The act of commencing has already established the connection.

Rav Chananel's formulation is illuminating in a different way. By framing the matter as a count of twenty-one days from *Rosh Hashanah*, he conspicuously avoids anchoring the recitation to *Shmini Atzeret* as a *yom tov*. He does not say "begin on *Yom Tov ha'acharon shel chag*"; he says count twenty-one days after *Rosh Hashanah*, and it happens to land on *Shmini Atzeret*. This reframes the entire question: the link is not to the *yom tov* per se, but perhaps to the *din* of *Rosh Hashanah* and the principle of *bechag nidonim al ha'mayim*. In this reading, it is the period of divine judgment regarding water, anchored in *Rosh Hashanah*, that determines when we begin invoking rain — and the *yom tov* of *Shmini Atzeret* is incidental to that count. This is a meaningfully different way of understanding when and why we begin saying *mashiv ha'ruach*, and the contrast between the two framings runs quietly through the entire sugyah.

## When to Stop Rain Prayers

### Until When Ask Rain

The Mishnah now asks: *ad matay sho'alin et ha'geshamim* — until when does one recite *veten tal u'matar*? One might expect the Mishnah to have addressed when one *begins* asking for rain before addressing when one stops. The reversal of order is deliberate: the following Mishnah takes up the question of when one begins asking for rain in *Marcheshvan*, and that discussion opens naturally into the arc of what happens when rain does not come — the progression toward public fasting. In order to flow into that narrative of escalating response to drought, the tractate first dispatches the question of when the rain prayers cease, and only then returns to when they begin.

### Rabbi Yehuda: Until Pesa…

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *ad she'ya'avor ha'Pesach* — until *Pesach* has passed. This can be reconciled with another formulation attributed to Rabbi Yehuda specifying the first day of *Pesach*; the two may represent variant traditions of his view.

### Rabbi Meir: Until Nisan…

Rabbi Meir goes further: *ad she'yeitzei Nisan* — one continues reciting *veten tal u'matar* through the entire month of *Nisan*.

### Yoel's Verse Cited

Rabbi Meir derives this from the verse in *Yoel*: *ve'yored lachem geshem moreh u'malkosh ba'rishon* — God will bring down for you rain, *moreh* being the early rain and *malkosh* the late rain, *ba'rishon*, in the first month — that is, in *Nisan*. The verse describes an event during the days of *Yoel* in which both the early rain and the late rain fell during *Nisan*, demonstrating that rain remains appropriate, and therefore petitionable, through the end of *Nisan*.

## Yoreh in Nisan Probl (II)

### Early Rain in Nisan?

Rav Nachman challenges the verse cited by Rabbi Meir. He poses his difficulty to Rav Yitzchak: *yoreh b'Nisan* — fine, the *malkosh*, the late rain, falls in *Nisan*, that much is understandable. But *yoreh* is the *early* rain. Since when does the early rain fall in *Nisan*?

### Braita on Seasons

The challenge is grounded in a *Braita*: *yoreh b'Marcheshvan u'malkosh b'Nisan* — the *yoreh* comes in *Marcheshvan* and the *malkosh* in *Nisan*. This is indeed the normal annual pattern reflected in *Kri'at Shema* itself, *yoreh u'malkosh*. If that is the fixed seasonal reality, how can the verse in *Yoel* speak of *yoreh* occurring in *Nisan*?

### Rabbi Yochanan's Answer

Rav Yitzchak responds in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *b'imei Yoel ben Petu'el nitkayem mikra zeh* — this verse was fulfilled uniquely in the days of Yoel ben Petu'el. It describes not a normative seasonal pattern but an exceptional miracle.

## Yoel's Miracle Story

### Drought Context

The background to the miracle is established from the verse in *Yoel* itself: *yeter ha'gazam achal ha'arbeh* — what one plague of locusts left, the next consumed. It was a period of devastating destruction. The Gemara then reports: *oso shanah yatza Adar ve'lo yardu geshamim* — that year, *Adar* had already passed and no rain had fallen. The entire rainy season had come and gone without rain. Then: *yardah lahem revi'ah rishonah be'echad b'Nisan* — the first rainfall, the *revi'ah rishonah*, came down on the first of *Nisan*.

A word on terminology: the word *yoreh* connotes shooting, like *yoreh cheitz*, shooting an arrow. The word *revi'ah* carries connotations of *hav'a'ah*, a term used for sexual union, particularly in the context of animals — as does the word *yoreh* in a certain register. The imagery is deliberate: rain descends and penetrates the earth, from which grows forth life. These are the words the Gemara will use throughout.

### Eat or Plant Choice

When Yoel instructed the people to go out and plant, they confronted an agonizing dilemma. They said to him: *mi she'yesh lo kav chitin o kabayim se'orim, yochlenu ve'yichyeh o yizra'enu ve'yamut* — whoever has a measure of wheat or two measures of barley: should he eat it and live, or plant it and risk death from starvation while waiting for the harvest? The people were in the grip of famine; planting seemed to prioritize distant hope over immediate survival.

### Yoel's Command

Yoel's response was unequivocal: *amar lahem af al pi chen, tze'u ve'zar'u* — nevertheless, go out and plant. Plant in trust.

### Grain Discovery

A miracle then occurred: *ve'nitgaleh lahem mah she'biketalim u'mah she'be'chorim nemalim* — grain was discovered stored within the walls of houses and in the burrows of ants, in ant-hills. There is a dispute between Rabbeinu Chananel and Rashi regarding the sequence. Rashi holds that the people first planted what they had stored for their own sustenance — an act of extraordinary faith — and *then* the hidden grain was revealed to sustain them while they waited for the harvest. Rabbeinu Chananel reverses the order: the miracle of the discovered grain occurred first, and they used that surplus for planting while retaining their original stores for eating. Rashi's reading makes the act of planting the more dramatic gesture of *emunah*.

### Eleven-Day Growth

The sequence of events: they went out and planted on the second, third, and fourth of *Nisan*. On the fifth of *Nisan*, the second rainfall, the *revi'ah shniyah*, came down. The *Omer* was brought on the sixteenth of *Nisan*, meaning the grain had reached full maturity. *Nimtza tevuah gedolah be'shishah chodashim gedelah be'echad asar yom* — grain that normally takes six months to grow reached full maturity in eleven days. The *Omer* offering, ordinarily brought from grain that requires six months, was brought from grain that had grown in eleven days. An additional detail: *kaneh zeret, shibolet zartayim* — normally a stalk of grain is overwhelmingly straw with a small proportion of actual grain; in this miracle, one unit of stalk yielded two units of grain, reversing the ordinary proportions.

### Sowing in Tears

The Gemara links this to the verse: *ha'zor'im be'dimah be'rinah yiktzoru* — those who sow in tears will reap in joy. They sowed in tears, not only from the remembered anguish of the famine, but because they were surrendering the very grain they had stored to keep themselves alive. Yet they reaped in joy — within eleven days. The verse continues: *haloch yelech u'vacho nosei meshech ha'zara* — one goes forth and weeps, carrying the pouch of seed. Rav Yehuda interprets this as referring even to the ox: as it plows, it weeps from exertion and hunger, yet as it returns along the furrow it can already eat *chaziz min ha'telem* — the first shoots already sprouting from the ground so rapidly had growth commenced. Even the ox did not complete a single pass before the miracle of growth was visible.

## Aggadic Dialogue Ser (II)

### Consumption Progression

Rav Yitzchak replied in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, tracing a grim progression across the seven years. *Shanah rishonah achlu mah she'be'vatim* — in the first year they consumed what was stored in the house. *Sheniyah achlu mah she'be'sadot* — in the second year they ate whatever continued to grow on its own in the fields; trees and residual vegetation perhaps, whatever nature yielded without cultivation. *Shelishit besar behemah tehorah* — in the third year they slaughtered and ate *kosher* animals. *Revi'it besar behemah teme'ah* — in the fourth year, *non-kosher* animals. *Chamishit besar shekatzim u'remasim* — in the fifth year, rodents and creeping things. *Shishit besar beneihem u'venoseihem* — in the sixth year, evoking the terrible verses in *Yirmiyahu* and *Eichah* regarding the siege of *Yerushalayim*, the flesh of their own sons and daughters. *Sheviit besar zero'oseihem* — in the seventh year, even the flesh of their own arms. *Le'kayem mah she'ne'emar: ish besar zero'o yoChelu* — fulfilling the verse, "A man shall eat the flesh of his own arm."

### Jerusalem Above & Below

Rav Nachman then posed a second question to Rav Yitzchak: *mai de'chtiv, be'kirbecha kadosh ve'lo avo be'ir* — what is meant by the verse, "In your midst is the Holy One, and I will not enter the city"? The apparent difficulty is stark: *because* the Holy is in your midst, God will *not* enter the city? Rav Yitzchak replied in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *amar HaKadosh Baruch Hu, lo avo bi'Yerushalayim shel ma'alah ad she'avo li'Yerushalayim shel matah* — God says: I will not enter the heavenly Jerusalem until I enter the Jerusalem below. The verse is to be read: because the Holy is in your midst — that is, because My presence must first dwell among you below — I will not yet enter the city above.

### Twin Jerusalems

The Gemara then asks: is there truly such a concept as a *Yerushalayim shel ma'alah*, a heavenly Jerusalem? The answer is yes, derived from the verse: *Yerushalayim ha'bnuyah ke'ir she'chubrah lah yachdav* — the built Jerusalem, like the city that is twinned with it in perfect correspondence. Rashi explains: the Jerusalem below shall be built as a city twinned with its counterpart, which is its heavenly reflection — *she'hu ke'yotzei bah chavertah*, one corresponds exactly to the other.

This is a powerful idea that resonates across Jewish thought, with roots reaching back to the *Sulam shel Ya'akov* and *Sha'ar ha'Shamayim* — the notion that the earthly *Har ha'Bayit* has a celestial counterpart, that what transpires below is bound to and reflects what exists above. It is a concept that was also developed extensively in early Christian theological literature, notably by Augustine in *City of God*, who built an elaborate theology around the image of an earthly city reflecting the divine city of God. Whether the Christian tradition drew from the same earlier sources or developed the concept independently, its centrality in that literature is striking.

### One Sin, Two Evils

Rav Nachman posed a third question to Rav Yitzchak: *mai de'chtiv, u've'achat yiva'aru ve'yichsalu musar havalim eitz hu* — what is meant by this verse? The JPS translation itself acknowledges the meaning of the Hebrew as uncertain; the verse is genuinely obscure. Rav Yitzchak answered in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *achat hi she'meva'eret resha'im ba'Gehinnom* — there is one sin that destroys the wicked in *Gehinnom*, one sin above all others. What is it? *Avodah zarah* — idolatry. The word *ba'achat yiva'aru* is linked to texts that speak of hewn images and worthless idols, and the term *musar havalim* — the chastisement of emptiness — clearly refers to the folly of idol worship.

### Shtayim Raot Asah Ami

Rav Nachman posed a fourth question: *mai de'chtiv, ki shtayim ra'ot asah ami* — what is meant by "My nation did two evils to Me"? The response Rav Yitzchak gives in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is at first glance more puzzling than clarifying. Is it only two evils? *Esrin ve'arba shviku lehu* — they abandoned twenty-four things. Some have the reading of twenty-two. In any case, Israel committed far more than two sins — whether one counts the twenty-four books of *Tanakh* against whose teachings they transgressed, or the twenty-four sins enumerated in *Yechezkel*. So what does the verse mean when it says "two evils"?

Rabbi Yochanan explains: *achat she'hi ke'shtayim* — there was one sin that counts as two. And that sin is, again, *avodah zarah*. The verse in *Yirmiyahu* continues: *oti azavu mekor mayim chayim, ve'lachtzov lahem borot borot nishbarim* — they abandoned Me, the source of living water, to dig for themselves broken cisterns that hold no water. The first evil is the abandonment of God; the second is that the replacement they chose was utterly worthless.

### Loyalty vs. Truth

To underscore the double offense, the Gemara cites the surrounding verses from *Yirmiyahu*: *ki avru Iyei Kittiyim u're'u, u'Kedar shalchu ve'hitbonenu me'od* — they looked across to the islands of *Kittiyim* and to *Kedar* and observed with astonishment: *ha'heimir goy elohim ve'hemah lo elohim* — has any nation ever exchanged its gods? And these nations have not done so — *even though their gods are not real gods*. Yet my nation exchanged My *kavod* for something worthless.

The Gemara adds a striking detail by way of *Baraita*: the *Kutim* worshiped fire, and the *Kadriyim* worshiped water. The fire-worshipers understood perfectly well that water extinguishes fire — meaning, they had rational grounds for concluding that the god of water was more powerful and should be adopted in place of their god. Yet they did not exchange their god. The double indictment against Israel thus comes into sharp focus. The first evil is relational: abandonment of the covenant. The second evil compounds it: if at least there had been some intelligible reason for the exchange — some superior competing attraction — the sin might have been comprehensible, however terrible. But there was no such reason. The gods they chose had no power, no reality, no advantage. The pagan nations who worshiped false gods of fire and water at least maintained *loyalty* to what they had. Israel abandoned the true God in exchange for nothing.

It is also notable that the questions in this dialogue seem to come in thematic pairs. The first two questions concerned famine — first the verse in *Yoel* about *yoreh u'malkosh ba'rishon*, which arises from the famine in *Yoel*'s time, and then the seven-year famine and its stages of consumption. The next two questions both concern *avodah zarah*: the verse *ba'achat yiva'aru* and the verse *shtayim ra'ot asah ami*.

## Conduct & Blessings

### No Talk at Meals

The Gemara now shifts from these extended textual dialogues to a scene: *Rav Nachman ve'Rav Yitzchak havu yasvei bi'sudasa* — Rav Nachman and Rav Yitzchak were sitting together at a meal. Rav Nachman invited Rav Yitzchak to share a *dvar Torah*. Rav Yitzchak's response was itself a ruling: *hachi amar Rabbi Yochanan, ein mesichin bi'sudah* — one does not converse during a meal, *shema yakdim kaneh le'veshet* — lest the trachea precede the esophagus, meaning lest food enter the windpipe rather than the food pipe, which could lead to danger.

Rashi's clarification here has practical relevance beyond the immediate context. In tractate *Pesachim*, the requirement of *haseivah* — reclining during the *Seder* — is explained in part by the concern that reclining on the wrong side will cause *yakdim kaneh le'veshet*. Many *Rishonim* interpret this as implying that the trachea and esophagus are positioned side by side, left and right, such that reclining on the right side literally causes food to enter the trachea first. If that anatomical picture is accurate, then even a left-handed person should recline on the left side just as a right-handed person does, since the geographical relationship between *kaneh* and *veshet* is fixed regardless of handedness. However, Rashi's formulation here suggests a different reading: *yakdim kaneh le'veshet* simply means food will go down the wrong pipe — it describes the general hazard of speaking while eating, not a left-right anatomical sequence. If one adopts this understanding, the logic of *haseivah* changes: the concern is that reclining on the side of one's dominant hand makes eating awkward, and awkward eating increases the risk of food going down the wrong way. Under this interpretation, a left-handed person reclines on the right. This is a consequential distinction for the *halacha* of a left-handed person at the *Seder*, and the operative *psak* in most authorities — that even a left-handed person reclines to the left — follows the anatomical reading rather than Rashi's functional one.

After they finished eating, Rav Yitzchak said: now we may speak Torah.

### Yaakov Never Died

Rabbi Yochanan's teaching, as conveyed by Rav Yitzchak, is this: *Yaakov Avinu lo meis* — Yaakov our father never died.

### Linked to Descendants

Rav Nachman's immediate objection is pointed: *ve'chi be'chidi safdu safdanaya ve'chantu chantanaya ve'kavru kavraya* — was it for nothing that the eulogizers eulogized him, the embalmers embalmed him, and the buriers buried him? Rav Yitzchak replies: *mikra ani doresh* — I derive this from a verse. The verse in *Yirmiyahu* reads: *ve'atah al tira avdi Yaakov ne'um Hashem ve'al tichat Yisrael, ki hineni moshi'acha me'rachok ve'et zar'acha me'eretz shivyam* — do not fear, My servant Yaakov, says God, and do not be dismayed, Yisrael, for I will save you from afar and your children from the land of their captivity. The Gemara reads this verse as drawing an analogy between Yaakov and his descendants: *makish hu le'zar'o — mah zar'o ba'chayim af hu ba'chayim* — just as his descendants are alive, so too he is alive.

Tosafot adds a textual observation: by every other *tzaddik* whose death the Torah records, the verses use both *vayigva* (he expired) and *vayamot* (he died). By Yaakov, the word *vayamot* is conspicuously absent. This anomaly points to what Rabbi Yochanan is drawing out.

Rashi seems to treat the statement quite literally — Yaakov was not truly dead even during the embalming and burial. The *Maharsha* interprets it as referring to the soul's continued life, but then asks: why is this unique to Yaakov? The Gemara in various places teaches that *tzaddikim afilu be'mitatam keruyim chayim* — the righteous are called alive even in death. The *Maharsha* suggests the concern may specifically relate to the fact that Yaakov was buried in *chutz la'aretz*, unlike Avraham and Yitzchak who were buried in *Eretz Yisrael*; the Gemara elsewhere implies a connection between burial in *Eretz Yisrael* and resurrection, and so the verse must underscore Yaakov's continuing vitality.

There is, however, a deeper dimension. Avraham had descendants outside the covenant — Yishmael; Yitzchak had Esav. But Yaakov's descendants are entirely identified with *Bnei Yisrael*; the nation itself carries his name, *Yisrael*. The equation *zaro ba'chayim af hu ba'chayim* — his descendants are alive, therefore he is alive — carries particular force by Yaakov because his identity is fully continuous with theirs. The people *are* Yaakov. *Mah zar'o ba'chayim af hu ba'chayim* is thus not merely a claim about personal immortality but about the way a person lives on through what they have created and transmitted. The teaching is well-suited for a *hesped* — a eulogy — precisely because it articulates how the living presence of one's children and descendants constitutes a form of the parent's own continued life.

### Rachav's Name Danger

Rav Yitzchak then offered an independent teaching: *kol ha'omer Rachav Rachav miyad nikri* — anyone who says the name "Rachav" — the woman from the story of *Yehoshua*, who was a prostitute — twice in succession immediately experiences a seminal emission. Rav Nachman responded: *ana amina ve'lo ichpat li* — I tried saying it, and nothing happened. Rav Yitzchak clarified: *beyoda u'makira* — this applies only to one who knew her and recognized her. Tosafot goes further, suggesting that *yoda* implies actual intimate knowledge. It is the evocation of a real memory that produces the effect, not the mere utterance of a name.

### Tree Parable

As they prepared to part, Rav Nachman asked Rav Yitzchak for a *berachah*. Rav Yitzchak responded with a *mashal*, a parable that has become among the most celebrated in the Gemara. *Le'adam she'hayah holech ba'midbar ve'hayah ra'ev ve'ayef ve'tzameh u'matza ilan she'perotav metukim ve'tzilo na'eh ve'amat ha'mayim overet tachtav* — a man was traveling through the wilderness, hungry, weary, and thirsty. He found a tree whose fruit was sweet, whose shade was pleasant, and beneath which ran a brook of water. He ate of the fruit, drank of the water, and rested in the shade. When the time came to leave, he addressed the tree: *ilan ilan, ba'meh avarechecha?* — Tree, tree, what blessing can I give you? If I bless you that your fruit should be sweet — your fruit is already sweet. That your shade should be pleasant — your shade is already pleasant. That a brook should pass beneath you — the brook already flows. So he said: *yehi ratzon she'kol neti'ot she'not'im mimcha yihyu kamotcha* — may it be God's will that all the saplings planted from you be just like you.

### Blessing Future Generati…

Rav Yitzchak then turned this *mashal* on Rav Nachman: *af atah* — so too you. How shall I bless you? *Im ba'Torah harei Torah, im be'osher harei osher, im be'vanim harei vanim* — in Torah, you already have Torah; in wealth, you already have wealth; in children, you already have children. Therefore: *yehi ratzon mi'pi ha'Gadol she'yihyu tze'etza'ei me'echa kamotcha* — may it be the will of the Great One that the descendants who emerge from you be just like you.
