# Bava Batra 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Creditor Seizes Inheritance

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: אַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ, וּבָא בַּעַל חוֹב וְנָטַל חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד מֵהֶן
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed about another related matter: If **two brothers divided** their father's estate between them, **and** then their father's **creditor came and took the portion of one of them** as repayment for the father's debt

The *sugya* on daf 107a opens with *itmar*, signaling a new *amoraic* dispute, and it constitutes the second half of the *sugya* begun on the previous daf. Yesterday's discussion concerned a case in which two brothers divided an inheritance and a third brother subsequently appeared to claim his share, raising the question of how the property is to be re-divided. The present case is structurally similar but involves a different kind of intrusion upon the original division.

It is critical to the case that the debt in question is the father's debt, a lien that encumbered the estate prior to the division. The creditor, rather than pursuing the estate collectively, went to one brother alone and seized his portion. The result is that one brother retains a full half of the inheritance while the other is left with nothing. The question before the *Gemara* is how, if at all, the injured brother can claim redress from his sibling.

### Rav: Division Nullified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: בָּטְלָה מַחְלוֹקֶת
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: The** original **division** of the property **is void,** and the brothers must now redivide the remaining assets.

*Rav* holds that the original division of the property is retroactively canceled on account of this change of circumstances, and the brothers must go back and re-divide whatever property remains. The practical implication is that the surviving brother cannot simply walk away with the entirety of the undisturbed half; the injured brother has a claim to re-divide it, and the assessment would presumably be based on the value of the land at the time of the original inheritance, leaving any improvements made by the retaining brother as his own investment and benefit.

### Shmuel: Brother Loses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: וִיתֵּר
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says:** Each brother, upon receiving his portion, **has foregone** his right to be reimbursed if his portion is lost.

*Shmuel* takes the opposite view. The brother who had his share seized simply loses out. His loss is his own misfortune and he has no claim against his sibling.

### Rav Assi: Quarter Share

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר: נוֹטֵל רְבִיעַ בְּקַרְקַע וּרְבִיעַ בְּמָעוֹת
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi says:** The brother whose portion was seized is entitled to half the remaining inheritance: **He takes one-quarter in land and one-quarter in money.**

Rav Assi offers a middle position. The *Rashbam* explains that the *vav* here functions as *or* rather than *and*, so that Rav Assi is offering the brother a choice of receiving one quarter of the remaining field or its equivalent value in cash — not both simultaneously, which would make his position indistinguishable from Rav's. The reason the brother gets only a quarter rather than a full half, as Rav would grant, is that Rav Assi treats this as a genuine *safek* between Rav and Shmuel and splits the disputed half accordingly.

The structure is familiar in *Shas*: a three-way *machloket* in which two *amoraim* take opposite extreme positions and a later authority — Rav Assi, one generation after Rav and Shmuel — attempts a *peshara*, splitting the difference between them. As for the option of cash, the *Rashbam* explains that the injured brother can argue: had he possessed cash at the time, he could have paid off the creditor himself and retained his land. The creditor seized land only because land was the available asset.

### Heirs vs. Purchasers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: בָּטְלָה מַחְלוֹקֶת – קָא סָבַר: הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ, יוֹרְשִׁין הֵן. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: וִיתֵּר – קָא סָבַר: הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ לָקוֹחוֹת הָווּ, וּכְלוֹקֵחַ שֶׁלֹּא בְּאַחְרָיוּת דָּמֵי
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the rationale for each opinion: **Rav says** that **the** original **division** of the property **is void.** This is because **he holds that brothers who divided** property received as an inheritance **are** still considered to be **heirs** with regard to the inheritance as if they never divided the property, so that they continue to share joint responsibility for their father's debts. Therefore, if a creditor seizes the portion received by one of them, it is as if he repaid the debt on behalf of all the heirs. Accordingly, they must once again divide the remaining property between them. **And Shmuel says** that each brother, upon receiving his portion, **has foregone** his right to be reimbursed if his portion is lost, **as he holds** that **brothers who divided** property received as an inheritance **are** considered as **purchasers** from each other. **And** each one is considered **like a purchaser** who bought his portion **without a guarantee** that if the field is seized in payment of a debt, the seller will compensate the buyer for his loss. Accordingly, the brother whose portion of the estate was seized by the creditor has no claim against the brother whose portion remained untouched.

The stakes of this conceptual disagreement are considerable. According to Shmuel, the inheritance exists as one stage — the entire plot is jointly held by the heirs — and when they come to divide it, that subdivision constitutes a secondary act analogous to purchasing from the corporate estate. The one who "buys" without *achrayut*, without any guarantee or warranty, has no recourse when a pre-existing lien surfaces and extinguishes his share. He is no different from any other purchaser who loses property to a creditor of his seller.

According to Rav, both the initial inheritance and the subsequent subdivision are parts of a single unified process of *yerusha*. Each brother's share was always his inheritance; the subdivision merely gave that inheritance concrete form.

### Breira Principle

The mechanism by which Rav achieves this result is the principle of *breira* — retroactive clarification. *Yesh breira* means that once the brothers have divided and each has taken his portion, we say retroactively that this was always what each of them was meant to inherit, as if it had been designated from the outset. Since the father's debt encumbered the entire estate before either brother took anything, the lien applied to both halves. The creditor's decision to seize only one brother's portion does not mean that only that brother was subject to the debt; rather, because the entire inheritance was one unified process, the injured brother can return and claim that his brother must share the burden, and the division must be reconsidered.

Shmuel, by contrast, holds *ein breira* — that no such retroactive clarification operates, or at minimum that it does not apply here. The two brothers, in his view, are like purchasers who bought their respective halves from the jointly held estate. A purchaser who loses his property to a creditor of the seller has no claim against anyone else who purchased from the same seller; *viter*, he simply loses.

The broader context of *breira* across *Shas* is worth noting. There are approximately twelve *sugyot* that discuss this principle, and they do not all point in the same direction. The practical *halakhic* conclusion that has emerged — though it represents a compromise among the *rishonim* trying to reconcile these sources — is that in matters of *d'oraita* law we generally rule *ein breira*, while in *d'rabbanan* matters we generally rule *yesh breira*. The present case, touching as it does on the *d'oraita* framework of *yerusha*, would seem to fall into the category where we would normally say *ein breira*, which makes the ultimate *halakhic* ruling here all the more striking.

### Halacha: Rav's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הִלְכְתָא בְּכׇל הָנֵי שְׁמַעְתָּתָא – מְקַמְּצִין. אַמֵּימָר אָמַר: בָּטְלָה מַחְלוֹקֶת. וְהִלְכְתָא: בָּטְלָה מַחְלוֹקֶת
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: The *halakha* in all** the cases dealt with in **these statements** recording disagreements between Rav and Shmuel is that the brothers must each **take off a share** from their portion in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel. Rather, any brother currently in possession of his portion must give part of it to his brother who lacks a portion, so that in the end they have equal shares. **Ameimar says:** The *halakha* in all of these cases is that **the** original **division** of the property **is void,** in accordance with the opinion of Rav. The Gemara concludes: **The *halakha* is** in fact that **the** original **division** of the property **is void,** in accordance with the opinion of Rav.

This ruling is notable because in monetary matters (*dinei mamonot*) we generally follow Shmuel over Rav. One possible explanation is that Rav Assi's position, which at least partially aligns with Rav's by granting the injured brother some claim on his sibling, tipped the scales in Rav's direction. Alternatively, this may be one of those exceptional cases where the specific considerations of *yerusha* law override the usual decisional rule in favor of Shmuel.

### Rashi vs. Rashbam

The *Rashbam* explicitly addresses — and explicitly disagrees with — his grandfather Rashi, whose understanding of this *sugya* appears in the first chapter of Bava Kama, where the same case is discussed. The *Rashbam* prefaces his own explanation with the phrase *zekeni peresh hacha* — my grandfather explained it this way — before setting out his own divergent reading.

The crux of the disagreement concerns Rav Assi's position. The *Rashbam* understands Rav Assi as a pure *safek* between Rav and Shmuel: uncertain whether the brothers are *yorshin* or *lokchim*, he splits the contested half and awards the injured brother one quarter — half of what Rav would give him, nothing more than what Shmuel would leave him with. This gives Rav Assi a position that is structurally a midpoint between the two earlier *amoraim*, which is precisely the expected shape of a third position in such a debate.

Rashi, however, appears to understand Rav Assi's quarter-plus-quarter formulation literally: the injured brother receives one quarter of the field in land *and* one quarter in cash. This reading implies that Rav Assi's position is not simply halfway between Rav and Shmuel but is rather a hybrid of *yoresh* and *lokhe'ach be-achrayut* — the brother is treated as half *heir* and half *purchaser with guarantee*. The *Rashbam's* critique is structural: given the literary pattern of three-way *machlokot* in *Shas*, where the third position consistently combines or mediates the two prior ones, it would be anomalous for Rav Assi to introduce a third, independent option not derived from the earlier debate. The *Rashbam* therefore insists that Rav Assi's *and* must be read as *or*, and the position is simply the midpoint between Rav and Shmuel.

## Three Judges Assessment

### Two vs. One Majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁיָּרְדוּ לָשׁוּם – אֶחָד אוֹמֵר בְּמָנֶה, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים בְּמָאתַיִם; אֶחָד אוֹמֵר בְּמָאתַיִם, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים בְּמָנֶה – בָּטֵל יָחִיד בְּמִיעוּטוֹ
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Ketubot* 11:2): In a case of **three** experts **who went down to assess** a certain property in order to determine the amount to be collected from it for repayment of a debt, and **one says** it is worth **one hundred dinars, and** the other **two say** it is worth **two hundred,** or **one says** it is worth **two hundred** dinars **and** the other **two say** it is worth **one hundred,** the assessment of **the single** expert **is nullified, since** his is **the minority** opinion, and the assessment of the two others is accepted.

The *Gemara* first addresses the straightforward case: *echad omer be-maneh*, one judge says the property is worth 100, *shnayim omrim be-matayim*, and two judges say it is worth 200. Alternatively, *echad omer be-matayim u-shnayim omrim be-maneh*, one says 200 and two say 100. The rule in this case is simple: *batel yachid be-miuto* — the minority opinion is nullified by the majority. A *beit din* of three in monetary cases follows a two-against-one majority, and we assume that the lone dissenter simply erred. There is no attempt to average the three figures; the majority opinion stands entirely.

One might think that in a matter of property valuation — inherently an exercise in estimation — it would be appropriate to average all three assessors' figures. The *Gemara* rejects this: when two agree and one dissents, the two are presumed correct and the one is presumed to have made a mistake.

### Three Valuations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר בְּמָנֶה, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר בְּעֶשְׂרִים, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים – נִדּוֹן בְּמָנֶה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר: נִדּוֹן בְּתִשְׁעִים. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: עוֹשִׂין שׁוּמָא בֵּינֵיהֶן, וּמְשַׁלְּשִׁין
>
> **Translation:** If **one says** the property is worth **one hundred dinars, and another says** it is worth **twenty** *sela*, which is equivalent to eighty dinars, since four dinars equal a *sela*, **and** yet **another says** it is worth **thirty** *sela*, which is equivalent to one hundred and twenty dinars, **it is assessed at one hundred dinars,** which is the average of the assessments, as it is equivalent to twenty-five *sela*. **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says: It is assessed at ninety** dinars, as will be explained below. ***Aḥerim* say: An appraisal** is **performed** of the sum **between** the two most extreme assessments **and** then **divided by three.** This sum is then added to the lowest assessment.

The hard case, and the one the *Gemara* devotes its attention to, is where all three judges disagree with one another. One says the property is worth 100 (that is, 25 *sela*), one says it is worth 80 (20 *sela*), and one says it is worth 120 (30 *sela*). No two of the three agree, and there is no automatic majority rule to apply. Three different answers require three different approaches, each offering a distinct algorithm for resolving the deadlock.

### Tanna Kamma: 100

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר נִדּוֹן בְּמָנֶה – מִילְּתָא מְצִיעֲתָא
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the various opinions: **The one who says** that the property **is assessed at one hundred dinars** holds that **the middle** of the two extreme assessments is followed.

The first opinion, the *Tanna Kamma*, holds that the property is assessed at 100, the middle value. The question whether this means the *median* or the *mean* is worth pausing on. In the specific case before us the two coincide — 100 is both the middle figure of the three and the arithmetic average of 80, 100, and 120 — which is why the *Rashbam* flags the question. Were the three valuations asymmetrical, the median and the mean would diverge, and it would matter greatly which one the *Tanna Kamma* intends. The *Rashbam* discusses this explicitly, and the *Rambam* rules that the median — the middle value — is the operative figure. In the present case, with the values arranged as 80, 100, 120, the middle value and the average are both 100, so the *Tanna Kamma's* ruling is unambiguous in its result if not always in its rationale.

### R. Elazar: 90

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר: נִדּוֹן בְּתִשְׁעִים – קָא סָבַר: הָא אַרְעָא תִּשְׁעִים שָׁוְיָא; וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר עֶשְׂרִים, דְּקָא טָעֵי עַשְׂרָה לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ; וְהַאי דְּקָא אָמַר מָנֶה, קָא טָעֵי עַשְׂרָה לְקַמֵּיהּ
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says** that **it is assessed at ninety** dinars because **he holds that this land is** in fact **worth ninety** dinars, **and the one who says** it is worth **twenty** *sela*, which is equivalent to eighty dinars **errs** with an assessment that is **ten** dinars **behind,** i.e., too low, **and the one who says** it is worth **one hundred dinars errs** with an assessment that is **ten** dinars **ahead,** i.e., too high.

Rabbi Elazar berabbi Tzadok says the property should be assessed at 90. His reasoning: *kasavar hai ara tishim shavei* — he believes the true value of the property is 90. The judge who said 80 underestimated by 10, *katai asara le-achorav*, and the judge who said 100 overestimated by 10, *katai asara le-kamei*. The two of them, in his view, each erred by an equal and opposite amount, and their errors cancel out, placing the true value at 90 — the average of the two lower assessments.

This immediately raises the question: what does Rabbi Elazar berabbi Tzadok do with the judge who said 120? In his framework, that judge is simply wrong — dramatically so — and is entirely disregarded. The entire weight of his analysis rests on judges one and two, whose errors he treats as symmetrical. Judge three does not figure into his calculation at all.

### Acherim: 93⅓

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: עוֹשִׂין שׁוּמָא בֵּינֵיהֶן, וּמְשַׁלְּשִׁין – קָא סָבְרִי: הַאי אַרְעָא – תִּשְׁעִים וּתְלָתָא וְתִילְתָּא שָׁוְיָא; הַאי דְּקָא אָמַר עֶשְׂרִים – קָא טָעֵי תְּלֵיסַר וְתִילְתָּא לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר מָנֶה – קָא טָעֵי תְּלֵיסַר וְתִילְתָּא לְקַמֵּיהּ; וּבְדִין הוּא דְּלֵימָא טְפֵי, וְהַאי דְּלָא קָאָמַר, סָבַר: מִיסָּתַאי דְּקָא מְטַפֵּינָא כּוּלֵּי הַאי אַחַבְרַאי
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that ***Aḥerim* say** that **an appraisal** is **performed** of the sum **between** the two most extreme assessments **and** then **divided by three.** This sum is then added to the lowest assessment. The Gemara explains this opinion: *Aḥerim* **hold** that **this land is** in fact **worth ninety-three and one-third** dinars. **The one who says** it is worth **twenty** *sela*, the equivalent of eighty dinars, **errs** with an assessment that is **thirteen** dinars **and one-third behind,** and **the one who says it is** worth **one hundred dinars errs** with an assessment that is **thirteen** dinars **and one-third ahead. By right,** that assessor **should have said more,** i.e., quoted a higher sum, since according to this calculation, he should have said it is worth one hundred and six and two-thirds dinars. **And** the reason **that he did not** do so is that he **thinks** as follows: **It is enough that I add this much above** and beyond the assessment of **my colleague** who says it is worth eighty dinars. Therefore, he lowers the sum of his assessment to one hundred dinars.

The *Acherim* say you assess between them and divide into thirds. Their position holds that the true value of the property is 93⅓.

The algorithm works as follows: take the full range from the lowest valuation (80) to the highest (120), a spread of 40. Divide that spread by three — the number of judges — yielding 13⅓. Add that figure to the lowest valuation: 80 plus 13⅓ equals 93⅓. That is the true value according to *Acherim*.

Under this framework all three judges erred, not just one. The judge who said 80 underestimated by 13⅓. The judge who said 100 overestimated by 13⅓ — meaning his "true" valuation, had he been fully accurate, would have been 93⅓; but since he was already saying 20 more than the lowest judge, he rounded down to 100 out of deference to his colleagues: *mistaya deka matfina kulei hai chavrai*. And the judge who said 120 overestimated by 26⅔ — two full increments above the true value — placing him two steps away from the correct figure rather than one.

### Challenge to Acherim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה! הַאי אַרְעָא מְאָה וְעַשְׂרָה שָׁוְיָא; וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר מָנֶה – קָא טָעֵי עַשְׂרָה לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר שְׁלֹשִׁים – קָא טָעֵי עַשְׂרָה לְקַמֵּיהּ! נְקוֹט מִיהַת תְּרֵי קַמָּאֵי בִּידָךְ, דְּמִתּוֹרַת מָנֶה לָא מַפְּקִי לֵיהּ
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **On the contrary,** say that **this land is** in fact **worth one hundred and ten** dinars, **and the one who says** it is worth **one hundred dinars errs** with an assessment that is **ten** dinars **behind, and the one who says** it is worth **thirty** *sela*, which is equivalent to one hundred and twenty dinars, **errs** with an assessment that is **ten** dinars **ahead.** If so, the average of these two assessments, one hundred and ten dinars, should be followed. The Gemara replies: **In any event, grasp the first two** assessments **in your hand, as neither** of them **takes** the assessment **beyond the sum of one hundred dinars.**

The *Gemara* raises an immediate and powerful objection to *Acherim*: *adrava*. If you are going to use this method, why should the true value be 93⅓ rather than 113⅓? The alternative construction is equally coherent: suppose the true value is 113⅓. Then the judge who said 100 underestimated by 13⅓, the judge who said 120 overestimated by 13⅓ (and should have said 126⅔ but rounded down — *mistaya deka matfina kulei hai chavrai*, he was already saying 20 more than his colleague), and the judge who said 80 was simply wildly wrong, two increments below the true value. This produces a perfectly parallel structure. There is no internal asymmetry in *Acherim's* method that would privilege the lower two judges over the upper two. The *Gemara* concedes this difficulty.

### Lower Two Preferred

The answer offered is: *nakot mi-ha terei kama'ei be-yadcha de-mitorat me'ah lo mafkat lei* — you should take the first two, the lower two, in your hand, because the *Tanna Kamma's* position — that the value is 100 — is never really abandoned. That is, both Rabbi Elazar berabbi Tzadok and *Acherim* operate with 100 as their baseline: they are explaining how the assessments relate to a figure that is close to the arithmetic mean of all three. The result that privileges the lower two is closer to 100, and thus more consonant with the *Tanna Kamma*, than the result that privileges the upper two. This explanation is admittedly somewhat unsatisfying — it amounts to saying that we prefer the lower estimate as a matter of default, not because the logic of *Acherim's* own method compels it.

### Rav Huna: Acherim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הֲלָכָה כַּאֲחֵרִים
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of *Aḥerim*,** who say that the sum of the difference between the two most extreme assessments is calculated and then divided by three, and this sum is then added to the lowest assessment.

*Rav Huna* rules that the *halakha* follows *Acherim*, that is, the true value is assessed at 93⅓.

### Rav Ashi Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: טַעְמָא דַּאֲחֵרִים לָא יָדְעִינַן, הִלְכְתָא עָבְדִינַן כְּווֹתַיְיהוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We do not** even **understand the reason of *Aḥerim*; shall we** then **establish the *halakha* in accordance with their** opinion?

*Rav Ashi* objects sharply: if we do not understand *Acherim's* reasoning, should we nevertheless rule *halakha* like them? This is a pointed methodological challenge. It is technically possible to follow a *halakhic* opinion even without fully endorsing its rationale — one can simply adopt the algorithm and apply it mechanically. Rav Huna was apparently willing to do exactly that, instructing practice to follow *Acherim* perhaps because of the principle of *halakha ke-rabbim* or for other reasons. Rav Ashi refuses: an opinion whose reasoning is opaque should not be elevated to *halakha le-ma'aseh*.

The *machloket* between Rav Huna and Rav Ashi thus has a meta-halakhic dimension: can a decisional ruling be issued in favor of a position whose internal logic has not been satisfactorily established? The practical upshot is that because Rav Ashi's objection is unresolved, the *halakha le-ma'aseh* appears to revert to the *Tanna Kamma* — the middle value, 100.

### Dayyanei Golah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ דַּיָּינֵי גוֹלָה: עוֹשִׂין שׁוּמָא בֵּינֵיהֶן, וּמְשַׁלְּשִׁין. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הִלְכְתָא כְּדַיָּינֵי גוֹלָה. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: טַעְמָא דְּדַיָּינֵי גוֹלָה לָא יָדְעִינַן, הִלְכְתָא עָבְדִינַן כְּווֹתַיְיהוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **The judges of the Diaspora taught** a *baraita* that accords with the opinion of *Aḥerim* in the previously cited *baraita*: **An appraisal** is **performed** to determine the sum of the difference **between** the two most extreme assessments **and** then that sum is **divided by three** and added to the lowest assessment. **Rav Huna said: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with the judges of the Diaspora. Rav Ashi said: We do not** even **understand the reasoning of the judges of the Diaspora; shall we** then **establish the *halakha* in accordance with their** opinion?

A parallel formulation is then introduced: *dayyanei golah* — the judges of the diaspora, identified as Shmuel and Karna, two of the earliest *amoraim* — likewise ruled *osim shum beineihem u-meshalshin*, following the same method as *Acherim*. *Amar Rav Huna hilcheta ke-dayyanei golah* — Rav Huna again rules in their favor. *Amar Rav Ashi ta'ama de-dayyanei golah lo yadina hilcheta avdina ke-vateihu?* — and Rav Ashi again poses the identical challenge: the reasoning of *dayyanei golah* is no clearer than that of *Acherim*, so why should we follow their method?

The repetition is instructive. Rav Huna consistently rules in favor of the *Acherim* / *dayyanei golah* algorithm in both versions of the dispute, and Rav Ashi consistently refuses to accept a ruling whose rationale he cannot endorse. The *machloket* between them on how to *pasken* in this case remains unresolved, and the practical conclusion appears to favor the *Tanna Kamma's* simpler approach.

## Half Field Sale (II)

### Mishnah: Half Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״חֲצִי שָׂדֶה אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְנוֹטֵל חֲצִי שָׂדֵהוּ. ״חֶצְיָהּ בַּדָּרוֹם אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְנוֹטֵל חֶצְיָהּ בַּדָּרוֹם. וְהוּא מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו מְקוֹם הַגָּדֵר, חָרִיץ וּבֶן חָרִיץ. וְכַמָּה הוּא חָרִיץ? שִׁשָּׁה טְפָחִים. וּבֶן חָרִיץ – שְׁלֹשָׁה
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one says to another: I am selling you half a field,** without specifying which half he is selling, **an assessment is made** of the field, which is then divided **between them, and** the buyer **takes half of** the seller's **field.** If the seller says: **I am selling you the half that is on the southern side** of the field, **an assessment is made** of the northern and the southern sides of the field, which is then divided **between them, and he takes the half on the southern side. And he accepts upon himself** to provide **the space for the fence** between the two halves of the field out of his own property. He also accepts to provide out of his own property the space for the larger **ditch and the smaller ditch,** which are meant to keep animals out of the field. **And how** wide **is** the larger **ditch? Six handbreadths. And** how wide is **the smaller ditch? Three** handbreadths.

## Half Field Sale (II)

### Purchaser Gets Less

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לוֹקֵחַ נוֹטֵל כָּחוּשׁ שֶׁבּוֹ
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** When one sells half of his field to another person, **the buyer takes the leaner part of** the field, the part that is of lower quality.

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *loke'ach notel kachush shebo* — the purchaser always receives *kachush shebo*, the weaker or thinner portion of the field. The principle that the purchaser occupies the lower hand (*yad al ha-tachtonah*) applies here: in any assessment, when the field is divided, the buyer receives the half that is worth less.

### Chiyya's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהָא אֲנַן ״מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם״ תְּנַן! אָמַר לֵיהּ: אַדַּאֲכַלְתְּ כַּפְנְיָיתָא בְּבָבֶל – תַּרְגֵּימְנָא מִסֵּיפָא
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: But didn't we learn** in the mishna that in such a case **an assessment is made** of the field, which is then divided **between them,** which indicates that the buyer and the seller are given similar parcels of land? How then can you say that the buyer takes the leaner part? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him** in a sarcastic manner: **While you were eating dates in Babylonia** and neglecting your studies, **we explained** the matter **based on the latter clause** of the mishna, which proves that my understanding is correct.

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba pressed Rabbi Yochanan: *ve-ha-anan mishmenim beineihem tenan* — but the *Mishnah* says we do an assessment between them! If there is a proper *shuma*, how can the purchaser end up with the inferior portion? The *Mishnah* implies a fair and equal division by value, not a systematic disadvantage to one party.

Rabbi Yochanan's sharp reply — *adachalta kafneita be-Vavel*, you have been fattening yourself on dates in Bavel — was a pointed rebuke: Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba had spent his time in Bavel rather than properly mastering his *Mishnayot*. The answer, Rabbi Yochanan says, is found by reading the *reisha* of the *Mishnah* in light of the *seifa*: *targamna mi-seifa*, the explanation comes from the latter clause.

### Value vs. Area

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: ״חֶצְיָהּ בַּדָּרוֹם אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם וְנוֹטֵל חֶצְיָהּ בַּדָּרוֹם. וְאַמַּאי מְשַׁמְּנִין בֵּינֵיהֶם? וְהָא ״חֶצְיָהּ בַּדָּרוֹם״ אָמַר לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לִדְמֵי; הָכָא נָמֵי לִדְמֵי
>
> **Translation:** **As the latter clause teaches:** If the seller says: **I am selling you the half that is on the southern side** of the field, **an assessment is made** of the northern and the southern sides of the field, which is then divided **between them, and he takes the half on the southern side.** If taken literally, this passage gives rise to a difficulty: **Why is an assessment made** of the northern and the southern sides of the field, which is then divided **between them?** In any case, **didn't he say to him** that he is selling him **the southern half?** Let the seller give the buyer the southern half of the field. Why is an assessment necessary? **Rather,** it must be that the matter is more complicated than it seems, and the mishna is referring **to money.** That is to say, the buyer takes the southern half, but the seller must reimburse him with money for the difference in value between the two halves of the field. **Here too,** in the first case, the mishna is referring **to money:** The buyer takes the leaner half, but the seller must reimburse him with money for the difference in value between the two halves of the field.

In the *seifa* of the *Mishnah*, when the seller says *chatziyah ba-darom ani mocher lach* — I am selling you the southern half — the *Mishnah* still requires an assessment, even though the location of the half being sold was specified explicitly. *Ve-amai mishamninan beineihu* — why is an assessment necessary if the southern half was explicitly designated? *Ela le-damei* — it must be that the assessment is performed not in terms of *area* but in terms of *value*. The field is divided so that each party receives a portion equal in monetary value to half the total. This will almost certainly not produce two equally sized parcels of land, since one part of a field may be more fertile, better situated, or otherwise more valuable than another.

*Ve-hacha nami le-damei* — and so too in the opening case: the assessment is by value, not by area. The purchaser receives a parcel whose monetary value equals half the total price of the field, but in terms of actual land area he may receive less than half. This is what Rabbi Yochanan means by *kachush shebo*: not that the purchaser is cheated in terms of value, but that in terms of sheer area he ends up with the smaller piece of ground, because the portion he receives is assessed as equal in value to the more spacious but less valuable half retained by the seller.

## Fence & Ditch (II)

### Ditch Configuration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְקַבֵּל עָלָיו מְקוֹם גָּדֵר כּוּ׳. תָּאנָא: חָרִיץ מִבַּחוּץ, וּבֶן חָרִיץ מִבִּפְנִים. וְזֶה וָזֶה אֲחוֹרֵי גָדֵר, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא חַיָּה קוֹפֶצֶת
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the buyer **accepts upon himself** to provide out of his own property **the space for the fence** between the two halves of the field and for the larger and smaller ditches. A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: The larger **ditch** is dug **on the outside, while the smaller ditch** is dug **on the inside,** closer to the field. Both **this and that** are dug **behind the fence, so that an animal will not jump** over the fence, enter the field, and cause damage.

The *Tana* specifies the physical arrangement: *charitz mi-bachutz u-ven charitz mi-bifnim* — the outer ditch (*charitz*) runs on the outside of the field, and the secondary ditch (*ben charitz*) runs on the inside. *Ve-zeh ve-zeh achorei geder* — and both of them are positioned behind, that is, adjacent to, the *geder*, the fence or wall. The practical picture that emerges is: going from the seller's land toward the purchaser's field, one first encounters the boundary, then the *charitz* (six *tefachim* wide) on the outer side, then the *geder*, then the *ben charitz* (three *tefachim* wide) on the inner side, and only then the actual cultivable field the purchaser has acquired. The purchaser thus bears responsibility for this entire strip — the ditch, the fence, and the secondary ditch — within his portion.

### Animal Protection

The reason for this configuration is stated directly: *kedei shelo tehei chayah kofetzet* — so that an animal cannot jump over the fence. The ditches serve a dual purpose. On the one hand they function as irrigation channels. On the other, they effectively increase the functional height of the wall: if an animal approaches from outside, the *charitz* drops away immediately at the base of the fence, making the fence appear far taller from the animal's perspective; and on the inside the *ben charitz* similarly prevents an animal from gaining the purchase it would need to leap outward. Both ditches together ensure that the wall is an effective barrier in both directions.

### Cannot Omit Ditches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְלַעֲבֵיד חָרִיץ – וְלָא לַעֲבֵיד בֶּן חָרִיץ! אַיְּידֵי דְּרָוַוח, קָיְימָא בְּגַוֵּיהּ וְקָפְצָה. וְלַעֲבֵיד בֶּן חָרִיץ – וְלָא לַעֲבֵיד חָרִיץ! אַיְּידֵי דְּקַטִּין, קָיְימָא אַשִּׂפְתֵּיהּ וְקָפְצָה
>
> **Translation:** Let him make** only a larger **ditch and not make a smaller ditch.** The Gemara replies: **Since** the ditch **is wide,** the animal **can stand inside it and jump** from there over the fence. The Gemara asks: If so, **then let him make** only **a smaller ditch and not make** a larger **ditch? Since the ditch is small,** the animal **stands on its edge and jumps** over the fence.

One might ask whether the purchaser could simply omit one of the two ditches and reclaim that strip of land for cultivation. The *Gemara* addresses both possibilities and finds them wanting. If the purchaser builds the *charitz* but omits the *ben charitz* — *aidi de-ravach kayama be-gavei ve-kaftza* — the open width on the inner side now provides enough of a platform that an animal standing in that space could jump over the fence. The larger ditch on the outside is no longer sufficient protection on its own once there is room to maneuver on the inside. Conversely, if the purchaser builds the *ben charitz* but omits the outer *charitz* — *aidi de-katin kayama asfatei ve-kaftza* — the three-*tefachim* inner ditch is simply too narrow to deter an animal; it can stand at the edge and leap the fence without difficulty. Both ditches are thus indispensable, and neither may be omitted in favor of reclaiming extra land. The purchaser must include the full configuration — *charitz*, *geder*, and *ben charitz* — within his allocated portion.
