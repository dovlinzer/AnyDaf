# Bava Batra 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Creditor Seizes Inherita…

### Case Setup

The *sugya* on daf 107a opens with *itmar*, signaling a new *amoraic* dispute, and it constitutes the second half of the *sugya* begun on the previous daf. Yesterday's discussion concerned a case in which two brothers divided an inheritance and a third brother subsequently appeared to claim his share, raising the question of how the property is to be re-divided. The present case is structurally similar but involves a different kind of intrusion upon the original division.

The *Gemara* states: *achim she-chalku u-va ba'al chov ve-natal chelko shel echad me-hem* — two brothers divided an inherited estate, and a creditor then came and seized the share of one of them. It is critical to the case that the debt in question is the father's debt, a lien that encumbered the estate prior to the division. The creditor, rather than pursuing the estate collectively, went to one brother alone and seized his portion. The result is that one brother retains a full half of the inheritance while the other is left with nothing. The question before the *Gemara* is how, if at all, the injured brother can claim redress from his sibling.

### Rav: Division Nullified

*Rav amar batla machloket* — Rav holds that the original division of the property is retroactively canceled on account of this change of circumstances, and the brothers must go back and re-divide whatever property remains. The practical implication is that the surviving brother cannot simply walk away with the entirety of the undisturbed half; the injured brother has a claim to re-divide it, and the assessment would presumably be based on the value of the land at the time of the original inheritance, leaving any improvements made by the retaining brother as his own investment and benefit.

### Shmuel: Brother Loses

*Shmuel amar viter* — Shmuel takes the opposite view. The brother who had his share seized simply loses out. His loss is his own misfortune and he has no claim against his sibling.

### Rav Assi: Quarter Share

Rav Assi offers a middle position: *notel revi'a be-karka u-revi'a be-ma'ot* — the injured brother receives one quarter either in land or in cash. The *Rashbam* explains that the *vav* here functions as *or* rather than *and*, so that Rav Assi is offering the brother a choice of receiving one quarter of the remaining field or its equivalent value in cash — not both simultaneously, which would make his position indistinguishable from Rav's. The reason the brother gets only a quarter rather than a full half, as Rav would grant, is that Rav Assi treats this as a genuine *safek* between Rav and Shmuel and splits the disputed half accordingly.

The structure is familiar in *Shas*: a three-way *machloket* in which two *amoraim* take opposite extreme positions and a later authority — Rav Assi, one generation after Rav and Shmuel — attempts a *peshara*, splitting the difference between them. As for the option of cash, the *Rashbam* explains that the injured brother can argue: had he possessed cash at the time, he could have paid off the creditor himself and retained his land. The creditor seized land only because land was the available asset.

### Heirs vs. Purchasers

The *Gemara* now provides the conceptual underpinning for the dispute between Rav and Shmuel. *Rav amar batla machloket kasavar ha-achin she-chalku yorshin hein* — Rav's ruling that the division is nullified flows from his view that when brothers subdivide inherited property they remain in the category of *yorshin*, inheritors. *U-Shmuel amar viter kasavar ha-achin she-chalku lakochot havu* — Shmuel's ruling that the brother simply loses out flows from his view that when brothers subdivide inherited property they become *lakochot*, purchasers.

The stakes of this conceptual disagreement are considerable. According to Shmuel, the inheritance exists as one stage — the entire plot is jointly held by the heirs — and when they come to divide it, that subdivision constitutes a secondary act analogous to purchasing from the corporate estate. The one who "buys" without *achrayut*, without any guarantee or warranty, has no recourse when a pre-existing lien surfaces and extinguishes his share. He is no different from any other purchaser who loses property to a creditor of his seller.

According to Rav, both the initial inheritance and the subsequent subdivision are parts of a single unified process of *yerusha*. Each brother's share was always his inheritance; the subdivision merely gave that inheritance concrete form.

### Breira Principle

The mechanism by which Rav achieves this result is the principle of *breira* — retroactive clarification. *Yesh breira* means that once the brothers have divided and each has taken his portion, we say retroactively that this was always what each of them was meant to inherit, as if it had been designated from the outset. Since the father's debt encumbered the entire estate before either brother took anything, the lien applied to both halves. The creditor's decision to seize only one brother's portion does not mean that only that brother was subject to the debt; rather, because the entire inheritance was one unified process, the injured brother can return and claim that his brother must share the burden, and the division must be reconsidered.

Shmuel, by contrast, holds *ein breira* — that no such retroactive clarification operates, or at minimum that it does not apply here. The two brothers, in his view, are like purchasers who bought their respective halves from the jointly held estate. A purchaser who loses his property to a creditor of the seller has no claim against anyone else who purchased from the same seller; *viter*, he simply loses.

The broader context of *breira* across *Shas* is worth noting. There are approximately twelve *sugyot* that discuss this principle, and they do not all point in the same direction. The practical *halakhic* conclusion that has emerged — though it represents a compromise among the *rishonim* trying to reconcile these sources — is that in matters of *d'oraita* law we generally rule *ein breira*, while in *d'rabbanan* matters we generally rule *yesh breira*. The present case, touching as it does on the *d'oraita* framework of *yerusha*, would seem to fall into the category where we would normally say *ein breira*, which makes the ultimate *halakhic* ruling here all the more striking.

### Halacha: Rav's View

*Amar Rav Papa hilcheta be-khol hani shema'ata ke-Rav* — Rav Papa rules that in all the related *sugyot* the *halakha* follows Rav. *Amemar amar batla machloket ve-hilcheta batla machloket* — Ameimar likewise rules that the division is nullified, and the *halakha* is accordingly *batla machloket*, following Rav.

This ruling is notable because in monetary matters (*dinei mamonot*) we generally follow Shmuel over Rav. One possible explanation is that Rav Assi's position, which at least partially aligns with Rav's by granting the injured brother some claim on his sibling, tipped the scales in Rav's direction. Alternatively, this may be one of those exceptional cases where the specific considerations of *yerusha* law override the usual decisional rule in favor of Shmuel.

### Rashi vs. Rashbam

The *Rashbam* explicitly addresses — and explicitly disagrees with — his grandfather Rashi, whose understanding of this *sugya* appears in the first chapter of Bava Kama, where the same case is discussed. The *Rashbam* prefaces his own explanation with the phrase *zekeni peresh hacha* — my grandfather explained it this way — before setting out his own divergent reading.

The crux of the disagreement concerns Rav Assi's position. The *Rashbam* understands Rav Assi as a pure *safek* between Rav and Shmuel: uncertain whether the brothers are *yorshin* or *lokchim*, he splits the contested half and awards the injured brother one quarter — half of what Rav would give him, nothing more than what Shmuel would leave him with. This gives Rav Assi a position that is structurally a midpoint between the two earlier *amoraim*, which is precisely the expected shape of a third position in such a debate.

Rashi, however, appears to understand Rav Assi's quarter-plus-quarter formulation literally: the injured brother receives one quarter of the field in land *and* one quarter in cash. This reading implies that Rav Assi's position is not simply halfway between Rav and Shmuel but is rather a hybrid of *yoresh* and *lokhe'ach be-achrayut* — the brother is treated as half *heir* and half *purchaser with guarantee*. The *Rashbam's* critique is structural: given the literary pattern of three-way *machlokot* in *Shas*, where the third position consistently combines or mediates the two prior ones, it would be anomalous for Rav Assi to introduce a third, independent option not derived from the earlier debate. The *Rashbam* therefore insists that Rav Assi's *and* must be read as *or*, and the position is simply the midpoint between Rav and Shmuel.

## Three Judges Assessment

### Two vs. One Majority

*Tanu rabanan* — the *Rabbanan* taught. The context the *Rashbam* supplies is an estate that must be assessed not only for the purposes of dividing among the brothers, but also because there is a widow with a claim to *mezonot* from the estate, and daughters who likewise have a maintenance claim. Three judges go down — *yardu*, a technical verb for entering a judicial process or descending into the field — to assess the property's value.

The *Gemara* first addresses the straightforward case: *echad omer be-maneh*, one judge says the property is worth 100, *shnayim omrim be-matayim*, and two judges say it is worth 200. Alternatively, *echad omer be-matayim u-shnayim omrim be-maneh*, one says 200 and two say 100. The rule in this case is simple: *batel yachid be-miuto* — the minority opinion is nullified by the majority. A *beit din* of three in monetary cases follows a two-against-one majority, and we assume that the lone dissenter simply erred. There is no attempt to average the three figures; the majority opinion stands entirely.

One might think that in a matter of property valuation — inherently an exercise in estimation — it would be appropriate to average all three assessors' figures. The *Gemara* rejects this: when two agree and one dissents, the two are presumed correct and the one is presumed to have made a mistake.

### Three Valuations

The hard case, and the one the *Gemara* devotes its attention to, is where all three judges disagree with one another. One says the property is worth 100 (that is, 25 *sela*), one says it is worth 80 (20 *sela*), and one says it is worth 120 (30 *sela*). No two of the three agree, and there is no automatic majority rule to apply. Three different answers require three different approaches, each offering a distinct algorithm for resolving the deadlock.

### Tanna Kamma: 100

*Man de-amar nidon be-maneh — milta metziata* — the first opinion, the *Tanna Kamma*, holds that the property is assessed at 100, the middle value. The question whether this means the *median* or the *mean* is worth pausing on. In the specific case before us the two coincide — 100 is both the middle figure of the three and the arithmetic average of 80, 100, and 120 — which is why the *Rashbam* flags the question. Were the three valuations asymmetrical, the median and the mean would diverge, and it would matter greatly which one the *Tanna Kamma* intends. The *Rashbam* discusses this explicitly, and the *Rambam* rules that the median — the middle value — is the operative figure. In the present case, with the values arranged as 80, 100, 120, the middle value and the average are both 100, so the *Tanna Kamma's* ruling is unambiguous in its result if not always in its rationale.

### R. Elazar: 90

*Rabbi Elazar berabbi Tzadok omer nidon be-tishim* — Rabbi Elazar berabbi Tzadok says the property should be assessed at 90. His reasoning: *kasavar hai ara tishim shavei* — he believes the true value of the property is 90. The judge who said 80 underestimated by 10, *katai asara le-achorav*, and the judge who said 100 overestimated by 10, *katai asara le-kamei*. The two of them, in his view, each erred by an equal and opposite amount, and their errors cancel out, placing the true value at 90 — the average of the two lower assessments.

This immediately raises the question: what does Rabbi Elazar berabbi Tzadok do with the judge who said 120? In his framework, that judge is simply wrong — dramatically so — and is entirely disregarded. The entire weight of his analysis rests on judges one and two, whose errors he treats as symmetrical. Judge three does not figure into his calculation at all.

### Acherim: 93⅓

*Acherim omrim: osim shuma beineihem u-meshalshin* — the *Acherim* say you assess between them and divide into thirds. Their position holds that the true value of the property is 93⅓.

The algorithm works as follows: take the full range from the lowest valuation (80) to the highest (120), a spread of 40. Divide that spread by three — the number of judges — yielding 13⅓. Add that figure to the lowest valuation: 80 plus 13⅓ equals 93⅓. That is the true value according to *Acherim*.

Under this framework all three judges erred, not just one. The judge who said 80 underestimated by 13⅓. The judge who said 100 overestimated by 13⅓ — meaning his "true" valuation, had he been fully accurate, would have been 93⅓; but since he was already saying 20 more than the lowest judge, he rounded down to 100 out of deference to his colleagues: *mistaya deka matfina kulei hai chavrai*. And the judge who said 120 overestimated by 26⅔ — two full increments above the true value — placing him two steps away from the correct figure rather than one.

### Challenge to Acherim

The *Gemara* raises an immediate and powerful objection to *Acherim*: *adrava*. If you are going to use this method, why should the true value be 93⅓ rather than 113⅓? The alternative construction is equally coherent: suppose the true value is 113⅓. Then the judge who said 100 underestimated by 13⅓, the judge who said 120 overestimated by 13⅓ (and should have said 126⅔ but rounded down — *mistaya deka matfina kulei hai chavrai*, he was already saying 20 more than his colleague), and the judge who said 80 was simply wildly wrong, two increments below the true value. This produces a perfectly parallel structure. There is no internal asymmetry in *Acherim's* method that would privilege the lower two judges over the upper two. The *Gemara* concedes this difficulty.

### Lower Two Preferred

The answer offered is: *nakot mi-ha terei kama'ei be-yadcha de-mitorat me'ah lo mafkat lei* — you should take the first two, the lower two, in your hand, because the *Tanna Kamma's* position — that the value is 100 — is never really abandoned. That is, both Rabbi Elazar berabbi Tzadok and *Acherim* operate with 100 as their baseline: they are explaining how the assessments relate to a figure that is close to the arithmetic mean of all three. The result that privileges the lower two is closer to 100, and thus more consonant with the *Tanna Kamma*, than the result that privileges the upper two. This explanation is admittedly somewhat unsatisfying — it amounts to saying that we prefer the lower estimate as a matter of default, not because the logic of *Acherim's* own method compels it.

### Rav Huna: Acherim

*Amar Rav Huna halacha ke-Acherim* — Rav Huna rules that the *halakha* follows *Acherim*, that is, the true value is assessed at 93⅓.

### Rav Ashi Objects

*Amar Rav Ashi ta'ama de-Acherim lo yadina hilcheta avdina ke-vateihu?* — Rav Ashi objects sharply: if we do not understand *Acherim's* reasoning, should we nevertheless rule *halakha* like them? This is a pointed methodological challenge. It is technically possible to follow a *halakhic* opinion even without fully endorsing its rationale — one can simply adopt the algorithm and apply it mechanically. Rav Huna was apparently willing to do exactly that, instructing practice to follow *Acherim* perhaps because of the principle of *halakha ke-rabbim* or for other reasons. Rav Ashi refuses: an opinion whose reasoning is opaque should not be elevated to *halakha le-ma'aseh*.

The *machloket* between Rav Huna and Rav Ashi thus has a meta-halakhic dimension: can a decisional ruling be issued in favor of a position whose internal logic has not been satisfactorily established? The practical upshot is that because Rav Ashi's objection is unresolved, the *halakha le-ma'aseh* appears to revert to the *Tanna Kamma* — the middle value, 100.

### Dayyanei Golah

A parallel formulation is then introduced: *dayyanei golah* — the judges of the diaspora, identified as Shmuel and Karna, two of the earliest *amoraim* — likewise ruled *osim shum beineihem u-meshalshin*, following the same method as *Acherim*. *Amar Rav Huna hilcheta ke-dayyanei golah* — Rav Huna again rules in their favor. *Amar Rav Ashi ta'ama de-dayyanei golah lo yadina hilcheta avdina ke-vateihu?* — and Rav Ashi again poses the identical challenge: the reasoning of *dayyanei golah* is no clearer than that of *Acherim*, so why should we follow their method?

The repetition is instructive. Rav Huna consistently rules in favor of the *Acherim* / *dayyanei golah* algorithm in both versions of the dispute, and Rav Ashi consistently refuses to accept a ruling whose rationale he cannot endorse. The *machloket* between them on how to *pasken* in this case remains unresolved, and the practical conclusion appears to favor the *Tanna Kamma's* simpler approach.

## Half Field Sale

### Mishnah: Half Field

The *Mishnah* states: *ha-omer la-chaveiro chatzi sadeh ani mocher lach* — one who says to his fellow "I am selling you half of my field." *Mishmenim beineihem ve-notel chatzi sadehu* — an assessment is made between them and he takes half the field. If he specified which half — *chatziyah ba-darom ani mocher lach*, I am selling you the southern half — then likewise an assessment is made and he takes the southern half.

## Half Field Sale (II)

### Purchaser Gets Less

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *loke'ach notel kachush shebo* — the purchaser always receives *kachush shebo*, the weaker or thinner portion of the field. The principle that the purchaser occupies the lower hand (*yad al ha-tachtonah*) applies here: in any assessment, when the field is divided, the buyer receives the half that is worth less.

### Chiyya's Challenge

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba pressed Rabbi Yochanan: *ve-ha-anan mishmenim beineihem tenan* — but the *Mishnah* says we do an assessment between them! If there is a proper *shuma*, how can the purchaser end up with the inferior portion? The *Mishnah* implies a fair and equal division by value, not a systematic disadvantage to one party.

Rabbi Yochanan's sharp reply — *adachalta kafneita be-Vavel*, you have been fattening yourself on dates in Bavel — was a pointed rebuke: Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba had spent his time in Bavel rather than properly mastering his *Mishnayot*. The answer, Rabbi Yochanan says, is found by reading the *reisha* of the *Mishnah* in light of the *seifa*: *targamna mi-seifa*, the explanation comes from the latter clause.

### Value vs. Area

In the *seifa* of the *Mishnah*, when the seller says *chatziyah ba-darom ani mocher lach* — I am selling you the southern half — the *Mishnah* still requires an assessment, even though the location of the half being sold was specified explicitly. *Ve-amai mishamninan beineihu* — why is an assessment necessary if the southern half was explicitly designated? *Ela le-damei* — it must be that the assessment is performed not in terms of *area* but in terms of *value*. The field is divided so that each party receives a portion equal in monetary value to half the total. This will almost certainly not produce two equally sized parcels of land, since one part of a field may be more fertile, better situated, or otherwise more valuable than another.

*Ve-hacha nami le-damei* — and so too in the opening case: the assessment is by value, not by area. The purchaser receives a parcel whose monetary value equals half the total price of the field, but in terms of actual land area he may receive less than half. This is what Rabbi Yochanan means by *kachush shebo*: not that the purchaser is cheated in terms of value, but that in terms of sheer area he ends up with the smaller piece of ground, because the portion he receives is assessed as equal in value to the more spacious but less valuable half retained by the seller.

## Fence & Ditch (II)

### Ditch Configuration

The *Tana* specifies the physical arrangement: *charitz mi-bachutz u-ven charitz mi-bifnim* — the outer ditch (*charitz*) runs on the outside of the field, and the secondary ditch (*ben charitz*) runs on the inside. *Ve-zeh ve-zeh achorei geder* — and both of them are positioned behind, that is, adjacent to, the *geder*, the fence or wall. The practical picture that emerges is: going from the seller's land toward the purchaser's field, one first encounters the boundary, then the *charitz* (six *tefachim* wide) on the outer side, then the *geder*, then the *ben charitz* (three *tefachim* wide) on the inner side, and only then the actual cultivable field the purchaser has acquired. The purchaser thus bears responsibility for this entire strip — the ditch, the fence, and the secondary ditch — within his portion.

### Animal Protection

The reason for this configuration is stated directly: *kedei shelo tehei chayah kofetzet* — so that an animal cannot jump over the fence. The ditches serve a dual purpose. On the one hand they function as irrigation channels. On the other, they effectively increase the functional height of the wall: if an animal approaches from outside, the *charitz* drops away immediately at the base of the fence, making the fence appear far taller from the animal's perspective; and on the inside the *ben charitz* similarly prevents an animal from gaining the purchase it would need to leap outward. Both ditches together ensure that the wall is an effective barrier in both directions.

### Cannot Omit Ditches

One might ask whether the purchaser could simply omit one of the two ditches and reclaim that strip of land for cultivation. The *Gemara* addresses both possibilities and finds them wanting. If the purchaser builds the *charitz* but omits the *ben charitz* — *aidi de-ravach kayama be-gavei ve-kaftza* — the open width on the inner side now provides enough of a platform that an animal standing in that space could jump over the fence. The larger ditch on the outside is no longer sufficient protection on its own once there is room to maneuver on the inside. Conversely, if the purchaser builds the *ben charitz* but omits the outer *charitz* — *aidi de-katin kayama asfatei ve-kaftza* — the three-*tefachim* inner ditch is simply too narrow to deter an animal; it can stand at the edge and leap the fence without difficulty. Both ditches are thus indispensable, and neither may be omitted in favor of reclaiming extra land. The purchaser must include the full configuration — *charitz*, *geder*, and *ben charitz* — within his allocated portion.
