# Zevachim 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Resh Lakish vs. R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – הַאי קְרָא לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ: אַזְהָרָה לְאוֹכֵל בְּשַׂר קוֹדֶשׁ! דְּאִיתְּמַר: אַזְהָרָה לָאוֹכֵל בְּשַׂר קוֹדֶשׁ מִנַּיִן? רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע״.
>
> **Translation:** But does **Reish Lakish** actually hold that **this verse comes to** teach **this** *halakha*? **He requires this** verse to teach **a prohibition for** a ritually impure person **who eats sacrificial meat. As it was stated: From where** is **the prohibition for** a ritually impure person **who eats sacrificial meat** derived? **Reish Lakish says** it is derived from the verse: **"Every consecrated item she shall not touch,"** which the Gemara will explain is referring to eating.

### Verse Context

The daf opens three lines from the top of 34a, in the middle of an ongoing debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish. The verse under examination is *bechol kodesh lo tiga v'el hamikdash lo tavo* — "do not touch anything holy, and do not enter the sanctuary" — a verse originally addressed to a woman after childbirth. This verse had previously served as the entry point into a broader discussion about *bi'ah b'miktzat*, namely whether partial entry into the *mikdash* by a *tamei* person constitutes a violation. That question was left unresolved. Now the Gemara turns to ask: setting aside that derivation, what else do we learn from this verse?

### Resh Lakish's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר לוֹקֶה – ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע״, לָא שְׁנָא לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה וְלָא שְׁנָא לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** He is **flogged,** as it is written: **"Every consecrated item she shall not touch,"** without limiting the prohibition to a specific time, indicating that it **is no different** if one eats the sacrificial meat **prior to sprinkling** the blood, **and** it **is no different** if one does so **after sprinkling** the blood.

Resh Lakish reads the verse at its most straightforward level. *Bechol kodesh lo tiga* — don't touch anything sanctified — constitutes a simple *lav*, a negative prohibition, and one who violates it receives lashes. But Resh Lakish goes further. Since the verse concludes with a reference to entering the *mikdash* — an act punishable by *kareis* — the context is clearly one of great weight. Mere touching of an object, absent any *kareis* connection, would ordinarily not rise to that level. Therefore Resh Lakish understands the verse as encompassing not only touching but also eating sacrificial food while *tamei*, an act that does carry *kareis*. Both prohibitions — touching and eating — are thus derived from this single verse.

### R. Yochanan's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה – כִּדְתָנֵי בַּרְדְּלָא: אָתְיָא ״טוּמְאָתוֹ״–״טוּמְאָתוֹ״, וְכִי כְּתַב הָהוּא – לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged, as** the Sage **Bardela teaches** that the prohibition **is derived** by means of the verbal analogy cited before, as the verse states **"his impurity"** with regard to an impure person who eats sacrificial food, and states **"his impurity"** with regard to an impure person entering the Temple. **And when** "his impurity" **is written,** it is with regard to partaking of sacrificial meat **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He holds that this verse is not speaking about sacrifices at all but about *terumah*. This is admittedly a striking position — it implies that one would receive lashes for touching *terumah* but not for touching a sacrifice — and the *Shita Mekubetzes* offers an alternative reading. Nevertheless, Rabbi Yochanan does not derive the prohibition against a *tamei* person eating *kodesh* from this verse. He arrives at it through a different route.

### Methodological Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״בְּקֹדֶשׁ״; מַאי ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers for Reish Lakish: **If so,** that the verse was referring only to partaking of sacrificial meat after the sprinkling of the blood, **let the verse say: A consecrated item** she shall not touch. **What** is the reason for using the phrase **"every consecrated item"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** i.e., it also includes not eating sacrificial meat before the sprinkling of the blood.

The methodological contrast between the two *amoraim* is sharp. Resh Lakish derives the prohibition of a *tamei* person eating sacrifices from the verse itself; Rabbi Yochanan derives it instead from the fact that the act is punishable by *kareis*, drawing on the juxtaposition of two separate *pesukim*, each of which uses the word *tumaso*, through a *gezeirah shavah*. The verse *vetumato alav venichreta* (Vayikra 7:20) concerns eating sacrificial meat while *tamei*; the verse *o tumaso bo* concerns entering the *beis hamikdash* while *tamei*. Since the latter is accompanied by an explicit *lav* — *velo yetame'u es machanechem* — Rabbi Yochanan infers by a kind of transitive equivalence that a *lav* applies to eating *kodesh* while *tamei* as well. For him, the verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga* plays no role in this derivation.

## Tamei Before Zerika

### Third Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְטָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל בְּשַׂר קוֹדֶשׁ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה! דְּאִתְּמַר: טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל בְּשַׂר קוֹדֶשׁ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לוֹקֶה, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** this verse is **still necessary for** Reish Lakish to teach the *halakha* of **a ritually impure** person **who ate sacrificial meat before** the **sprinkling** of the blood of the offering on the altar, when the meat is not yet permitted. **As it was stated:** With regard to **an impure** individual **who ate sacrificial meat before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, **Reish Lakish says:** He is **flogged** for doing so, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged.**

With the methodological background established, the Gemara now presses Resh Lakish: having derived two things from the verse — touching and eating — is there yet a third? The answer is yes: the verse also teaches that a *tamei* person who eats sacrificial meat *before the blood has been placed on the altar* — before *zerikah* — is in violation.

This requires some unpacking. The pre-*zerikah* stage is one in which the meat has not yet become fit for human consumption. The *zerikah* — the throwing of the blood on the altar — is the act that renders the meat permissible to eat. Before that stage, no human being is permitted to consume the meat in any case. One might therefore argue that the prohibition against a *tamei* person eating *kodesh* applies only to meat that is in a state fit to be eaten, and that this prohibitory framework simply does not extend to meat that no one is yet permitted to consume. Perhaps, in other words, the prohibition is contingent on edibility.

### Baraita Dispute

The Gemara presents a *baraita* that directly frames the debate. A *tamei* person who eats sanctified meat before *zerikah*: Resh Lakish says he receives lashes; Rabbi Yochanan says he does not. Resh Lakish's reasoning is that the verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga* makes no distinction — *lo shana lifnei zerikah velo shana le'achar zerikah* — and the extra word *bechol* ("any") is precisely what broadens the prohibition to include circumstances in which the meat is not yet fit to be eaten. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, draws his prohibition from the *gezeirah shavah* linking *vetumato alav venichreta* to the *bi'at mikdash* verses. As he reads it, the entire context of that verse — *vehabasar kol tahor yochal basar*, all that is *tahor* may eat the meat — presupposes meat that is already in a state of permitted consumption. The whole *inyan* of that verse postdates *zerikah*, and therefore his prohibition does not reach the pre-*zerikah* stage.

It is worth noting a recurring pattern. Throughout these two *dappim*, Resh Lakish is consistently prepared to assign lashes, while Rabbi Yochanan is more reticent. This is somewhat surprising: in tractate *Avodah Zarah*, the dynamic appears to be reversed, with Resh Lakish generally more lenient and Rabbi Yochanan more stringent. Whether the reversal is connected to the particular subject matter of *Kodshim* or to the specific question of *malkus* is unclear, but the pattern here is pronounced.

### Abaye's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לוֹקֶה. דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר״ – לְרַבּוֹת עֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה דְּלָאו בְּנֵי אֲכִילָה נִינְהוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי רַבִּינְהוּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself:** With regard to **an impure** individual **who ate sacrificial meat before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, **Reish Lakish says:** He is **flogged** for doing so, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged. Abaye says:** This **dispute** applies **with regard to** a case of **impurity of the body** of the one who eats the meat, **but with regard to impurity of the flesh** itself, i.e., if the sacrificial meat was ritually impure, **all agree** that he is **flogged.**
>
> This is **as the verse states:** "And the flesh that touches any impure item shall not be eaten; it shall be burned with fire; and the flesh, every one that is pure may eat the flesh" (Leviticus 7:19). The Sages derived that the extra term **"and the flesh"** serves **to include wood and frankincense, which are not fit for consumption, and even so the verse included them** as being susceptible to impurity, and one who eats them while he is impure is flogged. Therefore, sacrificial meat before the sprinkling of the blood, which is fit for consumption, is certainly included in the prohibition.

Abaye now enters to reframe the debate. The dispute between Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan, he says, concerns only *tum'at haguf* — the case where the *person* is *tamei* — and whether that person's *tumah* generates lashes when he eats pre-*zerikah* meat. But if we are speaking of *tum'at habasar* — the case where the *meat* itself is *tamei* — everyone agrees the person receives lashes, even if the blood has not yet been placed on the altar.

Why would *tum'at habasar* be different? Because there is a separate verse: *vehabasar asher yiga bechol tamei lo ye'achel* — meat that has come into contact with anything *tamei* shall not be eaten. And then there is an additional phrase: *vehabasar kol tahor yochal basar*, all that is pure may eat the meat. This extra phrase — and specifically the word *vehabasar* — is read as including things that are not literally meat at all: wood that goes on the altar, frankincense, items that are entirely inedible. If the verse extends its prohibition to frankincense and wood — objects no human would eat — then it certainly extends to meat before *zerikah*, whose only disqualification is that it has not yet reached the stage of permitted consumption.

### Vehabasar Inclusion

The inclusion of wood and frankincense through the word *vehabasar* is one of those arresting moments in the Gemara. The emphasis on the word "meat" is used precisely to include what is *not* meat. If one causes these inedible sanctified items to become *tamei* and then eats them — however bizarre that sounds — the prohibition applies. And if that is true for things that are inherently inedible, then *a fortiori* it applies to meat that will become edible once *zerikah* is performed, but happens not to be edible yet. So says Abaye: when the meat is *tamei*, the dispute vanishes and everyone agrees lashes apply even before *zerikah*.

A related question arises about whether wood susceptible to *tum'ah* is a *de'oraita* or *derabbanan* category — *Tosafot* engages with this — but the Gemara proceeds without resolving that point.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְאַת בָּשָׂר – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּלָא קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ ״וְטוּמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו וְנִכְרְתָה״, לָא קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** This **dispute** applies **with regard to** a case of **impurity of the body, but with regard to impurity of the meat all agree** that **he is not flogged. What is the reason? Since** one **does not apply to** meat before the sprinkling of the blood the verse: "But the soul that eats of the flesh of the sacrifice of peace offerings of the Lord, **having his impurity upon him,** that soul **shall be cut off** from his people" (Leviticus 7:20), which is referring to meat after the sprinkling of the blood, so too, one **does not apply to it** the prohibition: **"And the flesh that touches any impure item shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 7:19).

Rava takes the opposite position from Abaye. He agrees that the dispute of Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan concerns *tum'at haguf*, but he holds that when the meat is *tamei*, everyone agrees the person does *not* receive lashes — not merely before *zerikah*, but in any case of *tum'at habasar*.

His reasoning is rooted in the structure of the verses. The prohibition against a *tamei* person eating *kodesh*, which yields *kareis*, appears in the same context as the verse about *tum'at habasar*. Even Resh Lakish agrees that the *kareis* penalty for eating *kodesh* applies only when the meat is in a state fit to be eaten — that is, when the meat is *tahor* and the blood has been placed on the altar. If the sanctity of the meat has been compromised by *tumah*, the *kareis* no longer applies. And since the verse prohibiting a *tamei* person from eating *kodesh* is embedded in and contextually linked to the *kareis* framework, the *lav* likewise extends only to situations where the *kareis* would apply — i.e., when the meat is *tahor* and post-*zerikah*. If the meat itself is *tamei*, the negative prohibition simply does not engage, and therefore even Resh Lakish would agree there are no lashes.

### Matirim Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר מָר: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר״ – לְרַבּוֹת עֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁקָּדְשׁוּ בִּכְלִי, דְּנַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁקָּרְבוּ כָּל מַתִּירָיו. דִּתְנַן: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִים – מִשֶּׁקָּרְבוּ מַתִּירָיו. כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מַתִּירִין – מִשֶּׁקָּדַשׁ בִּכְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the statement of Rava: **But doesn't the Master say** that the term **"and the flesh"** serves **to include wood and frankincense** that became impure as items that are forbidden to be eaten, despite the fact that they are not fit for consumption? Certainly, then, sacrificial flesh before the sprinkling of the blood should also be included in the category of items forbidden to be eaten.
>
> The Gemara answers: **What are we dealing with here,** that one would be liable for eating wood and frankincense that are impure? It is a case **where** the wood and frankincense **were sanctified in a vessel,** and the reason for the liability is **that** they are then **considered like** an item for **which all of its permitting factors were sacrificed,** such as flesh after the sprinkling of the blood, and only then is one liable for eating it while impure.
>
> This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Me'ila* 10a): With regard to **anything that has permitting factors,** i.e., rites that must be performed or items that must be sacrificed before the meat of the offering may be eaten, such as the meat of an offering that is permitted to be eaten by the sprinkling of the blood; one is liable for eating it while impure **from** the time **that its permitting factors were sacrificed.** With regard to **anything that does not have permitting factors,** such as the handful removed from a meal offering and the frankincense, which themselves render the rest of the meal offering permitted for consumption, one is liable for eating it while impure **from** the time **it is sanctified in a vessel.**

Having explained why Rava excludes *tum'at habasar* from lashes, the Gemara presses Rava with Abaye's argument: what about the wood and the frankincense? If those inedible items are included through the word *vehabasar*, why would *tamei* meat before *zerikah* be any less subject to the prohibition?

Rava's answer introduces the *matirim* principle. The inclusion of frankincense and wood through *vehabasar*, he explains, applies only *shekidash bicheili* — once the item has been sanctified by being placed in a *kli sharet*, a sacred vessel. The act of placing frankincense into a sacred vessel constitutes what one might call "stage two" in the sacrificial process. Physically, frankincense is no more edible after being placed in a vessel than before. But functionally, it has reached the stage at which it is ready to be offered on the altar — it has undergone its *matir*, the act that makes it fit for its purpose. For items that have a separate *matir* — like sacrificial meat, where the blood is the *matir* — that threshold is crossed only when the blood is placed on the altar. For items that have no separate *matir* — like frankincense or wood, where the object itself is what goes on the altar — the threshold is crossed when it is placed in a *kli sharet*.

The principle is then confirmed by the *mishnah*: *kol sheyesh lo matirin*, anything that has things that make it permissible — the obligation attaches once the *matirim* have been brought, *mishekerivu matirav*. *Kol she'ein lo matirin*, anything without a separate *matir* — the obligation attaches once it has been sanctified in a vessel, *mishekidsho bicheili*.

So Rava's conclusion is precise: you are not exempt from *tum'at basar* before *zerikah* because the meat is not edible. The meat of a *minchah*'s *levonah*, after all, is not edible by humans either. You are exempt because the sacrifice has not yet reached stage two. And for exactly the same reason, frankincense only enters the prohibition framework once placed in a *kli sharet* — not because it becomes edible, but because it has then reached its own stage two. *Tosafot* raises the independent question of how wood is ever sanctified in a *kli sharet*, since wood that is going to be burned on the altar does not obviously pass through a *kli* at all — an issue the Gemara leaves open.

## Behema Temeia on Altar

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמַּעֲלֶה אֵבְרֵי בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לוֹקֶה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** In the case of **one who offers up the limbs of a non-kosher animal upon the altar, Reish Lakish says:** He is **flogged** for doing so, while **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged.**

The Gemara now introduces a new *itmar* — a reported dispute — also between Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan, but on a different topic. The case: *hama'aleh eiverei beheimah temei'ah agabei mizbe'ach* — someone who takes the limbs of a non-kosher animal, say a pig or a horse, and places them on the altar. Note carefully: this is not about meat that has become *tamei*. This is about a *beheimah temei'ah* — a non-kosher species — whose limbs are brought up to the *mizbe'ach*. What has such a person violated?

### Resh Lakish's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר לוֹקֶה – טְהוֹרָה אִין, טְמֵאָה לָא, וְלָאו הַבָּא מִכְּלַל עֲשֵׂה לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the logic for each opinion: **Reish Lakish says** that he is **flogged,** because there is a positive mitzva to sacrifice an offering from the herd and the flock (see Leviticus 1:2), which are kosher animals. Therefore, it can be inferred that **a kosher** animal, **yes,** one may sacrifice, but **a non-kosher** animal one may **not** sacrifice, **and** one who transgresses **a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva** is **flogged for it.**

Resh Lakish says he receives lashes. The Torah states *min habakar umin hatzon takrivu es korbanchem* — from the cattle and the sheep you shall bring your sacrifices. Implicit in this positive command is: not from a horse, not from a pig. *Tehorah in, temei'ah lo* — a kosher animal yes, a non-kosher animal no. Resh Lakish further holds that *lav haba miklal asei lokin alav* — a negative prohibition implicit in a positive command still subjects the transgressor to lashes. This is a striking claim, one we have not encountered before in this context.

### R. Yochanan's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו – לָאו הַבָּא מִכְּלַל עֲשֵׂה אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** One is **not flogged for it,** as one who transgresses **a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva** is **not flogged for it.**

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He accepts the logical inference — *tehorah in, temei'ah lo* — but holds that *lav haba miklal asei ein lokin alav*. Since the prohibition is never stated explicitly as a "do not," but only exists as the implicit flip side of a positive command, it does not generate lashes.

### Kosher Animals Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתֵיב רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: ״אֹתָהּ תֹּאכֵלוּ״ – וְלֹא בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה; וְלָאו הַבָּא מִכְּלַל עֲשֵׂה, עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises an objection** to the opinion of Reish Lakish from a *baraita* cited in *Torat Kohanim*: "Whatsoever parts the hoof, and is wholly cloven-footed, and chews the cud, among the beasts, **that may you eat"** (Leviticus 11:3). One can infer: **But** you may **not** eat **a non-kosher animal; and a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva** has the status of **a positive mitzva.**

Rabbi Yirmiyah challenges the version of the dispute that ascribes *lav haba miklal asei lokin alav* to Resh Lakish by bringing a *baraita* about kosher animals. The Torah states *kol mafereses parsa ma'alas geirah babeheimah osah socheilu* — anything with split hooves and that chews its cud you may eat, *osah tocheilu*, "this you shall eat." Implicit in that formulation is *velo beheimah temei'ah* — not the non-kosher animals. The *baraita* identifies this as a positive command whose flip side functions as a *lav*, and then notes that one would not receive lashes on that basis alone. Lashes come only because the Torah goes on to state explicitly *velo tochlu mehem* — an explicit negative prohibition. This *baraita* demonstrates, according to Rabbi Yirmiyah, that no one actually holds that an implicit negative prohibition generates lashes. How then can the dispute be presented as it was?

### Correction: Chaya Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב לְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא, אַסְבְּרַהּ לָךְ: בְּאֵבְרֵי בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי. כִּי פְלִיגִי – בְּחַיָּה; וְהָכִי אִיתְּמַר: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר בִּוְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov said to Rabbi Yirmeya bar Taḥlifa: I will explain** it **to you: With regard to** one who sacrifices the **limbs of a non-kosher animal** upon the altar, **everyone agrees** that he is not flogged, as he violates only a positive mitzva. **When they disagree,** it is **with regard to** one who sacrifices a kosher **undomesticated animal** on the altar, **and it was stated like this: Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He transgresses a positive mitzva. Reish Lakish says: He does not transgress anything.**

Rav Yaakov bar Yirmiyah bar Tachlifa corrects the record. The dispute was misattributed. Regarding a *beheimah temei'ah* — a non-kosher animal — there is actually no dispute at all. Everyone agrees you do not receive lashes, because the prohibition is only derived implicitly from a positive command. The real dispute, properly stated, concerns the *chayah* — a wild kosher animal such as a deer. The question there is not about lashes but about whether bringing a *chayah* on the altar violates even a positive command. That is where Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan actually disagree.

## Behema vs. Chaya

### R. Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה – בְּהֵמָה אִין, חַיָּה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their reasoning: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He transgresses a positive mitzva,** since the Torah commanded that **a domesticated animal, yes,** should be sacrificed, from which it can be inferred that **an undomesticated animal** may **not** be sacrificed, and a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva has the status of a positive mitzva.

With the case restated, the Gemara presents the corrected positions. Rabbi Yochanan holds *over be'asei* — one who brings a *chayah* on the altar transgresses the positive command of *bakar vaTzon*. The Torah specifies sheep and cattle; therefore, by implication, a deer falls outside the permitted category, and offering one violates that *mitzvah*.

### Resh Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו בִּוְלֹא כְּלוּם – הָהוּא לְמִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: He does not transgress anything,** since **that** verse which instructs one to sacrifice offerings from the herd and the flock is referring **to** the optimal manner of fulfilling the **mitzva,** but if he sacrificed an undomesticated animal, he has not transgressed the mitzva.

Resh Lakish holds *eino over belo klum* — one who brings a *chayah* does not transgress at all. The verse *min habakar umin hatzon takrivu* is, in his reading, expressing the ideal *mitzvah* — the normative, meritorious manner of bringing a sacrifice — but not a limitation that excludes all else. If the *spirit* should move someone to bring a deer, there is, in Resh Lakish's view, nothing technically wrong with doing so. It may not accrue *mitzvah* credit, but neither does it constitute a violation. The verse is read as prescriptive of the *mitzvah*, not prohibitive of alternatives.

This is, frankly, a remarkable position. It seems to suggest that there is a category of *reshut* — discretionary space — in the world of *korbanos*, in which one may bring offerings not mandated by the Torah as long as they are kosher animals.

### Nadav and Avihu

This is precisely where a powerful objection arises. The entire story of Nadav and Avihu — who brought *aish zarah* "which God had not commanded them" — is the Torah's most emphatic statement that in the *beis hamikdash*, discretionary initiative carries no shelter. The verse *asher lo tzivah osam* is itself the condemnation; they did something God had not specified, and they were punished with death. The very concept that there could be a category of *reshut* when approaching the altar runs directly against the message of that narrative. The notion that one could say "I feel like bringing a deer today, there is no prohibition" seems incompatible with what the Torah communicates through Nadav and Avihu.

### Baraita Clarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתֵיב רָבָא: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״קׇרְבָּן לַה׳ בְּהֵמָה״ – הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: חַיָּה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה, כְּעִנְיָן שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֹאת הַבְּהֵמָה אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכֵלוּ שׁוֹר שֵׂה כְשָׂבִים וְשֵׂה עִזִּים אַיָּל וּצְבִי וְגוֹ׳״. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בָּקָר וָצֹאן״ – בָּקָר וָצֹאן אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ, וְלֹא חַיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection** from a *baraita*: **Had** the verse (Leviticus 1:2) **stated** only: When any man of you brings **an offering to the Lord, animals [*behema*]**, **I would say** that even **an undomesticated animal** is included **in the category of an animal [*behema*], like that** which **is stated: "These are the animals [*behema*] that you may eat: The ox, the sheep, and the goat, the deer, and the gazelle,** and the fallow deer, and the wild goat, and the oryx, and the aurochs, and the mountain sheep" (Deuteronomy 14:4–5), and, for example, the deer and gazelle are undomesticated animals. Therefore, **the verse states:** "From animals, from the **cattle and** from the **flock"** (Leviticus 1:2), which indicates that God says: **I have told you** to bring offerings from the **cattle and** the **flock, but not an undomesticated animal.**

The *baraita* brought by Rava settles the question. The verse says *min habeheimah min habakar umin hatzon takrivu es korbanchem*. If the Torah had simply said *korban l'Hashem beheimah*, one might have thought *beheimah* includes *chayah*, since elsewhere — in the context of the laws of permitted animals — *beheimah* is used to include deer and similar species. But the Torah specifies: *min habakar umin hatzon*, sheep and cattle specifically. This list is exhaustive.

### Rava's Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יָבִיא, וְאִם הֵבִיא כָּשֵׁר? הָא לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה – לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ רַבּוֹ ״הָבֵא לִי חִטִּים״, וְהֵבִיא לוֹ חִטִּים וּשְׂעוֹרִים, שֶׁאֵינוֹ כְּמַעֲבִיר עַל דְּבָרָיו אֶלָּא מוֹסִיף עַל דְּבָרָיו, וְכָשֵׁר. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בָּקָר וָצֹאן״ – בָּקָר וָצֹאן אָמַרְתִּי לְךָ, וְלֹא חַיָּה. הָא לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה? לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ רַבּוֹ ״אַל תָּבִיא לִי אֶלָּא חִיטִּין״, וְהֵבִיא לוֹ חִיטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִים, שֶׁאֵינוֹ כְּמוֹסִיף עַל דְּבָרָיו אֶלָּא כְּמַעֲבִיר עַל דְּבָרָיו, וּפָסוּל. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that one should **not bring** an undomesticated animal *ab initio*, **but if** one **did bring** it, it is **valid,** and **to what is this comparable? To a student whose teacher says to him: Bring me wheat, and** the student **brought him wheat and barley.** In this case, **it is not as** though the student is **disobeying the statement of** the teacher; **rather,** he is merely **adding to his statement, and** that should be **valid.** Therefore, **the verse states** again: "From the **cattle"** (Leviticus 1:3), **and:** "From the **flock"** (Leviticus 1:10), to reiterate that God says: **I have told you** to bring offerings from the **cattle and** the **flock, but not an undomesticated animal. To what is this comparable? To a student whose teacher told him: Bring me only wheat, and** the student **brought him wheat and barley. It is not as** though the student is **adding to the statement of** the teacher; **rather, it is as** though **he is disobeying his statement,** since his teacher instructed him to bring only wheat. Consequently, one who sacrifices an undomesticated animal does not merely add to a mitzva of the Torah, but also violates a prohibition, and** the offering is therefore **disqualified.** The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

The *baraita* then formulates two analogies. One might think: you're not supposed to bring a deer, but if you did, perhaps it is at least valid after the fact — like a student told to buy wheat who also brings barley; he exceeded the instruction but did not defy it. The *baraita* rejects this with a second analogy: the Torah repeated *bakar vaTzon* not once but twice in the opening chapters of Vayikra, and the repetition signals not "here is the preferred item" but rather "this is the exclusive list." The second analogy is that of a student told "bring nothing but wheat" — bringing barley in that case is not adding to the instruction, it is defying it. The *baraita* concludes: *pesul* — a *chayah* is invalid. This is a decisive refutation of Resh Lakish's position. *Tiuvta d'Reish Lakish, tiuvta.*

The Gemara's conclusion here carries its own weight. Even as we note the *tiuvta*, the underlying halachic point — that placing a pig on the altar technically violates only a positive command and not a negative one — remains striking. A fine piece of trivia: what is the prohibition involved in bringing a pig as a sacrifice? The answer the Gemara yields is that it violates the *asei* of *min habakar umin hatzon*, nothing more.

## Invalid Kohen & Shirayim

### Question Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ כּוּ׳. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פָּסוּל – מַהוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה שִׁירַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** with regard to **all of them,** in a case **where they collected** the blood with the intent to offer it beyond its designated time or outside its designated area, if there is blood of the soul that remains in the animal, the priest fit for Temple service should again collect the blood and sprinkle it on the altar. **Reish Lakish asked Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If an individual **unfit** for Temple service presented the blood, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the blood that remains in the animal? Does the fact **that he** presented some blood **render** the rest of it **a remainder** and no longer fit to be presented, or perhaps the presenting performed by an unfit individual is not considered valid, and therefore a priest fit for Temple service may present the blood again?

The Gemara now returns to the framework of the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* had established that certain invalid persons — a *tamei kohen*, an *onen*, a non-*kohen*, and others — cannot perform the acts of *kabbalah* (receiving the blood), *holachah* (carrying the blood to the altar), or *zerikah* (placing the blood on the altar). Their thoughts at *shechitah* may generate *piggul*, but their invalid performance of subsequent acts does not. The Gemara now asks a specific follow-on question.

Resh Lakish posed the following question to Rabbi Yochanan: *pasul mahu she-ya'aseh shirayim?* — if an invalid person caught the blood and then placed it on the altar, does that make the remaining blood *shirayim*? In other words, after blood is placed on the altar, whatever blood remains cannot be used to repeat or supplement the *zerikah*; it becomes *shirayim*, leftover blood poured at the base of the altar. But does this "leftover" status depend on the *zerikah* having been performed validly? If an invalid *kohen* performed the *zerikah*, was that truly a *zerikah* — such that the remaining blood is now *shirayim* — or was the entire act null, leaving the blood still usable for a valid *kohen* to perform *zerikah* again?

### R. Yochanan's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין עוֹשֶׂה שִׁירַיִם אֶלָּא חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, הוֹאִיל וּמְרַצֶּה לְפִיגּוּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** An invalid presenting **does not render** the rest of the blood **a remainder unless** it is a case where a priest fit for Temple service presented the blood with the intent to offer it **beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area, since** it is only in that case that the presenting of the blood **effects acceptance,** i.e., it is considered a valid presenting, **with regard to** rendering the offering ***piggul*,** i.e., an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to consume it after its designated time.

Rabbi Yochanan's answer is precise: *ein oseh shirayim ela chutz lizmano v'chutz limkomo, ho'il u'meratzeh l'piggulo.* The only invalidity that converts remaining blood into *shirayim* is a *piggul*-category thought — *chutz lizmano* (improper time-intention) or *chutz limkomo* (improper location-intention). Any other invalidity — including a *tamei* person or non-*kohen* performing the *zerikah* — results in nothing happening, and the remaining blood can still be used for a valid *zerikah*.

### Piggul Logic

The logic behind this ruling is one of the more elegant features of the laws of *piggul*. *Piggul* only takes effect when everything else in the sacrificial process is performed correctly. This creates a structural paradox: in order to assess whether *piggul* has occurred, we must evaluate the remainder of the service as if the *piggul* thought had not invalidated it — we must bracket the invalidity and ask whether, in a hypothetical valid world, the act would be complete. Since we are forced by the logic of *piggul* to view the *zerikah* through the lens of theoretical validity, we also conclude that blood has genuinely been applied to the altar. The remaining blood is therefore genuinely *shirayim* — it cannot be used again. In contrast, any other invalidity is simply null. Nothing happened. The remaining blood retains its status as blood available for *zerikah*, not as leftover *shirayim*.

## Invalid Person vs. Blood

### Rav Zevid's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד מַתְנֵי הָכִי – בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כּוֹס פָּסוּל, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה שִׁירַיִם? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פָּסוּל גּוּפֵיהּ מַאי סְבִירָא לָךְ? אִי פָּסוּל מְשַׁוֵּי שִׁירַיִם – כּוֹס פָּסוּל נָמֵי מְשַׁוֵּי שִׁירַיִם. אִי פָּסוּל לָא מְשַׁוֵּי שִׁירַיִם – כּוֹס פָּסוּל נָמֵי לָא מְשַׁוֵּי שִׁירַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid teaches** the matter in **this** way: **Reish Lakish asked Rabbi Yoḥanan:** Concerning **a cup** of **disqualified** blood, e.g., one which had been taken out of the Temple courtyard and was then nevertheless presented on the altar, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to it **rendering** the remaining blood **a remainder** and unfit to be presented? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** In the case of **an unfit** individual who **himself** presented the blood, **what do you hold** to be the *halakha*? **If an unfit** individual who presented the blood **renders** the remaining blood **a remainder,** then **a cup** of **disqualified** blood should **also render** the remaining blood **a remainder. If an unfit** individual does **not render** the remaining blood **a remainder,** then **a cup** of **disqualified** blood should **also not render** the remaining blood **a remainder.**

Rav Zevid presents an alternative version of this exchange. Rather than asking whether an invalid *person* performing *zerikah* makes the remainder *shirayim*, Resh Lakish's question was actually about a *kos pasul* — invalid blood. Imagine that the blood itself has become *pasul* — say, a *tamei* person touched the blood in the vessel, or the blood was brought outside the *beis hamikdash* — and then a valid *kohen* takes that invalid blood and places it on the altar. Does the remaining valid blood in a separate vessel become *shirayim*, or can a valid *kohen* now use it?

### Equivalence Principle

Rabbi Yochanan's response in this version is telling. He pushes back on the framing of the question itself: *pasul gufei mai svira lach?* — what is your assumption about a *pasul* person? If you think a *pasul* person's *zerikah* makes the remainder *shirayim*, then the same would be true when the blood is *pasul*. If you think a *pasul* person's act is null and the remainder can still be used, the same would apply when the blood is *pasul*. There is no asymmetry between the two cases; Rabbi Yochanan sees them as governed by identical principles. He refuses to let the question about invalid blood stand as an independent inquiry separate from the question about an invalid person, insisting that whatever answer one gives to one, one must give to the other. Interestingly, Resh Lakish's reason for distinguishing the two cases — whatever it might have been — is left unexplained by the Gemara, and that remains an open question for the reader.

## Two Vessels Question

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּיפְתִּי מַתְנֵי הָכִי – בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי (מֵרָבָא) [מֵרַבָּה]: כּוֹס, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ דָּחוּי אוֹ שִׁירַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya of Difti teaches** the discussion **this** way: **Abaye asked Rabba:** If the blood was collected in more than one cup and the presenting of the blood on the corners of the altar was performed with one **cup, what is** the *halakha*? Does the cup **render** the blood of **the other** cup **rejected,** and it is therefore poured into the drain running through the Temple courtyard, **or** does it render it **a remainder,** which is poured on the base of the altar?

The Gemara now records a third version of a related question, attributed to Rabbi Yirmiyah of Difti. Here the question is not one that Resh Lakish posed to Rabbi Yochanan, but one that Abaye posed to Rabbah. The case: an animal is slaughtered and its blood is caught in *two separate vessels*. Nothing is invalid — neither the person nor the blood. A valid *kohen* then takes the blood from vessel one and performs *zerikah* on the wall of the altar. What is the status of the blood remaining in vessel two?

### The Question

Two possibilities present themselves. One is that vessel two's blood is *dichuy* — pushed away and rejected. The *zerikah* has been fulfilled; vessel two's blood was a candidate for the altar and has now been displaced. As rejected blood, it is invalid, and it gets poured into the *amah*, the channel that carries invalid matter out of the *beis hamikdash*. The alternative is that vessel two's blood is *shirayim* — not rejected, but leftover in the same sense that the remnant in vessel one is leftover. Just as the small amount remaining in vessel one after *zerikah* is poured at the base of the altar, so too vessel two's blood is treated as *shirayim* and poured at the base. The question is not trivial; these two dispositions represent meaningfully different halachic statuses.

### Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא, לְמַעְלָה הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אֶת דָּמוֹ יִשְׁפֹּךְ״, לְמַטָּה הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאֶת כׇּל דָּמָהּ יִשְׁפֹּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him:** This is **a dispute between Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, and the Rabbis. As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the remainder of the blood of the external sin offering: In the verse **above,** discussing the sin offering of a *Nasi*, **it states:** "And **he shall pour its blood** out at the base of the altar of the burnt offering" (Leviticus 4:25). In the verse **below,** discussing the sin offering of an individual, **it states: "And he shall pour all its blood** out at the base of the altar" (Leviticus 4:30).

Rabbah informs Abaye that this is in fact a dispute of *Tannaim* — specifically, a debate between the Sages and Rabbi Eliezer son of Rabbi Shimon. A *baraita* presents the framework. The Torah uses two formulations in proximity: *et damo yishpach* — "its blood" — and *v'et kol damo yishpach* — "all of its blood." The former implies *some* blood; the latter emphasizes *all* the blood. How do these two phrasings interact?

### Sages' Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לְחַטָּאת שֶׁקִּיבֵּל דָּמָהּ בְּאַרְבָּעָה כּוֹסוֹת, וְנָתַן מַתָּנָה אַחַת מִזֶּה וּמַתָּנָה אַחַת מִזֶּה – שֶׁכּוּלָּן נִשְׁפָּכִין לַיְסוֹד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶת כׇּל דָּמָהּ יִשְׁפֹּךְ״. יָכוֹל נָתַן אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת מִכּוֹס אֶחָד, יְהוּ כּוּלָּן נִשְׁפָּכִין לַיְסוֹד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶת דָּמוֹ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? הוּא נִשְׁפַּךְ לַיְסוֹד, וְהֵן נִשְׁפָּכִין לָאַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The discrepancy between the first verse and the second verse, which contains the additional term "all," is explained as follows: **From where** is it derived **with regard to a sin offering which** the priest **collected its blood in four cups and placed one placement** of blood onto the altar **from this** cup, **and one placement** of blood **from that** cup, and likewise for all four corners of the altar, **that** the remainder of blood from **all** the cups **are poured** out **onto the base** of the altar? **The verse states: "And he shall pour all its blood."**
>
> One **might** have thought that if **he placed four placements** of blood of the sin offering **from one cup,** the blood in **all** the rest of the cups **should be poured onto the base** of the altar. Therefore, **the verse states: "And** he shall pour **its blood,"** and not all of its blood. **How so?** Only **that** blood in the cup from which blood was properly presented on the altar **is poured onto the base** of the altar, **and** the rest of the cups of blood are rejected and **are poured into the** Temple courtyard **drain.**

The Sages read the two verses as governing two distinct cases. Take a *chatas* whose blood was caught in four vessels. If the *kohen* took blood from each of the four vessels and performed one of the four applications from each — *nasan matanah achas mizeh v'nasan matanah achas mizeh* — then all four vessels had blood that participated in the *zerikah*, and the remnant in all four is considered *shirayim*: *v'et kol damo yishpoch*, all the blood is poured at the base. But if the *kohen* performed all four applications from a single vessel — ignoring the other three — then only the remnant of that one vessel is *shirayim* and goes to the base. The other three vessels, whose blood never participated in any *zerikah*, are *dichuy*: *hein nishpachin l'amah*, they are poured out into the channel. Only the blood in the vessel that was actually used for *zerikah* achieves the status of *shirayim*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְחַטָּאת שֶׁקִּיבֵּל דָּמָה בְּאַרְבָּעָה כּוֹסוֹת וְנָתַן אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת מִכּוֹס אֶחָד, שֶׁכּוּלָּן נִשְׁפָּכִין לַיְסוֹד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאֶת כׇּל דָּמָהּ יִשְׁפֹּךְ״. וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת דָּמוֹ יִשְׁפֹּךְ״! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָהוּא לְמַעוֹטֵי שִׁירַיִם שֶׁבְּצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: From where** is it derived **with regard to a sin offering which** the priest **collected its blood in four cups and** then **placed four placements from one cup, that** the blood from **all** the cups **are poured** out **onto the base** of the altar? **The verse states: "And he shall pour all its blood."** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "And he shall pour its blood,"** which indicates that not all of the blood is poured? **Rav Ashi said: That** verse serves **to exclude** the **remainder that is in the throat of** the **animal** that was never collected in a vessel to be presented, which is not poured onto the base of the altar but into the Temple courtyard drain.

Rabbi Eliezer son of Rabbi Shimon disagrees. Even if all four applications were performed from a single vessel, all four vessels' blood is considered *shirayim* and is poured at the base. He derives this from the verse *v'et kol damo yishpoch* — "all of its blood" — reading it expansively to include even blood that was never drawn upon in the *zerikah* process. Once *zerikah* has been performed, all blood caught in advance for the sacrifice is retrospectively treated as leftover, not as rejected.

### Resolution

The Gemara then asks: how does Rabbi Eliezer son of Rabbi Shimon handle the verse that says *et damo* — "the blood," without *kol* — implying only *some* blood? Rav Yoshiya answers: that verse comes to exclude *shirayim shebitzavar habeheimah* — blood that was never captured in a vessel at all. Blood that remains in the neck of the animal after *zerikah* has already been performed, still spurting out and never caught, is not considered *shirayim* and is not poured at the base. The verse *et damo* — "its blood," a limited quantity — excludes that blood from the *shirayim* category. But any blood that was caught in a vessel before *zerikah* and then *zerikah* was performed — even from a different vessel — is *shirayim* according to Rabbi Eliezer, and goes to the base. The debate between the Sages and Rabbi Eliezer thus maps directly onto the question Abaye raised: whether unused vessels of blood are *dichuy* (the Sages' position) or *shirayim* (Rabbi Eliezer's position).
