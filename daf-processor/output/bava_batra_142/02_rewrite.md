# Bava Batra 142 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumtum & Gender

### Tumtum Background

Today's *daf* is 142. We pick up at the bottom of 141b, in the context of the *Mishna* presenting scenarios where a man declares: "If my wife gives birth to a male child, let him receive a hundred dollars; if to a female child, two hundred," and so on — with the *Gemara* then drawing out the implications that spin off from this. One issue that arose was the case of a *tumtum*, a child of indeterminate sex. The *Gemara* had a rich discussion about whether a *tumtum* is a *biryah bifnei atzmo* — an entirely independent category — and therefore falls into neither the male nor the female designation and receives nothing, a position that, as noted, touches on broader questions about the gender binary.

The alternative view is that a *tumtum* represents a *safek*, a genuine case of doubt. Even so, one position holds that this *safek* status does not entitle the *tumtum* to receive even the lower common denominator of the two amounts.

### Abaye's Tumtum

This is where Abaye's position enters: *v'yeiti v'lo yahavinan lei* — the *tumtum* comes forward but receives neither the male nor the female portion. One way to understand this is as treating the *tumtum* as a categorically distinct creature (*biryah*). But another way to understand it is rooted in the *da'at* of the donor — the subjective intent behind the declaration. When a person says "if it's a boy" or "if it's a girl," he likely means someone who is unambiguously identifiable as one or the other, not someone who may objectively belong to one category but cannot be clearly identified as such. The ambiguity in the recipient's identity defeats the mechanism of the gift.

### Daughter Preference Joke

The *Gemara* also raised the question of why the father in our *Mishna* gives more money to his daughter than to his son, running against the general cultural assumption that sons are preferred. One answer offered is that everyone wants a daughter to be the eldest child, since she helps raise the younger siblings. The *Gemara* then cites a tradition that a certain Sage preferred daughters because his sons-in-law turned out to be greater *talmidei chachamim* than his sons.

In the midst of this discussion, the *Gemara* raises the verse that God blessed Avraham *bakol* — "with everything" — and a tradition that Avraham had a daughter whose name was *Bakol*. Does this not prove that daughters are superior? Tosafot seizes on this with a pointed question: if Avraham had a daughter named *Bakol*, why did he go to such lengths to find a wife for Yitzchak from far away? Since a *ben Noach* is permitted to marry his half-sister on his father's side, why did Yitzchak not simply marry his half-sister *Bakol*? Tosafot provides its own answers — though one suggestion, offered more lightly, is that Yitzchak wanted to marry a real woman, not a *Midrash*.

## Transfer to Ubar

### Rav Huna's Position

The *Gemara* now turns to what is the central question animating the rest of today's discussion: how does the mechanism of the *Mishna* actually function? When the father declares that his unborn son or daughter shall receive this gift, he is attempting to transfer ownership to a fetus — to an *ubar*. But we have a principle: *hamezakeh l'ubar lo kanah* — one who attempts to transfer property to a fetus, even through a third party acting on the fetus's behalf (invoking the principle of *zachin l'adam shelo b'fanav*), has effected no transfer, because the fetus is not yet a legal person who can take possession. This is the position of Rav Huna.

The *Gemara* notes that Rav Huna therefore cannot identify who authored the *Mishna*, since he is unaware of any position that would permit transferring ownership to a fetus. The *Gemara* attempts to rescue the *Mishna* by suggesting it reflects Rav Meir's view that *adam makneh davar shelo ba l'olam* — a person can transfer an object not yet in existence. But the *Gemara* pushes back: in Rav Meir's case, the recipient at least exists; here, even the recipient is not yet a legal person. Another possible rescue — that we have evidence a fetus can inherit — is also deflected: natural inheritance (*yerusha*) that occurs automatically is different from actively conferring possession on a fetus.

### Reinterpretation Attempt

The *Gemara* proposes a creative reinterpretation: perhaps the *Mishna* is not describing a case where the father is attempting to transfer property to a still-unborn *ubar* at all. Perhaps the father is in a distant location, his wife has already given birth, and he simply does not know the sex of the child. His declaration — "if it's a boy, give him a hundred; if it's a girl, give her two hundred" — would then be a gift to a child already born, just one whose sex is as yet unknown to him. This would sidestep the *ubar* problem entirely, since the transfer would be to a child already in existence.

The *Gemara*, however, rejects this reading from the language of the *Mishna* itself. The closing clause of the *Mishna* uses the future tense — *kol mah sheteiled ishti*, "whomever my wife will give birth to" — rather than the past tense *kol mah sheyalda*, "whomever my wife gave birth to." The future tense makes clear that the child is not yet born at the time of the declaration, and so the *ubar* problem cannot be avoided.

### Rav Huna Rejects

The *Gemara* then raises another possible workaround: *v'leima lei d'amar liksheteiled* — what if the declaration is structured so that the *kinyan* takes effect not while the child is still a fetus, but at the moment of birth? The act of transfer would be performed now, but would only take legal effect once the child enters the world.

This is a conceptually interesting mechanism. If a third party is acting to acquire property on behalf of the *ubar* — representing him — the difficulty is that a fetus is not a legal entity that can be represented. But there is arguably a distinction between representing someone who does not yet exist as a legal person, on the one hand, and performing an act now that is designed to take effect for a person once they do exist, on the other. Perhaps a person can initiate a *kinyan* now, with the effect suspended until the recipient becomes a cognizable legal entity.

Rav Huna rejects this as well. His position is *d'amar Rav Huna af liksheteiled lo kanah* — even if the intent is that the *kinyan* should take effect only upon birth, it still does not work. Since the fetus is *ubar yerech imo* — legally regarded as a limb of the mother, not an independent entity — no act performed now can be grounded in the fetus's eventual legal personality. The act has no one to latch on to. Rav Huna therefore maintains that the *Mishna* cannot be reconciled with any known position, and so the question of its authorship remains open for him.

### Rav Nachman's Compromise

Against Rav Huna's sweeping rejection, Rav Nachman offers a more calibrated position: *hamezakeh l'ubar lo kanah, liksheteiled kanah* — a fetus cannot take possession while still a fetus, but one may perform an act that takes effect for the fetus once the fetus is born. The *Mishna* can therefore be explained: the transfer takes legal effect at the moment of birth, not during gestation. Rav Nachman thus accepts the first part of Rav Huna's position — *hamazakeh l'ubar lo kanah* — while rejecting the second. *Liksheteiled*, he holds, works.

### Rav Sheshet Permits

Rav Sheshet goes further still: *echad zeh ve'echad zeh kanah* — both work. A fetus can take possession even while still a fetus, and certainly when born. This is the most expansive position, and it rejects Rav Huna's foundational premise entirely.

It is worth pausing to appreciate why Rav Sheshet's position has some intuitive force. The *Gemara* earlier analogized the *ubar* problem to Rav Meir's principle that one can transfer something not yet in existence. But one might actually argue that the fetus is in a better position than Rav Meir's case, not a worse one. When transferring fruit that has not yet grown, there is no object whatsoever to point to. When a woman is already pregnant, the fetus is at least partially present in the world. So Rav Sheshet's view — that *mezakeh l'ubar kanah* — is quite coherent, at least within the framework of Rav Meir, and perhaps even more defensible.

## Ger Case Study

### Ger Dies & Looting

Rav Sheshet now provides his source. *Detanya*: a *ger* — a convert from a non-Jewish background — dies. Because a *ger* who has converted carries no halachic family relationships with his biological relatives, he has no natural heirs unless he sired children after converting. If he dies without such children, his estate becomes *hefker* — ownerless — available to whoever takes possession first. And so, the Baraita relates, Israelites swooped in and looted his property — *vizbezu Yisrael nechasav* — the verb carrying the full weight of wholesale plunder.

Then they heard: *she-yesh lo ben* — he actually has a son, a biological son born after his conversion. Or — since a wife does not inherit her husband even in the case of a *ger* — perhaps his wife was pregnant: *o she-hayetah ishto me'uberet*. The relevance of the wife's pregnancy is that if she gives birth to a viable child, that child is an heir and the estate is not *hefker*. Upon hearing this, the Baraita rules *chayavim lehachzir* — they are obligated to return everything. The looting must be undone; the estate must be restored and held for the potential heir.

### Return & Miscarriage

They restore everything. And then — *ve'achar kach shamu she-met beno o she-hipilah ishto* — they subsequently hear that the son died, or that the wife miscarried. So the sequence, reconstructed, runs as follows: the *ger* dies; the Israelites loot his property; they hear there is a son or a pregnant wife; they return everything; they then hear the son died or the wife miscarried.

In that case, the Baraita rules: *chayavin haleh shehechazir karka vechazar vehechezik basheniyah kanah* — whoever returned the property and then re-took possession in this second round has acquired it. *Uvarishonah lo kanah* — but the original taking of possession was not valid.

### Why First Kinyan Fails

This Baraita is what Rav Sheshet uses as his proof. The striking feature is the case of the pregnant wife. If the *ubar* cannot take possession of anything — cannot even inherit — then from the moment the *ger* died, the estate was pure *hefker*. There was no heir. Why, then, did the first round of possession-taking not work? If the fetus was never going to be a valid heir (either because it died or because a fetus cannot inherit), then the property was already ownerless at that first moment, and whoever grabbed it first should have acquired it legitimately.

The fact that the first *chazaka* did not work, and a second *chazaka* was required after the miscarriage, implies that during the pregnancy the fetus did have some proprietary claim over the estate. This, Rav Sheshet argues, demonstrates that *mezakeh l'ubar kanah* — a fetus can take possession.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye responds: *yerushah me'eleha shani* — natural inheritance operates by its own rules and is categorically different. A fetus may be able to inherit automatically through the operation of halachic succession, without any active acquisition being performed on its behalf. That natural capacity tells us nothing about whether one can affirmatively *mezakeh* — actively transfer — property to a fetus. The Baraita therefore provides no proof for Rav Sheshet's expansive position.

### Rava's Rifyon Answer

Rava takes a different approach. He does not concede that a fetus can inherit at all. Rather, he explains the Baraita's ruling differently: *shani hasam derafui mirafya biyadei me'ikara* — the situation there is different because even the original act of taking possession was weak, *rifyon*, from the outset.

What does this mean? At the moment the Israelites first grabbed the property, they had already heard — or at a minimum, had reason to suspect — that the wife was pregnant. Even if the fetus cannot technically inherit and the estate was therefore technically *hefker*, the subjective state of the person taking possession was not one of complete certainty. He knew, or strongly suspected, that there was a possibility he would have to give it back. That awareness — that background doubt — undermined the completeness of his *da'at*, his intent, at the moment of the *chazaka*. A *kinyan* requires full and settled intent; a *kinyan* performed while the person already anticipates having to relinquish the property is weakened, *rifyon*, from the start.

This distinction between the two approaches — Abaye's and Rava's — is significant. For Abaye, the *ubar* actually owns the property during gestation through the mechanism of natural inheritance; the first *chazaka* failed because the property was not truly *hefker*. For Rava, the *ubar* owns nothing; the first *chazaka* failed because the possessor's own subjective intent was incomplete.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Nafka Mina Scenario

The *Gemara* probes the practical difference between Abaye and Rava with a *nafka mina* scenario: *deshama bo shemet velo met ve'achar kach met* — a person heard that the son (or that the fetus) had died; in fact it had not died at that point; and only subsequently did it actually die.

In this scenario, someone goes to take possession of the *ger*'s estate. At the moment he takes possession, he has heard — and sincerely believes — that the heir is dead. He is not proceeding with any background awareness that he might have to give the property back. His *da'at* is full and confident. But as a matter of objective fact, at that moment, the heir had not yet died. Only afterward did the heir die.

### Abaye: Subjective

According to Abaye, the fetus actually did own the property at the time of the first *chazaka*. The possessor's sincere but mistaken belief that the heir was dead does not change the objective reality that the property had an owner. His confident *da'at* is irrelevant — the property was not *hefker* at that moment, so his act of possession did not acquire anything. Only once the heir actually died did the property become *hefker*, and a new *chazaka* would be required to acquire it at that point.

### Rava: Objective

According to Rava, the problem with the original *chazaka* was never that the fetus owned the property — Rava holds the fetus cannot inherit at all. The problem was the possessor's subjective uncertainty. But in this *nafka mina* case, his subjective mindset was one of complete certainty: he had heard and believed the heir was dead. There was no *rifyon* — no weakening of his intent. He proceeded with total confidence. Rava would therefore say this *chazaka* worked. The property was genuinely *hefker*, he had complete *da'at*, and the acquisition was valid, even though his belief about what had happened to the heir was factually incorrect. His sincere but erroneous certainty was sufficient.

The two positions thus hinge on whether the impediment to *kinyan* is objective — the fetus actually holds the property and so it is not truly *hefker* (Abaye) — or subjective — the possessor's own internal state of doubt or certainty (Rava). In the *nafka mina* scenario where the person had mistaken but sincere confidence, Abaye says the *kinyan* fails (objectively, property was not *hefker*), while Rava says it succeeds (subjectively, his *da'at* was complete).

## Fetus Inheritance

### Mishna: One Day Old

The *Gemara* now turns to a famous *Mishna* in *Niddah* that catalogs the halachic statuses of children at various ages. The *Mishna* states: *tinok ben yom echad nochal u'manchil* — a one-day-old infant can inherit and can bequeath. *Ben yom echad in, ubaro lo* — only a one-day-old infant qualifies; a fetus does not.

This *Mishna* appears to prove once again that a fetus cannot inherit, seemingly contradicting Rav Sheshet's position. Rav Sheshet, recall, is the one who holds not only that a fetus can take possession when directly transferred property, but that a fetus can inherit through natural succession as well.

### Why It Matters

Before engaging with whether a fetus inherits, the *Gemara* pauses to ask why it matters that the *Mishna* says a one-day-old infant inherits and bequeaths. After all, if a one-day-old infant inherits from his father and then dies, his estate passes to his brothers. But had he never been born, the father's property would have gone directly to those same brothers. Either way, the brothers receive the property — so what is the practical significance of this rule?

### Rav Sheshet's Read

Rav Sheshet answers: the *Mishna* is not speaking about the infant inheriting from his father. It is speaking about *nochal b'nichsei ha'em l'hanchil l'achin min ha'av* — the infant inheriting from his mother, so as to then bequeath to his paternal brothers.

Consider the following scenario: the mother has property of her own. She dies. If the infant is alive at the time of her death, the property passes to him as her son. When he then dies, it passes to his brothers on his father's side — the paternal brothers. But if the infant had not been halachically capable of inheriting at that moment, the mother's property would pass to her own family — her father, her brothers — and would never reach the paternal brothers at all. The *Mishna*'s ruling that *nochal u'manchil* therefore has real consequences: it determines whether property crosses from the mother's family line to the father's family line through the child.

Now, the *Mishna* specifies one day old, not a fetus. Rav Sheshet explains: he would have been perfectly willing to say that a fetus can inherit, but the scenario envisioned by the *Mishna* involves the mother dying. If the mother died, a fetus that was in her womb at the time cannot inherit from her, because the fetus does not survive the mother's natural death — *d'hu mayis b'reisha*, the fetus dies first.

### Fetus Dies First

And since the fetus dies before the mother in such a case, we cannot apply the principle of having the fetus inherit and then bequeath, because *ein haben yoresh et imo b'kever l'hanchil l'achin min ha'av* — a son does not inherit from his mother while in the grave in order to bequeath to his paternal brothers. Once the fetus has died first, and then the mother dies, we do not fictionally pass the estate through the already-dead fetus to his paternal heirs. The estate goes instead to the mother's own heirs. Therefore the *Mishna* correctly limits the ruling to a *ben yom echad*, a live-born infant, not because a fetus categorically cannot inherit, but because in this specific scenario a fetus would have already died when the mother did.

The *Gemara* then challenges this: is it really true that the fetus always dies first? The *Gemara* cites an incident where, after a woman died, the fetus was observed quivering in the womb three times. Mar bar Rav Ashi dismisses this as analogous to the tail of a lizard that continues to twitch after being severed — the movements are post-mortem reflexes, not signs of life.

Tosafot, however, points out that this cannot be categorically correct. Other Talmudic sources make clear that a fetus can survive the mother's death in certain circumstances — specifically when the mother suffers a violent, sudden death, as opposed to a slow natural death. In a slow natural dying process, the fetus's vitality ebbs along with the mother's and it dies first. But in the case of sudden trauma — an accident or a violent act — the fetus can survive. Tosafot additionally notes a second scenario: if the child was already *ne'ekar*, already in the process of being born such that it had begun to detach from the mother biologically, it may survive even if the mother does not, since at that point its life is no longer fully dependent on hers.

This does leave the *Mishna* somewhat imprecise, since there are cases where a fetus could in theory inherit from a dying mother — and Tosafot acknowledges this without providing a fully resolved answer.

## Chelek Bechora

### Initial Question

Having established the relevance of the *Mishna*'s ruling in the context of inheriting from the mother, the *Gemara* now raises a further question. Even setting aside the mother-to-paternal-brothers scenario offered by Rav Sheshet, why tell us that the one-day-old infant inherits and bequeaths at all? If the infant inherits from his father, and then when he dies his estate passes to his brothers, the brothers ultimately get what they would have gotten anyway had the infant never existed. What halachic difference does it make that the property passed through the infant?

### Double Portion Math

Mar bereih deRav Yosef in the name of Rava provides the answer: *lomar shemema'et b'chelek bechorah* — the infant's existence and legal status as an heir at the moment of the father's death affects the calculation of the *bechor*'s double portion.

The *bechor* — the firstborn son — is entitled to a double share of the estate. The size of that double share is calculated by treating the *bechor* as receiving two ordinary shares. The calculation thus depends on how many heirs there are in total: the more heirs, the smaller each ordinary share, and therefore the smaller the *bechor*'s double portion as well.

If the infant is recognized as an heir at the moment of the father's death, he increases the number of heirs by one. This means the estate is divided into more units, each unit is smaller, and the *bechor*'s double portion — pegged to the size of two such units — is correspondingly reduced.

### Math Example

To illustrate: suppose there are four units of property and, at the father's death, three sons are already born. The *bechor* receives two shares; each of the other two receives one. Under this calculation, each ordinary share is one unit, and the *bechor* takes two units.

Now suppose the father also had a fourth son who was born after the father's death — or who was a fetus at the time of death. If this fourth child is counted among the heirs for purposes of calculating the *bechor*'s double portion, the estate would be divided into five ordinary shares (since the *bechor* takes two and the remaining three brothers each take one, for five total). Each ordinary share would then be four-fifths of a unit, and the *bechor*'s double portion would be four-fifths of a unit as well — smaller than the full unit he would have received with only three heirs.

The infant's existence at the moment of death thus directly diminishes the *bechor*'s portion, even though the infant's own share will eventually be redistributed to the brothers when he dies.

### Only Born Count

However — and this is Rava's ruling — only children already born at the time of the father's death factor into this calculation. *Aval ubar lo*: a fetus does not diminish the *bechor*'s double portion, even if the fetus will go on to inherit. The reason is derived from the verse *yaldu lo banim* — the Torah's language concerning the *bechor*'s calculation refers to children who have been born, not to fetuses.

### Born After Father

The same principle governs whether a *bechor* himself receives a double portion. *Mar bereih deRav Yosef mishmeih deRava*: *bechor shenolad le'achar mitat ha'av eino notel pi shnayim* — a firstborn son born after the father's death does not receive the double portion of the *bechor*. This scenario can arise, for instance, if the father had multiple wives and died leaving behind pregnant wives, and the firstborn of one wife is born after the father's death. Despite being the *bechor* by order of birth, he does not receive the double portion.

The reason: *yakir amar Rachmana* — the Torah says the father must "recognize" the firstborn, which requires the *bechor* to already be born and recognizable at the time of the father's death. As the *Rashbam* clarifies, this does not mean the father must have literally laid eyes on the child — a father who was in a distant country and died without ever meeting his son certainly does not forfeit his son's status as *bechor*. What is required is that the child be born, and thus capable of being recognized, even if only in principle.

## Final Rulings

### Rule Like Rava

The *Gemara* now delivers the decisive rulings. *Vehilcheta kechol hanei shema'ateta de'amar Mar bereih deRav Yosef mishmeih deRava* — we rule in accordance with all the positions stated by Mar bereih deRav Yosef in the name of Rava. This means: to calculate the *bechor*'s double portion, we count only children already born at the time of the father's death; and a *bechor* himself must be born before the father's death in order to receive the double portion.

### Rav Yochanan Rule

With respect to the core question of *mezakeh l'ubar*, the *Gemara* records: *Amar Rav Yitzchak amar Rav Yochanan: hamezakeh l'ubar lo kanah* — actively transferring property to a fetus does not work. This is the operative halachic ruling.

### Own Child Exception

However, Tosafot notes that the *Gemara* appears to carve out an exception for one's own child. *Vehilcheta dehamezakeh l'ubar lo kanah* — the ruling stands as a general matter; but in the case of our *Mishna*, where a father is gifting property to his own unborn son or daughter, *hava da'ateh de'adam krova etzel beno* — a person's *da'at* is uniquely close when it comes to his own child. Because the father feels such powerful subjective connection and intent toward his own unborn child, the mechanism of transfer may function even though it ordinarily would not. The subjective barrier that defeats *mezakeh l'ubar* in the general case is overcome by the intensity of paternal intent.

This is admittedly a striking result. It might seem that if the fetus is not a legal person capable of taking possession, the sincerity of the giver's intent cannot conjure that capacity into existence. Yet the *Gemara* appears to locate at least part of the problem of *mezakeh l'ubar* in the giver's subjective orientation — in how fully and genuinely the giver relates to the fetus as a recipient — rather than purely in an objective determination about the fetus's legal status. When that subjective barrier is removed by the closeness of the father-child bond, the *kinyan* can take effect.

### Shmuel's Minority

Finally, the *Gemara* records that Shmuel had at one point sought to publicize the opposite ruling. He told Rav Chanana bar Bagta'a — the epithet apparently referring to Baghdad — *puk aytei li bei asara*: go and gather ten people for me, so that I may declare before them publicly that *hamezakeh l'ubar kanah*, that transferring to a fetus works. Shmuel wanted to issue this as a practical ruling, not merely a theoretical position.

But the *Gemara* closes with the definitive word: *vehilchata hamezakeh l'ubar lo kanah* — the halacha is that it does not work. Shmuel's view does not prevail. The exceptions remain: possibly *yerusha haba'ah me'eleha*, natural inheritance, where Rav Yosef's formulation in the name of Rava leaves open that an *ubar* may inherit; and the case of one's own child, where paternal *da'at* appears to be a sufficient foundation for the transfer. But as a general operative rule, *mezakeh l'ubar lo kanah*.
