# Gittin 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jewish Witnesses

### Tosafos Four Reasons

The discussion opens not with the material formally beginning on daf 12 but with a brief doubling back to a *tosafos* on 9b that bears directly on the central question: why are non-Jewish signatures invalid on a *get*? The *tosafos* there, citing the *Ri*, enumerates no fewer than four distinct reasons.

The first is the *gemara*'s own formulation: *lav bnei kerisus ninhu* — non-Jews are not within the category of those for whom *gittin* apply. Since divorce is a concept that exists only within a community bound by the relevant laws, those standing entirely outside that framework cannot participate in the execution of its documents.

### Lav Bnei Kerisus

The second reason is the requirement of *lishma*: the signing must be done with the specific intent that this document serve as a *get* for this woman. A non-Jew, the *tosafos* reasons, acts *ada'ata d'nafshei* — according to his own purposes — and we cannot presume he directs his mind to the required *lishma*, as established in the second chapter.

### Non-Jews as Shelichim

Third, *tosafos* notes that non-Jews cannot serve as agents: *lav bnei shlichus ninhu*. The basis for requiring *shlichus* here is that the husband must direct the *sofer* to write and the witnesses to sign, which means the entire execution of the *get* is mediated through agency. Since non-Jews are categorically excluded from the laws of *shelichut*, they cannot function in that role.

Only at this point — fourth and finally — does *tosafos* add that non-Jews are *pesulim l'edus*, disqualified as witnesses. The fact that this is the last rather than the first reason offered is itself striking. It might seem that disqualification from testimony is the most obvious and straightforward ground for invalidating non-Jewish signatures, yet *tosafos* requires three prior reasons before arriving there. The implication, as the lecturer observes, is that none of the four reasons standing alone was felt to be fully airtight, and that together they form a cumulative argument. What is especially remarkable is how the *gemara* itself treats the disqualification of non-Jews as witnesses as something less than self-evident — the *tosafos* on 9b is actually working to establish, not simply assert, that non-Jews are *pesulim l'edus*.

## Get vs. Manumission

### Rabbi Meir Retracts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר ״תֵּן גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי, וּשְׁטַר שִׁחְרוּר זֶה לְעַבְדִּי״, אִם רָצָה לַחֲזוֹר בִּשְׁנֵיהֶן – יַחְזוֹר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who says** to another: **Give this bill of divorce to my wife, or:** Give **this bill of manumission to my slave, if** before the document reaches the woman or the slave the giver **wishes to retract** his decision, then **with regard to both of them, he can retract.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

This ruling rests on the principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* — one may acquire a benefit on behalf of another person even without that person's knowledge or presence. The corollary is equally important: *ein chavin lo ela befanav* — one may not cause injury to another except in his presence, meaning only the affected party himself, or an agent he has appointed, can receive something that works to his detriment.

### Zachin L'Adam

### Chakhamim on Shichrur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בְּגִיטֵּי נָשִׁים, אֲבָל לֹא בְּשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים; לְפִי שֶׁזָּכִין לָאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, וְאֵין חָבִין לוֹ אֶלָּא בְּפָנָיו; שֶׁאִם יִרְצֶה שֶׁלֹּא לָזוּן אֶת עַבְדּוֹ – רַשַּׁאי, וְשֶׁלֹּא לָזוּן אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ – אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** One can retract his decision **in** the case of **bills of divorce but not in** the case of bills **of manumission.** The Rabbis explain the reason for their ruling: This is **because one can act in a person's interest in his absence,** and therefore the agent acquires the document on behalf of the slave from the moment the owner hands the bill of manumission to the agent. **But one can act to a person's detriment only in his presence.** The receipt of a bill of divorce is considered to be to a woman's detriment, and therefore an agent cannot receive it for her without her consent. They explain further: The emancipation of a slave is in his interests, despite the fact that he receives sustenance from his master while a slave, **as, if** the master **wishes not to sustain his slave he is allowed** not to provide him with sustenance. This demonstrates that slavery is not in the interest of the slave, as he does not receive any guaranteed benefit. **But** if a husband wishes **not to sustain his wife, he is not allowed** to proceed in this manner. Consequently, marriage is in the interests of the woman.

The *chakhamim* distinguish between the two cases. For the *get*, they agree with Rabbi Meir: since being divorced may well be a detriment to the wife, the *get* cannot be delivered by someone acting on her behalf without her appointment of that agent. But for the *shtar shichrur*, the *chakhamim* hold that we may act on the slave's behalf without his presence, because freedom is an unqualified benefit — *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* applies, and the *shaliach* may take possession of the document for the slave immediately.

### Feeding Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: וַהֲרֵי הוּא פּוֹסֵל אֶת עַבְדּוֹ מִן הַתְּרוּמָה, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהוּא פּוֹסֵל אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קִנְיָינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir **said to** the Rabbis: **But** even so, it is not in the interest of a slave to be emancipated, **as,** if his master is a priest, he **disqualifies his slave from** partaking of ***teruma*** by emancipating him, **just as** a husband who is a priest **disqualifies his** Israelite **wife** from partaking of *teruma* by divorcing her. The Rabbis **said to him:** It is permitted for a priest's slave to partake of *teruma* not because he has a right to sustenance, but rather **because he is his** master's **acquisition.**

But is freedom truly an unqualified benefit for the slave? One might argue that the master provides food and shelter, and that this represents a tangible benefit the slave would lose upon manumission. The *chakhamim* preempt this argument: *she-im ratza shelo lazon et avdo rashai* — a master may, if he wishes, simply refuse to feed his slave. There is, it appears from the *mishna*, no legal obligation to do so. By contrast, a husband may not refuse to feed his wife; that obligation is part of his *ketubah* duties. Therefore the slave gains nothing material from his servitude, since the master can always opt out of feeding him, while the wife does enjoy a concrete financial benefit from marriage that her husband cannot unilaterally withdraw.

Rabbi Meir responds that freedom is nonetheless not an unqualified benefit. *Ve-harei hu posel et avdo min ha-terumah* — a slave owned by a *kohen* may eat *terumah*, whereas a freed slave may not. This represents a concrete loss. Rabbi Meir's argument appears to be not that in the aggregate it is better to be a slave, but that the moment any element of concrete harm exists, the act cannot be performed on someone's behalf without their knowledge. The *chakhamim* counter that the slave is simply the master's property — *mipnei she-hu kinyano* — and this particular loss is subsumed within the nature of that ownership relationship rather than constituting a free-standing injury that would block the principle of *zachin*.

## Tofes L'Ba'al Chov

### Rav Huna Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב הוּנָא וְרַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, וְיָתֵיב רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה וְקָא מְנַמְנֵם, וְיָתֵיב רַב הוּנָא וְקָאָמַר: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַבָּנַן, הַתּוֹפֵס לְבַעַל חוֹב, קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara relates: **Rav Huna and Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef were sitting before Rabbi Yirmeya, and Rabbi Yirmeya was sitting and dozing** while the other two Sages conversed. **And Rav Huna was sitting and saying:** With regard to the statement **of the Rabbis** that a master cannot retract a bill of manumission once he has given it to an agent, one can **conclude from** it **that** if a third party **seizes** a debtor's property **on behalf of a creditor,** an act that is certainly in the interests of the creditor, he **acquires** this property on his behalf. This is similar to the case here, where the agent acquires the bill of manumission on behalf of the slave.

While Rabbi Yirmiyah dozes, his students Rav Huna and Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef continue the discussion. Rav Huna proposes an inference: *shma mina midirabbanan* — from the position of the *chakhamim*, who hold that the *shaliach* may take the *shtar shichrur* on behalf of the slave even before the master has fully resolved to complete the transfer, we may infer that *ha-tofes le-ba'al chov bimkom she-chav le-acherim kana* — one who seizes property on behalf of a creditor, even in a case where doing so harms third parties, acquires it validly on the creditor's behalf.

The analogy requires unpacking. In a classic application of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav*, the person acting on behalf of another does so entirely in accordance with the principal's will — no one is being overridden. The case of the *shtar shichrur* is subtler, because the master, as the *mishna* shows, wants to retain the ability to retract. When the *shaliach* takes possession of the document on behalf of the slave, he is in some sense jumping ahead before the master is fully ready to release his property. This gives the case a flavor of *tofes le-ba'al chov*, seizing something on behalf of someone even while the current owner is not entirely prepared to let go. Rav Huna reasons that if this works in the *mishna*'s case, then seizing property for a creditor must also work even when other parties are harmed in the process.

### Tosafot Objection

*Tosafot* raises a sharp objection to this analogy: in the case of seizing property for a creditor, there is a genuine underlying debt — the debtor truly owes the money. But the master does not owe the slave his freedom. What basis is there for treating the *shaliach*'s taking possession of the *shtar shichrur* as analogous to collecting a genuine debt? *Tosafot* grapples with this and suggests that perhaps the *gemara* understands the master's expressed will to free the slave as creating some form of obligation or entitlement, even if imperfect — enough to provide at least a partial analogy. Crucially, the act of saying "deliver this *shtar shichrur*" has already occurred; the master has expressed his desire, and the *shaliach*'s taking possession can be understood as the execution of that expressed will rather than an act hostile to it.

### Rav Yochanan vs Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּהָכִי אִיתְּעַר בְּהוּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה. אֲמַר לְהוּ: דַּרְדְּקֵי! הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַתּוֹפֵס לְבַעַל חוֹב בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁחָב לַאֲחֵרִים – לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **In the meantime Rabbi Yirmeya woke up, due to their** conversation, as he was not sleeping deeply. **He said to them: Children [*dardekei*], this is** what **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One who seizes** property on behalf **of a creditor in a case where it is to the detriment of others does not acquire.**

Rav Yirmiyah wakes up and tells his students: *dardakei* — children — and reports the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan: *ha-tofes le-ba'al chov bimkom she-chav le-acherim lo kana*. When seizing property for a creditor causes harm to third-party creditors, the seizure does not take effect. Rav Yosef bar Yosef had said it does work even in such a case; Rabbi Yochanan rules it does not.

The *gemara* then needs to salvage the ruling of the *mishna*: if seizing property at the expense of others does not work, how can the *shaliach* validly take possession of the *shtar shichrur* on behalf of the slave when the master is not yet ready to release it?

### When One Says Give

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תֹּאמַר: מִשְׁנָתֵינוּ! כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״תְּנוּ״, כְּאוֹמֵר ״זְכוּ״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** And if you say** that **the mishna** apparently teaches the opposite, as the agent acquires the bill of manumission on behalf of the slave despite the fact that this causes a loss for the master, that case is different. **anyone who says: Give** to so-and-so, **is like one who says: Acquire** on behalf of so-and-so. Since the master said: Give this bill of manumission to my slave, the agent immediately acquires it on the slave's behalf, despite the fact that the bill is to the detriment of the master. However, this *halakha* has no bearing on a case where a person independently seizes property on behalf of another, and by doing so acts to the disadvantage of others.

The *gemara* answers with a fundamental principle: *kol ha-omer tnu k'omer zachi dami* — when one says "give this to so-and-so," that formulation is legally equivalent to saying "take possession of it for him right now." By handing the document to the *shaliach* with the instruction to deliver it, the master is implicitly authorizing the *shaliach* to acquire it on the slave's behalf at that very moment. The act is therefore not against the master's will — we interpret his instruction as immediate authorization. This removes the case entirely from the category of *tofes le-ba'al chov*, since there is no seizure against anyone's will. The *shaliach* is not grabbing property ahead of a reluctant owner; the owner, by saying "give it," has already consented.

This principle also explains why the husband can still retract in the case of the *get*. Even if we say the master is prepared for the transfer to be immediate, the question remains whether the *shaliach* is authorized to represent the *other* side — the wife. The answer is no, because *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* does not apply where the act might constitute a detriment to the recipient. The master's readiness to proceed does not overcome the requirement that the wife be represented only by someone she herself has appointed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַתּוֹפֵס לְבַעַל חוֹב בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁחָב לַאֲחֵרִים, בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבָּנַן. דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁלִּיקֵּט אֶת הַפֵּאָה, וְאָמַר ״הֲרֵי זוֹ לִפְלוֹנִי עָנִי״, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אָמַר: זָכָה לוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יִתְּנֶנּוּ לְעָנִי הַנִּמְצָא רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says:** With regard to this issue of **one who seizes** property **on behalf of a creditor in a case where it is to the detriment of others, we have arrived at the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis.** What is this dispute? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 4:9): With regard to **one who** is not poor but **collected produce in the corner of the field, which is given to the poor [*pe'a*], and said: This** produce that I have collected is **for so-and-so,** who is **a poor person, Rabbi Eliezer said: He has acquired** it **on his behalf, and the Rabbis say:** He has not acquired it on his behalf; rather, **he gives** the produce he gathered **to** whichever **poor person appears first** before him. Apparently, Rabbi Eliezer holds that one can gather *pe'a* on behalf of a poor person, despite the fact that he acts to the detriment of other paupers, while the Rabbis disagree.

The *gemara* then considers whether the principle of *tofes le-ba'al chov* can be derived from the *mishna* in *Pe'ah*: one who harvests *pe'ah* on behalf of a specific poor person — *mi she-likat et ha-pe'ah ve-amar harei zeh le-ploni ani*. Rabbi Eliezer holds the acquisition is valid; the *chakhamim* hold the harvested *pe'ah* must be given to the first poor person who arrives. This seems to map onto the *tofes le-ba'al chov* debate, but Amemar (or Rav Pappa) resists the equation. Rabbi Eliezer may permit the *pe'ah* case for a specific reason: *demigo de-ei ba'ei mafkir le-nichsei ve-havei ani ve-chazi leh* — since the harvester could himself renounce all his property, become poor, and thereby be personally entitled to the *pe'ah*, he may acquire it for someone else. This logic does not extend to the case of a genuine creditor seizing the debtor's assets, where the seizing party has no independent entitlement to the property. Conversely, the *chakhamim* who prohibit in the *pe'ah* case may rely on the specific verse *lo telaket* — you shall not harvest for the poor — which creates a particular exclusion not applicable to the general *tofes* question. The two debates cannot be straightforwardly equated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב פָּפָּא: דִּלְמָא לָא הִיא; עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּמִיגּוֹ דְּאִי בָּעֵי מַפְקַר לְהוּ לְנִכְסֵיהּ, וְהָוֵי עָנִי וַחֲזֵי לֵיהּ – וּמִיגּוֹ דְּזָכֵי לֵיהּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ זָכֵי לְחַבְרֵיהּ; אֲבָל הָכָא, לָא. וְעַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן הָתָם, אֶלָּא דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תְלַקֵּט לְעָנִי״ – לֹא תְלַקֵּט לוֹ לְעָנִי; אֲבָל הָכָא, לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Perhaps** that **is not so.** Perhaps this is not the point of dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis. There are two ways to explain why this might not be the case. First, it is possible that **Rabbi Eliezer states his opinion only there,** when the person took possession of the *pe'a* despite not being eligible to collect it for himself, **since if** the one who collected the produce **desired, he** could **declare his property ownerless and become a poor person, and** then the produce would have been **fit for him** to retain for himself. **And since** he could have **acquired** the *pe'a* **for himself,** he can likewise **acquire** it **for another** as well. **However, here,** where he seizes property on behalf of a creditor, he cannot seize this property for himself. Consequently, Rabbi Eliezer does **not** rule that he can do so when it is against the interests of another person. Second, one can argue from the opposite perspective: **The Rabbis state** their opinion **only there,** that one cannot acquire on behalf of the poor person, **as it is written:** "The corner of your field you shall not reap and the gleaning of your harvest **you shall not gather; for the poor** you shall leave them" (Leviticus 23:22), and the Sages expounded this verse by reading it as though there were no break in the middle: **You shall not gather for the poor,** i.e., the poor person must collect the produce for himself. **However, here,** where one seizes property on behalf of a creditor, this verse does not apply, and therefore the Rabbis did **not** rule that the action of the third party is ineffective. Consequently, it is possible that the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis is not with regard to the issue of whether one can seize property on behalf of a creditor in a case where it is to the detriment of others.

## Master's Obligation

### No Obligation Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאִם יִרְצֶה שֶׁלֹּא לָזוּן אֶת עַבְדּוֹ – רַשַּׁאי, וְשֶׁלֹּא לָזוּן אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ – אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי. ... שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, יָכוֹל הָרַב לוֹמַר לָעֶבֶד: ״עֲשֵׂה עִמִּי וְאֵינִי זָנָךְ״?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated that a master may refuse to feed his slave — **as, if** the master **wishes not to sustain his slave he is allowed** not to provide him with sustenance. ... The *gemara* comments: **Conclude from** the mishna that **the master is** legally **able to say to** his **slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you.**

With the *tofes* discussion concluded, the *gemara* returns to the substantive question raised by the *mishna*: does a master have a legal obligation to feed his slave? The *mishna* stated that a master may refuse to feed his slave — *she-im ratza shelo lazon et avdo rashai*. The natural inference is *shma mina*: *yachol ha-rav lomar le-eved asei imi ve-eini zonecha* — the master may say "work for me and I will not feed you."

### Counter: Must Feed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דַּאֲמַר לוֹ: ״צֵא מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיךָ לִמְזוֹנוֹתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this claim: This is not a conclusive proof, as **with what are we dealing here,** in the mishna? We are dealing with a case **where he said to** the slave: **Spend your earnings to sustain yourself.** In other words, I will not provide for you; rather, you must work and earn money for your sustenance on your own. However, this does not mean that a master can force his slave to work for him without providing him with sustenance.

The *gemara* resists this inference. *Hacha be-mai askinan* — the *mishna* is not saying there is no obligation. Rather, the case is one where the master says *tze ma'asei yadecha bi-mezonotecha* — "go use your own labor to feed yourself." The master does have a legal obligation to feed the slave, but he may discharge that obligation indirectly: rather than providing food himself, he may waive his claim to the slave's labor for the purpose of allowing the slave to earn his own food. The master cannot demand the slave's labor and simultaneously refuse to feed him; but if he releases the labor, the feeding obligation is effectively transferred.

### Wife vs Slave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי אִשָּׁה – דַּאֲמַר לַהּ: ״צְאִי מַעֲשֵׂה יָדַיִךְ בִּמְזוֹנוֹתַיִךְ״?! אִשָּׁה אַמַּאי לָא? אִשָּׁה – בִּדְלָא סָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **in the corresponding** situation in the mishna **with regard to a wife,** will one explain **that he** could have **said to her: Spend your earnings to sustain yourself?** The mishna rules that one cannot exempt himself from his obligation to provide sustenance for his wife. In the case of **a wife, why** may he **not** do so? It is certainly permitted for a husband to stipulate with his wife that she will keep her earnings and not receive sustenance from him, yet it is clear in the mishna that there is a difference between a slave and a wife with regard to his ability to refuse to provide sustenance. The Gemara answers: The **wife** mentioned in the mishna is one whose earnings **are not sufficient** for her sustenance, and in that case the husband may not stipulate in this manner.

A difficulty arises immediately: the same logic seems applicable to a wife. A husband could equally say *tze ma'asei yadecha le-mezonotecha* — earn your own living — and thereby avoid his obligation to feed her. If so, why is the wife's situation treated as superior to the slave's?

The *gemara* answers: *isha de-la safka* — the wife's situation is categorically different because we can easily imagine a case where she cannot earn enough to support herself. In that event, the husband must make up the difference. His obligation is not merely to release her labor; it is to ensure she is actually fed, regardless of her earning capacity. Therefore marriage does confer a genuine financial benefit: if she cannot earn her keep, he is obligated to supplement.

### Slave Without Means

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶבֶד נָמֵי בִּדְלָא סָפֵק! עַבְדָּא דִּנְהוֹם כְּרֵסֵיהּ לָא שָׁוֵיא, לְמָרֵיהּ וּלְמָרְתֵיהּ לְמַאי מִיתְבְּעֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If so, the **slave also** is referring to a situation where his earnings **are not sufficient** for his sustenance, and he is unable to sustain himself. Nevertheless, his master can refuse to sustain him. Therefore, one can conclude from the mishna that a master can say to his slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you. The Gemara explains: **A slave who is not worth the bread that he consumes, for what is he needed by his master or his mistress?** If the value of his labor does not even pay for the cost of his sustenance why is he needed at all? The mishna certainly does not refer to a case of this kind. By contrast, with regard to a wife, the marriage does not depend on her ability to provide for herself by means of her earnings. Consequently, one cannot prove from the mishna that a master can say to his slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you.

What about a slave who similarly cannot earn his own keep? Here the *gemara* responds sharply: *avda de-nahom karsei lo shavya le-marei le-martei le-mai mitvabei* — a slave who cannot earn the food his stomach requires, what use is he to his master? The practical implication, as Rashi explains, is that there is no legal obligation to make up the difference for a slave who cannot earn his own upkeep. Since the entire framework of the master-slave relationship is premised on the slave's productive labor, where that labor does not cover even basic sustenance, the master has no obligation to supplement. The *gemara* does not provide a positive rationale grounded in a *pasuk* or contractual principle; it simply asserts the distinction.

The ethical weight of this position is not lost. The moment a slave becomes ill or incapacitated — precisely when he most needs support — the master appears to have no legal obligation to provide it. The *gemara* arrives at this conclusion without adverting to a verse or a contract, which the lecturer flags as notable.

### Cities of Refuge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: עֶבֶד שֶׁגָּלָה לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט אֵין רַבּוֹ חַיָּיב לְזוּנוֹ, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁמַּעֲשֵׂה יָדָיו לְרַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Makkot* 2:8): With regard to **a slave who** unintentionally killed someone and **was exiled to** one of the **cities of refuge, his master is not required to sustain him. And not only that, but his earnings** in his city of refuge belong **to his master.**

The *gemara* now adduces further evidence from a *baraita* concerning a slave who has accidentally killed someone and fled to one of the *arei miklat*, the cities of refuge. The *baraita* states: *ein rabo chayav le-zono* — the master has no obligation to feed him there; *ve-lo od, she-ma'aseh yadav le-rabo* — moreover, if the slave can perform labor while in the city of refuge, his earnings belong to the master. This seems to resolve the question directly: no feeding obligation, yet full entitlement to earnings.

### Slave's Own Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, יָכוֹל הָרַב לוֹמַר לָעֶבֶד ״עֲשֵׂה עִמִּי וְאֵינִי זָנָךְ״! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּאָמַר לוֹ: ״צֵא מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיךָ לִמְזוֹנוֹתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Conclude from** the *baraita* that **a master can say to** his **slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you,** as in the case of a slave exiled to a city of refuge the master is entitled to his earnings despite not providing for him. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** With a case **where** the master **said to him: Spend your earnings to sustain yourself.**

The *gemara* again refuses to read this at face value. The master's lack of obligation to feed means he has arranged that the slave feeds himself from his own earnings — *tze ma'asei yadecha le-mezonotecha*. The slave's earnings that "go to the master" refer only to the surplus beyond what the slave needs for food. On its face this is a very forced reading of the *baraita*, which seems simply to say "don't feed him, keep his earnings." The *gemara* acknowledges this by asking: if the slave keeps his own earnings for food, and any surplus goes to the master, why does the *baraita* need to specify that the surplus goes to the master — is this not obvious?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַעֲשֵׂה יָדָיו אַמַּאי לְרַבּוֹ? לְהַעְדָּפָה. הַעֲדָפָה, פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, כֵּיוָן דְּכִי לֵית לֵיהּ לָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ, כִּי אִית לֵיהּ נָמֵי לָא לִישְׁקוֹל מִינֵּיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** if the slave keeps his own earnings for food, and any surplus goes to the master, then **why do his earnings** belong **to his master?** After all, his master stipulated that he should sustain himself. The Gemara answers: This *halakha* is referring **to** his **surplus** earnings, i.e., if the slave works and earns more than the cost of his sustenance, his surplus income belongs to the master. The Gemara asks: **Isn't** it **obvious** that any **surplus** belongs to the master, as he owns the slave? The Gemara answers: This ruling is necessary, **lest you say that since, when he does not** work enough the master does **not give him** all he needs to eat, then **when he has** a surplus from his work the master **should also not take it.** Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that despite this consideration the surplus belongs to the master.

The *gemara* answers: *mahu deteima* — one might have thought that since the master has no obligation to make up the difference when the slave falls short, the slave should be entitled to accumulate a rainy-day reserve from his surplus earnings against future days when he cannot earn. The *baraita* therefore tells us this is not the case: the arrangement is strictly day-to-day, and any surplus belongs to the master.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט? סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״וָחָי״ – עָבֵיד לֵיהּ חִיּוּתָא טְפֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is different** with regard to a slave who was exiled **to** one of the **cities of refuge,** concerning whom the *baraita* was stated? According to this reasoning, the surplus should belong to the master no matter where the slave is located. The Gemara answers: It might **enter your mind to say** that as the verse states with regard to one who is exiled to a city of refuge: "And that fleeing to one of these cities **he might live"** (Deuteronomy 4:42), one should **act for him** so that he has **extra life,** i.e., in this specific case the slave should be allowed to retain any additional amount he earns. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that no special obligation of this kind applies even if the slave had been exiled to a city of refuge.

The *gemara* further explains why the *baraita* specifically chose the scenario of *arei miklat*: one might have thought that the verse commanding the fugitive to *live* in the city of refuge — *venas el achat he-arim ha-el ve-chai* — creates a heightened obligation to ensure the slave's sustenance there. The *baraita* teaches that even in that context, no such obligation exists.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אֲבָל אִשָּׁה שֶׁגָּלְתָה לְעָרֵי מִקְלָט – בַּעְלָהּ חַיָּיב בִּמְזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ; מִכְּלָל דְּלָא אֲמַר לַהּ, דְּאִי אֲמַר לַהּ, בַּעְלַהּ אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? וּמִדְּסֵיפָא דְּלָא אֲמַר לַהּ, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי דְּלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But from** the fact **that the latter clause** of that *baraita* **teaches: However,** with regard to **a woman who was exiled to** one of the **cities of refuge, her husband is obligated in her sustenance, by inference** the *baraita* is dealing with a case **where he did not say to her:** Spend your earnings to sustain yourself, which is why he must sustain her. **As, if he said** this **to her, why is her husband obligated** to provide her with sustenance? **And from** the fact **that the latter clause** of the *baraita* is speaking about a case **where he did not say** this **to her,** it may be inferred that the **first clause** is **also** dealing with a situation **where he did not say to** the slave: Spend your earnings to sustain yourself. The two clauses of the *baraita* must be referring to similar cases or they would not be taught together.

The subsequent discussion in the *gemara* involves a *baraita* that contrasts the slave with a wife who has fled to a city of refuge: *ba'alah chayav bi-mezonoteiha* — her husband remains obligated to feed her. The *gemara* navigates the inference carefully: since the wife-case explicitly requires feeding only where no special arrangement was made, the slave-case must also be read as involving an arrangement (*tze ma'asei yadecha*) and not as a flat declaration of no obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ, וְאִשָּׁה בִּדְלָא סָפְקָה. וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְאִם אָמַר לָהּ ״צְאִי מַעֲשֵׂה יָדַיִךְ בִּמְזוֹנוֹתַיִךְ״ – רַשַּׁאי, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא דְּלָא אֲמַר לַהּ! הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְאִם מַסְפֶּקֶת, וְאָמַר לַהּ: ״צְאִי מַעֲשֵׂה יָדַיִךְ בִּמְזוֹנוֹתַיִךְ״ – רַשַּׁאי. מַסְפֶּקֶת, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״כׇּל כְּבוּדָּה בַת מֶלֶךְ פְּנִימָה״, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this claim: **Actually,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the master **said to** the slave: Spend your earnings to sustain yourself, and the husband issued a similar statement to his wife. However, in the case of **a woman** it is referring to a situation **when** her earnings are **not sufficient** for her sustenance. The Gemara raises an objection: **But from** the fact **that the last clause teaches: And if** the husband **said to her: Spend your earnings to sustain yourself, he is permitted** to act accordingly, **it may be inferred that the first** half of the last **clause** is referring to a case **where he did not say** this **to her.** The Gemara explains that **this is what** the *baraita* **is saying: And if** her earnings **suffice** to pay for her sustenance, **and he said to her: Spend your earnings to sustain yourself, it is permitted** for him to say that. The Gemara asks: If the *baraita* is referring to a case where her earnings **suffice, what** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha* with regard to one who was exiled to a city of refuge? The same *halakha* applies to all wives. The Gemara answers: It is necessary, **lest you say** that in light of the verse: **"All glorious is the king's daughter within the palace"** (Psalms 45:14), from which it is derived that it is improper for a wife to spend too much time outside her home, this woman's husband should be concerned that while his wife is in a city of refuge she should not work out of the home, but only in the home. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that there is no need to be concerned about this matter, and her husband may say to her: Spend your earnings to sustain yourself, even in a city of refuge.

The *gemara* then adds a final nuance: even where a wife could theoretically earn enough to feed herself, one might think her dignity prevents us from requiring her to hustle for work — *kol kevuda bat melech penima*. The *gemara* rules that we do in fact require her to earn what she can, and only the gap beyond what she can reasonably earn must be made up by the husband.

Throughout this extended passage, the *gemara* consistently refuses to accept the plain reading of texts that seem to say the master has no obligation to feed his slave, insisting instead that an obligation exists — while never grounding that obligation explicitly in a verse or formal legal category. The lecturer notes this is a striking feature of the *sugya*.

## Sanctifying Labor

### Hedesh Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ, אוֹתוֹ הָעֶבֶד לֹוֶה וְאוֹכֵל, וְעוֹשֶׂה וּפוֹרֵעַ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, יָכוֹל הָרַב לוֹמַר לָעֶבֶד: ״עֲשֵׂה עִמִּי וְאֵינִי זָנָךְ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another suggestion: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a statement **that Rav said:** In the case of **one who consecrates the hands of his slave,** so that all the work he does becomes Temple property, preventing the slave from working on his own behalf, then **that slave borrows and eats, and** afterward **performs** work **and repays** what he borrowed, as will be explained. **Conclude from** Rav's statement that **the master is able to say to** his **slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you.** In this case the master has prevented his slave from working for himself, and yet he is not required to provide for him.

The *gemara* now turns to a specific case that illuminates the question from a different angle. Rav rules: *ha-makdish yedei avdo* — if a master declares his slave's labor *hekdesh*, sanctified to the Temple, *oto eved loveh ve-ochel ve-oto poreia* — the slave borrows money to eat and repays from his labor.

The scenario is this: the master has sanctified the slave's labor, meaning whatever the slave earns passes automatically to *hekdesh*. The master, not wishing to feed the slave out of his own pocket now that he is deriving no direct benefit from the labor, refuses to provide food. But the slave's own earnings also go to *hekdesh* and cannot be used for food. The solution the *gemara* describes is that the slave borrows money, purchases food with the borrowed funds, and then repays the loan from his subsequent earnings.

The question is how the earnings can go toward repaying the loan if they are *hekdesh* the moment they are earned.

### Borrowing to Eat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמַעֲלֶה לוֹ מְזוֹנוֹת. אִי הָכִי, לְמַאי לֹוֶה וְאוֹכֵל? לְהַעְדָּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** This is referring to a case **where** the master **provides sustenance for him.** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, for what** reason **does he** find it necessary to **borrow and eat?** After all, his master provides for him. The Gemara answers: This is referring **to surplus** food, i.e., additional food that the slave wants to eat.

A closer look at this case, however, reveals a tension. The *gemara* argues that the master is entitled to sanctify the slave's labor only because he is in fact feeding the slave — *hacha be-mai askinan be-ma'aleh lo mezonot*. But if the master is feeding the slave, why does the slave need to borrow money to eat?

The *gemara* answers: *le-hadafah* — for the extra. The master is providing the bare minimum of food, just sufficient to ground his legal entitlement to the slave's labor. The slave, however, wants to eat more — or needs more for his health and productivity. It is for this additional food beyond the bare minimum that he must borrow. The *hekdesh* arrangement, with its sub-*prutah* mechanism, applies only to the repayment of this supplemental borrowing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ: עַד הַשְׁתָּא סַגִּי לָךְ בְּלָא הַעֲדָפָה, וְהַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי תִּיסְגֵּי לָךְ בְּלָא הַעֲדָפָה! הֶקְדֵּשׁ גּוּפֵיהּ נִיחָא לֵיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלַשְׁבַּח עַבְדֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if so, **let** the **Temple** treasurer **say to him: Until now it was enough for you without a surplus; now too it should be enough for you without a surplus.** Why do you seek more now? Consequently, the consecration should apply to this surplus as well. The Gemara answers: **It is preferable to** the **Temple** treasury **itself** that he work and eat more, **so that** the value of **its slave will be enhanced** when he consumes this extra amount, as he will become stronger.

### Sub-Prutah Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוֹשֶׂה וּפוֹרֵעַ, קַמָּא קַמָּא קָדֵישׁ לֵיהּ! בְּפָחוֹת פָּחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated that the slave borrows and eats and afterward **performs** work **and repays** the loan. The Gemara asks: But his work **immediately becomes consecrated,** as his hands have been consecrated. How, then, can he repay this loan? The Gemara answers: The slave performs work **of less than the value of one *peruta*.** Anything worth less than one *peruta* does not become consecrated. Therefore he can collect these small sums and repay his debt little by little.

The *gemara* explains the mechanism: the repayment occurs in increments smaller than a *prutah*. Since *hekdesh* acquires rights only when an amount reaches the threshold of a *prutah*, each sub-*prutah* increment is transferred to the creditor before *hekdesh*'s claim attaches. *Hekdesh* never actually gets hold of those amounts, and the creditor is paid through this continuous micro-transfer.

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא; דְּאָמַר רַב: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ יְדֵי עַבְדּוֹ – אוֹתוֹ הָעֶבֶד עוֹשֶׂה וְאוֹכֵל, דְּאִי לָא עַבְדָּא מַאן פָּלַח לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** that Rav is referring to a case where the master provides sustenance for him. **As Rav says:** With regard to **one who consecrates the hands of his slave, that slave works and eats, as, if** the slave does **not work** for himself, **who will work** and provide **for him?** This ruling indicates that Rav maintains that consecration does not apply to the slave's hands.

This reading is confirmed by Rav's own language elsewhere: *amar Rav ha-makdish yedei avdo, oto eved oseh ve-ochel* — the slave works and eats. The slave, in Rav's formulation, is entitled to eat from his labor. Rav's underlying principle is that if the master is not feeding the slave, he is not entitled to the slave's labor. If he has sanctified the labor while refusing to feed the slave, the sanctification is invalid and the slave is entitled to work and feed himself directly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא הָךְ בְּמַעֲלֶה וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל, וְהָא בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מַעֲלֶה, שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ הָךְ בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מַעֲלֶה, וְיָכוֹל, דְּאִי לָא עַבְדָּא מַאן פָּלַח לֵיהּ?! מַאן דְּבָעֵי נִיפְלְחֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Granted, if you say** that **this** earlier *halakha* that Rav said, that the slave borrows and repays, refers to a situation **where** his master **provides sustenance for him, and** this is because in general **he cannot** say to him: Work for me but I will not sustain you, **and this** second *halakha* is referring to a case **where** his master **does not provide** him with sustenance, and therefore he cannot consecrate the slave's hands, it **is well,** as there is no contradiction between the two statements of Rav. **However, if you say** that **this** first *halakha* is **also** speaking of a case **where** his master **does not provide** sustenance for him, and the consecration is effective because he **can** say to his slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you, then what is the meaning of the statement: **As, if** the slave does **not work** for himself **who will work for him?** Let **whoever desires work for him,** i.e., the slave will be compelled to sustain himself from charity, but this is not the master's concern. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** here that Rav holds that a master **cannot** say to his slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you? The Gemara comments: Indeed **conclude from** here that this is the case.

### Bare Minimum Feeding

The *gemara* draws these threads together. In the case where the sanctification is valid — the master is feeding the slave — the master provides the bare minimum, the slave may borrow for additional food and repay from earnings via the sub-*prutah* mechanism, and *hekdesh* acquiesces because the slave's productivity benefits it. In the case Rav addresses where the sanctification is invalid, the master was not feeding the slave, and therefore he never had the entitlement to sanctify the labor in the first place. The *gemara* affirms: *shma mina eino yachol* — one may not demand a slave's labor while refusing to feed him. This is Rav's clear ruling.

## Rabbi Yochanan Dissent

### Severing Hand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַקּוֹטֵעַ יַד עַבְדּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – נוֹתֵן שִׁבְתּוֹ וּרְפוּאָתוֹ לְרַבּוֹ, וְאוֹתוֹ הָעֶבֶד נִיזּוֹן מִן הַצְּדָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another source concerning the same dilemma, **as Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One who severs the hand of another's slave** must **give** compensation for **his** loss of **livelihood,** i.e., compensation for the slave's inability to work as a result of his injury, **and** the slave's **medical costs to his master. And that slave,** who can no longer work, is **sustained from charity.**

Against Rav's position, the *gemara* introduces a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan. If someone severs the hand of another person's slave, Rabbi Yochanan rules: *noten shevet ve-refuah le-rabo* — the tortfeasor pays *shevet* and *refuah* to the master. *Shevet* is the compensation for lost labor during the period of incapacitation — not the permanent diminishment of the slave's value, which is addressed by the main injury payment, but the specific loss of labor during the recovery period: work the slave could have done, such as light tasks not requiring the severed hand, that he did not do because he was hospitalized. *Refuah* is the payment for medical treatment.

### Charity Food

The remarkable element of Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is the next clause: *ve-oto eved nizon min ha-tzedaka* — and the slave himself is fed from charity. The master is collecting *shevet* payments representing the slave's foregone labor, yet the slave's food during the recovery period comes not from the master but from communal charity funds.

### No Feeding Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, יָכוֹל הָרַב לוֹמַר לָעֶבֶד: ״עֲשֵׂה עִמִּי וְאֵינִי זָנָךְ״! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמַעֲלֶה לוֹ מְזוֹנוֹת. אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי נִיזּוֹן מִן הַצְּדָקָה? לְהַעְדָּפָה. אִי הָכִי, ״נִיזּוֹן״?! ״מִתְפַּרְנֵס״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ יָכוֹל! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Conclude from** Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that **the master is able to say to his slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you,** as otherwise the master would not receive compensation for his time in the form of payment for the slave's earnings while the slave lives off of charity. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** This is referring to a case **where** his master **provides sustenance for him.** The Gemara asks: **If so, why is he sustained from charity?** He already has sustenance. The Gemara answers: This is referring **to surplus** food, i.e., additional food that the slave wants to eat. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so,** the phrase: **Sustained** from charity, is imprecise, as it indicates that he is provided only with enough for his basic needs. **It should have** said: He **earns a living** from charity. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** his statement that Rabbi Yochanan maintains that the master **can** say to his slave: Work for me but I will not sustain you? The Gemara comments: Indeed, **conclude from** his statement that this is the case.

According to Rav's position, this is difficult: if the master is entitled to the slave's labor — here in the form of *shevet* — he should be obligated to feed the slave. The *gemara* draws the inference explicitly: *shma mina* — according to Rabbi Yochanan, *yachol ha-rav lomar le-eved asei imi ve-eini zonecha* — the master may say "work for me and I will not feed you." Rabbi Yochanan and Rav disagree directly: Rav holds that entitlement to the slave's labor is conditional on feeding him; Rabbi Yochanan holds that the master may collect the benefit of the slave's labor without any corresponding obligation to provide food.

The *gemara* attempts to harmonize Rabbi Yochanan's ruling with the position that a feeding obligation exists. Perhaps *nizon min ha-tzedaka* means the master is providing the bare minimum and charity supplements it — *le-hadafah*. But the *gemara* rejects this: *nizon* specifically denotes the bare minimum subsistence level, whereas *mitparnes* would indicate something above the minimum. The word *nizon min ha-tzedaka* means that even the bare minimum is coming from charity, not from the master. The inference stands: *shma mina* that according to Rabbi Yochanan, the master has no obligation to feed his slave even while collecting the proceeds of the slave's labor.

The lecturer observes that this may reflect a broader geographic or institutional disagreement — Rav, the Babylonian authority, insisting on the feeding obligation; Rabbi Yochanan, the Palestinian *amora*, permitting its waiver.

### Doctor Bills

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: נוֹתֵן שִׁבְתּוֹ וּרְפוּאָתוֹ לְרַבּוֹ. שִׁבְתּוֹ, פְּשִׁיטָא! רְפוּאָתוֹ אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ. רְפוּאָתוֹ דִּידֵיהּ הִיא, דְּבָעֵי אִיתַּסּוֹיֵי בֵּהּ! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאַמְדוּהּ לְחַמְשָׁא יוֹמֵי, וַעֲבַדוּ לֵיהּ סַמָּא חָרִיפָא וְאִתַּסִּי בִּתְלָתָא יוֹמֵי; מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: צַעֲרָא – דִּידֵיהּ הוּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the aforementioned *halakha*. **The Master says** earlier: **He** must **give** compensation for the slave's loss of **livelihood and his medical costs to his master.** The Gemara comments: With regard to compensation for loss of **livelihood, it is obvious** that this is the *halakha*, as the master incurs the loss when his slave does not work, and therefore **it was necessary for** the Sages to mention only that the compensation for **his medical costs** goes to his master. The Gemara comments: Why is the payment for his medical costs given to his master? Shouldn't the compensation for **his medical costs belong to him, as he needs to be healed with it?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** doctors **estimated** that the blow or wound **would heal in five days and they made a strong medicine for him, and he was healed in three days** instead of five. **Lest you say:** Since **the pain** suffered by the slave by taking the strong medicine that caused a decrease in the costs of healing was his, the money should belong **to him,** Rabbi Yoḥanan therefore **teaches us** that this is not the case, as even this money belongs to the master, not the slave.

The *gemara* closes with a final question about the *refuah* payment. If the tortfeasor must pay *refuah* to the master, why? The medical bills are incurred for the slave's treatment and would seem to belong to the slave so he can pay his doctors. The *gemara* explains with a specific case: the doctors assessed that recovery would take five days and payment was made accordingly, but through more aggressive treatment — harder on the slave, but more effective — he recovered in three days. Two days of medical expenses were saved. Who keeps those two days of savings?

One might think the slave is entitled to them, since it was his suffering — *tza'ara didei hu* — that produced the savings. The *gemara* rules otherwise: *kamashma lan* — just as the master is entitled to the fruit of the slave's labor generally, he is entitled to the savings realized through the slave's endurance of harder treatment. The doctor-bill savings, like the *shevet* payments, belong to the master.

With this, the *sugya* of *asei imi ve-eini zonecha* concludes: Rav holds that a master may not demand a slave's labor while refusing to feed him; Rabbi Yochanan holds that there is no legal obligation to feed a slave, and the master may collect *shevet* while leaving the slave to charity. In either case — whether because the master need not feed at all, or because he may release the slave's labor in lieu of feeding — the *chakhamim*'s position that manumission is an unqualified benefit for the slave stands. The slave is never in a position where his status as owned property guarantees him sustenance, and therefore the act of freeing him involves no element of harm that would require the slave's own presence or consent.
