# Gittin 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Non-Jewish Witnesses

### Tosafos Four Reasons

The discussion opens not with the material formally beginning on daf 12 but with a brief doubling back to a *tosafos* on 9b that bears directly on the central question: why are non-Jewish signatures invalid on a *get*? The *tosafos* there, citing the *Ri*, enumerates no fewer than four distinct reasons.

The first is the *gemara*'s own formulation: *lav bnei kerisus ninhu* — non-Jews are not within the category of those for whom *gittin* apply. Since divorce is a concept that exists only within a community bound by the relevant laws, those standing entirely outside that framework cannot participate in the execution of its documents.

### Lav Bnei Kerisus

The second reason is the requirement of *lishma*: the signing must be done with the specific intent that this document serve as a *get* for this woman. A non-Jew, the *tosafos* reasons, acts *ada'ata d'nafshei* — according to his own purposes — and we cannot presume he directs his mind to the required *lishma*, as established in the second chapter.

### Non-Jews as Shelichim

Third, *tosafos* notes that non-Jews cannot serve as agents: *lav bnei shlichus ninhu*. The basis for requiring *shlichus* here is that the husband must direct the *sofer* to write and the witnesses to sign, which means the entire execution of the *get* is mediated through agency. Since non-Jews are categorically excluded from the laws of *shelichut*, they cannot function in that role.

Only at this point — fourth and finally — does *tosafos* add that non-Jews are *pesulim l'edus*, disqualified as witnesses. The fact that this is the last rather than the first reason offered is itself striking. It might seem that disqualification from testimony is the most obvious and straightforward ground for invalidating non-Jewish signatures, yet *tosafos* requires three prior reasons before arriving there. The implication, as the lecturer observes, is that none of the four reasons standing alone was felt to be fully airtight, and that together they form a cumulative argument. What is especially remarkable is how the *gemara* itself treats the disqualification of non-Jews as witnesses as something less than self-evident — the *tosafos* on 9b is actually working to establish, not simply assert, that non-Jews are *pesulim l'edus*.

## Get vs. Manumission

### Rabbi Meir Retracts

The *mishna* on 11b introduces a parallel between the *get* and the *shtar shichrur*, the writ of manumission. If a man says *"ten get ze l'ishti v'shtar shichrur ze l'avdi"* — give this *get* to my wife and this *shtar shichrur* to my slave — Rabbi Meir holds that he may retract either of them before delivery. The *shaliach* does not automatically stand in for the recipient; the transfer is not yet final.

### Zachin L'Adam

This ruling rests on the principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* — one may acquire a benefit on behalf of another person even without that person's knowledge or presence. The corollary is equally important: *ein chavin lo ela befanav* — one may not cause injury to another except in his presence, meaning only the affected party himself, or an agent he has appointed, can receive something that works to his detriment.

### Chakhamim on Shichrur

The *chakhamim* distinguish between the two cases. For the *get*, they agree with Rabbi Meir: since being divorced may well be a detriment to the wife, the *get* cannot be delivered by someone acting on her behalf without her appointment of that agent. But for the *shtar shichrur*, the *chakhamim* hold that we may act on the slave's behalf without his presence, because freedom is an unqualified benefit — *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* applies, and the *shaliach* may take possession of the document for the slave immediately.

### Feeding Obligations

But is freedom truly an unqualified benefit for the slave? One might argue that the master provides food and shelter, and that this represents a tangible benefit the slave would lose upon manumission. The *chakhamim* preempt this argument: *she-im ratza shelo lazon et avdo rashai* — a master may, if he wishes, simply refuse to feed his slave. There is, it appears from the *mishna*, no legal obligation to do so. By contrast, a husband may not refuse to feed his wife; that obligation is part of his *ketubah* duties. Therefore the slave gains nothing material from his servitude, since the master can always opt out of feeding him, while the wife does enjoy a concrete financial benefit from marriage that her husband cannot unilaterally withdraw.

Rabbi Meir responds that freedom is nonetheless not an unqualified benefit. *Ve-harei hu posel et avdo min ha-terumah* — a slave owned by a *kohen* may eat *terumah*, whereas a freed slave may not. This represents a concrete loss. Rabbi Meir's argument appears to be not that in the aggregate it is better to be a slave, but that the moment any element of concrete harm exists, the act cannot be performed on someone's behalf without their knowledge. The *chakhamim* counter that the slave is simply the master's property — *mipnei she-hu kinyano* — and this particular loss is subsumed within the nature of that ownership relationship rather than constituting a free-standing injury that would block the principle of *zachin*.

## Tofes L'Ba'al Chov

### Rav Huna Analogy

While Rabbi Yirmiyah dozes, his students Rav Huna and Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef continue the discussion. Rav Huna proposes an inference: *shma mina midirabbanan* — from the position of the *chakhamim*, who hold that the *shaliach* may take the *shtar shichrur* on behalf of the slave even before the master has fully resolved to complete the transfer, we may infer that *ha-tofes le-ba'al chov bimkom she-chav le-acherim kana* — one who seizes property on behalf of a creditor, even in a case where doing so harms third parties, acquires it validly on the creditor's behalf.

The analogy requires unpacking. In a classic application of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav*, the person acting on behalf of another does so entirely in accordance with the principal's will — no one is being overridden. The case of the *shtar shichrur* is subtler, because the master, as the *mishna* shows, wants to retain the ability to retract. When the *shaliach* takes possession of the document on behalf of the slave, he is in some sense jumping ahead before the master is fully ready to release his property. This gives the case a flavor of *tofes le-ba'al chov*, seizing something on behalf of someone even while the current owner is not entirely prepared to let go. Rav Huna reasons that if this works in the *mishna*'s case, then seizing property for a creditor must also work even when other parties are harmed in the process.

### Tosafot Objection

*Tosafot* raises a sharp objection to this analogy: in the case of seizing property for a creditor, there is a genuine underlying debt — the debtor truly owes the money. But the master does not owe the slave his freedom. What basis is there for treating the *shaliach*'s taking possession of the *shtar shichrur* as analogous to collecting a genuine debt? *Tosafot* grapples with this and suggests that perhaps the *gemara* understands the master's expressed will to free the slave as creating some form of obligation or entitlement, even if imperfect — enough to provide at least a partial analogy. Crucially, the act of saying "deliver this *shtar shichrur*" has already occurred; the master has expressed his desire, and the *shaliach*'s taking possession can be understood as the execution of that expressed will rather than an act hostile to it.

### Rav Yochanan vs Yosef

Rav Yirmiyah wakes up and tells his students: *dardakei* — children — and reports the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan: *ha-tofes le-ba'al chov bimkom she-chav le-acherim lo kana*. When seizing property for a creditor causes harm to third-party creditors, the seizure does not take effect. Rav Yosef bar Yosef had said it does work even in such a case; Rabbi Yochanan rules it does not.

The *gemara* then needs to salvage the ruling of the *mishna*: if seizing property at the expense of others does not work, how can the *shaliach* validly take possession of the *shtar shichrur* on behalf of the slave when the master is not yet ready to release it?

### When One Says Give

The *gemara* answers with a fundamental principle: *kol ha-omer tnu k'omer zachi dami* — when one says "give this to so-and-so," that formulation is legally equivalent to saying "take possession of it for him right now." By handing the document to the *shaliach* with the instruction to deliver it, the master is implicitly authorizing the *shaliach* to acquire it on the slave's behalf at that very moment. The act is therefore not against the master's will — we interpret his instruction as immediate authorization. This removes the case entirely from the category of *tofes le-ba'al chov*, since there is no seizure against anyone's will. The *shaliach* is not grabbing property ahead of a reluctant owner; the owner, by saying "give it," has already consented.

This principle also explains why the husband can still retract in the case of the *get*. Even if we say the master is prepared for the transfer to be immediate, the question remains whether the *shaliach* is authorized to represent the *other* side — the wife. The answer is no, because *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* does not apply where the act might constitute a detriment to the recipient. The master's readiness to proceed does not overcome the requirement that the wife be represented only by someone she herself has appointed.

The *gemara* then considers whether the principle of *tofes le-ba'al chov* can be derived from the *mishna* in *Pe'ah*: one who harvests *pe'ah* on behalf of a specific poor person — *mi she-likat et ha-pe'ah ve-amar harei zeh le-ploni ani*. Rabbi Eliezer holds the acquisition is valid; the *chakhamim* hold the harvested *pe'ah* must be given to the first poor person who arrives. This seems to map onto the *tofes le-ba'al chov* debate, but Amemar (or Rav Pappa) resists the equation. Rabbi Eliezer may permit the *pe'ah* case for a specific reason: *demigo de-ei ba'ei mafkir le-nichsei ve-havei ani ve-chazi leh* — since the harvester could himself renounce all his property, become poor, and thereby be personally entitled to the *pe'ah*, he may acquire it for someone else. This logic does not extend to the case of a genuine creditor seizing the debtor's assets, where the seizing party has no independent entitlement to the property. Conversely, the *chakhamim* who prohibit in the *pe'ah* case may rely on the specific verse *lo telaket* — you shall not harvest for the poor — which creates a particular exclusion not applicable to the general *tofes* question. The two debates cannot be straightforwardly equated.

## Master's Obligation

### No Obligation Inference

With the *tofes* discussion concluded, the *gemara* returns to the substantive question raised by the *mishna*: does a master have a legal obligation to feed his slave? The *mishna* stated that a master may refuse to feed his slave — *she-im ratza shelo lazon et avdo rashai*. The natural inference is *shma mina*: *yachol ha-rav lomar le-eved asei imi ve-eini zonecha* — the master may say "work for me and I will not feed you."

### Counter: Must Feed

The *gemara* resists this inference. *Hacha be-mai askinan* — the *mishna* is not saying there is no obligation. Rather, the case is one where the master says *tze ma'asei yadecha bi-mezonotecha* — "go use your own labor to feed yourself." The master does have a legal obligation to feed the slave, but he may discharge that obligation indirectly: rather than providing food himself, he may waive his claim to the slave's labor for the purpose of allowing the slave to earn his own food. The master cannot demand the slave's labor and simultaneously refuse to feed him; but if he releases the labor, the feeding obligation is effectively transferred.

### Wife vs Slave

A difficulty arises immediately: the same logic seems applicable to a wife. A husband could equally say *tze ma'asei yadecha le-mezonotecha* — earn your own living — and thereby avoid his obligation to feed her. If so, why is the wife's situation treated as superior to the slave's?

The *gemara* answers: *isha de-la safka* — the wife's situation is categorically different because we can easily imagine a case where she cannot earn enough to support herself. In that event, the husband must make up the difference. His obligation is not merely to release her labor; it is to ensure she is actually fed, regardless of her earning capacity. Therefore marriage does confer a genuine financial benefit: if she cannot earn her keep, he is obligated to supplement.

### Slave Without Means

What about a slave who similarly cannot earn his own keep? Here the *gemara* responds sharply: *avda de-nahom karsei lo shavya le-marei le-martei le-mai mitvabei* — a slave who cannot earn the food his stomach requires, what use is he to his master? The practical implication, as Rashi explains, is that there is no legal obligation to make up the difference for a slave who cannot earn his own upkeep. Since the entire framework of the master-slave relationship is premised on the slave's productive labor, where that labor does not cover even basic sustenance, the master has no obligation to supplement. The *gemara* does not provide a positive rationale grounded in a *pasuk* or contractual principle; it simply asserts the distinction.

The ethical weight of this position is not lost. The moment a slave becomes ill or incapacitated — precisely when he most needs support — the master appears to have no legal obligation to provide it. The *gemara* arrives at this conclusion without adverting to a verse or a contract, which the lecturer flags as notable.

### Cities of Refuge

The *gemara* now adduces further evidence from a *baraita* concerning a slave who has accidentally killed someone and fled to one of the *arei miklat*, the cities of refuge. The *baraita* states: *ein rabo chayav le-zono* — the master has no obligation to feed him there; *ve-lo od, she-ma'aseh yadav le-rabo* — moreover, if the slave can perform labor while in the city of refuge, his earnings belong to the master. This seems to resolve the question directly: no feeding obligation, yet full entitlement to earnings.

### Slave's Own Labor

The *gemara* again refuses to read this at face value. The master's lack of obligation to feed means he has arranged that the slave feeds himself from his own earnings — *tze ma'asei yadecha le-mezonotecha*. The slave's earnings that "go to the master" refer only to the surplus beyond what the slave needs for food. On its face this is a very forced reading of the *baraita*, which seems simply to say "don't feed him, keep his earnings." The *gemara* acknowledges this by asking: if the slave keeps his own earnings for food, and any surplus goes to the master, why does the *baraita* need to specify that the surplus goes to the master — is this not obvious? The *gemara* answers: *mahu deteima* — one might have thought that since the master has no obligation to make up the difference when the slave falls short, the slave should be entitled to accumulate a rainy-day reserve from his surplus earnings against future days when he cannot earn. The *baraita* therefore tells us this is not the case: the arrangement is strictly day-to-day, and any surplus belongs to the master.

The *gemara* further explains why the *baraita* specifically chose the scenario of *arei miklat*: one might have thought that the verse commanding the fugitive to *live* in the city of refuge — *venas el achat he-arim ha-el ve-chai* — creates a heightened obligation to ensure the slave's sustenance there. The *baraita* teaches that even in that context, no such obligation exists.

The subsequent discussion in the *gemara* involves a *baraita* that contrasts the slave with a wife who has fled to a city of refuge: *ba'alah chayav bi-mezonoteiha* — her husband remains obligated to feed her. The *gemara* navigates the inference carefully: since the wife-case explicitly requires feeding only where no special arrangement was made, the slave-case must also be read as involving an arrangement (*tze ma'asei yadecha*) and not as a flat declaration of no obligation. The *gemara* then adds a final nuance: even where a wife could theoretically earn enough to feed herself, one might think her dignity prevents us from requiring her to hustle for work — *kol kevuda bat melech penima*. The *gemara* rules that we do in fact require her to earn what she can, and only the gap beyond what she can reasonably earn must be made up by the husband. 

Throughout this extended passage, the *gemara* consistently refuses to accept the plain reading of texts that seem to say the master has no obligation to feed his slave, insisting instead that an obligation exists — while never grounding that obligation explicitly in a verse or formal legal category. The lecturer notes this is a striking feature of the *sugya*.

## Sanctifying Labor

### Hedesh Case

The *gemara* now turns to a specific case that illuminates the question from a different angle. Rav rules: *ha-makdish yedei avdo* — if a master declares his slave's labor *hekdesh*, sanctified to the Temple, *oto eved loveh ve-ochel ve-oto poreia* — the slave borrows money to eat and repays from his labor.

The scenario is this: the master has sanctified the slave's labor, meaning whatever the slave earns passes automatically to *hekdesh*. The master, not wishing to feed the slave out of his own pocket now that he is deriving no direct benefit from the labor, refuses to provide food. But the slave's own earnings also go to *hekdesh* and cannot be used for food. The solution the *gemara* describes is that the slave borrows money, purchases food with the borrowed funds, and then repays the loan from his subsequent earnings.

The question is how the earnings can go toward repaying the loan if they are *hekdesh* the moment they are earned. The *gemara* explains the mechanism: the repayment occurs in increments smaller than a *prutah*. Since *hekdesh* acquires rights only when an amount reaches the threshold of a *prutah*, each sub-*prutah* increment is transferred to the creditor before *hekdesh*'s claim attaches. *Hekdesh* never actually gets hold of those amounts, and the creditor is paid through this continuous micro-transfer. *Hekdesh* itself consents to this arrangement because a healthier, better-fed slave earns more — *ki heichi d'lishvach* — so *hekdesh*'s own interests are served.

### Borrowing to Eat

A closer look at this case, however, reveals a tension. The *gemara* argues that the master is entitled to sanctify the slave's labor only because he is in fact feeding the slave — *hacha be-mai askinan be-ma'aleh lo mezonot*. But if the master is feeding the slave, why does the slave need to borrow money to eat?

The *gemara* answers: *le-hadafah* — for the extra. The master is providing the bare minimum of food, just sufficient to ground his legal entitlement to the slave's labor. The slave, however, wants to eat more — or needs more for his health and productivity. It is for this additional food beyond the bare minimum that he must borrow. The *hekdesh* arrangement, with its sub-*prutah* mechanism, applies only to the repayment of this supplemental borrowing.

### Rav's Position

This reading is confirmed by Rav's own language elsewhere: *amar Rav ha-makdish yedei avdo, oto eved oseh ve-ochel* — the slave works and eats. The slave, in Rav's formulation, is entitled to eat from his labor. Rav's underlying principle is that if the master is not feeding the slave, he is not entitled to the slave's labor. If he has sanctified the labor while refusing to feed the slave, the sanctification is invalid and the slave is entitled to work and feed himself directly.

### Bare Minimum Feeding

The *gemara* draws these threads together. In the case where the sanctification is valid — the master is feeding the slave — the master provides the bare minimum, the slave may borrow for additional food and repay from earnings via the sub-*prutah* mechanism, and *hekdesh* acquiesces because the slave's productivity benefits it. In the case Rav addresses where the sanctification is invalid, the master was not feeding the slave, and therefore he never had the entitlement to sanctify the labor in the first place. The *gemara* affirms: *shma mina eino yachol* — one may not demand a slave's labor while refusing to feed him. This is Rav's clear ruling.

## Rabbi Yochanan Dissent

### Severing Hand

Against Rav's position, the *gemara* introduces a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan. If someone severs the hand of another person's slave, Rabbi Yochanan rules: *noten shevet ve-refuah le-rabo* — the tortfeasor pays *shevet* and *refuah* to the master. *Shevet* is the compensation for lost labor during the period of incapacitation — not the permanent diminishment of the slave's value, which is addressed by the main injury payment, but the specific loss of labor during the recovery period: work the slave could have done, such as light tasks not requiring the severed hand, that he did not do because he was hospitalized. *Refuah* is the payment for medical treatment.

### Charity Food

The remarkable element of Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is the next clause: *ve-oto eved nizon min ha-tzedaka* — and the slave himself is fed from charity. The master is collecting *shevet* payments representing the slave's foregone labor, yet the slave's food during the recovery period comes not from the master but from communal charity funds.

### No Feeding Required

According to Rav's position, this is difficult: if the master is entitled to the slave's labor — here in the form of *shevet* — he should be obligated to feed the slave. The *gemara* draws the inference explicitly: *shma mina* — according to Rabbi Yochanan, *yachol ha-rav lomar le-eved asei imi ve-eini zonecha* — the master may say "work for me and I will not feed you." Rabbi Yochanan and Rav disagree directly: Rav holds that entitlement to the slave's labor is conditional on feeding him; Rabbi Yochanan holds that the master may collect the benefit of the slave's labor without any corresponding obligation to provide food.

The *gemara* attempts to harmonize Rabbi Yochanan's ruling with the position that a feeding obligation exists. Perhaps *nizon min ha-tzedaka* means the master is providing the bare minimum and charity supplements it — *le-hadafah*. But the *gemara* rejects this: *nizon* specifically denotes the bare minimum subsistence level, whereas *mitparnes* would indicate something above the minimum. The word *nizon min ha-tzedaka* means that even the bare minimum is coming from charity, not from the master. The inference stands: *shma mina* that according to Rabbi Yochanan, the master has no obligation to feed his slave even while collecting the proceeds of the slave's labor.

The lecturer observes that this may reflect a broader geographic or institutional disagreement — Rav, the Babylonian authority, insisting on the feeding obligation; Rabbi Yochanan, the Palestinian *amora*, permitting its waiver.

### Doctor Bills

The *gemara* closes with a final question about the *refuah* payment. If the tortfeasor must pay *refuah* to the master, why? The medical bills are incurred for the slave's treatment and would seem to belong to the slave so he can pay his doctors. The *gemara* explains with a specific case: the doctors assessed that recovery would take five days and payment was made accordingly, but through more aggressive treatment — harder on the slave, but more effective — he recovered in three days. Two days of medical expenses were saved. Who keeps those two days of savings?

One might think the slave is entitled to them, since it was his suffering — *tza'ara didei hu* — that produced the savings. The *gemara* rules otherwise: *kamashma lan* — just as the master is entitled to the fruit of the slave's labor generally, he is entitled to the savings realized through the slave's endurance of harder treatment. The doctor-bill savings, like the *shevet* payments, belong to the master.

With this, the *sugya* of *asei imi ve-eini zonecha* concludes: Rav holds that a master may not demand a slave's labor while refusing to feed him; Rabbi Yochanan holds that there is no legal obligation to feed a slave, and the master may collect *shevet* while leaving the slave to charity. In either case — whether because the master need not feed at all, or because he may release the slave's labor in lieu of feeding — the *chakhamim*'s position that manumission is an unqualified benefit for the slave stands. The slave is never in a position where his status as owned property guarantees him sustenance, and therefore the act of freeing him involves no element of harm that would require the slave's own presence or consent.
