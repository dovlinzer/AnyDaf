# Nedarim 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dairy Products

### Milk & Whey Dispute

The *mishna* at the bottom of 51b opens with the case of one who takes a *neder* against *chalav* — milk. The ruling is *muttar b'kum*: the *kum*, the whey produced during the cheese- or yogurt-making process, is not included in the *neder* and remains permitted. Whey is the liquid that separates out when milk curdles, distinct from the protein solids that become cheese. *Rabbi Yose oser* — Rabbi Yose forbids the whey as well.

The way the Bavli understands this *machloket* is not as a technical dispute over how to classify whey in relation to milk, but rather as a question of *lashon bnei adam* — the vernacular usage of ordinary people — which is the animating principle throughout *Nedarim*. What do people mean when they say "milk"? The Yerushalmi, by contrast, appears to treat this as a genuine *machloket* between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yose over the ontological nature of whey — whether it truly qualifies as milk. Worth noting as well: when a person says he is refraining from milk, he does not mean he is refraining from *milchik* broadly. The *neder* tracks the word, not the category.

### Lashon Bnei Adam

The entire framework of *nedarim* follows *lashon bnei adam* — what the speaker's words mean in ordinary usage. Points that might otherwise appear to be technical classification disputes turn out, at least in the Bavli's reading, to be more superficial questions about how people actually use certain words in their locale. The *mishna* thus encodes a sensitivity to lived linguistic practice rather than formal taxonomic reasoning.

There is an interesting tension worth pausing on. The *gemara* in *Shabbos* makes clear that the cheese-making process involves *mechabetz* — the curdling that separates solids from liquid — which is a *toladah* of *borer*, a derivative of the *melacha* of separating a mixture. That framing suggests that *halacha* conceives of milk as already a mixture of cheese solids and whey together. Yet our *mishna* treats whey as a new substance, distinct from the milk out of which it came. Reconciling these two positions is a genuine question that deserves further attention.

### Reciprocal Cases

The *mishna* continues with the reciprocal cases. If a person takes a *neder* against *kum* — whey — he is *muttar b'chalav*: even Rabbi Yose would agree that a *neder* against whey does not include milk. This is also noteworthy because it implies that the curdling process produces two genuinely new substances. Otherwise one might argue that since milk is simply a mixture of curds and whey, drinking milk always involves consuming whey, and a *neder* against whey would thus apply to milk as well. The *mishna*'s permissive ruling reflects the view that whey, once separated, is a new and distinct entity.

### Abba Shaul & Cheese

Abba Shaul adds: *ha-noder min ha-gevina asur bah bein melucha u'tefeila* — one who takes a *neder* against cheese is forbidden from eating it whether it is salted or unsalted. The novelty of the ruling is that one might have thought the term *gevina* in ordinary usage referred specifically to salted cheese, the more common form, and that unsalted cheese might not be included. Abba Shaul teaches that even unsalted cheese is called *gevina* — there is no other adequate word for it — and therefore both fall within the scope of the *neder*. This parallels other cases throughout the *mishna* where different preparation methods raise questions about which items are included in a given *neder* term.

## Meat & Flavor

### Meat/Gravy Rule

The *mishna* continues: *ha-noder min ha-basar muttar b'rotev* — one who takes a *neder* against meat is permitted to eat the gravy — *u'v'kippa*, and also the cracklings or drippings. The *rotev* is a gravy or sauce that carries the flavor of the meat and may contain small fragments of it, but is not itself a piece of meat. The *kippah*, according to the *mefaresh*, refers to a thick residue — drippings or compressed matter that accumulates during meat preparation, something like what falls to the bottom of the pan. Since the person's *neder* was against meat — a steak, a chicken leg, a discrete piece — the gravy and drippings are not included.

This point opens onto a major discussion in the *Ran* — which we will return to shortly — concerning *davar she-yesh lo matirin*, *bittul*, *min b'mino*, and *min b'she'eino mino*.

### Rabbi Yose Opinion

*Rabbi Yose oser* — Rabbi Yose forbids the gravy and cracklings as well. His reasoning is that the gravy contains pieces of meat and carries the *ta'am* — the flavor — of the meat, and therefore it falls within the scope of the *neder*.

### Rabbi Tarfon Case

Rabbi Yehuda adds a proof narrative: *ma'aseh v'asar aleinu Rabbi Tarfon b'veitzim she-nitbashlu imo* — there was an incident in which Rabbi Tarfon forbade eggs that had been cooked together with the meat to those who had taken a *neder* against meat. The scenario resembles a Sephardi-style stew where eggs are cooked directly in the meat pot and absorb its flavor. Rabbi Tarfon was *machmir* that even an egg cooked in a meat stew is considered meat for the purpose of *nedarim*. This serves as a *kal v'chomer* proof for Rabbi Yehuda's position that the gravy and *kippah* should similarly be forbidden.

The questioners responded: *amru lo kein hadavar — aval eimatai? Bizman she-yomar basar zeh alay* — granted, but under what circumstances does this apply? Only when the person specified *this* meat — *basar zeh*. The principle is: *she-ha-noder min ha-davar v'nitarev b'acher im yesh bo b'noten ta'am asur* — if one took a *neder* against something and it then became mixed with something else, it is forbidden only if there is enough of it to impart a *ta'am*, a flavor. The principle of *ta'am k'ikar* — that flavor carries the legal status of the substance itself — is a foundational concept in *kashrut* and *issur v'heter* broadly, and this passage is among its significant sources.

### Noten Ta'am Scope

The later *tannaim* thus clarify Rabbi Tarfon's position: *noten ta'am* applies only when the *neder* was articulated with *zeh* — "this" — as in *basar zeh*, this specific piece of meat. If the person simply said *basar* — meat in general — then even if a dish carries the full flavor of meat, it is not included. The logic is that the *neder* against a specific item causes that item's status to travel into whatever it subsequently flavors. A *neder* against meat in general, by contrast, is about the substance "meat" as a category and does not extend to *noten ta'am*.

This same framework applies to the question of whether funds from items made *asur* by a *neder* are themselves *asur*. If a person declared his entire purse *asur* to someone, would every subsequent coin spent in the marketplace carry that *issur*? The *gemara* implies that for consumable foods, *bittul* may apply — the prohibited item can be nullified in a mixture — a point that will be developed through the *machloket* between Rashi and the *Ran*.

## Wine & Flavor

### Wine Dishes

*Ha-noder min ha-yayin* — if one takes a *neder* against wine, *muttar b'tavshil she-yesh bo ta'am yayin* — he is permitted to eat a cooked dish prepared with wine, even one where the wine is a significant ingredient and its flavor is clearly detectable. Wine in a beef stew or a wine-braised dish is not "drinking wine" — it is eating a stew. The person who vowed against wine meant the wine itself, not every dish in which wine played a role. This stands in instructive contrast to the meat case: meat's *noten ta'am* is invoked only when the person said *basar zeh*, whereas with wine, ordinary *lashon* is already sufficient to exclude wine-flavored dishes.

It is worth noting as well the distinction between a *neder* against wine and a *neder neziroot*. A *nazir* acquires a comprehensive personal status — the prohibitions of *tameh meis*, grape products, and haircut are all bound together as dimensions of a single identity. Saying "I will be a *nazir* from wine" would actually create a full *nezirut*. But a *neder* against wine is simply a *neder* against wine, with no further extensions.

### This Wine

*Amar konam yayin zeh she'eini to'em* — if one said, "this wine I will not taste," and it subsequently fell into a dish, then *im yesh bo ta'am yayin harei zeh assur* — if it imparts a flavor to the dish, the dish is forbidden. The same *noten ta'am* principle that operates with *basar zeh* now operates with *yayin zeh*. Specifying *zeh* — this particular wine — causes that wine's prohibition to travel wherever its flavor travels.

A question arises regarding the precise role of the phrase *she'eini to'em* — "I will not taste" — as distinct from *zeh*. Why does the *mishna* formulate the wine case as *yayin zeh she'eini to'em* while the meat case uses *basar zeh alai*? One might distinguish between *issurei hana'ah* and *issurei achilah*, but this thread will be resolved more precisely through the *sugya* of Rami bar Chama, to be addressed below.

### Stam Yeinam

*Stam yeinam* refers to wine produced or handled by non-Jews, which is not necessarily wine used for *avodah zarah* but which carries a rabbinic prohibition extending from the concern over *yayin nesech* — libation wine. This prohibition may also be related to broader concerns about social mingling and the avoidance of close relationships that could lead to intermarriage, placing *stam yeinam* in the same family of rabbinic enactments as *bishul akum* and *pas akum*.

For purposes of *bittul*, *stam yeinam* follows a lower threshold than standard prohibited substances: it is *batel b'shisha* — nullified one-to-six — rather than the standard *batel b'shishim*. At one-in-six, the wine would certainly still be *noten ta'am*, so the ratio itself is set at a level that includes the *ta'am* threshold.

### Nine Days Custom

A related practical question concerns the *minhag* of *Ashkenazim* not to eat meat during the nine days before *Tisha B'Av*. What exactly does that *minhag* encompass? The *gemara* speaks of reducing *simcha* during *shavua she-chal bo* — the week in which *Tisha B'Av* falls — but the *Ashkenazi* community has extended and formalized the practice. This raises several questions: Does the *minhag* apply to chicken as well as beef? The *Magen Avraham* in *Shulchan Aruch* takes a stringent position that extends the restriction broadly. What about a dish that is technically *fleishig* — prepared with chicken broth — but contains no pieces of meat and has no detectable *ta'am* of meat? And what about grape juice, which carries the full *halachic* status of wine? These questions flow naturally from the *sugya*'s distinctions between the substance of a prohibited item and the flavor or derivative status it can impart.

## Meat & Flavor (II)

### Ran's Framework

Before turning to the *Ran*'s full conceptual analysis, it is worth establishing the question he is responding to. The *mishna* appears to assume that items made *asur* through a *neder* can in principle become *batel* in a mixture — that the usual rules of *bittul* apply. This assumption is what the *Ran* examines, because it intersects with the principle of *davar she-yesh lo matirin* and with the distinct phenomenon of *min b'mino*, each of which represents a case where *bittul* ordinarily does not operate.

---

## Davar She-Yesh Matirin

### Definition

*Davar she-yesh lo matirin* refers to something that can or will become permitted later. The general rule in *hilchot bittul* is that such an item cannot be nullified in a mixture. Even if the prohibited item is outnumbered sixty-to-one by the permitted substance it has fallen into, the fact that it will eventually become permitted on its own means we do not allow the mechanism of *bittul* to render it permitted now.

### Rambam Examples

The Rambam cites *chadash* — new grain — as a paradigmatic example. *Chadash* is prohibited *d'oraisa* until after the *omer* sacrifice is brought on the second day of *Pesach* (or, in the absence of the Temple, until that date passes). Since *chadash* will become permissible automatically after that date, it is *davar she-yesh lo matirin*, and a piece of *chadash* bread that falls into a basket of *yashan* — old-grain — flour cannot be nullified by the majority. The same logic could theoretically extend to *chametz* on *Pesach*: since *chametz* becomes fully permitted once *Pesach* ends, it too would appear to qualify as *davar she-yesh lo matirin*.

### Rashi Position

Rashi in *Beitzah* 3a explains the underlying rationale directly: since the prohibited item will eventually become permitted on its own, there is no need to rely on *bittul* — one should simply wait. *Bittul* is a mechanism for dealing with prohibitions that are inherently and permanently *asur*. Where the *issur* is time-bound, waiting is the appropriate response, and the Torah did not grant the mechanism of *bittul* for such cases.

### Mordechai View

The Mordechai takes issue with applying *davar she-yesh lo matirin* to *chametz* after *Pesach*. His argument is that *chametz* never truly becomes unconditionally *mutar*: every year it becomes *asur* again on *Pesach*. Its status is not simply "prohibited now, permitted later" but rather an ongoing cycle of alternating permissibility. Because of this vacillating nature, *chametz* does not fit the clean conceptual definition of *davar she-yesh lo matirin*, and so it should not be governed by that principle. The rabbinic prohibition on *chametz she-avar alav ha-Pesach* — *chametz* owned by a Jew over *Pesach*, which remains forbidden even after *Pesach* as a *knas* — is a further complication. That prohibition is *miderabbanan*, imposed as a penalty for failing to eliminate *chametz* before *Pesach*; it does not reflect the intrinsic nature of the substance.

### Ran Position

The *Ran* here mounts a fundamental challenge to Rashi's rationale. If the entire logic of *davar she-yesh lo matirin* is simply that one should wait for it to become permitted, then *nedarim* should also qualify as *davar she-yesh lo matirin* — one can always do *sheilah*, asking a *chacham* to be *matir* the *neder*. Yet our *mishna* clearly implies that items made *asur* through a *neder* can become *batel* in a mixture. This contradicts Rashi's explanation.

The *Ran* therefore offers a different conceptual framework, anchoring the principle of *davar she-yesh lo matirin* in the distinct phenomenon of *min b'mino*. The *gemara* in *Menachos* establishes that *min b'mino* — a substance mixed with something of the same kind — is not subject to *bittul*, deriving this from the *Yom Kippur* Temple service: even though the blood of the *par* (bull) vastly outnumbers the blood of the *se'ir* (goat), the *Torah* continues to reference each separately after they are mixed. The reason *min b'mino* is not *batel* is conceptual: *bittul* works through conflict — an *asur* item and a *mutar* item are sufficiently different that one can override the other. But when two items are of the same type, there is no conflict; they are too similar to contradict each other, and therefore neither nullifies the other.

The *Ran* extends this logic to *davar she-yesh lo matirin*: an item that will become *mutar* is not genuinely "in conflict" with the *mutar* substance surrounding it. A truly *asur* item — one that is permanently prohibited — exists in genuine tension with its *mutar* surroundings, which is precisely what allows *bittul* to operate. But *davar she-yesh lo matirin*, which is not a *davar ha-asur* in any absolute sense since it will become permitted, cannot be said to "conflict" with the permitted mixture in the way required for *bittul* to apply. The item's pending permissibility removes it from the category of genuine *issurim* that *bittul* is designed to address.

## Lentils Contradiction

### Lentil Problem

The *gemara* raises a *riminhu* — a contradiction — against what we have just seen in the *mishna*. A *braita* states: *min ha-adashim* — if one took a *neder* against lentils — *asur ba-ashishim* — he is also forbidden from *ashishim*. The *mefaresh* understands *ashishim* as some form of preparation made from lentil flour mixed with other flour and baked into bread, or perhaps lentils prepared with honey — a preparation that has undergone a meaningful transformation from plain lentils. The intuition that the transformation should matter — just as whey is permitted to one who took a *neder* against milk — would suggest *ashishim* should be permitted. Yet the *braita* forbids them.

The contradiction runs in both directions. The *Tanna Kamma* of our *mishna* permits whey to one who vows against milk, yet the *Tanna Kamma* of this *braita* forbids *ashishim* to one who vows against lentils. And Rabbi Yose, who forbids whey, here permits *ashishim*. Neither position is internally consistent on its face.

### Mar Ke-Atreih

The *gemara* resolves this with a principle already encountered earlier in the tractate: *mar ke-atreih mar ke-atreih* — each follows the vernacular of his place. The resolution is deliberately anti-theoretical. Do not attempt to construct a comprehensive conceptual account of why Rabbi Yose holds as he does in one case and differently in another, or why the *Tanna Kamma* takes the positions it does. The disagreements are not about ontology or classification at all — they are entirely about local usage. In one locale, the word for a thing extends to cover its derivatives; in another locale, it does not. The *halakha* of *nedarim* follows what words mean to the people who speak them.

### Milk Whey Terminology

The *gemara* makes this concrete: *be-atra de-rabbanan* — in the place of the Rabbis, meaning the *Tanna Kamma* — people call milk *chalba* and whey *kuma*. They are simply different words for different things. *Be-atra de-Rabbi Yose* — in Rabbi Yose's locale — whey is not called *kuma* but *kuma de-chalba*, "milk-whey." The word for milk is embedded in the very name of the whey. Consequently, when someone in Rabbi Yose's place takes a *neder* against *chalav*, the word *chalav* is heard in the name of the whey as well, and so it is included in the *neder*.

This is *lashon bnei adam* operating at a hyperlocal level. Not only do we follow how people generally speak — we follow how people speak *in a particular place*. The entire *machloket* dissolves into a question of regional terminology. The *braita* about lentils and *ashishim* follows the same logic: in the locale of the *Tanna Kamma* of that *braita*, *ashishim* is still called by a name close enough to lentils that it falls within the *neder*; in Rabbi Yose's locale, it does not.

## Grapes & Olives

### Grapes/Olives Rule

The *mishna* now turns to fruit: *ha-noder min ha-anavim muttar b'yayin* — one who takes a *neder* against grapes is permitted to drink wine. *Min ha-zeitim muttar b'shamen* — one who takes a *neder* against olives is permitted to use olive oil. The transformation from grape to wine, and from olive to olive oil, is sufficiently complete that wine is not thought of as grapes and oil is not thought of as olives. The process of producing wine does not merely extract something that was already inside the grape; it creates a new substance.

### Seven Mashkim

*Halacha* singles out wine and olive oil as two of the seven *mashkim* — the seven liquids that carry *halachic* significance, particularly in the laws of *tumah v'taharah*. The seven are wine, oil, blood, water, milk, dew, and *dvash* (bee honey). Only these seven liquids can render food susceptible to *tumah* or become *tameh* in the relevant ways. Other fruit juices — orange juice, for instance — do not carry this status. They are not considered *mashkim* in the *halachic* sense; from the *Torah*'s perspective they remain, as it were, extensions of the fruit itself rather than independent liquids.

This special status of wine and oil is also reflected in the distinct *brachot* recited over them. Wine receives *borei pri ha-gafen* and oil, in certain contexts, receives its own designation — in recognition of the transformation from raw fruit to processed liquid. Other juices, by contrast, take the same *bracha* as the fruit from which they came.

### These Grapes/Olives

*Amar konam zeitim va-anavim eilu she'eini to'em asur ba-hem u-va-yotzei ba-hem* — if one said "these olives, these grapes, I will not taste them," then not only the fruits themselves but also *kol ha-yotzei mei-hem* — whatever is produced from them — is forbidden. Just as *basar zeh* causes the meat's status to travel into whatever it flavors, and *yayin zeh* causes the wine's status to travel into whatever dish it enters, so too *eilu* — these specific fruits — causes the prohibition to extend to whatever emerges from them, including the wine pressed from them or the oil extracted from them.

But this applies only when one specifies *zeh* or *eilu* — "this" or "these." If one simply said *anavim* — grapes — without specification, the wine remains permitted.

### Rami Bar Chama Q

This generates a pointed question from Rami bar Chama: *eilu davka o she'eini to'em davka?* — Is it the word *eilu* — "these" — that is the operative element, or is it the phrase *she'eini to'em* — "I will not taste them"? The two elements appear together in the *mishna*'s formulation, and it is unclear which one is doing the legal work.

If it is *eilu davka*, then *she'eini to'em* should be superfluous — one could simply say "these grapes are *asur* to me" without adding "I will not taste them." Conversely, if it is *she'eini to'em* that is operative, then the word *eilu* is what requires explanation. Rami bar Chama's question sets up the *sugya* that follows, which will work through several attempted proofs before arriving at Rava's resolution.

## Fish Brine Case

### Rabbi Yehuda Baraita

The *gemara* revisits the *baraita* of Rabbi Yehuda with an eye toward Rami bar Chama's question. As established earlier, Rabbi Yehuda's proof from Rabbi Tarfon's case was qualified by the response that *noten ta'am* applies only when one says *basar zeh alai* — "this meat is forbidden to me" — after which everything that meat flavors is also forbidden. The *sugya* now attempts to leverage this *baraita* to determine whether the key variable is *zeh* specifically or *she'eini to'em* specifically.

### Fish Mishna

The *gemara* cites the *mishna* concerning *dagim* — fish. One who says *she'eini to'em dagim* — "I will not taste fish" — is forbidden from fish of all sizes and preparation methods, whether large or small, raw or cooked. The *mishna* also states *u'mutar b'tzir* — he is permitted the *tzir*, the brine or liquid that comes out of the fish. This appears to be a case involving *yotzei mehen* — the liquid produced by the prohibited item — and it is invoked here to try to answer Rami bar Chama's question. Notably, the *mishna* uses *she'eini to'em* without specifying *dag zeh* — "this fish" — which seems to suggest that *she'eini to'em* alone is sufficient.

### Rava Resolution

Rava intervenes to block this inference. The *tzir* that is *mutar*, he says, is only *kvar yatza mehen* — brine that had already come out of the fish before the *neder* was made. That pre-existing brine was never part of the fish at the moment of the *neder* and so was never included in its scope. But *tzir* produced from the fish *after* the *neder* — that is genuinely *yotzei mehen*, liquid emerging from a *davar ha-asur*, and would be prohibited. Rava's reading prevents the fish case from serving as direct proof one way or the other about the primacy of *she'eini to'em*.

### She'eini To'em

Despite Rava's qualification, the *gemara* ultimately resolves that *she'eini to'em* — "I will not taste" — is what generates the *issur* on *yotzei mehen*, not the specification of *zeh*. The fish *mishna* uses *she'eini to'em* without *dag zeh*, yet the post-*neder tzir* is still included. This demonstrates that when a person frames the *neder* in terms of tasting — "I will not taste X" — the prohibition extends to whatever emerges from X afterward, because whatever comes out of it also carries the taste he has foresworn. The word *zeh*, by contrast, serves a different function: it ties the prohibition to a specific object and its subsequent *noten ta'am* in other foods, rather than extending to *yotzei mehen*. The two formulations thus generate distinct *halachic* consequences, and Rami bar Chama's question is answered accordingly.

## Dates & Winter Grapes

### Dates/Date Honey

The *mishna* at the top of 53a turns to dates. *Ha-noder min ha-temarim muttar b'dvash temarim* — one who takes a *neder* against dates is permitted to eat date honey. The inverse is also stated: *min ha-dvash* — one who takes a *neder* against date honey — is permitted to eat dates. This contrasts with the grapes and wine case in an instructive way. Wine and olive oil are among the seven *mashkim*, halakhically independent liquids; date honey is not. It does not constitute a *mashkeh* in the technical sense — it is more properly understood as the syrup or extracted juice of the date rather than a transformed independent substance. The *neder* against dates, then, does not include date honey because date honey is simply a separate food, not an extension of the date in the way wine is an extension of the grape.

The question of whether *dvash* in the *Torah* refers to bee honey or date honey — with significant *nafka minas* for the prohibition on bringing honey as an offering and for the description of *Eretz Yisrael* as *zavat chalav u'dvash* — is a distinct and important topic. For the laws of the seven *mashkim*, the *dvash* that counts is *dvash devorim* — bee honey — not date honey, as established in *Masechet Machshirin*.

### Winter Grapes

*Istavnit* — winter grapes, or more precisely *istevimit* — are grapes that remain on the vine through autumn, either because they are too hard to ripen properly or because they are deliberately left late to develop a high acidity. The product made from them is something resembling vinegar — a very sour pressed liquid with a high content of malic and citric acids, similar to a *verjuice*. Alternatively, the reference may be to grapes left so long they become overly sweet, after which wine and then vinegar is made from them. One who takes a *neder* against winter grapes is *muttar b'chometz stavnit* — permitted to consume the vinegar made from them.

### Edible Principle

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar formulates a general principle: *kol she-darko le-ekhol ve-derekh ha-yotzeh mimenu le-ekhol* — anything that is itself customarily eaten and whose extracted liquid is also customarily eaten — such as dates and date honey — if one takes a *neder* against it, one is also forbidden from what comes out of it. And if one takes a *neder* against what comes out of it, one is forbidden from the item itself. The bidirectional inclusion follows from the fact that both the fruit and its liquid are regarded as edible foods in their own right, and neither is so distinct from the other as to escape the *neder*.

### Inedible Principle

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's principle has a second half: *kol she-ein darko le-ekhol ve-derekh ha-yotzeh mimenu le-ekhol* — anything not customarily eaten on its own, whose extracted liquid *is* customarily consumed — *nadar bo ein assur ela be-yotzeh mimenu*: if one takes a *neder* against such a thing, only what comes out of it is forbidden, because *she-lo nitkaven zeh ela le-yotzeh mimenu* — one's intention was only for the edible product it yields, not the inedible item itself.

Winter grapes appear to be precisely such a case, according to the Steinsaltz reading of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's position. Since the grapes themselves are not normally eaten, one who takes a *neder* against them had in mind only the vinegar or sour liquid they produce. Accordingly, the liquid is forbidden, but the grapes themselves remain *mutar*, even though the *lashon* of the *neder* named the grapes — because the person had no genuine intent toward the inedible fruit, only toward its edible derivative. This may place Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in tension with the earlier *mishna* that stated *ha-noder min ha-anavim muttar b'yayin*, or it may be that he simply excludes grapes and olives from his principle, treating their wine and oil as categorically different by virtue of their status among the seven *mashkim*. The *sugya* will continue into 53a.
