# Arakhin 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Trumpets & Instru…

### Opening Remarks

Daf 10 opens with the *mishna*, and the shift is immediately palpable — after the demanding material of the previous daf, involving *pitchei nida* and the calendar calculations of months, we now enter considerably more accessible and, as will become clear, remarkably relevant terrain. The topics of this *mishna* touch on questions that remain very much alive in Jewish practice today.

### Trumpet Blasts Range

The *mishna* opens: *Ein pochtin me-esrim v'achas teki'os ba-mikdash* — there would be no fewer than twenty-one trumpet blasts in the *mikdash*, and *v'lo mosifin al arba'im u-shmonah* — no more than forty-eight. These are not *shofar* blasts but *chatzotzrot* blasts, the priestly trumpets sounded in connection with the Temple service.

### Seven Sets Structure

To understand the range, one must recognize that every blast comes in a set of three: *tekiah*, *teruah*, *tekiah*. The minimum of twenty-one blasts thus represents seven such sets, and the maximum of forty-eight represents sixteen sets. What are the standard seven? One set accompanies the opening of the gates at the start of the day. Three sets are sounded at the *tamid shel shachar* — the morning daily offering — specifically during the three pauses in the Levitical song. And three sets accompany the *tamid shel bein ha-arbayim*, the afternoon daily offering. Those seven sets, representing twenty-one blasts, constitute the baseline for any ordinary day in the *mikdash*.

### Additional Days & Occasi…

The number expands on special occasions. On *Erev Shabbat*, two additional sets are sounded: one to signal the approaching *Shabbat* and give people in the fields time to cease their activities and return, and one at the precise onset of *Shabbat* itself — bringing the total to nine sets. On a *Yom Tov* with a *korban musaf*, three more sets are added, reaching twelve. Notably, when *Erev Shabbat* coincides with *Yom Tov*, those two additional *Erev Shabbat* blasts are still sounded, since work must cease before *Shabbat* even when *Yom Tov* immediately precedes it.

The maximum of sixteen sets — forty-eight blasts — is reached on *Sukkot*, where the special ritual of *nisuch ha-mayim*, the water libation, was accompanied by additional blasts: one when the upper gate was opened for the procession descending from the *Beit Hamikdash* to draw the water, one when the lower gate was opened, one when the water was drawn, and one when it was poured upon the *mizbeiach*. Those four extra sets, added to the twelve of an *Erev Shabbat Yom Tov*, yield sixteen sets on *Erev Shabbat* of *Sukkot* — forty-eight blasts in total. This is spelled out in the *mishna* in *Sukkah*.

### Harps & Flutes Numbers

The *mishna* continues with the musical instruments that accompanied the Levitical song. *Ein pochtin mishnei nevalim v'lo mosifin al shisha* — there would be no fewer than two lyres and no more than six. *Rashi* notes that the reason for these limits is not explained, and he candidly says *lo nitparesh ta'ama* — he does not know why. *Tosafot* likewise offers nothing, which is itself striking.

Regarding the flute: *ein pochtin mishnei chalilin v'lo mosifin al shneim asar* — no fewer than two flutes and no more than twelve. *Rashi* explains that the upper limit of twelve corresponds to the twelve special days per year on which the flute was played — which the *mishna* is about to enumerate. *Rashi* holds that the flute was played exclusively on those twelve days, and on no others. The *Rambam* implies a different reading: that flutes were played on other days as well, but perhaps on these twelve days the flute played in a solo capacity to distinguish them.

### Twelve Days of Flute

The *mishna* states: *u-vishneim asar yom ba-shanah ha-chalil makeh lifnei ha-mizbeiach* — the flute would play before the altar on twelve special days of the year. *Rashi* notes that the word *makeh* — literally "strikes" — refers to the tapping of the fingers over the holes of the flute, and hence the playing of the flute is described this way.

### Twelve Days Listed

What are the twelve days? *Shechitat ha-pesach rishon v'shechitat ha-pesach sheini* — the bringing of the *korban pesach* on the fourteenth of *Nisan* (*pesach rishon*) and on the fourteenth of *Iyar* (*pesach sheini*), *v'yom tov ha-rishon shel pesach* — the first day of *Pesach*, *u-v'yom tov shel atzeret* — *Shavuot*, and *u-vishmonat yemei ha-chag* — the eight days of *Sukkot*. That is eight days for the *shalosh regalim* plus two occasions of bringing the *korban pesach*, totaling twelve.

### Flute Days = Hallel Days

Bracketing the two days of *korban pesach* for a moment, the remaining ten days — the first day of *Pesach*, *Shavuot*, and all eight days of *Sukkot* — immediately call to mind the days on which *Hallel* is recited. The question that will drive the *Gemara*'s discussion is why only the first day of *Pesach* and all eight days of *Sukkot*, rather than every day of *Pesach* as well. The *Gemara* will make this connection explicit: the days on which the flute is played correspond to the days on which *Hallel* is recited — and the *Gemara* will analyze that correspondence in depth.

### Flute Materials & Players

The *mishna* adds that they would not use a copper flute — *v'lo haya makeh be-avuv shel nechoshet* — but rather a reed flute — *ella be-avuv shel kaneh* — because the reed produced a more beautiful, mellifluous sound. The *mishna* also states *v'lo haya machlit ella be-avuv yachidi*: the conclusion of the flute playing — the coda, as it were, the moment that brought closure to the musical passage — would be done with a solo flute. Although twelve flutes might be playing together, the final phrase was left to a single instrument alone, *mipnei she-hu machlit yafeh* — because it made for a beautiful ending.

### Musicians' Status

Who actually played the musical instruments? The *mishna*'s answer is striking: *v'avdei kohanim hayu* — they were the slaves of the *kohanim*. The primary mode of the Levitical musical service was, after all, *ikar shirah b'peh* — the song itself, which was vocal — and the instrumental accompaniment was not limited to *Levi'im*. That the slaves of the *kohanim* performed this function is surprising. While slaves of *kohanim* are considered part of the priestly household in certain respects — they eat *terumah*, for instance — one would not ordinarily imagine them playing a role inside the sacred precincts.

The *mishna* also mentions two Israelite families, from the places referred to as Pagrim and Tzipara, who came from the town of Ammaus. These families were of distinguished *yichus*, such that *kohanim* would marry their daughters — *mesi'in li-khuna*. The precise significance of this detail is worth noting: the point may simply be that they were families of recognized lineage and standing, whose proximity to the *kohanim* made their presence in the *mikdash* in a musical role intelligible — or it may be that the marital connection to *kohanim* gave them a more formal association with the priestly household. These two readings carry different weight: the former takes *mesi'in li-khuna* as merely a testimony to their *yichus*, while the latter treats it as the operative rationale for their participation.

### Rabbi Chanina: Levites

*Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos* offers a different position entirely: *Levi'im hayu* — the musicians were *Levi'im*. This is a simple factual assertion that pushes back on the assumption that it was non-*Levi'im* who filled this role. The question of who actually performed the instrumental music in the *Beit Hamikdash* thus remains genuinely contested, and one might wonder whether archaeology has shed any light on the matter.

## Tekiah-Teruah-Tekiah Unit

### Mishna vs. Rabbi Yehuda

The *Gemara* opens its analysis with the observation that *masnisin d'lo ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — the counting method of our *mishna*, which enumerates the blasts individually as twenty-one to forty-eight, does not follow the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*. A *baraita* records that *Rabbi Yehuda omer ha-pochet lo yifchos mi-sheva ve-ha-mosif lo yosif al shesh esrei* — the minimum is seven and the maximum is sixteen. This is in fact the same range as the *mishna*, except *Rabbi Yehuda* counts each set of *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* as a single unit rather than three.

### One Unit vs. Three Units

The conceptual dispute, then, is whether *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* constitutes one indivisible unit or three separate units. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds *tekiah teruah u-tekiah chada hi* — it is one unit. The *Rabbanan* hold *tekiah l'chudei u-teruah l'chudei ve-tekiah l'chudei* — each of the three is distinct.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Proofs

What is *Rabbi Yehuda*'s reasoning? He draws on the grammatical tension in two *pesukim*. One verse says *u-tekatem teruah* — using the verb of *tekiah* to describe the act of *teruah*. Another verse says *teruah yitka'u* — using the verb of *tekiah* with *teruah* as its subject. The mixing of the verb associated with one sound with the noun of another, *Rabbi Yehuda* argues, reveals that these sounds are conceptually unified. If they were truly separate units, each would carry its own distinct verbal form. The intermingling of verb and noun across the two types of blasts demonstrates that *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* is a single composite unit — one sound with distinct internal components.

### Rabbanan's Proofs

The *Rabbanan* counter with a verse describing the gathering of the camp: *u-ve-hak-hil et ha-kahal titke'u ve-lo tari'u* — when assembling the congregation, one sounds a *tekiah* without a *teruah*. This shows that a *tekiah* can stand entirely alone, independent of a *teruah*. And if a *tekiah* can exist as a self-sufficient act, it must be considered a distinct unit. The *Rabbanan* further derive from the interplay of the same two verses that a *tekiah* precedes every *teruah* and a *tekiah* follows it — but they insist this is structural information about the sequence, not proof that the three constitute a single unit.

Why, the *Rabbanan* argue, would the Torah ever command performing half of a unit? *De-i salka dachtatich tekiah teruah u-tekiah chada hi, amar Rachmana avid palga de-mitzvah* — if *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* were one indivisible unit, then the Torah, by directing a *tekiah* alone for the gathering of the camp, would be instructing the performance of only half a unit.

### Yehuda's Reply

*Rabbi Yehuda* responds: *simana b'alma hu* — the lone *tekiah* for gathering the camp is not a formal ritual act at all. It is merely a signal, the equivalent of an announcement. Because it is not a halakhic sound in the full ritual sense, the fact that a *tekiah* can be used alone in that context does not prove that a *tekiah* is a self-contained halakhic unit. Only where we are dealing with something ritualized — a formally constituted act, not a loudspeaker — does the full composite of *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* apply, and there it is one unit.

### No Interruption Rule

The *Gemara* now turns to practical implications. The question is raised: *ke-man azla ha de-amar Rav Kahana* — whose position does *Rav Kahana*'s statement follow?

### Rav Kahana's Statement

*Rav Kahana* states: *ein bein tekiah le-teruah ve-lo klum* — there cannot be even the smallest interruption between *tekiah* and *teruah*. They must flow one into the other without any pause whatsoever — essentially in a single breath. The *Gemara* identifies this as following *Rabbi Yehuda*: if *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* is one unit, one sound, then any break within it is a disruption of the sound itself.

### Rabbi Yochanan Rosh Hash…

The *Gemara* then asks: is *Rav Kahana*'s statement not self-evident? Of course it follows *Rabbi Yehuda*. The answer is that it comes to exclude the position of *Rabbi Yochanan*, who said regarding *Rosh Hashana* that *shama tisha tekiyot be-tisha sha'ot ba-yom yatza* — if one heard the nine required blasts of *shofar* spread across nine different hours of the day, one has fulfilled the obligation.

### Rejection of Yochanan

One might have thought that *Rav Kahana*'s only intent was to rule out hearing the blasts hours apart — that is, to exclude *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position — while still allowing for a brief pause between *tekiah* and *teruah* within a single sequence. The *Gemara* rejects this: the phrase *ve-lo klum* — absolutely nothing — is too strong to allow even the slightest interruption. It establishes that the sounds must be literally continuous, not merely in proximity.

### Independent vs. Group Bl…

This analysis yields three distinct positions. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* is one unit — one sound — and there can be no interruption whatsoever between its components. According to the *Rabbanan*, the three are distinct units, but they form a group: they must cohere, there must be some proximity, though one may pause to take a breath between them. And the most extreme position, that of *Rabbi Yochanan* regarding *Rosh Hashana*, treats the nine blasts as completely independent sounds, such that one could hear them spread across nine different hours of the day and still fulfill the obligation. The *Rabbanan* clearly reject this final position. This same conceptual issue — whether the sequence is one sound, a group, or entirely independent units — applies equally to the trumpet blasts in the *Beit Hamikdash* as to the *shofar* on *Rosh Hashana*.

## Flute-Hallel Connection

### Gomair Hallel Principle

The *Gemara* now provides its analysis of why the flute is played specifically on these twelve days. *Hoil ve-yachid gomer bahen et ha-Hallel* — because it is on these days that an individual completes — *gomer* — the full *Hallel*. Both words in this phrase deserve attention and will generate important discussion shortly.

### Eighteen Hallel Days

*Rabbi Yochanan* says in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak*: there are eighteen days in the year on which an individual completes the *Hallel* — *shmonah yemei ha-chag*, the eight days of *Sukkot*, already mentioned in our *mishna*; *shmonah yemei Chanukah*, eight days of *Chanukah*; *yom tov ha-rishon shel Pesach*, the first day of *Pesach*; and *yom tov shel atzeret*, *Shavuot*. That yields eighteen.

The reason *Chanukah* appears here but not in our *mishna* is straightforward: the *mishna* deals with the special playing of instruments in connection with *korbanot*, and *Chanukah* has no special *korban* associated with it. The days of full *Hallel* and the days of the flute thus overlap substantially but do not map onto each other perfectly. There are also two days that the *mishna* includes — the bringing of the *korban pesach rishon* and *pesach sheini* — that do not appear in *Rabbi Yochanan*'s list, because the *Hallel* on those days was tied entirely to the act of bringing the *korban*. Without the *korban*, there is no corresponding *Hallel* obligation on those dates, and so those days have no relevance outside the *Beit Hamikdash*.

### Diaspora: Twenty-one Days

Outside of Israel, where second days of *Yom Tov* are observed, the number of full *Hallel* days rises to twenty-one: nine days of *Sukkot*, eight days of *Chanukah*, two days of *Pesach*, and two days of *Shavuot* — three additional days for the three *regalim* bringing the total from eighteen to twenty-one.

### Succos vs. Pesach

The *Gemara* now takes up the central question that the *mishna* already prompted: *mai shna de-chag d'amrinan kol yoma ve-yoma u-mai shna de-Pesach de-lo amrinan* — why is *Hallel* said every single day of *Sukkot*, while on *Pesach* it is said only the first day?

### Midrash: Egyptians Drown…

Before turning to the *Gemara*'s answer, it is worth noting the explanation most commonly taught: the *Midrash* records that when the Egyptians were drowning in the *Yam Suf*, the angels wished to sing *shira*, and God replied, *ma'aseh yadai tove'im ba-yam ve-atem omrim shira* — My creations are drowning in the sea and you would sing song? The argument then proceeds that if one does not say full *Hallel* on the seventh day of *Pesach* because of the Egyptian deaths, one certainly does not say it on *Chol Hamoed*, which is less distinguished than the seventh day itself. This *Midrash* raises its own conceptual questions — notably, *Bnei Yisrael* did sing *shira* at the sea. The point may be that we, removed from the direct experience of salvation, are positioned more like the angels than like the generation that witnessed it firsthand. But in any event, this is not what the *Gemara* says.

### Distinct Korbanot Logic

The *Gemara*'s answer is halakhic in character: *kol yoma de-chag chalukin be-korbanoteihem* — every day of *Sukkot* is distinct in its *korbanot*, since the number of bulls brought as *musaf* decreases each day — thirteen, twelve, eleven, and so forth, down through seven. *Pesach ein chalukin be-korbanoteihen* — on *Pesach*, the *korban musaf* is identical every day. Since there is no distinction among the days of *Pesach* as expressed in the *korbanot*, there is nothing to mark each day as a separate unit.

This answer, however, requires careful handling when applied to our practice today, since we no longer bring *korbanot*. One position would be that our *Hallel* is essentially a *zecher*, a memorial act linked to the *Hallel* that was once said in connection with the *korbanot*. That is, however, a position one would generally resist — we normally understand our *Hallel* as a response to the *kedusha* and significance of the day itself, not merely as a commemoration of a Temple-era practice. And in any case, such an explanation could not account for *Chanukah*, where full *Hallel* is said without any *korban* connection whatsoever. The more satisfying reading is that the difference in *korbanot* is a *siman* — an indicator of a deeper distinction — rather than the cause in itself.

### Pesach Unity vs. Succos…

What is that deeper distinction? *Pesach* was a process, a narrative. The *geulah* began with *Yetziat Mitzrayim* and culminated in *Kriat Yam Suf*. The entire seven-day period is one connected story — each day leads into the next, and the whole constitutes a single arc of redemption. On *Sukkot*, by contrast, the commemoration is *ki ba-sukkot hoshavti et Bnei Yisrael* — that God caused the Jewish people to dwell in *sukkot*. Sitting in a *sukkah* could have been commemorated on any single day of the year. There is no narrative that binds day two to day one, no progression by which each subsequent day is intelligible only in light of what preceded it. Therefore, if one wishes to observe *Sukkot* for eight days, each day stands as an independent commemoration. Each day of *Sukkot* is a separate unit; the entirety of *Pesach* is one unit.

### Bracha Wording Evidence

This distinction is not merely theoretical — it manifests in *halacha*. On *Shabbat Chol Hamoed Sukkot*, when the *haftarah* is concluded, one mentions *ve-al yom chag ha-Sukkot hazeh* — the specific day of *Sukkot* is acknowledged in the *beracha*. On *Shabbat Chol Hamoed Pesach*, however, one says only *ve-al yom ha-Shabbat hazeh* — the day of *Pesach* is not mentioned alongside it, because on *Pesach* the individual day does not carry a distinctive identity of its own. The day is part of *Pesach* as a whole, not a separately identifiable occasion. Similarly, a *she-hecheyanu* is recited on *Shemini Atzeret* — the last day of *Sukkot* — but not on the last day of *Pesach*, precisely because on *Sukkot* each day is a distinct unit warranting its own recognition, while *Pesach* is conceived as a single extended occasion.

### Yachid Gomair Phrase

Returning now to the phrase in the *Gemara* — *yachid gomer et ha-Hallel* — two aspects deserve attention. First, the word *gomer*, "completes," implies that on other days there is some partial form of *Hallel* being said. *Tosafot* is uncertain on this point. There are *Gemarot* that indicate a practice of saying a *chatzi Hallel* — an abbreviated *Hallel*, skipping certain sections — on days such as *Rosh Chodesh* and *Chol Hamoed Pesach*, though *Tosafot* raises the possibility that the *minhag* of saying even the partial *Hallel* on those days developed later and was not yet implied by this statement.

### Half-Hallel Bracha Dispu…

Second, the word *yachid* — "individual" — raises the question of whether there is a difference between an individual saying *Hallel* and a congregation saying *Hallel*. This has implications for whether one says *Hallel* at all on those lesser days when alone, and whether a *beracha* is recited on *chatzi Hallel*. The *beracha* on *chatzi Hallel* is a major debate. If the *minhag* to say it is later than the Talmudic period, and possibly not even directed by the *rabbanim* in a binding way, the question of making a *beracha* on a *minhag* becomes central.

In practice, *Ashkenazim* do recite a *beracha* on *chatzi Hallel*. When *Sefardim* say a full *Hallel*, the *beracha* is *ligmor et ha-Hallel*, echoing the language of *gomer et ha-Hallel*. When saying *chatzi Hallel*, *Sefardim* recite no *beracha* at all, and moreover say it only *be-tzibbur* — as a congregation — not in private. The *Ashkenazic* practice of reciting a *beracha* even when saying *chatzi Hallel* in private follows *Rabbeinu Tam*'s position, and the *pesak* is that even one saying it alone recites the *beracha* — though one should be aware that there are authoritative opinions to the contrary.

## Why Hallel: Kedusha & Mi…

### Shabbat Objection

The *Gemara* now presses further: *Shabbat d'chaluka be-korbanoseiha leimra* — *Shabbat* has distinct *korbanot* just as *Sukkot* does; why not say *Hallel* on *Shabbat*?

### Shabbat Not Moed

The answer: *lo ikri moed* — *Shabbat* is not called a *moed* in the manner of *Yom Tov*. Before one can say *Hallel*, the day must qualify as a *moed*.

### Soloveitchik Distinction

This raises a natural question: if *Hallel* is a response to *kedushat hayom*, is not the *kedusha* of *Shabbat* higher than that of *Yom Tov*? The *Rav* — *Rav Soloveitchik* — offers an elegant distinction here. He observes that on *Shabbat*, we welcome the *kedusha* into our space — we perform *Kabbalat Shabbat*, as though inviting God's sanctity into our week. On *Yom Tov*, particularly understood through the lens of *aliyah la-regel*, it is we who enter God's space. The absence of a *Kabbalat Yom Tov* parallel to *Kabbalat Shabbat* is telling. However different the qualities of *kedusha* may be, *Yom Tov* carries a more acute sense of directly standing before God, of entering a sacred realm — and perhaps that is what the *Gemara* means by *moed*, the sense of a special appointed encounter that is by its nature less regular than *Shabbat*.

### Rosh Chodesh: No Melacha…

The *Gemara* continues: *Rosh Chodesh d'ikri moed leima* — *Rosh Chodesh* is also called a *moed*, so why not say *Hallel* on *Rosh Chodesh*? The answer: *lo ikadesh be-asiyat melacha* — *Rosh Chodesh* was not sanctified through the prohibition of *melacha*. There is no formal *issur melacha* on *Rosh Chodesh*.

### Song & Kedusha Link

The source for this linkage between *Hallel* and the prohibition of *melacha* is a verse: *ha-shir yihiyeh lachem ke-leil hitkadesh chag* — the song shall be for you like the night sanctified for the festival. *Laila ha-mikudash le-chag taun shirah* — a night sanctified through the *chag*, which means sanctified through the prohibition of *melacha*, requires *shira*. *She-eino mekudash le-chag ein taun shirah* — when there is no such sanctification, *shira* is not required. This is a notably technical formulation — nothing here about miracles, history, or themes. The operative criterion is *kedushah* expressed through *issur melacha* in the context of a *moed*.

### Rosh Hashana & Yom Kippur

The *Gemara* presses further: *Rosh Hashana ve-Yom ha-Kippurim d'ikrei moed*, and *d'itkadesh be-asiyat melacha* — both *Rosh Hashana* and *Yom Kippur* are called *moed*, and both carry a prohibition of *melacha*. Why not say *Hallel* on those days?

### Midrash: Books of Life

The *Gemara* answers with a statement of *Rabbi Abbahu*: the ministering angels asked God, *mipnei mah ein Yisrael omrim shirah lefanecha be-Rosh Hashana u-ve-Yom ha-Kippurim* — why do Israel not sing before You on *Rosh Hashana* and *Yom Kippur*? God replied: *efshar melech yoshev al kisei ha-din, ve-sifrei chayim ve-sifrei meitim ptuchim lefanav, ve-Yisrael omrim shirah* — is it possible that the King sits upon the seat of judgment, with the books of life and death open before Him, and Israel should be singing?

### Lack of Simcha

What does this *Midrash* tell us? One reading is that *Hallel* is ultimately linked to *simchat haregel*, the mitzvah of *simcha* on *Yom Tov* — *ve-samachta be-chagecha* — and the anxiety of *Rosh Hashana* and *Yom Kippur* precludes the kind of *simcha* that *Hallel* requires. The *Gemara*, it should be noted, did not invoke *simcha* in its earlier reasoning. One possibility is that the *Gemara* is now adding a further dimension: even given the *moed* and *issur melacha*, there must also be *simcha*, and where there is profound concern about divine judgment, that *simcha* cannot be fully realized.

### Simcha on Rosh Hashana

This *Midrash* gives rise to an interesting dispute among the *Rishonim* and *Poskim*: is there a *mitzvah* of *simcha* on *Rosh Hashana* at all? One practical expression of this debate is whether it is permitted to fast on *Rosh Hashana*. Some permit it; others hold it is *assur* because there is a *mitzvah* of *simcha* that one would thereby be violating. A famous story is told of the *Beit Halevi* — *Rav Chaim Brisker*'s father — who said: "People call rabbis strict; I am a great *meikel*! Those who say one may not fast on *Rosh Hashana* — I say one may!"

### Ashkenazi vs. Sephardi

The tension is also visible in the divergence between *Ashkenazic* and *Sefardic* practice on *Rosh Hashana*. For *Ashkenazim*, the dominant tone is *eimas ha-din* — awe and fear before the divine judgment. The query of the angels — *heikha shira* — resonates deeply with this sensibility. For *Sefardim*, the *simcha* dimension is more prominent. The two approaches need not be seen as contradictory. Another *Midrash* captures the balance: God tells the angels that while an ordinary person coming before a judge dresses in mourning clothes to demonstrate humility, *Bnei Yisrael* — *lovshei levanim* — dress in white and come before God with confidence and joy. The confidence that one will be found meritorious does not eliminate all gravity; it means one does not go so far as to sing *Hallel*, which would be, in the language of the *Gemara*, going too far.

*Tosafot* notes that the *Midrash* asks why *Israel* does not sing — not why *we* do not sing — implying that the angels themselves do sing *Hallel* on *Rosh Hashana* and *Yom Kippur*. He cites a *piyut* unique to these days: *v'hachayot yishoreru u-cheruvim yefaeru* — the *chayot* will burst into song and the *cheruvim* will offer praise. Some questioned whether this *piyut* should be recited since it would imply that the angels do sing, but *Tosafot* affirms that indeed they do — the prohibition is on *Israel*, not on the heavenly host.

### Chanukah: Miracle Basis

The *Gemara* now addresses *Chanukah*: *la-Chanukah d'lo hachi ve-lo hachi ve-ka'amar* — *Chanukah* has none of these qualifications: it is not a *Yom Tov*, there is no *issur melacha*, it is not a *moed* in the formal sense. Why then is *Hallel* recited on *Chanukah*? *Ah, mishum nisa* — because of the miracle. This is a remarkable moment in the *Gemara*, because up until this point the entire discussion has proceeded without invoking the concept of miracle at all. The answer for *Chanukah* introduces the *nes* as a separate, independent basis for *Hallel* — and *Chanukah* in particular has the additional dimension of *pirsumei nisa*, the proclamation of the miracle, which makes the recitation of *Hallel* especially apt.

### Purim: Why No Hallel

The obvious next question: *Purim d'ika nisa leima* — *Purim* also has a miracle, so why not say *Hallel* on *Purim*?

### Outside Eretz Yisrael Lo…

*Rabbi Yitzchak* answers: *lefi she-eino omrim shira al nes she-be-chutz la-Aretz* — because one does not say *shira* over a miracle that occurred outside *Eretz Yisrael*. The miracle of *Purim* lacked the eternal significance that would warrant *Hallel*.

### Objection: Exodus Outside

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* immediately challenges this: *harei Yetziat Mitzrayim de-nes she-be-chutz la-Aretz hu* — the Exodus from Egypt was also a miracle that occurred outside *Eretz Yisrael*, and we do say *Hallel* for it. The same could be said for the wandering commemorated by *Sukkot*.

It should be noted here that the most natural response — that *Hallel* on *Pesach* has nothing to do with miracles and everything to do with *kedushat ha-yom*, as the entire earlier discussion established — is conspicuously not given. The *Gemara* at this point shifts into a mode where it treats the miracle as relevant to the question, which in turn creates tension with everything that preceded it. The *Gemara*'s handling of this is noteworthy.

### Before vs. After Entry

The *Gemara* answers with a *baraita*: *ad she-lo nichnesu Yisrael la-Aretz huchshru kol ha-aratzot lomar shirah* — before the Jewish people entered *Eretz Yisrael*, all lands were eligible, as it were, for the singing of *shira*. Since *Eretz Yisrael* had not yet assumed its full distinction over all other lands, miracles performed anywhere could warrant *shira*. But *mishe-nichnesu la-Aretz, lo huchshru kol ha-aratzot lomar shirah* — once they entered the land, events outside of *Eretz Yisrael* lost that status. The miracle of *Purim* occurred after the entry into the land, during *galut*, and is therefore subject to this diminishment.

### Megilla = Hallel

*Rav Nachman* offers an alternative approach: *kriotah zu hi Hallel* — the reading of the *Megillah* itself functions as the *Hallel* for *Purim*. The reading of the *Megillah* constitutes a highly articulate form of *pirsumei nisa* — a verbal proclamation of the miracle — and therefore serves the purpose that *Hallel* would otherwise serve. The *Meiri* draws out the practical implication: if one were in circumstances where the *Megillah* was unavailable, one would be obligated to say *Hallel* on *Purim* in its place.

### Rava: Still Subjects

*Rava* offers yet another explanation: *bishlama hatam halelu avdei Hashem ve-lo avdei Pharoh* — there, in the context of the Exodus and *Chanukah*, we sing *halelu avdei Hashem* — praise ye the servants of God — because the miracle brought about a real change in status. We are no longer the servants of *Pharoh*; the liberation was complete and lasting. But in the context of *Purim*: *halelu avdei Hashem ve-lo avdei Achashverosh* — praise ye servants of God and not servants of *Achashverosh*? That cannot honestly be said. *Akti avdei Achashverosh anan* — we are still subjects of *Achashverosh*. The miracle of *Purim* saved us from destruction but did not change our fundamental political condition. We remain in exile, subjects of foreign rulers.

### Chanukah Independence

By contrast, *Chanukah* represented a moment of genuine independence — the *Hasmoneans* threw off foreign domination and achieved actual sovereignty. The *nes* of *Chanukah* thus crossed the threshold where one can honestly say *halelu avdei Hashem ve-lo avdei* whoever came before. The *Purim* miracle, as wondrous as it was, left the Jewish people in the same unredeemed condition. In this sense, *Rava*'s answer converges thematically with the *chutz la-Aretz* reasoning: both point to a *nes* that was transformative but incomplete, a salvation embedded within the condition of *galut* rather than transcending it.

### Rav Nachman Reconciliati…

The *Gemara* reconciles *Rav Nachman*'s position — that reading the *Megillah* constitutes *Hallel* — with the *baraita* that states one does not say *shira* over a miracle in *chutz la-Aretz* after entering the land. *Rav Nachman* holds that we do in fact have an obligation to say some form of *Hallel* for *Purim*, fulfilled through the reading of the *Megillah*. The *baraita*'s restriction applies to the direct singing of *shira*; the *Megillah* reading satisfies the obligation through a different medium. He further argues that since the miracle of *Purim* occurred during *galut*, after *Churban ha-Bayit*, it falls under a different framework — *keivan she-galu chazru le-heteran ha-rishon* — returning, in a sense, to the pre-land status where miracles in *chutz la-Aretz* retain their significance. During the *galut* period, the *nes* in *chutz la-Aretz* was very much relevant and very much a reason for *Hallel*.

## Instruments: Materials &…

### Reed vs. Copper Flute

The *Gemara* turns to the practical details of the instruments. The *mishna* stated that a copper flute was not used — *lo haya makeh be-avuv shel nechoshet* — but only a reed flute, *be-avuv shel kaneh*, because the reed produced a superior sound.

### Chalil Etymology

*Rav Papa* observes that the *mishna* opens by calling the instrument a *chalil* and then shifts to calling it an *avuv*. He clarifies: *hainu chalil hainu avuv* — they are the same instrument. As for the name *chalil*, *Rashi* explains: *de-chali kalei* — because its sound is so sweet and mellifluous. The word *chalil* derives from an Aramaic term for sweetness.

### Ancient Reed Flute

A *baraita* elaborates on the instrument's history. *Abuv hayah ba-mikdash chalak* — the flute in the *mikdash* was smooth, *hayah dak* — thin, *shokana hayah* — made of reed, *u-mimot Moshe hayah* — and it had been passed down continuously from the time of *Moshe Rabbeinu* himself.

### Gold Plating Incident

At some point, a king — the identity is not specified — commanded that the flute be plated with gold to make it more beautiful. The result: *ve-lo hayah kolo areiv* — it no longer produced a pleasant sound. So *natlu et tzipuyo* — they removed the gold plating — *ve-hayah kolo areiv ke-mot she-hayah* — and the beautiful sound returned, just as it had been. The *drush* practically writes itself: the perfection of the original cannot be improved upon by later embellishment. What was fashioned from the time of *Moshe* had a quality that gilding could only diminish.

### Copper Cymbals History

*Tziltzal hayah ba-mikdash* — there were cymbals in the *mikdash*, *shel nechoshet hayah* — made of copper, *ve-hayah kolo areiv* — and they produced a beautiful sound. *Ve-nifgam* — at some point they were damaged. *Ve-shalkhu chakhamim ve-hevi'u umanin me-Aleksandrya shel Mitzrayim* — the Sages sent and brought master craftsmen from Alexandria in Egypt.

### Cymbals Repair Issue

*Ve-tiknuhu* — the artisans repaired the cymbals. *Ve-lo hayah kolo areiv* — but afterward the sound was no longer beautiful. So *natlu et tikuno* — they removed whatever the craftsmen had done — *ve-hayah kolo areiv ke-mot she-hayah* — and the beautiful sound was restored, exactly as it had been before.

### Incense Mortar

*Machteshet hayah ba-mikdash* — there was a mortar in the *mikdash* used *le-pitum ha-ketoret* — for grinding the spices of the incense — *shel nechoshet haytah, mimot Moshe haytah* — also of copper, also passed down from the time of *Moshe* — *ve-haytah mefattemet et ha-besamim* — and it would grind the fragrances effectively.

### Mortar Repair Failure

*Nitpagmah* — it was damaged. Again, *ve-hevi'u umanin me-Aleksandrya shel Mitzrayim* — the Alexandrian craftsmen were brought in — *ve-tiknuah* — and they repaired it. *Ve-lo haytah mefattemet ke-mot she-haytah* — but it no longer released the same quality of fragrance. The manner of grinding affects the release of aroma — much as crushing garlic one way yields a different flavor than crushing it another way — and the repair had altered something essential in the mortar's function. *Natlu et tikunah* — they removed the repair work — *ve-haytah mefattemet ke-mot she-haytah* — and it resumed working as it always had.

### Drush: Perfection Theme

All three episodes — the reed flute, the copper cymbals, and the incense mortar — follow the same pattern: an ancient vessel of surpassing quality, damage, an attempt at repair by the finest craftsmen of the age, failure of the repair to restore the original quality, and finally the removal of the repair to restore what was. The *drush* is rich. The perfection of what was fashioned in the time of *Moshe* cannot be replicated or improved by later human artistry, however skilled. The Alexandrian craftsmen were the best available — and still they could not match what was. There is something here about the irreducibility of the original, the impossibility of truly recovering through technical means what was once possessed organically, and the wisdom of recognizing when to simply restore rather than improve.
