# Pesachim 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Definition of Meat

### Meat Definition Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַנֶּאֱכָל בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל — יֵאָכֵל בִּגְדִי הָרַךְ, וְרָאשֵׁי כְנָפַיִם וְהַסְּחוּסִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Anything that is** fit to be **eaten in an adult ox,** whose bones have fully hardened, may **be eaten in a young kid.** One may register for a Paschal offering in order to eat any of these parts, and eating any such part is considered a fulfillment of the mitzva to eat the Paschal lamb. However, any part of the animal that is inedible in an adult ox is not considered meat, even if it is soft enough to be eaten in a young kid. One may not register for a Paschal offering in order to eat one of these parts, and eating it is not a fulfillment of the mitzva to eat the Paschal lamb. **And** the soft **ends of the ribs and the cartilage** are soft enough to be considered edible and may therefore be eaten from the Paschal lamb.

The daf opens with a continuation of the discussion surrounding the *korban pesach* — specifically, what constitutes *basar* (meat) for purposes both of fulfilling the *mitzvah* of eating the *korban* and of the *nosar* prohibition governing leftover meat. The preceding sugyot established that bones containing marrow follow the status of the marrow within them, and that various sinews — including the *gid hanasheh*, which one participant noted is more precisely a nerve (the sciatic nerve) than a sinew in the strict sense, though the term *gid* is used broadly for stringy tissue — had been under discussion. The present *mishnah* advances a formal and somewhat striking definition of what counts as *basar* in the context of a young animal offered as the *korban pesach*.

### Comparative Standard

The *mishnah* states: *kol hane'echal beshor gadol ye'achel begedi harach* — whatever would be edible in a large ox is likewise edible in a tender young kid. The inverse implication is equally significant: if a given tissue would *not* be edible in a fully grown ox, it does not qualify as *basar* even in the tender young animal, despite being edible in its current state. This is a formal, comparative standard — the edibility of the tissue is assessed not by reference to the animal being offered, but by reference to what its anatomical counterpart would yield in a large mature animal.

This is, on reflection, a quite surprising definition. One might have assumed that the relevant question is simply whether the tissue is edible now, at the time of eating the *korban*. Yet the *mishnah* imposes a standard that is partly external to the animal itself: a tissue that is perfectly edible in this young kid may nonetheless fail to qualify as *basar* if the corresponding part in a grown cow would be inedible. The consequence is apparently that one neither fulfills the *mitzvah* of eating the *korban pesach* through such tissue, nor would the tissue become *nosar* requiring burning if left over.

### Cartilage Analysis

The *mishnah* then includes *rashei knafayim* (the tops of the forelegs or shoulder joints — Rashi glosses this as *sheberosh gaf hakasef*) and *sachchusim* (cartilage, possibly of the ear or more generally) within the category of meat for these purposes. This inclusion is itself puzzling in light of the preceding standard: would the cartilage of an ear or the tip of a foreleg really be edible in a large ox? The apparent tension between the general rule and these specific inclusions drives the *gemara*'s discussion below.

### Cartilage Hardening

A note in the Artscroll edition illuminates an anatomical point that is crucial to understanding the *mishnah*'s logic: the skeleton of a young animal is composed largely of cartilage, much of which gradually hardens into bone as the animal matures. This means there is a broad class of tissues that are genuinely edible in a young lamb or kid offered as the *korban pesach* but which would, in the course of normal development, become hard bone — inedible and indeed breakable in the full sense. The formal definition of *basar* in the *mishnah* thus maps onto a real biological phenomenon: tissues that are presently soft and edible may have a future identity as bone. This transformation is what gives the comparative standard its practical bite, and it also clarifies why the prohibition of *sheviras etzem* (breaking bones) is not implicated by these tissues in their current state — they are not yet structurally bone.

## Mishnah Contradiction

### Rabba's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַבָּה רָמֵי, תְּנַן: כׇּל הַנֶּאֱכָל בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל — יֵאָכֵל בִּגְדִי הָרַךְ, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל — לֹא. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: רָאשֵׁי כְנָפַיִם וְהַסְּחוּסִים. וְהָא הָנֵי לָא מִתְאַכְלִי בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabba raised a contradiction: We learned** in the mishna that **anything that is** fit to be **eaten in an adult ox may be eaten in a young kid,** which indicates clearly that anything **that is not eaten** in an adult ox is **not** eaten even when it comes from a young kid. **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **The ends of the ribs and the cartilage** of the young kid may be eaten. **But these are not eaten in an adult ox,** because they have already become as hard as bone and are no longer edible.

Rabba identifies a sharp internal contradiction in the *mishnah*. The opening clause establishes: *kol hane'echal beshor gadol ye'achel begedi harach* — if it can be eaten in a large ox, it counts as meat in the young kid; if not, it does not. Yet the closing clause includes *rashei knafayim* and *sachchusim* as meat. The problem: *vehani lo mistachli beshor gadol* — these very tissues cannot be eaten in a large ox. The general rule and the specific inclusion appear to be in direct conflict.

### Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: כׇּל הַנֶּאֱכָל בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל — יֵאָכֵל בִּגְדִי הָרַךְ, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל — לֹא. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף רָאשֵׁי כְנָפַיִם וְהַסְּחוּסִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, it is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** and both opinions are mentioned in the mishna, **and this is what it is teaching: Anything eaten in an adult ox may be eaten in a young kid, and** anything **that is not eaten** in an adult ox is **not** eaten in a young kid. **And some say** that **even the ends of the ribs and the cartilage** are eaten from a young kid, because even these parts of an adult ox can be made edible through extensive cooking.

The *gemara*'s first resolution is striking: *ela tannai* — the two parts of the *mishnah* represent the opinions of two different *tannaim* that have been collated together without an explicit marker of disagreement. The *gemara* reconstructs the *mishnah* as follows: the first clause represents the majority view (*kol hane'echal beshor gadol ye'achel begedi harach, veshe'eino ne'echal lo*), while the second clause, beginning with *veyesh omrim* ("there are those who say"), represents a dissenting position holding that *rashei knafayim* and *sachchusim* count as meat even though they would not be edible in a large animal.

This solution — that Rebbi, in editing the *mishnah*, pasted together two opposing positions without clearly flagging the disagreement — is something the *gemara* generally resists. The editorial puzzle of why one would juxtapose contradictory rulings without a *veyesh omrim* or similar marker is worth noting, and indeed the *gemara* itself is somewhat uncomfortable with it. The more fundamental question underlying the debate is whether the criterion for *basar* is grounded in the tissue's present edibility or in some objective, forward-looking standard.

### Rava's Shalka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: ״מָה הֵן״ קָתָנֵי, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: כׇּל הַנֶּאֱכָל בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל בְּשִׁלְקָא — יֵאָכֵל בִּגְדִי הָרַךְ בְּצָלִי. וּמָה הֵן: רָאשֵׁי כְנָפַיִם וְהַסְּחוּסִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The mishna **teaches** employing the style: **What are they,** in which the mishna establishes a principle and then provides detail, **and this is what it is teaching: Anything that is** fit to be **eaten in an adult ox through cooking may be eaten in a young kid through roasting,** even if this part of an adult ox cannot be made edible through roasting. **And what are these** parts? They are **the ends of the ribs and the cartilage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: כׇּל הַנֶּאֱכָל בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל בְּשִׁלְקָא — יֵאָכֵל בִּגְדִי הָרַךְ בְּצָלִי, וּמָה הֵן: רָאשֵׁי כְנָפַיִם וְהַסְּחוּסִים, וְגִידִין הָרַכִּין נִידּוֹנִין כְּבָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava: Anything that is** fit to be **eaten in an adult ox through cooking may be eaten in a young kid through roasting; and what are these** parts? They are **the ends of the ribs and the cartilage; and the soft sinews are judged as meat.**

Rava offers a far more elegant resolution. He argues that there is no contradiction and no editorial patchwork: the *mishnah* is all one position, and a qualifying word has simply been omitted. The correct reading is *kol hane'echal beshor gadol beshalka* — whatever could be eaten in a large ox *if boiled* (*shalka*, or *beshaluk*, a form of thorough boiling that softens otherwise tough tissue) — that tissue *ye'echel begedi harach betzli*, may be counted as meat in the roasted young kid. And *mahein rashei knafayim vehasachchusim* — the specific examples are precisely the cartilaginous tissues that, though they could not be eaten raw or roasted in a large ox, *could* be eaten if thoroughly boiled.

This is a very satisfying resolution. The standard is not "edible in any state in a large ox" but "edible in the most favorable conceivable state — even boiled — in a large ox." The criterion for exclusion is therefore reserved for tissues that are so far beyond the pale of edibility that even boiling would not help. What exactly those would be in a large ox is left implicit, but the existence of such tissues is assumed. A *beraita* then confirms Rava's reading: *kol hane'echal beshor gadol beshalka ye'echel begedi harach betzali*, concluding with *u'mai hein — rashei knafayim vehasachchusim*. The *gemara* notes the philological curiosity that *shalka* is Aramaic whereas the *beraita* uses the Hebrew *beshaluk*, and also raises the broader question of whether this *beraita* is truly an independent tannaitic source or whether it so closely mirrors Rava's own language — the *beraita* appears nowhere else — that it may reflect the phenomenon whereby amoraic rulings, delivered in the *mishnaic* Hebrew register rather than Aramaic, were sometimes transmitted and received as independent *beraitot*.

The *mishnah* then adds that *gidim harachim* (soft sinews) also count as *basar*. The natural follow-up question is: what then is being excluded?

## Yochanan/Lakish Debate

### Hardening Sinews

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: גִּידִין שֶׁסּוֹפָן לְהַקְשׁוֹת, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: נִמְנִין עֲלֵיהֶן בַּפֶּסַח, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵין נִמְנִין עֲלֵיהֶן בַּפֶּסַח. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר נִמְנִין עֲלֵיהֶן — בָּתַר הַשְׁתָּא אָזְלִינַן. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר אֵין נִמְנִין עֲלֵיהֶן — בָּתַר בַּסּוֹף אָזְלִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the Sages disputed the issue of **sinews that will eventually harden** but are currently soft. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One may register for them in the Paschal lamb. Reish Lakish said: One may not register for them in the Paschal lamb.** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **one may register for them** in the Paschal lamb because **we follow the current** condition of the sinews; since they are edible in their current state, they are considered meat. **Reish Lakish said** that **one may not register for them** in the Paschal lamb because **we follow the eventual** condition of the sinews, and they eventually become inedible.

An independent discussion is now introduced with the marker *itamar*, signaling a teaching from a separate context — the same dispute is in fact recorded in tractate Chullin, where the question of what constitutes food or meat has ramifications for susceptibility to *tumah*. The subject is *gidim shesofam lehakshot* — sinews that will eventually harden. These seem to be precisely the category of soft tissue that the *mishnah* was navigating: presently edible, but destined for inedibility as the animal matures.

### Yochanan Position

Rabbi Yochanan holds: *nimnim aleihem bepesach* — one may count such sinews as one's portion in the *korban pesach*, meaning they qualify as *basar* and one can designate them as one's share of the offering. Since the *korban pesach* is communally purchased and eaten by a group, each participant has a designated portion; Rabbi Yochanan holds that soft sinews that will harden may constitute such a portion.

### Lakish Position

Reish Lakish disagrees: *ein nimnim aleihem bepesach* — one may not designate these sinews as one's portion. His reasoning is that the sinew's future state determines its present classification: since it will harden into inedibility, it is objectively not *basar*, even now. This position aligns with what appeared to be the plain sense of the *mishnah* — the determinant of *basar*-status is forward-looking.

On its face, Rabbi Yochanan's position seems intuitive: why should the future state of a tissue override its present edibility? Yet the *mishnah*'s comparative standard (*kol hane'echal beshor gadol*) seems to vindicate Reish Lakish.

### Yochanan's Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַנֶּאֱכָל בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל — יֵאָכֵל בִּגְדִי הָרַךְ, וּמָה הֵן רָאשֵׁי כְנָפַיִם וְהַסְּחוּסִים. הָנֵי אִין, אֲבָל גִּידִין שֶׁסּוֹפָן לְהַקְשׁוֹת — לָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּנָא הָנֵי וְהוּא הַדִּין לְהָנָךְ. הָנֵי מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּהָא מִתְאַכְלִי בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל בְּשִׁלְקָא, הָנָךְ נָמֵי מִתְאַכְלִי בְּשׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל בְּשִׁלְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish raised an objection to Rabbi Yoḥanan:** It was stated that **anything eaten in an adult ox may be eaten in a young kid, and what are these** parts? They are **the ends of the ribs and the cartilage.** This indicates that with regard to **these** items, **yes,** they may be eaten; **but** with regard to **sinews that will eventually harden, no,** they may not. This contradicts the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: It taught these,** the ends of the ribs and the cartilage, **and the same is true for those,** i.e., the same *halakha* applies to sinews that will eventually harden. **What is the reason** that people may register for **these,** the ends of the ribs and the cartilage? It is **because they are eaten in an adult ox through cooking. These, too,** the sinews that will eventually harden, **are eaten in an adult ox through cooking.**

Reish Lakish presses the challenge directly, citing the *mishnah*: *kol hane'echal beshor gadol ye'achel begedi harach* — the cartilage (*knafayim* and *sachchusim*) is included precisely because it *is* edible in a large animal; sinews that will harden apparently are not, and should therefore be excluded. How does Rabbi Yochanan respond?

### Liberal Edibility

Rabbi Yochanan's response is: *tanna hani vehu hadin lehanach* — the hardening sinews are analogous to the cartilage mentioned in the *mishnah*, because they too are theoretically edible in a large ox if boiled. *Mai ta'ama hani? — demitachli beshor gadol beshluka*. The cartilage qualifies because it can be eaten when boiled; *hacha nami mitachli beshor gadol beshluka* — so too these sinews, if boiled thoroughly, would be edible even in a large animal. Thus Rabbi Yochanan is not truly holding that one goes by present status alone; he is offering a more expansive understanding of what qualifies as potentially edible in the future. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish thus narrows: both agree in principle that the criterion is future edibility in the mature animal; they disagree about whether the thoroughly-boiled standard (*beshluka*) is sufficient to bring hardening sinews within the category of *basar*. Rabbi Yochanan's position, though intellectually coherent, is a generous application of the edibility standard, and the *gemara* notes that in the end it somewhat undercuts the sharpness of the original debate.

## Chulin Parallels

### Abbahu Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי אָבִין: כִּי אָזְלַתְּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, רְמִי לֵיהּ: מִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן גִּידִין שֶׁסּוֹפָן לְהַקְשׁוֹת נִמְנִין עֲלֵיהֶן בַּפֶּסַח, אַלְמָא: בָּתַר הַשְׁתָּא אָזְלִינַן? וְהָא בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עוֹר הָרֹאשׁ שֶׁל עֵגֶל הָרַךְ, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּטַמֵּא? וְאָמַר לוֹ: אֵין מְטַמֵּא. אַלְמָא: בָּתַר בַּסּוֹף אָזְלִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Avin: When you go before Rabbi Abbahu, raise** the following **contradiction to him: Did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say:** With regard to **sinews that will eventually harden,** people **may register for them in the Paschal lamb,** which would **apparently** indicate that **we go** according to **the current** condition of the sinews? **But Reish Lakish asked Rabbi Yoḥanan:** With regard to **the hide of the head of a young calf,** which is still edible, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to the possibility of it **becoming ritually impure** as a food? Do we view it as a food and apply the rules of ritual impurity of foods, or do we view it as a hide? **And he said to him: It does not become ritually impure. Apparently, we go** according to **the eventual** condition of the hide, which contradicts Rabbi Yoḥanan's own opinion with regard to sinews.

Rav Yirmeyah sends a question to Rabbi Abbahu via Rav Avin: did Rabbi Yochanan truly hold that *gidim shesofam lehakshot* count as one's portion in the *korban pesach*, implying that one goes by present status (*batar hashta azlinan*)? For there is a parallel discussion in Chullin where Reish Lakish challenged Rabbi Yochanan's position with a *mishnah*, and the resolution of that exchange appears to yield a different conclusion.

### Skin Precedent

The Chullin precedent concerns the skin of the head of a tender young calf (*or harosh shel eigel harach*): can it become susceptible to *tumah* as *ochel* (food)? Rabbi Yochanan ruled: *ein mitamei* — it cannot, because it is not classified as food. Now, in its present state, the skin of a tender calf is in fact edible. If Rabbi Yochanan held that we go by present status, he should have ruled that it *is* food and *does* become *tamei*. His ruling that it does not become *tamei* implies that he actually holds *batar besof azlinan* — we follow the future status. The tension with his apparent position in the *pesach* context is, on its face, a genuine contradiction.

### Abbahu Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּרְמָא לָךְ הָא — לָא חַשׁ לְקִמְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him:** Whoever **asked you this** question **was not concerned for his flour,** meaning he did not carefully consider what he said.

Rabbi Abbahu's response to Rav Avin is sharp and dismissive: *derama lach ha lo chash lekimchei* — the one who posed this contradiction "was not careful with his wheat," meaning he was grinding carelessly, not attending to the quality of his material. Rashi explains the idiom: *lemah dehu tachin im chitin solet* — he was not discriminating between grinding wheat and grinding chaff. The question *seems* strong but betrays an inattention to what happened at the end of the relevant *sugya*.

### Yochanan Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְגַבֵּי דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַל תַּקְנִיטֵנִי, שֶׁבִּלְשׁוֹן יָחִיד אֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan retracted** his opinion in this regard in favor of the opinion of **Reish Lakish, and** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him,** at the end of their discussion on the topic: **Do not trouble me** by asking a question based on a mishna that seems to prove that we follow the current state of the hide, **as I teach it in the singular.** That mishna, on which I previously relied, is the opinion of one Sage and should not be relied upon. This proves that Rabbi Yoḥanan changed his mind and concluded that the status of parts of the animal is established based on their eventual condition. The opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan with regard to the Paschal lamb was stated before he changed his mind.

The point is that Rav Yirmeyah, in formulating the contradiction, knew the *beginning* of the Chullin discussion but not its resolution. In Chullin, Reish Lakish had challenged Rabbi Yochanan's ruling about the calf's skin with a *mishnah* that seemed to support going by present status — apparently cutting against Rabbi Yochanan's ruling there. Rabbi Yochanan responded that he did not accept that *mishnah* as authoritative, regarding it as a *da'as yachid* (a lone opinion). In rejecting that *mishnah*, Rabbi Yochanan was effectively abandoning any position that one goes by present status, and aligning himself with the view that future status governs — the very position of Reish Lakish in the *pesach* context. In other words, Rabbi Yochanan reversed himself: what appeared to be his position in the *pesach* debate (present status controls) was, upon further examination, a position he relinquished in Chullin. So the question Rav Yirmeyah posed to Rabbi Abbahu was indeed a good question — it revealed a real contradiction — but the answer was already embedded in the Chullin *sugya*: Rabbi Yochanan conceded the point and came around to Reish Lakish's view.

The upshot is that ultimately both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish agree that future status governs the classification of *basar*. The plain sense of the *mishnah* is vindicated: one must assess whether the tissue would be edible when the animal is mature. The *beshluka* standard (edibility if boiled) sets the outer boundary — something that could not be eaten even under those ideal conditions is not *basar*. But something presently edible that will become inedible as the animal grows is also not formally classified as *basar*, and cannot constitute one's portion in the *korban pesach*, and perhaps also is not subject to *nosar*.

## Rambam Analysis

### Rambam's Position

Before proceeding to the next *mishnah*, it is worth examining how the Rambam codifies the foregoing discussion. The relevant passage is in *Hilchos Chametz U'Matzah*, chapter 8, halachah 11 — though halachah 10 in that same chapter encapsulates the *mishnah* just studied. The Rambam writes regarding *gidim harachim shesofan lehakshot*: *af al pi she'einan re'uyim la'achilah atah, ne'echalin bePesach ve'ein nimnin aleihem* — even though they are not presently fit for eating, they may be eaten as part of the *pesach*, but one may not designate them as one's portion.

### Meat/Partnership

This ruling is illuminating and resolves a question that had lingered in the *mishnah* itself. The Rambam draws a distinction between two questions that the *gemara* somewhat conflates: first, whether the tissue counts as *basar* such that eating it discharges one's obligation; and second, whether it is substantial enough to constitute one's designated share (*nimnim aleihem*) in the *korban pesach*. According to the Rambam, such soft sinews that will harden do qualify as *basar* in the sense that one may eat them as part of fulfilling the *mitzvah* — since they are currently edible — but they do not rise to the level of a formal portion in the partnership of the *korban pesach*. This distinction addresses the earlier question of whether *nosar* implications would apply to other *korbanot*: the Rambam's reading suggests that the *basar* status (with its *nosar* implications) and the *nimnim aleihem* status are separate determinations with potentially different criteria.

### Roasting Procedure

Halachah 11 of the Rambam then describes the procedure for eating the *pesach*: one cuts meat and eats it, cuts bones at the joint (*min haparak*) rather than through them, and may separate joints without transgressing *sheviras etzem*. When one reaches the *gid hanasheh*, the Rambam instructs: *motzio* — remove it — and set it aside with the other inedible *gidim*, bones, and membranes (*kromos*) that emerge during the meal. Crucially, the Rambam emphasizes that one does *not* remove the *gid* before roasting, as one would normally do with other meat — instead, the *korban* is roasted whole and intact, with the *gid hanasheh* still inside. If the *korban* was cut into pieces rather than roasted whole, it is *kasher* after the fact (*bedi'eved*), as long as no limb is missing.

### Ra'avad Objection

The Ra'avad's objection to this ruling is fierce. In one of the more colorful passages of the *hassagot*, he writes: *bechai roshi ein issur gadol mizeh* — by his life, he has never seen a greater prohibition than roasting the *pesach* with the *gid hanasheh* inside, and moreover with its fat (*im shamno*), and with the fat beneath the sides (*tarba d'susei masnei*), and with the membranes of the head (*kromos shebarosh*), all of which are items that may not be eaten. The Ra'avad could not fathom how the Rambam would be unconcerned about *nosein ta'am* — the taste and flavor transferred from these forbidden or inedible components into the meat during roasting. His conclusion: *ve'im ezkeh ve'ochal mehaPesach veyaviu lefanai kizeh — hayisi chovto bakarka lefanav* — if he lived to see the *korban pesach* restored and someone brought him such a Pesach, he would throw it on the ground before him.

One may offer a partial defense of the Rambam: the fat of the *gid hanasheh* is a *derabanan* prohibition, not *d'oraisa*; the *gid* itself may not impart discernible taste; and the Rambam may have regarded these concerns as overridden by the *mitzvah* of roasting the *korban* whole and intact — even though, *bedi'eved*, one may be *yotzei* in other ways. The Ra'avad, of course, takes strong exception to that calculus.

## Nosar/Sheviras

### Lashes Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹבֵר אֶת הָעֶצֶם בַּפֶּסַח הַטָּהוֹר — הֲרֵי זֶה לוֹקֶה אַרְבָּעִים. אֲבָל הַמּוֹתִיר בַּטָּהוֹר וְהַשּׁוֹבֵר בְּטָמֵא — אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who breaks the bone of a Paschal lamb that is ritually pure receives forty lashes** for having violated a prohibition stated in the Torah. **But one who leaves over** part **of a ritually pure** Paschal lamb **and one who breaks** the bone **of a ritually impure** Paschal lamb **do not receive forty lashes.**

The next *mishnah* addresses two prohibitions in tandem: *hashover et ha'etzem bePesach hatahor harei zeh lokeh arba'im* — one who breaks a bone of a valid (*kasher* or *tahor*) *pesach* incurs lashes. However, *aval hamoseir batahor eino lokeh arba'im* — one who leaves over the meat of a valid *pesach* (transgressing the prohibition of *nosar*) does not incur lashes. Similarly, *vehashover betamei eino lokeh arba'im* — one who breaks a bone of an invalid (*tamei*) *pesach* also does not incur lashes.

### Nosar Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא מוֹתִיר בַּטָּהוֹר — דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תוֹתִירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר וְהַנּוֹתָר מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר וְגוֹ׳״, בָּא הַכָּתוּב לִיתֵּן עֲשֵׂה אַחַר לֹא תַּעֲשֶׂה לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין לוֹקֶה — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara analyzes the mishna's rulings: **Granted, one who leaves over** part **of a ritually pure** Paschal lamb is not flogged for having violated Torah law. There is good reason for this, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall not leave any of it until morning; and that which remains of it until morning** you shall burn with fire" (Exodus 12:10). **The verse comes to provide a positive** mitzva to burn the leftover **after the prohibition** against leaving it over, **to say that one is not flogged** because any prohibition that can be rectified by the performance of a positive mitzva does not carry a punishment of lashes. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִן הַשֵּׁם הוּא זֶה, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, וְאֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov says: This is not for** that **reason. Rather,** it is **because it is a prohibition that does not involve an action.** The transgression is simply the failure to consume all the meat during the allotted time rather than the performance of an action. **And one is not flogged for** any prohibition that does not involve an action.

The *gemara* explains why *nosar* in a valid *pesach* does not incur lashes. A *beraita* cites the verse: *lo sosiru mimenu ad boker, vehanosar mimenu ad boker ba'esh tisrofu* — do not leave over from it until morning; that which remains until morning shall be burned in fire. Rabbi Yehuda derives from this that since the Torah follows the negative prohibition with a positive commandment (*asei*) — burn it — this constitutes a *lav hanitak l'asei*, a prohibition rectifiable through a subsequent positive act, and one does not receive lashes for such a prohibition. Rabbi Yaakov offers a different rationale: the transgression is *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — a purely passive violation, since leaving over meat requires no active deed, and passive violations do not incur lashes.

Rashi provides two complementary explanations of the *nitak l'asei* exemption. The first is substantive: the *asei* serves as a *tikkun*, a partial correction of the transgression — burning the *nosar* does not undo the leaving-over, but it is a remedial act that mitigates its severity. The second is formal-technical: the rules governing lashes require that the violation resemble the prohibition of *lo sachsom shor b'disho* (muzzling an ox while it threshes), and a *lav hanitak l'asei* structurally does not resemble that paradigm case. The interaction between these two explanations is itself instructive: formal categories in halachah typically track substantive rationales, even when, once a category is defined, its boundaries become formally determined.

### Tumah Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שׁוֹבֵר בַּטָּמֵא מְנָלַן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעֶצֶם לֹא תִשְׁבְּרוּ בוֹ״, ״בּוֹ״ בְּכָשֵׁר וְלֹא בְּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **one who breaks** the bone **of a ritually impure** Paschal lamb, **from where do we** derive that he, too, does not receive lashes? The Gemara answers that the source is **as the verse states:** "In one house shall it be eaten; you shall not remove any of the meat from the house to the outside, **and you shall not break a bone in it"** (Exodus 12:46). It may be inferred that the prohibition applies **"in it," in a valid** Paschal lamb, **and not in a disqualified** one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְעֶצֶם לֹא תִשְׁבְּרוּ בוֹ״, ״בּוֹ״ — בְּכָשֵׁר וְלֹא בְּפָסוּל. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״בְּבַיִת אֶחָד יֵאָכֵל ... וְעֶצֶם לֹא תִשְׁבְּרוּ בּוֹ״. כׇּל הָרָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת עֶצֶם, וְשֶׁאֵין רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת עֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to that same topic: **"And you shall not break a bone in it";** the prohibition applies **in it,** i.e., **in a valid** Paschal lamb **and not in a disqualified** one. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that this *halakha* should be learned in the following manner: It states: **"In one house shall it be eaten,"** and shortly thereafter the verse states: **"And you shall not break a bone in it,"** from which one can derive that **any** Paschal lamb **fit for eating is subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone, and** any Paschal lamb **that is not fit for eating is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone**.

The *gemara* then turns to the case of breaking a bone in an invalid (*tamei*) *pesach*. The verse states *v'etzem lo sishberu vo* — the word *bo* ("in it") is taken to limit the prohibition: *bo b'kasher, velo b'pasul* — only in a valid *pesach*, not in an invalid one. Rebbi (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi) derives the same exemption differently, connecting the prohibition of breaking bones to the verses describing eating (*b'vayis echad ye'achel*): *kol hara'ui l'achilah yesh bo mishum sheviras etzem, veshe'ein ra'ui l'achilah ein bo mishum sheviras etzem* — only that which is fit to be eaten is subject to *sheviras etzem*; what is not fit to be eaten is not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: פֶּסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between them? Rabbi Yirmeya said: The practical difference between them is** with regard to **a Paschal lamb that is brought** when the majority of the nation is **in** a state of **ritual impurity.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר — הַאי פָּסוּל. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — הַאי נָמֵי רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **According** **to the one who says** that the prohibition of breaking a bone applies only to **a valid** Paschal lamb, **this one is disqualified,** and the prohibition does not apply. However, **according to the one who says** that it depends on whether the offering is **fit for eating, this** offering that is brought in a state of ritual impurity **is also fit for eating,** and the prohibition of breaking a bone therefore applies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם, דְּרַבִּי לְאַקּוֹלֵי קָא אָתֵי, וְהַאי הָא פָּסוּל הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: In a case like this,** in which the Paschal lamb was brought in a state of ritual impurity, **everyone agrees that it is not subject to** the prohibition **of breaking a bone.** This is **because Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi only **came to be lenient, and this** offering **is disqualified** due to the fact that it is ritually impure, despite the fact that such an offering is accepted and therefore obligatory.

Rav Ami identifies the practical difference between the *Tanna Kamma* (who focuses on *kasher/pasul*) and Rebbi (who focuses on *ra'ui l'achilah*): a *pesach* offered in *tumah* by the community (*tzibur*). According to those who say the criterion is *kasher/pasul*: such a *pesach*, though technically *pasul* in nature, is permitted to be eaten — but it is still technically a *pasul korban*, so one would not transgress *sheviras etzem*. According to those who say the criterion is *ra'ui l'achilah* (fit to be eaten): since such a *pesach* is in fact permitted for consumption, one would transgress *sheviras etzem* if one broke a bone.

Rav Yosef, however, disagrees with Rav Ami's framing. He argues that in the case of a *pesach* brought in communal *tumah*, *everyone* would agree there is no transgression for breaking a bone, since the *korban* is technically *tamei* even if consumption is permitted. Rav Yosef adds that Rebbi's position is oriented entirely toward leniency (*Rebbi l'akulei ka'asi*): Rebbi is not introducing an independent criterion that might sometimes *add* liability — he is only introducing an additional basis for *exemption*. Accordingly, Rebbi would concede that wherever the *Tanna Kamma* also exempts (i.e., in a *pasul* case), Rebbi certainly exempts as well. The debate between them, Rav Yosef says, arises specifically in the case of a *pesach* that *was* once *kasher* and then became *pasul* (*haysa lo sha'as kosher v'nifsal*): the *Tanna Kamma* would say it is subject to *sheviras etzem* (it was once *kasher*), while Rebbi would say it is not (since right now it is not fit to be eaten).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר וְנִפְסַל אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר — הָא כָּשֵׁר הוּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — הָא הַשְׁתָּא לָאו רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the practical difference between them is** with regard to a Paschal lamb that **had a time when it was valid and** then **became disqualified. According to the one who says** that the verse should be understood as indicating that the prohibition of breaking a bone applies only to **a valid** Paschal lamb, **this one is valid** and the prohibition applies. **According to the one who says** that the prohibition applies only to an offering that is **fit for eating, it is not fit for eating now,** and therefore the prohibition does not apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כָּל כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם, מַאי טַעְמָא? הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת הָא פָּסוּל הוּא. אֶלָּא, שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר — הָא כָּשֵׁר הוּא, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — הַשְׁתָּא לָאו בַּר אֲכִילָה הוּא,
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: In any case like this, everyone agrees that it is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone. What is the reason? Now, at least, it is disqualified. Rather,** the *halakha* with regard to **breaking a bone while it is still day** on Passover eve **is the practical difference between them. According to the one who says** the verse should be understood as applying the prohibition to any Paschal lamb that is **valid, this one is valid** and the prohibition applies. **According to the one who says** the prohibition applies to a Paschal lamb that is **fit for eating, now it is not fit for eating** and therefore the prohibition does not yet apply.

Abaye then weighs in: in *that* case as well (a *pesach* that was once *kasher* and became *pasul*), everyone would agree there is no transgression (*kulei alma ki hai gavna ein bo mishum sheviras etzem*), since it is currently *pasul*. The real debate, Abaye proposes, is the case of *sheviras etzem mibod yom* — breaking a bone on the fourteenth of Nissan before nightfall, when the *pesach* has been slaughtered but the time of eating has not yet arrived.

## Timing Issues

### 14th Breaking

Abaye's proposal brings the discussion to the case of breaking a bone before nightfall on the fourteenth. The animal is entirely *kasher*; the only issue is that the time for eating has not yet arrived. According to the *Tanna Kamma* (*kasher* is the criterion): it is *kasher*, and one transgresses. According to Rebbi (*ra'ui l'achilah* is the criterion): since right now it is not yet fit to be eaten (the time of eating has not arrived), one does not transgress.

### Marrow Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: נִמְנִין עַל מוֹחַ שֶׁבָּרֹאשׁ וְאֵין נִמְנִין עַל מוֹחַ שֶׁבַּקּוּלִית. עַל מוֹחַ שֶׁבָּרֹאשׁ מַאי טַעְמָא — הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְגוֹרְרוֹ וּלְהוֹצִיאוֹ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי — קוּלִית נָמֵי, נִתְבְּרֵיהּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם, וְנַפְּקוּהּ לְמוֹחַ דִּידֵיהּ, וְנִמְנוֹ עֲלֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: One may register for** a Paschal lamb to eat **the marrow of the head, and one may not register for** the purpose of eating **the marrow of the thigh bone.** The Gemara clarifies: **What is the reason** one may register for **the marrow of the head?** It is **since one can extract it** from inside the head **and remove it** without breaking any of the bones, which is not the case with regard to marrow from the thigh bone. **And if it should enter your mind** to say **that breaking a bone while it is still day seems well,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi should hold that **we may break the thigh bone as well and remove its marrow, and** it should be permitted to **register for it.**

A challenge is raised from a teaching attributed to Rebbi himself: *nimnin al hamoach shebarosh ve'ein nimnin al hamoach shebekullis* — one may designate the brain marrow (accessible through the nasal cavity) as one's portion in the *pesach*, but one may not designate the marrow in the thigh bone (*kullis*) as one's portion. The reason one *can* designate the brain marrow is *hoil veyachol legorero ulehotzi'o* — it can be scraped out and extracted without breaking any bone. The marrow in the thigh bone, by contrast, cannot be extracted without breaking the bone.

### Burning Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ אַבָּיֵי: וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה נָמֵי: נַיְיתֵי גּוּמַרְתָּא וְנַיחֵות עֲלֵיהּ וְנִקְלֵהּ, וְנַפְּקֵהּ לְמוֹחַ דִּידֵיהּ, וְנִימְּנֵי עֲלֵיהּ. דְּהָא תַּנְיָא: אֲבָל הַשּׂוֹרֵף בַּעֲצָמוֹת וְהַמְחַתֵּךְ בְּגִידִין — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Abaye could have said to you: And according to your reasoning, after nightfall** there is **also** a permissible way to consume the marrow of the thigh bone: **We should bring a coal, and place it on** the bone, **and burn it, and remove its marrow, and** therefore it should be permissible to **register for it, for it was taught** explicitly in a *baraita*: **But one who burns bones** of the Paschal lamb **and one who cuts sinews do not** transgress the prohibition of **breaking a bone.**

The *gemara* challenges Abaye: if Rebbi holds that breaking bones before nightfall on the fourteenth does not constitute *sheviras etzem*, then one could simply extract the thigh-bone marrow before nightfall, and why could that not serve as one's portion? Abaye responds: *uletaimaich* — even by your own reasoning, one could also accomplish this after nightfall by applying a hot coal to the bone (*neisei gumreta, venichos aleih, venikla*), thereby burning rather than breaking the bone open. The *beraita* explicitly states: *aval hasoreif atzamos ein bo mishum sheviras etzem* — burning bones does not constitute *sheviras etzem*. So why can't the thigh-bone marrow serve as one's portion in any event?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר — אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם פֶּקַע, רָבָא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם הֶפְסֵד קָדָשִׁים, דְּקָא מַפְסֵיד לֵיהּ בְּיָדַיִם — דִּילְמָא אָכֵיל נוּרָא מִמּוֹחַ דִּידֵיהּ. מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם נָמֵי, גְּזֵירָה מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם אַטּוּ מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what have you to say** in order to explain why one may not remove the marrow from the thigh bone by burning the bone? It must be due to a rabbinic prohibition. Why did the Sages enact this prohibition? **Abaye said:** It is **because** the heat might cause the bone to **burst** at a location other than where the coal is placed, which would be considered breaking rather than burning. **Rava said:** It is **due to ruining sacrificial food, as one** who burns a hole in the bone **ruins it actively** because **it is possible that the fire will consume some of its marrow.** Similarly, breaking a bone **while it is still day is also** prohibited due to rabbinic **decree.** The Sages prohibited the breaking a bone while it still day **due to** the concern lest one do so **after dark.**

Two answers are given. Abaye himself says: *mishum pak paka* — we are concerned that in the process of burning the bone, the heat will cause other parts of the bone to crack and split, which would involve at least an indirect transgression (*grama*) of *sheviras etzem*. Rava offers a different concern: *mishum hefsed kodashim* — we are concerned about the destruction of sacred property (*kodashim*). If one applies fire to the bone, one may inadvertently burn some of the marrow itself. Although the marrow would eventually become *nosar* and require burning passively, it is preferable to allow that to occur on its own rather than to actively destroy a part of the *korban* that could theoretically have been consumed.

### Rabbinical Safeguard

Returning to Abaye's proposal that the *machlokes* concerns *sheviras etzem mibod yom*: the *gemara* resolves why, even according to Rebbi, one cannot designate the thigh-bone marrow as one's portion (and hence cannot extract it before nightfall) — *gezeirah mibod yom atu mishechasheicha*: the Rabbis decreed against breaking bones even before nightfall, lest people continue breaking bones after nightfall as well. Even if one does not transgress *d'oraisa* before nightfall according to Rebbi, the Rabbis forbade it as a protective measure. Since one is not permitted to break the bone even before nightfall, the marrow cannot serve as one's designated portion.

## Partial Disqualification

### Half-Limb Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כָּל כִּי הַאי גַוְנָא — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם. מַאי טַעְמָא? לְאוּרְתָּא מִיחֲזֵי לַאֲכִילָה. אֶלָּא, בְּאֵבֶר שֶׁיָּצָא מִקְצָתוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר — הָא כָּשֵׁר הוּא, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — הַאי לָאו בַּר אֲכִילָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** With regard to **any case like this, everyone agrees it is subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone. What is the reason?** It is because **at night it will be fit for eating. Rather,** it is **with regard to a portion of a limb** that **has gone out** of its permissible boundary and thereby become disqualified that **they disagree.** In such an instance, one removes the disqualified part in order to be able to eat the rest, and the Sages disagree about whether one may cut the bone. According to **the one who said** that the verse limits the prohibition of breaking a bone to a Paschal lamb that is **valid, this one is valid,** and it is therefore prohibited to break a bone. **And** according to **the one who said** the prohibition applies to any Paschal lamb that is **fit for eating, this one is not fit for eating,** and it should therefore be permitted to break its bones.

Rav Pappa proposes that the *machlokes* concerns *sheviras etzem* when the bone is *raw* (*bena*) — the animal has not yet been roasted. It is the fifteenth and the time of eating has arrived, but the animal is still uncooked. According to the *Tanna Kamma* (criterion: *kasher*), the animal is *kasher* and one transgresses. According to Rebbi (criterion: *ra'ui l'achilah*), since it is not yet in a state fit for eating, one does not transgress — because Rebbi's position is always intended to be a *kula*, an additional lenient ground. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak counters: in that case, everyone agrees one transgresses — *de'chazi l'achilah, dematu lei ve'achil lei* — all it takes is roasting it, and it is fit to eat.

The *gemara* then determines that the dispute concerns *eiver sheyatza miktzato* — a limb half of which has gone outside the boundaries of Jerusalem and become *pasul yotzei*. The part that crossed the boundary is *pasul*; the part within Jerusalem remains *kasher*. According to the *Tanna Kamma*: since part of the limb is *kasher*, breaking the bone (which affects the entire limb) involves breaking a bone of a *kasher pesach*, and one transgresses. According to Rebbi: since half the limb is *pasul* and cannot be eaten, the limb as a whole is not *ra'ui l'achilah*, and one does not transgress.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אֵבֶר שֶׁיָּצָא מִקְצָתוֹ וּשְׁבָרוֹ — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says:** If **a portion of a limb has gone out** of its permissible boundary **and one broke it,** one has not transgressed. **It is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone. This is** in accordance with the view that the prohibition of breaking bones applies only to a Paschal lamb that is fit for eating.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי אָמַר: כָּל כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם, דְּהַאי אֵבֶר הָא פָּסוּל הוּא. אֶלָּא, שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם בְּנָא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר — הָא כָּשֵׁר הוּא, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — הַשְׁתָּא אֵינוֹ רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet, son of Rav Idi, said:** With regard to **any case like this, everyone agrees that it is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone, as this limb is disqualified** and not merely unfit for eating. **Rather, breaking a bone** of a Paschal lamb **when** it is not yet fully roasted and still **raw is the practical difference between them.** According to **the one who said** the prohibition applies only to a Paschal lamb that is **valid, this one is valid,** and it is therefore prohibited to break a bone. **And** according to **the one who said** the prohibition applies to any Paschal lamb that is **fit for eating,** the prohibition does not apply to this offering because **now it is not fit for eating.**

Rav Sheshet the son of Rav Idi argues that in this case, everyone would agree there is no transgression, since the limb (*eiver*) itself is *pasul* — even though part of it remains technically *kasher*. A *beraita* confirms: *Rebbi Yishmael beno shel Rebbi Yochanan ben Beroka omer — eiver sheyatza miktzato veshavro, ein bo mishum sheviras etzem* — if a limb half extended outside Jerusalem's boundary and one broke it, there is no transgression, since at least the portion of the bone being broken is *pasul*. Tosafot notes a difficulty: from the perspective of the *kasher* portion, the bone *is* being broken; why should we focus only on the *pasul* portion? The precise formulation of this position is not without its difficulties.

### Fat Tail Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, שְׁבִירַת הָאַלְיָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר — הָא כָּשֵׁר הוּא, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — הַאי אֵינוֹ רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה, דְּאַלְיָה לְגָבוֹהַּ סָלְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, breaking** a bone in **the tail is the practical difference between them. According to the one who says** the prohibition applies to any Paschal lamb that is **valid, this one is valid** and the prohibition applies. **According to the one who says** the prohibition applies to any Paschal lamb that is **fit for eating, this is not fit for eating, as the tail** is consecrated **to God,** meaning that it is burned on the altar.

The *gemara* then considers whether the *machlokes* applies to *shevirat ha'alyah* — breaking the bone of the fat tail. The fat tail (*alyah*) of the sheep is the part that is burned on the altar; it is not eaten by the participants. According to the *Tanna Kamma*: it is part of the *kasher korban*, and one might transgress. According to Rebbi: *hai eino ra'ui l'achilah, d'alyah l'gavoha salka* — the fat tail is not subject to eating; it ascends to the altar for the Divine Service, and therefore the criterion of *ra'ui l'achilah* is not met. One therefore does not transgress. Whether the fat tail in fact contains bone — and thus whether *sheviras etzem* is anatomically relevant here — is itself uncertain; participants noted that a sheep's fat tail is composed almost entirely of fat, though any structure supporting it would presumably have some bony element.

### Stripped Limb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כָּל כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא וַדַּאי אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם, דְּהָא וַדַּאי אֵינוֹ רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה כְּלָל. אֶלָּא, אֵבֶר שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו כַּזַּיִת בָּשָׂר אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר — הָא כָּשֵׁר הוּא, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — בָּעֵינַן שִׁיעוּר אֲכִילָה, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: A case like this is certainly not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone, as** the tail **is certainly not fit for eating at all. Rather,** the case of **a limb upon which there is not an olive-bulk of meat is the practical difference between them. According to the one who says** the prohibition applies to any Paschal lamb that is **valid, this is valid,** and the prohibition applies. **According to the one who says** the prohibition applies to any Paschal lamb that is **fit for eating, we require the** minimal **measure of** meat that would constitute an act of **eating, and there is not** enough meat on this bone; therefore, the prohibition does not apply.

Rav Ashi objects to the framing entirely: regarding the fat tail, *vadai ein bah mishum sheviras etzem — d'vadai einah ra'uyah l'achilah klal* — it is certainly not subject to *sheviras etzem*, since it is certainly not fit to be eaten at all. Even the *Chachamim*, who do not use a *ra'ui l'achilah* criterion explicitly, must at minimum intend that *sheviras etzem* applies to portions of the *korban* that are in the realm of being eaten.

Rav Ashi therefore locates the *machlokes* in the case of *eiver she'ein alav kezayis basar* — a limb stripped of meat, with less than a *kezayis* (olive-size) of meat remaining on it. According to the *Tanna Kamma* (criterion: *kasher*): the bone is part of a *kasher korban*, and one transgresses. According to Rebbi (criterion: *ra'ui l'achilah*): *be'inan shiur achilah ve'leika* — we require an edible amount on the specific limb being broken, and it is lacking. The *chiddush* here is significant: Rebbi's criterion is not about the *korban* as a whole, but about the specific piece in one's hand. If the bone one is about to break does not have a *kezayis* of meat on it, Rebbi exempts one from lashes.

### Meat Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: כׇּל כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא לֵית בֵּיהּ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם, דְּבָעֵינַן שִׁיעוּר אֲכִילָה. אֶלָּא, אֵבֶר שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר בְּמָקוֹם זֶה וְיֵשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר בִּמְקוֹם אַחֵר אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כָּשֵׁר — הָא כָּשֵׁר הוּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר רָאוּי לַאֲכִילָה — בָּעֵינַן שִׁיעוּר אֲכִילָה בִּמְקוֹם שְׁבִירָה, וְהָא לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Any case like this is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone, as we require the** minimal **measure** of meat that would constitute an act of **eating. Rather,** the case of **a limb upon which there is not an olive-bulk of meat in this place and there is an olive-bulk of meat in another place is the practical difference between them. According to the one who says** the prohibition applies to any Paschal lamb that is **valid, this one is valid,** and it is prohibited to break a bone. **According to the one who says** the prohibition applies to any Paschal lamb that is **fit for eating, we require the** minimal **measure** of meat that would constitute an act of **eating in the place of the break, and there is not** enough meat there. Therefore, the prohibition of breaking a bone does not apply.

Ravina then addresses the extreme case: a limb with no meat at all. Surprisingly, Ravina holds that in *that* case, everyone agrees one does not transgress — *d'be'inan shiur achilah*. The assumption had been that even without meat, the bone might still count as part of the *korban*. Ravina insists there must be at minimum an edible amount associated with the bone.

The next refinement: a limb that has a *kezayis* of meat not *at the point of breaking* but elsewhere on the limb (*bemakom acher*). This is the case that most directly tests whether Rebbi's criterion is tied to the immediate point of fracture or to the limb as a whole. And further: what if the limb has no external meat at the point of breaking, but contains a *kezayis* of marrow internally (*mibifnim*)? Rav Pappa rules that marrow on the inside (*moach mibifnim*) can satisfy the *kezayis* requirement — the marrow counts as the edible substance associated with the bone, even if it is not visible on the outside.

## Final Beraita

### Four Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּאַרְבְּעָה מִינַּיְיהוּ. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״בְּבַיִת אֶחָד יֵאָכֵל ... וְעֶצֶם לֹא תִשְׁבְּרוּ בּוֹ״, עַל הַכָּשֵׁר הוּא חַיָּיב, וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַל הַפָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out that **it was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with four of** the interpretations cited above. **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The verse **"In one house shall it be eaten…and you shall not break a bone in it"** (Exodus 12:46) teaches that **one is liable for** breaking the bone of **a valid** Paschal lamb, **and one is not liable for** breaking the bone of a **disqualified** Paschal lamb.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר וְנִפְסַל בִּשְׁעַת אֲכִילָה — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת עֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** If **it had a time in which it was valid and it became disqualified at the time of eating, it is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone.** This supports the opinion of Rav Yosef.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ בּוֹ שִׁיעוּר אֲכִילָה — יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת עֶצֶם, אֵין בּוֹ שִׁיעוּר אֲכִילָה — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת עֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** If **it has the** minimal **measure** of meat that would constitute an act of **eating, it is subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone; if it does not have the** minimal **measure** of meat necessary to constitute an act of **eating, it is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone.** This supports the opinion of both Rav Ashi and Ravina.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרָאוּי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **That which is fit for the altar,** such as the tail, **is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone.** This supports the opinion of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁעַת אֲכִילָה — יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת עֶצֶם, שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת אֲכִילָה — אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת עֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **At the time of eating, it is subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone; when it is not the time for eating, it is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone.** This supports the opinion of Abaye.

A *beraita* is cited that encapsulates four distinct positions regarding when *sheviras etzem* applies, corresponding to the various approaches surveyed by the *amoraim*.

### Rebbi Standard

Rebbi's position in the *beraita* synthesizes his multi-pronged approach to leniency. He holds that one is liable only for a bone of a *kasher pesach* (not a *pasul* one); that if the *korban* had a *sha'as kosher* (a period of validity) and was subsequently disqualified, one is not liable (since right now it is *pasul* and not fit to be eaten); that if there is a *shiur achilah* (sufficient quantity for eating) on the limb, one is liable, and if not, one is not; that if the part is *ra'ui l'mizbeach* (fit for the altar, like the fat tail), one is not liable; that during the time of eating (*beshas achilah*) one is liable, but *shelo beshas achilah* (not during the time of eating, such as on the fourteenth before nightfall), one is not liable.

### Location Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: אֵבֶר שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר בִּמְקוֹם זֶה וְיֵשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר בִּמְקוֹם אַחֵר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת עֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* argued about the following matter: In the case of **a limb upon which there is not an olive-bulk of meat in this place** in which one breaks the bone, **and there is an olive-bulk of meat in a different place,** has one violated the prohibition of breaking a bone? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: It is not subject to** the prohibition of **breaking a bone.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: ״וְעֶצֶם לֹא תִשְׁבְּרוּ בּוֹ״ — אֶחָד עֶצֶם שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר, וְאֶחָד עֶצֶם שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר. מַאי אֵין עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר? אִילֵּימָא דְּאֵין עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר כְּלָל — אַמַּאי יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם?! אֶלָּא לָאו, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶחָד עֶצֶם שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר בְּמָקוֹם זֶה, וְאֶחָד שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר בְּמָקוֹם זֶה וְיֵשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר (— קַשְׁיָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ)!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Reish Lakish** from a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall not break a bone in it,"** which indicates that the prohibition applies to **both a bone upon which there is an olive-bulk of meat and a bone upon which there is not an olive-bulk of meat. What** is the meaning of the phrase: **Upon which there is not an olive-bulk of meat? If we say that it does not have an olive-bulk of meat on it at all,** it is not fit for eating at all. **Why,** then, **is it subject to** the prohibition against **breaking a bone? Rather, is it not** true that **this is what it is saying:** The prohibition applies **both** to **a bone upon which there is an olive-bulk of meat in this place, and** to a bone **upon which there is not an olive-bulk of meat in this place and there is an olive-bulk of meat in a different place?** This poses **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish.**

The final dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerns the case of a limb with a *kezayis* of meat at one location but not at the point of breaking. Rabbi Yochanan holds *yesh bo mishum sheviras etzem* — one transgresses even if the *kezayis* is located elsewhere on the limb, not at the point of fracture. Reish Lakish holds *ein bo mishum sheviras etzem* — without a *kezayis* at the precise point of breaking, one does not transgress.

### Outside/Inside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֶחָד עֶצֶם שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר מִבַּחוּץ, וְאֶחָד עֶצֶם שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו כַּזַּיִת בָּשָׂר מִבַּחוּץ, וְיֵשׁ עָלָיו כַּזַּיִת בָּשָׂר מִבִּפְנִים בִּמְקוֹם שְׁבִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him:** **No,** that is not what it means to say. Rather, **this is what it is saying:** It is prohibited to break **both a bone upon which there is an olive-bulk of meat on the outside and a bone upon which there is not an olive-bulk of meat on the outside,** but **there is an olive-bulk of meat,** i.e., bone marrow, **on** the **inside at the place of the break.**

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish from the *beraita*: *echad etzem sheyesh alav kezayis basar, ve'echad etzem she'ein alav kezayis basar* — the prohibition applies both when there is a *kezayis* of meat on the bone and when there is not. If "no *kezayis* of meat" means literally no meat at all, why would anyone be liable? There must be some edible substance present for the prohibition to apply. The *beraita* must therefore mean that even when there is no *kezayis* at the point of breaking but there is one elsewhere on the limb, one is still liable — supporting Rabbi Yochanan.

Reish Lakish deflects: the *beraita* does not mean "meat elsewhere on the limb" but rather refers to marrow inside the bone. *Echad etzem sheyesh alav kezayis basar mibachutz, ve'echad etzem she'ein alav kezayis basar mibachutz veyesh alav kezayis basar mibifnim bemakom shevirah* — one bone with external meat at the point of breaking, and another with no external meat but with internal marrow (*moach*) at the point of breaking. The *beraita* teaches that internal marrow satisfies the *kezayis* requirement. Reish Lakish thus concedes that marrow on the *inside* at the point of fracture counts — but insists that a *kezayis* located elsewhere on the exterior of the limb does not. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, holds that a *kezayis* anywhere on the limb suffices for the prohibition to apply.
