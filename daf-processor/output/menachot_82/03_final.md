# Menachot 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stipulated Conditions

### Intro: Todah & Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי תּוֹדָה בְּלֹא לֶחֶם״ וְ״זֶבַח בְּלֹא נְסָכִים״ – כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ, וּמֵבִיא תּוֹדָה וְלַחְמָהּ, זֶבַח וּנְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the statement of Rav Huna and raises a difficulty. **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* that contradicts the opinion of Rav Huna: In the case of **one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a thanks offering without loaves, or** an animal **offering without libations,** the court **coerces him, and he brings a thanks offering with its loaves,** or **an offering with its libations.**

The daf picks up in the middle of Menachot 81b, where the discussion concerns a case introduced in the previous Baraita: when somebody declares that he will bring a Todah on the condition that he not be required to bring the accompanying bread, the ruling is that we force him to bring the bread. The Gemara raises an immediate challenge to this ruling — why not say that this is a case of *neder u'pitcho imo*, a vow with its own opening? The reasoning would be as follows: since the person explicitly declared that he wanted to bring a Todah *without* the bread, we ought to look at the statement in its totality. He never truly intended to obligate himself in the complete package, so his vow should not be binding at all — he should bring nothing. Why do we sever the first half of his declaration ("I want to bring a Todah") from the second half ("without the bread") and hold him to the first part alone?

### Forcing Full Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: הָא מַנִּי? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: תְּפוֹס לָשׁוֹן הָרִאשׁוֹן. דִּתְנַן: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִן הַגְּרוֹגְרוֹת וּמִן הַדְּבֵלָה״ – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who say:** In the case of one who stated a declaration comprising two contradictory statements, **attend** to only **the first statement. As we learned** in a mishna (*Nazir* 9a): If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite from dried figs and from** pressed **figs,** which is a contradictory statement, as figs are not prohibited to a nazirite, **Beit Shammai say:** He is a full-fledged **nazirite,** as one attends only to the first statement, i.e., I am hereby a nazirite, and the second part is discounted. **And Beit Hillel say:** The second part of his statement is not discounted, and therefore **he is not a nazirite,** as he did not accept naziriteship upon himself.

Chizkiyah answers that the ruling follows *beit shammai*, who hold *tfos lashon rishon* — we seize the first part of the statement. *beit shammai*, in general, take a more objective view of declarations in the domains of nedarim and hekdesh, giving primacy to what was actually said rather than to what the speaker had in mind. The Gemara cites a Mishnah in support: if someone says *hareini nazir min ha-grogrot u'min ha-dveilah* — "I am going to be a nazir so as to avoid eating figs or dates" — *beit shammai* rule that he is a nazir, a genuine nazir bound by the restrictions concerning wine, because he uttered the word *nazir*. Beit Hillel rule that he is not a nazir, because we assess the totality of the statement and it is clear he had no intent to become a nazir in any recognized sense. So too in the case of the Todah: *beit shammai* hold that the person said the word *todah*, and that alone is binding, regardless of the larger context in which the declaration was made.

### Beit Shammai's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בֵּית הִלֵּל, בְּאוֹמֵר: אִילּוּ הָיִיתִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין נוֹדְרִין כָּךְ, לֹא הָיִיתִי נוֹדֵר כָּךְ, אֶלָּא כָּךְ. וּמַאי כּוֹפִין? דְּקָא בָּעֵי הָדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: You** may **even say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Hillel,** and the ruling of the *baraita* is **with regard to one who says: Had I known that one does not vow** in **this** manner, i.e., to bring a thanks offering without loaves or an animal offering without libations, **I would not have vowed** in **this** manner **but** in **that** manner, i.e., I would have vowed to bring a thanks offering with its loaves. There is therefore no opening for a dissolution. **And what** does the *baraita* mean when it states that the court **coerces** him? Why would he require coercion if he says that he would have willingly taken the proper vow? The *baraita* is referring to a case **where,** despite the fact that he would have taken the proper vow initially, **he** now **wants to retract his** vow altogether.

Rabbi Yochanan argues that the original ruling can be maintained even according to Beit Hillel. In his reading, the case involves someone who says explicitly: *ilu hayiti yodea she-ein nodvin kach lo hayiti noder kach ela kach* — "Had I known that one cannot bring a Todah without its bread, I would of course have obligated myself to bring the Todah with the bread." That is, he is clarifying that his preference for a Todah without bread stemmed from his mistaken belief that such a thing was possible. Had he known that the choice was all or nothing, he would have chosen the full Todah.

The question this raises is whether the person literally said those words or whether we simply assume that this is what any reasonable person would have meant. The two cases are quite different. Someone who declares himself a *nazir* from dates clearly has in mind that he does not want to eat dates — it is not at all reasonable to infer that he would have wanted to become a nazir from wine had he known there was no such thing as a nazir from dates. Those are completely different intentions. But someone who says "Todah without bread" may plausibly be understood as preferring the partial arrangement only insofar as it is available, and as being prepared to bring the full Todah if it is not.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי תּוֹדָה בְּלֹא לֶחֶם״, וְ״זֶבַח בְּלֹא נְסָכִים״, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״הָבֵא תּוֹדָה וְלַחְמָהּ״, וְ״זֶבַח וּנְסָכִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״אִילּוּ הָיִיתִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הָיִיתִי נוֹדֵר״ – כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״שְׁמֹר וְשָׁמַעְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a *baraita* in support of the response of Ḥizkiyya: **Come** and **hear:** In a case involving **one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a thanks offering without loaves, or:** It is incumbent upon me to bring an animal **offering without libations, and** the Sages **said to him: Bring a thanks offering with its loaves, or:** Bring an animal **offering with its libations, and he says: Had I known that such** is the requirement **I would not have vowed** at all; the *halakha* is that the court **coerces him, and says to him: "Observe and hear"** (Deuteronomy 12:28).

A further Baraita clarifies the situation. It reads: one who says *harei alai Todah belo lechem ve-zevach belo nesachim* — "I obligate myself to bring a Todah without its bread, or a sacrifice without its libations" — is told by the court: bring the Todah with its bread and the sacrifice with its libations. He then responds: *ilu hayiti yodea sheken lo hayiti noder* — had I known you would force me to bring the whole package, I would never have taken the vow at all. I wanted to do it partially, and if that is not permitted, I want to do nothing. The ruling is *kofin oto* — he is compelled to bring the full Todah, and we say to him *shemor ve-shama'ta*, observe and listen.

The Gemara then notes that this Baraita sits comfortably with Chizkiyah, who assigns the ruling to *beit shammai*. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, who sought to reconcile even such a case with Beit Hillel, the difficulty is acute: here the person explicitly states he would prefer to do nothing at all, so why should Beit Hillel hold him to his original declaration? Rabbi Yochanan's resolution is that this second Baraita is indeed *beit shammai*. He was attempting to explain a *different* Baraita according to Beit Hillel. In the end, Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan do not fundamentally disagree about the *beit shammai*–Beit Hillel debate itself; the dispute between them concerns only which Baraita is attributed to which school. All agree that *beit shammai* binds a person by the words he uttered, without regard to the broader context, while Beit Hillel looks at the whole statement.

### Baraita Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״שְׁמֹר וְשָׁמַעְתָּ״? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״שְׁמֹר״ – הָבֵא תּוֹדָה, ״וְשָׁמַעְתָּ״ – הָבֵא לַחְמָהּ. רָבָא אָמַר: ״שְׁמֹר״ – הָבֵא תּוֹדָה וְלַחְמָהּ, ״וְשָׁמַעְתָּ״ – שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא רָגִיל לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns to analyze the *baraita* itself: **What** is the relevance of the phrase in the verse that says: **"Observe and hear,"** to one who vows to bring a thanks offering without its loaves? **Abaye says: "Observe"** means: **Bring a thanks offering; "and hear"** means: **Bring its loaves. Rava says: "Observe"** means: **Bring a thanks offering and its loaves; "and hear"** means **that you should not act** in **this** manner **regularly.**

The Gemara turns to explain the phrase *shemor ve-shama'ta* that is cited at the conclusion of the Baraita. Abaye says: *shemor* — observe what you said, namely the Todah, and bring it; *ve-shama'ta* — and hear what the *torah demands*, namely the bread that accompanies it. Rava says: *shemor* — observe and bring the Todah with its bread, as the Torah requires; *ve-shama'ta* — and take heed, so that you do not put yourself in these situations in the future, so that you understand the laws before making stipulations that cannot be satisfied.

## Ma'aser & Todah

### Shamor Veshamata


The Gemara now returns to the Mishnah. The Mishnah teaches: *hi ve-lachmah min ha-ma'aser* — if someone says he obligates himself to bring a Todah, stipulating from the very outset that he may bring it from *ma'aser sheni* funds, he may do so. Although as a general rule — a rule the next Mishnah will articulate explicitly as *davar shebechova eino ba min hachulin* — an obligatory sacrifice cannot be brought from sanctified *ma'aser sheni* funds, when a person stipulates this condition from the outset, it is permitted. The underlying logic is that one may in any case use *ma'aser sheni money* to purchase voluntary offerings such as Shelamim or a Todah brought not as a personal obligation. When someone stipulates from the beginning that his Todah obligation is conditioned on the ability to fund it from *ma'aser sheni*, he is in effect structuring his vow in a manner that the halacha recognizes.

### Stipulation Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תּוֹדָה הִיא וְלַחְמָהּ מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר״ – יָבִיא. יָבִיא? לָא סַגִּי דְּלָא מַיְיתֵי? רַב נַחְמָן וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמְרִי: רָצָה מֵבִיא, לֹא רָצָה לֹא יָבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one states: It is incumbent upon me to bring **a thanks offering and its loaves from** second-**tithe** money, **he shall bring** it in that manner. The Gemara asks: Why does the mishna teach: **He shall bring,** which suggests that he must bring the thanks offering and loaves from second-tithe money? Is it **not sufficient** to fulfill his vow even **if he does not bring** it from second-tithe money but from non-sacred money? In fact, it is preferable that he bring the offering from non-sacred money. **Rav Naḥman and Rav Ḥisda say:** The mishna does not mean that he must bring it specifically from second-tithe money; rather, if he **wants, he brings** it from second-tithe money, and if he **does not want, he does not** need to **bring** it from second-tithe money.

The Gemara asks: *yavi* — must he bring it from *ma'aser sheni money*? Does his stipulation create a requirement to use those funds? Rav Papa and Rav Chisda answer: no. *ratzah meivi* — if he wishes, he may bring it from *ma'aser sheni money*, because he stipulated that this should be permitted. *lo ratzah lo meivi* — if he prefers to use Chulin funds instead, he may do so. The stipulation creates a permission, not an obligation.

One might ask why we read his declaration as creating an option rather than a binding commitment to use *ma'aser sheni* funds. Part of the answer lies in the mechanics of nedarim: a neder creates a status, not a personal obligation to perform a specific action with specific funds. If one truly wished to obligate himself to use *ma'aser sheni* funds and no others, the appropriate vehicle would be a shevua, which governs actions. A neder, by contrast, creates the status of being obligated in a Todah; it cannot itself restrict which funds satisfy that obligation. The Gemara therefore reads the declaration charitably — as allowing the use of *ma'aser sheni* funds, rather than requiring it — consistent both with the technical workings of nedarim and with the most natural understanding of what the person was trying to accomplish. He wanted to ensure that his *ma'aser sheni* funds could be used; he was not trying to preclude himself from using Chulin funds.

### Yavi Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יָבִיא מֵחִיטֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, אֶלָּא מִמְּעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי. רַב נַחְמָן וְרַב חִסְדָּא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מֵחִיטֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, אֲבָל מֵחִיטִּין הַלְּקוּחוֹת מִמְּעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי – יָבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And he may not bring** the loaves **from second-tithe wheat; rather,** he purchases the flour **with second-tithe money.** With regard to this, **Rav Naḥman and Rav Ḥisda both say:** The mishna **taught only** that the loaves may not be brought **from second-tithe wheat** itself, **but he may bring** the loaves **from wheat purchased with second-tithe money.**

The Mishnah adds: *ve-lo yavi me-chitei *ma'aser sheni* ela mi-me'os ma'aser sheni* — but one should not bring the Todah bread from the actual wheat grain that was set aside as *ma'aser sheni*. Rather, one should redeem the *ma'aser sheni* produce into money, and use that money to purchase the Todah and its bread.

Rav Nachman and Rav Chisda both explain: the prohibition is specifically against using the actual *ma'aser sheni* grain itself. But if one used *ma'aser sheni money* to purchase grain or flour, that purchased grain may be used to make the bread of the Todah — which seems straightforward, since this is precisely how one would use *ma'aser sheni money* for bread-baking in this context.

### Permission Not Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מֵחִיטֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, אֲבָל מֵחִיטִּין הַלְּקוּחוֹת בִּמְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי – יָבִיא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי, אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן? אֲנִי אוֹמֵר אֲפִילּוּ מֵחִיטִּין הַלְּקוּחוֹת בִּמְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי לֹא יָבִיא. וְאֵימָא טַעְמָא דִידִי, וְאֵימָא טַעְמָא דִּידָךְ. אֵימָא טַעְמָא דִּידָךְ: תּוֹדָה מֵהֵיכָא קָא יָלְפַתְּ לַהּ? מִשְּׁלָמִים,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Yirmeya was sitting before Rabbi Zeira, and he was sitting and saying:** The mishna **taught only** that the loaves may not be brought **from second-tithe wheat** itself, **but he may bring** the loaves **from wheat purchased from second-tithe money.** Rabbi Zeira **said to him: My teacher,** do **you say so? I say** that **he may not bring** the loaves **even from wheat purchased from second-tithe money. And I will say my reasoning and I will say your reasoning. I will say your reasoning** first: **From where do you derive** that the loaves of **a thanks offering** may be brought from wheat purchased with second-tithe money? You derive it from the *halakha* of **a peace offering.** A thanks offering is a type of peace offering, as the verse states: "And the flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offerings for thanks" (Leviticus 7:15), and a peace offering may be brought from second-tithe money.

Rabbi Yirmeyah was sitting before Rabbi Zeira and stated the same position as Rav Nachman and Rav Chisda: only the original *ma'aser sheni* grain is forbidden; wheat purchased with *ma'aser sheni money* may be used. Rabbi Zeira challenged him sharply: do you really say this? I say that even wheat purchased with *ma'aser sheni money* may not be used to make the Todah bread.

Rabbi Zeira then explains both positions. He begins with Rabbi Yirmeyah's reasoning: where do we know that *ma'aser sheni money* may be used for a Todah at all? We derive it from Shelamim — *sham sham mi-ma'aser*, a gezeira shava linking the word *sham* in the verse concerning Shelamim to the word *sham* in the verse concerning *ma'aser sheni*. Since Shelamim may be purchased with *ma'aser sheni money*, so too may a Todah. Just as the Shelamim offering itself is never *ma'aser sheni* grain — cattle and sheep are never tithe produce — so too when funding a Todah from *ma'aser sheni*, the Todah itself cannot be the *ma'aser sheni* grain directly. But if one uses *ma'aser sheni money* to buy wheat, that wheat is not itself *ma'aser sheni* grain, so it is permitted. That is Rabbi Yirmeyah's reasoning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁלָמִים ״שָׁם״ ״שָׁם״ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר. וּמִינַּהּ: מָה שְׁלָמִים אֵין גּוּפָן מַעֲשֵׂר – אַף תּוֹדָה נָמֵי אֵין גּוּפָהּ מַעֲשֵׂר, וְהָנֵי חִיטֵּי הַלְּקוּחוֹת בִּמְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי נָמֵי אֵין גּוּפָן מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *halakha* that **a peace offering** may be brought from second-tithe money is derived by a verbal analogy between **"there"** and **"there" from** the verse discussing second **tithe.** The verse states with regard to a peace offering: "And you shall sacrifice peace offerings and you shall eat there" (Deuteronomy 27:7), and the verse states with regard to second tithe: "And you shall eat before the Lord your God, in the place that He shall choose to cause His name to dwell there" (Deuteronomy 14:23). **And from this** it may be concluded: **Just as peace offerings are not themselves** brought from second **tithe,** as they are not produce, **so too** with regard to the loaves of **a thanks offering, they are not themselves** brought from second **tithe. And that wheat which is purchased with second-tithe money is also not itself** second **tithe;** it may therefore be used for the loaves of the thanks offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא טַעְמָא דִידִי: תּוֹדָה מֵהֵיכָא קָא יָלֵיפְנָא לַהּ? מִשְּׁלָמִים, וּשְׁלָמִים – ״שָׁם״ ״שָׁם״ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר. מָה שְׁלָמִים אֵין מִין מַעֲשֵׂר, אַף תּוֹדָה אֵין מִין מַעֲשֵׂר, לְאַפּוֹקֵי חִיטִּין הַלְּקוּחוֹת בִּמְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, דְּמִין מַעֲשֵׂר נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Now **I will say my reasoning** for why one may not prepare the loaves of a thanks offering with wheat that was purchased with second-tithe money: **From where do I derive** the *halakha* that **a thanks offering** may be brought from second-tithe money? I derive it **from** the *halakhot* of **a peace offering. And** the *halakha* that **a peace offering** may be brought from second-tithe money is derived by a verbal analogy between **"there"** and **"there" from** second **tithe.** Therefore, **just as a peace offering is not** of the same **species** as second **tithe, so too** the loaves of **a thanks offering** may **not** be of the same **species** as second **tithe.** This serves **to exclude wheat purchased with second-tithe money, which is** of the same **species** as second **tithe.**

Rabbi Zeira then presents his own position: the inference from Shelamim to Todah tells us not merely that the actual *ma'aser sheni* grain cannot become Todah bread, but that anything used for the Todah bread must be entirely of a type that *could never be Ma'aser Sheni*. *shelamim meat*, by its nature, is never a category of produce that can be *ma'aser sheni* — meat is never tithed. Therefore, when one uses *ma'aser sheni* funds for a Todah, one must use them to purchase items that are likewise not of a kind that could ever be *ma'aser sheni*. Wheat is precisely a kind of produce that can be *ma'aser sheni*. Therefore one may not use *ma'aser sheni money* to purchase wheat for Todah bread. The implication — and Rashi understands it this way — is that the *ma'aser sheni money* should be used to buy the bread itself, already baked, rather than wheat or flour. This is admittedly a striking position, since it requires that the bread be produced by a third party, but Rabbi Zeira's reasoning about the category-type of the purchased item leads inescapably there.

## Wheat vs. Bread

### Grain vs. Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הַמַּתְפִּיס מְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי לִשְׁלָמִים – לֹא קָנוּ שְׁלָמִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּלָא אַלִּימָא קְדוּשַּׁת שְׁלָמִים לְמֵיחַל אַקְּדוּשַּׁת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ami says:** In a case involving **one who designates second-tithe money for a peace offering,** the money does **not assume** the status of **a peace offering. What is the reason?** The reason is **that the sanctity of the peace offering is not strong** enough **to take effect upon** items that have **the sanctity of second tithe.**

Rabbi Ammi's statement now introduces the broader conceptual question about doubling sanctity. Before reaching that, the Gemara establishes that the basis for using *ma'aser sheni* funds for Shelamim or Todah is the gezeira shava of *sham sham*, linking the verse about Shelamim — *ve-achalta sham* — to the verse about *ma'aser sheni* — *ve-natata ba-kesef be-yadecha ve-*achalta sham* lifnei Hashem Elokecha*. Both contexts speak of eating before God in Yerushalayim.

This connection is not arbitrary. The entire institution of *ma'aser sheni* is designed to bring the person into the center of kedusha, to eat before God in Yerushalayim. The Torah frames it as *lifnei Hashem Elokecha*, even though one is not required to enter the Beit HaMikdash itself. The Shelamim offering shares precisely this character: the eating is in the presence of God, evoking the *simcha* and wholeness of being in communion with the Divine. In fact, the Mishnah in *ma'aser sheni* makes clear that if one wished to eat meat with *ma'aser sheni* funds, one was expected to bring a Shelamim rather than purchasing *basar ta'avah* — ordinary unsanctified meat — because the goals of *ma'aser sheni* and the goals of eating Shelamim in Yerushalayim are deeply aligned. Since Todah is a form of Shelamim, *ma'aser sheni* funds may equally be directed toward it.

### Rav Nachman's Position


It is from this same conceptual framework that the Mishnah's restriction about using the actual *ma'aser sheni* grain for Todah bread emerges. To take the *ma'aser sheni* grain itself — the produce that grew and was separated as *ma'aser sheni* — and transform it into bread of a Todah, represents a kind of compromise of its original identity. When one uses *ma'aser sheni* *money* to purchase an item that then assumes its own sanctity as a Shelamim or Todah, the money has been appropriately deployed toward the ends of *ma'aser sheni* without any confusion of identity. But to take the grain itself, which *is* the *ma'aser sheni*, and overlay upon it the identity of *lachmei Todah*, is to try to make it two things at once in a way that undermines the clarity of each.

### Rabbi Zeira's Stricture


Rabbi Ammi states: *ha-matpis ma'os *ma'aser sheni* le-shelamim lo kanu shelamim* — if one attempts to impose the sanctity of Shelamim upon *ma'aser sheni money* itself — to declare that the money has the status of Shelamim funds — it does not work. The Shelamim sanctity does not take hold. This is a striking ruling. After all, one is permitted to use *ma'aser sheni money* to purchase an animal and bring it as a Shelamim. So what is the difference between using the money to buy an animal that becomes a Shelamim versus imposing Shelamim sanctity on the money itself?

Rabbi Ammi's reasoning is: *de-lo alima kedushas ha-shelamim le-meichel kedushas ma'aser sheni* — the sanctity of Shelamim is not powerful enough to take effect on top of the existing sanctity of *ma'aser sheni*. The analogy to the grain case is illuminating. Just as it is problematic to take the *ma'aser sheni* grain itself and turn it into Todah bread — because that forces the same object to carry two competing identities — so too it is problematic to impose Shelamim sanctity directly upon money that already carries the identity of *ma'aser sheni* funds. *ma'aser sheni money* has a very defined character: it is to be used *be-chol asher te'aveh nafshecha* — for whatever food one desires, within the framework of eating before God in Yerushalayim. When one purchases an animal with that money, the money fulfills its function and the animal now stands separately as a Shelamim. That is not a conflict. But to declare the money itself to be simultaneously *ma'aser sheni money* and Shelamim money is to redefine its character and limit what it can be used for — something the sanctity of Shelamim cannot accomplish over and above the pre-existing sanctity of *ma'aser sheni*.

### Rabbi Zeira Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַלּוֹקֵחַ חַיָּה לְזִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים, וּבְהֵמָה לִבְשַׂר תַּאֲוָה – לֹא יָצָא הָעוֹר לְחוּלִּין. לָאו לְמֵימְרָא דְּקָנֵי שְׁלָמִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 1:4): Ideally, one should use second-tithe money to purchase peace offerings. If one purchases a peace offering with second-tithe money, only the flesh of the animal is consecrated, while its hide is non-sacred. The mishna teaches: With regard to **one who** uses second-tithe money in an improper manner and **purchases an undomesticated animal for a peace offering,** a purpose for which an undomesticated animal cannot be used, **or** purchases **a domesticated animal for meat of desire,** i.e., ordinary meat, the Sages penalized him and decreed that **the hide does not become non-sacred.** The Gemara asks: **Is that not to say that** the hide of the animal purchased with the second-tithe money **assumes** the status of **a peace offering,** and it must be sold and the money used to purchase a peace offering, in contradiction to the statement of Rabbi Ami?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב: לָא קָנֵי שְׁלָמִים, וּמַאי ״לֹא יָצָא הָעוֹר לְחוּלִּין״? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵינוֹ בְּתוֹרַת לָצֵאת הָעוֹר לְחוּלִּין, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: נַעֲשָׂה כְּלוֹקֵחַ שׁוֹר לַחֲרִישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Wasn't it stated concerning that** mishna that **Rav said:** The hide of the animal purchased with the second-tithe money does **not assume** the status of **a peace offering; and what** is the meaning of the mishna that **the hide does not become non-sacred? This** is what the mishna **is saying:** The hide, as part of an undomesticated animal, is **not in the category of** a peace offering at all so that its **hide would become non-sacred. What is the reason? Rabba says:** Since he used the second-tithe money in an improper manner, **it is considered as if he purchased an ox for plowing,** to which no sanctity of second tithe applies.

Under ordinary circumstances, when one makes a standard, approved purchase with *ma'aser sheni money* — such as buying wine, where part of the cost goes to the bottle — the container or accessory element is considered as freed from *ma'aser sheni* sanctity, in the same way that the leather of an animal properly purchased for Shelamim is freed from *ma'aser sheni* status once the transaction is legitimate. The principle of *yatza or le-chulin* reflects the general rule that a normal, appropriate purchase with *ma'aser sheni money* frees the non-food components from further *ma'aser sheni* obligations. But in these reversed, irregular cases, the purchase is considered abnormal, and so the leather does not gain this benefit.

The Gemara assumes that when the Mishnah says the leather *lo yatza le-chulin* in the case of one who bought livestock for ordinary meat, it may be implying that the leather has both *ma'aser sheni* sanctity *and* Shelamim sanctity — since Shelamim sanctity apparently attached despite the irregular purchase. If so, this would contradict Rabbi Ammi's ruling that Shelamim sanctity cannot take hold on *ma'aser sheni money* or property.

Rav responds: *lo kani Shelamim* — in fact, no Shelamim sanctity attaches at all. When the Mishnah says the leather *lo yatza le-chulin*, it means only that the leather retains its *ma'aser sheni* sanctity. *lo yatza le-chulin* describes the failure of the leather to be freed from *ma'aser sheni*, nothing more. Why does it retain this sanctity and not benefit from *yatza le-chulin*? Rava explains: *na'aseh ke-kone'ach shor la-charishoh* — this purchase is like using *ma'aser sheni* funds to buy an ox for plowing, a complete misappropriation. In such a complete misuse, the normal rules of *yatza le-chulin* do not apply at all; the entire transaction retains its *ma'aser sheni* character, and the skin is simply not freed. There is no doubling of sanctity, and Rabbi Ammi's position stands.

### Analogy to Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, הַמַּתְפִּיס מְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי לִשְׁלָמִים: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: קָנֵי, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute with regard to **one who designates second-tithe money for a peace offering. Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The money **assumes** the status of a peace offering, and **Rabbi Elazar says:** The money does **not assume** the status of a peace offering.

The Gemara then presents a fresh formulation of the original debate as a direct Tannaitic dispute. *ha-matpis ma'os *ma'aser sheni* le-shelamim* — one who imposes Shelamim sanctity upon *ma'aser sheni money*: Rabbi Yochanan says it takes effect; Rabbi Elazar says it does not.

### Avoiding Double Identity


The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar concerning whether one can impose Shelamim sanctity on *ma'aser sheni money* is now framed in terms of the classic dispute about the underlying nature of *ma'aser sheni*.

### Practical Difficulty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: מַעֲשֵׂר מָמוֹן הֶדְיוֹט הוּא – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּקָנֵי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: מַעֲשֵׂר מָמוֹן גָּבוֹהַּ הוּא. מַאן דְּאָמַר לָא קָנֵי – כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר קָנֵי – כֵּיוָן דְּמַעֲשֵׂר (קרי) [קָרֵיב] לֵיהּ שְׁלָמִים, כִּי מַיתְפְּסַתְּ לֵיהּ נָמֵי תָּפֵיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **And according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who said:** Second **tithe is non-sacred property, everyone agrees that** the money **assumes** the status of a peace offering. **When they disagree,** it is **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Meir, who said:** Second **tithe is property** belonging to the **Most High. The one who says** that the money does **not assume** the status of a peace offering holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** and one may not designate property of the Most High for another purpose. **And the one who says** that the money **assumes** the status of a peace offering holds that **since** second **tithe is called a peace offering,** as an animal purchased with second-tithe money without specification is brought as a peace offering, **when he designates** the money for a peace offering, it **as well** is **designated** with the sanctity of a peace offering.

The Gemara explains: *ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda de-amar ma'aser *mamon hedyot* hu de-kuli alma lo pligi de-kani* — according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *ma'aser sheni* is *mamon hedyot*, lay property that essentially belongs to the owner, all agree that Shelamim sanctity can take effect upon it. If it is your property, you have the ability to impose an additional sanctity upon it.

### Rabbi Ammi's Rule


The classic dispute in the Gemara between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir concerns whether *ma'aser sheni* is *mamon hedyot* or *mamon gavoah* — whether it is fundamentally the owner's property or temple property. The kedusha of *ma'aser sheni* defines restrictions on its use and the way in which it is consumed, but unlike hekdesh in the sense of a donation to the Beit HaMikdash, *ma'aser sheni* is ultimately eaten by the owner. So the question is whether the concept of kedusha here is one of ownership — like hekdesh, where the temple owns the item — or one of status, where the owner retains the item but it has a defined sacred character. Rabbi Yehuda holds it is the owner's property; Rabbi Meir holds that because its use is so constrained, it is in effect temple property. One of the classic tests for this question in the Gemara is whether one may use *ma'aser sheni* produce to betroth a woman — *harei at mekudeshet li* — since that requires giving her something of value that is one's own.

The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar, the Gemara explains, is relevant only according to Rabbi Meir, who holds it is *mamon gavoah*. *ki pligi aliba de-rabbi meir*: the one who says the Shelamim sanctity does *not* take hold follows Rabbi Meir straightforwardly — if it is not your property, you have no authority to impose a new sanctity upon it. But the one who says it *does* take hold — *keivan de-ma'aser karev Shelamim ki matpis lei nami tapis* — argues that since *ma'aser sheni* is naturally and characteristically brought as a Shelamim, imposing Shelamim sanctity upon it is not really a reorientation of its character but rather a directing of it toward its most natural use, and therefore even if it is not fully your property, imposing this sanctity does not constitute a contradiction.

One might ask: why frame this entire debate within Rabbi Meir's position? Even according to Rabbi Yehuda, who says it is *mamon hedyot*, there could still be a question of whether the competing kedushos conflict. The answer implicit in the Gemara seems to be that once you have resolved the problem of the competing sanctities — which the reasoning about Ma'aser naturally being brought as Shelamim addresses — and once you have established that the person has the property rights to impose a new sanctity, both conditions are satisfied. The Gemara places the debate within Rabbi Meir because that is where the more interesting question lies: can one address the conflict-of-sanctities problem even when the prerequisite of ownership is itself in doubt?

### Sanctity Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמַּתְפִּיס מְעוֹת מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי לִשְׁלָמִים, kshe-podan mosif aleihem shnei chumashim — echad le-kodesh ve-echad le-ma'aser.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the statement of Rabbi Elazar from a *baraita*: In a case involving **one who designates second-tithe money for a peace offering,** the sanctity of a peace offering is applied to this money in addition to the sanctity of second tithe. Therefore, **when he redeems** this money, **he adds to it two-fifths:** One-fifth **for the** redemption of the **sacrificial** sanctity of a peace offering **and** one-fifth **for** the redemption of second **tithe.** Evidently, the sanctity of a peace offering does take effect on second-tithe money.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הִיא? הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Do you maintain** that this *baraita* **is agreed** upon **by everyone?** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that second-tithe money is non-sacred property. Accordingly, the sanctity of a **peace** offering takes effect on the money. Rabbi Elazar holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir.

A Baraita is cited that directly assumes one can impose Shelamim sanctity upon *ma'aser sheni money*: *ha-matpis ma'os *ma'aser sheni* le-shelamim, kshe-podan mosif aleihem shnei chumashim — echad le-kodesh ve-echad le-ma'aser* — one who subsequently redeems this doubly-sanctified money adds two fifths: one for the Shelamim sanctity and one for the *ma'aser sheni* sanctity.

This is itself a notable ruling, since we do not normally find the requirement to add a fifth when redeeming Shelamim sanctity — the fifth is a feature of redeeming *ma'aser sheni* and of redeeming certain forms of hekdesh, but it is not routinely applied to Korban sanctity. Tosafot notes the difficulty, citing a passage in Perek HaZahav which implies there is no fifth for redeeming Shelamim. In any case, the Baraita clearly assumes that both sanctities are present and that the matpis indeed takes effect.

The Gemara responds: *mi savas de-be'al korcho* — who said this Baraita represents a unanimous position? *ha mani* — this is Rabbi Yehuda. We already established that according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *ma'aser sheni* is *mamon hedyot*, all agree the matpis takes effect. A Baraita consistent with Rabbi Yehuda's position does not resolve the dispute as it stands according to Rabbi Meir.

## Doubled Sanctities

### Ownership vs. Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִנַּיִן לָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי תּוֹדָה״, שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְזָבַחְתָּ פֶּסַח לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ צֹאן וּבָקָר״. וַהֲלֹא פֶּסַח אֵין בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים וּמִן הָעִזִּים, אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״צֹאן וּבָקָר״? לְהַקִּישׁ כׇּל הַבָּא מִן הַצֹּאן וּמִן הַבָּקָר לְפֶסַח: מָה פֶּסַח דָּבָר שֶׁבְּחוֹבָה וְאֵין בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁבְּחוֹבָה אֵין בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **From where** is it derived with regard to **one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a thanks offering, that he may bring** it **only from non-sacred** money? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "And you shall sacrifice the Paschal offering to the Lord your God, of the flock and the herd"** (Deuteronomy 16:2). The verse is difficult: **Doesn't the Paschal offering come only from lambs and goats? If so, why does the verse state: "The flock and the herd"?** It is **to juxtapose all** offerings **that come from the flock and from the herd to** the **Paschal offering,** teaching that **just as** the **Paschal offering** is **a matter of obligation and comes only from non-sacred** money, **so too any matter of obligation comes only from non-sacred** money.

The Gemara now turns to the main rule of the Mishnah that follows: *minayin le-omer harei alai Todah shelo yavi ela min ha-chulin* — from where do we know that if someone says *harei alai Todah*, I undertake to bring a Todah, he may only bring it from Chulin funds, not from *ma'aser sheni money*? The source is the verse: *ve-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar* — you shall slaughter the Pesach offering to Hashem your God, sheep and cattle. This verse presents a well-known halachic difficulty: the Pesach comes only from sheep and goats, not from cattle. What then does the verse mean by *tzon u-vacar*?

The answer is: the verse creates a *hekesh*, linking all offerings brought from sheep and cattle to the Pesach. *mah pesach davar shebechova ve-ein ba ela min ha-chulin* — just as the Pesach is an obligatory offering and comes only from Chulin funds, so too *kol davar shebechova ein ba ela min ha-chulin* — all obligatory offerings may only come from Chulin funds. Therefore, if someone says *harei alai Todah* or *harei alai Shelamim* — once he has made it an obligation — he may only bring it from Chulin funds.

The best available reconciliation of *tzon u-bacar* with the plain sense of the text is that the cattle (*bakar*) refer to the Shelamim offerings that accompanied the Pesach, not to the Pesach animal itself. That reading at least makes the verse internally coherent, even if it is not the most obvious pshat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיכָךְ, הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי תּוֹדָה״, ״הֲרֵי עָלַי שְׁלָמִים״, הוֹאִיל וּבָאִים חוֹבָה – לֹא יָבֹאוּ אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין, וּנְסָכִים בְּכׇל מָקוֹם – לֹא יָבֹאוּ אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Therefore,** in the case of **one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a thanks offering,** or: **It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a peace offering, since** these offerings **come** as **an obligation** due to his vow, **they may be brought only from non-sacred** money. **And libations, in any case, may be brought only from non-sacred** money, and not from second-tithe money, because second-tithe money must be used to purchase items eaten by people, while libations are poured out next to the altar.

The Mishnah adds that Nesachim — libation offerings — may *never* be brought from *ma'aser sheni* funds, even without an explicit obligation, because Nesachim are never consumed by the owner. The entire rationale for permitting *ma'aser sheni* funds for voluntary Shelamim or Todah is tied to the eating — the *ve-achalta sham* framework, the consumption before God that is the common thread between *ma'aser sheni* and these offerings. Nesachim are poured on the Mizbeach and nobody eats them. Using *ma'aser sheni money* for something that will never be eaten at all is a fundamental contradiction of the purpose of *ma'aser sheni* funds.

### Improper Uses


The same principle extends to other offerings that are not eaten by the owner. An Olah, for example, is entirely consumed on the Mizbeach, and there would be no basis for using *ma'aser sheni* funds toward it, since the rationale of *ve-achalta sham* does not apply. The Mishnah chose Nesachim as its example, perhaps because they represent a somewhat lower-profile case than Olah, but the logic applies equally. The permissibility of *ma'aser sheni* funds for Shelamim and Todah is rooted specifically in the shared character of eating before God in Yerushalayim; remove that element and the permission falls away.

### Yatza Or L'Chullin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּפֶסַח גּוּפֵיהּ מְנָא לַן? דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר פֶּסַח בְּמִצְרַיִם, וְנֶאֱמַר פֶּסַח לְדוֹרוֹת, מָה פֶּסַח הָאָמוּר בְּמִצְרַיִם לֹא בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין, אַף פֶּסַח הָאָמוּר לְדוֹרוֹת לֹא בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the *halakha* that any matter of obligation must be brought from non-sacred property is derived from the *halakha* of the Paschal offering. The Gemara asks: **And** concerning **the Paschal offering itself, from where do we** derive that it is brought only from non-sacred property? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: "Paschal offering" is stated** in the Torah **with regard to** the offering sacrificed in **Egypt** (see Exodus 12:3), **and "Paschal offering" is stated with regard to** the yearly obligation throughout the **generations** (see Exodus 12:25). Therefore, **just as the Paschal offering stated with regard to Egypt was brought only from non-sacred** property, as there was no second tithe in Egypt, **so too the Paschal offering stated with regard to** the obligation throughout the **generations may be brought only from non-sacred** property.

The Gemara now asks: we have used the Pesach as the paradigm case for the rule that obligatory offerings come only from Chulin — but how do we know that the Pesach itself may not be brought from *ma'aser sheni money*? A Baraita records the dispute. Rabbi Eliezer argues by a gezerah shavah of sorts, comparing the *pesach mitzrayim* to the *pesach le-dorot*: just as the Pesach in Egypt did not come from *ma'aser sheni money* — because *ma'aser sheni* had not yet been commanded, and in any case *ma'aser sheni* applies only to produce grown in the Land of Israel — so too the Pesach for all future generations comes only from Chulin.

### Direct Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וְכִי דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר? אָמַר לוֹ: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר, רְאָיָיה גְּדוֹלָה הִיא, וְנִלְמַד הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: But does one derive** the **possible from** the **impossible?** Does one derive the *halakha* with regard to the Paschal offering of the generations, when there exists the possibility of using second-tithe money, from the *halakha* with regard to the Paschal offering sacrificed in Egypt, when there was no second tithe? Rabbi Eliezer **said to him: Even though** it was **impossible** to bring the Paschal offering in Egypt from consecrated money, **it is a great proof, and we shall derive** this *halakha* **from it.**

Rabbi Akiva challenges Rabbi Eliezer: *ve-chi danin efshar mi-she'ee efshar* — can one learn from a case where something was possible to a case where it was impossible? In Egypt, using *ma'aser sheni money* was not merely impermissible — it was categorically unavailable. One cannot derive a prohibition for later generations from a situation where there was simply no option.

Rabbi Eliezer responds: *af al pi she'ee efshar, raya gedolah hi* — even though it was impossible, it is still a significant proof. The fact that the Pesach could only come from Chulin funds, even in a situation where *ma'aser sheni* did not exist and never could have applied, establishes the essential character of the Pesach as a Chulin obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָזַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְדָנוֹ דִּין אַחֵר: מָה לְפֶסַח מִצְרַיִם, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין טָעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, תֹּאמַר בְּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת שֶׁטָּעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva then presented a different logical derivation** to reject the proof of Rabbi Eliezer: One cannot derive the *halakha* with regard to the Paschal offering of the generations from that of the Paschal offering in Egypt, as **what** is notable **about the Paschal offering** sacrificed in **Egypt?** It is notable in **that** it **did not require** the **placement of blood** on the altar nor that the **sacrificial portions** be consumed **by the altar.** Can **you say** the same **with regard to the Paschal offering** of the **generations, which requires** the **placement of blood** on the altar **and** that the **sacrificial portions** be consumed **by the altar?**

Rabbi Akiva presses again with a different challenge: even setting aside the *efshar/ee efshar* problem, the *pesach mitzrayim* was fundamentally different from the Pesach le-dorot — it did not involve the placing of blood and the *eimurim* on the Mizbeach (*matan damim ve-eimurim le-gabei mizbeach*). How can one derive rules for the Pesach le-dorot, which requires this altar service, from a Pesach that had none of it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ אֶת הָעֲבֹדָה הַזֹּאת בַּחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה״, שֶׁיִּהְיוּ כׇּל עֲבוֹדוֹת שֶׁל חוֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה כָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to him:** The Paschal offerings should be compared, **as the** verse **states** with regard to the Paschal offering of the generations: "And it shall be when the Lord shall bring you into the land of the Canaanite, and the Hittite, and the Amorite, and the Hivite, and the Jebusite, which He swore to your fathers to give you, a land flowing with milk and honey, **that you shall perform this service in this month"** (Exodus 13:5). This indicates **that all the services of this month** for the generations **shall be like this,** the Paschal offering of Egypt.

Rabbi Eliezer responds with a verse: *ve-avadta et ha-avoda ha-zot ba-chodesh ha-zeh* — "and you shall perform this service in this month." This verse, given during the *pesach mitzrayim*, explicitly states that all future observances of the Pesach will follow the model of the first. Thereby all future Pesachim are linked to the *pesach mitzrayim*, and the rules of the latter apply to the former.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אִי סָבַר לַהּ דְּאֵין דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר – לֵיקוּ בְּמִילְּתֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the opinion of Rabbi Akiva: Initially, Rabbi Akiva rejects the statement of Rabbi Eliezer based on the claim that one cannot derive the possible from the impossible. He then rejects the statement of Rabbi Eliezer on a technicality, namely that the two Paschal offerings have different sacrificial requirements. The Gemara therefore challenges: **And** as for **Rabbi Akiva, if he holds that one does not derive** the **possible from** the **impossible,** then **let him stand by his statement.**

### Mamon Hedyot vs. Gavoah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הֲדַר בֵּיהּ, וְהַאי דְּלָא גָּמַר מִפֶּסַח מִצְרַיִם מִשּׁוּם הַאי פִּירְכָא הוּא, פֶּסַח מִדְבָּר יוֹכִיחַ!
>
> **Translation:** And **if he retracted** and conceded that one derives the possible from the impossible, **and** the fact **that he did not derive** the *halakha* with regard to the Paschal offering of the generations **from** the *halakha* of **the Paschal offering** in **Egypt is due to that refutation** that he proposed, i.e., that the Paschal offerings are different, then **the Paschal offering** that was sacrificed in the second year in **the wilderness will prove** that this refutation is incorrect. That offering did require placement of the blood and sacrificial portions on the altar, yet it was brought only from non-sacred money, as there were no tithes in the wilderness.

The Gemara then notes an apparent difficulty in the flow of the argument: why did Rabbi Eliezer, when faced with Rabbi Akiva's second challenge about the absence of Mizbeach service in the *pesach mitzrayim*, not simply shift to the *pesach midbar* — the Pesach brought in the desert, which did involve blood and eimurim on the Mizbeach, and equally could not have involved *ma'aser sheni money* since there was no *ma'aser sheni* produce growing in the desert? Why did Rabbi Eliezer need the *ve-avadta* verse at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא קָאָמַר: לְדִידִי אֵין דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר, לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר – מָה לְפֶסַח מִצְרַיִם שֶׁכֵּן אֵינוֹ טָעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, תֹּאמַר בְּפֶסַח דּוֹרוֹת שֶׁטָּעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Rabbi Akiva **stated** this objection **in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.** He meant as follows: **According to my** opinion, **one does not derive** the **possible from** the **impossible,** and this is a sufficient reason why one cannot derive the *halakha* with regard to the Paschal offering of the generations from the *halakha* of the Paschal offering in Egypt. And even **according to your** opinion, in **which you said** that **one derives** the **possible from** the **impossible,** this comparison can be refuted: **What** is notable **about the Paschal offering** sacrificed in **Egypt?** It is notable in **that** it **did not require** the **placement of blood** on the altar nor that the **sacrificial portions** be consumed **by the altar.** Can **you say** the same **with regard to the Paschal offering** of the **generations, which requires** the **placement of blood** on the altar **and** that the **sacrificial portions** be consumed **by the altar?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in response to this, **Rabbi Eliezer said to him** that the *halakha* with regard to the Paschal offering of the generations should be derived from the *halakha* of the Paschal offering in Egypt, since the verse states with regard to the Paschal offering of the generations: **"That you shall keep** this service in this month."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: פֶּסַח מִדְבָּר יוֹכִיחַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why must **Rabbi Eliezer** cite this verse? **Let him say to** Rabbi Akiva: **The Paschal offering** brought in **the wilderness will prove** that this refutation is incorrect, since it did require placement of the blood and consumption of sacrificial portions on the altar, yet it was brought only from non-sacred money.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ, לְדִידִי דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר, וּמִשּׁוּם הַאי פִּירְכָא – פֶּסַח מִדְבָּר יוֹכִיחַ, לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ אֵין דָּנִין אֶפְשָׁר מִשֶּׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר – ״וְעָבַדְתָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rabbi Eliezer **stated** his opinion **in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Akiva.** He meant as follows: **In my** opinion, **one derives** the **possible from** the **impossible. And as** for **that refutation** that you raised, that the Paschal offering in Egypt and the Paschal offering of the generations have different sacrificial requirements, **the Paschal offering** of **the wilderness will prove** that this is not a valid refutation. **According to your** opinion, in **which you said** that **one does not derive** the **possible from** the **impossible,** the verse states: **"That you shall keep** this service in this month," indicating that the Paschal offering of the generations is compared to the Paschal offering in Egypt.

The Gemara resolves this by explaining that Rabbi Eliezer was addressing Rabbi Akiva on his own terms. Rabbi Akiva holds that one cannot derive rulings from *ee efshar* situations. Both the *pesach mitzrayim* and the *pesach midbar* were equally impossible cases for *ma'aser sheni*, so according to Rabbi Akiva's principle, neither can serve as a source. Therefore Rabbi Eliezer shifts strategies: instead of offering another *ee efshar* example, he produces the *ve-avadta* verse, which explicitly links the *pesach mitzrayim* to all future Pesachim. Once there is an explicit textual link, the objection that one cannot derive from the possible from the impossible becomes irrelevant.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי לִיפְרוֹךְ? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, אֵין מְשִׁיבִין עַל הַהֶיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But even now, let** Rabbi Akiva **refute** this comparison by the same claim that one cannot derive the possible from the impossible. **Rav Sheshet says: That is to say** that **one cannot refute a juxtaposition,** i.e., one cannot refute such a derivation based on reasoning.

This leads to Rav Sheshet's conclusion: *zot omeret ein meshivin al ha-heikesh* — this tells us the famous principle that one does not refute a hekesh. Once the Torah explicitly links two cases, as *ve-avadta* links the *pesach mitzrayim* to the Pesach le-dorot, the ordinary categories of objection that might be raised against a logical inference — such as the *efshar/ee efshar* distinction or the difference in Mizbeach service — no longer apply. The textual equation overrides them. This principle, noted by Rav Sheshet here, is cited frequently throughout the Gemara and reflects the qualitatively different and conclusive force of an explicit Scriptural equation compared to an inferential one.
