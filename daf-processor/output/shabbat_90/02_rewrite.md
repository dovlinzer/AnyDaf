# Shabbat 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Matznia & Intent

### Context & Review

The sugya opens by recalling the context established in the preceding discussion. The *mishna* had ruled that if one sets aside even a smaller-than-standard quantity of an item — a practice known as *matznia* — and subsequently carries it out on Shabbat, one is *chayav*. The *gemara* then made the bold extension that even without having formally set the item aside beforehand, the very act of taking it out for an express purpose suffices to confer subjective significance upon it and render one liable. This extension, however, raises the obvious question: if intent alone suffices, what does the *mishna*'s case of *matznia* add? The answer is that the *mishna* addresses the situation where one originally set the item aside but then, at the moment of carrying it out, was not consciously thinking of that purpose — he was carrying it out without any particular intent at all.

### Intent Negation

The *gemara* begins the current amud by asking: why was it not self-evident that the original act of *matznia* would still govern? One might have argued *bitulei batlei machshavto* — that by subsequently carrying the item out without any focused purpose, one has effectively negated one's original intent, and with it the subjective significance one had assigned to the smaller quantity. The *gemara* therefore teaches *kol ha'oseh al da'at rishon hu oseh*: since one is acting in accordance with a broad, general intent, the original intent that gave significance to the lesser quantity remains operative.

### One Kernel Rule

*Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* then states that *Rabbi Meir* — assumed to be the *stam Tanna* of the *mishna* — held that one is *chayav* for carrying out even a single kernel of wheat, provided one is taking it out *lizriah*, for the purpose of planting. The *gemara* asks: is this not obvious? The *mishna* already says *kol shehu* — even the smallest amount — so of course a single kernel suffices. The *gemara* answers that one might have thought *kol shehu* means not literally any amount whatsoever, but rather the smallest of the standard rabbinic measurements — a *kezayis*, a *k'adashah*, or the like — as opposed to the larger *grogeret*. One might not have realized it means something truly beyond the scale of standard *shiurim* entirely. The *gemara* therefore teaches that yes, it means the absolute minimum. But as noted in the prior discussion, the deeper *chiddush* of *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* is the *gemara*'s implicit assumption from the previous session: that even without prior *matznia*, the mere fact of taking the item out for a specific purpose confers the subjective significance. Until now, all the *mishnayot* had addressed only cases of prior *matznia*.

### Kol Shehu Debate

This understanding that subjective intent can redefine the minimum quantity — even below any standard rabbinic measure — opens the floor to a broader inquiry into the limits of subjective redefinition.

## Challenge Minimums

### Symmetry Problem

*Rav Yitzchak brei d'Rav Yehuda* presses the logic: if intent can shrink the minimum below the standard *shiurim*, why cannot intent also expand it? If someone decides that his act of significance is removing the entire contents of his house as one grand unit — *cheishev lehotzi kol beito* — should we not say he is not *chayav* until he has taken out everything, since in his subjective definition that is the singular act of significance? Some *Rishonim*, including *Tosafot*, understand this question as directed not specifically at *Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* but at the *mishna* itself: why, if subjective intent governs, does it operate only in the direction of stringency and not leniency?

### Stringency Only

The *gemara* answers that the principle of subjective intent operates only to increase liability, not to diminish it. Intent can make something *chayav* that would otherwise be exempt; it cannot make something exempt that would otherwise be *chayav*.

### Objective Norms

The underlying principle is *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — when one's personal intent conflicts with the objective norm shared by all people, one's individual intent is negated. Where ordinary people would regard a given item or act as significant, that significance stands regardless of whether a particular individual assigns it weight. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* operates in precisely the opposite direction: when an individual assigns significance to something that other people would not consider significant, his subjective weight elevates it. He cannot, however, use his subjective frame to strip significance from something that the objective world regards as meaningful. This, one notes, also effectively answers the question implicitly: the answer was in some sense obvious, but the question was raising a more fundamental issue — where else in *halacha* do we find minimum quantities defined by subjective intent, even in the stringent direction? The case of *achshavei*, where one's act of eating something normally unfit confers significance upon it, is a related but distinct phenomenon — it concerns the act of giving significance, not the quantitative threshold as such.

## R. Shimon Uniform Rule

### Significance Binding

The *gemara* now notes that our *mishna* does not follow the position of *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*. The *braita* states: *klal amar Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* formulated a general principle. Anything that is not ordinarily the type of object people set aside, and whose quantity is not ordinarily one that people preserve — *huchshar l'zeh vehitznio* — yet this particular person found it meaningful and set it aside. We know that if he himself takes it out, he is *chayav*. But *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* adds: *uva acher vehootzio* — if someone else then comes and takes out that same object — *nischayav zeh bemachshavto shel zeh* — that other person is liable on account of the first person's intent.

What troubles *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* is the lack of uniformity. If a quantity is *chayav* for one person and not for another, the minimum has become a moving target depending on whose perspective governs. He therefore maintains that once someone has conferred significance upon an object, it carries that significance for everyone. The *gemara* notes that our *mishna* does not follow this view — we do not say that one person's *matznia* creates universal liability for all subsequent actors. For everyone else, it remains the standard minimum. Only the one who set it aside bears the elevated standard for himself.

### Absurd Examples

To illustrate how counterintuitive this principle can become: consider someone who removes chewing gum from one's mouth, wraps it, and sets it aside for later use. For virtually everyone else, this object carries no significance whatsoever — no one would think to set aside chewed gum. Yet if one has done so, according to *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*, someone else who then carries it out would be *chayav* on the strength of the original owner's intent. The tea bag one intends to reuse serves as a similarly striking example. The *mishna*, by rejecting *Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*, limits subjective significance to the person who created it.

## Intent Changes

### Simple Swap

*Rava* in the name of *Rav Nachman* addresses a new set of cases in which intent changes during the course of carrying. The baseline ruling: if one took out food the size of a *grogeret* intending to eat it — the standard minimum for food — and then, while still in transit and before putting it down, changed one's mind and decided to use it as seeds for planting, one is *chayav*. The reverse — taking it out to plant and then deciding to eat it — is equally *chayav*. The *gemara* asks: *p'shita* — is this not obvious? A *grogeret* is the minimum for eating, and planting requires only a few seeds, so in either direction one had the minimum. The *gemara* answers that one might have thought that the two stages of the carrying act — *akira* (picking up) and *hanachah* (putting down) — must be defined by the same intent. Since subjective intent has been given such weight as to redefine the minimum itself, perhaps an act begun with one definition and concluded with another is not legally a single unified act. The *gemara* rules against this concern: the *grogeret* quantity suffices at either stage regardless of the change in intent, and the act remains one *melacha*.

### Half Size Expansion

*Rava* then poses a genuine question. One took out half a *grogeret* intending to plant it — a minimum for planting. During transit, the item expanded — perhaps it was tightly compressed and unfolded, or perhaps it absorbed moisture — and by the time of *hanachah* it had grown to the size of a full *grogeret*. One then changed one's mind and decided to eat it. *Mahu* — what is the *halacha*?

### Combining Halves

The question is not trivial. At the moment of *akira*, the item did not qualify for the food category — it was only half a *grogeret*. It qualified only for the planting category. By the moment of *hanachah*, it had become a full *grogeret* and one was treating it as food. The question is whether the two halves of the *melacha* can combine: the beginning half defined the act as a planting act, and the concluding half defined it as a food act. Since the subjective intent has been shown to be definitionally significant, perhaps an act whose beginning and end are qualitatively different — each qualifying under a different category — cannot be viewed as one unified *melacha*.

### Original Intent

The *gemara* presents the counterargument: *keivan d'ilu ishtika hava michayev amachshava d'zria* — since, had one simply not changed one's mind and proceeded with the original planting intent, one would have been *chayav*, the subsequent change of intent does not break the continuity of the *melacha*. One cannot escape liability by having added a new intent on top of an already-complete set of conditions for *chiyuv*. Thus one would be *chayav* even here.

### Reverse Scenario

The question is then flipped: one took out a full *grogeret* intending to eat it — satisfying the food minimum at the outset — and during transit it shriveled (or some fell away), reducing the quantity below a *grogeret*. One then changed one's mind and decided to use what remained for planting. *Mahu*? Here the asymmetry is more acute. Had one maintained the original intent of eating, one would have been *patur* at the end, because there was no longer enough for the food category. The only thing that could potentially render one liable is the new intent to use the reduced quantity for planting. But the *akira* was performed treating it as food, and the *hanachah* treats it as seeds. Are these two legally incompatible halves of a *melacha*? The *gemara* leaves open whether *batar hashta azlinan u'mechayev* — we follow the end-state and one is *chayav* — or whether the discontinuity between the two halves is fatal to *chiyuv*.

### Shrink Reexpand

An even more extreme case: one took out a *grogeret* for eating — minimum satisfied. During transit it shriveled below the minimum. Before being put down, it re-expanded to a full *grogeret*. The endpoints both satisfy the minimum; the intermediate period did not. This question is qualitatively different from the preceding ones, because here there is no change of intent at all. The issue is not whether the subjective redefinition of the minimum can bridge two differently-intended halves; it is a question about the nature of the carrying act itself. Does the transporting process itself need to maintain the minimum at every moment? While one might expect the *gemara* to frame this as a question about the centrality of the transporting phase, the *gemara* instead frames it strikingly in terms of *dichui* — the concept of disqualification.

### Permanent Disqualificati…

*Yesh dichui le'inyan Shabbat o ein dichui le'inyan Shabbat*? — Is there a principle of permanent disqualification (*dichui*) applicable to the laws of Shabbat? Normally *dichui* arises in the context of *mitzvot* and *korbanot*: if an animal had a *mum* (blemish) that temporarily rendered it unfit for sacrifice, and the *mum* later healed, some hold it remains permanently rejected. The *gemara* here applies this logic — strikingly — not to qualifying for a *mitzvah* or a *korban*, but to qualifying as the object of a *melacha*. Did the temporary shrinking below the minimum "reject" the object, such that even after re-expansion it can no longer serve as the object of a complete act of *hotza'ah*? By framing it this way, the *gemara* is not emphasizing that the carrying process itself requires an ongoing minimum, but rather that once disqualified at a moment in the middle, perhaps the object is permanently disqualified. All of these questions are left unresolved — *teiku*.

## Throw Food Tamei

### Setup

*Rava* poses a further question to *Rav Nachman*: *Zarak kezayis truma levayis tamei* — one threw a *kezayis* of *terumah* into a house containing a corpse, thereby rendering the house a *ohel* for the purposes of *tum'at meit*.

### Category Separation

The *gemara* immediately asks: *lemai* — for what legal question is this scenario being posed? If the question is about Shabbat liability for carrying from *reshut harabim* into the house — *kegrogeret ba'inan* — food must be the size of a *grogeret*, which is larger than a *kezayis*, so there is no *chiyuv* in any case. If the question is about *tum'ah* — whether the food becomes *tamei* — *k'beitzah ochlin ba'inan*: we require food the size of an egg. At this point, an important dispute between *Rashi* and *Rabbeinu Tam* is directly relevant throughout *Shas*. *Rashi* holds in many places that the *beitzah* standard applies only to the capacity of food to transmit *tum'ah* to the next object in the chain; for food itself to become *tamei*, even a tiny amount suffices. *Rabbeinu Tam* disagrees and holds that even for food to become *tamei* in the first instance, a *beitzah* is required. The *gemara* here states *k'beitzah ochlin ba'inan*, which can be read consistently with either position.

The *gemara* then clarifies: the question is indeed about Shabbat. The scenario is refined: *kegon d'ika pachas mibeitzah ochlin* — in the house there was already less than a *beitzah* of food. Specifically, there was a *beitzah* minus one *kezayis*. The thrown *kezayis* flew in and landed on the existing food, combining with it — *vehai mashlimei lebeitzah* — completing it to a full *beitzah*. This combined quantity now becomes *tamei* in a more significant sense: it can transmit *tum'ah* or, according to *Rabbeinu Tam*, it has now reached the threshold for becoming *tamei* at all. The question is: since the act of throwing the *kezayis* in and having it land has now activated the *tum'ah* category — because the combined food has reached the *beitzah* threshold — does the significance it acquires in the realm of *tum'ah* transfer to confer Shabbat liability, even though a *kezayis* is ordinarily insufficient for *hotza'ah* on Shabbat?

### Why No Transfer

The *gemara* cites a *braita* in which *Abba Shaul* states that one who carries *shtei halechem* or *lechem hapanim* from the *Beis Hamikdash* is liable for Shabbat only at the *grogeret* standard. The *gemara* asks: *v'amai* — why? One might have argued that since the *halacha* of *yotzei* — food taken out beyond the walls of the *Azarah* — applies at a *kezayis*, the Shabbat liability should similarly apply at a *kezayis*. Analogously, why not say that carrying *chametz* or *chazir* should trigger Shabbat liability at the *kezayis*, since that is the quantity of significance in those areas of *halacha*? The *braita* thus implies that the fact that a certain quantity carries significance in another domain of *halacha* does not automatically transfer that significance to the domain of Shabbat carrying. This should answer *Rava*'s question as well.

### Different Moments

The *gemara* responds that the cases are not comparable. *Miyad d'afkeih chutz lechomat ha'azarah ipsil lei b'yotzei* — the *issur* of *yotzei* takes effect at the moment the food crosses the wall of the *Azarah*. The relevant *kezayis* is measured at that moment. By contrast, *ashabbat lo mechayev ad d'mafik lei lereshut harabim* — Shabbat liability does not arise until the carrier brings the food all the way down into the public domain and performs *hanachah*. The two moments of legal focus — the moment of *yotzei* and the moment of Shabbat carrying — are temporally distinct. Because they occur at different times, the *shiur* that applies at one moment cannot be borrowed to satisfy the requirement of the other.

### Same Moment Issue

But the case *Rava* has posed is different: *hacha shabbat v'tum'ah bahadi hadadi ka'asyan* — here, Shabbat and *tum'ah* occur at the same moment. The instant the *kezayis* of *terumah* lands on the existing food and combines with it, two questions become simultaneously relevant: has the combined food crossed the threshold for *tum'ah*? And has an act of Shabbat carrying been completed? Since both questions are asked at the identical moment, perhaps the *shiur* operative for *tum'ah* at that moment can also supply the Shabbat minimum. The question remains open and unresolved. As noted, what the *gemara*'s entire line of inquiry reflects is the broader consequence of having opened the door to subjective redefinition of minimum quantities. Once the *mishna* established that intent can move the minimum, the *gemara* consistently asks: can other frameworks similarly shift what counts as a sufficient quantity for Shabbat liability?

## Bring Back Inside

### Intent Negation

The *mishna* states: *chazar v'hichniso eino chayav ela k'shiuro* — if after having taken the item out one brings it back inside without having used it for its original purpose, one is no longer *chayav* except according to the standard minimum. The subjective significance that had been conferred, for example by intending to plant a single seed, evaporates once one has abandoned that purpose by returning the item without using it. One might have thought that once significance is conferred, it persists — but the *gemara* says it does not.

### Remains Distinct

*Abaye* refines the case: this ruling applies specifically to a scenario *k'gon shezarko l'otzar umikomo nikar* — where one threw the item back into one's warehouse and it remains identifiable in its location, having landed separately from the main pile rather than merging back into it. The item is thus distinct and has not been fully reabsorbed into the general store. One might have thought that since it remains physically separate — *keivan d'mikomo nikar b'milta kamaitah kai* — it retains the significance originally given to it, as if it had never truly been "returned."

### Negation by Warehouse

The *gemara* teaches otherwise: *mid'zarko l'otzar batulei batlei* — the very act of throwing it back into the warehouse constitutes a negation of the original planting intent. Even if the seed remains visually distinct on the warehouse floor rather than having fallen into the main grain pile, the act of return carries legal weight. Since one is no longer planning to plant it, and one has restored it to the domain of stored grain, the subjective significance is extinguished. It reverts to the objective standard minimum — a quantity that a single seed obviously does not meet.

## Unbroken Carrying

### Two Acts Rule

The *mishna* states: *hamotzi ochlim unetanam al ha-iskupa* — one who takes out food and places it on the threshold — *bein she-chazar v'hotzi'an bein she-hotzi'an acher patur* — whether one subsequently takes it from there into the street, or someone else does, both are exempt. The reason is *mipnei she-lo asa melacha b'vat achat* — the *melacha* was not performed as one continuous act. The two stages were separated by an intervening putting-down.

### Karmelit Status

The *gemara* analyzes the nature of the *iskupa*. It cannot be a *reshut harabim*, because then taking the food from the house to the porch would itself be a complete act of *hotza'ah* from *reshut hayachid* to *reshut harabim*, and one would be *chayav* at that point. It cannot be a *reshut hayachid*, because then taking it from the porch to the street would be going from *reshut hayachid* to *reshut harabim*, again producing *chiyuv*. The *gemara* therefore concludes the *iskupa* must be a *karmelit* — an intermediate domain that is neither *reshut hayachid* nor *reshut harabim*. Taking food from a private domain to a *karmelit* and from a *karmelit* to a public domain are each half-acts, neither of which independently constitutes a complete prohibited *melacha*.

## Basket of Fruit

### Whole Basket Rule

The *mishna* further states: *kupa she-mele'ah peirot* — a basket full of fruit — *venetnah al ha-askufa ha-chitzona* — placed on the outer porch, which here functions as a *reshut harabim* — *af al pi she-rov peiroteiha mi-bachutz patur* — even though most of the fruit overhangs into the public domain, one is exempt — *ad she-yotzi et kol ha-kupa* — until the entire basket has been taken out. Even if numerous individual pieces of fruit are wholly within the public domain, since they remain contained within a basket that is not entirely in the public domain, the carrying act is deemed incomplete. This introduces the principle *eged kli shmei eged* — that being bound within a vessel constitutes a form of legal unification — though the precise scope of this principle will be debated.

## Unbroken Carrying…

### Rest Principle

The *gemara* explains the *mishna*'s teaching about the *iskupa* more precisely. *Ta'ama denach bekarmelit* — the reason one is exempt is specifically because the food came to rest in the *karmelit*. *Ha lo nach bekarmelit chayav* — had it not rested there, one would be *chayav*. This might have seemed obvious, but it is not, because this ruling is *matisin delo k'Ben Azzai* — our *mishna* does not follow *Ben Azzai*. A *braita* teaches: one who carries from a shop to the main street (*paltya*) by way of the *stav* — the colonnaded walkway in front of the shops, a space that is neither *reshut hayachid* nor *reshut harabim* and functions as a *karmelit* — is *chayav*, because one moved without interruption from one domain to the other. *Ben Azzai* rules *patur*. *Ben Azzai* either holds that traversing an intermediate domain breaks the continuity of the act, or applies the principle of *mehalech ke-omeid* — that walking constitutes a series of discrete micro-resting-points, such that passage through an intervening domain counts as an interruption. Our *mishna*, by ruling that it is only the actual putting-down in the *karmelit* that creates the exemption, implicitly rejects *Ben Azzai*: the mere traversal of a *karmelit* without rest does not break the act.

## Basket of Fruit…

### Chizkiyah: Fruits

*Chizkiyah* offers a reading of the *mishna* that sidesteps the concept of *eged kli shmei eged* entirely. He says the ruling — *lo shanu ella b'kupa mele'ah kishuin v'diluin* — applies only to a basket filled with cucumbers and gourds, which are long objects. In such a case, since each individual cucumber extends from inside the house out into the street, no single cucumber has been fully transferred from one domain to the other. The *petur* has nothing to do with the basket as a binding unit; it is simply that each individual fruit object is not yet fully in the public domain. *Aval mele'ah chardal* — but if the basket is filled with mustard seeds, which are tiny, any seed that is over the threshold has been fully carried out, and one would be *chayav*. *Almah kasavar eged kli lav shemei eged* — *Chizkiyah* thus holds that being contained within a vessel does not create a legal unit.

### R. Yochanan: Unit

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees. *Afilu mele'ah chardal patur* — even a basket filled entirely with mustard seeds: if the basket is not fully out, one is exempt. *Almah kasavar eged kli shmei eged* — *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that containment within a vessel creates a single legal unit. The individual seeds or fruits are not evaluated independently; what matters is whether the basket — as the containing unit — has been fully transferred.

### Ambiguous Text

*Rabbi Zeira* observes that the *mishna* is read by each *amora* as supporting his own view, but in fact does not definitively prove either position. On one hand, the *mishna*'s concluding phrase — *ad she-yotzi et kol ha-kupa* — emphasizes the basket as the operative unit, suggesting *eged kli shmei eged* in keeping with *Rabbi Yochanan*. On the other hand, the *mishna*'s opening — *af al pi she-rov peiroteiha mi-bachutz* — implies that if all the fruit were outside, even with part of the basket remaining inside, one might be *chayav*, suggesting the individual fruits are what matters, in keeping with *Chizkiyah*. *Chizkiyah* reconciles the *mishna* by reading *kol ha-kupa* as meaning: when it is the type of basket where taking out the "whole basket" is what matters — i.e., long-object baskets — you are *patur* until all the fruit is out. *Rabbi Yochanan* reconciles it by reading *rov peirot* as describing even the scenario where all the fruit are outside, ruling one still *patur* until the basket itself is fully out. The more natural reading of the *mishna*, it must be said, favors *Rabbi Yochanan*: the *mishna* speaks of a basket of fruit, not specifically of long-stemmed vegetables, and its climactic ruling focuses on the basket as the decisive unit.

### Merchant Baskets

A *meitivei* challenges *Chizkiyah* from a *braita* about a *kupas ha-rochlin* — the spice-merchant's basket, which contained multiple compartments each holding different spices or fragrances. *Af al pi she-rov minin bachutz* — even if most of the different varieties are on the outside — *patur ad she-yotzi et kol ha-kupa* — one is exempt until the entire basket is taken out. This appears to present a case where complete, distinct objects of different types are fully in the public domain yet liability is withheld until the basket itself is fully out — which looks like a clear proof for *eged kli shmei eged* and against *Chizkiyah*.

### Long Objects

*Chizkiyah* responds: *hacha b'mai askinan b'urnosi* — this case also involves long objects. *Rashi* explains these as long cinnamon sticks or similar elongated spices, such that most of each individual object's length is on the outside but the trailing end of every item still remains inside. There is no case here of a fully transferred individual item; each object straddles the threshold. Admittedly, this is a strained reading of a *braita* that would naturally suggest independent, distinct bundles of spice — but it is *Chizkiyah*'s only available response.

## Stealing on Shabbat

### Override Principle

*Rav Bibi bar Abaye* challenges from a ruling familiar from *Ketubot* and elsewhere regarding the principle of *kam lei b'drabba minei* — when two transgressions are committed simultaneously, the more severe overrides, and the lesser penalty is not collected. In the paradigmatic case: one who steals a wallet on Shabbat is *chayav* to pay for the theft. The reason is that the two violations — *geneivah* and Shabbat — do not occur simultaneously. *Geneivah* is effected by *kinyan* — acquisition — which takes place when the thief picks up the wallet or draws it into his possession, occurring before the moment Shabbat liability attaches. Shabbat *chiyuv* for *hotza'ah* comes only when the object is brought into *reshut harabim* and put down. Since the temporal gap between the two violations exists, *kam lei b'drabba minei* does not apply, and the thief pays for the theft.

### Dragging Loot

*Haya megarer v'yotze patur she-issur geneivah v'issur Shabbat ba'im k'echad* — but if the thief was dragging a large bag of loot and it crossed the threshold in a continuous drag, the Shabbat violation and the act of *kinyan* for theft occurred simultaneously, and he is *patur* from the monetary payment. This is because *meshichah* — drawing an object into one's domain — serves as the *kinyan* for theft, and if this *meshichah* occurs at the same moment as the *hanachah* completing the *melacha* of Shabbat, both violations arise at once, and the more severe Shabbat violation overrides.

### Vessel Unity

The *gemara* now draws in *eged kli shmei eged*: if we hold that the vessel creates a single legal unit, then when the thief drags the bag, the *kinyan* might be effected as soon as the opening of the bag crosses the threshold — since access to the interior, and thus effective acquisition of everything within, is obtained at that point — even while part of the bag's body still remains in the private domain. But Shabbat liability would not attach until the entire bag — as one unit — has been brought into *reshut harabim*. That would mean *geneivah* precedes Shabbat, and *kam lei b'drabba minei* would not apply, making the thief liable — yet the *mishna* rules him *patur*.

### Mouth Away

The *gemara* resolves this: *d'afkeia derech shulav* — the bag was dragged out with its opening facing away from the thief. If the mouth of the sack faces away, then even with *eged kli shmei eged*, access to the interior is only obtained when the mouth itself crosses the threshold — and by that same moment, the entire bag has crossed. Thus the *kinyan* for theft and the Shabbat *hanachah* occur at the same instant. The ruling of *patur* is coherent.

### Straps & Stitches

The *gemara* probes whether this scenario truly works. *V'ika makom cholna* — what about the stitching at the rear of the bag? One could theoretically unpick the stitching and access the contents from behind even before the mouth crosses the threshold. The *gemara* responds that the bag contains *naskei* — large ingots or bars of silver, objects too large to be extracted through a small tear in the stitching. The only effective access point is the mouth, so the analysis stands.

Further: once we have established the scenario with the mouth facing away and large objects inside, what about the bag's *shnatzim* — straps that encircle the mouth? As the mouth crosses the threshold, the straps, which are still trailing on the private-domain side, have not yet fully crossed. If *eged kli shmei eged* makes the straps part of the vessel-unit, Shabbat liability would not attach until the straps too have crossed — at which point the *kinyan* has long since been effected, restoring the temporal gap between theft and Shabbat. The *gemara* answers: *d'michrach kerichei* — the straps are wrapped tightly around the mouth. When the mouth of the sack crosses the threshold, the coiled straps come with it simultaneously. There is thus a configuration in which the moment of *kinyan* for theft and the moment of Shabbat liability coincide exactly, rendering the thief *patur* — and this remains consistent with *eged kli shmei eged*.
