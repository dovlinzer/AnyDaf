# Ketubot 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Knas: Betrothal & Di (II)

### Three Opinions on Knas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קְנָסָהּ לְמִי, לְאָבִיהָ. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: לְעַצְמָהּ. לְעַצְמָהּ אַמַּאי! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָכָא בְּנַעֲרָה שֶׁנִּתְאָרְסָה וְנִתְגָּרְשָׁה עָסְקִינַן, וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּמַתְנִיתִין וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּבָרַיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to a young woman who was raped, **to whom** is **her fine** paid? It is paid **to her father; and some say:** It is paid **to her.** The Gemara asks: **To her? Why?** The verse explicitly states that the fine is paid to her father. **Rav Ḥisda said: Here we are dealing with a young woman who was betrothed and divorced, and** these *tanna'im* in the *baraita* **disagree in the dispute between Rabbi Akiva of the mishna and Rabbi Akiva of the *baraita*,** with regard to whom the rapist pays the fine in that case.

Today's daf is 39, picking up from 38b. The *gemara* at the end of yesterday's daf worked out the implications of the various *psukim* centered on the phrase *asher lo orasa* — "who has not been betrothed." The Torah states: *naara besula asher lo orasa veshachav ima*, a young virgin who has not been betrothed. The question is: what is the law when a girl has been betrothed but is no longer? If she is currently betrothed — even at the level of mere *erusin* rather than *nissuin* — everyone agrees there is no *knas* payment, because *kim lei bidraba minei*: she is a married woman, and the offender is liable to capital punishment, which supersedes the monetary penalty. But if she had been betrothed at the level of *erusin* only, and was subsequently divorced — such that she can still be a *besula* — and is then violated, a dispute emerges.

### Rabbi Yose vs. Two Akivas

Three distinct positions emerge. Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds that she does not satisfy the criteria of the verse: she is not fully a *naara* in the relevant sense, nor a *besula* in the relevant sense, and therefore no *knas* is paid at all. Rabbi Akiva, as he appears in our *Mishna*, holds that she gains a degree of independence from her father, and therefore the *knas* is paid — but it goes to her, not to him. This position requires explanation: normally, a girl who was betrothed at the level of *erusin* and then divorced does not become independent of her father. Full independence from the father's jurisdiction is achieved only through *nissuin* followed by divorce, not through *erusin* followed by divorce. Nevertheless, Rabbi Akiva reads the Torah as signaling that for the specific purposes of this *halakha*, she has sufficient independence that the *knas* goes to her. The third position is Rabbi Akiva as he appears in the *baraita*, who holds that the verse carries no exclusionary implication, and the standard law applies: in a case of *erusin* followed by divorce, the payment goes to the father, as in any other case. So there are three possibilities: no one receives the *knas*; the father receives it, with no change in the standard law; or she herself receives it.

### Baraita: Knas to Father

The *gemara* now picks up at the *baraita* beginning *tanu rabbanan*, approximately ten lines from the bottom of 38b. The *baraita* states: *knaso l'mi? l'aviha* — who receives the *knas*? The father. *Ve'yesh omrim le'atzmah* — and some say she receives it herself. Since this *baraita* appears without a limiting context, it sounds as though it is discussing an ordinary case. The *gemara* therefore asks: why would she receive it herself? Rav Chisda explains: *hacha b'naara shenisorasa venisgarsha askinan* — we are dealing with the case of our *Mishna*, where there was *erusin* followed by divorce. This *baraita* thus mirrors precisely the debate between the two versions of Rabbi Akiva: Rabbi Akiva of the *Mishna* who says she keeps it, and Rabbi Akiva of the *baraita* who says the father keeps it.

## Knas: Obligation vs. Transfer

### Obligation vs. Transfer

Underlying this entire discussion is a deeper conceptual question: is the *knas* fundamentally owed to the girl, with the father merely collecting on her behalf, or is it owed directly to the father? One way of understanding the father's entitlement is that the payment is conceptually made to her but then transfers to him — analogous to the principle of *ma shakana eved kana rabo*, what an *eved* acquires belongs to his master. Any financial benefit accruing from a *naara* is channeled to her father by virtue of the principle of *shvach neurim l'aviha*; the payment originates as hers but he is entitled to it. This distinction will have significant practical implications.

## Death Before Trial

### Abaye's Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בָּא עָלֶיהָ וּמֵתָה — פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתַן לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה״ — וְלֹא לַאֲבִי מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** If **one had intercourse with** a young woman, **and she died** before he was sentenced, he is **exempt** from paying the fine, **as it is stated: "And** the man who lay with her **shall give to the father of the young woman"** (Deuteronomy 22:29), from which it is inferred, **and not to the father of a dead** girl.

This ruling can be understood as a *gezeiras hakasuv*, a decree of Scripture with no further rationale required. But it also resonates with the conceptual framework described above. If the fundamental obligation is to her, and she is not alive to be the object of that obligation, then there is nobody for whom payment can be made. An important qualification emerges, however. There is a distinction between the moment he physically hands over the money and the moment *beit din* issues its ruling. Once *beit din* adjudicates and declares him liable — fifty *kesef* to the father — a concrete debt crystallizes. From that point forward, even if the girl were to die, the obligation to the father stands as an independent debt. Abaye's ruling therefore concerns the case where she died *before* the adjudication, before *beit din* rendered its verdict. At that point, since she is not alive, there is no one to bring him to court and no one in relation to whom the obligation exists. But once *beit din* rules and the obligation has crystallized as a debt owed to the father, her subsequent death does not dissolve it.

## Bagrut & Death Issues

### Maturation in Grave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבָעֵי רָבָא: יֵשׁ בֶּגֶר בַּקֶּבֶר, אוֹ אֵין בֶּגֶר בַּקֶּבֶר? יֵשׁ בֶּגֶר בַּקֶּבֶר — וְדִבְנָהּ הָוֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא: אֵין בֶּגֶר בַּקֶּבֶר, וּדְאָבִיהָ הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rava raised a dilemma: Is there** achievement of **grown-woman status in the grave or is there not** achievement of **grown-woman status in the grave?** The *halakha* is that if a young woman is raped and the rapist did not pay the fine until she became a grown woman, the rapist pays the fine to her and not to her father. Rava's dilemma is in a case where a young woman dies and her rapist was convicted only after the time elapsed that were she alive she would have reached grown-woman status. **Is there** achievement of **grown-woman status in the grave, and** therefore she is entitled to the fine and **it is** the property of **her son** as his mother's heir? **Or perhaps there is no** achievement of **grown-woman status in the grave, and the fine is** the property of **her father,** as she was a young woman when she died.

A passage the *gemara* presents as straightforward for Abaye was a matter of genuine uncertainty for Rava. The *gemara* introduces Rava's question: *yesh bagar bakever o ein bagar bakever?* — does maturation occur in the grave, or not? The scenario Rava envisions is this: a girl is violated while a *naara*, then she dies, and by the time the case reaches *beit din*, enough time has passed that, had she been alive, she would have attained the status of *bogeret*. Does the law take account of this hypothetical maturation? And if so, does the *knas* now pass to her child rather than to her father?

The implications are striking. If we say *yesh bagar bakever* — yes, maturation occurs in the grave — then we treat her as though she were a *bogeret* at the time the case reaches court. A *bogeret* is fully emancipated from her father's jurisdiction, and everyone agrees the *knas* would go to her. Since she is dead but is treated as a *bogeret*, the father drops out of the equation, and the payment would pass to her child. If we say *ein bagar bakever* — no, we look at her actual status at death, which was *naara* — then the father remains in the picture and would collect. Crucially, both positions assume, against Abaye, that a payment can still be made even though she is dead. The only question is to whom.

### Pregnancy Impossibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מִעַבְּרָא? וְהָתָנֵי רַב בִּיבִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים מְשַׁמְּשׁוֹת בְּמוֹךְ, אֵלּוּ הֵן: קְטַנָּה, וּמְעוּבֶּרֶת, וּמְנִיקָה. קְטַנָּה — שֶׁמָּא תִּתְעַבֵּר וְתָמוּת, מְעוּבֶּרֶת — שֶׁמָּא תַּעֲשֶׂה עוּבָּרָהּ סַנְדָּל, מְנִיקָה — שֶׁמָּא תִּגְמוֹל אֶת בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava's dilemma is based on the assumption that a rape victim is able to conceive before she is a grown woman. The Gemara asks: **And can** a minor **conceive? But didn't Rav Beivai teach** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman:** It is permitted for **three women** to **engage in relations with a** contraceptive **resorbent. These are they: A minor, and a pregnant woman, and a nursing woman.** The *baraita* elaborates: **A minor** may do so **lest she conceive and die; a pregnant** woman, **lest her** existing **fetus** be crushed by another fetus and **assume** the shape of **a sandal** fish if she conceives a second time; and **a nursing** woman, **lest** she conceive, causing her milk to spoil, which will lead **her** to **wean her son** prematurely, endangering his health.

The *gemara* immediately challenges the scenario itself: how can this woman have a child? For the scenario to work, she must have become pregnant as a *naara* and given birth before becoming a *bogeret*, yet died before reaching *bagrut*. But is that possible?

### Three Women & Moch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזוֹהִי קְטַנָּה — מִבַּת אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד עַד שְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה וְיוֹם אֶחָד. פָּחוֹת מִיכֵּן וְיָתֵר עַל כֵּן מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת כְּדַרְכָּהּ וְהוֹלֶכֶת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת כְּדַרְכָּהּ וְהוֹלֶכֶת, וּמִן הַשָּׁמַיִם יְרַחֵמוּ, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוֹמֵר פְּתָאִים ה׳״!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *baraita* further states: **What is a minor girl?** A minor girl is a girl **from eleven years and one day old until twelve years and one day old.** If she was **less than that** age or **more than that** age, **she proceeds and engages in relations in her usual manner;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: Both this** woman **and that** woman, i.e., in the cases of all these women, **she proceeds and engages in relations in her usual manner, and from Heaven they will have mercy** and prevent any mishap, **due to** the fact **that it is stated: "The Lord preserves the simple"** (Psalms 116:6). Apparently, a minor is unable to conceive.

At this point the *gemara* invokes the famous teaching transmitted by Rav Bibi before Rav Nachman: *shlosh nashim meshamshot bamoch* — three women may use a *moch*, a contraceptive sponge. These are: a *ktana*, an underage girl; a *meuberet*, a pregnant woman; and a *meinika*, a nursing woman. A *ktana* may use it *shema titaber vetamut* — lest she become pregnant and die as a result. A pregnant woman may use it *shema ta'aseh ubarah sandal* — lest the second conception harm the first fetus, either by crushing it (Rashi's explanation) or by adversely affecting it in some other way (Tosafot's approach). A nursing woman may use it lest a new pregnancy compromise her ability to nurse her current child, endangering its health.

The *gemara* further specifies: which age of girl is considered a *ktana* for these purposes? From eleven years and one day until twelve years and one day. Younger than eleven, there is no concern about pregnancy. Older than twelve years and a day, pregnancy will no longer endanger her life. Rabbi Meir's position is that these three women are permitted or obligated to use this form of contraception, while the Sages say *achas zu v'achas zu meshamshot kedarka v'oleches* — all of them, including the *ktana* and the pregnant woman, engage in relations normally, relying on the principle of *shomer psa'im Hashem* — God protects the simple. This passage is the central Talmudic source for contemporary discussions of permissible birth control. The debates about whether *moch* is used before or after intercourse (Rashi versus Tosafot), and whether the other women are merely not obligated to use it or are actively forbidden from using it, carry enormous weight for how halakha approaches various modern contraceptive methods, particularly regarding the issue of *zera l'vatalah*.

### Six Months Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּאִיעַבַּרָא כְּשֶׁהִיא נַעֲרָה, וְאוֹלִידָה כְּשֶׁהִיא נַעֲרָה. וּבְשִׁיתָּא יַרְחֵי מִי קָא יָלְדָה? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין בֵּין נַעֲרוּת לְבַגְרוּת אֶלָּא שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים. וְכִי תֵּימָא: בְּצִיר הוּא דְּלֵיכָּא, הָא טְפֵי — אִיכָּא, הָא ״אֶלָּא״ קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that she conceived when she was a young woman,** twelve years old, **and gave birth when she was a young woman,** and died before she reached the status of a grown woman, can a woman **give birth in six months** after conception? **But didn't Shmuel say: There are only six months between** becoming **a young woman and** becoming **a grown woman? And if you say** that Shmuel is saying that it is **less** than six months **that there is not** transition from young woman to grown woman status; however, **more** than six months **there is** transition, as different women develop differently, and therefore she could conceive and give birth while she is a young woman, that is not so, **as** Shmuel **said: Only,** indicating that the period is neither less nor more than six months.

What is relevant to the present discussion is the premise embedded in the *ktana* ruling: a girl who becomes pregnant below the age of twelve may die as a result. The *gemara* therefore assumes she cannot become pregnant before twelve. But the status of *naara* begins at twelve, with the appearance of two pubic hairs, and Shmuel teaches: *ein bein na'arut l'bagrut ela shisha chodashim* — there are only six months between the status of *na'arut* and the status of *bagrut*. The *gemara* addresses whether Shmuel means this as a minimum (at least six months) or a maximum (no more than six months). It concludes that Shmuel's formulation means it will never take more than six months: *bagrut* is determined by the physical signs of bodily maturation, but Shmuel assures us those signs will manifest within six months of *na'arut* at the latest.

This is structurally analogous to other areas of *halakha* where a measurable time threshold substitutes for case-by-case physical assessment. The *gemara* elsewhere determines *chametz* formation by the visual appearance of the dough — cracking, crusting, and so forth — but a single time marker (eighteen minutes) is derived from the principle that the physical signs will never appear in less than that time, allowing us to dispense with individual inspection. Similarly, *bagrut* is definitionally a physical state of the body, but six months after *na'arut* we may declare it certain.

### Reformulated Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הָכִי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: יֵשׁ בֶּגֶר בַּקֶּבֶר וּפָקַע אָב, אוֹ דִלְמָא: אֵין בֶּגֶר בַּקֶּבֶר, וְלָא פָּקַע אָב? מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי בָּעֵי לַהּ הָכִי: מִיתָה עוֹשָׂה בַּגְרוּת, אוֹ אֵין עוֹשָׂה בַּגְרוּת? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rava's dilemma is unrelated to whether or not her son inherits the fine payment. **This is his dilemma: Is there** achievement of **grown-woman status in the grave and** therefore the right of the **father** to receive payment of the fine **lapsed;** and since there is no one claiming the payment, the rapist need not pay? **Or perhaps there is no** achievement of **grown-woman** status **in the grave, and** the right of the **father did not lapse. Mar bar Rav Ashi raised the dilemma** in **this** manner: Does **death effect grown-woman status or does it not effect grown-woman status?** No resolution was found for this dilemma, and the Gemara concludes that the dilemma **stands** unresolved.

Given all this, the scenario of *yesh bagar bakever* as originally formulated is impossible. She cannot become pregnant as a *ktana* without dying, so she can only conceive after turning twelve. But she has only six months from *na'arut* to *bagrut*, and a woman cannot give birth in six months. She therefore cannot have a child before becoming a *bogeret*, and if she has no children, the question of whether her child inherits is moot — since her next heir would simply be the father, and then whether or not there is *bagrut bakever*, the father collects regardless.

The *gemara* therefore reformulates Rava's question: *yesh bagar bakever ufaka av?* — does *bagrut* occur in the grave, thereby removing the father from the equation? Or *ein bagar bakever velo paka av* — is there no *bagrut* in the grave, leaving the father within the equation? If *yesh bagar bakever*, we treat her as a *bogeret* at the time the case reaches court: she is dead, but the father, who is only relevant in relation to a *naara*, is no longer relevant. Since she is dead and no longer a *naara*, nobody collects. If *ein bagar bakever*, she retains the status of *naara*, the father remains in the picture, and he collects. Both versions still assume against Abaye that even when she is dead the case can proceed.

### Death as Emancipation

Mar bar Rav Ashi offers yet another formulation: *mita osa bagrut o ein osa bagrut?* — does death itself function as emancipation from the father, the way *bagrut* does? The principle *lametim chofshi* — the dead are free — might suggest that death itself removes a woman from the father's jurisdiction. If so, since she died, the father is no longer relevant; since she is also not alive to collect, nobody collects. This is essentially Abaye's position, arrived at through different reasoning. Alternatively, death does not sever the father's entitlement, and the court case may proceed with payment to the father.

The question of whether the father collects even after the girl's death is also bound up with the distinctive nature of *knas* as opposed to ordinary monetary obligations. An ordinary debt becomes part of an estate and can be collected regardless of whether the original obligee is alive. A *knas*, by contrast, is personal: it is assessed and collected in relation to a specific individual. The question *ein adam morish knas levanav* — whether one can bequeath a *knas* to one's heirs — reflects this tension. The *gemara* leaves these questions unresolved: *teiku*.

## Status Change Mid-Case

### Betrothal After Rape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵאַבָּיֵי: בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְנִתְאָרְסָה, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, מִי כְּתִיב: ״וְנָתַן לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה אֲשֶׁר לֹא אֲרוּסָה״? וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דְּתַנְיָא: בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְנִשֵּׂאת, לְעַצְמָהּ: מִי כְּתִיב ״וְנָתַן לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה אֲשֶׁר לֹא נְשׂוּאָה״?
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, **Rava inquired of Abaye:** If he forcibly **had intercourse with** a young woman **and she was** later **betrothed, what is** the *halakha*? Abaye **said to him: Is it written: And he shall give to the father of the young woman who is not betrothed?** Actually it is written: "If a man finds a young woman…who was not betrothed" (Deuteronomy 22:28), which indicates that the determining factor is whether she was betrothed before the rape and not whether she is engaged at the moment of payment. Rava asked him: **And according to your reasoning, that which was taught** in a *baraita*: If he forcibly **had intercourse** with a young woman **and she** later **married,** the fine is paid **to her,** not to her father. There too, ask: **Is it written: And he shall give to the father of the young woman who is not married?** Even though the verse does not address the moment of payment, if she married the fine is paid to her.

With these foundational questions in view, Rava poses a further question to Abaye: *ba'al ve'nisarsa mahu?* — he violated her, and then, before the case came to *beit din*, she became betrothed. What does Rabbi Akiva say? Rabbi Akiva of the *Mishna* holds that if she was betrothed and then divorced *before* the rape, the *knas* goes to her. But what if she was raped first and only became an *arusah* afterwards? Does Rabbi Akiva say the *knas* now follows her changed status and goes to her?

### Time of Act Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, הוֹאִיל וּבַגְרוּת מוֹצִיאָה מֵרְשׁוּת אָב, וְנִישּׂוּאִין מוֹצִיאִין מֵרְשׁוּת אָב, מָה בַּגְרוּת בָּא עָלֶיהָ וּבָגְרָה — לְעַצְמָהּ, אַף נִישּׂוּאִין בָּא עָלֶיהָ וְנִשֵּׂאת — לְעַצְמָהּ. אֶלָּא אֵירוּסִין, מִי קָא מַפְּקִי מֵרְשׁוּתָא דְּאָב לִגְמָרֵי? הָא תְּנַן: נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה אָבִיהָ וּבַעְלָהּ מְפִירִין לָהּ נְדָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye retorted: **How can** these cases be **compared? There,** in the case of marriage, there is reason to diverge from the plain meaning of the verse, **as grown-woman status removes her from** the **authority of the father and marriage removes her from** the **authority of the father. Just as** with regard to **grown-woman status,** if he forcibly **had intercourse with her and she became a grown woman,** the fine is paid **to her,** as it is written: "And he shall give to the father of the young woman" (Deuteronomy 22:29); **so too,** with regard to **marriage,** if he forcibly **had intercourse with her and she** later **married,** the fine is paid **to her. However,** with regard to **betrothal, does** it **remove** her **from** the **authority of the father entirely? Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 66b): With regard to **a betrothed young woman, her father and her husband** together **nullify her vows?** Apparently, betrothal does not remove her entirely from her father's authority, and therefore, the *halakha* with regard to betrothal cannot be derived from the *halakha* with regard to grown-woman status. Therefore, Rava's question is not difficult.

Abaye responds by pressing on the textual logic. The Torah does not say *venasan l'avi hana'arah asher lo orasah* — "he shall give to the father of the *naara* who is not betrothed" — referring to her status at the time of payment. The phrase *asher lo orasah* in the verse refers to her status at the time of the act itself. What matters is her condition when the violation occurred. Her status at the time of payment is irrelevant. She was not betrothed at the time of the rape, so the *knas* goes to the father regardless of what happens afterward.

### Nisuin Counterexample

Rava challenges this: we know from a *baraita* that if she was raped and subsequently contracted full marriage — *nisuin* — before the case came to *beit din*, she retains the *knas* for herself. Where does that ruling come from? The Torah does not say *venasan l'avi hana'arah asher lo nesuah* — "the father of the girl who is not married." Why, then, does her *nissuin* after the rape change who receives payment?

### General vs. Specific Rul…

Abaye distinguishes: *bagrut* and *nissuin* are general modes of emancipation from the father. When a woman reaches *bagrut*, she is fully emancipated from her father in all areas of *halakha*. When a woman contracts *nissuin*, she likewise exits her father's complete jurisdiction in every domain. These are categorical, comprehensive legal transitions. Therefore, even without an explicit verse, there is sound legal logic to say that if she becomes a *bogeret* or becomes *nesuah* between the rape and the court case, and she is at that point fully emancipated, the *knas* follows her emancipated status and goes to her. Rashi notes that there is indeed a textual peg for the *bagrut* case: the verse says *l'avi hana'arah* — "the father of the *naara*" — at the moment of payment; if she is by then a *bogeret*, the father is no longer *avi hana'arah* in the relevant sense, and she retains the money. And since *nissuin* also constitutes complete emancipation just as *bagrut* does, the same conclusion applies.

### Erusin Lacks Logic

*Erusin*, however, is a different matter entirely. As the *Mishna* elsewhere teaches, a *naara hame'orasah* — a betrothed young woman — has *both* her father and her betrothed able to annul her vows. She is not emancipated from the father; she is in fact under dual jurisdiction. *Erusin* is not a general mode of emancipation. Therefore, if she becomes betrothed *after* the rape, there is neither textual support nor logical compulsion to say the *knas* follows her to herself. Unlike *bagrut* and *nissuin*, which by their nature fully remove her from the father's domain, *erusin* does no such thing. Rabbi Akiva's ruling in the *Mishna* — that a woman who had been betrothed and then divorced at the time of the rape stands in a special category — was a *gezeiras hakasuv* read from the specific language of the verse, not a general principle of emancipation. It therefore cannot be extended to cover a subsequent *erusin* after the act. The conclusion is that in such a case, there is neither a source nor a logic to move the *knas* from the father to her, and the father collects.

## Mefateh vs. Ones

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְפַתֶּה נוֹתֵן שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים, וְהָאוֹנֵס אַרְבָּעָה. הַמְפַתֶּה נוֹתֵן בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם וּקְנָס, מוֹסִיף עָלָיו אוֹנֵס שֶׁנּוֹתֵן אֶת הַצַּעַר. מָה בֵּין אוֹנֵס לִמְפַתֶּה: הָאוֹנֵס נוֹתֵן אֶת הַצַּעַר, וְהַמְפַתֶּה אֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן אֶת הַצַּעַר. הָאוֹנֵס נוֹתֵן מִיָּד, וְהַמְפַתֶּה לִכְשֶׁיּוֹצִיא. הָאוֹנֵס שׁוֹתֶה בַּעֲצִיצוֹ, וְהַמְפַתֶּה, אִם רָצָה לְהוֹצִיא — מוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The seducer gives** the father of his victim **three things, and the rapist** gives the father **four.** The mishna specifies: **The seducer gives** the father payments for **humiliation, degradation, and** the **fine. A rapist adds** an addition to **his** payments, **as he** also **gives** payment for the **pain. What** are the differences **between** the *halakha* of **a rapist and** that of **a seducer? The rapist gives** payment for **the pain, and the seducer does not give** payment for **the pain. The rapist gives** payment **immediately, and the seducer** does not pay those payments immediately but only **when he releases** her. **The rapist drinks from his vessel [*atzitzo*],** i.e., marries the woman he raped, perforce, **and the seducer, if he wishes to release** her, **he releases** her.

The *gemara* now arrives at the next *Mishna*. The *mefateh* — the seducer — pays three things: *boshet*, *pgam*, and *knas*. The *ones* — the rapist — pays four, adding *tzaar*, payment for pain and suffering. Beyond this difference in payments, there is a structural difference in obligation: the *ones* pays immediately and is required to marry the girl; the *mefateh* pays the *knas* only if he refuses to marry her.

### Three Payments Explained

The three payments common to both cases require explanation. *Boshet* is the payment for shame and embarrassment — a standard element of assault liability. *Pgam* is the diminishment of the woman's value, the word the *Mishna* uses in place of the more general term *nezek*. The choice of *pgam* over *nezek* is significant: *nezek* connotes a visible, externally observable physical injury, while *pgam* carries a connotation of stigma and moral diminishment, appropriate for the loss of virginity and the reduction in marriage prospects that results from it. The standard elements of assault liability that are absent in these cases are *shevet* — compensation for lost wages during recovery — and *ripuy* — medical expenses. The *gemara* assumes these are not present even in rape, on the premise that no additional violence accompanied the act itself.

The absence of *tzaar* in the case of *mefateh* rests on the premise that since she consented, there was no pain and suffering to compensate. As to why *boshet* and *pgam* are paid at all when she agreed, this requires further examination — and that is precisely what the *gemara* will take up.

### Mefateh Conditions

The seducer pays the *knas* only if he declines to marry her. The Torah's language in the case of *mefateh* is: *mahor yimahrena lo l'ishah* — he shall marry her as a wife — and *v'im ma'ein yima'ein aviha* — and if the father refuses, *kessef yishkol k'mohar habetulos* — he shall pay money equal to the bride-price of virgins. The *mefateh* is thus not compelled to marry against his will; if he chooses not to marry, he pays the *knas*. The father, too, retains veto power over the marriage.

### Ones Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד שׁוֹתֶה בַּעֲצִיצוֹ: אֲפִילּוּ הִיא חִיגֶּרֶת, אֲפִילּוּ הִיא סוֹמָא, וַאֲפִילּוּ הִיא מוּכַּת שְׁחִין. נִמְצָא בָּהּ דְּבַר עֶרְוָה, אוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לָבֹא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְקַיְּימָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלוֹ תִהְיֶה לְאִשָּׁה״, אִשָּׁה הָרְאוּיָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna clarifies: **How does** the rapist **drink from his vessel? Even if** the woman he raped **is lame, even if she is blind, and even if she is afflicted with boils,** he is obligated to marry her and may not divorce her. However, if **a matter of licentiousness is found in her,** e.g., if she committed adultery, **or if she is unfit to enter the Jewish people,** e.g., if she is a *mamzeret*, **he is not permitted to sustain her** as his wife, **as it is stated: "And to him she shall be as a wife"** (Deuteronomy 22:29), from which it is inferred that she must be **a woman who is** legally **suitable for him.**

The *ones*, by contrast, pays the *knas* immediately and is required to marry the woman. The *Mishna* expresses this with the vivid phrase *ha'ones shoseh b'atzitzo* — the rapist drinks from his own pottery shard. He made his choice; he must live with it, whatever she is like. Even if she is lame, even if she is blind, even if she is afflicted with boils, he must marry her and may not divorce her. The only exception is if she is forbidden to him *halakhically*: if she committed adultery, or if she is a *mamzeret* and is thus legally prohibited from marrying a Jew, the verse *velo tihyeh le'ishah* — "she shall be his wife" — is read as applying only to *ishah re'uyah lo*, a woman who is legally permitted to him. In that circumstance the obligation to marry does not apply, and the marriage may not be contracted or must be dissolved.

### Key Questions

Two major questions emerge from this *Mishna*. First: why is there any payment at all by the *mefateh* if the woman consented? Second: why does the *ones* pay immediately and must marry, while the *mefateh* pays only if he declines to marry, and the father may refuse the match? The *gemara* will address these, but it begins with a prior issue: why is there no payment for *tzaar* by the *mefateh*, even granting that all these other payments apply?

## Definition of Tzaar

### Tzaar Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ צַעַר דְּמַאי? אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: צַעַר שֶׁחֲבָטָהּ עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna taught that a rapist pays for the pain that he caused. The Gemara asks: **For what pain** is he obligated to pay? **Shmuel's father said:** It is for the **pain that** he caused when **he slammed her onto the ground** while raping her.

The *gemara* asks: what exactly is the *tzaar* that the *ones* pays? Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel answers: *tzaar shechivtah al gabei karka* — the pain of being thrown to the ground in the act of rape. The payment for *tzaar* is not primarily understood here as compensation for the act of intercourse itself, but rather for the attendant physical violence of the assault.

### Challenge to Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, חֲבָטָהּ עַל גַּבֵּי שִׁירָאִין, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפָטוּר? וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי — וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אוֹנֵס אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַצַּעַר — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסּוֹפָהּ לְהִצְטַעֵר תַּחַת בַּעֲלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira** strongly **objects to this: But if** what you say is **so,** if **he slammed her onto silk, so too** is the *halakha* **that** he is **exempt** from payment for pain? **And if you say indeed** that it is **so, but isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: A rapist does not pay** for **the pain due to** the fact **that she will ultimately suffer** the same pain during intercourse when **under** the authority of **her husband?**

Rav Zeira challenges: if he threw her onto soft garments rather than bare ground, would there be no *tzaar* and no payment? The challenge points to the instability of grounding *tzaar* in the violence of the fall rather than in something more intrinsic to the act. The *baraita* of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, citing Rabbi Shimon, offers a more sweeping position: the *ones* does not pay *tzaar* at all, *mipnei shesofah lehitztayeir tachat ba'alah* — because she will eventually experience the same pain with her husband. This view sees the pain of first intercourse as an inevitable life experience that the rapist merely caused to happen earlier; since she would have suffered it eventually with a husband, the rapist owes no special payment for it. This reasoning, strikingly, presupposes no meaningful difference between the experience of forced intercourse and consensual intercourse.

### Ones vs. Consent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה נִבְעֶלֶת בְּאוֹנֶס לְנִבְעֶלֶת בְּרָצוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** They said to him: One who has intercourse against** her **will is not comparable to one who has intercourse willingly.** Apparently, the pain associated with rape is a direct result of the forced intercourse and not of some associated cause.

The *gemara* responds on behalf of the majority: *ein domeh niva'alah be-ones li-niva'alah be-ratzon* — intercourse by rape is not comparable to intercourse by consent. This seems to be exactly the right answer: the two experiences are fundamentally different. Yet, as will become apparent, the *gemara* does not press this insight to its full conclusion.

### Consent Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: צַעַר שֶׁל פִּיסּוּק הָרַגְלַיִם. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַתְּפַשְּׂקִי אֶת רַגְלַיִךְ לְכׇל עוֹבֵר״. אִי הָכִי, מְפוּתָּה נָמֵי! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מָשָׁל דִּמְפוּתָּה לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְאָדָם שֶׁאָמַר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: קְרַע שִׁירָאִין שֶׁלִּי וְהִפָּטֵר. שֶׁלִּי?! דַּאֲבוּהּ נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** It refers to the **pain of spreading** her **legs** during intercourse. **And likewise,** the verse **says: "And you opened your legs to every passerby"** (Ezekiel 16:25). The Gemara asks: **If so, a seduced woman** should **also** be obligated to make that payment as well. **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh stated a parable: To what can** this **matter** of a seducer **be compared?** It can be compared **to a person who said to another: Tear my silk and be exempt** from payment. Since she engaged in relations of her own volition, she certainly absolved him of payment for the pain. The Gemara asks: Tear **my** silk? It is not her silk, and therefore she may not waive payment for damage to it; **it is** the silk **of her father,** as the fine and the other payments are paid to him.

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, offers an alternative account of the *tzaar*: it is *tzaar she-pissuk haraglayim* — the pain of having one's legs forced open. The verse cited is *va-tifteki et raglayikh le-khol over* — "you opened your legs to all who passed," interpreted here as referring to the physical act of forcing a woman's legs apart. This reformulation leads immediately to an objection.

### Father's Rights

If the *tzaar* of the *ones* consists in forcing her legs open, why is there no such payment in the case of *mefateh*? He too opens her legs. Rav Nachman responds by analogy: *mashal de-mefuttah le-ma dabar domeh* — to what is the seduction case compared? To a person who says to his fellow: tear my silk garment and you are exempt. The act is the same; but since she consented, she has, in effect, waived the claim. The *gemara* then raises an objection: her waiver is not effective, since *d'avu'ah nihu* — it is her father's. This is why *boshet* and *pgam* are still paid even in the case of *mefateh*: she cannot give away rights that belong to her father. And if the same logic applied to *tzaar*, the father should collect that as well. The *gemara*'s implicit answer is that in the case of consensual intercourse there is simply no real *tzaar* to speak of; the pain is so minimal relative to the pleasure that it does not register as a cognizable harm.

### Women's Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: פִּקְּחוֹת שֶׁבָּהֶן אוֹמְרוֹת: מְפוּתָּה אֵין לָהּ צַעַר. וְהָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּאִית לַהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲמַרָה לִי אֵם, כְּמַיָּא חַמִּימֵי עַל רֵישֵׁיהּ דְּקַרְחָא. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲמַרָה לִי בַּת רַב חִסְדָּא: כִּי רִיבְדָּא דְכוּסִילְתָּא. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אֲמַרָה לִי בַּת אַבָּא סוּרָאָה: כִּי נַהֲמָא אַקּוּשָׁא בְּחִינְכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said** that **the clever** women **among them say** that **a seduced woman has no pain** during intercourse, as she is a willing participant. The Gemara asks: **But don't we see that** even a married woman **has** pain when she engages in sexual relations for the first time? **Abaye said: My** foster **mother told me** that the pain is **like hot water on the head of a bald** man. **Rava said:** My wife, **Rav Ḥisda's daughter, told me** that it is **like the stab of a bloodletting knife. Rav Pappa said:** My wife, **Abba Sura's daughter, told me** that it is **like** the feeling of **hard bread on the gums.** When a woman engages in intercourse willingly, the pain is negligible. Therefore, the seducer is not obligated to pay for pain.

The *gemara* now puts this to the test: *pikchos she-bein omros mefuttah ein lah tzaar* — wise women say a seduced woman has no *tzaar*. But the *gemara* challenges: *va-ha ka chazinan de-it lah* — we observe that she does have pain, even in consensual first intercourse. Three *amoraim* report what their wives told them on this subject. Abaye reports that his nursemaid — his mother having died in his infancy — described the pain as *k'maya chamimei al reisha d'karcha*, like hot water poured over a bald head: somewhat painful but also pleasurable. Rava reports that his wife, the daughter of Rav Chisda, described it as *ki rivda d'kuslisa*, like the puncture of a bloodletter's instrument. Rav Papa reports that his wife, the daughter of Abba Sura, described it as *ki nahama d'kushk'shei b'chinkhei*, like coarse bread ground against the cheek — again a mix of sensation that implies pain alongside pleasure.

### Pain-Pleasure Balance

These testimonies lead the *gemara* to its resolution: yes, there is some pain for the *mefuttah* in first intercourse, but in the consensual case the pleasure outweighs the pain. The act as a whole is experienced as pleasurable, and therefore there is no cognizable *tzaar* for which payment is owed. In the case of *ones*, however, the pain is not outweighed by pleasure, and payment is therefore owed.

### Critical Gap

What is striking — and troubling — about this entire discussion is what the *gemara* never quite says directly. The line *ein domeh niva'alah be-ones li-niva'alah be-ratzon* is cited and then essentially set aside without being developed. Rav Nachman's formulation locates the *tzaar* of rape in the forcing open of the legs — an attendant circumstance — and the subsequent discussion turns on whether that circumstance is present or absent in seduction, with the only meaningful distinction identified as her consent rather than any difference in the experience itself. The *gemara* never directly acknowledges that the act of forced intercourse is intrinsically and dramatically more physically painful than consensual intercourse — quite apart from any attendant violence, and leaving aside entirely the question of psychological trauma and its lasting effects. Even if we bracket the traumatic dimension, the physical reality of intercourse when a woman is not physiologically prepared and is actively resisting is categorically different from the experience when she is willing. The *gemara* consistently focuses on peripheral circumstances — the fall to the ground, the forcing open of the legs, the question of consent as a waiver — rather than on the nature of the act itself. This is a genuinely difficult *gemara* to sit with.
