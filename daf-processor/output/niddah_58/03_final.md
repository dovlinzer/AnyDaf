# Niddah 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hargasha & Niddah Status

### Shmuel's Hargasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בָּדְקָה קַרְקַע עוֹלָם, וְיָשְׁבָה עָלֶיהָ, וּמָצְאָה דָּם עָלֶיהָ — טְהוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בִּבְשָׂרָהּ״ — עַד שֶׁתַּרְגִּישׁ בִּבְשָׂרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says:** If a woman **examined the ground** beneath her to see if it was clean from blood and other substances, and she found nothing, **and** subsequently she **sat upon it and** then **found blood on it,** although it might be assumed that this blood came from her, **she is ritually pure.** The reason is **as it is stated:** "And her issue **in her flesh** shall be blood, she shall be in her menstrual state seven days" (Leviticus 15:19). This verse teaches that a woman does not become impure **unless she senses,** i.e., experiences some type of sensation, **in her flesh** that she emitted blood from her uterus. Since this woman did not sense an emission of blood, she is pure.

The daf opens in the middle of 57b, picking up from a *tashma* — an attempt to challenge or support a ruling from *sugyot* already in view. The subject is the statement of Shmuel, which introduces a fundamentally new criterion into the laws of *niddah*: *hargasha*, bodily sensation. Prior to Shmuel, the analysis of a *kesem* — a bloodstain — operated along the following lines: if the stain could be attributed to an external source, it was *tahor*; and even when it was *tamei*, it was only *derabbanan*, understood to reflect a situation of doubt regarding the stain's origin. Shmuel, however, reframes the entire structure. He holds that without *hargasha* — without the woman having experienced a bodily sensation — she can never attain the status of *niddah de'oraisa*. All stains, on his view, are at most rabbinic, precisely because they are unaccompanied by sensation.

### Three Doubts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נִמְצָא עַל בְּשָׂרָה, סָפֵק טָמֵא סָפֵק טָהוֹר — טָמֵא. עַל חֲלוּקָהּ סָפֵק טָמֵא סָפֵק טָהוֹר — טָהוֹר. וּבְמַגָּעוֹת וּבְהֶיסֵּטוֹת, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** from a *baraita*: **You are found** to **say** that there are **three uncertainties** involving cases where blood is found **on a woman** or her garments. If a stain is discovered **on her flesh** and there is **uncertainty whether it is impure or pure,** the *halakha* is that it is **impure.** If it is found **on her robe** and there is **uncertainty whether it is impure or pure,** the *halakha* is that it is **pure. And** in the case of a woman whose status as a menstruating woman is uncertain, **with regard to** her **touching** items **and with regard to** her **moving** items, one should **follow the majority.**

The *Gemara* introduces a *tashma* — come and hear — from a source that enumerates three categories of doubt with respect to a woman's ritual status: *nimtzas hi omeres gimel sefeikos betahara*. The three are: (1) *al besarah* — if the blood is found on her body, then *safek tamei safek tahor tamei* — in a case of doubt we rule *tamei*, since we assume it came from her; (2) *al chalukah* — if found on her garment, *safek tamei safek tahor tahor* — there we incline toward attributing it to an external source; (3) *u've'maga'os u've'hesseisos halach achar harov* — and when it comes to touching and moving, one follows the majority.

### Tosafot on Maga'ot

Rashi reads the third category as referring to a woman who bleeds irregularly and with great frequency — one who never knows when her period will arrive. In such a case, one follows the majority: if most of her days she is bleeding, we assume she is *tamei*. Tosafot, however, disagrees sharply. How, Tosafot asks, could we say that we assume a woman is *tamei* half the time simply because she has no regularity? That is not how we treat any woman. Tosafot therefore reads *maga'os ve'hesseisos* — touching and moving — as still referring to the scenario of a stain found on the garment. Even though we said that a stain on the garment ordinarily leads us to attribute it to an external source, that default changes when the woman is the type who bleeds very frequently. In that situation, even a stain on the garment is attributed back to her.

### Majority Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״הַלֵּךְ אַחַר הָרוֹב״? לָאו אִם רוֹב יָמֶיהָ טְמֵאִין — טְמֵאָה, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אַרְגִשָׁה. לָא, אִם רוֹב יָמֶיהָ בְּהַרְגָּשָׁה חָזְיָא — טְמֵאָה, דְּאֵימוֹר אַרְגִשָׁה וְלָאו אַדַּעְתַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **Follow the majority,** with regard to this woman of uncertain status? Does this **not** mean that **if** for **the majority of her days** she is in a state of **ritual impurity,** as she emits impure blood on many days, then **she is** considered **impure** even when her status is uncertain? **And** since this *baraita* does not differentiate between cases where she did and did not sense, the ruling that one follows the majority, and that she is impure, evidently applies **even though she did not sense,** which contradicts the opinion of Shmuel. ... **No** proof may be brought from here, as this is what the *baraita* means: **If** on **the majority of her days** this woman **sees** blood accompanied **by a sensation, she is impure,** despite the fact that she is uncertain whether she had a sensation, **as** one can **say she sensed** on this occasion as well, **but** it was **not on her mind,** i.e., she did not pay attention to it at the time.

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai halach achar harov* — what exactly does following the majority mean? The implication seems to be: *lav de'amras im rov yameha teme'im temeiah* — if most of her days she is bleeding, we declare her *tamei*, *ve'af al gav de'la argashah* — even without *hargasha*. After all, in a case of doubt we use majority as the deciding factor, and the only way there is doubt is if she has no sensation. So it would seem we declare her *tamei* even without *hargasha*. The *Gemara* responds: *lo*. The ruling is *im rov yameha be'hargashah chazya temeiah* — if most of the time she bleeds with sensation, and she bleeds very frequently, then when a stain appears we may assume she is *tamei* on those grounds.

### Sensation Unaware

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאֵימוּר אַרְגִשָׁה וְלָאו אַדַּעְתַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** as** one can **say she sensed** on this occasion as well, **but** it was **not on her mind,** i.e., she did not pay attention to it at the time.

But then there arises a complication: she bled frequently, most days with sensation, and now a stain is found — but this time there was no sensation. Here the *Gemara* introduces a pivotal concept: *de'eima argashah ve'lav ada'atah* — perhaps she did experience the sensation but was not aware of it. This idea might initially seem paradoxical: how can one have a sensation without being aware of it? Yet on reflection it is not so strange. There are experiences occurring in our bodies to which we do not attend — the weight of a watch on one's wrist, the feel of glasses on one's nose — which are perceptible the moment one focuses on them, yet go entirely unnoticed otherwise. The sensation may occur and simply not register in one's conscious awareness.

### Subjectivity Turn

Shmuel's introduction of *hargasha* as the decisive criterion represents a significant conceptual shift in *niddah* law. Before Shmuel, the legal analysis was directed outward, toward the objective status of the blood: where was it found, could it be attributed elsewhere, was there doubt? Shmuel relocates the critical inquiry into the woman's own subjective experience. This is why one finds that later *poskim*, in the generations following the *Gemara*, begin for the first time to direct sustained attention to women themselves — asking them what this sensation feels like, whether they experience it, trying to understand their reality.

### Women's Reality

This relocation creates a profound practical problem. If *niddah de'oraisa* requires *hargasha*, and women report that they do not recognize or experience this sensation, then the question arises: is any woman a *niddah de'oraisa* today? The rabbis who tried to engage with this question after the *Gemara* found themselves frustrated: what women reported did not map onto what the *Gemara* described, and the effort to bridge that gap — to connect the halakhic category to a lived female reality — proved genuinely difficult. Some suggested that the modern disconnection from bodily experience, the structure of artificial schedules and environments, might explain why such sensations are less accessible. Others explored whether menstrual cramping might serve as the relevant indicator, though cramping often precedes rather than accompanies the actual bleeding.

### Bridging Reality

The concept of *argasha ve'lav ada'atah* — sensation without awareness — becomes one of the principal tools for bridging this gap. When a woman is clearly menstruating, when the flow is substantial and there is no question that she is getting her period, no authority would declare her only rabbinically *tamei* on the grounds that she reports no *hargasha*. In that case we assume *argasha ve'lav ada'atah* — the sensation occurred even if she did not register it. But this principle cannot be applied indiscriminately: if one invokes it every time, Shmuel's entire ruling becomes meaningless. As Tosafot observes, once we say "perhaps she had a sensation she didn't notice," we could always say that, rendering the criterion inoperative. Tosafot therefore limits this argument to a specific case — where the stain is an old one and it is unclear over what span of time it appeared. In that context, covering perhaps the last two weeks, we can plausibly say that at some point during that period she experienced a sensation she did not notice. But in a case of a recent stain, Shmuel's requirement retains its force: no *hargasha*, no *niddah de'oraisa*.

The practical stakes of Shmuel's ruling are enormous. Consider a woman who finds a stain on her undergarments — tight-fitting clothing that would seem, on any common-sense analysis, to leave no doubt that the blood came from her body. Or a woman who finds a stain on toilet paper. Before Shmuel, one might have said: there is no doubt here, this is clearly from her body, treat it as a full biblical *re'iyah*. Shmuel allows us to classify these as *ketamim* — stains — which then activates the entire rabbinic framework of leniencies: a stain smaller than the size of a *gris* is not a problem; a stain not on a white garment is not a problem; a stain not on something *mekabel tumah* is not a problem. All of these leniencies depend on classifying the blood as a *kesem* rather than a *re'iyah*, and that classification is made possible by Shmuel's ruling. Without *hargasha*, even blood whose origin is not in doubt is only a *kesem*. This is one of the most consequential rulings for contemporary *hilchos niddah*.

There is also the question of a stain found on the day a period is expected. Even in that more fraught scenario, the basic framework holds, though there it becomes more complex. And some *acharonim* argue that when it is clear from the quantity of bleeding that she is truly menstruating, we invoke *hargasha* rather than relying on *argasha ve'lav ada'atah*, treating the presence of substantial flow as itself sufficient to ground the presumption of sensation.

## Body vs. Garment

### Braita Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל בְּשָׂרָה, סָפֵק טָמֵא סָפֵק טָהוֹר — טָמֵא. עַל חֲלוּקָהּ סָפֵק טָמֵא סָפֵק טָהוֹר — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** If a stain is discovered **on her flesh** and there is **uncertainty whether it is impure or pure,** the *halakha* is that it is **impure.** If it is found **on her robe** and there is **uncertainty whether it is impure or pure,** the *halakha* is that it is **pure.**

The *Gemara* returns to analyze the *braita* that enumerated the three doubts: *al besarah safek tamei safek tahor tamei*, if found on her body it is *tamei*; *al chalukah safek tamei safek tahor tahor*, if found on her garment it is *tahor*. The *braita* thus draws a distinction not only between locations on the body and garment, but between the body and garment themselves — body is treated more strictly, garment more leniently.

### Mishna Conflict

This stands in apparent tension with our *Mishna*, which draws its distinctions not between body and garment per se, but between locations: *min hachagor u'lemata tamei* — from the belt downward is *tamei* — and *ra'ata dam al besarah shelo k'neged beit haturpa tehora hi* — if she sees it on her body but not opposite the genital area, she is *tehora*. The *Mishna* thus differentiates based on where on the body or garment, not simply whether it is on the body or garment. The *braita*, by contrast, seems to say that body is categorically more strict than garment. How are these two reconciled?

### Belt Division

The *Gemara* proposes that the *braita* can be read in either of two ways — *ei ba'it eima mehachagor u'lemata*, below the belt, or *ei ba'it eima mehachagor u'lemala*, above the belt — and that in each reading, the distinction between body and garment carries a specific logical rationale.

### Marketplace Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵחֲגוֹר וּלְמַטָּה, כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָבְרָה בְּשׁוּק שֶׁל טַבָּחִים. עַל בְּשָׂרָה — מִגּוּפַהּ אֲתָאי, דְּאִי מֵעָלְמָא אֲתָאי — עַל חֲלוּקָהּ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ אִשְׁתְּכוֹחֵי. עַל חֲלוּקָהּ — מֵעָלְמָא אֲתָא, דְּאִי מִגּוּפַהּ אֲתָא — עַל בְּשָׂרָה מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ אִשְׁתְּכוֹחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** that the blood was discovered **from the belt and below,** as the *baraita* is referring to a situation where the blood can be attributed to an external factor, **e.g.,** a case **where she passed through a marketplace of butchers [*tabbaḥim*].** Consequently, if the blood is found **on her flesh** it is assumed that **it came from her body, as, if it came from the** outside **world, it should have been found on her robe** as well. If it is discovered **on her robe** it is assumed that **it came from the** outside **world, as, if it came from her body, it should have been found on her flesh** as well.

Reading the *braita* as dealing with a case below the belt: *kegon she'avra beshuk shel tabachim* — she passed through the marketplace of butchers. Below the belt is ordinarily the zone of concern, but here there is a countervailing factor: she walked through a meat market, the kind of place where blood is everywhere, where animals are being slaughtered and blood is flying in all directions. In such a context, there is reason to attribute the stain to an external source. The question then becomes: where exactly is the stain?

### Direct Contact Logic

*Al basara* — if it is on her body itself — *migupha ati* — we still assume it came from her. Why? *De'ei me'alma ati, al chaluka mibei lei ishtakuchi* — if it came from outside, from the butcher's blood, it would have to have landed on her garment first before reaching her skin. How does blood from the marketplace get all the way to her skin without also appearing on her clothing? The logic of contiguity demands that external blood arriving from outside would first touch the outermost layer. If it is found only on the skin with no corresponding stain on the garment, the inference is that it came from within — from her body. *Al chalukah* — if it is on her garment — *me'alma ati* — then we may be lenient and say it came from the butchers, because *de'ei migupha ati, al basara mibei lei ishtakuchi* — if it had come from her body, it would have also appeared on her skin.

### Calisthenics Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, מֵחֲגוֹר וּלְמַעְלָה, כְּגוֹן דְּאִזְדְּקַרָה עַל בְּשָׂרָה — וַדַּאי מִגּוּפַהּ אֲתָאי, דְּאִי מֵעָלְמָא אֲתָאי — עַל חֲלוּקָהּ אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ אִשְׁתְּכוֹחֵי. עַל חֲלוּקָהּ — מֵעָלְמָא אֲתָאי, דְּאִי מִגּוּפַהּ אֲתָאי — עַל בְּשָׂרָה אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ אִשְׁתְּכוֹחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the blood was found **from the belt and above,** and the *baraita* is referring to a situation where it is possible the blood came from her body, **e.g.,** a case **where she jumped** backward. Therefore, if the blood is found **on her flesh it certainly came from her body, as, if it came from the** outside **world, it should have been found on her robe** as well. And if it is discovered **on her robe** the assumption is that **it came from** the outside **world, as, if it came from her body, it should have been found on her flesh** as well.

Reading the *braita* as dealing with above the belt: *kegon de'istarka* — she was engaged in calisthenics, vigorous physical movement. Above the belt, we would not ordinarily expect a menstrual stain, but physical exertion might have caused blood to travel upward. *Al basara* — if on her skin — *migupha ati* — we still attribute it to her body, because *de'ei me'alma ati, al chaluka ibei lei ishtakuchi* — had it come from outside, it would first have appeared on her garment. *Al chalukah* — if on her garment — even without the external context of a marketplace, since it is above the belt, we say *me'alma ati*, it came from elsewhere, because *de'ei migupha ati, al basara ibei lei ishtakuchi* — had it come from her body, there would also be a stain on her skin.

### Multi-Factor Analysis

What emerges is not a single decisive rule but a system of multiple factors that together shape the legal conclusion. One factor is location: above the belt or below it, following the *Mishna*'s framework. Another factor is the relationship between skin and garment: if the stain appears only on one without the other, that asymmetry becomes evidence of origin. A third factor is context: the butcher's marketplace, physical calisthenics, or other circumstances that make external attribution plausible. When one has a good argument that the blood came from elsewhere — not an absurd argument, not a mere theoretical possibility, but a cogent and coherent one — then one is *toleh*, one may attribute the stain to that external source. The principle is not that one must prove where the blood came from, only that one must be able to say plausibly that it did not come from her. All of these factors — belt position, skin versus garment, and contextual circumstances — feed into that determination.

## Stain Status

### Stains Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: עַל בְּשָׂרָהּ סָפֵק טָמֵא סָפֵק טָהוֹר טָמֵא, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא הִרְגִּישָׁה. וְעוֹד תְּנַן: הָרוֹאָה כֶּתֶם עַל בְּשָׂרָה כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית הַתּוּרְפָּה טְמֵאָה, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא הִרְגִּישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** that if a stain is discovered **on her flesh** and there is **uncertainty whether it is impure or pure,** it is **impure, and** this is the *halakha* **even though she did not sense,** which contradicts the opinion of Shmuel. **And furthermore, we learned** in the mishna that a woman **who sees** a blood **stain on her flesh adjacent to** her **vagina becomes ritually impure, and** this is apparently the *halakha* **even though she did not sense.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a more fundamental question. We have said that Shmuel's ruling is that without *hargasha* a woman is not a *niddah de'oraisa*. But the laws of *ketamim* — stains — as they appear throughout the *mishnayot* make no mention of *hargasha* at all. The *Mishna* states: *ha'roah ketem al besarah keneged beit ha'turpa temeiah, ve'af al gav de'lo hirgisha* — if a woman sees a stain on her body opposite the genital area, she is *tamei*, even without *hargasha*. The laws of *ketamim* function entirely on their own, without any reference to the criterion Shmuel introduces. And we have just said from the *braita* that *al besarah safek tamei safek tahor tamei* — even in a case of doubt, a stain on the body is *tamei* — *ve'af al gav de'lo hirgisha*, without *hargasha*. How then can Shmuel say that without *hargasha* she is not a *niddah de'oraisa*?

### Rav Yirmiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה מִדִּפְתִּי: מוֹדֶה שְׁמוּאֵל שֶׁהִיא טְמֵאָה מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yirmeya of Difti says:** The ruling of Shmuel that a woman must sense in order to be rendered impure applies only by Torah law. But **Shmuel concedes that** in all the cases cited above, where it is indicated that a woman is impure despite the fact that she did not sense, **she is impure by rabbinic law.**

*Ve'amar Rav Yirmiyah mi-Difti modeh Shmuel she'hi temeiah mi'derabbanan* — Rav Yirmiyah of Difti clarifies that Shmuel readily acknowledges the rabbinic category of stains. He is not saying that without *hargasha* a woman is entirely *tahor*. What Shmuel is saying is that without *hargasha* she is not a *niddah de'oraisa*. The rabbinic category of *ketamim* remains intact.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

One might ask: who cares whether her status is biblical or rabbinic? She is *tamei* either way. But the answer is that the distinction is enormously consequential. The rabbinic category of *ketamim* comes with a full apparatus of leniencies that the biblical category does not. A stain smaller than the size of a *gris* — roughly the size of a penny or nickel — is not a problem at all. A stain on a garment that is not white presents no issue. A stain on something that is not *mekabel tumah* — incapable of becoming ritually *tamei* — does not render her *tamei*. All of these leniencies apply only within the rabbinic framework of stains. Shmuel, then, is not overturning the law of *ketamim* — he is saying that all stains, even those definitely originating from her body, fall into the rabbinic rather than the biblical category, because the biblical category requires *hargasha*.

### Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: שְׁמוּאֵל הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה — אֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל כְּתָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Shmuel said** this woman is pure even by rabbinic law, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, as we learned** in a mishna (59b) that **Rabbi Neḥemya says: Any item that is not susceptible to ritual impurity,** e.g., the ground, **is not susceptible to** ritual impurity due to blood **stains.**

Rav Ashi offers an alternative reading: *Shmuel hu de'amar ke'Rabbi Nechemiah* — Shmuel follows the position of Rabbi Nechemiah. As is taught: *Rabbi Nechemiah omer kol davar she'eino mekabbol tumah eino mekabbol ketamim* — Rabbi Nechemiah holds that anything incapable of becoming ritually *tamei* does not come under the laws of stains at all. Shmuel's case, which involved a woman who sat on bare ground — on *karka* — and found blood there, is explained on this reading by the fact that ground cannot become *tamei*. It is not subject to ritual impurity at all, and therefore falls entirely outside the rabbinic framework of *ketamim*. The *Gemara* notes the virtue of this reading: *bishlama le'Rav Ashi hainu de'ka'amar karka* — according to Rav Ashi, we understand perfectly why Shmuel chose the example of ground: it is a case where the woman is completely *tehora*, because the surface does not even enter the rabbinic category. According to Rav Yirmiyah, by contrast, the choice of ground seems unnecessary — even a garment would have sufficed to illustrate that without *hargasha* she is only rabbinically *tamei*. The *Gemara* resolves this: *lo miba'ya ka'amar* — Shmuel is using the stronger case. Had he given the example of a garment, one might have dismissed the leniency by saying the garment was not well-examined and the stain may have pre-existed. The case of ground, which is flat and hard and leaves no room for such arguments — and where it is plausible to say *ve'ika lemeimar migupha atya*, the blood likely came from her body — nevertheless yields *tehorah* on account of the absence of *hargasha*.

### Mekabel Tumah

The question then arises: why does the entire law of *ketamim* apply only to things that are *mekabel tumah*? Tosafot's explanation is both important and surprising. The entire category of *ketamim* did not originate as a law about the woman's personal status vis-à-vis her husband. It originated in the world of *tumah ve'taharah* — the broader system of ritual purity in which the *tamei* status of garments and objects affected whether one could eat *terumah*, handle consecrated items, and so forth. In that world, rabbis determined that stains likely originating from a menstruating woman's contact with a garment would render that garment *tamei*. But having made the garment *tamei*, consistency required that the woman herself be *temeiah* as well — otherwise a *tamei* status would be attributed to the object without the corresponding status in the woman who caused it. Once that connection was drawn, the woman's *temeiah* status in the rabbinic framework of *ketamim* became established. But the entire structure began with the garment. If the object cannot become *tamei* in the first place, the chain does not begin, and there is no basis to render the woman *temeiah* either.

### Rif's Position

It is worth noting that the Rif — Rav Yitzchak Alfasi — does not record any laws of *ketamim* at all. Some *Rishonim* have speculated that this reflects the Rif's view that the laws of *ketamim*, rooted as they are in the world of *tumah ve'taharah*, are not applicable in an era when that world no longer governs daily life. None of the other *Rishonim* follow this position — all subsequent *poskim* rule that *hilchos ketamim* apply fully today — but the Rif's silence is itself a datum worth acknowledging.

### Possible Origin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קַרְקַע, דְּמִבְּדַק שַׁפִּיר, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִגּוּפַהּ אָתְיָא — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is not necessary** to state that if a woman examined **a cloak,** found it pure, sat upon it, and then saw a blood stain on it, she is pure. The reason is **that** it is **not** easy to **examine** a cloak **well,** due to its creases, **and** therefore **there is** room **to say** the stain on the cloak **came from the** outside **world,** i.e., from some external factor. **Rather, even** in the case of the **ground, which** one can **examine well** and therefore **there is** room **to say** the stain on the ground must have **come from her body** when she sat upon it, Shmuel teaches that the blood is nevertheless **ritually pure.**

One further nuance deserves attention. In concluding the discussion of Shmuel's case with ground, the *Gemara* does not say *vada'ei migupha atya* — it is certainly from her body — but rather *ve'ika lemeimar migupha atya* — it is possible to say it is from her body. The language is of possibility, not certainty.

### Acharonim Debate

This linguistic choice has generated debate among the *acharonim*. Some want to argue that Shmuel's requirement of *hargasha* is not an independent criterion but rather a refinement of the general doubt structure of *ketamim*: the absence of *hargasha* is an indicator that the blood may not have come from her body. On this view, when there is absolutely no doubt — when the blood is certainly from her — Shmuel's rule would not apply, and she would be a *niddah de'oraisa* even without *hargasha*. These *acharonim* ground their argument precisely in the *Gemara*'s language of *ve'ika lemeimar* — "it is possible to say" — rather than "it is certain." The majority position, however, takes Shmuel's rule to its full logical conclusion: even certainty of bodily origin does not suffice without *hargasha*. And again, the gap between this conclusion and practical reality — the question of whether women today experience *hargasha* at all — is navigated through the concept of *hargasha lo ada'atah*, with the understanding that when real and substantial menstrual flow is present, we presume that the sensation occurred even if the woman did not consciously register it.

## Direct Transfer Rule

### Heel & Toe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל עֲקֵבָהּ וְעַל רֹאשׁ גּוּדָלָהּ טְמֵאָה וְכוּ׳. בִּשְׁלָמָא עֲקֵבָהּ, עֲבִיד דְּנָגַע בְּאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם, אֶלָּא רֹאשׁ גּוּדָלָהּ מַאי טַעְמָא? וְכִי תֵּימָא זִימְנִין דְּנָגַע בַּעֲקֵבָהּ, וּמִי מְחַזְּקִינַן טוּמְאָה מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If a stain was discovered on her heel or on the tip of her large **toe,** although it is not adjacent to her vagina **she is ritually impure,** as blood from the uterus could have reached there. The Gemara comments: **Granted,** when blood is found on **her heel** she is impure, as **it is wont to touch that place,** her vagina, when she kneels. **But** if the blood is discovered **on the tip of her large toe, what is the reason** she is thereby rendered impure? **And if you would say** the reason is that **sometimes** the tip of one large toe might **touch her heel,** i.e., the heel of the other foot, **do we presume** that **ritual impurity** travels **from place to place?**

The *Gemara* now addresses a series of cases involving stains found on different parts of the body. *Al ikva d'al rosh kutzta d'tzameha* — a stain found on the heel or on the toe. *Bishlama ikva* — it is understandable that a stain on the heel would be a problem: *avid d'naga b'hahu makom* — it is possible for a woman to sit cross-legged such that the heel comes into direct contact with the pubic area, from which blood could transfer directly. *Ela rosh godla mai taama* — but what is the reason for a stain on the toe? Can we really say that the toe touches the genital area directly?

### No Indirect Leniency

The *Gemara* considers: perhaps the toe contacts the heel, and from there the blood transferred? *U'mi machzikinan tumah mi'makom l'makom* — but do we really establish *tumah* through indirect transfer? The answer is that we do not. There is a firm principle that indirect transfer — from one location to another through an intermediary — is not used to establish either *tumah* or *taharah*.

### Neck Wound Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הָיְתָה לָהּ מַכָּה בְּצַוָּארָהּ, שֶׁתּוּכַל לִתְלוֹת — תּוֹלָה, עַל כְּתֵפָהּ, שֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לִתְלוֹת — אֵינָהּ תּוֹלָה. וְאֵין אוֹמְרִים ״שֶׁמָּא בְּיָדָהּ נְטָלַתּוּ וֶהֱבִיאַתּוּ לְשָׁם״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the source of this principle. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If a woman **had a wound on her neck** in an area **where she can attribute** the blood she found adjacent to her vagina to that wound, **she may attribute** the blood to the wound and she is pure. But if the wound was **on her shoulder,** which is a place **where she cannot attribute** the blood she found adjacent to her vagina to that wound, **she may not attribute** it to the wound, and she is ritually impure. **And** it is clear from this *baraita* that **we do not say** that **perhaps she took** blood from her wounded shoulder **in her hand and brought it there,** near her vagina.

A *braita* is cited to illustrate this: *haita lah maka b'tzavarah* — if a woman had a wound on her neck, *she'tuchal litlos* — such that she can attribute the stain on her thigh to blood dripping from her neck wound — *toles* — she may do so. The general principle of *toelet* — attribution to an external source — applies here: if there is a wound accessible enough that blood from it could plausibly have reached the relevant location, we allow that attribution.

### Shoulder Wound

*Af ktefah, she'eina yechola litlos, eina toles* — but if the wound is on her back shoulder, there is no logical route by which that blood could reach her thigh, and she may not attribute the stain to it. *V'ein omrim shema b'yadah natlah v'hevi'ah l'sham* — we do not say perhaps she touched the wound with her hand and transferred the blood to her thigh. Indirect transfer is not invoked to create a leniency.

### Toe Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שָׁאנֵי רֹאשׁ גּוּדָלָהּ, דְּבַהֲדֵי דְּפָסְעָה עֲבִיד דְּמִתְרְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rather,** this is the reason for the ruling of the mishna: **The tip of her large toe is different, as when she walks it might happen** that the tip of this toe is positioned under her vagina and blood drips onto it from there.

So why then is the toe a problem? Because *shani rosh godla d'bahadei d'pasa avid d'mirmi* — the toe is exceptional. As a woman walks, it is possible for the toe to come into direct proximity beneath the genital area, such that blood falls directly onto it — not via the heel, but by direct gravity. So the toe, unlike other extremities, can be reached through a direct route.

### Hand Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָתַנְיָא: נִמְצְאַת עַל קִשְׁרֵי אֶצְבְּעוֹתֶיהָ — טְמֵאָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּדַיִם עַסְקָנִיּוֹת הֵן. וְלָא מְחַזְּקִינַן טוּמְאָה מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם? וְהָתַנְיָא: נִמְצְאַת עַל קִשְׁרֵי אֶצְבְּעוֹתֶיהָ — טְמֵאָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּדַיִם עַסְקָנִיּוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If blood **was found on the joints of her fingers** on the back of her hand **she is impure,** despite the fact that blood from her source is not usually found on that part of her hand **because hands are active?**

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge: *nimtzet al kishrei etzbe'oteha, temeiah mipnei she'yadeha askaniyot hen* — if blood is found on her knuckles, she is *tamei* because hands are always active and could have directly touched the relevant area. But does this not involve indirect transfer — perhaps she checked with one finger and that finger contacted a knuckle on the other hand? The *Gemara* answers: *shani yad d'kula avida d'naga* — hands are different because the entire hand is in constant motion and any part of it could have been in direct contact. It is not a case of indirect transfer at all.

### Symmetry Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* שָׁאנֵי יְדַהּ, דְּכוּלַּהּ עֲבִידָא דְּנָגְעָה. וְלָא מְחַזְּקִינַן טוּמְאָה מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם? וְהָתַנְיָא: נִמְצְאַת עַל קִשְׁרֵי אֶצְבְּעוֹתֶיהָ — טְמֵאָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּדַיִם עַסְקָנִיּוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **The tip of her large toe is different, as when she walks it might happen** that the tip of this toe is positioned under her vagina and blood drips onto it from there. For this reason she is rendered impure by a stain on that toe.

The governing rule emerges clearly: *v'lo machzikinan tumah mi'makom l'makom v'lo machzikinan taharah mi'makom l'makom* — one does not establish *tumah* through indirect transfer, and equally, one does not establish *taharah* — leniency — through indirect transfer. The principle of direct contact is symmetrical: it applies both to stringency and to leniency. Attribution to an external source is legitimate only if the blood could have traveled directly from that source to the location where it was found. Just as one cannot invoke indirect transfer to render her *tamei*, one cannot invoke it to render her *tehora*.

## Anatomy & Pattern

### Calf & Instep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* עַל שׁוֹקָהּ וְעַל פַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ, מִבִּפְנִים וְכוּ׳. מִבִּפְנִים — עַד הֵיכָא? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: עַד מָקוֹם חֲבָק.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: In a case where the stain was discovered **on her leg or on her feet,** if it was **on the inner** side she is ritually impure, as blood from the uterus could have reached there. If it was on the outer side of the leg or foot she is ritually pure. The Gemara asks: With regard to the term: **On the inner** side, **until where** does it extend? The students of **the school of Rabbi Yannai said: Until the place of the joint [*mekom ḥavak*]** of the thigh and shin, the ligaments on the inside of the kneecap.

*Al shokah v'al parsoteiha mi'bifnim* — if blood is found on her calves and on her inner instep. The *Gemara* asks: *mi'bifnim ad heicha* — how far inward, and up to what point on the leg, does the relevant zone extend?

### Hamstring Boundary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מְקוֹם חֲבָק כְּלִפְנִים אוֹ כְּלַחוּץ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַב קַטִּינָא: עַד מְקוֹם חֲבָק, וַחֲבָק עַצְמוֹ כְּלִפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** In this regard, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is **the place of the joint** itself considered **as** part of **the inner** side **or as** part of **the outer** side? **Come** and **hear, as Rav Ketina** explicitly **teaches:** The inner side extends **until the place of the joint, and the joint itself** is considered **as** part of **the inner** side.

*Amar d'bei Rabbi Yannai ad makom chavak* — the school of Rabbi Yannai ruled that the relevant zone extends to the place of the *chavak*, which Rashi identifies as the area behind the knee where the calf joins the thigh — the hamstring region. *Ve'chavak atzmo kelifnim* — and the *chavak* itself is counted as the inner side. This is confirmed both by a teaching of Rav Katina and by the school of Rabbi Yannai as cited by Rav Papa and Rav Chiya, son of Rav Avya. The question of whether the *chavak* location is being defined in terms of the inner-versus-outer axis or the vertical axis of the leg is not entirely clear from the *Gemara*'s structure, but the ruling that the *chavak* itself counts as inside is unambiguous.

### Pattern Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: ״כְּשֵׁיר״ מַהוּ? ״כְּשׁוּרָה״ מַהוּ? ״טִיפִּין טִיפִּין״ מַהוּ? ״לְרוֹחַב יְרֵכָהּ״ מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If the stain on a woman's body was in the shape **of a bracelet, what is** the *halakha*? Likewise, if it was in the shape **of a straight line, what is** the *halakha*? If it was not in a single defined shape but was **a series of drops, what is** the *halakha*? Furthermore, if the stain extended **along the width of her thigh, what is** the *halakha*?

*Ba'ei Rabbi Yirmeyah* — Rabbi Yirmeyah posed a series of questions about the shape and configuration of stains found in the relevant area. *Kashir mahu* — what if the stains form a circle? *Kashur mahu* — what if they form a line? *Tipin tipin mahu* — what if they appear as scattered dots, a kind of scatterplot pattern? In each case, the stain is located in a zone that would ordinarily lead to a ruling of *tamei*, but the configuration is unnatural — blood flowing from the body would not ordinarily produce a perfect circle, a clean straight line, or a scatter pattern. Does the anomalous configuration create sufficient doubt to be lenient?

### Configuration Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תָּא שְׁמַע: עַל בְּשָׂרָהּ, סָפֵק טָמֵא סָפֵק טָהוֹר — טָמֵא. עַל בְּשָׂרָהּ — מַאי לַָאו כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא? לָא, דִּלְמָא דַּעֲבִיד כִּרְצוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: If a stain is discovered **on her flesh** and there is **uncertainty whether it is impure or pure,** it is **impure. What, is it not** that the case of blood found **on her flesh** is referring to **a case like this,** i.e., those cases mentioned by Rabbi Yirmeya, and therefore she is impure? The Gemara answers: There is **no** proof from here, as **perhaps** the *baraita* is referring to a stain **shaped like a strip** along the length of her thigh, as this is the usual form of a stain from the vagina.

One might observe that an unusual configuration can cut both ways. If the configuration is odd enough that it could not plausibly have come from normal menstrual flow, that itself may be a reason to say it did not come from her — one does not necessarily need to identify the alternative source; one need only have a cogent argument that it did not originate from her. At the same time, a perfectly circular stain is equally puzzling as to its external origin. The *Gemara* does not resolve Rabbi Yirmeyah's question definitively. A *tashma* is proposed: *al besarah safek tamei safek tahor tamei* — when in doubt about a stain on the body, we rule *tamei*. Perhaps this means that even in these anomalous configurations, because it is on the body itself, we are strict. The *Gemara* rejects this: *dilma dehvei kiretsuah* — perhaps the case in which we rule *tamei* on the body refers specifically to a stain that looks like a straight stripe — *retsuah* — running lengthwise down the leg. That configuration is consistent with blood flowing from above and is treated strictly on the body. But the unusual patterns Rabbi Yirmeyah asks about may be treated differently. The *Gemara* leaves the question unresolved.

### Loom Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הַהִיא אִיתְּתָא דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח לַהּ דְּמָא בְּמַשְׁתִּיתָא, אֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי, אֲמַר לַהּ: תֵּיזִיל וְתֵיתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that there was **a certain woman who discovered blood in the warp** on the loom where she was weaving. She was concerned that while she was weaving, the threads of the warp might have come between her legs and been stained. **She came before Rabbi Yannai** to inquire about the status of this blood. Rabbi Yannai **said to her: Let her go and come,** i.e., she should go and weave in her usual manner.

*Hahi iteta, ishtakach lah dama bemeshichta* — a certain woman found a drop of blood on threads of her loom. She came before Rabbi Yannai.

### Movement Test

Amar lah: teizil veteiti — he told her: walk back and forth the way you normally move around, and let us see whether in your natural movement you pass over the loom area. The logic is that if she naturally steps over or near the loom, blood from her could plausibly have fallen directly onto it, rendering her *tamei*. If her normal movement keeps her clear of the loom, the blood must have come from elsewhere.

### Redo Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין שׁוֹנִין בִּטְהָרוֹת! כִּי אָמְרִינַן אֵין שׁוֹנִין — לְקוּלָּא, אֲבָל לְחוּמְרָא — שׁוֹנִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **one does not** rely on **repeated** actions **with regard to ritually pure** items? Since the second action might not precisely mimic the first it cannot be relied upon to determine ritual-purity status. The Gemara answers that **when we say one does not** rely on **repeated** actions it is only in cases where it would lead **to a leniency. But** if it leads **to a stringency,** as in this case where the woman is currently pure, since the blood was not found on her body or her garments **one does** rely on **repeated** actions.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *ve'hatanya ein shonin betaharot* — does not a *braita* teach that one does not repeat the action when there is a question about *taharot*? In a case of doubt, we do not ask the person to redo what they were doing and then base our ruling on how it looks the second time, since the redo may not accurately replicate the original. The *Gemara* resolves this: *ki amrinu ein shonin lekula* — the prohibition on redoing applies when the purpose of the redo is to achieve a leniency. If we expect that watching her walk will show she does not pass over the loom, and we intend to use that to rule *tehora*, that is impermissible. *Aval lechumra shonin* — but if the redo is for strictness — if we already lean toward *tamei* and want to confirm by seeing that her natural movement does bring her over the loom — that is permitted. Rabbi Yannai's instruction to the woman was aimed at a potential stringency, not a leniency, and is therefore within the rules.

## Garment Context

### Three Layers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: לָבְשָׁה שְׁלֹשָׁה חֲלוּקוֹת הַבְּדוּקִין לָהּ, אִם יְכוֹלָה לִתְלוֹת — תּוֹלָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ בַּתַּחְתּוֹן. אֵין יְכוֹלָה לִתְלוֹת — אֵינָהּ תּוֹלָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ בָּעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of a woman who **wore three robes,** one on top of the other, **that had been examined by her** for blood stains, and she subsequently found a stain on one of the robes, **if she can attribute** the blood on the robe to an external source, **she may attribute** it to that source, and she is pure. **And** this is the *halakha* **even** if the stain was **on the lower** robe, closest to her skin. But if she **cannot** reasonably **attribute** the blood to an external factor **she may not attribute** it to an external factor, and she is impure, **and** this is the *halakha* **even** if the stain was **on the upper** robe.

*Lavsha shalosh chalukkos habdukin lah* — if a woman wore three layers of checked, examined garments and subsequently finds a stain on one of them. The principle: *im yechola litlos tola va'afilu betachton* — if she has a basis for attribution to an external source, she may attribute even a stain on the innermost garment to that source. *Ve'im eina yechola litlos, eina tola va'afilu be'elyon* — and if she has no basis for external attribution, even a stain on the outermost garment cannot be attributed elsewhere. The *Gemara* illustrates: *avra beshuk shel tabachim* — if she passed through a butcher's marketplace, she may attribute even a stain on the innermost garment to that source. *Lo avra beshuk shel tabachim* — if she did not, even the outer garment cannot be excused.

### Blanket Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָיְתָה פּוֹשַׁטְתּוֹ וּמִתְכַּסָּה בּוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה, כָּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנִּמְצָא בּוֹ כֶּתֶם — טְמֵאָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא חוֹזֵר, וְכֵן בַּפּוּלְיוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** If it was a robe which **she would remove and with which she would cover** herself **at night, wherever** on the robe **that the stain is found,** the stain renders her **ritually impure, due to** the fact **that** the robe **moves** while the woman is asleep and therefore the blood could have originated in the uterus. **And likewise with regard to a kerchief [*bapoleyos*],** no matter where the blood is found on the kerchief, the woman is impure.

*Haista pushtata* — if she took off her garment and used it as a blanket during the night. In that case, the location of the stain on the garment loses its diagnostic value, because the garment shifts position during sleep. Any part of it could have ended up in proximity to her body, so one cannot rule out that the stain came from her simply because of where on the garment it appears. A *braita* records that Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yossi ruled this way in Rome — that even a stain found on the top of a garment used as a blanket constitutes a problem — and when he later came before the sages in the south, they told him: *yafeh horeita* — you ruled correctly.

### Height Mismatch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּנּוּ רַבָּנַן: אֲרוּכָּה שֶׁלָּבְשָׁה חֲלוּקָהּ שֶׁל קְצָרָה, וּקְצָרָה שֶׁלָּבְשָׁה חֲלוּקָהּ שֶׁל אֲרוּכָּה — אִם מַגִּיעַ כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית הַתּוּרְפָּה שֶׁל אֲרוּכָּה, שְׁתֵּיהֶן טְמֵאוֹת. וְאִם לָאו, אֲרוּכָּה טְהוֹרָה וּקְצָרָה טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a tall** woman **who wore the robe of a short** woman without first examining it to see if it was clean of stains, **and** likewise **a short** woman **who wore the robe of a tall** woman, if a stain was subsequently found on the robe and it is unknown from which woman it came, the *halakha* is as follows: **If** the location of the stain **reaches adjacent to the vagina of the tall** woman **they are both ritually impure.** The reason is that in this case it certainly reached that area of the short woman. **And if** it does **not** reach adjacent to the vagina of the tall woman, **the tall** woman **is pure,** as the stain is definitely not from her, **and the short** woman **is impure.**

*Arucha shelavsha chaluka shel ktzara* — a tall woman wearing the garment of a short woman, or *ktzara shelavsha chaluka shel arucha* — a short woman wearing the garment of a tall woman. In either case, the relationship between the woman's anatomy and the garment's dimensions is shifted, so that a stain's position on the garment does not straightforwardly correspond to its position relative to her body. The ruling: *im magiya keneged beit haturpa shel arucha, shteihem temeiot* — if the stain is located on the garment in a position that would be opposite the genital area of the tall woman, both are *tamei*, since for the tall woman herself it is clearly in the relevant zone, and for the short woman wearing the tall woman's garment it is also likely to be in a relevant position. *Im lav, arucha tehora* — if the stain is above the tall woman's waist area, the tall woman is *tehora*, since the garment is well above her relevant zone. *Uktzara temeiah* — but the short woman wearing the oversized garment remains *temeiah*, because even though it is above the midpoint of the garment, it may still correspond to her own below-the-belt area.

### Borrowed Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בָּדְקָה חֲלוּקָהּ וְהִשְׁאִילַתּוּ לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ, הִיא טְהוֹרָה וַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ תּוֹלָה בָּהּ. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: וּלְעִנְיַן דִּינָא תְּנַן, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה — הִיא טְהוֹרָה וַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If a woman **examined her robe** and did not find a stain, **and** then **lent it to another** woman, after which a stain was discovered on the robe, what is the *halakha*? **She,** the woman who lent the robe, **is pure, and the other** woman, the one who borrowed the robe, **may attribute** the stain **to** the woman who lent the robe to her, i.e., she may say that she does not rely on the lender's examination. **Rav Sheshet said** in explanation of this *baraita*: **And we learn** this ruling that the borrower can say she does not rely on the lender only **with regard to the matter of** a monetary **judgment** as to which of the women must pay for the laundering of the robe. **But with regard to the matter of ritual impurity, she,** the lender, **is pure, and the other** woman, the borrower, **is impure.**

*Badka chaluka v'hishpiltah l'chavertah* — she checked her garment, confirmed it was stain-free, and lent it to her friend. The friend later finds a stain on it. The *braita* states: *hi tehora v'chavertah tola bah* — the owner is *tehora* and her friend attributes the stain to the owner. They are both, on the face of it, *tehoros*.

But Rav Sheshet intervenes: *ul'inyan dina tnan* — this ruling applies only to monetary disputes. If the owner wants to charge the borrower for cleaning, the borrower can deflect by saying the stain was there when she received it — that is a claim in *dinei mamonot*, matters of civil law, and without evidence, the borrower's claim cannot be disproven. *Aval l'inyan tumah, hi tehora v'chavertah temeiah* — but in matters of ritual *tumah*, the owner's verified inspection creates legal clarity: she inspected the garment, it was clean, and therefore the friend is *tamei* while the owner is *tehora*.

### Two Women Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים שֶׁנִּתְעַסְּקוּ בְּצִפּוֹר אֶחָד, וְאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא כְּסֶלַע דָּם, וְנִמְצָא כְּסֶלַע עַל זוֹ וּכְסֶלַע עַל זוֹ — שְׁתֵּיהֶן טְמֵאוֹת? שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאִיכָּא סֶלַע יַתִּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** this case **different from that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **two women who were occupied with one** slaughtered **bird, and** the bird **contained only** an amount of **blood** capable of producing a stain as big **as a *sela*** coin, **and** blood the size **of a *sela* was found on this** woman **and** blood the size **of a *sela*** was found **on that** woman, **they are both impure,** despite the fact that the blood of one of them can be attributed to the bird. Likewise, in the case of Rav Sheshet the lender should be impure as well, as she might not have examined the robe properly. The Gemara answers: **There it is different, as there is an additional *sela*.**

The *Gemara* then asks: how is this case different from another *braita*? *Shtei nashim shenitasu b'tzipor echad v'ein bo ela k'sela dam* — two women who both handled a single small bird that contained only enough blood to fill a *sela*-sized stain. After handling it, each woman finds a *sela*-sized stain on her garment. The total is two stains, but the bird only had enough blood for one. *Shteihem temeiot* — both are *tamei*. Neither can say her stain came from the bird, because if hers came from the bird, the other woman's stain is unexplained, and vice versa. So neither can escape.

### Extra Stain

Why is this case different from the borrowed garment scenario, where one woman's inspection allows her to be *tehora*? *Shani hatam d'ika sela yesira* — the borrowed garment case differs because there is no surplus stain creating an irresolvable problem. One woman checked and confirmed cleanliness; the other is left with the stain and cannot shift it. In the bird case, the total amount of blood on the stains exceeds what the bird could have provided, so there is an extra stain — a *sela yesira* — that cannot be attributed to the external source. Since neither woman has any privileged claim that her stain is the problematic one and the other's is the bird's, both bear the consequence.

## Toelet Principle

### Toelet Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַתְנִי' וְתוֹלָה בְּכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁהִיא יְכוֹלָה לִתְלוֹת. שָׁחֲטָה בְּהֵמָה, חַיָּה וָעוֹף, נִתְעַסְּקָה בִּכְתָמִים, אוֹ שֶׁיָּשְׁבָה בְּצַד הָעֲסוּקִין בָּהֶן, הָרְגָה מַאֲכוֹלֶת — הֲרֵי זוֹ תּוֹלָה בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And** a woman who discovers a blood stain on her body or her garment **may attribute** its existence **to any matter to which she can attribute** it: If she **slaughtered a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or a bird;** or if she **was occupied with** the removal of blood **stains** from the garments of other women or from her own garment, from any source, such as blood that originated from a wound elsewhere on her body or even her own menstrual blood from a prior menstrual cycle; **or if she sat alongside** others **who were occupied with** removing blood stains; or if **she killed a louse;** in all of these cases, **that** woman **may attribute** the blood stain **to it.**

The *Mishna* now turns to the general framework of *toelet* — attribution of a stain to an external cause as a basis for leniency. *Tola bechol davar sheyechola litlos* — a woman may attribute her stain to any cause she is able to reasonably invoke.

### Slaughter Context

*Shachta behema chaya ve'of* — if she slaughtered animals, domesticated or wild or fowl. The *Mishna*'s language is noteworthy: the woman herself is the *shochet*. This is consistent with what Tosafot establishes in *Chullin* — that a woman may perform *shechita*. If she was engaged in slaughter, blood from the animal is a plausible source for a stain on her garment.

### Louse Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* עַד כַּמָּה תּוֹלָה? רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: עַד כִּגְרִיס שֶׁל פּוֹל, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הָרְגָה.
>
> **Translation:** **How large** a stain may a woman **attribute** to a louse? **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says:** It can be **up to** the area **of a split bean. And** she may attribute the stain to a louse **even if she does not** remember that **she killed** it.

*Harga macholes* — if she killed a louse. A louse contains blood, and squashing it could leave a bloodstain. She may attribute the stain to the louse. The *Mishna* then asks: *ad kama tola* — how large a stain may she attribute to a louse? This matters because a louse is a small creature and would not ordinarily produce a very large stain.

### Louse Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְתוֹלָה בִּבְנָהּ אוֹ בְּבַעְלָהּ אִם יֵשׁ בָּהּ מַכָּה, וְהִיא יְכוֹלָה לְהִגָּלֵעַ וּלְהוֹצִיא דָּם — הֲרֵי זוֹ תּוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And she may attribute** the blood stain **to her son or to her husband** in a case where one of them is near her and has a wound. Furthermore, **if** the woman herself **has a wound,** even if the wound scabbed over and is no longer bleeding, **but it can reopen and bleed, that** woman **may attribute** the blood stain to that wound.

This ruling becomes one of the central leniencies in all of *hilchos ketamim*. The *shiur* of a *gris* — the minimum size threshold below which a stain is not problematic — is formally established here, and the *acharonim* almost universally rule that this remains operative as a formal rabbinic category even in conditions, such as today, where lice are not prevalent. The original basis — louse attribution — gave rise to a formal *shiur*, and that *shiur* stands independent of the specific factual scenario that generated it. All credit, as it were, goes to Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos, who was willing to extend the leniency beyond cases where a louse was actually squashed.

### Family Attribution

*Ve'tola bivna u've'ba'ala* — she may also attribute a stain to a wound on her son or husband. If a family member with a wound, a nosebleed, or some other source of blood was nearby and the blood could plausibly have come from them and landed on her, she may attribute the stain to that source.

### Healed Wound

*Im yesh ba maka, vehi yechola lehisgaleya ulehotzi dam* — if the family member has a wound that is scabbed or crusted over but could still be exposed and bleed, *harei ze tola* — she may still attribute a stain to it. The wound need not be actively bleeding; it is sufficient that it is the kind of wound that could reopen and produce blood.

### Students React

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת שֶׁבָּאת לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אָמְרָה לוֹ: רָאִיתִי כֶּתֶם. אָמַר לָהּ: שֶׁמָּא מַכָּה הָיְתָה בִּיךְ? אָמְרָה לוֹ: הֵן. וְחָיְתָה, אָמַר לָהּ: שֶׁמָּא יְכוֹלָה לְהִגָּלֵעַ וּלְהוֹצִיא דָּם? אָמְרָה לוֹ: הֵן. וְטִהֲרָהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** There was **an incident involving one woman who came before Rabbi Akiva. She said to him: I saw** a blood **stain.** Rabbi Akiva **said to her: Perhaps** there **was a wound on your** body? **She said to him: Yes,** there was a wound **and it healed. He said to her:** Was it **perhaps** a wound that **could reopen and bleed? She said to him: Yes** it was. **And Rabbi Akiva deemed her ritually pure.**

*Ma'aseh b'isha achas sheba'a lifnei Rabbi Akiva* — there is a story of a woman who came before Rabbi Akiva with a stain. He asked her: *shema makah hayisa bach* — perhaps you had a wound somewhere on your body? She said yes, she had, but it had healed. He asked: *shema yechola lehigalot ul'hotzi dam* — perhaps it is still possible for the wound to open and bleed? She said that is possible. *Ve'sit'harah Rabbi Akiva* — and Rabbi Akiva declared her *tehora*. When his students exchanged bewildered glances at this seemingly sweeping leniency — a healed wound that might conceivably reopen is enough to attribute the stain? — Rabbi Akiva explained: *shelo amru chachamim hadavar l'hachmir ela l'hakel* — when it comes to stains, the sages did not establish this law to be strict; they established it to be lenient.

## Dam vs. Kesem

### Akiva Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* רָאָה תַּלְמִידָיו מִסְתַּכְּלִין זֶה בָּזֶה, אָמַר לָהֶם: מָה הַדָּבָר קָשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיכֶם? שֶׁלֹּא אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים הַדָּבָר לְהַחְמִיר אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִשָּׁה כִּי תִהְיֶה זָבָה דָּם יִהְיֶה זֹבָהּ בִּבְשָׂרָהּ״ — ״דָּם״ וְלֹא כֶּתֶם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **saw his students looking at each other,** wondering why he ruled leniently in this case. Rabbi Akiva **said to them: What** in this **matter is difficult in your eyes?** The reason I ruled this way is **that the Sages did not state the matter** of the impurity of blood stains in order **to be stringent; rather,** they instituted this impurity in order **to be lenient, as it is stated: "And if a woman has an issue, and her issue in her flesh shall be blood"** (Leviticus 15:19), from which it is derived that by Torah law, **"blood"** deems her impure, **but not a stain.** Impurity from a blood stain was instituted by the Sages, and they rule leniently in any case where the stain can be attributed to another source.

The basis for Rabbi Akiva's ruling, and indeed for the entire rabbinic framework that distinguishes between *re'iyah* and *kesem*, is a *drasha* from the Torah's language. The *pasuk* states: *v'ishah ki sihiyeh zava, dam yihyeh zuvah bivsarah* — if a woman flows, blood will flow in her flesh. Rabbi Akiva draws from this: *dam v'lo kesem* — the Torah is speaking of blood that is seen flowing, not of a stain that is found after the fact.

### Three Approaches

This *drasha* presents a third approach to understanding the distinction between *re'iyah* and *kesem*, alongside the two we have already encountered. The first approach is that a *kesem* involves an element of doubt as to whether the blood came from the woman, whereas *re'iyah* involves no such doubt. The second is Shmuel's criterion: the distinction lies in the presence or absence of *hargasha*. Rabbi Akiva's *drasha* offers a third: the distinction is between blood that is directly observed as it issues from the body — blood that is *seen* in the act — and blood that is *found* after the fact as a stain, even if there is no doubt it came from her body.

### Rashi's Use

It is important to note that throughout *Shas*, when Rashi discusses what defines a *kesem* and why stains are only rabbinic, he consistently quotes Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva's *drasha* is the anchor Rashi uses across tractates to explain the fundamental nature of the distinction.

### Why Rabbinic

The logical conclusion of Rabbi Akiva's *drasha*, taken to its full extent, is striking: even if there is absolutely no doubt that the blood came from the woman's body, if she did not observe it flowing — if she found it as a stain — it falls outside the Torah's language of *dam yihyeh zuvah* and is therefore only rabbinic. This aligns with Shmuel's conclusion but grounds it differently: not in the absence of sensation, but in the absence of direct observation of the blood's flow. The two criteria — Shmuel's *hargasha* and Rabbi Akiva's *dam v'lo kesem* — converge in their practical effect: when blood is found rather than seen, it is a *kesem*, and the full apparatus of leniencies applies.

## Pillow Cloth Case

### Circular Stain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* עֵד שֶׁהוּא נָתוּן תַּחַת הַכַּר, וְנִמְצָא עָלָיו דָּם — עָגוֹל טָהוֹר, מָשׁוּךְ טָמֵא; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **an examination cloth that was placed beneath the pillow and blood was found on** the cloth, and it is unclear whether it is the blood of an examination or the blood of a louse that was crushed beneath it, if the stain is **round** the woman is **ritually pure,** as an examination to determine whether a woman is menstruating would not leave a round stain. If the stain is **elongated** the woman is **ritually impure;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Tzadok.**

The *Mishna* concludes with a final case. *Ed shehu nasun tachas hakar v'nimtza alav dam agol tahor* — if a woman placed her checking cloth under the pillow overnight and in the morning it is found with a circular stain, it is *tahor*. A circular stain of this kind is characteristic of an insect that was pressed or crushed under pressure, and since the cloth was under the pillow all night, a louse or similar insect could have been trapped and squashed, producing exactly such a mark.

### Elongated Stain

*Mashuch, tamei* — if the stain is elongated, it is *tamei*. An elongated stain does not resemble the mark of a crushed insect; rather, it is the shape produced by the act of using the cloth as a checking implement — the cloth wiping or drawing across a surface. That shape cannot be attributed to an insect.

### Elazar Bar Tzadok

*Divrei Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok* — these are the words of Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok, who formalizes the shape of the stain as legal evidence. The configuration of the stain — circular or elongated — is itself diagnostic: it tells us about the process that produced it, and that process in turn determines the woman's status. Shape becomes a form of testimony.
