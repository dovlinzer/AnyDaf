# Nazir 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nazir Tamei - Restart

### Four Positions Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אָמַר קְרָא ״וְקִדַּשׁ אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו. וְרַבָּנַן: ״הַהוּא״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא אֲשָׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to Rabbi Eliezer,** his reason is that **the verse states: "And he shall sanctify his head on that day"** (Numbers 6:11), which indicates: **Even though he has not brought his offerings,** the eighth day determines the start of his ritually pure naziriteship. **And the Rabbis** agree that the phrase "on **that** day" is extra, and it teaches that he begins counting from that day **even though he has not brought his guilt-offering,** but he does not begin counting until after bringing his sin-offering.

The daf opens in the middle of an ongoing discussion about a *nazir tamei* — a *nazir* who began his *nezirut* in a state of *taharah*, subsequently became *tamei*, underwent the full purification process (sprinkling on days three and seven, shaving on day seven, bringing *korbanot* on day eight), and is now required by the *Torah* to restart his *nezirut*. The simple question is: when exactly does that restart occur?

### Day 7 vs Day 8

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, ״הָהוּא״ לְמָה לִי? אָמַר לָךְ: ״הַהוּא״ — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא עוֹלָתוֹ. וְרַבָּנַן: עוֹלָה לָא בָּעֵי מִיעוּטָא — דּוֹרוֹן בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** according to **Rabbi Yishmael, why do I** need the phrase "on **that** day"? According to his opinion the issue depends on the offerings, not the day. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yishmael could have **said to you:** The phrase "on **that** day" teaches that he can begin counting **even though he has not brought his burnt-offering,** as he agrees that not bringing the burnt-offering does not preclude him from counting his naziriteship. **And the Rabbis** respond to this: **A burnt-offering does not require a limitation** in the text to teach that its omission does not preclude the start of his naziriteship in ritual purity, since **it is merely a gift** and not part of his atonement process.

Four *tannaitic* opinions address this question. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *nezirut* restarts on day seven — the day the *nazir* achieves *taharah*. Rabbi holds that it restarts on day eight, the moment the time arrives that is fit for bringing the *korban*, whether that is technically the nighttime or the daytime of day eight, depending on one's broader position regarding whether nighttime counts as the appropriate time vis-à-vis *korbanot*.

### Korban Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְהִזִּיר לַה׳ אֶת יְמֵי נִזְרוֹ וְהֵבִיא כֶּבֶשׂ בֶּן שְׁנָתוֹ לְאָשָׁם״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ שֶׁכׇּל אֲשָׁמוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁהֵן מְעַכְּבִין, יָכוֹל אַף זֶה מְעַכְּבוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהִזִּיר ... וְהֵבִיא״, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא — הִזִּיר. רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: ״וְהִזִּיר ... וְהֵבִיא״, אֵימָתַי הִזִּיר — בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason of the Rabbis** for ruling that only refraining from bringing his sin-offering precludes the start of his new count of naziriteship? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a nazirite who became ritually impure: **"And he shall consecrate to the Lord the days of his naziriteship, and he shall bring a lamb in its first year for a guilt-offering"** (Numbers 6:12). **What does this teach?** Doesn't he wait to begin the days of his naziriteship until after he has brought all of his offerings? Rather, **since we have found that all guilt-offerings that are** mentioned **in the Torah are indispensable** for atonement, and before he has brought his guilt-offering he is forbidden from partaking of any sacred offerings, one **might** have thought that not having brought **this** guilt-offering **also precludes him** from counting his ritually pure naziriteship, **the verse** therefore **states: "And he shall consecrate** to the Lord the days of his naziriteship, **and he shall bring,"** indicating: **Even though he has not brought** his guilt-offering **he has** nevertheless **consecrated** his days for the start of a new term of **naziriteship.** The opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka,** is as follows: **The verse states: "And he shall consecrate** to the Lord the days of his naziriteship, **and he shall bring,"** which means: **When has he consecrated** his days of **naziriteship,** i.e., when does his new term of naziriteship begin? It begins **when he has** already **brought** his guilt-offering.

Two further opinions appear in a *braisa*. The *Chachamim* hold that the *nazir* only restarts after bringing the *chatat*. Rabbi Yishmael b'no shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka goes further, requiring not only the *chatat* but also the *asham* — the one *korban* he excludes from that requirement is the *olah*. The *Chachamim* and Rabbi derive their positions from the verse *vikiddash et rosho bayom hahu* — "that day" carries halachic weight, allowing a restart without all the *korbanot*. Rabbi requires none of the *korbanot* at all; the *Chachamim* require only the *chatat*; and Rabbi Yishmael b'no shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka requires the *chatat* and the *asham*, but not the *olah*.

## Woman Nazira - Husband Annuls

### Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִשָּׁה שֶׁנָּדְרָה בְּנָזִיר וְנִטְמְאָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הֵפֵר לָהּ בַּעֲלָהּ — מְבִיאָה חַטַּאת הָעוֹף, וְאֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עוֹלַת הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught this that the Sages taught:** With regard to **a woman who vowed to be a nazirite and became ritually impure,** leading her to designate a bird for a sin-offering, a bird for a burnt-offering, and a sheep for a guilt-offering, **and afterward her husband nullified** her vow of naziriteship **for her, she brings the bird sin-offering and she does not bring the bird burnt-offering?**

The *Gemara* now asks: *man tanna leha detanu rabbanan* — who is the *tanna* behind a particular *braisa* that the Rabbis taught? The *braisa* presents the following case: a woman takes a vow of *nezirut*, becomes *tamei*, and — as *Tosafot* reads the case — has already sanctified two birds, one as a *chatat* and one as an *olah*, which she would offer once her *tumah* process is complete. After this, her husband performs *hafara* — annulment — on the day he heard of her vow (*beyom shamo*). He need not have heard her take the vow in person; it suffices that he learned of it. The annulment might be on the same day as everything else or several days later.

The result is a peculiar legal situation: this woman is no longer a *nezirah*, yet she had been a *nezirat tamei* while she was a *nezirah* and was therefore obligated in *korbanot*. Ordinarily, a *nazir* cannot cease being a *nazir* without undergoing the full process. But this woman has managed to exit *nezirut* while still technically obligated in the *korbanot* of a *nazir tamei*. The question is: what does she bring?

### Braisa: Chatas Not Olah

The *braisa* rules: *mavia chatat ha'of ve'eina mavia olat ha'of* — she brings the bird *chatat* but not the bird *olah*. This ruling is striking in several ways. First, it confirms that she is still obligated in at least some of the *korbanot* even though she is no longer a *nezirah* — meaning the *korbanot* of a *nazir tamei* are not exclusively future-oriented. They address the past violation of *nezirut* status, regardless of whether a new *nezirut* will follow.

Second, and more puzzling, is the split: she brings the *chatat* but not the *olah*. One might try to frame this as: the *chatat* addresses the past while the *olah* is future-oriented — but that framing is difficult, since the *olah* is ordinarily the least consequential *korban* with respect to starting a new status. As the *Gemara* itself notes elsewhere, the *olah* seems to function as a kind of *doron b'alma* — a gift offering — perhaps meant to get the renewed *nezirut* started on a good footing after the *chatat* and *asham* have addressed the past violation. Why the *olah* of all things would be the one excluded here is what the *Gemara* will need to explain.

One might further ask: why doesn't she simply bring the *olah* as a voluntary *olah*? *Tosafot* addresses this directly. The bird had already been sanctified specifically as an *olat nazir*. A *stam olah* she could bring, but this bird, labeled as an *olat nazir*, cannot be brought by someone who is no longer a *nazir*. For all other *tannaim*, who see the *olah* as having no independent functional role in the *nezirut* process, this labeling would be irrelevant — the bird is just an *olah* and she could bring it regardless. But according to the *tanna* of this *braisa*, the *olah* does play some role in the world of *nezirut*, and that label therefore matters.

### Rabbi Yishmael Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka. According to the opinion of the Rabbis, the burnt-offering is a gift, and she would bring it despite the fact that her naziriteship was nullified. According to Rabbi Yishmael, the burnt-offering is part of the atonement process, and since her naziriteship was nullified, there is no longer a need for atonement.

Rav Chisda identifies the author of this *braisa* as Rabbi Yishmael b'no shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka. The connection, as *Tosafot* explains, lies precisely in his treatment of the *olah*. All other *tannaim* held that the *olah* is simply irrelevant to the *nezirut* process — it requires no *pasuk* to exclude it from the conditions for restarting *nezirut*, because it plays no functional role. Rabbi Yishmael b'no shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, by contrast, needed a *pasuk* — *bayom hahu* — specifically to exclude the *olah*. The implication is that he held the *olah* might otherwise have been considered necessary to begin the new count. He is the one *tanna* who sees the *olah* as potentially playing some functional role in the *korbanot* of a *nazir*. Therefore, when the bird has been sanctified as an *olat nazir* and the woman is no longer a *nezirah*, she cannot bring it — precisely because according to him, it was not merely an *olah* but something embedded in the *nezirut* framework.

### Retroactive Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר בַּעַל מִיעְקָר עָקַר — חַטַּאת הָעוֹף נָמֵי לָא לַיְיתֵי. אִי קָסָבַר בַּעַל מִיגָּז גָּיֵיז, עוֹלַת הָעוֹף נָמֵי לַיְיתֵי! לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר בַּעַל מִיעְקָר עָקַר, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַקַּפָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What does he hold? If he holds** that the **husband uproots** a vow entirely when he nullifies it, and she is considered not to have vowed at all, **she should not bring the bird sin-offering,** as she was never a nazirite, and she does not need atonement. Conversely, **if he holds** that the **husband severs** the vow from that point onward, but it did take effect beforehand, **she should also bring the bird burnt-offering,** as she requires atonement for becoming impure while she was a nazirite. The Gemara answers: **Actually, he holds** that the **husband uproots** the vow, **and** why is she obliged to bring a sin-offering? **Rabbi Yishmael holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar HaKappar.**

This raises the central difficulty the *Gemara* now poses. *Mai kasavar* — what exactly does this *tanna* hold about the nature of *hafara*? If he holds *baal meaker aker* — that the husband's *hafara* retroactively uproots the vow entirely, as if she were never a *nezirah* — then how could she bring a *chatat* at all? Retroactively she was never a *nezirah* who became *tamei*, so there is no *chatat* obligation. On the other hand, if he holds *baal meigez gaiaz* — that the husband merely cuts the *nezirut* from this point forward while leaving intact everything that preceded — then she was fully a *nezirah* who became *tamei*, and she should bring the *olah* just as she brings the *chatat*. The *Gemara* cannot find a clean middle ground: either both or neither.

The *Gemara*'s answer is striking: *le'olam kasavar baal meaker aker* — he holds that *hafara* is indeed retroactive. This is a significant position, running contrary to the standard assumption throughout Masechet Nedarim that a husband only cuts the vow forward and does not retroactively undo it. But the *Gemara* is not saying this is *halacha*; it is working within this *braisa*. If the *hafara* is retroactive and she was never a *nezirah*, how can she bring a *chatat*? The *Gemara* answers: this *tanna* holds like Rabbi Elazar HaKappar — and here the *Gemara* transitions into a broader discussion.

## Elazar HaKappar Sinner

### Soul/Nefesh Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַקַּפָּר בְּרַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו מֵאֲשֶׁר חָטָא עַל הַנָּפֶשׁ״? וְכִי בְּאֵיזוֹ נֶפֶשׁ חָטָא זֶה? אֶלָּא שֶׁצִּיעֵר עַצְמוֹ מִן הַיַּיִן. וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה זֶה שֶׁלֹּא צִיעֵר עַצְמוֹ אֶלָּא מִן הַיַּיִן נִקְרָא חוֹטֵא, הַמְצַעֵר עַצְמוֹ מִכׇּל דָּבָר עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Elazar HaKappar, the esteemed one, says: What** is the meaning when **the verse states** with regard to a nazirite: **"And make atonement for him, for he sinned by the soul"** (Numbers 6:11)? **And with which soul did this** person **sin** by becoming a nazirite? **Rather,** in **afflicting himself** by abstaining **from wine,** he is considered to have sinned with his own soul, and he must bring a sin-offering for the naziriteship itself, for causing his body to suffer. **And an *a fortiori*** inference can be learned from this: **Just as this** person, in **afflicting himself** by abstaining **only from wine, is** nevertheless **called a sinner,** in the case of **one who afflicts himself** by abstaining **from everything,** through fasting or other acts of mortification, **all the more so** is he described as a sinner. According to this opinion, Rabbi Yishmael holds that since the woman afflicted herself by abstaining from wine she must bring a sin-offering, even though, due to her husband's nullification, she did not actually become a nazirite.

The *Gemara* cites a *braisa*: Rabbi Elazar HaKappar beRabbi says — *mah talmud lomar, vechiper alav measher chata al hanefesh* — what does it mean that the verse says the *nazir tamei* is atoned because he sinned *al hanefesh*, against the soul? Rabbi Elazar HaKappar asks: against which *nefesh* did he sin? His answer: *ela shetzier atzmo min hayayin* — he sinned in that he denied himself wine.

This is a creative and multilayered interpretation. The verse is ostensibly about the *nazir tamei* who brings *korbanot* after becoming impure. Rabbi Elazar HaKappar reads it instead as a general statement about the *nazir*'s act of self-denial.

### Nefesh Meaning

The word *nefesh* itself rewards attention here. The verses about fasting on Yom Kippur — *ki kol hanefesh asher teuneh*, *veinithem es nafshoseichem* — use the word *nefesh* in the context of affliction and self-denial. We typically think of *nefesh* as something spiritual, yet in the *Torah* it more commonly refers to life and living beings: *kol nefesh asher hu chai*, *shivim nefesh*. Scholars of the ancient Near East have noted that the Akkadian cognate of the word *nefesh* means "throat" — connecting it to appetite and the physical experience of eating. This linguistic background makes the connection to fasting and self-denial through food and drink feel more direct and *pshat*-oriented than it might at first appear. Rashi understands the *nefesh* in the verse as relating specifically to pleasure and appetite — the *nazir* sinned against his *nefesh* by not drinking wine.

### Asceticism Problem

The broader point Rabbi Elazar HaKappar is making is that *nezirut* is problematic because of its ascetic dimension, the self-denial it imposes. Yet one must pause here: a *nazir* can eat fatty meat, drink other beverages, have relations, and engage in most of life's pleasures — the restriction is only wine. Is wine alone what makes *nezirut* an act of asceticism? It would seem more *pshat* to say that wine's intoxicating properties, not its pleasure value, are what make it the subject of *nezirut*. Rabbi Elazar HaKappar is reframing the entire institution: he emphasizes the denial aspect of the wine prohibition as representative of what is problematic about *nezirut* as a path.

This stands in a broader rabbinic context. The *Gemara* elsewhere, and even more prominently in Masechet Nedarim, contains sharp voices condemning the taking of *nedarim*. Interestingly, those voices seem somewhat less prominent and dominant with respect to *nezirut* specifically — as if the *Gemara* is slightly more tolerant of *nezirut* than of random *nedarim*. But Rabbi Elazar HaKappar's voice is critical nonetheless.

He then concludes: *umah zeh shelo tzi'er atzmo ela min hayayin nikra chotei* — this person who only denied himself wine is called a sinner; *hametza'er atzmo mikol davar al achat kama vekama* — someone who denies himself everything, such as one who undertakes an unnecessary *ta'anit*, is all the more so a sinner. This connects to the broader rabbinic debate about whether someone who fasts unnecessarily is called *kadosh* or *chotei*.

### Moral Sin Justification

The *Gemara* now asks how any of this actually solves the problem. If the *hafara* was retroactive and she was never a *nezirah*, how does her bringing a *chatat* make sense? Calling the vow of *nezirut* a "sin" is all very well, but moral or religious censure does not ordinarily create a halachic obligation to bring a *chatat*. One cannot bring a *chatat* simply because one did something considered blameworthy — a *chatat* requires a specific, concrete *aveirah* of the sort for which one is liable to *karet*, and so on. A vague sense that something was religiously problematic is insufficient.

*Tosafot* explains that this is why the *braisa* is read as a case where she had already sanctified the bird. The bird exists; it has the sanctity of a *chatat ha'of nazir*. The question is not whether to sanctify it but whether it may be offered. Here *Tosafot* invokes an important principle: unlike an animal *chatat*, which may never be brought unless the obligation is certain, a bird *chatat* may sometimes be brought in a case of doubt. The classic example is a woman who miscarried and cannot determine whether the fetus was viable — is she a *yoleded* obligated in *korbanot* or not? She cannot remain in permanent limbo, unable to enter the *Beit HaMikdash* for the rest of her life. The tradition — *mesorah* — permits her to bring a *chatat ha'of al hasafek*, on the basis of uncertainty. A *beheima chatat* carries no such latitude; only a bird *chatat* can be brought under conditions of doubt or marginal obligation.

With that principle in place, the *Gemara*'s reasoning can be reconstructed: retroactively, she was never a *nezirah*; but the bird has already been sanctified as a *chatat ha'of*. Since there was an element of sin involved in her having accepted *nezirut* and denied herself wine — even if the *nezirut* itself was retroactively annulled — that is sufficient justification to offer the bird, given the additional leniency that attaches to a *chatat ha'of*. The *Gemara* readily acknowledges that this is a strange conclusion. It would have been simpler and more intuitive to say that *hafara* is only *mikan ulehaba* — prospective rather than retroactive — and then analyze which *korbanot* relate to the past violation and which to the future *nezirut*. But that is not the path the *Gemara* took.

*Tosafot* also addresses the obvious question: if this justification works, why couldn't a woman who took *nezirut*, never became *tamei*, had an animal sanctified as a *chatat*, and had her vow annulled also bring the *chatat*? The answer is that in the case of a *nezirah tehorah*, the *chatat* would be a *beheima*, an animal *chatat*, which categorically cannot be brought under conditions of doubt or marginal justification. Only the *chatat ha'of* of a *nazir tamei* benefits from this leniency.

As for the *olah*: here the logic runs in the opposite direction. An *olah* is normally the easiest *korban* to bring — anyone can bring one voluntarily at any time. But because according to Rabbi Yishmael b'no shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka the *olah* does carry some functional role in the *nezirut* framework, this bird labeled as an *olat nazir* cannot be brought by someone who is no longer a *nezirah*. There is no basis to justify it: the *chatat* could be justified by the element of *chet* involved in the *nezirut*, but the *olah* of a *nazir* has no anchor in sin — and a pure voluntary *olah* this bird is not, since it was designated as an *olat nazir*. It falls into an unresolvable middle ground, and therefore she does not bring it.

### Challenge Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בְּנָזִיר טָמֵא כְּתִיב, וַאֲנַן אֲפִילּוּ נָזִיר טָהוֹר קָאָמְרִינַן! קָסָבַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַקַּפָּר נָזִיר טָהוֹר נָמֵי חוֹטֵא הוּא, וְהַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּכְתִיב בְּנָזִיר טָמֵא — הוֹאִיל וְשָׁנָה בַּחֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with Rabbi Elazar HaKappar's dictum: **But this** verse, labeling the nazirite a sinner, **is written with regard to an impure nazirite, and we are saying** that **even a pure nazirite** is a sinner. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Elazar HaKappar holds** that **a pure nazirite is also a sinner. And this is the reason that** the statement that a nazirite is a sinner **is written** in reference **to an impure nazirite** rather than a pure one: **Since he repeated his sin,** as his impurity causes him to start his naziriteship again, he thereby deprives himself for a longer period. He should have taken extra care to prevent this from happening.

The *Gemara* raises one final question against Rabbi Elazar HaKappar: *ve-ha be-nazir tamei kesiv* — the verse he is interpreting, which speaks of sinning *al hanefesh*, is written specifically in the context of a *nazir tamei*. How can he derive from it a general principle that *nezirut* itself constitutes a sin, applying even to a *nazir tahor*?

The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar Rabbi Elazar HaKappar, nazir tahor nami chotei hu* — Rabbi Elazar HaKappar holds that even a *nazir tahor* is called a sinner. The reason the verse specifically mentions the *nazir tamei* is *de-hainu ta'ama* — because this one is *hoveh ve-shoneh be-cheit*, he compounded his sin. Either he extended his *nezirut* beyond its intended term by allowing himself to become *tamei* and thereby prolonging the denial, or, more pointedly, the very fact that he became *tamei* reveals that his commitment to the *nezirut* was insufficiently serious — he did not guard himself carefully enough. As the saying goes, the more one practices, the luckier one gets; accidents happen, but careful people have fewer of them. So the *nazir tamei* is not just a sinner for the self-denial of wine; he is doubly a sinner because his *tumah* demonstrates the superficiality of his commitment.

*Tosafot* prefers the more textual reading: the *nazir tamei* is called a sinner specifically because he stretched out the suffering unnecessarily — *she'lo nizhar mitumah veherich tzar al tza'aro milehanot beyayin* — he was not careful to avoid *tumah* and thereby extended the period during which he denied himself wine. Either way, the principle Rabbi Elazar HaKappar derives is broader than the verse's context: all *nezirut* involves an element of sin.

## Leaves & Re-enters

### Mishnah: Yatza Venichnas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא וְנִכְנַס — עוֹלִין לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן. קָתָנֵי עוֹלִין לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן. מִשּׁוּם דְּיָצָא חָל עֲלֵיהּ נְזִירוּת? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּצָא וְהִזָּה וְשָׁנָה וְטָבַל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that if one took a vow of naziriteship while in a cemetery, **left** the cemetery, **and** then **entered** it again, the days he spent outside **do count** as part **of his tally** of his term of naziriteship, and he is obligated to bring the offerings of ritual impurity upon reentering the cemetery. The mishna **teaches: They do count** as part **of his tally.** The Gemara questions the meaning of this linkage: **Does naziriteship take effect for him because he** merely **left** the ritually impure place? He is still ritually impure, and he cannot begin counting his term of naziriteship until after he has undergone the purification process. **Shmuel said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where he left and** received the **sprinkling** of the ashes of the red heifer on the third day, **and** he **again** received the sprinkling on the seventh day **and immersed,** after which he entered the cemetery a second time. Since he is now ritually pure, his naziriteship takes effect.

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*: *yatza venichnas oleh lo min haminyan* — if a *nazir* who accepted *nezirut* in a cemetery leaves and re-enters, it counts toward his *minyan*. The phrase *oleh lo min haminyan* is potentially confusing here, since it cannot mean he is accumulating days toward his thirty. Every time he re-enters the cemetery, he begins again. What the *mishnah* means is clarified by its continuation: *ve'eino mevi korban, oleh lo min haminyan mevi korban tumah* — he does not bring a *korban tumah* from simply having started his *nezirut* in the cemetery; but once he left, became *tahor*, and then re-entered — now violating an established *nezirut of taharah* — he is obligated in a *korban tumah*. The *chiddush* is that we consider his *nezirut* to have genuinely begun.

### Shmuel's Explanation

The *Gemara* immediately asks: simply walking out of a cemetery does not make one *tahor* — one is still *tamei*. So how can leaving the cemetery trigger the beginning of a new *nezirut*? Shmuel explains: *kegon sheyatza vehiza beshlishi uvishvii vetaval* — the *mishnah* does not mean he simply stepped out. It means he left, underwent the full purification process — the sprinklings on days three and seven, and immersion in a *mikvah* — and thereby became *tahor*. Only then, when he is genuinely *tahor*, does his *nezirut* of *taharah* begin. If he subsequently re-enters the cemetery, he has violated that *nezirut* and is obligated in a *korban*.

### Even Re-entering Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא נִכְנַס הוּא דְּעוֹלִין לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן, לֹא נִכְנַס אֵין עוֹלִין לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן?! לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא יָצָא, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ נִכְנַס — עוֹלִין לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses another question: According to the precise reading of the mishna, his term of naziriteship starts only if he reentered the cemetery; **however,** is it only if he returned and **entered** the cemetery that those days **count** as part **of his tally,** but if he did **not enter,** and remained outside the cemetery, those days **do not count** as part **of his tally?** Why should the start of the naziriteship be dependent upon his reentering the cemetery? The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** as follows: **It is not necessary** to state this *halakha*, that those days count as part of his tally, in the case of one who **left** the cemetery and began his naziriteship, **but even if he entered** the cemetery again immediately after his purification, those days **count** as part **of his tally,** and he will be obligated to bring the offerings of ritual impurity upon his reentry.

The *Gemara* then sharpens the *mishnah*'s formulation. One might have thought: surely leaving and becoming *tahor* is enough to begin the *nezirut* — why does the *mishnah* need to specify that re-entering counts? The answer is that the *chiddush* lies in the following scenario: what if he became *tahor* and re-entered on the very same day he completed his purification? He was *tamei* right up until that point; there is only the briefest sliver of *taharah* separating his old *tumah* from his new violation. One might argue that this thin interval, with no true establishment as a *nazir tahor*, cannot count as the start of a genuine *nezirut* — that the entire period should be seen as one continuous state of *tumah*. The *mishnah* teaches otherwise: *afilu nichnas oleh lo min haminyan* — even if he re-entered on that very same day he became *tahor*, we treat his *nezirut* as having genuinely begun. He has therefore violated it and is obligated in a *korban*. This is the genuine *chiddush*.

### Challenge to Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא מְפָרְשַׁתְּ לַן כְּהָלֵין מִילֵּי? אָמַר לְהוֹן: אָמֵינָא דִּלְמָא לָא צְרִיכִיתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana and Rav Asi said to Rav: What is the reason you did not explain** it **to us with these words** of Shmuel, as explained above? **He said to them: I said** to myself that **perhaps you do not require that** explanation, as I thought it was apparent that this is the proper explanation of the mishna.

Rav Kahana and Rav Assi said to Rav: *mai taama lo mefarshet lan kulhu hanei milei* — why did you not explain all of this to us? Shmuel provided a careful reading of the *mishnah* — clarifying that *yatza* means he left and became *tahor*, and that *venichnas* teaches a *chiddush* even about re-entry on the very same day. These students had apparently been present in Rav's *yeshiva* without receiving this explanation. Rav replied: *amina dela tzerichitu* — I assumed you did not need it, that you understood it yourselves. Why did you not simply ask?

## Rabbi Eliezer Days Rule

### Not Same Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לֹא בּוֹ בְּיוֹם — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַיָּמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים יִפְּלוּ״ — עַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ יָמִים רִאשׁוֹנִים. אָמַר עוּלָּא: לָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אֶלָּא בְּטָמֵא שֶׁנָּזַר, אֲבָל בַּנָּזִיר טָהוֹר שֶׁנִּטְמָא — אֲפִילּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד סוֹתֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna also taught an additional *halakha*: **Rabbi Eliezer says:** This *halakha* does **not** apply to one who entered the cemetery **on that very day** that he left it, **as it is stated** with regard to the *halakhot* of an impure nazirite: **"But the first days shall be void"** (Numbers 6:12), which indicates that he does not bring the offerings **unless he had** his **"first days"** of ritual purity, during which he observed his naziriteship. **Ulla said: Rabbi Eliezer said** this *halakha*, that one day of naziriteship in purity is not sufficient to obligate him to bring offerings if he becomes impure, **only with regard to an impure** person **who took a vow of naziriteship from** the fact **that it teaches: I am hereby a nazirite** for **one hundred** days, **and he became impure** immediately **at the beginning** of the **one hundred,** indicating that it is discussing one who became impure after his term had already started. **And it** further **teaches: Until he will have "first days,"** which proves that Rabbi Eliezer states his *halakha* even with regard to a pure nazirite who later became impure. This is **a conclusive refutation** of Ulla, and his opinion is rejected.

Rabbi Eliezer now introduces a further qualification: *lo bo bayom* — not on the same day. If a *nazir* became *tamei* on the same day he became *tahor*, he does not bring a *korban* and is not considered to have fully violated his *nezirut* in the sense that triggers the *korban* obligation. For that, he must have been established as a *tahor nazir* for at least a second day.

### Earlier Days Fall

The source is the verse: *vehayamim harishonim yipulu, ki tamei nizro* — the earlier days shall fall away, because his *nezirut* was made *tamei*. The word *yamim* — days, in the plural — indicates that there must be multiple days that are falling away. A single day is insufficient.

### Ulla Distinction

Ulla qualifies Rabbi Eliezer's position: *lo amar Rabbi Eliezer ela betamei shenitnazar* — Rabbi Eliezer's requirement of multiple days applies only to the case of one who began his *nezirut* in a state of *tumah*, i.e., the *nazir* who accepted *nezirut* in a cemetery. But if one began as a *nazir tahor* and subsequently became *tamei*, *afilu yom echad soter* — even one day is sufficient, and the *rabbanan*'s rule applies.

### Rava Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי טָמֵא נִזְרוֹ״ — מִשּׁוּם דִּבְטוּמְאָה נְזַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer,** according to Ulla's explanation? **The verse states:** "But the first days shall be void **because his consecration was ritually impure"** (Numbers 6:12), which he explains as follows: Why are his first days rendered void? They are void **because he took a vow of naziriteship,** consecrating himself, **when** he was **in** a state of **ritual impurity.**

Rava explains why Rabbi Eliezer limits his ruling to the cemetery case. The verse says *ki tamei nizro* — and Rava reads this phrase not as "because he made his *nezirut* *tamei*" (i.e., violated it by becoming *tamei*) but rather as *mishum debetumah nazar* — because his *nezirut* itself began in *tumah*. The verse connects the falling away of days (*yamim harishonim yipolu*) specifically to the scenario where the *nezirut* was established in *tumah* from the outset. In that case, Rabbi Eliezer requires a more thorough establishment as a *nazir tahor* — two days — before the violation triggers a *korban* obligation. The logic is comprehensible: a *nazir* who began in *tumah* is still in some sense in the shadow of that original *tumah*; he needs to be more solidly grounded as a *tahor nazir* before a new violation becomes fully operative. A *nazir* who began *betaharah*, by contrast, is clearly violated from the first day, and even one day triggers the obligation.

## Abaye: 100-Day Case

### Tamei Day One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מֵאָה יוֹם״, וְנִטְמָא בִּתְחִלַּת מֵאָה, יָכוֹל יְהֵא סוֹתֵר, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַיָּמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים יִפְּלוּ״ — עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ לוֹ יָמִים רִאשׁוֹנִים, וְזֶה אֵין לוֹ רִאשׁוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rava from a *baraita* that is not in accordance with the opinion of Ulla: One who said: **I am hereby a nazirite** for **one hundred days, and he became ritually impure** immediately, **at the beginning** of the **one hundred** days, one **might** have thought **it should negate** the time he spent as a nazirite. **The verse** therefore **states: "But the first days shall be void"** (Numbers 6:12), which indicates that this *halakha* does not apply **until he will have "first days"** as a nazirite, **and** in **this** case the nazirite **does not have** his **first** days completed, as he became ritually impure right away.

Abaye challenges this reading with a *braisa*: *hareini nazir meah yom* — one declares a hundred-day *nezirut* — *venitma betechilat meah* — and becomes *tamei* at the beginning of that hundred, on day one. The *braisa* asks: might this be *soter*, destroying the days and requiring him to bring a *korban*? The answer: *talmud lomar, vehayamim harishonim yipolu* — the verse requires that there be multiple earlier days. *Ad sheyihyu lo yamim rishonim* — until there are actually earlier days, plural, to fall away. This person has only day one, so there are no *yamim rishonim* — no multiple days — and therefore no *korban* obligation.

The *braisa* directly challenges the attempt to limit Rabbi Eliezer's position to the cemetery case. Here the person began his *nezirut betaharah* — it says he declared a hundred-day *nezirut* and became *tamei* at the start — and yet Rabbi Eliezer's requirement of multiple days still applies. This is a *tiyuvta*, a refutation: Rabbi Eliezer holds his position even for a standard *nazir tahor* who becomes *tamei* on day one.

### Tamei Last Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמָא בְּסוֹף מֵאָה, יָכוֹל יְהֵא סוֹתֵר, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַיָּמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים יִפְּלוּ״ — מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא אַחֲרוֹנִים, וְזֶה אֵין לוֹ אַחֲרוֹנִים. נִטְמָא בְּיוֹם מֵאָה חָסֵר אַחַת, יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא סוֹתֵר, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַיָּמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים יִפְּלוּ״ — מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא אַחֲרוֹנִים, וְזֶה יֵשׁ לוֹ רִאשׁוֹנִים וְאַחֲרוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If one **became ritually impure at the end of one hundred** days, that is, on the hundredth day, one **might** have thought **it should negate** the days he had counted. **The verse** therefore **states: "But the first days shall be void,"** indicating **by inference that there are** other days that can be called the **last** ones, **while this** nazirite **does not have last** days, as he has already completed the tally of his naziriteship. If **he became impure on the one hundredth day less one,** one **might** have thought **it should not negate** the days he had counted. **Therefore, the verse states: "But the first days shall be void,"** indicating **by inference that there are last** ones, **and this** nazirite **has first** ones **and last** ones.

The *braisa* continues: what if he became *tamei besof meah*, on the last day? Might one think he would have to restart the entire hundred days? The verse says *vehayamim harishonim yipolu* — the earlier days fall away. The implication of *rishonim* — "earlier" — is that there are also *acharonim*, later days, days yet to come. If there are earlier days, there must also be later ones. On the very last day, there are no later days remaining — *ein lo acharonim* — and therefore this case is not one where all the days fall away. He brings a *korban* but does not restart the full hundred; he need only add thirty more days, in keeping with Rabbi Eliezer's general position about becoming *tamei* on the final day of a *nezirut*.

The *braisa* then addresses day 99 — *yom meah chasar achat*. Here there are both *yamim rishonim* — the first 98 days — and *acharonim* — day 100 still remains. Accordingly, this case falls squarely within the verse's description: *vehayamim harishonim yipolu*, and he does bring a *korban* and the earlier days are lost. This is why the *braisa* chose a hundred-day *nezirut* as its example: the hundred-day structure makes visible the distinctions between day one (no *rishonim*), the last day (no *acharonim*), and the middle days (both present), in a way that a standard thirty-day *nezirut* would not, since becoming *tamei* on day thirty of a standard *nezirut* already triggers a thirty-day restart and thus produces no practical difference from any other middle day.

One might ask whether *yamim* requires two full days, such that becoming *tamei* on day 99 should be borderline. *Tosafot* raises this question, but the *braisa* explicitly says *meah chasar achat* — the remainder of day 99 plus the entirety of day 100 together constitute sufficient *yamim*. This connects to a further question the *Gemara* will address.

The *soter* language — and the word *yipolu* — are worth noting here. The metaphor embedded in *yipolu* (they fall away) and *soter* (destroying) evokes the image of building a wall: each day is like a brick, and for the structure to fall it must have achieved a certain height. This is not merely poetic; it frames the legal question of when accumulated *nezirut* days are vulnerable to being swept away.

## Yipolu & Yamim Analysis

### Full vs Partial Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: הָלֵין יָמִים דְּקָאָמְרִינַן, דְּנָפֵק חַד וּמַתְחִילִין תְּרֵין, אוֹ דִּלְמָא דְּנָפְקִין תְּרֵין וּמַתְחִילִין תְּלָתָא? לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ, אֲתָא שַׁיְילֵיהּ לְרָבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״יִפְּלוּ״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Those** first **days that we said** he must observe in ritual purity according to Rabbi Eliezer, does it mean **that one** day **has finished and a second has started,** so that if he became impure on the second day it negates his tally, **or perhaps** it means **that two** days **have finished, and a third has started,** which would mean it negates his tally only if he became impure after the beginning of the third day? An answer **was not available to him,** so Rav Pappa **went** to **ask Rava,** who **said to him:** It **is written:** "But the former days **shall be void [*yippelu*]"** (Numbers 6:12) in the plural, which means at least two days need to have passed.

Rav Pappa asks Abaye: when Rabbi Eliezer requires *yamim*, does he mean two full days? Specifically, must the *nazir* have completed all of day one and entered day two before a violation on that second day triggers the *korban* obligation? Or does even a partial day one followed by a partial day two — where he became *tamei* partway through the second day — satisfy the requirement? Abaye did not have an answer. They went and asked Rava.

### Both Terms Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״יָמִים״ וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״יִפְּלוּ״, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״יָמִים״ וְלָא כְּתַב ״יִפְּלוּ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: עַד דְּנָפְקִין תְּרֵין וְעָיְילִין תְּלָתָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״יִפְּלוּ״. וְאִי כְּתַב ״יִפְּלוּ״ וְלָא כְּתַב ״יָמִים״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ חַד, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״יָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it was necessary** for the verse **to write "days" and it was** also **necessary** for it **to write "shall be void"** in the plural. **For if the Merciful One wrote** in the Torah only **"days" and had not** also **written "shall be void"** in the plural, **I would say that** the *halakha* applies only **if two** days **have finished and a third has started. The Merciful One** therefore **wrote** in the Torah the plural form of **"shall be void." And if** the Merciful One **wrote** in the Torah **"shall be void" and had not** also **written "days," I would say even one** day, that is, the *halakha* applies even if he became ritually impure on the first day. **The Merciful One** therefore **wrote** in the Torah **"days,"** indicating that he must have observed at least part of the second day.

Rava answers: *yipolu ketiv* — the verse writes *yipolu*. The word *yipolu* somehow signals that the falling away itself is relatively minor — an action of falling, not a deliberate established loss — and therefore partial days suffice. Each of the two terms in the verse — *yamim* and *yipolu* — contributes something irreducible, and both are necessary.

**If the verse had written only *yamim* without *yipolu*, one would have thought:** *ad denafki tarein umatchil tlata* — you would need two complete days to have passed before becoming *tamei* on the beginning of the third day. The word *yamim* alone implies full days.

**Conversely, if the verse had written only *yipolu* without *yamim*, one might have concluded:** *afilu chad* — even one day (or part of a day) would suffice. The plural implication would be lost, and any period of *taharah* might trigger the obligation upon violation. Therefore both are needed: *yamim* establishes that there must be at least two periods (even if partial), and *yipolu* establishes that those periods need not be full days. All of this operates within Rabbi Eliezer's framework requiring multiple days before a *korban* obligation is triggered.

## Outside EY - Beit Shammai/Hillel

### Aliyah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁנָּזַר נְזִירוּת הַרְבֵּה, וְהִשְׁלִים אֶת נְזִירוּתוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּא לָאָרֶץ, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וּבֵית הִילֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר בַּתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who vowed many** days of **naziriteship** while outside Eretz Yisrael, **and completed his naziriteship, and afterward came to Eretz** Yisrael, in order to bring the offerings at the end of his naziriteship, **Beit Shammai say:** He must be **a nazirite** for **thirty days,** so that he has observed a term of naziriteship in ritual purity in Eretz Yisrael, **and Beit Hillel say:** He is **a nazirite from the beginning,** that is, he must observe his entire naziriteship again.

The *mishnah* now introduces a new case: *mi shenazar nezirot harbe* — one who took upon himself a large *nezirut*, meaning a single extended *nezirut* rather than multiple separate ones. He completed his *nezirut* outside of *Eretz Yisrael* and then came to *Eretz Yisrael* in order to bring his *korbanot* and conclude the process. What must he now do?

**Beit Shammai rules:** *nazir shloshim yom* — he must be a *nazir* for another thirty days. Arriving in *Eretz Yisrael* with a completed *nezirut* in hand, he is still required to do thirty additional days. This is hardly an incentive for *aliyah*.

**Beit Hillel goes further:** *nazir batchila* — he must start all over again, as if his *nezirut* never happened. Whatever years he invested in *chutz la'aretz* count for nothing; he must begin from the beginning. Beit Hillel is not always the more lenient opinion, and this is a striking example.

### Tumat Outside EY

The underlying rationale connects directly to the preceding discussion of a *nazir* in a cemetery. There is a concept of *tumat eretz ha'amim* — the *tumah* of the lands of non-Jewish peoples. A *nazir* who spent years in *chutz la'aretz* is, in a sense, analogous to a *nazir* who spent years in a *beit hakevarot*. The juxtaposition in the *Gemara* seems deliberate: the *tumah* of *chutz la'aretz* is at least partly rooted in the practice of non-Jews burying their dead in all manner of places, not necessarily in formal cemeteries, leaving the ground of the *diaspora* in a state of pervasive *tumah*. This *tumah* is rabbinic — *d'rabbanan* — which is why the Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel dispute operates on a *m'drabbanan* level. The person who completed his *nezirut* outside of *Eretz Yisrael* has fulfilled his *d'oraita* obligations; it is only the rabbinically imposed treatment of *chutz la'aretz* as *tamei* territory that requires him to do something upon arrival in *Eretz Yisrael*.

The *tumah* of *chutz la'aretz* is not a simple technical *tumah*. It is a mixture of actual concern about corpse *tumah* from bones near the surface and an attitudinal dimension — a rabbinic expression of the sanctity of *Eretz Yisrael* relative to *chutz la'aretz*. This is why it is only *d'rabbanan*. For Beit Hillel, only when one arrives in *Eretz Yisrael* can one truly say: *now* you are beginning your genuine *nezirut shel taharah*. The entire *chutz la'aretz* period, whatever its length, is treated as if it were conducted in a state of *tumah*, and must be redone entirely. For Beit Shammai, thirty days is a symbolic acknowledgment of the distinction, without requiring a full restart.

One might ask about the reverse case: does a *nazir* who begins in *Eretz Yisrael* and then leaves for *chutz la'aretz* and returns face a similar obligation? The *Gemara* on this *mishnah* does not address that question directly. It also bears noting that a *nazir* who observes his *nezirut* in *chutz la'aretz* has not sinned *d'oraita* in doing so — he simply cannot conclude the process until he arrives in *Eretz Yisrael* where the *korbanot* can be brought.

### Nazir = Kohen

An important parallel emerges here: the *nazir* and the *kohen* share several defining characteristics — both are forbidden wine when in the *Beit HaMikdash*, both are forbidden to become *tamei laMeit*, and both are not supposed to leave *Eretz Yisrael* for *chutz la'aretz*. The *nazir* is, in a sense, someone striving for the *kedushah* of a *kohen* even outside the *Beit HaMikdash* context. Both prohibitions against leaving *Eretz Yisrael* operate on a *d'rabbanan* level — violating them does not invalidate one's status *d'oraita* — but the parallel is recognized and legally significant.

## Queen Helni Story

### Helni Background

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּהֵילֵנִי הַמַּלְכָּה שֶׁהָלַךְ בְּנָהּ לְמִלְחָמָה, וְאָמְרָה: אִם יָבוֹא בְּנִי מִן הַמִּלְחָמָה בְּשָׁלוֹם — אֱהֵא נְזִירָה שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים. וּבָא בְּנָהּ מִן הַמִּלְחָמָה, וְהָיְתָה נְזִירָה שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים, וּבְסוֹף שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים עָלְתָה לָאָרֶץ, וְהוֹרוּהָ בֵּית הִלֵּל שֶׁתְּהֵא נְזִירָה עוֹד שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים אֲחֵרוֹת. וּבְסוֹף שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים נִטְמֵאת. וְנִמְצֵאת נְזִירָה עֶשְׂרִים וְאַחַת שָׁנָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא הָיְתָה נְזִירָה אֶלָּא אַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna cites a related story: **An incident** occurred **with regard to Queen Helene, whose son had gone to war, and she said: If my son will return from war safely, I will be a nazirite** for **seven years. And her son returned** safely **from the war, and she was a nazirite** for **seven years. And at the end of seven years, she ascended to Eretz** Yisrael, **and Beit Hillel instructed her,** in accordance with their opinion, **that she should be a nazirite for an additional seven years. And at the end of** those **seven years she became ritually impure,** and was therefore required to observe yet another seven years of naziriteship, as ritual impurity negates the tally of a nazirite. **And she was found to be a nazirite** for **twenty-one years. Rabbi Yehuda said: She was a nazirite** for **only fourteen years** and not twenty-one.

The *mishnah* brings a famous narrative: *maaseh be'Helni HaMalka* — the story of Queen Helni. Queen Helni was the ruler of Adiabene, a tributary kingdom in what is today northwestern Iraq, within the orbit of the Parthian Empire. She converted to Judaism along with her family, and her descendants became notable figures in the Jewish world. Her son Munbaz is mentioned in the sources. She was a woman of considerable wealth, and her family's contributions to the Jewish community in Jerusalem are documented.

### Seven-Year Vow

When her son went off to war, Queen Helni took a vow: *im yavo beni min hamilchamah beshalom, harei nezirah sheva shanim* — if my son returns safely from war, I will be a *nezirah* for seven years. This is a striking example of the phenomenon of vowing in exchange for divine protection — bargaining with God — a theme that reaches back to *Yaakov Avinu*: *im Elokim imadi, ushmarani baderech hazos, kol asher titein li*. The *mishnah* makes this motivation explicit in a way few other texts do.

### Aliyah After 7 Years

Her son returned safely. She fulfilled her vow and was a *nezirah* for seven years. At the end of those seven years — *uvesof sheva shanim* — she made *aliyah* to *Eretz Yisrael*, presumably in order to bring her *korbanot* and conclude the *nezirut* formally.

### Another 7 Years

Beit Hillel ruled: *shetihi nezirah od sheva shanim acheros* — she must be a *nezirah* for another seven years. The entire seven years in *chutz la'aretz* counted for nothing under Beit Hillel's ruling. She had to start from the beginning. One might observe wryly that this is not the most encouraging *kiruv* strategy, but the *halacha* is as it is.

### Becomes Tamei Day 14

She observed the second seven years. Then — *uvesof sheva shanim nitmeis* — at the end of that second seven years, she became *tamei*. She therefore had to do another seven years — bringing her total to 21 years as a *nezirah*: the first seven in *chutz la'aretz* (retroactively not counted), the second seven required by Beit Hillel upon arriving in *Eretz Yisrael*, and the third seven required because of *tumah* at the end of the second. This is *21* years of *nezirut*.

### Rabbi Yehuda: 14 Years

Rabbi Yehuda offers an alternative account: *lo haysah nezirah ela arba esreh shanah* — she was a *nezirah* for only 14 years, not 21. This raises two interpretive possibilities. Either Rabbi Yehuda agrees with Beit Hillel that she had to redo the *nezirut* upon *aliyah*, but holds that she did not become *tamei* at the end of the second seven years — and the total is therefore 14. Or Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with Beit Hillel's ruling entirely, holding that the *aliyah* did not require a full restart (perhaps he follows Beit Shammai with thirty additional days, or some other position), and the 14 years represent the original seven plus seven more for having become *tamei* at some point — with no additional seven required for *aliyah* itself. The *Gemara* will explore both possibilities in the continuation.
