# Zevachim 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Holacha Shelo Baragel

### Intro: Holacha Shelo Bar…

The sugya opens in the middle of a discussion concerning the act of *holacha* — the conveying of the blood, after *kabbalah*, from the point of slaughter to the *mizbeach* for *zerika*. The preceding *mishnah* had focused on whether *machshava she-lo lishma* (an invalidating intent) during *holacha* disqualifies a sacrifice, and on Rabbi Shimon's position that since *holacha* is theoretically dispensable — the animal could in principle have been slaughtered right next to the *mizbeach* — a disqualifying thought during *holacha* cannot affect the sacrifice's status. Setting aside *machshava* for the moment, the *gemara* now turns to a distinct question: *holacha she-lo baragel* — conveyance accomplished not by walking but by passing the vessel from hand to hand. If the *mizbeach* is at point B and the blood is at point A, and the *kohen* hands it across rather than physically walking it over, does this constitute a valid act of *holacha*?

### Yochanan: Not Valid

The *gemara* cites an *itmar*: Ulla said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — *holacha she-lo baragel lo shema holacha*. Non-foot conveyance does not constitute *holacha*. The blood must be physically walked from point A to point B; merely handing it across does not fulfill the *avodah*.

### Efshar Letikuna Question

This ruling immediately raises a further question: *efshar letikuna o lo efshar letikuna?* Suppose the *kohen* handed the vessel to someone standing at point X, a midpoint between A and B — an act that, per Rabbi Yochanan, does not count as *holacha*. Is this a nullity that can simply be undone — bring it back to A and walk it over properly — or does this non-foot conveyance actively invalidate the *holacha*, rendering it beyond repair? Rashi notes that the same question applies when a *zar* (non-*kohen*) was involved, but the *gemara*'s primary focus is on the *she-lo baragel* case.

### Mishna: Reversibility

In search of an answer, the *gemara* invokes a *mishnah* cited earlier in the tractate: *kibel hachasher v'natan l'pasul, yachazir l'kasher* — a fit *kohen* received the blood and handed it to an unfit one; the unfit *kohen* then returns it to the fit *kohen*. The plain sense of this *mishnah* is that the fit *kohen* takes it back at point A and walks it properly to B. This seems to demonstrate decisively that the situation is *efshar letikuna* — it can be corrected.

The *gemara* initially deflects by invoking the alternative reading proposed the previous day: perhaps the *mishnah* envisions that the fit *kohen* walks to point X and retrieves the vessel from the unfit *kohen* there, so that the hand-off itself counted as *holacha* — a reading consistent with the view that *holacha she-lo baragel* is valid. But the *gemara* now reads the *mishnah* in its simple sense: the vessel must be returned to point A. Even so, a different version of the ruling now surfaces: *holacha she-lo baragel pesula* — non-foot conveyance is not merely null but actively invalid, which implies it cannot be corrected. The *gemara* then draws the logical inference: *alma lo efshar letikuna* — this language of *pesulah* indicates the act cannot be undone.

### Gemara: Direction Matters

But the *mishnah* of *kibel hachasher v'natan l'pasul* still seems to imply reversibility. The *gemara* resolves this by reframing the case: *mi savrat dekai zar gavai? — dekai zar barai*. Who said that the unfit *kohen* at point X is standing *between* A and B, i.e., closer to the *mizbeach*? Perhaps he is standing on the far side of A, further from the *mizbeach* than the starting point. In that case, the hand-off moved the blood in the wrong direction — away from the *mizbeach* entirely — which everyone agrees is simply nothing. It did not constitute *holacha* in the negative sense; it was zero. In that scenario, returning to point A and proceeding properly is an obvious corrective with no obstacle.

The *gemara* thus introduces a crucial distinction. If the blood is passed in the wrong direction — away from the *mizbeach* — the act is simply null, and a do-over is straightforward. If the blood is passed in the right direction — toward the *mizbeach* — but without walking, our question remains open: is it null or actively invalidating?

## Spilled Blood Issue

### Spilled Blood Case

A fresh challenge is brought. Rav Nachman challenges Ulla from the following ruling: *nishpach min hakli al haritzpa, ve'asfo kasher* — if the blood spilled from the vessel onto the floor during the *kohen*'s conveyance from A to B, one may collect it and continue. The sacrifice is not invalidated.

### Problem: Spillage Toward…

The difficulty is immediate. If the *kohen* spills the blood at point X — some midpoint between A and B — some of the spilled blood will naturally flow in the direction of the *mizbeach*, toward B. That forward movement is *holacha she-lo baragel*: the blood has been propelled toward the *mizbeach* not by deliberate walking but by the spillage. Yet the *mishnah* says we collect it and proceed — implying that this movement does not invalidate.

One might argue that spillage is categorically different, since it may not qualify as human conveyance in the meaningful sense. But there is a counter-argument: the *kohen*'s own energy caused the spill, so in some sense the blood was propelled toward the *mizbeach* through his act, even if not by walking.

### Gemara Solutions

The *gemara* first attempts to resolve this by limiting the case. Perhaps the *mishnah* is discussing a case where the blood spilled *away* from the *mizbeach* — *kesheyatza lachutz* — not toward it. The *gemara* finds this implausible: *levarai nafik legavai lo ayeil?* If it can spill outward, away from the *mizbeach*, can it really be that none of it spills inward, toward it? The *gemara* then offers three technical scenarios in which forward movement could be prevented: *bemakom midron* — a slight incline in the floor such that all the blood flows backward; *beguma* — a small indentation right at point X into which all the blood collected; or *besmicha* — the blood was thick and coagulated, landing exactly where it fell.

### Proof: Initial Kabbalah…

But the *gemara* then objects to this entire approach. Would the *tanna* really go out of his way to construct the ruling only for these narrow, specialized circumstances? Furthermore, the *gemara* adduces a proof from another *perek*: *nishpach al haritzpa ve'asfo pasul* — if the blood went directly from the neck of the animal to the floor, without ever being received into a vessel, and one then collected it, the sacrifice is invalid. The invalidity there is due to the absence of an initial act of *kabbalah*. Critically, the *gemara* points out: if forward spillage during *holacha* were itself invalidating, the later *mishnah* need not have gone to the extreme case of no initial *kabbalah* at all. It could have drawn the distinction internally within the *holacha* case: spillage toward the *mizbeach* is invalid, spillage away is not. The fact that it does not make this distinction proves that forward spillage does not invalidate.

### Conclusion: No Invalidat…

The *gemara* concludes: *tyuvta tyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation of the position that non-foot movement actively invalidates. Even if blood moves toward the *mizbeach* in a manner other than walking, it does not render the *holacha* *pasul*. It is simply null — zero — and the process can be corrected. Whether the blood was handed over or spilled forward, the answer is the same: it does not count as *holacha*, but neither does it destroy the *holacha*. One can return to point A and proceed properly.

## Reinterpretation Attempt

### Radical Proposal

The *gemara* now advances a startling proposal, which Rashi reads as follows. An *itmar* is cited: *holacha she-lo baragel, machloket Rabbi Shimon ve-Rabanan* — the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabanan* in the *mishnah* is not, as one would naturally read it, a dispute about ordinary walking-*holacha* and whether *machshava she-lo lishma* invalidates during such conveyance. Rather, what they are actually debating is the case of *holacha she-lo baragel* — non-foot conveyance. According to this radical reframing, when Rabbi Shimon says *holacha* lacks the power to be affected by disqualifying intent, he is talking specifically about a *holacha* accomplished by handing rather than walking. The *Rabanan*, who say it does invalidate, hold that *holacha she-lo baragel* is a valid *avodah* whose intent matters. This reading sits very awkwardly with the plain text of the *mishnah*, where Rabbi Shimon's stated reason — that the animal could in principle have been slaughtered next to the *mizbeach* — has nothing to do with the mode of conveyance. The *gemara* is aware of this difficulty and will ultimately reject the proposal.

### Large vs. Small Holacha

The *gemara* attempts to make this reframing work by introducing the distinction between *holacha rabta* — the large *holacha*, i.e., ordinary conveyance by foot from point A to the *mizbeach* — and *holacha zutrata* — a small *holacha*. According to Rashi, *holacha zutrata* refers not to a different category of non-foot conveyance but to the minor movement that takes place at the moment of *zerika* itself: even as the *kohen* stands beside the *mizbeach*, the act of extending his arm and propelling the blood forward toward the wall of the *mizbeach* involves a small degree of *holacha she-lo baragel*. The blood is being moved forward — not by foot — from where the *kohen* holds it to where it strikes the *mizbeach*. The *gemara* proposes that on this *holacha zutrata* Rabbi Shimon says *holacha* is not a real *avodah*, while the *Rabanan* say it is. On *holacha rabta*, everyone would agree.

This is difficult on multiple levels: why should the forward extension of the arm during *zerika* be classified as *holacha* rather than as *zerika* itself? And how does this interact with the explicit text of the *mishnah*? The *gemara* proceeds anyway.

### Chatas Ha'of Application

Those in Eretz Yisrael laughed at this proposal — *machu alah be-ma'arava* — and their objection is telling. If the debate of Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabanan* is only about *holacha zutrata*, the question arises: by a *chatas ha'of*, where is *holacha* ever a venue for invalidating *machshava* according to Rabbi Shimon?

In a *chatas ha'of*, the *kohen* uses his fingernail to cut the back of the neck of the bird right at the *mizbeach*, and the blood sprays directly onto the *mizbeach*. There is no separate *kabbalah* into a vessel and no extended conveyance. Now, the *Rabanan* can still find a *holacha* in the movement of the bird itself toward the *mizbeach* after the neck is cut — treating the blood still in the bird somewhat like blood in a vessel, and the arm's motion as the *holacha*. But for Rabbi Shimon, if that very arm-motion is *holacha she-lo baragel* and therefore not a real *avodah*, where can *machshava* invalidate during *holacha*? The *gemara* considers the possibilities: if one had the thought before the blood emerged, that is nothing; if after the blood emerged, that is already *zerika*. So the question is genuine: *depsula be-machshava l'Rabbi Shimon heichi mashkachat lah?*

### Hand-Cutting Hypothetical

The *gemara* responds by directing attention to a different moment: the blood in midair, after it has left the bird's neck but before it strikes the *mizbeach*. At this instant, one could have a *machshava* that invalidates. The proof is a famous *she'elah* posed by Rabbi Yirmeyah to Rabbi Zeira: *haya mazeh ve-nikte'ah yado* — if the *kohen* was in the process of squirting or sprinkling the blood and his hand was severed while the blood was in midair, has the *avodah* been completed by a whole *kohen* or does the cutting — which renders him a *ba'al mum* — invalidate the *zerika*? Rabbi Zeira answers: *pesulah* — it is invalid.

### Avodah Until Impact

The reasoning is derived from the verse, which implies a dual requirement: the act of squirting and the act of placement. *Ve-hizah ve-natan* — he shall sprinkle *and* he shall place it. This indicates that the *avodah* is not complete at the moment the blood leaves the *kohen*'s hand; it extends through the arc of the blood's flight until the moment of contact with the *mizbeach*. This principle has interesting structural parallels in other areas of *halacha* — such as the *melacha* of planting on *Shabbat*, where the act is only fully realized when the seed takes root, yet retroactively the placing of the seed in the ground is the definitive act. Here, however, the ruling goes the other direction: the entire arc of the blood's flight belongs to the *avodah*, so a disqualification occurring mid-flight retroactively contaminates the *zerika*.

Once this is established, a *machshava* during the blood's flight through the air — between neck and *mizbeach* — can also invalidate. And this is where the *gemara* locates the *holacha*-adjacent moment of *chatas ha'of* during which intent matters: not the walking, not the *kabbalah* (which does not exist in this *avodah*), but the blood in flight — which, while technically part of the *hazaya* process, still serves as the moment where *machshava* can take effect. Rashi essentially concedes that this is not quite *holacha* proper; rather, the *gemara* is saying that whatever moment one was positing for *machshava* in *holacha*, one can still find an equivalent moment — the blood mid-flight — that achieves the same invalidating effect.

### Rejection from E.Y.

When Rav Pappa and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua returned from *bei rav* — from the academy — they reported that this is precisely why Eretz Yisrael found the entire reinterpretation laughable. The claim was that on *holacha rabta*, normal walking conveyance, everyone agrees — there is no debate. But this is manifestly false. The *mishnah* in front of us is plainly about ordinary *holacha*, and Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabanan* plainly disagree about it. Are they not in fact debating? Of course they are.

### Correction: Debate Scope

The correction is thus stated cleanly: *ela be-holacha zutrata kulei alma lo pligi depasla* — on the small *holacha*, the minor forward arm-motion during *zerika*, everyone agrees that it does invalidate (with the caveat that there is a *girsa* question here). *Ki pligi be-holacha rabta* — the debate of Rabbi Shimon and the *Rabanan* is precisely about the large, ordinary *holacha* by foot. The strange reinterpretation that attempted to relocate the entire dispute into the domain of *holacha she-lo baragel* is rejected, and the plain reading of the *mishnah* is restored.

## Zar's Holacha

### Zar Conveys

The *gemara* now transitions to a separate but parallel question concerning *holacha* performed by a *zar* — a non-*kohen* — rather than by a *kohen*. The *mishnah* cited near the top of 14b lists *holacha* done by a *zar* as *pasul*. The question now is the same structural question asked earlier by *she-lo baragel*: is this *pesul* correctable?

### Debate: Fixable?

The scenario: a *zar* conveys the blood from point A to point X. This is *pasul*. Can a *kohen* take the vessel, walk it back to point A, and then walk it properly to B? The *gemara* reports that the sons of Rabbi Chiya and Rabbi Yanai debated this: *chad amar kasher, ve-chad amar pasul* — one said it is valid after the correction, one said it remains invalid. The *gemara* glosses this as a dispute about *efshar letikuna* versus *lo efshar letikuna*, exactly parallel to the earlier debate about *she-lo baragel*.

It is worth noting that *holacha* is peculiarly suited to this question of reversibility. Of the four *avodot*, *holacha* alone is non-transformative. *Shechita* by a *zar* cannot be undone; the animal is already slaughtered. *Kabbalah* of the blood by a *zar* cannot be undone; that blood cannot be re-caught. *Zerika* onto the *mizbeach* is final. *Holacha* alone leaves open the theoretical possibility of reversion — the vessel can be walked back, and the process begun again. This quality of *holacha* somewhat resembles the *melachah* of carrying on *Shabbat*, which is classified as a *melachah geru'ah* — qualitatively different in certain respects from the other principal *melachot*, though still fully subject to the *chiyuv*.

### Reverse Scenario

The *gemara* then presents the inverse case: a *kohen* begins the *holacha* and walks the blood from A to X. Then a *zar* picks it up from the *kohen*, walks it back to A, and walks it forward again to X. In absolute terms, the net result is identical to what the *kohen* accomplished on his own — the vessel is at the same point X. Has anything changed?

### Last Act Matters

Rav Shimi bar Ashi analyzes this: *ledivrei hamachshir pasul, ledivrei hapasul machshir* — the debate flips. The disputant who validated the correction when a *kohen* followed a *zar* was reasoning that we define the *holacha* by the last, most proximate act. Since the *kohen* acted last, the *holacha* is defined by him and is kosher. Applying that same logic to the reverse scenario: the *zar* acted last, so the *holacha* is now defined by the *zar* — *pasul*. Conversely, the disputant who invalidated even after the *kohen*'s correction was reasoning that the initial disqualified conveyance is definitive; subsequent acts are superfluous. Applying that logic to the reverse scenario: the *kohen*'s initial conveyance is definitive; the *zar*'s subsequent movement is superfluous — so it remains *kasher*.

### Rava: Ongoing Obligation

Rava rejects this symmetrical analysis: *af ledivrei hapasul, pasul* — even the authority who said it cannot be fixed in the original case (where the *zar* went first) would also say it is *pasul* in this reverse case. His reasoning is *tzarich le-amtuye* — there is an ongoing obligation to bring the blood to the *mizbeach*. Once the *zar* walks the blood back to point A, the blood now sits at A and must be conveyed forward to B. That obligation of *holacha* now rests on a *kohen*, and a *zar* cannot discharge it. It is not sufficient to say that zooming out, the *zar*'s interference was duplicative and the *kohen*'s prior act suffices. Once the blood is returned to A, the obligation of *holacha* from A to B is real and present, and a *zar* discharging it is *pasul*.

Similarly, when the *kohen* acts first: just because the *kohen* walked it to X does not mean the status is locked in permanently. The *zar* then takes it back to A. At that point, A to B must be walked by a *kohen*, and since the *zar* does it, it is *pasul*. In short, Rava's principle is: regardless of who acted first, once the blood arrives at any given point through a reversal, the obligation of *holacha* is freshly reconstituted at that point and must be fulfilled by a *kohen*.

## Rabbi Eliezer's View

### Eliezer: Necessity Requi…

Rabbi Yirmeyah, citing this to Rav Ashi, adds: the precise principle of *tzarich le-amtuye* — that at each stage the *holacha* obligation is freshly constituted and must be fulfilled by the appropriate person — is itself subject to a *machloket*. Specifically, it is the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim* as found in our *mishnah*. The relevant portion of the *mishnah* states: *Rabbi Eliezer omer, ha-mahalach mi-makom she-tzarich le-halech, machshavah poselet; makom she-ein tzarich le-halech, ein machshavah poselet* — if the *kohen* moved through a space he had to traverse in order to bring the blood to the *mizbeach*, *machshavah* invalidates. If he moved through a space he had no obligation to traverse, *machshavah* does not invalidate.

### Rava: Doubling Back

Rava steps in to clarify the precise contours of this debate. One might have assumed the debate is about moving in non-optimal directions — taking a circuitous route, going sideways rather than directly from A to B — such that some segments of movement are necessary and others are not. Rava rejects this. The debate is specifically about a case where the *kohen* moved from A toward B, then doubled back toward A, and then moved again from A toward B.

### Common Ground

Rava first establishes what is uncontested on both sides. *Hakol modim kibel be-chutz ve-hichniso be-fnim, zehu hilucha she-tzarich le-halech* — everyone agrees that catching the blood at point A and walking it toward the *mizbeach* at B is necessary *holacha*, and *machshavah* there invalidates. And *kiblo be-fnim ve-hotzio la-chutz* — if after catching it at A one then walks it in the wrong direction, *away* from the *mizbeach*, that is *hilucha she-ein tzarich le-halech*, and everyone agrees *machshavah* does not invalidate during that movement.

### The Real Debate

The dispute, says Rava, arises in the case of *she-hichniso ve-chazar ve-hotzio* — the *kohen* walked from A toward B (the right direction), then doubled back toward A (the wrong direction). Now he is going to proceed again from A toward B. The *Chachamim* say: right now, moving from A toward B is precisely what must be done — *ha-tzorech le-amtuye* — and therefore *machshavah* here invalidates. Rabbi Eliezer says: looked at in the aggregate, this entire segment of movement from A back to X (where you already were before you doubled back) is unnecessary; it is merely undoing a deviation you introduced yourself. Therefore, it has the status of *hilucha she-ein tzarich le-halech* and *machshavah* there does not invalidate.

In other words, the *Chachamim* look at the micro-level — at each step, is the current movement toward the *mizbeach*? Rabbi Eliezer looks at the macro-level — is this segment of movement, considered in context, genuinely necessary, or is it merely correcting a self-inflicted detour?

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges Rava's reading by pointing to a *braita* that elaborates on Rabbi Eliezer's position. The *braita* says: *kiblah be-chutz ve-hichnisah lifnim, zehu hiluch she-tzarich le-halech* — if one moved from outside toward the *mizbeach*, that is necessary *holacha*. *Kiblah be-fnim ve-hotziah la-chutz, zehu hiluch she-ein tzarich le-halech* — moving away from the *mizbeach* is unnecessary *holacha*. Then the *braita* continues: *chazar ve-hichnisah, zehu hiluch she-tzarich le-halech* — and if one then brought it back toward the *mizbeach*, that is also necessary *holacha*. Explicitly, Rabbi Eliezer considers this return movement to be *tzarich le-halech*. This directly contradicts Rava's reading, which had placed Rabbi Eliezer's innovation precisely in denying that the return movement is necessary.

### Resolution

Rava's response to Abaye is simply: *ei tanya, tanya* — if that is what the *braita* says, then so be it. If Rabbi Eliezer explicitly considers the return movement *tzarich le-halech*, then his position does not serve as a precedent for the principle that *tzarich le-amtuye* can be denied in any case. But then, what exactly is Rabbi Eliezer saying that differs from the *Chachamim*? According to Rashi's reading, the conclusion is that Rabbi Eliezer is not, in the end, making a substantive halachic distinction that differs from the *Chachamim* on any practical outcome. Rather, he is articulating a principle in contrast to his father — Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon said that *holacha* can be theoretically dispensed with, and therefore *machshavah* during *holacha* is harmless even in the standard case. Rabbi Eliezer's response is: I do not reason by theoretical dispensability. I look at whether the movement is practically necessary right now. If it is, *machshavah* invalidates; if not, it does not. On that basis, Rabbi Eliezer agrees with the *Chachamim* about the practical result — once you are moving toward the *mizbeach*, even after a detour, *machshavah* matters — but he is fundamentally rejecting his father's theoretical framework.

With this, the *gemara* closes the first *perek* — *Kol HaZvachim* — which centered primarily on *machshava she-lo lishma* and its relationship to the four *avodot*, with this concluding dive into the nature and weight of *holacha* as an *avodah*.

## Person-Based Invalidatio…

### New Perek Overview

The *gemara* now begins a new *perek*: *Kol HaZvachim She-kiblu Daman*. Where the first *perek* focused on invalidation through improper *intent*, this second *perek* shifts to invalidation through the status of the *person* performing the *avodah*. The *mishnah* begins with *kabbalah* — not *shechita* — because *shechita* may be performed by a non-*kohen* without invalidating the sacrifice. Since we wish to list all the personal statuses that disqualify the *avodah*, including those specific to *kohanim*, we begin with the first act that must be done by a *kohen*.

### Invalidating Categories

The *mishnah* enumerates the following categories: if a *zar* (non-*kohen*) received the blood; if an *onen* (one whose immediate relative died that day and has not yet been buried) received it; if a *shikor* (one who is intoxicated) received it; if a *ba'al mum* (a *kohen* with a disqualifying blemish) received it; if a *tevul yom* — one who has immersed in the *mikvah* that day to purify from *tumah* but upon whom sundown has not yet arrived, rendering his purification incomplete — received it; if a *mechussar kippurim* — one who has completed his immersion and awaited sundown but has not yet brought his concluding *korbanot*, and who therefore remains in a quasi-*tamei* status resembling a *shelishi* — received it; if the *kohen* was *mechussar begadim*, not wearing the required *bigdei kehunah*; if he had not washed his hands and feet as required before the *avodah*; if he was *arel*, uncircumcised — Rashi explains this might apply even to a pious Jew whose family had a history of hemophilia, where circumcision was contraindicated; if he was *tamei*; if he was *yoshev*, sitting rather than standing during the *avodah*; if he was standing on a vessel, a rock, a carpet, or even wearing something on his feet rather than standing directly on the floor; if he was standing on an animal; or if he was standing on someone else's feet — in all these cases the sacrifice is *pasul*. Additionally, if the *kohen* received the blood with his left hand, the sacrifice is *pasul* — though Rabbi Shimon is *machshir* in that case, a dispute noted here and to be elaborated upon.

### Deriving from Pessukim

The *gemara* immediately asks: *za minailan* — from where do we derive that a *zar* who performs the *avodah* invalidates the sacrifice? The *gemara* notes that the prohibition itself is not yet in question — it asks specifically about the *chilul*, the invalidating effect. Levi cites the verse in Vayikra 22: *ve-yinazru mi-kodshei Bnei Yisrael* — they shall separate themselves from the holy things of *Bnei Yisrael* — and *ve-lo yechalelu et shem kodshi* — they shall not desecrate My holy name. The surrounding context is about *kohanim* and *tumah*, suggesting that a *kohen* who is *tamei* and touches *kodshim* brings about *chilul*. But the *gemara* questions what function the phrase *Bnei Yisrael* serves in this verse. Does it exclude women — suggesting that *korbanot* of women are not subject to *tumah* concerns? Obviously not; that would be absurd. Does it exclude non-Jews — implying that their offerings are not subject to *tumah* invalidation? Also implausible: in fact, when non-Jews bring sacrifices and there is a *tumah* problem, the *tzitz* — the golden plate worn by the *Kohen Gadol* — does not atone for it, unlike with Jewish sacrifices. The *gemara* cites the principle: *ovdei kochavim, bein be-shogeig bein be-mezid, lo hurtzah* — for non-Jews' sacrifices, whether the *tumah* was incidental or deliberate, the *tzitz* does not effect atonement. This is because the verse says *le-ratzon lahem lifnei Hashem* — the *tzitz* atones on behalf of *them* specifically. So non-Jewish sacrifices face a *more* stringent situation regarding *tumah*, not a more lenient one. The exclusion of *Bnei Yisrael* cannot therefore be teaching that their sacrifices are exempt from *tumah* concerns.

The *gemara* concludes: *ela hachi ka'amar* — rather, the verse is saying this: *Bnei Yisrael* must separate themselves from My holy things — meaning non-*kohanim* must distance themselves from performing the sacrificial *avodah*, lest they *mechalel* it. The phrase *Bnei Yisrael* is thus read as identifying the class of people — non-priestly Israelites — who must not approach the *avodah*, and the verse teaches that their doing so constitutes *chilul*.

### Kal V'Chomer Logic

The *gemara* then cites a second approach, from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: the *zar*'s invalidation can be derived by *kal va-chomer* from *ba'al mum*. From Vayikra 21:17–23, we learn that a *kohen* with a *mum* may not perform the *avodah*, and the verse applies the language of *lo yechalel* — desecration — to his doing so. Now: a *ba'al mum* is permitted to *eat* from *kodshei kodashim*; yet if he performs the *avodah*, he invalidates it. A *zar* is not permitted even to eat from *kodshei kodashim* — his exclusion is total. Does it not follow *a fortiori* that if a *ba'al mum*'s partial exclusion causes *chilul* when he performs the *avodah*, a *zar*'s total exclusion certainly should?

The *gemara* deflects: *mah le-va'al mum she-ken asah bo karev ke-makriv* — there is a unique feature of *ba'al mum*: the laws of *mum* apply not only to the *kohen* but also to the animal. An animal with a *mum* is also disqualified from being offered. This dual application of *mum* — to both the officiant and the sacrifice — might be what gives it the power to cause *chilul* when the blemished *kohen* officiates, and this extra dimension is absent by a *zar*.

### Common Denominator

The *gemara* then proposes the resolution through a *tzad ha-shaveh* — a common denominator argument. *Tamei* — the other person explicitly mentioned in the verse — is *muzhrar* and causes *chilul*, yet *tumah* does not apply to animals in the same dual manner as *mum* (live animals cannot become *tamei*). Conversely, *ba'al mum* may lack the spreading dimension of *tumah*, which *tamei* possesses. Neither *ba'al mum* alone nor *tamei* alone suffices for the *kal va-chomer*, but the shared feature — *she-muzharin she-im avdu chalelu*, that both are warned not to perform the *avodah* and if they do they invalidate it — is the operative principle. From this common denominator we derive: *af ani avi zar she-hu muzhar* — I will also include a *zar*, who is equally warned against performing the *avodah*, and conclude *ve-im avad chillel* — if he did so, it is invalidated. The *gemara* notes that the derivation of the *zar*'s prohibition itself — from which his *muzhar* status is established — will be the subject of the next discussion.
