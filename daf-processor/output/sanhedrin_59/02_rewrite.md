# Sanhedrin 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arayos Dispute

### Retzicha & Fetuses

Today's *daf* is 59, and we are continuing from where the previous session left off, in the middle of 58b, picking up approximately twelve lines from the top, at the *tashma* concerning Kayin marrying his sister. The broader discussion at this point concerns the scope of *arayos* that apply to *Bnei Noach*.

The previous session raised a number of important preliminary issues. The first concerned *retzicha*: is there a unique *halacha* for *Bnei Noach* in this area, and how does it apply to *ubarim*, to fetuses? For *Yisrael*, abortion is not treated as a capital crime; for *Bnei Noach*, however, it is included in the *sheva mitzvos Bnei Noach* by virtue of the phrase *adam ba'adam*. One might suggest that this reflects a broader principle: by *Bnei Noach*, the concept of *shiurim* — formal minimum measures that define whether a prohibited act has been committed — does not apply in the same way. Anything that falls broadly under the rubric of a prohibition is included, without a minimum threshold. However, this will require qualification, because later in the *Gemara* we will encounter areas where, by contrast, the formal definitions actually seem *narrower* for *Bnei Noach* than for *Yisrael*.

This question has practical relevance. If a non-Jewish physician performs an abortion, there is a clear issue of *lifnei iver* with respect to the *sheva mitzvos Bnei Noach*. Whether that in turn reflects upon the status of abortion as an *averah* for Jews — not necessarily a capital crime, not *retzicha*, but some form of prohibition — is a question that will surface in the *Tosafot* later in this session.

### Women's Status

A further point reviewed from the previous session: women occupy an interesting position within the framework of *Bnei Noach*. On the one hand, the adjudicatory standards for *Bnei Noach* are notably non-formal — a single witness (*ed echad*) suffices and *hasra'ah* is not required — yet women were specifically excluded from serving in that judicial capacity. In the substantive obligations of the *sheva mitzvos Bnei Noach*, however, women are fully included.

### Marriage Concepts

With respect to *arayos*, one central distinction that emerged in the prior session is the definition of marriage for *Bnei Noach*. For a *ben Noach*, marriage is defined exclusively by *be'ulat ba'al* — the act of intercourse within a marital relationship. There is no concept of *erusin* for *Bnei Noach*. Accordingly, the category of *na'arah me'urasah* — a betrothed young woman — does not exist in the *Bnei Noach* framework. Nevertheless, if a non-Jew had intercourse with a Jewish *na'arah me'urasah*, since she constitutes an *ervah* under Jewish law, and since the act involved a Jewish woman, it would be punishable — governed by Jewish law, including *skilah*, rather than by the standards of *Bnei Noach*.

### Akiva vs. Eliezer

With that background established, the *Gemara* returns to determining the scope of *arayos* for *Bnei Noach*. Two principal positions have emerged. Rabbi Akiva limits the prohibited *arayos* for *Bnei Noach* to those relationships for which a Jew would be liable to the death penalty, deriving this from the *pasuk* of *al kein ya'azov ish et aviv ve'et imo* — which he reads as encompassing father, mother, father's wife, a male, an animal, and so forth, all of which carry a *chiyuv mita*. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, does not focus on the death penalty; rather, he centers his analysis on the principle of *achva* — the sisterhood relationship — and accordingly prohibits sisters, a father's sister, a mother's sister, and similar relationships, even where the violation carries only *karet* rather than *mita*. There is one point of agreement between all opinions: familial relationships are traced matrilineally, not patrilineally, which is practically sensible since the mother's identity is always certain.

## Adam & Kayin

### Kayin's Sister

With this framework in place, the *Gemara* introduces a *tashma* — a source that bears on the dispute. The question posed is: *mifnei mah lo nasa Adam et bito* — why did Adam not marry his own daughter? He had a wife in Chava, but in theory, after her sin, one might have expected Chava to die; even granting that she did not, the question remains why Adam, who could theoretically have taken any of the women available to him (limited as that pool was), did not choose a daughter. The answer given is: *kedei sheyisa Kayin et achoto* — so that Kayin could marry his sister. The *pasuk* cited is *ki amarti olam chesed yibaneh*: Adam's setting aside a woman he might otherwise have claimed was an act of *chesed* for his son. The implication that follows — *ha lav hachi asira* — is that without this special circumstance, a sister would have been forbidden to *Bnei Noach*. This appears to support Rabbi Eliezer, who prohibits sister relationships, and to work against Rabbi Akiva, since marrying one's sister carries only *karet*, not *mita*, and Rabbi Akiva should therefore permit it to *Bnei Noach*.

### General vs. Special

Rabbi Akiva's response is: *kivan de'ishtarei ishtarei* — once Kayin was permitted to marry his sister, the permission became generalized for all *Bnei Noach*. Since marrying a sister is not a *chiyuv mita* for *Yisrael*, Rabbi Akiva maintains that *Bnei Noach* are altogether *mutar* in this area. Rabbi Eliezer, for his part, reads the permission granted to Kayin as a special *chesed* — an exceptional allowance — and does not generalize it.

### Rashi Chesed

Rashi draws attention to a striking linguistic detail in the *parsha* of *arayos* itself: the *pasuk* concerning a man who marries his sister uses the word *chesed* — *ve'ish asher yikach et achoto bat aviv... chesed hu*. It is a peculiar term in the context of a prohibition. Rashi, both here and in his commentary on the *Torah*, connects this usage directly to the episode of Kayin: the permission granted for that union was a *chesed*, an act of grace, though in principle a sister is forbidden to *Yisrael*. The *Gemara* thus presents a hermeneutic tension: was the allowance for Kayin a special dispensation that defines the general *Bnei Noach* rule (Rabbi Eliezer), or was it an exception that once granted became universally normative for *Bnei Noach* (Rabbi Akiva)?

### Rav Huna Daughter

The *Gemara* then introduces a third position. *Amar Rav Huna: Kuti mutar bevito* — a non-Jew is permitted with his daughter. This is a striking ruling. Both Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Eliezer, despite their differences, would prohibit a daughter: marrying one's daughter carries a *chiyuv mita* for *Yisrael*, which would include it under Rabbi Akiva's criterion, and it is not a sister relationship, which is Rabbi Eliezer's criterion, yet it would still be covered by a general prohibition. Rav Huna's position takes a far narrower reading of the *pasuk* of *al ken ya'azov ish et aviv ve'et imo*: only those specific relationships explicitly derivable from that *pasuk* through *drashot* — father, mother, father's wife, a male, an animal — are prohibited. Sister and daughter are simply not textually included, and therefore *Bnei Noach* are *mutar* in those cases. This is also the position of the Rambam.

### Reconciliation

A potential objection presents itself: if a daughter is *mutar* for *Bnei Noach*, why did Adam not marry his daughter? The answer restates the *tashma*'s conclusion: *kedei sheyisa Kayin et ha'achot* — halachically, Adam *could* have done so, but he refrained as a *chesed* in order to leave a woman available for Kayin to marry as a sister. The fact that Adam did not marry his daughter proves nothing about the prohibition; it reflects only his *chesed*.

### Three Positions

An alternative formulation of Rav Huna is also reported: *Kuti assur bevito* — the daughter is forbidden. The proof offered is *teida shelo nasa Adam et bito* — the very fact that Adam did not marry his daughter demonstrates the prohibition for *Bnei Noach*. The *Gemara* rejects this: *veloha* — this is not a valid proof. As explained, Adam refrained not because of a prohibition but in order to allow Kayin to marry a sister, as expressed by the verse *olam chesed yibaneh*. Accordingly, three distinct positions emerge in summary: Rabbi Eliezer, who focuses on the principle of *achva* and prohibits sisterhood relationships while setting aside relationships defined through marriage (such as a father's wife); Rabbi Akiva, who focuses on *chayavei mita min haTorah* and accordingly prohibits the relationships that carry a death penalty while permitting sisters; and the third view — likely a refinement within Rabbi Akiva's position — which adopts the most restrictive reading of the *pasuk*, limiting the prohibitions to what is explicitly textually derived and thereby permitting even a daughter. It should be noted that under all views, familial relations for *Bnei Noach* are defined matrilineally, such that under Rabbi Eliezer a sister through the father's side would be *mutar*, while a full sister through the mother was permitted to Kayin as either a special exception (Rabbi Eliezer) or as the general rule (Rabbi Akiva).

## Slave Conversion

### Slave Relations

The *Gemara* now turns to a related but distinct category: the converting slave (*eved*). *Amar Rav Chisda: eved mutar be'imo* — a slave who undergoes conversion is permitted with his mother — *mutar bevito* — and with his daughter. The novelty here requires emphasis: nobody holds that a *ben Noach* is *mutar be'imo*. Mother is a universally prohibited relationship. And certainly *Yisrael* is prohibited with his mother. So how does this slave fall outside all categories?

### Quasi-Newborn

The underlying principle is *k'katan shenolad dami* — the converting slave is treated as one who has been newly born. Upon undergoing *tevilah* and conversion, he enters a quasi-Jewish status. Accordingly, the laws of *Bnei Noach* no longer apply to him. But what accounts for the *heter* with his mother and daughter? In principle, as a quasi-Jew, the negative prohibitions of Jewish law should bind him, and mother is prohibited to every Jew. The answer lies in the logic of *k'katan shenolad dami*: he is no longer halakhically related to his pre-conversion family. Technically, the mother he was born to is no longer, in Jewish legal terms, his mother.

### Paradox

This gives rise to a pointed paradox. We know from material covered in the prior session that when a non-Jew converts, the familial prohibitions from the non-Jewish period are *retained* by Rabbinic enactment — specifically to prevent someone from concluding that Jewish law is more lenient than non-Jewish law (*shelo yomru azavnu issur chumur venachnu ba'in le'kal*). That principle, rigorously applied when a full convert enters Jewish status, is evidently not applied here to the *eved*. The distinction seems to be that the *eved* is in an intermediate, quasi-Jewish state: Jewish law applies to him in virtually all respects, yet his pre-conversion family ties are not recognized for purposes of the prohibition. If he were now to father a daughter, that daughter *would* be prohibited to him — because his new familial relations, formed post-conversion, are fully operative.

The irony is complete: the Rabbinic enactment that protects against the perception of leniency kicks in once he becomes a full Jew, retroactively treating his pre-conversion mother as prohibited. But in the *eved* state, before that transition is complete, this concern is apparently set aside.

### Limbo Status

What remains somewhat puzzling is why the concern about the appearance of leniency does not apply here at all, given that the *eved* is a quasi-Jew, governed by Jewish law in most areas and living within a Jewish household. The best way to understand it may be that the *eved* occupies a genuinely liminal status — neither fully *Bnei Noach* nor fully *Yisrael*, in a kind of *nisht ahin nisht aher* category. The *Gemara* seems to tolerate the ambiguity of that transitional state rather than importing the full stringency of either legal framework into it. That said, there is something inherently strange about allowing this *limbo* to persist, and one might read into the *Gemara*'s implicit tolerance of that strangeness a certain hesitation about keeping people in this undefined condition too long.

## Yichud Marriage

### Designated Woman

The *Gemara* now introduces a different scenario. *Ben Noach she'yichad shifcha l'avdo* — a *ben Noach* who designated a maidservant for his slave — and then the gentile master himself had intercourse with her — *neherag aleha* — is liable to the death penalty. Why? At an earlier point in the *Gemara*, this was explained as a form of *gezel*: since the woman was designated for the slave, the master, by taking her, is effectively stealing her from his slave — treating her as an object of property. The Rambam, however, takes a different approach: he categorizes this case under *arayos* for *Bnei Noach*, specifically as a form of *eshes ish*.

The Rambam's reasoning is illuminating. For free persons, the *Bnei Noach* framework defines marriage through *be'ulat ba'al* — intercourse within an established marital relationship — and there is no parallel institution for slaves, since historically the institution of slave marriage was not socially recognized in the same way (families were routinely separated, and formal marriage was not accorded to enslaved persons in antiquity). Nevertheless, the *halacha* recognizes a de facto societal marriage for slaves through the mechanism of *yichad lo* — designation. What makes this even more striking is that, on the Rambam's reading, *yichad lo* constitutes marriage even *before* intercourse — it is earlier in the process than *be'ulat ba'al*, the standard marker for free persons. The Rambam thus understands the *chiddush* of this case as establishing that for slaves, *yichud* — social designation — replaces intercourse as the constitutive act of marriage.

### Yichud Definition

This framework means that for purposes of *arayos* and the prohibition of *eshes ish* within the *Bnei Noach* system, enslaved individuals acquire marital status through a different mechanism than free persons — one grounded in social recognition rather than in a formal legal act or in the act of intercourse itself.

### Recognition Point

*Me'eimasai* — from when does that designated status take effect? *Amar Rav Nachman: mi-d'karu lah rivisa d'planya* — from the time that people call her "the young woman of so-and-so," meaning when she is societally recognized as belonging to that slave's household. The marker is communal perception: once the surrounding community identifies her as this man's woman, the designated status has attached.

### Hair Uncovered

When does the status dissolve? *Amar Rav Huna: mi-she-par'ah rashah ba-shuk* — once her hair is uncovered in the marketplace. This reflects a broader societal reality that the *Gemara* acknowledges elsewhere as well: in ancient society, married women covered their hair. The *Gemara* derives that hair-covering is *das Yehudis* from the fact that the *sotah*'s hair is uncovered — implying that covered hair was the standard societal norm for married women. The dissolution of the slave's designated marital status follows the same logic of social reality: once her hair is publicly uncovered, the community understands that she is no longer designated.

### Rambam Divorce

For free non-Jews, the question of how marriage ends is equally important. The Rambam provides a clear formulation: *u'me'eimasai tihyeh eshes chaveiro k'grusha shelanu* — when does another man's wife become as a divorcée for us? *Mi-she-hotziah mi-beiso v'yeshalachah l'atzmah* — once he has sent her out from his home. Or alternatively, *o mi-she-teitzei hi mi-tachas reshuso v'telech lah* — if she leaves on her own, departing from his domain. *She'ein lahem gerushin b'chsav* — they have no written *get*. *V'ein ha-davar taluy bo l'vado* — the matter does not depend on the man alone. Rather, *kol zman she-yirtzeh hu o hi lifrosh zeh mi-zeh porshin* — whenever either party wishes to separate, they may do so.

This is a highly significant ruling: *Bnei Noach* divorce can be initiated by either party, husband or wife, with no formal document required. Either walking out of the marital home suffices.

### De Facto Marriage

This flows directly from the foundational principle that *Bnei Noach* marriage rests on *be'ulat ba'al* — on the lived reality of a couple cohabiting as husband and wife — rather than on any formal legal act. The Jewish system, by contrast, involves an act of *kiddushin* as a form of *kinyan*, initiated by the man, and it is dissolved only by *gerushin* initiated likewise by the man through the delivery of a *get*. The formal asymmetry of acquisition and dissolution is baked into the Jewish marital structure. The *Bnei Noach* system, lacking *erusin* entirely, is grounded in a de facto reality that both parties generate and either party can end. It is not analogous to *kiddushei biah* — intercourse as a formal act of *kiddushin* — but rather to something closer to common-law marriage: the ongoing reality of shared life, participated in by both, constitutes the marriage, and its cessation by either terminates it.

### Modern Aguna

This *Bnei Noach* model of marriage has attracted the attention of certain contemporary scholars and advocates in Israel who, confronting the *aguna* crisis, have proposed that Jews might contract marriage according to the *Bnei Noach* framework — based on *be'ulat ba'al* alone, bypassing *kiddushin* entirely — such that either party could walk away without needing a *get*. Whether *Bnei Noach* law can apply to Jews in this way, and whether such a scheme is legally achievable, remains deeply contested. But it is a fascinating instance of this *Gemara's* implications being brought to bear on a very live contemporary problem.

## Silo Kdarka

### Unnatural Acts

The *Gemara* now enters a distinct area. *Amar Rabbi Chanina: ben Noach she'ba al ishto she'lo k'darka chayav* — a *ben Noach* who has intercourse with his own wife in an unnatural manner is liable. This is surprising: we are no longer speaking about prohibited women, but about the nature of the act itself within marital intercourse. The source is the *pasuk* of *ve'davak* — he shall cleave to his wife — and the negative implication: *ve'lo she'lo k'darka*.

### Ve'davak

Why does *she'lo k'darka* fall outside the scope of *ve'davak*? One approach is simply physical: the bodies do not come together in the manner suggested by the word *ve'davak* — "cleaving." Rashi adds a different dimension: based on the *Gemara*'s assumption elsewhere that unnatural intercourse is generally not pleasurable for the woman, he understands *ve'davak* as requiring emotional adhesion, and that emotional adhesion — the genuine bonding implied by cleaving — is present only when both parties derive pleasure from the act. *She'mitoch she'eina nehenit ba'davar eina nidbeket imo* — since she does not experience pleasure, she does not truly cleave to him.

### Another's Wife

The *Gemara*, however, raises an immediate objection to Rabbi Chanina's formulation. *Amar Rava: mi ikka midi de'le'Yisrael lo mechayav u'le'Kuti mechayav?* — is there anything that is not a transgression for a Jew but *is* a transgression for a non-Jew? Some manuscripts have this even more starkly: *de'le'Yisrael shari u'le'Kuti assur* — that something is fully permitted for a Jew but prohibited for a non-Jew? This reflects a general *Gemara* principle. Indeed, with respect to this very act, the *rishonim* debate whether *she'lo k'darka* within marriage poses any problem for Jews — the simple reading of the *Gemara* is that for Jews it is not a transgression — and if so, how can it be a *chiyuv* for non-Jews?

Rava therefore reframes the principle entirely: *ben Noach she'ba al eshet chavero she'lo k'darka patur* — a *ben Noach* who has intercourse with *another man's wife* in an unnatural manner is *exempt*. A man and his own wife may have intercourse in any manner they choose. The prohibition of *she'lo k'darka* is not about the nature of the act within the marital relationship; rather, the *pasuk*'s prohibition of *eshes ish* is derived from *ve'davak be'ishto* — cleaving to *his* wife — implying *ve'lo be'eshet chavero*. What is prohibited with another man's wife is the act of *ve'davak*; but intercourse with another man's wife performed *she'lo k'darka* does not constitute that act of *ve'davak*, and therefore, for a *ben Noach*, is not a transgression.

### Narrow Definition

This is a remarkable ruling: unnatural intercourse with another man's wife, which for a Jew would constitute a severe violation of *arayos* and carry a *chiyuv mita* or *karet*, is simply not a transgression for a *ben Noach*. The formal definition of the prohibited act is narrower for *Bnei Noach* than for *Yisrael*.

### Shiurim Limits

This is worth pausing over, because it stands in tension with the earlier observation that *Bnei Noach* does not operate with *shiurim* — that their prohibitions cast a broader net than *Yisrael's*. We saw that anything broadly included in *gezel* or *retzicha* is forbidden to *Bnei Noach* even without the formal minimum measures that govern Jewish law. Yet here, in the realm of *arayos*, the formal definition of what constitutes a prohibited act is actually *narrower* for *Bnei Noach*. This asymmetry — broader liability in some areas, narrower in others — is striking and not immediately obvious to explain.

## Violence Acts

### Hit Jew Death

*Amar Rabbi Chanina: oveid kochavim she'hikkah et Yisrael chayav misa* — a non-Jew who strikes a Jew is liable to the death penalty. This goes beyond *retzicha*, which covers killing; here the *Gemara* is speaking of mere battery. The proof is drawn from Moshe's killing of the Egyptian: *vayifen koh vachoh vayach es haMitzri*. What had the Egyptian done? He was beating an Israelite — not, as far as the text indicates, with lethal intent. Yet Moshe killed him. The *Gemara* reads from this that even the act of beating a Jew is a *chiyuv misa* for a non-Jew. The historical and social context — a people living under the physical oppression of a foreign power — is palpable in this *halacha*.

### Slapping Cheek

Rabbi Chanina adds a further teaching: *ha-soter lo'o shel Yisrael* — one who slaps the cheek of a Jew — presumably a non-Jew, though the text is not entirely explicit — is *ke'ilu soter lo'o shel Shechinah* — as though he slapped the cheek of the Divine Presence. The *pasuk* cited is *mokash adam... yela kodesh* — striking a person is as striking the cheek of the Holy One. It is hard not to note the contrast with a certain famous dictum in another tradition, which counsels turning the other cheek to one who strikes it. The *Gemara* takes the diametrically opposite position: striking a Jew is not a forgivable affront between persons, but an offense against God Himself.

### Lifted Hand

*Amar Reish Lakish: ha-magbiah yado al chaveiro* — one who raises his hand against his fellow — *af al pi shelo hikah, nikra rasha* — even without having struck him, is called a *rasha*. The *pasuk* is: *vayomer la-rasha lamah takkeh re'echa* — Moshe said to the wicked one, "Why *will you* strike your fellow?" The verb is future tense — *takkeh*, not present — meaning Moshe called him a *rasha* not because he was already striking but because he *was about to*. The grammatical reading of *tashma mina* is explicit: *lamah hikisa lo ne'emar ela lamah takkeh* — the text does not say "why did you strike" but "why will you strike," demonstrating that the status of *rasha* attaches at the moment of raising the hand, before any blow is landed. It is worth noting that Reish Lakish himself was reputed to have come from a rough background, which lends a certain personal resonance to his concern with the transgression of violence. One might also note that the *pshat* of the text — both combatants were fighting, and Moshe identified who was at fault — does not require the reading the *Gemara* offers; nevertheless, the *Gemara* derives from it this principle of pre-blow culpability.

### Sinner Status

*Ze'eira amar Rabbi Chanina* offers a more lenient characterization: *nikra chotei* — such a person is called a *sinner* rather than a *rasha*. The *pasuk* brought is from the narrative of Eli's sons: *ve'im lo lakachti be'chozkah* — "if you do not give me the sacrifices, I will take them by force." The *pasuk* subsequently records: *vatehi chatas ha-ne'arim gedolah me'od* — the sin of Eli's sons was very great. So one who threatens forcible taking — which likely implies striking — is characterized as a *chotei*, which carries somewhat less weight than the designation *rasha*.

### Cut Hand

*Rav Huna amar tikatzeitz yado* — the hand of such a person deserves to be cut off. The *pasuk* is *uzroa ramah tishaveir* — a raised arm shall be broken/cut off. And this was not merely theoretical: in one actual case involving a habitual offender, Rav Huna did in fact cut off his hand. This is an extraordinarily severe measure, and it raises an immediate question about its legal basis.

### Din Debate

Rashi explains that Rav Huna acted in the framework of *beit din makin ve'onshin shelo min hadin* — the court's authority to administer punishment beyond the formal requirements of the law when circumstances demand it. This man was a habitual aggressor, and the extrajudicial punishment was necessary. Tosafot, however, reads Rav Huna differently: Tosafot holds that Rav Huna believed this to be the actual *din* — not a special *knas* or extraordinary measure, but the genuine *halacha* that a raised hand deserves amputation. This is a sharp dispute. The physical punishment of cutting off a hand has no parallel in the standard repertoire of *halachic* penalties, which reach as far as *malkos* and the various forms of *misat beit din* but not amputation. Rashi's reading is therefore far more comfortable: it was a *shelo min hadin* ruling, an exceptional judicial response to an exceptional situation. But even under Rashi's understanding, the severity is notable — *beit din* can impose unprecedented forms of punishment when the need arises, including, in appropriate cases, measures that would otherwise exceed what the formal *din* prescribes.

## Shabbat Ban

### Shabbat Prohibition

*Va'amar Reish Lakish: oved kochavim she'shavas chayav misa* — a non-Jew who rests on *Shabbos* is liable to the death penalty. This is one of the most famous and striking statements in this section of the *Gemara*. The ironies are sharp: the Jewish people spent centuries fighting for the right to a day of rest and were often persecuted for observing *Shabbos*; yet, according to Reish Lakish, non-Jews are *prohibited* from doing precisely that.

### Day Night Work

The *pasuk* is drawn from the covenant with Noach: *yom valaila lo yishbotu* — day and night shall not cease. Although the simple reading of this verse concerns the continuation of natural rhythms and seasons, the *Gemara* reads it as a directive: non-Jews may not cease from their labors. Day and night, there is no permitted cessation.

### Weekday Rest

*Amar Ravina: afilu sheini be'Shabbos* — even if a non-Jew takes rest on a Monday, or on any weekday at all, this is problematic. This is in addition to *Shabbos* and Sunday.

### Rashi Secular

Rashi's reading is carefully delineated: *lo teima shevisa deka'amar Reish Lakish leshum chova ka'amar* — do not think that the *shevisa* Reish Lakish speaks of refers specifically to adopting a day as a religious obligation, like *Shabbos* for *Yisrael* or Sunday for Christians. Rashi emphasizes *menucha be'alma* — even a purely secular day of rest. The *chiddush* of Ravina's statement is that even *sheini*, a Monday, with no religious character whatsoever, is included. The implication, as Rashi reads it, is that *Bnei Noach* are not entitled to any day off at all, whether religious or secular.

### Passive Violation

The *Gemara* anticipates the obvious question: if a non-Jew who rests on *Shabbos* is *chayav misa*, why is *Shabbos* not counted among the *sheva mitzvos Bnei Noach*? The answer: *shev v'al ta'aseh, kum va'aseh lo ka'chashiv* — the *sheva mitzvos* list includes only prohibitions that are violated by active transgression. *Shabbos* is violated by being passive — by doing nothing. A prohibition violated by inaction alone is not included in the formal count. This answer implicitly takes the *Shabbos* prohibition quite seriously as a real *chiyuv* for *Bnei Noach*.

### Slave Rest

A related question arises: what about Jewish slaves? An *eved Kena'ani* has quasi-Jewish obligations. Does he get *Shabbos* rest? And conversely, if a non-Jewish slave had not yet undergone *tevilah* and did not yet have the quasi-Jewish status of an *eved Kena'ani*, does the prohibition against taking a *Shabbos* rest apply to him? One might also note the parallel to animals: the *Torah* requires animals to rest on *Shabbos*, which would presumably apply to a non-Jewish slave as well. Whether that obligation on the master's part supersedes the prohibition on the slave's resting is not fully resolved here.

### Dinim Exception

The *Gemara* raises a parallel question about *dinim*: *v'dinim, kum va'aseh hu v'ka'chashiv?* — but *dinim*, the obligation to establish courts of justice, involves a positive obligation (*kum va'aseh*), yet it is counted among the *sheva mitzvos*. The answer is that *dinim* is different: it has both an active and a passive dimension. The active dimension is actively perverting justice — *lo ta'avur mishpat*, the prohibition on corrupt judgment. The passive dimension is the failure to establish courts at all. Since *dinim* can be transgressed through active wrongdoing, it is appropriately included in the formal list. *Shabbos*, by contrast, can only be transgressed passively, by resting, and therefore does not make the list. This further confirms that included in *dinim* is not merely the institutional obligation to establish a court system, but also the active prohibition of perverting justice.

### Rambam Religious

The Rambam reads this entire passage differently from Rashi — and his reading connects directly to the next topic, the prohibition on non-Jews learning *Torah*. For the Rambam, the problem of a non-Jew keeping *Shabbos* is not about a secular day off or about depriving oneself of one's right to work. Rather, it is about *creating a new religious practice*. The Rambam makes this explicit: *velo manichim osan lechadesh das vela'asos mitzvos l'atzman mida'atan* — we do not allow them to innovate a religion or to create *mitzvos* for themselves on their own initiative. His formulation: *ela yihyeh ger tzedek veyekabel kol hamitzvos o ya'amod betorato velo yosif velo yigra* — a non-Jew must either become a full *ger tzedek* and accept all the *mitzvos*, or remain within *his* religious framework — the *sheva mitzvos Bnei Noach* — without adding or subtracting. Accordingly, even taking rest on a weekday is problematic if done as a religious practice. And crucially, the Rambam holds that while a non-Jew who does these things is in principle *chayav misa*, the sanction is actually more like *chayav karet* — not literal execution: *makkin oso ve'onshin oso umodi'in oso she-hu chayav misa al zeh aval eino neherag*.

## Torah Learning Ban

### Inheritance Pasuk

*Va'amar Rabbi Yochanan: nochri she'osek ba-Torah chayav misa* — a non-Jew who engages in Torah study is liable to the death penalty. The source: *Torah tziva lanu Moshe morasha* — Torah that Moshe commanded us is an inheritance. *Morasha lanu — lanu morasha velo lahem* — it is our inheritance, not theirs.

### Morasha Drasha

Why then is the prohibition of Torah learning not listed among the *sheva mitzvos Bnei Noach*? The *Gemara* addresses this through two competing *drashot* on the word *morasha*. According to the first: *migo d'gazal leh* — since the non-Jew steals it from us — it is already subsumed under *gezel*, one of the *sheva mitzvos*. According to the second, the word *morasha* is read as *me'orasah* — betrothed: the Jewish people are betrothed, as it were married, to the *Torah*. On this reading, a non-Jew who engages with *Torah* is analogous to one who has intercourse with a *na'arah me'orasah* — a betrothed young woman. Notably, this is a clever inversion: *na'arah me'orasah* is not naturally part of the *Bnei Noach* framework, since *Bnei Noach* only recognize *be'ulat ba'al* and have no concept of *erusin*. A non-Jew would normally not transgress the prohibition of *na'arah me'orasah* unless the woman was Jewish, because Jewish law governs that act in relation to a Jewish woman. Here, the *Torah* is cast as the Jewish woman who is *me'orasah* to the Jewish people — and the non-Jew who violates that bond transgresses accordingly.

### Kohen Gadol

The *Gemara* now introduces a directly contrasting *braisa*. Rabbi Meir asks: *minayin she'afilu oveid kochavim she'osek ba-Torah she-hu k'kohen gadol* — how do we know that even a non-Jew who engages in Torah study is like a *kohen gadol*, the highest possible religious status? The *pasuk*: *eileh ha-mitzvos asher ya'aseh osam ha-adam va-chai ba-hem* — these are the commandments that a person shall do and live by them. *Kohanim, Levi'im v'Yisraelim lo ne'emar* — the text does not say *Kohanim*, *Levi'im*, or *Yisrael*. It says *ha-adam* — any person. *Ha lamadta she'afilu oveid kochavim v'osek ba-Torah harei hu k'kohen gadol*. This is an extraordinary, universalist statement: the study and practice of *Torah* is open to all humanity on equal terms, and a non-Jew who engages in it stands on par with the highest Jewish religious office.

### Reconciliation

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Yochanan's prohibition and Rabbi Meir's expansive statement: *hatam b'sheva mitzvos did'hu* — Rabbi Meir's ruling applies when the non-Jew studies *Torah* within the context of his own *sheva mitzvos* — that is, learning the laws that pertain to him. Engaging with the broader corpus of *Torah* beyond that boundary is the transgression Rabbi Yochanan identifies.

### Universalist

That said, one should recognize what is actually at stake in these texts. The *braisa* cited — likely a *Tosefta* — contains a broader collection of universalist statements that go beyond the *Gemara's* harmonizing conclusion. One version reads not merely *osek ba-Torah* but *osek ba-Torah v'oseh es ha-Torah* — engaged in *Torah* and actually fulfilling it, meaning performing *mitzvos*. The collection includes the *pasuk* of *v'yavo goy tzaddik shomer emunim* — and a righteous nation that observes faithfulness shall come — on which the *braisa* similarly comments: *Kohanim, Levi'im v'Yisraelim lo ne'emar, ela v'yavo goy tzaddik*. Even a *goy tzaddik* enters the gates of God. And the *pasuk* of *ha-adam asher ya'aseh osam ha-adam va-chai ba-hem* — "a person" shall do them and live by them — applies to any person, with no ethnic qualification.

### Unique Framework

These statements articulate a strikingly universalist religious vision, and it is worth pausing to appreciate their significance. It is, in fact, remarkable — unusual within the broader history of religions — for a community to maintain that someone can fulfill the divine will, attain *olam haba*, and stand before God as righteous *without belonging to that community*. The very existence of the *sheva mitzvos Bnei Noach* as a coherent alternative framework for non-Jewish religious life is itself a major *chiddush* in the theology of world religions. It is not the dominant *Chazalic* voice — it tilts toward minority opinion — but it is present and powerful. Moreover, the *Torah*-as-equalizer trope connects to a familiar rabbinic theme: *afilu mamzer v'osek ba-Torah harei hu k'kohen gadol* — even a *mamzer* who studies *Torah* is like a *kohen gadol* — derived from *yekarah hi mipeninim, yekarah hi mi-kohen gadol ha-nichnas lifnai v'lifnim*. All internal Jewish hierarchy dissolves before *Torah* learning; and, on these readings, so too does the Jewish/non-Jewish boundary. The *Gemara* works to contain these statements within the structure of *sheva mitzvos*, but the original formulations carry a universalism that transcends that containment.

## Ever Dam

### Blood Included

*Ve'amar Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel: af dam min hachai* — even blood from a living animal is prohibited for *Bnei Noach*, not only *eiver min hachai* (a limb from a living animal).

### Double Prohibition

The *braisa* is brought: *ach basar benafsho damo lo tocheilu* — this verse is the source for *eiver min hachai*. Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel reads it as generating a double prohibition: *karei bei basar benafsho lo tocheilu, damo benafsho lo tocheilu* — "flesh while alive you shall not eat" *and* "blood while alive you shall not eat." Since the word *damo* was included alongside *basar*, he derives an independent prohibition on *dam min hachai*.

### Blood Distinct

The Rabbis respond: *hahu lemishrei sheratzim hu de'ata* — that textual element comes to teach something else. Specifically, it comes to distinguish between *eiver min hachai* in a standard animal and in a *sheretz* (rodent). In a standard animal, the blood is considered sufficiently distinct from the flesh that the prohibition of *eiver min hachai* applies separately to each. In a *sheretz*, however, the blood is legally considered merged with the flesh and therefore does not generate an independent category.

### Lifeblood

Returning to Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel, the *Gemara* continues: *rak chazak levilti achol ha-dam ki ha-dam hu ha-nefesh* — the verse's emphasis on *ki ha-dam hu ha-nefesh*, "for the blood is the soul," is understood similarly to what was seen regarding *Bnei Noach* — as indicating that *dam min hachai* is a distinct prohibition. The Rabbis demur: *hu le-dam ha-kazeh she-ha-neshamah yotzet bo* — the phrase *dam hu ha-nefesh* identifies only lifeblood, the blood from which life departs, as the category that carries *karet*. This is not blood as an independent prohibition of *dam min hachai*; it is rather a specification of *which* blood is the subject of the blood prohibition in general. Rashi explains further: the blood that constitutes *dam hu ha-nefesh* is not necessarily the first blood to flow — not the initial squirt — but the arterial blood, the sustained lifeblood. Technically all blood is biologically the same, but *Chazal* identified a particular quality of blood — the blood that the life itself depends upon — as the operative category for the prohibition carrying *karet*. The Rabbis thus resist the inference that *dam min hachai* is a separate and independent prohibition for *Bnei Noach*.

## Repetition Rule

### Why Repeat

The *Gemara* now raises a structural question: *lama li lemichtav le-Bnei Noach v'lama li lemishni be-Sinai* — if *eiver min hachai* was already prohibited to *Bnei Noach*, and *Yisrael* are themselves descendants of *Bnei Noach*, why did the prohibition need to be repeated at *Har Sinai*?

### Ever Application

The answer invokes the principle of Rabbi Yosi be'Rabbi Chanina, which the *Gemara* will now articulate. The repetition at Sinai was necessary to establish that *eiver min hachai* applies to *both* groups — *Bnei Noach* and *Yisrael* alike — rather than being absorbed entirely into the Jewish framework and ceasing to apply universally. Without the repetition, one might have mistakenly concluded otherwise.

### Both Groups

*D'amar Rabbi Yosi be'Rabbi Chanina: kol mitzvah she'ne'emrah le-Bnei Noach v'nishneis be-Sinai lezeh u'lezeh ne'emrah* — any commandment that was given to *Bnei Noach* and repeated at Sinai applies to both groups. This part is intuitive: if a law was given universally and then reaffirmed at Sinai, it remains universal.

### Only Yisrael

The counterintuitive half of the principle follows: *le-Bnei Noach v'lo nishneis be-Sinai, le-Yisrael ne'emrah v'lo le-Bnei Noach* — if it was given to *Bnei Noach* and not repeated at Sinai, it applies only to *Yisrael* and not to *Bnei Noach*. This is, as acknowledged, a deeply puzzling rule. The intuitive expectation would be the opposite: if a law was given to *Bnei Noach* but never repeated or incorporated into the Sinaitic corpus, one would expect it to remain binding on *Bnei Noach* and perhaps not apply to *Yisrael*, who received their own comprehensive legal system. Yet the *Gemara* inverts this: it is the *Bnei Noach* who are released from such a law, while *Yisrael* remains bound by it. The practical consequence for *eiver min hachai*: had it not been repeated at Sinai, one would have concluded it applied only to *Yisrael*. Since it was repeated, we know it applies to both.

### Gid Hanasheh

What is the one example of a *mitzvah* that was said before Sinai and not repeated at Sinai, such that it applies only to *Yisrael*? *Ve'anu ein lanu* — the only case is *gid hanasheh*, according to Rabbi Yehuda. This requires clarification. The *pasuk* says *al kein lo yochlu Bnei Yisrael et gid hanasheh* — therefore the children of Israel shall not eat the *gid ha-nasheh*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that this commandment was given at the time of Yaakov Avinu — i.e., before Sinai — and was not repeated at Sinai. The *Rabbanan* disagree: they hold the *mitzvah* was only given at Sinai and the *pasuk* was written earlier in the narrative but is not the original promulgation. Under Rabbi Yehuda's reading, *gid hanasheh* is the paradigmatic case of a pre-Sinaitic law that was never repeated — and it applies only to *Yisrael*, not to *Bnei Noach*. Of course, as the *Gemara* itself notes, there is an irony: the *pasuk* explicitly says *Bnei Yisrael* — on its face, it seems to confine the prohibition to *Yisrael* from the outset.

### Logic Objection

The *Gemara* raises the objection that logic would seem to demand: *adaraba* — shouldn't we say the opposite? Since it was *not* repeated at Sinai, we should say it applies only to *Bnei Noach* — that is, it retained its original pre-Sinaitic, universal character — and not to *Yisrael*, who received their own comprehensive code at Sinai?

### Avodah Zara Proof

The *Gemara* answers with empirical evidence rather than theoretical argument: *mide'isti ovdei kochavim be-Sinai* — we know that the prohibition of *avodah zara* was repeated at Sinai. And *ve'ashkachan de'anshu ovdei kochavim alavah* — we know that non-Jews are punished for *avodah zara* (the nations of Canaan were driven out for this very sin). And we know *Yisrael* is punished for *avodah zara*. Thus: a law that was stated to *Bnei Noach* and repeated at Sinai applies to *both* groups. *Shema mina lezeh u'lezeh ne'emrah* — this is confirmed by the case of *avodah zara*. This settles the first half of the principle.

### No Contradiction

The *Gemara* then addresses the counterintuitive second half — the rule that a law stated to *Bnei Noach* but not repeated at Sinai applies only to *Yisrael* — with a different logical argument: *leka midi de'le-Yisrael shari vel'ovdei kochavim assur* — there cannot exist a law that is permitted for *Yisrael* yet prohibited for *Bnei Noach*. It is inconceivable that the more comprehensive legal system governing *Yisrael* would permit something that the more basic *Bnei Noach* code forbids. Therefore, if a law was given to *Bnei Noach* and then incorporated into the Sinaitic corpus — even if it was not explicitly re-stated — it must apply to *Yisrael*. And conversely, the failure to repeat it would signal that it has been, in some sense, absorbed exclusively into the *Yisrael* framework rather than retained as a universal *Bnei Noach* obligation.
