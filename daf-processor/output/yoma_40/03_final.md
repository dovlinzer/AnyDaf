# Yoma 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hagralah Me'akev?

### Yannai vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: עֲלִיַּית גּוֹרָל מִתּוֹךְ קַלְפִּי — מְעַכֶּבֶת, הַנָּחָה — אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף עֲלִיָּיה אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai said:** The **drawing of the lot from inside the receptacle is an indispensable** part of the service, as it determines which goat will be for God and which for Azazel. However, the actual **placing** of the lots upon the goats **is not indispensable. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** the **drawing of the lots** from inside the receptacle **is not indispensable,** since the High Priest may designate the goats himself, without employing the lottery.

We pick up at the bottom of 39b with a question that has been developing: is the *hagralah* — the actual act of drawing lots — *me'akev*, that is, indispensable to the validity of the *avodah*, or is it merely the ideal procedure that can be bypassed *bedi'avad*?

This was the subject of a dispute between Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Yochanan. Rabbi Yannai holds that *aliyat hagoral mitoch hakalpi me'akevet* — the drawing of the lot from the box is indispensable. Rabbi Yochanan holds that it is not *me'akev*; the designation of the animals can be valid even without the formal lottery.

### Order Outside Kodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: דְּבָרִים הַנַּעֲשִׂין בְּבִגְדֵי לָבָן מִבַּחוּץ לָא מְעַכְּבָא — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא. כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, מַאן דְּאָמַר מְעַכְּבָא — כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא מְעַכְּבָא — הָנֵי מִילֵּי עֲבוֹדָה, הַגְרָלָה לָאו עֲבוֹדָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dispute: **In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that **matters that are performed in** the **white garments outside** of the Holy of Holies **are not indispensable, everyone agrees that** the drawing of the lots **is not indispensable,** since it is held outside the Holy of Holies. **When they disagree, it is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya.** He holds that all matters performed in the white garments, even those performed outside the Holy of Holies, are indispensable. **The one who said** the drawing of the lots **is indispensable** holds **in accordance with** the straightforward application of the principle of **Rabbi Neḥemya. And the one who said** the drawing of the lots **is not indispensable** claims that **this** principle **applies** only with regard **to** matters that are classified as a Temple **service.** The **drawing of the lots is not** a Temple **service,** therefore it is indispensable, even according to Rabbi Neḥemya's principle.

The *gemara* asks whether this dispute tracks a more fundamental debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemia regarding whether the sequential order of services performed *outside* the *Kodesh Kodashim* is *me'akev*. Rabbi Yehuda holds, broadly, that the order outside the *Kodesh Kodashim* is not *me'akev* — and the *gemara* understands this to imply that certain things done outside may not be *me'akev* in substance either. Rabbi Nechemia insists that even outside the *Kodesh Kodashim* the order is *me'akev*.

The *gemara* considers whether there might be a lineup between these positions: perhaps Rabbi Yochanan, who says the *hagralah* is not *me'akev*, follows Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Yannai, who says it is, follows Rabbi Nechemia. The *gemara* then notes, however, that the alignment is not clean in either direction. One could argue that even according to Rabbi Yehuda the *hagralah* is *me'akev* because the Torah repeats the phrase *asher ala*, *asher ala* — a doubling that would indicate indispensability. Conversely, one could argue that even according to Rabbi Nechemia the *hagralah* is not *me'akev*, because it is not technically an *avodah* at all — as evidenced by the fact that a non-*Kohen Gadol* can perform it and it may be done with the left hand.

### Mitzvah Lehagril

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוָה לְהַגְרִיל, וְאִם לֹא הִגְרִיל כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from that which was taught in a *baraita*: **It is a mitzva to draw** the lots, **and if** the High Priest **did not draw the lots** but instead designated the goats without using the lots, the designation **is valid.**

The *gemara* now brings the first *braitah* in support of Rabbi Yochanan. The text reads: *mitzvah l'hagril, v'im lo higril kasher* — it is a *mitzvah* to draw the lots, and if one did not draw them, it is nevertheless valid. This appears to be straightforward evidence that *bedi'avad* the *hagralah* is not *me'akev*.

### Mitzvah in Mikdash

Before proceeding, it is worth pausing on the word *mitzvah* in this context. When the *gemara* uses *mitzvah* in the context of *avodah* in the *Mikdash*, it does not carry the same meaning as an individual's personal obligation in the conventional sense — the question of whose *mitzvah* it is becomes blurred when we are discussing communal ritual and Temple service. The *Geonim* actually distinguished a third category alongside *asei* and *lo ta'asei*, called *mitzvot tzibburiot* — communal laws. In this context, *mitzvah* simply means this is the proper, prescribed way to perform the service, but *bedi'avad* if it is not done, the procedure is still valid.

The *gemara* says that this *braitah* works well according to one reading of the dispute — specifically the reading in which both Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yannai agree that according to Rabbi Yehuda the *hagralah* is not *me'akev*, and the debate is only about whether Rabbi Nechemia would also agree. On that reading, the *braitah* can simply be attributed to Rabbi Yehuda, and there is no difficulty.

The problem arises according to the other version of the dispute, in which Rabbi Yannai and Rabbi Yochanan are debating not within Rabbi Nechemia's position but within Rabbi Yehuda's, such that Rabbi Yannai holds that even Rabbi Yehuda would say the *hagralah* is *me'akev*. On this reading, the *braitah* stating it is not *me'akev* has no one to attribute it to. Everybody would say it is *me'akev*, and yet the *braitah* says otherwise.

### Hagril vs. Haniach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנִי: מִצְוָה לְהַנִּיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Perforce, the *baraita* cannot be referring to drawing the lots, and one must emend and **teach** the *baraita* as saying that **it is a mitzva to place** the lots on the goats.

The *gemara* resolves this difficulty by amending — or reframing — the text of the *braitah*. Instead of reading *mitzvah lehagril*, "it is a *mitzvah* to draw the lot," the *gemara* proposes reading *mitzvah lehaniach*, "it is a *mitzvah* to place the lot on the head of the animal." On this reading, the *braitah* is saying that *bedi'avad* if you drew the lot but did not actually place it upon the animal's head, the procedure is still valid. This does not say anything about whether drawing itself is *me'akev*.

### Tosafot Interpretation

Tosafot, in the second comment on 40a, notes that this emendation is not meant literally — the *gemara* is not physically rewriting the *braitah*. Rather, as Tosafot explains: *eino magiah braitah, ela perushei kamefaresh* — it is an interpretive gloss. When the *braitah* says *lo higril*, it means "did not complete the lot-process," which can include the act of placement. So the *braitah* is being reread to refer to *hanachah* — the placing of the lot — not to the drawing itself.

### Higril and Hitvadah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מִצְוָה לְהַגְרִיל וּלְהִתְוַדּוֹת. לֹא הִגְרִיל וְלֹא הִתְוַדָּה — כָּשֵׁר. וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי לְהַנִּיחַ, אֵימָא סֵיפָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא הִגְרִיל — כָּשֵׁר, לֹא הִתְוַדָּה — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge to Rabbi Yannai's opinion, as presented in the second version of the dispute, which maintains that both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya hold that drawing of the lots is indispensable. A *baraita* teaches: **It is a mitzva** for the High Priest **to draw the lots and to confess** upon the goat to be sent to Azazel. **If he did not draw the lots** or **did not confess,** the service is still **valid.** This *baraita* also appears to say that the drawing of the lots is not indispensable, in contradiction to Rabbi Yannai's opinion. **And if you say: So too,** the *baraita* should be emended to say it is a mitzva **to place** the lots on the goats, this is problematic. How will you then **say,** i.e., explain, **the latter clause** of that *baraita*, which teaches: **Rabbi Shimon says: If he does not draw the lots, it is valid. If he does not confess, it is invalid?**

The *gemara* then brings a second *braitah* to challenge this further. This *braitah* reads: it is a *mitzvah* to draw the lot (*lehagril*) and to recite the *viduy* — the confession; if one did not draw the lot and did not recite the *viduy* (*lo higril v'lo hitvadah*), it is nevertheless valid (*kasher*). This introduces a new element: not only does the *braitah* say the *hagralah* is not *me'akev bedi'avad*, it says the same of the *viduy* — the confession — which is separately striking.

The *gemara* anticipates that we might again apply the same solution used for the first *braitah* — that *lo higril* here also means "did not place the lot" — and rejects this on the basis of what comes next.

### Shimon's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לֹא הִגְרִיל״? אִילֵּימָא לֹא הִנִּיחַ, מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר הַגְרָלָה מְעַכְּבָא, וְהָתַנְיָא: מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, מֵבִיא חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בַּהַגְרָלָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the challenge from the latter clause: In this *baraita*, **what** is the meaning of: **He did not draw the lots? If we say** it means **he did not place** the lots on the goats, then **by inference Rabbi Shimon holds** that while the placing is not indispensable, the **drawing** of the lots **is indispensable.** But this is incorrect, since **wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If, following the designation of the goats, **one of them died,** a new goat **is brought** to be the **counterpart** of the surviving goat and is designated **without drawing lots; this is the statement of Rabbi Shimon.** It would therefore appear that the *baraita* should not be understood as referring to the mitzva to place the lots, but as referring to the drawing of the lots themselves. Therefore, the challenge to Rabbi Yannai's opinion remains.

The *braitah* continues: Rabbi Shimon says, *lo higril kasher, lo hitvadah pasul* — if you did not draw the lot, it is valid; but if you did not recite the *viduy*, it is invalid. Rabbi Shimon thus takes a position that diverges from the *Tanna Kamma* specifically on the question of *viduy*: everyone agrees on the lot, but Rabbi Shimon insists the *viduy* is *me'akev* while the *Tanna Kamma* holds it is not.

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* now challenges the reinterpretation. If we read *lo higril* as "did not place the lot" throughout this *braitah*, then the implication of Rabbi Shimon's concession — that *bedi'avad* not placing is acceptable — would be that *not drawing altogether is me'akev*. That is, Rabbi Shimon would be implicitly agreeing that drawing the lot is indispensable, with only the placement being dispensable *bedi'avad*.

But this cannot be Rabbi Shimon's position. The *gemara* demonstrates this from another teaching: *met echad meihen meivi chaveiro shelo behagra'ah* — if one of the two goats died, Rabbi Shimon holds that you bring a replacement without performing any new lottery. This is a strikingly radical ruling: not only does Rabbi Shimon hold that the drawing of the lot is not *me'akev*, he holds that the entire procedure of pairing the animals and designating them jointly is not *me'akev*. If the *se'ir lecha'tat* drops dead after *shechitah*, you can simply take a new goat and send it as the *se'ir la'Azazel* without repeating the *hagrala* process at all.

### Me'akev Framework

It is worth stepping back at this point. We have this major debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yannai, with considerable back-and-forth over whether the *hagralah* is *me'akev*. And now we discover that there is a *Tanna* — Rabbi Shimon — who very clearly holds it is not. However, Rabbi Shimon's position is being treated as exceptional and bracketed. The discussion proceeds within the assumption of those who regard the overall process as *me'akev*, asking only whether the drawing specifically is *me'akev* within that framework.

### Shimon Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא יָדַע מַאי קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לְהוּ: אִי הַגְרָלָה — הַגְרָלָה מַמָּשׁ קָא אָמְרִיתוּ, פְּלִיגְנָא עֲלַיְיכוּ בַּחֲדָא. אִי הַגְרָלָה דְּקָאָמְרִיתוּ הַיְינוּ הַנָּחָה — פְּלִיגְנָא עֲלַיְיכוּ בְּתַרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rabbi Yannai's opinion can still be defended by claiming that **Rabbi Shimon did not know** precisely **what the Sages were saying,** i.e., whether they were referring to the drawing or the placing of the lots. Therefore, in his response to them, **this is** what **he is saying: If** when you say: **Drawing of the lots, you are saying** that the **actual drawing of the lots** is not indispensable, as I also hold, then **I disagree with you** only **with regard to one** *halakha*, namely with regard to the indispensability of the confession. But **if,** when you say: **Drawing of the lots, you are saying** only that the **placing** of the lots is not indispensable, but you assume that the drawing of the lots is indispensable, then **I disagree with you with regard to two** *halakhot*, i.e., with regard to the indispensability of both the drawing of the lots and of the confession.

The *gemara* resolves the challenge regarding this second *braitah* by explaining that Rabbi Shimon was actually uncertain what the *rabbanan* meant when they said *bedi'avad* you do not have to do the lot. So Rabbi Shimon responds in two hypothetical registers: *i hagra'ah hagra'ah mamash kamarisu* — if you mean literally that you do not have to draw the lot, then I fully agree, and I will only argue with you about the *viduy*, where I think it is *me'akev*. *I hagra'ah d'kamrisu haynu hanachah* — but if what you meant by "not doing the lot" is only that you do not have to place the lot on the animal's head while you still hold that drawing is required, then I argue on both points. In other words, Rabbi Shimon is clarifying his position: if the *rabbanan* agree with him that drawing is not *me'akev*, he has only one disagreement with them — the *viduy*. If they only mean that placement is not *me'akev* while drawing remains required, he disagrees on both counts.

The *gemara's* conclusion is that the plain sense of the *braitah* is that both the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Shimon hold that drawing the lot is not *me'akev*. The *gemara*'s attempt to reframe their words as referring to *hanachah* runs into Rabbi Shimon's radical position, which demonstrates that the language really does refer to drawing. The *gemara* therefore attributes the framework position to Rabbi Shimon explicitly and acknowledges that the reinterpretation proposed earlier does not fully resolve all the textual evidence.

### Me'akev Assumption

One might ask: is it not strange that something so central to the *avodah* — or something so fundamental to a *korban* generally — should simply not be *me'akev*? The answer lies in a general principle that *Chazal* apply throughout the laws of *korbanot*. Outside the *Mikdash*, the assumption is that if the Torah says to do something, not doing it means you are not *yotzei* — there is no question. But in the laws of *korbanot*, where there is a vast number of details and particulars, *Chazal* begin with the opposite baseline: the assumption is that most details are *not me'akev* unless the Torah somehow underscores or repeats the requirement, or unless the Torah uses the language of *chok* — which does appear in the context of *Yom Kippur* and would indicate indispensability. Without such an indicator, many procedural details of *korbanot* are presumed to be *lech'tchila* requirements only. This principle applies broadly — even outside *Yom Kippur*, in the laws of *chatat* for instance, if only one of the four corners of the *mizbeach* received its placement of blood rather than all four, *bedi'avad* one may still be *yotzei*.

## Par vs. Seira Order

### Par vs. Seira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פַּר מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַשָּׂעִיר,
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge to Rabbi Yannai's opinion that all agree that the drawing of the lots is indispensable, from a *baraita*: Various services are performed with the bull and the goat and their blood. These services must be performed in a specific sequence, often alternating between the bull and the goat. If one service is neglected and a later service is advanced to be before it, the *halakha* is as follows: Failure to perform a service of **the bull** that was to precede a service of the goat **disqualifies** the service of **the goat,** if it was performed without that service of the bull preceding it.

The *gemara* now turns to a different *braitah* bearing on the same question. The *braitah* states: *arba'ah me'akvin b'de'oraisa* — four things are *me'akev* by Torah law. Among them: *par me'akev et ha'seira* — the *par* prevents the *seira* from being valid (if the *seira* goes out of sequence before the *par*), but *ein ha'seira me'akevet et hapar* — the *seira* does not prevent the *par* from being valid if the *par* goes out of sequence.

To understand this, the *par* here refers to the *Kohen Gadol's* bull, and the *seira* is the goat brought as *l'Hashem chatat* — not the *se'ir hamishta'leach*, but the communal goat whose blood is brought inside as part of the purification of the *Mikdash*. Both animals play roles in the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur*, but their services are interleaved in a specific sequence.

### Service Sequence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׂעִיר אֵין מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַפָּר בְּמַתָּנוֹת שֶׁבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** However, failure to perform a service of **the goat** that was to precede a service of the bull **does not disqualify** the service of **the bull;** rather, it is valid after the fact. This last rule **applies to the applications** of blood performed **inside** the Holy of Holies.

Rashi provides a remarkably clear summary of the entire order of services involving the *par* and *seira*, evidently having surveyed the broader *sugya* and compiled the sequence from across the relevant discussions. The order is as follows:

First, the *Kohen Gadol* recites *viduy* on the *par* — the first *viduy par*, covering himself and his household. Then he performs the *hagrala* — the drawing of the lots on the two goats. Then he recites a second *viduy* on the *par*, this time encompassing the *kohanim* as well. He then performs *shechitah* of the *par* and enters the *Kodesh Kodashim* carrying the *kaf* and *machtah* to offer the *ketoret*. He then gives the blood of the *par* on the *kaporet*. He then returns and performs *shechitah* of the *seira*, takes its blood, and gives it on the *kaporet* as well. Then he gives the blood of the *par* on the *parochet*, followed by the blood of the *seira* on the *parochet*. Finally, he mingles the two bloods and applies them to the inner *mizbeach*.

### Inside/Outside Kodesh

Looking at this sequence, we can identify which services are performed inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* and which are not. The services inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* are: the blood of the *par* on the *kaporet*, the blood of the *seira* on the *kaporet*, and the *ketoret*. The *parochet* sprinklings and the inner *mizbeach* are already outside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, in the *Heichal*. The *gemara* clarifies that the rule about *par* preventing *seira* applies only to *matanot shebifnim* — the services performed inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. This matters because, as we noted earlier, Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemia debate whether order is *me'akev* outside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, and the rule here operates specifically within the domain where order is unambiguously *me'akev*.

### Which Avoda?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא פַּר מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַשָּׂעִיר: דְּאִי אַקְדְּמֵיהּ לְשָׂעִיר מִקַּמֵּי פַּר — לָא עֲבַד וְלֹא כְּלוּם. אֶלָּא שָׂעִיר אֵין מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַפָּר — מַאי נִיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the challenge from the *baraita*: **Granted,** failure to perform the service of **the bull disqualifies** the service of **the goat** that is advanced ahead of it. This can be understood to mean **that if he advanced** the service **of the goat ahead** of the service **of the bull,** it is considered as though **he has not performed anything at all.** Once the service of the bull has been performed, the service of the goat must be repeated. **However,** the rule in the *baraita* that failure to perform a service of **the goat does not disqualify** the service of **the bull** that was advanced ahead of it. To **what** case **is it** referring?

The *gemara* then reframes the entire question. Rather than asking which animal — *par* or *seira* — disrupts the other when it goes out of sequence, the *gemara* suggests that the real issue is which *avodah* was performed out of order, and whether the particular *avodah* that was displaced belongs to the *par* or the *seira*. Framing it as *par* versus *seira* is somewhat misleading; the *gemara* wants to understand the precise scenario in terms of which specific act was jumped.

### Par Before Hagrala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא דְּאִי אַקְדֵּים מַתָּנוֹת דְּפַר בִּפְנִים מִקַּמֵּי הַגְרָלָה. וּמִדְּסִדְרָא לָא מְעַכְּבָא — (עִיקְרָא) הַגְרָלָה נָמֵי לָא מְעַכְּבָא!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that that the *baraita* means **that if one advanced** the **applications** of the blood **of the bull inside** the Holy of Holies **ahead of** the **drawing** of the lots? If so, **from** the fact that the **sequencing** of drawing the lots **is not indispensable,** one may infer that the **drawing** of the lots **itself is also not indispensable.** This would then refute Rabbi Yannai's opinion.

The *gemara* proceeds to analyze the scenario in which the *par* "goes first." The only meaningful way in which the *par bifnim* — the inside service of the *par* — could jump the line is if the *Kohen Gadol* performed the blood sprinklings of the *par* inside the *Kodesh Kodashim* before having performed the *hagrala* at all. That is: the *hagrala* should have been done prior to the *shechitah* of the *par*, but the *Kohen Gadol* went ahead and completed all the services of the *par* — including the *matanot bifnim* — without yet having drawn the lots. The *braitah* says that in this scenario, the *seira* does not prevent the *par* from being valid — meaning *bedi'avad* the *par* is still good. 

The *gemara* draws an inference: if doing all the inside services of the *par* while skipping the *hagrala* entirely still renders the *par* valid, this constitutes evidence that the *hagralah* is not *me'akev*. Because the only reason this out-of-sequence *par* service should be permissible is that the *hagralah* it bypassed was not indispensable to begin with.

The logic depends on understanding what was "out of order." If the *par* service skipped over the *hagrala*, and we say *bedi'avad* this is acceptable, we are implicitly saying that the fact that the *hagrala* had not yet been performed did not invalidate the *par* service. And if the *hagrala* is what was skipped, then its non-*me'akev* status must be the operative reason.

### Order vs. Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּאַקְדֵּים מַתָּנוֹת דְּפַר בְּמִזְבֵּחַ מִקַּמֵּי מַתָּנוֹת דְּשָׂעִיר בַּהֵיכָל, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: דְּבָרִים הַנַּעֲשִׂין בְּבִגְדֵי לָבָן בַּחוּץ — לָא מְעַכְּבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions whether this is the only possible interpretation of the *baraita*: **No,** the *baraita* could be referring to a case in which **one advanced** the **applications** of the blood **of the bull upon the altar ahead of the applications** of the blood **of the goat in the Sanctuary** made toward the curtain. **And** as such, ruling in the *baraita* that this is valid after the fact **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said: Matters that are performed in** the **white garments outside** of the Holy of Holies **are not indispensable.** Therefore, the applications of the bull's blood upon the altar cannot disqualify a different service.

The *gemara* immediately raises a counter. Perhaps this proves only that the *order* of the *hagrala* is not *me'akev* — that is, it can be performed later — but not that the *hagrala* itself is dispensable. There is a meaningful distinction between saying "the order doesn't matter as long as you do the thing" and "the thing itself can be omitted." The *gemara* thus preserves the possibility of holding: *bedi'avad* the *seder* is not *me'akev*, but the *hagrala* itself remains *me'akev*.

The *gemara* then considers another scenario: perhaps the *par* "going first" refers not to the *matanot bifnim* jumping ahead of the *hagrala*, but to the sprinklings of the *par* on the *parochet* — which are *beheichal*, outside the *Kodesh Kodashim* — jumping ahead of the *seira's* sprinklings *bifnim*. This would explain why it is not *me'akev*: these sprinklings are outside the *Kodesh Kodashim*, and according to Rabbi Yehuda, the order outside is not *me'akev*. The *gemara* cites this: *Rabbi Yehudah hi de'omar devarim hana'asim bevigdei lavan mibachutz lo me'akvei*.

But the *gemara* pushes back: the *braitah* explicitly states *matanot shebifnim* — it is specifically talking about services inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. This eliminates the option of attributing the leniency to the location being outside. The *gemara* therefore returns to the conclusion that the scenario involves the *par bifnim* having been done before the *hagrala*, and attributes the ruling to either Rabbi Shimon — who broadly holds the entire lot-process is not *me'akev* — or to Rabbi Yehuda, with the qualification that even Rabbi Yehuda can distinguish between order not being *me'akev* and the act itself being *me'akev*: *d'nehi d'sidra lo me'akev, hagralah miha me'akva*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בְּמַתָּנוֹת שֶׁבִּפְנִים קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this as a possible interpretation of the *baraita*: **But didn't** the conclusion of the *baraita* **teach** that the rule **applies to** the **applications** of blood performed **inside** the Holy of Holies? If so, the *baraita* cannot be referring to application of blood made upon the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: הַגְרָלָה לָא מְעַכְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* must understood in the way suggested by the Gemara previously, which implies that the drawing of the lots is not indispensable. However, this is not necessarily a refutation of the opinion of Rabbi Yannai. One could say: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said:** The **drawing** of the lots **is not indispensable.** Rabbi Yannai claimed only that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya agreed that it is indispensable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וּנְהִי דִּכְסִדְרָא לָא מְעַכְּבָא — הַגְרָלָה מִיהָא מְעַכְּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say: Actually,** the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** Nevertheless, Rabbi Yannai's opinion may still be defended: **Granted,** the *baraita* teaches **that** the **sequencing** of the drawing of the lots **is not indispensable;** however it may still be true that the **drawing** itself is **nevertheless indispensable.** This contradicts what was suggested previously, that one may infer from the indispensability of the sequencing to the indispensability of the drawing itself.

## Shimon's Philosophy

### Scapegoat Alive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יׇעֳמַד חַי לִפְנֵי ה׳ לְכַפֵּר עָלָיו״, עַד מָתַי יִהְיֶה זָקוּק לַעֲמוֹד חַי? עַד שְׁעַת מַתַּן דָּמוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַד שְׁעַת וִידּוּי דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "But the goat on which the lot came up for Azazel **shall be stood alive before the Lord, to make atonement over him,** to send him away for Azazel into the wilderness" (Leviticus 16:10). The verse indicates that the goat must remain alive until a certain time. Were it to die beforehand, it would have to be replaced. **Until when** is the goat for Azazel **required to stand alive? Until the time of the application of the blood of its counterpart,** i.e., the goat for God; **this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says:** It must remain alive **until the time of the verbal confession** over it.

The *gemara* now examines a *braitah* that illuminates the theological foundations of Rabbi Shimon's radical position. The *braitah* is grounded in the *pasuk* concerning the *se'ir hamishta'leach*: *ya'amad chai lifnei Hashem lechaper alav l'shalach oto la'Azazel ha'midbarah* — the goat designated for *Azazel* shall stand alive before God to atone upon it, to send it off to *Azazel* in the wilderness. The *braitah* asks: until when must this goat remain alive? What is the last moment at which, if the goat dies, one would not be *yotzei*?

### Lekapaer Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? כִּדְתַנְיָא: ״לְכַפֵּר״ — בְּכַפָּרַת דָּמִים הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְכִלָּה מִכַּפֵּר אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ״. מָה לְהַלָּן — בְּכַפָּרַת דָּמִים, אַף כָּאן — בְּכַפָּרַת דָּמִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what do they disagree? As it was taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states that the goat should remain alive **"to make atonement"** (Leviticus 16:10). This indicates that it must remain alive until it effects atonement. Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon dispute which atonement the verse is referring to. **The verse speaks of atonement through** the application of the **blood** of the sin-offering goat. **And similarly it states: "When he has made an end of atoning for the Sanctuary,** and the Tent of Meeting, and the altar, he shall present the live goat" (Leviticus 16:20). **Just as there** the reference is **to atonement through blood, so too here** the verse is referring to **atonement through blood. This is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *braitah* presents a dispute. Rabbi Yehuda says: *ad she'as matan damo shel chaveiro* — the *se'ir la'Azazel* must remain alive until the moment the blood of its counterpart, the *se'ir la'Hashem*, is offered. Once the blood of the *se'ir la'Hashem* has been presented inside, if the *se'ir la'Azazel* then dies, one is still *yotzei*. Rabbi Shimon says: *ad she'as viduy devarim* — the *se'ir la'Azazel* must remain alive only until the moment the *viduy* — the confession spoken over it — has been recited. Once that *viduy* is complete, even if the goat dies before being sent over the cliff, one is *yotzei*.

### When Purpose Done?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״לְכַפֵּר עָלָיו״, בְּכַפָּרַת דְּבָרִים הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: "To make atonement over it"; the verse speaks of atonement through speech,** i.e., the verbal confession that is recited over it.

Strikingly, both positions agree that the *se'ir la'Azazel* does not have to survive until the moment it is actually pushed off the cliff. Its function can be complete before that physical act. The disagreement is only about when that function is fulfilled. This is a remarkable shared premise: the dramatic act of sending the goat into the wilderness is not the operative moment of *kaparah*.

The order of events around the *se'ir la'Azazel* should be borne in mind. After the lottery, the *se'ir la'Hashem* is slaughtered and its blood is brought inside the *Kodesh Kodashim*. Then the *Kohen Gadol* recites the *viduy* over the *se'ir la'Azazel* — this *viduy* includes a confession on behalf of all of Israel. Only after that is the goat sent off to *Azazel*. The gap between the *viduy* and the sending is potentially several minutes. If the goat dies in that interval, the question becomes: is this after or before the critical threshold?

### Blood vs. Words

The *gemara* explores the textual basis for each position. The word *lechaper alav* in the *pasuk* — "to atone upon it" — is the crux. Rabbi Yehuda reads *lechaper alav* through the lens of *kaparat damim*: in the vocabulary of *Yom Kippur*, *kipper* refers to the blood rite in the sanctuary. The parallel phrase *v'chila mechaper et hakodesh* — "when he has finished atoning for the sanctuary" — uses *kipper* to mean exactly that. *Ma lehalan bechaparat damim, af kan bechaparat damim* — just as in that context *kipper* means the blood rite, so here it means the same: the *se'ir la'Azazel* must remain alive until the *se'ir la'Hashem's* blood has effected its *kaparah* inside.

Rabbi Shimon reads *lechaper alav* differently: *bechaparat devarim hakatuv medaber* — the text is speaking of *kaparah* through words. Rashi explains that the force of Rabbi Shimon's reading lies in the pronoun *alav* — "upon it." The immediately preceding antecedent of that pronoun is the *se'ir la'Azazel* itself. So *lechaper alav* means "to effect *kaparah* upon *it* — upon the *se'ir la'Azazel*." And the *kaparah* that pertains to the *se'ir la'Azazel* is not a blood rite — it has none — but rather the *viduy* spoken over it. Hence, once that *viduy* is complete, the purpose of the animal has been fulfilled.

### Tosafot Linkage

Tosafot offers a deeper reading of this dispute that goes beyond the question of which act constitutes *kaparah*. According to Tosafot, the fundamental question underlying this *braitah* is the degree to which the *se'ir la'Azazel* is linked to — dependent upon — the *se'ir la'Hashem*.

### Linked/Unlinked

According to Rabbi Yehuda, who says the *se'ir la'Azazel* must remain alive until the *damim* of the *se'ir la'Hashem* are completed, the two animals are intrinsically bound together. The validity and completion of the *se'ir la'Azazel* is dependent on what happens to the *se'ir la'Hashem*. This is why the *hagrala* — the lottery that establishes their paired identity and their respective destinies — is *me'akev*: you cannot separate the two animals' fates, and the lot-drawing is the act that joins them.

According to Rabbi Shimon, who says the *se'ir la'Azazel* only requires the *viduy* and is then complete, the two animals are fundamentally independent. The *se'ir la'Azazel* achieves its *kaparah* through what is done directly *to it* — not through what is done to its partner. Once that *viduy* is complete, the fate of the *se'ir la'Azazel* is no longer tied to its counterpart. This is precisely why Rabbi Shimon can hold that if one animal dies, you simply bring a replacement without repeating the *hagrala* — the two were never indivisibly linked to begin with.

### Theology

The theological implications, as Tosafot's reading makes clear, are profound. If the two animals are linked — if personal *kaparah* depends on the blood rite in the sanctuary — then the confession of Israel's sins and the purification of God's sanctuary are bound together as a single cosmic event. Individual atonement cannot occur independently of the cleansing of the *Mikdash*. If, on the other hand, the two are unlinked, then what the *Kohen Gadol* does to purify the *Mikdash* and what he does to achieve personal atonement for the people are two distinct processes, each standing on its own terms. The *Azazel* goat's *kaparah* through the *viduy* is a self-contained act; it does not wait upon the blood rite any more than the blood rite waits upon it.

## Reversing Lots

### Switch Hands?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: עָלָה בִּשְׂמֹאל, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּחֲזוֹר לְיָמִין? אָמַר לָהֶן: אַל תִּתְּנוּ מָקוֹם לַמִּינִין לִרְדּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof that the drawing of lots is not indispensable. A *baraita* teaches: **Rabbi Akiva's students asked him:** If the lot for God **was drawn by** the High Priest's **left** hand, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **he may transfer** the lot **to** his **right** hand? **He said to them: Do not give the heretics an opportunity to dominate.** If it is allowed, they will adduce this as proof of their claim that the *halakhot* are not absolute, and the Sages have the power to change them as they see fit.

A student of Rabbi Akiva poses a question: if the lot for *la'Hashem* was drawn in the left hand rather than the right — *ala b'smol* — is it permissible to transfer it to the right hand? Can the *Kohen Gadol* effectively reverse the mechanical result of the drawing after the fact?

### Tzadokim Concern

Rabbi Akiva's answer is not a categorical legal prohibition but rather a concern of a different kind: *al titnu makom l'tzadukim lirdos* — do not give the *Tzadokim* grounds to rebel. That is, the appearance of manipulating the results of the lottery would undermine confidence in Pharisaic authority over Temple procedure, providing the *Tzadokim* an opening to challenge the legitimacy of the *avodah*. The implication of this answer is striking: were it not for this concern, one presumably *could* make the switch.

### Hagralah vs. Reverse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּאַל תִּתְּנוּ מָקוֹם לַמִּינִין לִרְדּוֹת, הָא לָאו הָכִי מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ, וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ הַגְרָלָה מְעַכְּבָא, וְכֵיוָן דִּקְבַעְתֵּיהּ שְׂמֹאל הֵיכִי מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **The** only **reason** Rabbi Akiva provided to disallow it was **so as not to give heretics an opportunity to dominate,** which implies that if **not for this** reason, it would be permitted to **transfer** the lot to the right hand. How could this be true? **Didn't you say** that **the drawing** of the lots **is indispensable? And,** consequently, **once** the designation of the goat has been defined by the drawing of the **left** hand, **how** then **could we transfer** the lot to the right hand? Perforce, the designation is not created by the actual drawing, and as such it is not indispensable.

The *gemara* seizes on this implication and asks: but if the *hagralah* is *me'akev*, how could one reverse the lot at all? If the identity of the goat is determined by the lottery result and that result is indispensable, then ignoring it and switching the lot to the other hand should be prohibited on substantive grounds — not merely on grounds of appearance.

### Switch Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמְרִי: עָלָה הַגּוֹרָל בִּשְׂמֹאל, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּחֲזִירוּ לוֹ וְלִשְׂעִירוֹ לְיָמִין. אָמַר לָהֶם: אַל תִּתְּנוּ מָקוֹם לַמִּינִין לִרְדּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the *baraita* can be understood in a way in which it does not provide a proof: **Rava said: This is** what Rabbi Akiva's students **are saying:** If **the lot was drawn by** his **left** hand, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **he may transfer it,** the lot, **and its** associated **goat to** his **right** side. **He said to them: Do not give the heretics an opportunity to dominate.** Accordingly, there was never any suggestion of changing the designation of the goats. Therefore, no proof can be brought concerning the indispensability of the drawing of the lots.

Rava resolves this: what the student was asking, and what Rabbi Akiva addressed, was not simply flipping the lot from left to right. Rather, the question was *mah she'yachaziru lo u'l'se'iro l'yamin* — whether one could reverse *both* the lot *and* the goat simultaneously. That is, if the lot for *la'Hashem* ended up in the left hand, one would switch which lot is held in which hand *and simultaneously* exchange the positions of the two goats, so that the goat now on the right is still the one over which the *la'Hashem* lot was drawn. The actual pairing of lot to animal is preserved; only the physical positioning changes. This would not constitute reversing or ignoring the lottery result — the lot's designation follows its goat. Rava thus shows that the question was about external presentation, not about overriding the *hagrala*.

### Integrity

Rabbi Akiva's concern nevertheless stands: even this technically valid maneuver should not be done, because it *looks* like manipulation of the lottery. The integrity of the lottery system — and by extension, the trust that the public places in the Pharisaic management of the *avodah* — demands that no appearance of rigging be tolerated, regardless of whether the action would technically be permissible.

## Placing Lots

### Asher Alav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר: ״אֶת הַשָּׂעִיר אֲשֶׁר עָלָיו״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר יַנִּיחֶנּוּ עָלָיו, — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עָלָה״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָלָה שׁוּב אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof that the drawing of lots is indispensable. A *baraita* teaches (*Sifra*, *Aharei Mot* 2:4): **If** the verse **had stated** only: **"The goat upon which** the lot" (Leviticus 16:9), **I would have said** the intention is that **he** must physically **place** the lot **upon it.** Therefore, **the verse** also **states:** "On which **it came up"** (Leviticus 16:9), indicating that **once** the lot **has come up** from the receptacle, **it is not necessary** to do **more,** i.e., the lot does not have to be physically placed upon the goat.

The *gemara* now brings a *braitah* that introduces a distinction between two formulations: had the Torah written *et hasa'ir asher alav hagoral* — "the goat upon which the lot rests" — one might have thought that the lot must remain physically placed upon the animal throughout. But the Torah actually wrote *asher ala alav hagoral* — "upon which the lot ascended" or "which the lot fell upon" — using the past tense.

### Not Me'akev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְמִצְוָה, מִכְלָל דַּהֲנָחָה מִצְוָה נָמֵי לָא? אֶלָּא לָאו, לְעַכֵּב. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַגְרָלָה מְעַכְּבָא, הַנָּחָה — לָא מְעַכְּבָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **To what** does the *baraita* refer when it says it is not necessary to actually place the lot upon the goat? **If we say** it means that it is no longer necessary to do so even **as a mitzva,** then, **by inference,** one must conclude that **the placement** of the lot upon the goat is **not even a mitzva.** This is problematic. Since it is clearly part of the Yom Kippur service, it must at least be a mitzva. **Rather, is it not** that when the *baraita* teaches that the placing of the lot is not necessary, it means only that the placing of the lot is not **indispensable? And conclude from it** that the **drawing** of lots **is indispensable,** but **the placement** of the lot upon the goat **is not indispensable.**

The *braitah* derives from this: *keivan she'ala, shuv eino tzarich* — once the lot has been drawn and designated the animal, you do not have to keep it placed on the animal. The plain sense is that *hanachah* — the placement of the lot upon the goat's head — is not *me'akev*. Once drawn, you are *yotzei* even without physically placing the lot on the animal.

### Lechatchila?

The *gemara* asks a sharper question: does this mean you do not even have to place the lot *lechatchila* — from the outset, as an initial matter? Could the *braitah* be saying that the placing of the lot is not even *mitzvah* — not even the preferred procedure? The *gemara* considers this unthinkable. Surely there must at minimum be a *mitzvah* to place the lot upon the animal's head.

### Hagralah Yes/Hanachah No

The *gemara* concludes that the *braitah* must mean: *bedi'avad*, after the fact, if you did not place the lot on the animal, you are still *yotzei*. This is a *bedi'avad* ruling, not a negation of the *lechatchila* requirement. The inference follows: *shema mina hagralah me'akevet, hanachah lo me'akevet* — the drawing of the lot is *me'akev*, but the placing of the lot is not. The word *asher ala* — "that was drawn" — is what matters; the subsequent act of pressing the lot onto the animal's head is not essential.

### Rava Moment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״אֲשֶׁר עָלָיו״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר יַנִּיחֶנּוּ עָלָיו עַד שְׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר עָלָה״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָלָה שׁוּב אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava rejects the proof: **Rava said:** This cannot serve as a proof, since **this is what** the *baraita* **is saying: If** the verse **had stated** only: **"The goat upon which** the lot," **I would have said** that the intention is that **he should** physically **place** the lot **upon** the goat, and the lot should remain upon it **until the time of its slaughter.** Therefore, **the verse** also **states:** "On which **it came up,"** indicating that **once** the lot **has come up** from the receptacle and been placed upon the goat for even one moment, **it is not necessary** to do **more,** i.e., the lot does not have to remain on the goat anymore. Understood in this way, it cannot be determined from this *baraita* whether or not the placement is indispensable.

Rava, however, offers a different reading that avoids drawing any inference about the *hagralah*. He says: had the Torah said *asher alav*, I would have thought the lot must remain placed on the animal all the way until *shechitah*. The Torah's use of *asher ala* teaches instead that you need not keep the lot on the animal throughout — it need only be placed momentarily. The *braitah* is not saying placement is entirely dispensable; it is saying that *lechatchila* the placement need only be for a moment. The requirement of placement exists, but it is of brief duration.

### Avoids Hagralah

Rava's reinterpretation is strategically significant. By reading the *braitah* as addressing the duration of placement rather than its presence or absence, he avoids any implication that the drawing itself is or is not *me'akev*. The *braitah* becomes entirely about the mechanics of *hanachah* — how long must the lot rest upon the animal — and says nothing about whether the lot must have been drawn in the first place.

---

## Synthesis

### Reinterpretations

Throughout this *sugya*, multiple *braitot* appeared to indicate that the *hagralah* — the drawing of the lots — is not *me'akev*. In each case, the *gemara* reinterpreted them as referring not to the drawing but to the *hanachah* — the placing of the lot upon the animal's head — which is indeed not *me'akev bedi'avad*. The drawing itself remains, under this reinterpretation, an indispensable act.

### Shimon Radical

The one clear exception is Rabbi Shimon, whose position is explicit and cannot be reframed: he holds not only that the drawing of the lots is not *me'akev*, but that the entire paired process — the starting of the two animals together, the drawing, the designation — is dispensable *bedi'avad*. If one goat dies after the lot has been drawn, a replacement can be brought and sent as the *se'ir la'Azazel* without any renewed *hagrala*. This is a radical position that the *gemara* brackets when analyzing the mainstream view.

### Viduy Status

Similarly, there is a position that the *viduy* — the confession — is not *me'akev*, which is equally striking from the perspective of those for whom personal confession is the emotional and spiritual center of *Yom Kippur*. This position challenges us to recognize that the Torah's framework for *Yom Kippur* centers on the blood rites and the purification of the *Mikdash*, not on the confessional and penitential process that later tradition has come to emphasize.

### Cosmic Link

Tosafot's insight, as we explored, is that the debate is not merely about which acts are technically essential. It reaches to a deeper theological question: are the personal atonement for Israel — symbolized by the *se'ir la'Azazel* and its *viduy* — and the cosmic purification of the *Mikdash* by blood inextricably linked, or are they independent processes? Rabbi Yehuda links them, and in doing so makes the *hagrala* — the act that binds the two animals into a unified ritual — essential. Rabbi Shimon unlinks them, and in doing so frees the personal atonement from dependence on the sanctuary rite. Whether that link exists is not merely a technical *halacha* question; it is a question about the relationship between individual human confession and the metaphysical renewal of God's dwelling place.
