# Bava Metzia 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomer Shemasar

### Rav vs. R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הִלְכְתָא: שׁוֹמֵר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר – חַיָּיב, לָא מִבַּעְיָא שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם דְּגָרוֹעֵי גָּרְעַהּ לִשְׁמִירָתוֹ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם שֶׁמָּסַר לְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – חַיָּיב, מַאי טַעְמָא? דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ מְהֵימְנַתְּ לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה, הַאיְךְ לָא מְהֵימַן לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: The *halakha*** is: **A bailee who conveyed** a deposit **to** another **bailee** is **liable** to pay. **It is not necessary** to say that this is the *halakha* if he was **a paid bailee who conveyed** the deposit **to an unpaid bailee, as** in that case the first bailee **diminished** the level of **his safeguarding,** as an unpaid bailee is exempt from paying in instances where a paid bailee is obligated to do so. **But even** if it was initially **an unpaid bailee who conveyed** the deposit for safeguarding **to a paid bailee,** the first bailee is **liable** to pay. **What is the reason** that he is liable in that case? He is liable, **as** the owner of the deposit can **say to him: You are trustworthy to me when** you take **an oath** that the item was stolen or lost. **That** person **is not trustworthy to me when** he takes **an oath.**

Today's daf is 37. We pick up on 36b, approximately ten lines from the top, at the two dots marking a transition in the sugya. The immediately preceding discussion dealt with the question of *shomer shemasar leshomer* — whether the act of transferring a watched object to another *shomer*, even one with greater or lesser liability, itself constitutes an act of *pshia*, negligence. Rav held that it does not; Rabbi Yochanan held that it does — even if a *shomer chinam* transfers to a *shomer sachar*. The first rationale offered by the Gemara is that the original owner may claim, "I never wanted anyone else handling or watching my object, regardless of the level of liability the second *shomer* carries."

### Rava's Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (Continuation from above) דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ מְהֵימְנַתְּ לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה, הַאיְךְ לָא מְהֵימַן לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** (same as above)

In the end, the Gemara rules — in the passage just before the two dots — that Rava holds the transferring *shomer* is *chayav*: the act is *pshia*, negligence. But Rava's reasoning differs from the earlier formulation. In Rava's view, the owner can say, "I never trusted the other person to make an oath to me." As we shall see, this alternative rationale leads to meaningfully different applications and a different scope for when such transfers generate liability.

## T'chilato B'pshiya

### Abaye: Swamp Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: פָּשַׁע בָּהּ וְיָצָאת לַאֲגַם, וּמֵתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ. אַבָּיֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה אָמַר: חַיָּיב, רָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה אָמַר: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute: In the case of **one** who **was negligent** in safeguarding an animal, **and** it **went into a marsh,** where it was susceptible to thieves and predatory animals, **but it died in its** typical **manner** despite this negligence, i.e., it was neither stolen nor devoured, **Abaye says in the name of Rabba:** The bailee is **liable** to pay. **Rava says in the name of Rabba:** The bailee is **exempt** from doing so.

The Gemara now introduces a related topic. The text reads: *pasha bo v'yatzah l'agam, umesah kidarkah* — a *shomer* was negligent, the animal went out into the swamp, and it died of a natural death. On the face of it, the question is whether any causal relationship, even a speculative one, exists between the initial act of negligence and the ultimate loss. How does that initial *pshia* bear on the *shomer*'s liability?

Abaye, citing Rabbah, says *chayav*: you must pay, whether you are a *shomer chinam* or a *shomer sachar*, because of that initial act of negligence in leaving the door open. Rava, also citing Rabbah, says *patur*: you are exempt. Both report in the name of the same teacher — Rabbah — and they are disputing what Rabbah actually held.

Abaye's position is stated with striking rhetorical force. He says: *lo mibaya l'man d'amar techilato bipshia vesofo b'ones chayav* — it goes without saying that one who holds that when negligence precedes an unforeseen accident the *shomer* is liable would rule *chayav* here, since this is precisely that structure. But even one who normally rules *patur* in a case of *techilato bipshia vesofo b'ones* would agree that here the *shomer* is *chayav*. Why? *De'amrinan havla d'agma katla* — we say that perhaps the atmosphere of the swamp contributed to the animal's death. The negligence and the death are not cleanly separated; arguably they are all one act of *pshia*.

This raises an important conceptual question about what *techilato bipshia vesofo b'ones* actually means. Does it mean that the very act of negligence immediately creates monetary liability — so that even if the animal later dies of cancer, which clearly has nothing to do with the *pshia*, the *shomer* is nevertheless liable? That is generally not how the principle is understood. Rather, *techilato bipshia vesofo b'ones* operates by shifting the burden of proof: because the *shomer* committed a fundamental violation of his duty of care, there is a presumption of liability for whatever subsequently occurs, as long as some argument can be made connecting the later loss to the initial negligence. Where the loss is completely unconnected — the animal dying of cancer after the door was left open — even the stringent position would concede *patur*.

### Rava: Angel of Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה אָמַר: פָּטוּר, כֹּל דַּיָּינָא דְּלָא דָּאֵין כִּי הַאי דִּינָא לָאו דַּיָּינָא הוּא. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תְּחִילָּתוֹ בִּפְשִׁיעָה וְסוֹפוֹ בְּאוֹנֶס פָּטוּר, דְּפָטוּר, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַיָּיב, הָכָא פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמְרִינַן: מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת מָה לִי הָכָא וּמָה לִי הָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says in the name of Rabba:** He is **exempt,** and **any judge who does not rule in accordance with this *halakha* is not a judge. It is not necessary** to say that the bailee is exempt in this case, **according to the one who says:** In a case where the incident was **initially through negligence and ultimately by accident,** one is **exempt** from payment. According to this opinion, it is obvious **that** the bailee is **exempt. But even according to the one who says:** In a case where the incident was initially through negligence and ultimately by accident, one is **liable** to pay, **here** the bailee is still **exempt** from payment. **What is the reason** that he is exempt? It is **because we say** with regard to **the Angel of Death,** who causes death by natural causes: **What** difference is there **to me** if the animal was **here, and what** difference is there **to me** if the animal was **there?** The cause of the animal's death was natural, and there is no relevance given to the location of the death. Consequently, the bailee is exempt.

Rava, equally emphatic, rules *patur*, and likewise declares that any judge who fails to judge accordingly is unworthy of the title. *Lo mibaya l'man d'amar techilato bipshia vesofo b'ones patur* — it goes without saying that one who normally exempts in such cases would exempt here. But even one who holds *chayav* in the standard *techilato bipshia* case must concede *patur* here. Why? *Malach hamaves mah li hacha mah li hasam* — the angel of death will find the animal wherever it is. The death is entirely unrelated to the animal's location; it would have died anywhere. Therefore, despite the initial *pshia*, there is no liability for this death.

### Gray Zone Analysis

The debate between Abaye and Rava can be mapped onto a conceptual spectrum. At one end: I leave the door open and the animal runs away — the loss is immediately and obviously caused by the *pshia*; everyone agrees the *shomer* is *chayav*. At the other end: I leave the door open and the animal dies of cancer — plainly unrelated; everyone agrees *patur*. The entire controversy of *techilato bipshia vesofo b'ones* concerns the gray zone between these poles: cases where the subsequent loss may or may not be connected to the initial negligence.

Abaye's position is that the swamp case falls entirely outside the gray zone, on the side of clear causal connection. The death occurred in a place the animal reached because of the *shomer*'s negligence, and we attribute the death to the conditions of that place. Rava's position is that the swamp case falls entirely outside the gray zone on the opposite side — a natural death is simply a natural death, unrelated to location, and the angel of death finds the animal regardless. Both agree that the genuinely contested cases — those where the connection to the initial *pshia* is uncertain — are what the debate about *techilato bipshia vesofo b'ones* is really about. The disagreement is over how to classify the swamp scenario.

One might observe that this dispute seems to turn on an assessment of fact rather than a disagreement about legal doctrine. The extreme language used by both Abaye and Rava — "any judge who does not rule this way is unworthy" — is typically reserved for clear legal conclusions, not factual judgments. And yet it does seem that the two Amoraim are really arguing about how to characterize the factual circumstances, not about the doctrinal parameters of the rule. It is notable that the Gemara frames the debate in such terms, and it is worth acknowledging that a great deal of what is contested in law — in the Talmud no less than in contemporary jurisprudence — comes down to precisely this kind of assessment.

To illustrate the spectrum further: suppose the animal runs away because the door was left open, and is subsequently struck by lightning. In hindsight, there is a clear causal chain — had it remained in the house, it would not have been struck. But was this outcome something that could have been anticipated from the initial act of *pshia*? Arguably not. That case may still fall in the gray zone, even though post-hoc causation is demonstrable, because the *pshia* cannot be said to have directly embedded this outcome. Rava's position is that a natural death — *mesah kedarkah* — is like the cancer case, entirely disconnected from location. It is categorically not a meteor strike or a lightning bolt; it is simply what happens to animals in the ordinary course of nature.

### Animal Returns Home

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֵי אַבָּיֵי, דְּאִי הֲדַרָא לְבֵי מָרַהּ וּמִתָה – דְּפָטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָא הֲדַרָא לַהּ וְלֵיכָּא לְמֵימַר הַבְלָא דְּאַגְמָא קַטְלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye concedes that if** the animal **returned** from the marsh **to its owner's house and died** there **that** the bailee is **exempt. What is the reason** that he is exempt? He is exempt due to the fact **that** the animal **returned, and** since it was able to return **there is no** justification **to say** that **the air of the marsh killed it.**

Abaye concedes a partial case to Rava's logic. *Umodeh Abaye d'hadrah l'bei marei umesah d'patur* — Abaye agrees that if the animal went to the swamp and then returned home and died there, the *shomer* is exempt. The reason: *leika l'meimar havla d'agma katlah* — one can no longer attribute the death to the atmosphere of the swamp. This is a striking formal rule. A scientist might plausibly argue that the animal inhaled swamp air that contributed to its death even after it returned home. But the Gemara does not entertain that argument. Once the animal is back home, the formal presumption shifts: we attribute the death to natural causes rather than to the conditions of the swamp. This suggests that there are somewhat formalistic parameters operating here. If the death occurs in the swamp, we attribute it to the swamp; if the death occurs at home, we do not — regardless of what a medical examination might reveal. The rule does not rest on a purely objective scientific assessment.

### Theft After Release

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֵי רָבָא כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִי גַּנְבַהּ גַּנָּב בַּאֲגַם וּמֵתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ בֵּי גַנָּב – דְּחַיָּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאִי שַׁבְקַהּ מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת בְּבֵיתֵיהּ דְּגַנָּבָא הֲוָה קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava concedes that anytime** the animal **was stolen** from the marsh **and** then **dies in its** typical **manner in the house of the thief that** the bailee is **liable** to pay. **What is the reason** that he is liable to pay? He is liable **because** even **if the Angel of Death spared** the life of the animal, **it would be standing in the house of the thief** due to the negligence of the bailee.

Rava in turn concedes a partial case. *Umodeh Rava kol heicha d'ignavah ganav b'agam umesah kedarka bei ganav d'chayav* — Rava agrees that if a thief stole the animal from the swamp and it then died a natural death in the thief's possession, the *shomer* is *chayav*. Why doesn't Rava apply his own reasoning here — that the angel of death would find the animal anywhere, so the death is unrelated to the location?

The Gemara's answer is revealing: *d'ei shavkah malach hamaves, b'vei ganav havei ka'ima* — if the angel of death had passed over the animal, it would still have been in the thief's possession. The *shomer* became liable from the moment the theft occurred. The fact of theft — which is directly connected to the initial *pshia* of leaving the animal unprotected — created the monetary obligation then and there. Even if the animal subsequently died of completely natural causes, that does not dissolve the liability that crystallized at the moment of theft. This clarifies an important conceptual point about when the *shomer*'s obligation to the owner materializes: not at the moment of negligence, and not at the moment of natural death, but at the moment of a concrete loss that is causally traceable to the *pshia*.

## Shomer Debate

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרָבָא: לְדִידָךְ, דְּאָמְרַתְּ מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת מָה לִי הָכָא וּמָה לִי הָתָם, הַאי דְּאוֹתְבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל לְרַבִּי אַמֵּי וְשַׁנִּי לֵיהּ בְּשֶׁנָּתְנוּ לוֹ בְּעָלִים רְשׁוּת לְהַשְׁאִיל, וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת מָה לִי הָכָא וּמָה לִי הָתָם!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rava: According to you, who said** with regard to **the Angel of Death: What** difference is there **to me** if the animal was **here and what** difference is there **to me** if the animal was **there?** How will you explain the exchange between Rabbi Abba bar Memel and Rabbi Ami? As there is **that objection that was raised by Rabbi Abba bar Memel to Rabbi Ami** from the mishna with regard to one who rents a cow from another, and then lends it to another person, **and** Rabbi Ami **answered him:** It is a case **where the owner gave** the renter **permission to lend** the deposit. Abaye states his challenge: **But** according to your explanation, **let** Rabbi Ami **say to him** that the bailee is exempt because with regard to **the Angel of Death, what** difference is there **to me** if the animal was **here, and what** difference is there **to me** if the animal was **there.** If the death would have happened regardless of the location of the animal, it makes no difference whether it was in the possession of the first renter or in the possession of the one he lent it to.

The Gemara now returns to the discussion of *shomer shemasar leshomer*. Abaye poses a challenge to Rava. Recall from the previous discussion: the Mishnah presented a case in which a *shokher* — someone who rents an animal — lends it out to a *sho'el*, and the animal dies in the *sho'el*'s possession. The *sho'el* pays the *shokher* the full value, and the *shokher* takes an oath to the *ba'al habayit* — the original owner — and retains the payment, which seemingly means the *shokher* profits from the transaction. Rabbi Yossi objected to this arrangement on moral grounds, but the Gemara also needed to address a threshold question: isn't the *shokher* lending the animal to the *sho'el* itself an act of *shomer shemasar leshomer*, making the *shokher* liable to the original owner? The answer given was that the owner had granted permission to lend it out.

Abaye now asks: according to Rava, who holds that *malach hamaves mah li hacha mah li hasam* — natural death occurs regardless of where the animal is — why was that answer necessary? Rava should have been able to answer that even if the *shokher* lent out the animal without the owner's consent, even if that constituted an act of *pshia*, the *shokher* is nonetheless not liable to the *ba'al habayit* for a natural death, because a natural death is unconnected to the *shomer*'s location. Why invoke permission at all?

### Rava's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְדִידְכוּ דְּמַתְנִיתוּ ״אֵין רְצוֹנִי שֶׁיְּהֵא פִּקְדוֹנִי בְּיַד אַחֵר״ – אִיכָּא לְאוֹתֹבַהּ לְהַהִיא, לְדִידִי דְּאָמֵינָא: אַנְתְּ מְהֵימְנַתְּ לִי בִּשְׁבוּעָה וְהַאיְךְ לָא מְהֵימַן לִי – בִּשְׁבוּעָה לֵיכָּא לְאוֹתֹבַהּ כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Abaye: **According to you, that you teach** that a bailee who conveyed a deposit to another bailee is liable to pay because the owner can claim: **It is not my desire that my deposit be in the possession of another** bailee, **there is** room **to raise that objection.** But **according to me, as I say** that a bailee who conveyed a deposit to another bailee is liable to pay because the owner can claim: **You are trustworthy to me when** you take **an oath** that the item was stolen or lost; **that** person **is not trustworthy to me when** he takes **an oath, there is no** room **to raise that objection at all.**

Rava's answer is illuminating. *Ledidi de'amina at meheimant li bishvu'ah haich lo meheiman li bishvu'ah* — according to my reasoning, the problem with *shomer shemasar leshomer* is not that handing over the object to another person is itself an act of negligence. Rather, the problem is that the original owner never trusted the second person to take an oath. According to Rava, a *shomer* who transfers the object to a second *shomer* is liable because the owner can say, "I never authorized that person to be in the position of swearing to me."

But in the Mishnah's case — where the *shokher* transfers the animal to a *sho'el* and the *sho'el* pays and the *shokher* himself takes the oath to the *ba'al habayit* — there is no problem of a second person's oath at all. The original *shomer*, the *shokher*, is the one who swears to the *ba'al habayit*. *Leika l'okma klal* — the scenario presents no question to Rava's framework in the first place, because no issue of oath-substitution arises. The Mishnah's case, under Rava's approach, was never a *shomer shemasar leshomer* problem at all.

### Negligence vs. Oath

This exchange clarifies a critical *nafka mina* between the two explanations of *shomer shemasar leshomer*. If the reason the transferring *shomer* is liable is that the very act of handing over the object to an unauthorized party is negligence — the position rooted in "I never wanted my object in anyone else's hands" — then that negligence triggers the full *techilato bipshia* analysis, and every subsequent event must be evaluated in that framework. Under this view, the *shokher*-to-*sho'el* transfer is a case of negligence, and Rabbi Yossi's challenge requires the answer that the owner gave permission.

If, however, the reason is Rava's — that the owner's right to refuse the second person's oath is what generates liability — then the analysis is entirely different. The *shokher* is not liable under *shomer shemasar leshomer* at all in the Mishnah's scenario, because he is the one personally swearing to the *ba'al habayit*. The *ukimta* of "the owner gave permission" was never needed from Rava's perspective, because the question simply did not arise.

## Clifftop Negligence

### Baraita: Cliff Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיב רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הֶעֱלָהּ לְרָאשֵׁי צוּקִין וְנָפְלָה – אֵין זֶה אוֹנֶס, וְחַיָּיב. הָא מֵתָה כְּדַרְכָּהּ – הֲרֵי זֶה אוֹנֶס וּפָטוּר. וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא לֵיהּ: אַוֵּירָא דְהַר קַטְלַהּ, אִי נָמֵי אוּבְצָנָא דְהַר קַטְלַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raises an objection** to the opinion of Abaye from a mishna (93b): If **one brought** the animal **to the edge of a cliff and it fell, that is not** considered **an accident, and** he is **liable** to pay. One may infer **that** if he brought it to the edge of the cliff and **it died in its** typical **manner,** that is considered **an accident and** he is **exempt. But why? Let** the owner of the animal **say** to the bailee: It is **the air of the mountain** that **killed it, or: The exhaustion** from climbing **the mountain killed it.**

The Gemara now addresses a *baraita* that seems to challenge Abaye's position on the swamp case. *He'ela lerashei tzukin venafela ein zeh ones, vechayav* — if the *shomer* brought the animal to a clifftop and it fell off, that is not an *ones*; the *shomer* is liable. The clear implication of the *baraita* is that if the animal died naturally at the clifftop — *umesah kedarkah* — that would be an *ones* and the *shomer* would be *patur*.

The question arises for Abaye: if you are willing to say that dying in the swamp should be attributed to the conditions of the swamp, why would you not say — *leima lei avira deharka katlah*, or *uchtza deharka katlah* — that the thinner air at the clifftop, or the physical exertion of the climb, contributed to the natural death? By Abaye's own logic, the death in a different location should be attributed to that location's conditions.

### Good Grazing Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? שֶׁהֶעֱלָהּ לְמִרְעֶה שָׁמֵן וְטוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **With what are we dealing here?** It is with a case **where** the bailee **took** the animal **to a bountiful and high-quality pasture.**

The Gemara's answer is that the *baraita* is dealing with a case of *she'he'ela lemira shemen vatov* — the *shomer* brought the animal to the clifftop because that was where the good grazing was. In that case there is no initial *pshia* at all. Taking animals to elevated terrain where the grazing is rich is within the acceptable norms of shepherding practice. Even though it somewhat increases the risk of the animal falling, that increased risk falls within the tolerable range of ordinary care. Without an initial *pshia*, Abaye's presumption that the location contributed to the natural death does not engage.

### Control Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נָפְלָה נָמֵי! שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לְתוֹקְפָּהּ וְלֹא תְּקָפָהּ. אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא רֵישָׁא: עָלְתָה לְרָאשֵׁי צוּקִין וְנָפְלָה – הֲרֵי זֶה אוֹנֶס, אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִיתְקְפַהּ! לָא צְרִיכָא שֶׁתְּקָפַתּוּ וְעָלְתָה, תְּקָפַתּוּ וְיָרְדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then the bailee should be exempt **even** if the animal **fell.** The Gemara answers: He is liable to pay **because he should have subdued** the animal to prevent it from falling, **and he did not subdue it. The Gemara asks: If so, say the first clause** of the mishna: If the animal **climbed to the top of a cliff and fell, it is a circumstance beyond his control** and he is exempt. Shouldn't he be liable, since **he was required to subdue it** and prevent it from falling? The Gemara answers: **No, it is necessary** for the *tanna* to say that the bailee is exempt only in a case **where** the animal **overpowered him and ascended** and the animal **overpowered him and descended.** Although he attempted to prevent the animal from falling, it overpowered the bailee and fell.

The Gemara then asks: *ei hachi, nafela nami* — if there is no initial *pshia* in bringing the animal there, then why is the *shomer* liable when the animal falls off the cliff? If the entire basis for *techilato bipshia* analysis is removed, what grounds the liability for the fall?

The Gemara answers: *shehaya lo letokfa velo tokfa* — because the *shomer* could have grabbed the animal before it fell and failed to do so. The negligence is not in the act of bringing the animal to the clifftop but in failing to prevent the fall. The Gemara then addresses the beginning of the *baraita*, which states that if the animal climbed to the clifftop on its own and fell, that is an *ones* and the *shomer* is exempt. If the operative negligence is failure to grab it, why is the *shomer* not liable in that case too?

The Gemara distinguishes: *lo tzricha shetakfata ve'alta takfata veyerada* — the first case describes an animal that physically broke free from the *shomer*'s grip in climbing up; the same uncontrollable animal then fell off in the same manner. Since the animal escaped the *shomer*'s control on the way up, its fall is likewise an *ones*. In the second case, the *shomer* was leading and controlling the animal up the cliff, and therefore the failure to prevent the fall is attributable to him. This distinction may not be the *peshat* of the *baraita* — the plain reading suggests the issue is simply the initial negligence, not the failure to catch — but this is how the Gemara resolves the apparent difficulty.

The *baraita* thus emerges as supporting Rava: it is only when the outcome clearly results from the initial act of negligence that liability follows. Attributing a natural death merely to a different location — without further grounds — is not sufficient, and the case of dying in the swamp is, according to Rava, precisely that kind of unconnected natural event.

## Rabbi Yosi's Ethics

### Kefel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כֵּיצַד הַלָּה עוֹשֶׂה סְחוֹרָה בְּפָרָתוֹ שֶׁל חָבֵרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said: How does the other** party **do business with** and profit from another's **cow?**

The Gemara now returns to the *mishnah*. Rabbi Yossi asks: *keitzad hala oseh sechora beparato shel chaveiro* — how can this person be doing business with his friend's cow? The case he is addressing is the scenario earlier in our *perek* where a *shomer* agrees to pay the owner for an object that was stolen, and then the thief is found, entitling the *shomer* to collect the *kefel* — the double payment imposed on thieves. Rabbi Yossi is morally offended: the *shomer* is profiting from a transaction involving someone else's property.

One could understand Rabbi Yossi as making a technical legal argument — that the *sho'el*'s liability fundamentally runs to the original owner, not to the *shokher*, since it is ultimately the owner's animal. But that does not appear to be his point. His objection sounds more like a moral and policy principle: we cannot tolerate a legal structure that allows a person to profit from another's property, regardless of the technical justifications.

### Both Cases Disputed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה לְרַב יְהוּדָה: אֲמַרְתְּ לַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: חָלוּק הָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אַף בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ אַף בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda said to Rav Yehuda: You told us in the name of Shmuel** that **Rabbi Yosei was in disagreement even in the first** mishna, **and the *halakha*** is **in accordance with his** opinion **even in the first** mishna.

Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda reports to Rav Yehuda that Shmuel said: *chaluk haya Rabbi Yossi af berishona* — Rabbi Yossi disputed even the first *mishnah* of our *perek*. The first case is precisely the *kefel* case: the *shomer* paid the owner and then collected *kefel* from the thief. Rabbi Yossi's objection there is the same — the *shomer* is profiting from another person's property, and Rabbi Yossi regards this as impermissible. This confirms that his position is not a technical legal argument about to whom the *sho'el*'s obligation runs, but a broad moral principle: *ose sachar beparato shel chaveiro* — deriving profit from another's property — is categorically unacceptable. If that is the principle, it applies equally to the *kefel* scenario and to the *shokher*-*sho'el* scenario of our *mishnah*.

Rav Yehuda confirms: we rule like Rabbi Yossi even in the first case. Rabbi Elazar independently corroborates this ruling.

### Ownership Interest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מוֹדֶה הָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁכְּבָר שִׁילֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Yosei conceded in the first** mishna **because** the bailee **already paid.**

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. In his view, Rabbi Yossi never disputed the first case: *modeh haya Rabbi Yossi berishona* — Rabbi Yossi conceded the first case. Why? *Shekvar shilem* — because the *shomer* already paid the owner. Several considerations operate in the first case that are absent from the second. First, the owner is made whole; he is not disadvantaged. Second, once the *shomer* pays the owner, he can be viewed as having acquired an ownership interest in the object. Third, and perhaps most significantly, there is a broader societal interest at stake: the rule that a *shomer* who pays the owner gets to collect *kefel* creates an incentive for *shomrim* to compensate owners promptly rather than delay, which benefits the entire system. Rabbi Yossi, in Rabbi Yochanan's view, was sensitive enough to these considerations to concede the first case, even as he objected to the second.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא ״שִׁילֵּם״ שִׁילֵּם מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי מְשַׁלֵּם״ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא שִׁילֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** But doesn't **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** himself **says:** When the mishna says: If the bailee **paid,** it does **not** mean that he **actually paid; rather, once** the bailee **said: I hereby** choose to **pay, even if he did not** yet actually **pay,** he acquired the double payment?

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba further refines Rabbi Yochanan's view: the *shomer* need not have literally paid. *Lo shillem shillem mammash* — it is not literal payment that is required. Rather, *kivan she'amar hareni meshallem af al pi she'lo shillem* — once the *shomer* committed to paying, declaring "I will pay," that suffices for Rabbi Yossi to concede the case. The Gemara acknowledges that actual payment is a stronger basis for the concession — it is easier to view the *shomer* as the new owner when he has actually paid — but in order to reconcile Rabbi Yochanan's statements, the rule is read broadly: Rabbi Yossi agrees whenever the owner is assured of receiving his money, whether payment has been made or merely committed to.

### Banking Parallels

The principle of *ose sachar beparato shel chaveiro* has implications that extend well beyond the narrow case of *kefel*. One might observe that the entire contemporary banking system operates on this logic at scale: institutions receive money in bailment, as it were, and invest it for their own profit. The depositor does not have use of the money; the bank does. The critical distinction, as the Gemara's analysis implies, is the nature of the transaction. Borrowing money — a *halva'ah* — is entirely permissible, and the borrower may invest it and profit. The prohibition against profiting from another's property targets a different structure: where the property is not transferred for the recipient's use but is entrusted for safekeeping, and the *shomer* nonetheless extracts profit from it. The legitimacy of the profit depends on whether the legal relationship is one of borrowing or one of guardianship.

## Unknown Recipient

### Proactive Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר לִשְׁנַיִם: גָּזַלְתִּי לְאֶחָד מִכֶּם מָנֶה וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה מִכֶּם. אוֹ: אָבִיו שֶׁל אֶחָד מִכֶּם הִפְקִיד לִי מָנֶה וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה הוּא – נוֹתֵן לָזֶה מָנֶה וְלָזֶה מָנֶה, שֶׁהוֹדָה מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one said to two** people: **I robbed one of you of one hundred dinars, but I do not know** from **which of you** I took the money, **or** if one said to two people: **The father of one of you deposited one hundred dinars with me, but I do not know** the father of **which** of you **he is,** then he **gives one hundred dinars to this** person **and one hundred dinars to that** person. This is **because** there is no way to determine which of them is entitled to the money, and **he admitted** his obligation **at his own** initiative.

The *mishnah* continues with a series of special cases involving *shomrim* and uncertain obligations. The first: *amar lishnayim, gazalti l'echad mikem maneh v'eini yodeia eizeh mikem* — a person says to two others, "I stole one hundred dollars from one of you, and I don't know which." Alternatively: "One of your fathers entrusted me with one hundred dollars and I don't know which one." In both cases, the person is coming forward proactively. The ruling is: *noten lazeh maneh v'lazeh maneh* — he pays each one the full amount.

This is striking. Technically speaking, neither claimant has an actionable claim. *Hamotzi mechavero alav haraaya* — the burden of proof lies with the one seeking to extract money from another's possession, and neither of these two can identify himself as the definite creditor. The person coming forward has no strict legal obligation to pay either of them. Yet the *mishnah* rules that he pays both in full.

### Latzeit Yedei Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַלְמָא מִסְּפֵיקָא מַפְּקִינַן מָמוֹנָא, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן: אוֹקֵי מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: From the fact that the mishna teaches that if the bailee does not know whom he robbed, he gives one hundred dinars to this one and one hundred dinars to that one, **apparently, in** cases of **uncertainty, we expropriate property** and return it to those claiming it. **And we do not say: Establish the money in the possession of its owner.**

The Gemara explains that the *mishnah* itself signals its rationale: *shehoda mipi atzmo* — he admitted of his own accord. The case is not one of legal compulsion but of a person who voluntarily comes forward seeking to do the right thing. The entire framework of the ruling is *latzeit yedei shamayim* — fulfilling one's obligation before Heaven, beyond what the law strictly requires. If one wants to fully discharge the moral obligation, paying each person the full amount is the only way to ensure that the actual victim is made whole. Splitting the sum and giving each person fifty dollars would leave the actual victim uncompensated, which fails the goal of *latzeit yedei shamayim*. Accordingly, full payment to each person is what conscience demands.

### Reactive Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ אֵצֶל אֶחָד, זֶה מָנֶה וְזֶה מָאתַיִם. זֶה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁלִּי מָאתַיִם, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁלִּי מָאתַיִם. נוֹתֵן לָזֶה מָנֶה וְלָזֶה מָנֶה, וְהַשְּׁאָר יְהֵא מוּנָּח עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **two** people **who deposited** money **with one** person, and **this** one deposited **one hundred dinars and that** one deposited **two hundred** dinars, and when they come to collect their deposit, **this** one **says: My** deposit was **two hundred** dinars, **and that** one **says: My** deposit was **two hundred** dinars, the bailee **gives one hundred dinars to this** one **and one hundred dinars to that** one. **And the rest** of the money, i.e., the contested one hundred dinars, **will be placed** in a safe place **until Elijah comes** and prophetically determines the truth.

The *mishnah* then shifts to the second scenario: *shnayim shehifkidu eitzel echad, zeh maneh v'zeh matayim* — two people deposited funds with a single custodian; one deposited one hundred dollars and one deposited two hundred dollars. *Zeh omer sheli matayim v'zeh omer sheli matayim* — when it comes time for return, each claims to be the one who deposited the two hundred. This is the inverse of the previous case: it is they who are coming with claims against the custodian, not the custodian proactively admitting uncertainty.

### Escrow Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (continuation of above) נוֹתֵן לָזֶה מָנֶה וְלָזֶה מָנֶה, וְהַשְּׁאָר יְהֵא מוּנָּח עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** (same as above)

The ruling: *noten lazeh maneh v'lazeh maneh, v'hashar yehei munach ad sheyavo Eliyahu* — give each person the minimum amount they are certainly owed, one hundred dollars each, and the remaining hundred is held in escrow until Eliyahu HaNavi comes — meaning, until further evidence is produced. This ruling reflects the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraaya*: since the custodian is not the one creating the uncertainty, and both claimants are coming with competing demands, the disputed amount remains in the custodian's possession and neither claimant receives it.

The contrast with the previous case is instructive. In the first scenario, the custodian is coming proactively, seeking to fulfill his moral obligation, and the framework is *latzeit yedei shamayim*. In the second, the claimants are pressing him, and the framework is strictly legal: he pays what is certain and holds the rest in escrow.

## Two Vessels

### Vessel Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן שְׁנֵי כֵלִים, אֶחָד יָפֶה מָנֶה, וְאֶחָד יָפֶה אֶלֶף זוּז, זֶה אוֹמֵר: יָפֶה שֶׁלִּי, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: יָפֶה שֶׁלִּי – נוֹתֵן אֶת הַקָּטָן לְאֶחָד מֵהֶן, וּמִתּוֹךְ הַגָּדוֹל נוֹתֵן דְּמֵי קָטָן לַשֵּׁנִי, וְהַשְּׁאָר יְהֵא מוּנָּח עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise,** if two people deposited **two vessels, one worth one hundred dinars and one worth one thousand dinars,** and **this** one **says: The expensive** vessel **is mine, and that** one **says: The expensive** vessel **is mine,** the bailee **gives the small** vessel **to one of them, and from the** proceeds of the sale of **the large** vessel **he gives the value of the small** vessel **to the other, and the rest** of the money **is placed** in a safe place **until Elijah comes.**

A parallel case involving objects rather than money: two people deposit vessels with a custodian. One vessel is worth a hundred dollars; the other is worth a thousand. Each claimant asserts that the more valuable vessel is theirs. The ruling follows the same structure: give one claimant the smaller vessel outright, and from the larger vessel's value give the second claimant the equivalent of the smaller vessel's worth, with the remaining value held in escrow.

### Breaking Vessel

Rashi, in his final comment on the *mishnah*, reads *mitoch hagadol noten d'mei katan l'sheni* in a striking way, apparently based on the subsequent Gemara: *yeshabrenu* — literally, break the larger vessel. One would take the thousand-dollar menorah, break off a portion of it, and hand that fragment to the second claimant. This is, as one might note, a rather extreme measure — reminiscent of the judgment of King Shlomo — and the Gemara will interrogate whether this is indeed the operative ruling.

### Rabbi Yosi Escrow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מָה הִפְסִיד הָרַמַּאי? אֶלָּא, הַכֹּל יְהֵא מוּנָּח עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said: If so, what did the swindler lose? Rather, the entire** deposit, i.e., both vessels, **are placed** in a safe place **until Elijah comes** or one of them admits his deceit.

Here again Rabbi Yossi raises a policy objection: *mah hifsid haramai* — what does the dishonest claimant lose? Under the plain ruling, each person receives at minimum what they are certainly owed, and the disputed portion sits in escrow. The dishonest person who falsely claims the superior item suffers no real consequence: he gets his money back either way, and the disputed remainder, sitting indefinitely in escrow, may eventually be resolved by mutual agreement — perhaps by splitting it. There is no deterrent to dishonesty.

Moreover, the vessel case has a further dimension: if the genuine owner of the thousand-dollar menorah sees it about to be broken or divided, he is highly motivated to capitulate and accept a compromise simply to preserve the object's value. The dishonest claimant, knowing this, faces no risk. Rabbi Yossi therefore rules: *hakol yehei munach ad sheyavo Eliyahu* — everything goes into escrow. The custodian holds all the funds — or both vessels — until the matter is resolved. By putting even the undisputed hundred-dollar amount in escrow, Rabbi Yossi creates a real cost for dishonest claiming: the *ramai* may genuinely need his money and will find it costly to have it tied up indefinitely.

## Gemara Analysis

### Doubt Extraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַלְמָא מִסְּפֵיקָא מַפְּקִינַן מָמוֹנָא, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן: אוֹקֵי מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: From the fact that the mishna teaches that if the bailee does not know whom he robbed, he gives one hundred dinars to this one and one hundred dinars to that one, **apparently, in** cases of **uncertainty, we expropriate property** and return it to those claiming it. **And we do not say: Establish the money in the possession of its owner.**

The Gemara now addresses the theoretical underpinnings of the *mishnah*'s rulings. The first difficulty: why does the *mishnah*'s proactive admission case require paying each person in full? Normally, *mesafeka lo mafkinan mamona* — we do not extract money from a person's possession based on doubt — and *uki mamona b'chezkat marei* — money remains presumed to belong to whoever holds it until the other side proves otherwise. So why in this case do we override that principle and compel payment to both claimants?

### Contradictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ אֵצֶל אֶחָד, זֶה מָנֶה וְזֶה מָאתַיִם, זֶה אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁלִּי מָאתַיִם״ וְזֶה אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁלִּי מָאתַיִם״ – נוֹתֵן לָזֶה מָנֶה וְלָזֶה מָנֶה, וְהַשְּׁאָר יְהֵא מוּנָּח עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And raise a contradiction** from the continuation of the mishna: In the case of **two** people **who deposited** money **with one** person, and **this** one deposited **one hundred dinars and that** one deposited **two hundred** dinars, and when they come to collect their deposit, **this** one **says: My** deposit was **two hundred** dinars, **and that** one **says: My** deposit was **two hundred** dinars, the bailee **gives one hundred dinars to this** one **and one hundred dinars to that** one. **And the rest** of the money **will be placed** in a safe place **until Elijah comes** and prophetically determines the truth.

More puzzling still, the *mishnah* seems internally contradictory. The first half — both the theft case and the bailment case involving one of your fathers — rules that the uncertain party pays each person in full. The second half — the two depositors each claiming the higher sum — rules that only the minimum certain amounts are distributed and the disputed remainder is held in escrow. But the second half is also a *pikadon* case. Why does one *pikadon* scenario require full payment to both while the other does not?

### Theft Penalty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פִּקָּדוֹן אַגָּזֵל קָא רָמֵית, גָּזֵל דַּעֲבַד אִיסּוּרָא – קַנְסוּהּ רַבָּנַן, פִּקָּדוֹן דְּלָא עֲבַד אִיסּוּרָא – לָא קַנְסוּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said to** the one who raised the contradiction: **Are you raising a contradiction** between the *halakha* stated in the case of **a deposit and** the *halakha* stated in the case of **a robbery?** In the case of **robbery, where one transgressed a prohibition, the Sages penalized him** and ruled that he must pay both possible robbery victims. In the case of **a deposit, where he did not transgress a prohibition, the Sages did not penalize him.**

The Gemara's first proposed answer draws a distinction between *gezel* and *pikadon*. In the first case, the person committed a transgression — theft. *Gezel d'avid issura, kansu rabanan* — because he sinned, the Rabbis imposed a fine: rather than simply holding the disputed sum, he must pay each person in full. In the *pikadon* case — entrusting without wrongdoing — *lo kansu rabanan*, the Rabbis did not impose a fine; the disputed amount stays in escrow.

But the Gemara rejects this answer. The first half of the *mishnah* includes not only a theft case but also a *pikadon* case: "one of your fathers entrusted me with money and I don't know which one." That case also results in full payment to both. So there is a *pikadon* case where full payment is required, and a *pikadon* case where it is not. The theft/bailment distinction does not resolve the contradiction.

### Rava: Negligence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִי פִּקָּדוֹן אַפִּקָּדוֹן, וּרְמִי גָּזֵל אַגָּזֵל. פִּקָּדוֹן אַפִּקְדוֹן, דְּקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: אוֹ אָבִיו שֶׁל אֶחָד מִכֶּם הִפְקִיד אֶצְלִי מָנֶה, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה הוּא נוֹתֵן לָזֶה מָנֶה וְלָזֶה מָנֶה. וּרְמִינְהִי: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ וְכוּ׳! אָמַר רָבָא: רֵישָׁא נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ לוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי כְרִיכוֹת, דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵידַק. סֵיפָא נַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁהִפְקִידוּ לוֹ בְּכֶרֶךְ אֶחָד, דְּלָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵידַק, כְּגוֹן דְּאַפְקִידוּ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי בְּחַד זִימְנָא, דְּאָמַר לְהוּ: אַנְתְּ גּוּפַיְיכוּ לָא קָפְדִיתוּ אַהֲדָדֵי, אֲנָא קָפֵידְנָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And they raised a contradiction** between the *halakha* stated in the case of **a deposit and** the *halakha* stated in the case of **a deposit, and they raised a contradiction** between the *halakha* stated in the case of **a robbery and** the *halakha* stated in the case of **a robbery.** There is a contradiction between the *halakha* stated in the case of **a deposit and** the *halakha* stated in the case of **a deposit,** as **it is taught** in **the first clause** of the mishna: **Or,** if one said to two people: **The father of one of you deposited one hundred dinars with me, but I do not know** the father of **which** of you **he is,** he **gives one hundred dinars to this** person **and one hundred dinars to that** person. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the continuation of the mishna cited above: In the case of **two people who deposited** money with one person, the contested sum is placed in a safe place until Elijah comes. **Rava said:** In **the first clause** of the mishna, in the case where the bailee receives money from the father of one person, he **becomes like one with whom they deposited** sums of money in **two** separate **bundles, as the** bailee **should have been discerning** with regard to who gave him the money. His failure to do so constitutes negligence, and therefore he pays the sum to both claimants. In **the latter clause** of the mishna, in the case where he receives money from two people, he **becomes like one with whom they deposited** sums of money in **one bundle, as** there is **no** expectation **that he should have been discerning.** It is a case **where they both deposited** their money **together at one time, as** the bailee **says to them:** If **you yourselves were not suspicious of each other,** should **I be suspicious?** Therefore, he is required to pay them only the sum that they can prove is theirs.

Rava proposes a different framework. The operative variable is not whether a sin was committed, but whether the custodian was negligent in failing to keep track of to whom he owed the money. When his inability to identify the correct recipient is his own fault, the Rabbis impose liability: he must pay each person in full. When the uncertainty is not traceable to his negligence, the standard rules of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraaya* apply, and the disputed amount stays in escrow.

### Bundle Distinction

Applying this principle requires situating each *mishnah* case. The first case — "one of your fathers gave me money and I do not know which one" — involves a single depositor. There was one transaction, one person. The fact that the custodian cannot identify which of the claimants' fathers deposited the money reflects carelessness on his part. Rava describes this as resembling a case where two separate individuals delivered money on two separate occasions: *reisha na'aseh k'mi shehifkidu lo bishnei krichot* — the *reisha* is treated as if the money arrived in two separate bundles. When money arrives in distinct, sequential transactions, the custodian is obligated to track who gave what; his failure to do so is *pshia*, and he pays in full.

In the second case — two people who each claim the higher deposit — Rava reads the *mishnah* as describing a specific factual scenario: *na'aseh k'mi shehifkidu lo b'krach echad* — they came and deposited together, simultaneously. When both parties were present at the same time and deposited in each other's presence, the custodian's failure to distinguish who gave the larger sum is not negligence. He can reasonably say to them: *attu gufaichu lo kapditu ahadadi* — you yourselves were not suspicious of each other; you were willing to deposit in front of one another. If they trusted each other enough to deposit simultaneously and openly, why should the custodian have been more vigilant than they were?

This *ukimta*, establishing the factual circumstances, is what resolves the apparent internal contradiction of the *mishnah*: full payment flows from negligence, and escrow follows from its absence.

## Five Claimants

### One vs. Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: גָּזַל אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה מֵהֶן גָּזַל, זֶה אוֹמֵר: אוֹתִי גָּזַל, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: אוֹתִי גָּזַל, מַנִּיחַ גְּזֵילָה בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. אַלְמָא מִסְּפֵיקָא לָא מַפְּקִינַן מָמוֹנָא, וְאָמְרִינַן: אוֹקֵים מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Yevamot* 118b): If one **robbed one of five** people **and he does not know which of them** he robbed, and **this** one **says: He robbed me, and that** one **says: He robbed me,** the robber **places** the **stolen item between them and withdraws** from them; this is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon. Apparently,** contrary to the mishna, **we do not expropriate property due to an uncertainty** and return it to those claiming it, **and** instead **we say: Establish the money in the possession of its owner.**

Having established that the proactive admission case requires paying each person in full, the Gemara raises a further contradiction. Our *mishnah* says that when I come forward and say "I stole from one of two people," I must pay each one a hundred dollars. But a *baraita* discusses someone who stole from one of five people — *gezel echad mechamesha* — and all five are actively pressing claims, each asserting "you stole from me." The ruling there: *maniach gezeila beineihem umistalek* — place the stolen amount in the middle and walk away. That is the position of Rabbi Tarfon. If five active claimants are each pressing demands, and the ruling is merely to put the money in the middle and leave, why does the two-person proactive admission case require full payment to both?

### R. Tarfon Position

Rabbi Tarfon's position, taken at face value, represents a significant application of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraaya*: even in a case of admitted theft, where it is certain that one of the five claimants is owed money, the one who did the stealing need not pay each one in full when they are the ones pressing claims against him. He satisfies his obligation by placing the amount in the middle and stepping back.

### R. Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. In his view, that approach does not suffice to extract the person from his *aveirah* — his transgression of theft is not discharged until the actual victim is fully compensated. Rabbi Akiva would require payment to each of the five. The Gemara therefore asks: perhaps our *mishnah*, which requires paying each person in full in the two-person case, simply reflects the position of Rabbi Akiva and not Rabbi Tarfon?

### Proactive vs. Reactive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּמַתְנִיתִין דְּהָכָא רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן הִיא? דְּקָתָנֵי עֲלַהּ דְּהַהִיא מוֹדֶה רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן בְּאוֹמֵר לִשְׁנַיִם גָּזַלְתִּי לְאֶחָד מִכֶּם מָנֶה וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אֵיזֶה מִכֶּם שֶׁנּוֹתֵן לָזֶה מָנֶה וְלָזֶה מָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it known **that the mishna here** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Tarfon?** Perhaps the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who holds the robber must pay each of the five possible victims, and there is no contradiction at all. The Gemara answers: It is known that the mishna here is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Tarfon, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* **concerning** the *halakha* taught in **that** mishna, in tractate *Yevamot*: **Rabbi Tarfon concedes** that **in** a case where a robber **says to two** people: **I robbed one of you** of **one hundred dinars, but I do not know which of you** it was, **he gives one hundred dinars to this** person **and one hundred dinars to that** person, as he has already admitted his obligation on his own.

The Gemara resolves this by citing a *baraita* in which Rabbi Tarfon himself distinguishes between the two scenarios: *modeh Rabbi Tarfon b'omer lishnayim gazalti l'echad mikem maneh v'eini yodeia eizeh mikem shenosein lazeh maneh v'lazeh maneh* — Rabbi Tarfon concedes that when a person comes forward proactively and says "I stole from one of you and I do not know which," he must pay each one the full amount. It is only when the claimants are pressing demands against him — the reactive scenario of five people each asserting a claim — that Rabbi Tarfon allows placing the money in the middle.

This means the determinative variable is who initiates. When the person comes forward on his own, seeking to fulfill his moral obligation — *ba latzeit yedei shamayim* — full payment to each is required. When they come against him and he is defending against their claims, *hamotzi mechavero alav haraaya* governs, and he need not pay in full.

## Gemara Analysis…

### Self-Admission Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם דְּקָא תָבְעִי לֵיהּ, הָכָא בְּבָא לָצֵאת יְדֵי שָׁמַיִם. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי: שֶׁהוֹדָה מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There,** in the mishna where one robbed one of five people of money, it is referring to a case **where** the claimants **demand** payment **from him.** He is required to pay them only one hundred dinars, as the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. By contrast, **here,** i.e., in this mishna and the statement of Rabbi Tarfon in the *baraita*, it is referring to a case **where** the robber **comes to fulfill his obligation to Heaven.** Only by returning the money to the person he robbed can he atone for his transgression. Therefore, he goes beyond the halakhic requirement and pays both claimants. The Gemara notes: The language of this mishna **is also precise, as** the *tanna* **teaches: Because he admitted** his obligation **at his own** initiative. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from** the wording of the mishna that this is the explanation of the mishna.

The Gemara confirms this analysis by returning to the text of our *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* states the reason for full payment: *shehoda mipi atzmo* — because he admitted of his own accord. This phrase, the Gemara notes, is not merely a description of the factual circumstances. It is the stated rationale: the person's coming forward voluntarily, without external compulsion, is what places the case in the framework of *latzeit yedei shamayim* rather than in the framework of legal adjudication under *hamotzi mechavero alav haraaya*. *Shma mina* — this is indeed the operative distinction.

## Five Claimants…

### Silence Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הָתָם דְּקָא תָבְעִי לֵיהּ. וְהַלָּה מָה טוֹעֵן? רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הַלָּה שׁוֹתֵק. רַב מַתְנָה אָמַר רַב: הַלָּה צוֹוֵחַ. מַאן דְּאָמַר הַלָּה צוֹוֵחַ, אֲבָל שְׁתִיקָה – כְּהוֹדָאָה. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר הַלָּה שׁוֹתֵק, שְׁתִיקָה דְּהָכָא לָאו כְּהוֹדָאָה הוּא, מָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי דִּשְׁתִיקִי לְכֹל חַד וְחַד, דְּאָמֵינָא דִּלְמָא הַאי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to returning stolen money, **the Master said: There,** it is referring to a case **where** the claimants **demand** payment **from him.** The Gemara asks: **And the other** person, the thief, **what does he claim** in response? **Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says: The other** person **is silent,** as he does not know to whom he owes the money. **Rav Mattana** says that **Rav says: The other** person **screams** and says to each of the claimants: I do not recognize you and I find no basis for your claim. The Gemara comments: **The one who says** that **the other screams** holds that he need not return the money to each of them if he is screaming that he does not accept their claims. **But** a reaction of **silence is tantamount to admission** that the demands of the claimants are legitimate. **And the one who says** that **the other is silent** holds **that the silence here is not tantamount to admission,** as the robber **could say to him:** The fact **that I was silent to each and every one** of the claimants **is because I said: Perhaps this is he,** the robbery victim, but I do not admit that I owe money to more than one person.

The Gemara now examines the reactive scenario more carefully. When five people each press a claim against me, *v'hala ma to'ein* — what is my response to each of them? Rav Yehuda cites Rav: *halas shotek* — he is silent. Rav Menashya cites Rav differently: *halas tzoveiach* — he protests loudly, denying each claim. The debate concerns the doctrine of *shtika k'hoda'ah* — whether silence constitutes admission.

According to Rav Menashya's reading, the person actively denies each claim: "I did not steal from you, and not from you either." This is the straightforward reactive posture, and Rabbi Tarfon's leniency applies.

According to Rav Yehuda's reading, the person is silent in response to each claim. One might think that silence amounts to admission: if five people each say "you stole from me" and he says nothing, he has in effect admitted to each of them. But the Gemara explains why silence here is not admission. *Shtika dehacha lav k'hoda'ah hi* — in this context, silence is not an admission. The silent person can say: *matzi amar lei hai deshtiki dechol chad v'chad, d'amina dilma hai hu* — my silence when each person came forward with his claim was not an acknowledgment that he was the one; it was because I genuinely did not know. Maybe he was the one who was robbed. I could not say "it was not you" because I could not exclude the possibility. But neither was my silence a positive admission that it was him specifically.

The distinction is precise: silence here constitutes an admission of possibility — I acknowledge I cannot exclude you — but not an admission of fact — I am not confirming that I actually stole from you. *Shtika k'hoda'ah*, as a legal doctrine, operates on actual admissions; it does not convert an expression of uncertainty into a binding acknowledgment of liability.

### Escrow Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מַנִּיחַ גְּזֵילָה בֵּינֵיהֶם וּמִסְתַּלֵּק. וְשָׁקְלִי לַהּ כּוּלְּהוּ וְאָזְלִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אָמַר רַב: כֹּל סְפֵק הִינּוּחַ לְכַתְּחִילָּה לֹא יִטּוֹל, וְאִם נָטַל – לֹא יַחֲזִיר. אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: וְיַנִּיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said:** The robber **places** the **stolen item between them and withdraws** from them. The Gemara challenges: **And all of them take it and go,** possibly resulting in the robbery victim losing his property. **But doesn't Rabbi Abba bar Zavda say** that **Rav says** concerning found items: In **any** case of **uncertainty** whether the **placement** of a found item was deliberate, the finder **may not take** the item ***ab initio*, and if he took** it, **he should not return it** to one who claims ownership without conclusive proof? Here too, the robber should keep the money until one of the claimants provides conclusive proof that the money is his. **Rav Safra says:** The *baraita* means: **And** the robber **should place** it in his possession or before the court.

Finally, the Gemara interrogates the practical implementation of Rabbi Tarfon's ruling. *Maniach gezeila beineihem umistalek* — he puts the stolen amount in the middle and walks away. Does this mean he literally puts the money down and the five claimants fight over it? Evidently not. *V'ha'amar Rav Adda bar Zavda amar Rav, kol safek hinuach l'chatchila lo yitol* — there is an established principle regarding doubtful *aveidah* cases: an object that may have been intentionally left should not be picked up, because if it was, the finder becomes obligated to return it to its owner and cannot discharge that obligation simply by putting it back and walking away. *V'im natal, lo yachzir* — once he has taken it, he cannot simply replace it, because his obligation to the rightful owner is not discharged by abandonment.

The same logic applies here. The person who stole the money is obligated to return it to the rightful owner. He cannot discharge that obligation by placing the money in a pile and departing. The money must be held in escrow — *y'hei munach* — and preserved until the rightful owner can be identified, not simply abandoned for the claimants to divide as they see fit. Rav Safra makes this explicit: *v'yaniach* — "and he should place it" — means he places it in escrow and steps away from active custody, not that he abandons the money to an unresolved dispute. The word *yaniach* in Rabbi Tarfon's ruling echoes the language of *yihei munach ad sheyavo Eliyahu*, the escrow formula of the *mishnah*, and the two are understood in the same vein.
