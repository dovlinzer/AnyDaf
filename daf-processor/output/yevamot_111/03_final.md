# Yevamot 111 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chereshet Me'ikara

### Chereshet Yibbum Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא יָבָם עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַקְּטַנָּה, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַקְּטַנָּה — פָּסַל אֶת הַחֵרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** If **the *yavam* engaged in intercourse with the deaf-mute and then engaged in intercourse with the minor, or if his brother engaged in intercourse with the minor,** then the *yavam* or his brother **disqualified the deaf-mute** from staying married.

We pick up near the bottom of 110b, continuing a discussion about whether a woman who was married *me'ikara* as a *chereshet* — a marriage that is only *derabbanan* — may perform *yibbum*. Normally, a *chereshet* cannot perform *yibbum*, both because she lacks full *bar da'at* status and because she cannot recite the required verses. The question raised was whether the rabbinic character of her marriage changes this. Rava had held that we would permit her to do *yibbum*, given that her marriage was only *derabbanan* from the outset. The *Gemara* attempted to bring evidence against Rava from the *Mishna* at the beginning of the next *perek*, which deals with a *chereshet* in various situations and consistently assumes she cannot perform *yibbum*. When the *Gemara* tried to read those cases as involving women who were originally hearing and only later became deaf, working through the *Mishna* revealed that some of the cases clearly involved women who had been *cherashot* from the beginning. The conclusion reached was that even when she was married *me'ikara* as a *chereshet*, she cannot perform *chalitza* — and that Rava is therefore incorrect. The remaining portion of this discussion re-proves that conclusion from a different section of the same *Mishna*.

### Rava Challenged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא תּוֹתְבֵיהּ מֵהָא, דְּיָכֵול לְשַׁנּוֹיֵי לָךְ: אֲחָיוֹת — חֵרְשׁוֹת מֵעִיקָּרָא, נׇכְרִיּוֹת — פִּקְּחוֹת וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְחָרְשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When** Abaye **came before Rav Yosef** and told him of the matter, Rav Yosef **said to him: What is the reason that you raised an objection to him** based **on this? For he could teach,** i.e., explain **to you,** as follows: **The sisters** the mishna referred to were **deaf-mutes at the outset,** whereas **the unrelated women** it referred to were halakhically **competent women who later became deaf-mutes.**

*Ishtik* Rava — Rava fell silent. The *Gemara* then records that Abaye came before Rav Yosef, who said to him: *Mai ta'ama tistrei meha* — why did you challenge Rava from that earlier section of the *Mishna*? That section, dealing with two sisters who were both *cherashot*, Rava might have conceded is about women who were *cherashot me'ikara*. But the section about women who were *nokhriyot* — unrelated women — *pikchot ve'achar nitcharshu*, perhaps those were cases where the women were originally hearing and only later became deaf. So you have not fully proven your point from the most relevant section. Instead, Rav Yosef says, *ella iba'i lach le'akshuyei meha* — you should have challenged him from this section.

### Shnei Achim Cherashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבְּעִי לָךְ לְאוֹתֹבֵיהּ מֵהָא: שְׁנֵי אַחִין חֵרְשִׁין נְשׂוּאִין שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת פִּקְּחוֹת, אוֹ שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת חֵרְשׁוֹת, אוֹ שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת אַחַת פִּקַּחַת וְאַחַת חֵרֶשֶׁת. וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת חֵרְשׁוֹת נְשׂוּאוֹת לִשְׁנֵי אַחִין פִּקְחִים, אוֹ לִשְׁנֵי אַחִין חֵרְשִׁין, אוֹ לִשְׁנֵי אַחִין אֶחָד פִּקֵּחַ וְאֶחָד חֵרֵשׁ — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרוֹת מִן הַחֲלִיצָה וּמִן הַיִּיבּוּם. וְאִם הָיוּ נׇכְרִיּוֹת — יִכְנוֹסוּ, וְאִם רָצוּ לְהוֹצִיא — יוֹצִיאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, you should have raised an objection to him from this** mishna (112b): In the cases **of two deaf-mute brothers married** to **two** halakhically **competent sisters or** to **two deaf-mute sisters or** to **two sisters, one** halakhically **competent and one a deaf-mute; and likewise, two deaf-mute sisters married to two** halakhically **competent brothers or to two deaf-mute brothers or to two brothers, one halakhically competent and one a deaf-mute,** all **these** women **are exempt from *ḥalitza* and from levirate marriage** in the event of the death of one of the brothers while childless. **And if,** in these cases, the women **were unrelated** to one another, the surviving brothers **should consummate** levirate marriage with them, **and if they wish to divorce** them subsequently, **they may divorce** them.

The *Mishna* states: *shnei achim cherashim nesu'im shtei achayot pikchot* — two deaf brothers married to two hearing sisters. The *Gemara* then works through the various combinations. The key structural point is this: either both brothers are *cherashim* or both sisters are *cherashot*, and thus in every permutation, both marriages will be *kiddushin derabbanan*. Whatever the status of the other gender in any given case, the condition of one side — two deaf brothers or two deaf sisters — guarantees that both marriages remain within the rabbinic domain.

### Six Marriage Cases

The *Mishna* enumerates the cases. Two deaf brothers married to two sisters — those sisters either are both hearing, or both deaf, or one hearing and one deaf. Similarly, two deaf sisters married to two brothers — those brothers either are both hearing, both deaf, or one of each. Because the case of two deaf brothers married to two deaf sisters appears in both lists, there are effectively five distinct combinations rather than six. In all of them, the result is the same: at least one side of each marriage is deaf, making every marriage *kiddushin derabbanan*.

### Non-Sister Scenario

What is the *halakha*? *Harei elu pturot min ha-chalitza u-min ha-yibbum* — if one brother dies, the surviving brother is exempt from both *chalitza* and *yibbum*. Why? Because the women are each other's sisters — *achot ishto* — and since everything operates within the shared universe of *nissuei cheresh*, that exemption applies, and the woman simply goes free. But *ve'im hayu nokhriyot* — if the women were not related to one another — then the surviving brother has an obligation of *yibbum* or *chalitza* toward his deceased brother's wife, since she is his *yevamah* within the rabbinic world. When the women are unrelated, the *achot ishto* exemption does not apply, and the obligation remains.

### Chereshet Cannot Do Chal…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּפִקְחִים וּלְבַסּוֹף נִתְחָרְשׁוּ, מִי מָצֵי מַפְּקִי? וְהָתְנַן: נִשְׁתַּטֵּית — לֹא יוֹצִיא, נִתְחָרֵשׁ הוּא אוֹ נִשְׁתַּטָּה — לֹא יוֹצִיא עוֹלָמִית!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If we say** the mishna is referring to **men who were** halakhically **competent and later became deaf-mutes, then** in that case **can they release them? But didn't we learn** in the mishna (112b): **If she became mentally incompetent, he may not divorce her; if he became a deaf-mute or mentally incompetent, he may never divorce her?**

The *Mishna* says in that case: *yiknesu* — he may perform *yibbum*. But why does the text not also permit *chalitza*? Because in several of these configurations, the woman is herself a *chereshet*, and a *chereshet* cannot perform *chalitza*. Therefore *chalitza* is not discussed in this context — only *yibbum* is permitted.

### Cheresh Gives Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו אַחֵרְשִׁין מֵעִיקָּרָא, וּמִדְּהֵן חֵרְשִׁין מֵעִיקָּרָא — אִינְהוּ נָמֵי חֵרְשׁוֹת מֵעִיקָּרָא, וְקָתָנֵי: אִם הָיוּ נׇכְרִיּוֹת — יִכְנוֹסוּ. יִכְנוֹסוּ — אִין, יַחְלֹצוּ — לָא! תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to **men who were deaf-mutes at the outset? And since they were deaf-mutes at the outset, the women were also deaf-mutes at the outset, and it is taught** there: **If,** in these cases, **they were unrelated** to one another, the surviving brothers **should consummate** levirate marriage with them. This implies: **Yes, they should consummate** levirate marriage with them, **but no, they should not perform *ḥalitza*.** From this conclusion is apparent that a female deaf-mute may not perform *ḥalitza*; **the refutation** of the opinion **of Rabba is a conclusive refutation.**

The *Mishna* then adds: *ve'im ratzu lehotzi, yotzi'u* — and if they wish to divorce, they may divorce. This requires explanation. A *cheresh* normally cannot give a *get*, since he is not a full *bar da'at*. But since the entire marriage is rabbinic, the rabbis constructed a closed system: just as they allowed a *cheresh* to marry rabbinically, they allowed him to divorce rabbinically. The operative principle is *keshem she-koneis be-remiza kach motzi be-remiza* — just as he enters the marriage through gesture, so he may exit through gesture. The whole framework is self-contained within the rabbinic world of *nissuei cherashim*. So within this system, he may perform *yibbum* and thereafter give a *get*. What is strikingly not permitted — even though *yibbum* and a subsequent *get* are both allowed — is *chalitza*. The *Gemara* does not fully explain why *chalitza* is excluded while *yibbum* and *get* are permitted; it simply proves it.

### Braita Proof

The *Gemara* now establishes through the text of the *Mishna* itself that all of these cases involve people who were deaf from the outset. The question is raised: *heichi dami* — what is the case? If the marriages began when all parties were hearing and they only later became deaf, then the marriages would be on a *de'oraita* plane, and a *get* would not suffice — as the *Mishna* states, *nitcharesh hu o nishtata lo yotzi olamit*, if a husband who married while capable later became deaf or mentally incompetent, he can never divorce her. Since the *Mishna* permits giving a *get*, it must be that everyone was a *cheresh me'ikara* — all the marriages were purely rabbinic from the beginning, and no party was ever hearing. This is confirmed: *umide'inun chereshin me'ikara* — since the text requires that the men were *chereshin me'ikara* (or else no *get* could be given), the women must similarly have been *cherashot me'ikara*. And having established this, the *Gemara* reads: *ve-katani im hayu nokhriyot yiknesu* — even so, what is permitted is only *yibbum*, *yiknesu in, yachaltzu lo* — *yibbum* yes, *chalitza* no. *Tiyuvta de-Rabba tiyuvta* — this is a conclusive refutation of Rava. Even when it is perfectly clear that everything is operating within a purely rabbinic framework of marriages of *cherashim*, the women may not perform *chalitza*.

## Ktana v'Chereshet

### Two Different Wives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְטַנָּה וְחֵרֶשֶׁת וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ לְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה וּלְרַב חָנָא חַתְנֵיהּ, דְּיָתְבִי וְקָמַקְווּ אַקְווֹתָא בְּשׁוּקָא דְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא וְאָמְרִי, הָא דִּתְנַן: קְטַנָּה וְחֵרֶשֶׁת, אֵין בִּיאַת אַחַת מֵהֶן פּוֹטֶרֶת צָרָתָהּ, הָנֵי מִילֵּי דִּנְפַלָה לֵיהּ מֵאָחִיו פִּקֵּחַ, דְּלָא יָדְעִינַן אִי בִּקְטַנָּה נִיחָא לֵיהּ אִי בְּחֵרֶשֶׁת נִיחָא לֵיהּ
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna: If one wife is a **minor and** the other is **a deaf-mute,** consummation of levirate marriage or *ḥalitza* of the *yavam* with one of them does not exempt her rival wife. **Rav Naḥman said: I found Rav Adda bar Ahava and Rav Ḥana his son-in-law sitting and posing challenges [*kamakvu akvata*]** to one another **in the marketplace of Pumbedita, and saying** as follows: **That** which **we learned** in the mishna, **that in the case of a minor and a deaf-mute,** the **consummation** of levirate marriage **with one of them does not exempt her rival wife, applies when she happened** before **him** for levirate marriage as the widow **of his** halakhically **competent brother.** Under such circumstances, **we do not know if the minor was preferable to** the brother who married her initially, **or if the deaf-mute was preferable to him.**

The *Mishna* now introduces the case of a man who was married to a *ketanah* — a minor — and a *chereshet*, two wives simultaneously. The *chereshet* is an adult; she is a *gedolah*. He dies, and his brother now has an obligation of *yibbum* or *chalitza* toward both women. Both marriages are *derabbanan*, but here lies the crux of the problem: though both are rabbinic, they are rabbinic at *different levels*. In the previous case, two wives of identical status — both *cherashot* or both *ketanot* — could be treated symmetrically: *yibbum* or *chalitza* with one exempts the other. But when the two rabbinic marriages are of different quality, that symmetry breaks down, and the surviving brother cannot assume that what he does with one resolves his obligation to the other.

Without yet explaining the mechanism in full, the *Gemara* will shortly assume — and Rashi at the bottom of the *daf* demonstrates — that one of these marriages has the character of *mekudeshet u-meshuyeret*: a quasi-*kiddushin*, a partial but definite marriage, roughly described as fifty percent. The other has the character of *safek kiddushin* — *mekudeshet ve-einah mekudeshet* — either one hundred percent married or not married at all. Which type applies to which woman, and how that determination is derived, is what the *Gemara* now sets out to establish.

### Rav Nachman Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בִּקְטַנָּה נִיחָא לֵיהּ — דְּאָתְיָא לִכְלַל דֵּיעָה, אִי בְּחֵרֶשֶׁת נִיחָא — דִּגְדוֹלָה הִיא, וּבַת בִּיאָה הִיא. אֲבָל נָפְלָה מֵאָחִיו חֵרֵשׁ — וַדַּאי בְּחֵרֶשֶׁת נִיחָא לֵיהּ, דְּבַת בִּיאָה הִיא וּבַת מִינֵּיהּ הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: They wondered **if the minor was preferable to him, since she** eventually **would have come to full intellectual capacity** when she reached majority, **or if the deaf-mute was preferable, since she is an adult and she is suitable** for **sexual intercourse.** Given the uncertainty, it cannot be determined whose initial marriage was more complete and therefore the levirate marriage of one of them cannot exempt her rival wife. **However, if she happened** before him for levirate marriage as the widow of **his brother** who was **deaf-mute, certainly the female deaf-mute was preferable to him, as she was suitable for sexual intercourse and was of his kind,** and therefore the marriage to her was more complete.

Rav Nachman reports: *ashkachtei le-Rav Adda bar Ahava u-le-Rav Chana chasneih de-Chana* — I came upon Rav Adda bar Ahava and Rav Chana his son-in-law *de-yasvi kamei kavrei ve-kav'ei kavasah* — sitting and posing questions to one another, *be-shuka de-Pumbedita*, in the marketplace of Pumbedita. *Ve'amri* — and they were saying: *hazitan ketanah ve-chereshet, ein biat achat mehen poteret tzaratah* — in the case of a *ketanah* and a *chereshet*, the act of *yibbum* with one of them does not exempt the other.

### Husband's Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמֵינָא לְהוּ אֲנָא: אֲפִילּוּ נְפַלָה לֵיהּ מֵאָחִיו חֵרֵשׁ נָמֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And I,** Rav Nachman, **said to them: Even if she happened** before **him** as the widow **of his brother** who was **deaf-mute, it is still uncertain,** because the difference between the marriage of a minor and a deaf-mute in this case is independent of the original husband's preferences. They are different types of relationships.

The question then becomes: under what circumstances does this uncertainty apply? The answer given is: *hanei milei de-naflu leih me-ach pikei'ach* — this applies when the two women were married to a hearing, adult brother. In that case, *de-lo yadenan ei be-katanah nicha leih* — we do not know which wife he would have preferred. Would he have preferred the minor, *d'asya li-klal de'ah* — because she would eventually grow into a full adult, someone he could fully relate to? Or would he have preferred the *chereshet*, *de-gedolah hi u-vat bi'ah hi* — because she is already an adult with whom he could have relations now? The framing — phrased as which wife the husband would prefer — is admittedly curious. The more precise reading is a legal one: which marriage does *halacha* recognize as the primary, weightier marriage, based on assumptions about what men would generally value. The uncertainty about which marriage carries more weight is what prevents a single act of *yibbum* from resolving the obligation to both.

### Cheresh Husband

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד תַּקָּנָתָן? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב: כּוֹנֵס הַחֵרֶשֶׁת וּמוֹצִיאָהּ בְּגֵט, וּקְטַנָּה תַּמְתִּין עַד שֶׁתַּגְדִּיל, וְתַחְלוֹץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How can their** situation **be rectified,** so that they can remarry? **Rav Ḥisda said that Rav said: He consummates** the levirate marriage with **the deaf-mute and** then **releases her by means of a bill of divorce.** He cannot stay married to her because the subsequent *ḥalitza* of the minor will disqualify her as the rival wife of his *yevama* who has performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*]. **The minor must wait until she reaches majority and then perform *ḥalitza*.**

An alternative scenario is then proposed: what if both women had been married not to a hearing brother but to a *cheresh*? In that case, one might argue *vadai be-chereshet nicha leih* — surely it is clear that a deaf man would prefer his adult deaf wife over a minor. *De-vat bi'ah hi u-vas minei hi* — she can have relations with him now, and she is, so to speak, his counterpart, similarly situated. So perhaps when the husband was a *cheresh*, we should legally assume his marriage to the *chereshet* was the weightier one.

### Still Uncertain

Rav Nachman responds: *afilu naflu lo me-achiv cheresh nami mesapka lei* — even if the women fell to the brother from a deaf husband, I am still uncertain. This is not merely a question of what the husband would have preferred or what assumptions we make about men's preferences. There is a technical legal difference between *nissuei chereshet* and *nissuei ketanah* that is independent of who the husband was. It is that structural difference — the different legal quality of each rabbinic marriage — that produces the uncertainty, and no assumption about the husband resolves it.

### Dual Obligation Problem

The question then becomes practical: *keitzad takanasam* — how does the surviving brother fulfill his obligation to both women? He has a *mitzvah* of *yibbum* or *chalitza* toward each, but of different qualities, and each affects the other.

### Failed Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, קָסָבַר רַב: חֵרֶשֶׁת קְנוּיָה וּמְשׁוּיֶּירֶת, קְטַנָּה קְנוּיָה וְאֵינָהּ קְנוּיָה. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ חֵרֶשֶׁת קְנוּיָה וְאֵינָהּ קְנוּיָה, קְטַנָּה קְנוּיָה וּמְשׁוּיֶּירֶת, חֵרֶשֶׁת אַמַּאי כּוֹנֵס וּמוֹצִיאָהּ בְּגֵט?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: Learn from this** statement **that Rav holds** that **a married deaf-mute is partially acquired, and a minor is either acquired or not acquired,** i.e., there is uncertainty as to whether she was acquired completely or not acquired at all. **For if it enters your mind** to say the opposite, **that the deaf-mute is either acquired or not acquired, while the minor is partially acquired, then** with regard to **the deaf-mute** woman, **why should he consummate** the levirate marriage **and** then **release her with a bill of divorce?**

Consider the proposed solutions and why each fails. One might suggest: perform *chalitza* with the *ketanah* and *yibbum* with the *chereshet*. The problem is that *chalitza* with the *ketanah* — if the *ketanah*'s marriage was the weightier one — would conclude the entire obligation of *yibbum*, leaving no *mitzvah* remaining for the *chereshet*. So *yibbum* with the *chereshet* after *chalitza* with the *ketanah* would be unwarranted. Now reverse the order: perform *yibbum* with the *chereshet* first, and then *chalitza* with the *ketanah*. The problem there is that even after *yibbum* with the *chereshet*, because we do not know which marriage is stronger, *chalitza* with the *ketanah* remains possible and perhaps necessary — and once *chalitza* with the *ketanah* has occurred, if she were in fact the weightier *zikah*, he could not continue living with the *chereshet* as a *yevamah*, because she would now be the co-wife of a *chalutzah*. Regardless of order, the system locks up: one act undermines the other. And the simple escape — *chalitza* with both women — is unavailable, because a *chereshet* cannot perform *chalitza*.

### Rav Chisda Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיתֵּיב גַּבֵּיהּ מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי קְנוּיָה הִיא — הָא קְנוּיָה הִיא, וְאִי לָאו קְנוּיָה הִיא — הָא נׇכְרִית בְּעָלְמָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Let her stay with him,** as she is permitted **whichever way you** look at it: **If she is acquired, then she is** fully **acquired; and if she is not acquired** at all, **then she is merely an unrelated** woman. If the marriage to the deceased brother was not a true marriage, there is no reason for her to be forbidden to him.

Rav Chisda, citing Rav, provides the solution: *koneis et ha-chereshet u-motzi'ah be-get* — he performs *yibbum* with the *chereshet* through *bi'ah*, and then gives her a *get*. *Ketanah tamtin ad she-tagdil ve-tachloz* — the *ketanah* then waits until she becomes an adult and performs *chalitza*. The elegance of this solution is that it sidesteps the trap. He first performs *yibbum* with the *chereshet* and gives her a *get*, removing her from the picture entirely before *chalitza* with the *ketanah* occurs. Once she has received a *get* and is gone, the subsequent *chalitza* with the *ketanah* cannot retroactively undermine the completed relationship. The concern about preventing *agunot* motivates the entire construction: the solution is engineered so that no woman is left without recourse.

## Marriage Status Inference

### Deaf Quasi, Minor All/No…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, קְטַנָּה אַמַּאי תַּמְתִּין עַד שֶׁתַּגְדִּיל וְתַחְלוֹץ? תִּיתֵּיב גַּבֵּיהּ, אִי קְנוּיָה הִיא — הָא קְנוּיָה הִיא, אִי לָאו קְנוּיָה הִיא — נוּכְרִיתָא בְּעָלְמָא הִיא! אִם כֵּן, חֵרֶשֶׁת בְּמָה תִּיפּוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that the same question could be asked if you claim that it is the minor whose acquisition is uncertain: **Why should** she **wait until she reaches majority and performs *ḥalitza*? Let her stay with him: If she is acquired, then she is** fully **acquired;** and **if she is not acquired** at all, **then she is merely an unrelated** woman. However, you cannot say that because, **if that is so, how shall the deaf-mute be released?** As a deaf-mute, she cannot perform *ḥalitza*, and he cannot consummate the levirate marriage with her since the minor may be considered to be acquired by him, in which case the deaf-mute would be disqualified as the rival wife of his *yevama*. Rav's suggestion was meant to find a way for both of them to be able to remarry, and that is only possible if the minor and deaf-mute's statuses are as Rav Ḥisda argues.

From Rav's solution — *yibbum* followed by a *get* for the *chereshet*, then *chalitza* for the *ketanah* — the *Gemara* draws an inference: *kasavar Rav chereshet kenuyah u-meshuyeret* — Rav holds that the marriage to a *chereshet* is a quasi-*kiddushin*, a definite but partial relationship. *Ketanah kenuyah ve-einah kenuyah* — and the marriage to a *ketanah* is an all-or-nothing proposition: either one hundred percent married or not married at all. Counterintuitively, the *ketanah*'s marriage is the *safek* — all or nothing — while the *chereshet*'s marriage is the fifty percent. The *Gemara* now demonstrates this through a counterfactual.

### Counterfactual Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָכִי נָמֵי מִיסְתַּבְּרָא, כִּדְקָא מְתָרֵץ רַב חִסְדָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְרַב,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: Indeed, this too stands to reason,** i.e., only the way **that Rav Ḥisda explained** the *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav** is reasonable.

*Di salka da'atach* — suppose one assumed the reverse: that the *chereshet* is the all-or-nothing *safek*, and the *ketanah* is the quasi-fifty-percent. If that were so, why would Rav's solution require *yibbum* with the *chereshet* followed by a *get*? There would be a simpler solution available: the *Gemara* will now show what that simpler solution would be, and how Rav's deliberate choice of the more complex path reveals his underlying assumption.

### If Deaf Were Safek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁנֵי אַחִים נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת יְתוֹמוֹת, קְטַנָּה וְחֵרֶשֶׁת, מֵת בַּעְלָהּ שֶׁל קְטַנָּה — חֵרֶשֶׁת יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט, וּקְטַנָּה תַּמְתִּין עַד שֶׁתַּגְדִּיל, וְתַחְלוֹץ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* about the following case: **Two brothers married two orphaned sisters,** one of them **a minor and** one of them **a deaf-mute.** If **the husband of the minor dies** and she happens before the husband of the deaf-mute for levirate marriage, **the deaf-mute must be released by means of a bill of divorce** due to her sister's levirate bond, **and the minor must wait until she reaches majority and then perform *ḥalitza*.**

If the *chereshet* were the all-or-nothing *safek*, the following solution would present itself: perform *yibbum* with the *chereshet* and *chalitza* with the *ketanah* — and simply allow him to remain married to the *chereshet*, *mimah nafshach*, no matter what. Why? Because if she is a full one hundred percent married to his brother, then his *yibbum* with her is a complete *yibbum de-oraita*, and *chalitza* with the *ketanah* — a rabbinic, fifty-percent relationship — has no power to undermine it. And if she is zero percent — not married to his brother at all — then she is not his *yevamah*, she is simply a stranger, and again *chalitza* with the *ketanah* cannot affect his relationship with her. Either way, *chalitza* with the *ketanah* leaves his relationship with the *chereshet* intact. *Teishev gabei mimah nafshach* — she can simply remain with him. There would be no need for a *get* at all.

### Must Divorce Deaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת בַּעְלָהּ שֶׁל חֵרֶשֶׁת — קְטַנָּה יוֹצְאָה בְּגֵט, וְחֵרֶשֶׁת אֲסוּרָה לְעוֹלָם. וְאִם בָּא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת — נוֹתֵן לָהּ גֵּט, וְהוּתְּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If the deaf-mute's husband dies, the minor must be released by means of a bill of divorce and the deaf-mute is forbidden forever.** He must divorce the minor because of the levirate bond with her sister. He may not consummate the levirate marriage with the deaf-mute because she is his ex-wife's sister; and he cannot perform *ḥalitza* with her, because she is not capable of performing *ḥalitza*. **And if he** transgresses and **consummates** the levirate marriage **with the deaf-mute, he gives her a bill of divorce** afterward, **and she is** thereby **released.**

The very fact that Rav's solution *requires* giving the *chereshet* a *get* demonstrates that her marriage is not all-or-nothing but fifty percent. Because she is definitely — if only partially — his *yevamah*, the subsequent *chalitza* with the *ketanah* can affect his relationship with her. If the *ketanah*'s marriage is one hundred percent, then after *chalitza* with the *ketanah*, he would have performed *chalitza* with a woman who is fully his sister-in-law, and this would undermine his continuing relationship with the *chereshet* — who is herself definitely, if partially, in the *yibbum* relationship. Since she is quasi-married and definitely in the picture, a real *chalitza* elsewhere can destabilize that quasi-relationship. Therefore, he must first do *yibbum* with the *chereshet* and give her a *get*, resolving her status completely before *chalitza* with the *ketanah* can occur.

### Alternative Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חֵרֶשֶׁת קְנוּיָה וּמְשׁוּיֶּירֶת, קְטַנָּה קְנוּיָה וְאֵינָהּ קְנוּיָה — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי דְּכִי בָּא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת נוֹתֵן לָהּ גֵּט וְהוּתְּרָה, דְּאָמְרַתְּ מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי קְטַנָּה קְנוּיָה הִיא — הָא נָפְקָא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה, אִי לָאו קְנוּיָה — שַׁפִּיר מְיַבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** this argument works **if you say that the deaf-mute is partially acquired,** meaning that she was never fully acquired in the first place; that **a minor is either acquired or not acquired; and** that the *baraita* is **in accordance** with the opinion of **the Rabbis** who state that an invalid sexual act does not exempt a woman from *ḥalitza*. **For these** reasons, **the minor must wait until she reaches majority and then perform *ḥalitza*.** The Sages decreed that she must perform *ḥalitza* no matter what, **as perhaps he may precipitately have intercourse with the deaf-mute first, so that the** subsequent **intercourse with the minor is considered an invalid sexual act,** because he is already partially married to her rival wife. However, since the deaf-mute is only partially acquired, her levirate marriage does not exempt the minor and the minor still must perform *ḥalitza*.

An objection is raised: why go through the *chereshet* first at all? If the *ketanah* is all-or-nothing, why not simply perform *yibbum* with the *ketanah* instead? Either it will be a full *de-oraita* *yibbum*, or it will be a meaningless act toward a woman who was never his brother's wife. Either way, there seems to be no problem.

### Must Help Deaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ חֵרֶשֶׁת קְנוּיָה וְאֵינָהּ קְנוּיָה, קְטַנָּה קְנוּיָה וּמְשׁוּיֶּירֶת, כִּי בָא עַל חֵרֶשֶׁת אַמַּאי נוֹתֵן גֵּט וְהוּתְּרָה? הָוְיָא לֵיהּ בִּיאָה פְּסוּלָה — וּבִיאָה פְּסוּלָה לָא פָּטְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say that the deaf-mute is either acquired or not acquired,** i.e., the status of a marriage with a deaf-mute is uncertain, **while the minor is partially acquired,** then **when he** transgresses and **has intercourse with the deaf-mute, why should he give her a bill of divorce and** she thereby **be released? It is an invalid sexual act,** as the minor is partially acquired, which disqualifies her sister from levirate marriage. **And an invalid sexual act does not exempt her,** i.e., does not constitute full consummation of the levirate marriage, making it possible for him to divorce her. She still requires *ḥalitza*, and a deaf-mute cannot perform *ḥalitza*. Therefore it must be, as Rav Ḥisda suggested, that the deaf-mute is partially acquired while it is uncertain whether the minor is acquired or not.

The *Gemara* responds: *im kein, chereshet be-mai tipok?* — yes, but then what happens to the *chereshet*? Even if *yibbum* with the *ketanah* could work in principle, it abandons the *chereshet* with no solution. The *chereshet* cannot perform *chalitza*, and if he has already resolved the obligation through *yibbum* with the *ketanah*, she has no exit. The solution must begin with the *chereshet* precisely because she is the one who cannot do *chalitza* and therefore must be helped first. Rav's solution — *yibbum* and a *get* with the *chereshet*, followed by *chalitza* when the *ketanah* grows up — is thus shown to presuppose that the *chereshet*'s marriage is quasi (fifty percent) and the *ketanah*'s is all-or-nothing. Rav Sheshet confirms: *hachi nami mistavra* — this is indeed the logical conclusion. It is further demonstrated from the *braita* that Rav Chisda explained the *halakha* according to Rav precisely on this basis.

## Husband of Deaf Dies

### Obligation to Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בִּיאָה פְּסוּלָה, פּוֹטֶרֶת מֵחֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the conclusion that the *baraita* provides evidence for Rav Ḥisda's explanation, as it is possible to say: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, who said: An invalid sexual act** also **exempts** a woman **from *ḥalitza*.**

A *braita* is then cited: *shnei achim nesu'im shtei achayot yetomot, ketanah ve-chereshet* — two brothers married to two sisters, one a *ketanah* and one a *chereshet*, both marriages rabbinic but of different qualities, as we have now established. The two brothers die in sequence, leaving the surviving brother with obligations to both women, who are sisters of one another. Since the two marriages are of different qualities, neither woman can simply walk away on grounds of *achot ishto* — the interplay between them is precisely what creates the legal complication.

### Minor Husband Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: מִי שֶׁהָיָה נָשׂוּי שְׁתֵּי יְתוֹמוֹת, קְטַנָּה וְחֵרֶשֶׁת, וּמֵת. בָּא יָבָם עַל הַקְּטַנָּה, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת — נֶאֶסְרוּ שְׁתֵּיהֶן עָלָיו. כֵּיצַד תַּקָּנָתָן? חֵרֶשֶׁת יוֹצֵאת בְּגֵט, וּקְטַנָּה תַּמְתִּין עַד שֶׁתַּגְדִּיל, וְתַחְלוֹץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** one accepts the suggestion that **this** *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya, say the latter clause** of this same *baraita*: Consider the case of **one who was married** to **two orphans, a minor and a deaf-mute, and he died. If the *yavam* had intercourse with the minor and then had intercourse with the deaf-mute, or if one of his brothers had intercourse with the deaf-mute** after the first brother engaged in intercourse with the minor, **they are both forbidden to** the first brother. **How** can their situation **be rectified? The deaf-mute must be released with a bill of divorce, and the minor must wait until she reaches majority and** then **perform *ḥalitza*.**

*Meit ba'al ha-ketanah* — the husband of the *ketanah* dies. This is the relatively tractable case. The *chereshet* — who is the surviving brother's wife — must be divorced first: *chereshet yotzet be-get*. Why must he divorce his own wife? Because the women are sisters, and his obligation toward his *yevamah* might be the weightier one; she cannot simply walk away as *achot ishto* without some resolution. But if he were to perform *chalitza* with her, he would then be unable to continue living with his own wife — his wife would become *achot chalutzato*. And if he were to divorce his wife first and then perform *yibbum*, his wife would become *achot gerushato* and then *achot ishto*. Every path is blocked unless he first divorces his wife cleanly, severs that relationship entirely, and then, once the *yevamah* is no longer his wife's sister, addresses his obligation: *ketanah tamtin ad she-tagdil ve-tachloz* — the *ketanah* waits until she becomes an adult and performs *chalitza*.

### Deaf Husband Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חֵרֶשֶׁת קְנוּיָה וּמְשׁוּיֶּירֶת, קְטַנָּה קְנוּיָה וְאֵינָהּ קְנוּיָה, וְרַבָּנַן הִיא — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי תַּמְתִּין עַד שֶׁתַּגְדִּיל, וְתַחְלוֹץ — דְּדִלְמָא קָדֵים וּבָעֵיל חֵרֶשֶׁת בְּרֵישָׁא, וְהָוְיָא לֵיהּ בִּיאָה דִקְטַנָּה בִּיאָה פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** this *halakha* makes sense **if you say** that **a deaf-mute is partially acquired,** meaning that she was never fully acquired in the first place; that **a minor is either acquired or not acquired; and** that the *baraita* is **in accordance** with the opinion of **the Rabbis** who state that an invalid sexual act does not exempt a woman from *ḥalitza*. **For these** reasons, **the minor must wait until she reaches majority and** then **perform *ḥalitza*.** The Sages decreed that she must perform *ḥalitza* no matter what, **as perhaps he may precipitately have intercourse with the deaf-mute first, so that the** subsequent **intercourse with the minor is considered an invalid sexual act,** because he is already partially married to her rival wife. However, since the deaf-mute is only partially acquired, her levirate marriage does not exempt the minor and the minor still must perform *ḥalitza*.

*Meit ba'al ha-chereshet* — the husband of the *chereshet* dies. This is the harder case. The same logic applies: he cannot simply do *chalitza* with her because she is a *chereshet* and cannot perform *chalitza*. He cannot perform *yibbum* with her while still married to her sister, because the act of *bi'ah* would be *achot ishto*. And he cannot divorce his wife and then immediately perform *yibbum*, because she would be *achot gerushato*. So: *ketanah yotzet be-get* — he divorces the *ketanah*, severs that relationship. But even so: *ve-chereshet asurah lo le-olam* — the *chereshet* remains forbidden to him forever. She cannot perform *chalitza*, she cannot be reached through any permitted *bi'ah*, and there is simply no halakhically sanctioned path to resolve her status. She is in a state of complete *agunah* with no remedy.

### Post-Facto Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ רַבָּנַן הִיא. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, conclude from** this reasoning that this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** The Gemara concludes: **Learn from it.**

However, the *braita* adds a post-facto provision: *ve'im ba al ha-chereshet noten lah get ve-hutrah* — if he did have relations with the *chereshet* — which he was not permitted to do, since it was *achot ishto* — he may now give her a *get* and she is released. The logic: if his obligation to the *chereshet* was the stronger one, then despite the transgression, the act of *bi'ah* was functionally an act of *yibbum*, and a *get* can now dissolve it. This is a *bedi'avad* solution only, but it does resolve her status.

## Biah Pesulah Analysis

### Reverse Doesn't Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מֵרֵישָׁא נָמֵי שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּרַבָּנַן הִיא, דְּקָתָנֵי: אִם בָּא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת — נוֹתֵן לָהּ גֵּט, וְהוּתְּרָה, וְלָא קָתָנֵי: אִם בָּא עַל הַקְּטַנָּה — נוֹתֵן לָהּ גֵּט וְהוּתְּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: One may learn also from the first clause** of the *baraita* **that it is** in accordance with **the** opinion of the **Rabbis, as it teaches: If he** transgresses and **consummates** the levirate marriage **with the** sister, who is **a deaf-mute, he gives her a bill of divorce** afterward, **and she is** thereby **released. And it does not teach: If he consummates** the levirate marriage **with the minor, he gives her a bill of divorce and she is released.** This is presumably because consummation of levirate marriage with the minor is an invalid sexual act, as her deaf-mute sister is partially married to the *yavam*. Even if he does engage in intercourse with her, she will still require *ḥalitza*.

The *Gemara* now uses the *braita* to prove once again — this time from the *siyata* of the *braita* — that the *chereshet* is the fifty-percent relationship and the *ketanah* is the all-or-nothing. *Ai amarta bishlama chereshet kenuyah meshuyeret ketanah kenuyah ve-einah kenuyah* — if the *chereshet* is the fifty percent and the *ketanah* is the all-or-nothing, then the *bedi'avad* case makes sense. When he sleeps with the *chereshet*, *mimah nafshach*: if the *ketanah* was one hundred percent married to his brother, then she — the *chereshet*'s sister — was *achot ishto de-oraita*, not subject to *yibbum* at all, and walks away; the *chereshet*, having been dealt with through illicit *bi'ah*, can now receive a *get* and be released. And if the *ketanah* was zero percent, then she was never his *yevamah* at all, she is *nochreit be-alma*, and his *bi'ah* with the *chereshet* was an uncomplicated act of *yibbum*, after which a *get* works perfectly well. Either way, the *chereshet* can be freed.

Now suppose the reverse: the *chereshet* is the all-or-nothing and the *ketanah* is the fifty percent. In that case, *ki ba la-chereshet, amai noten lah get ve-hutrah?* — why should sleeping with the *chereshet* enable him to give her a *get* and free her? Because if the *chereshet* is either zero percent (in which case there is nothing to resolve) or one hundred percent (in which case she is his full *de-oraita* *yevamah*), and if she is one hundred percent, then his *bi'ah* with her was a *bi'ah pesulah* — a forbidden act of *bi'ah* — because he was at the same time definitely, fifty-percent quasi-married to her sister. A *bi'ah pesulah* does not complete *yibbum*; it does not permit a *get* to substitute for *chalitza*.

### Biah Pesulah Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא לָא אִירְיָא: חֵרֶשֶׁת, דְּלֵית לַהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא דְהֶיתֵּירָא — קָתָנֵי תַּקַּנְתָּא דְאִיסּוּרָא. קְטַנָּה, דְּאִית לַהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא דְהֶיתֵּירָא — לָא תָּנֵי תַּקַּנְתָּא דְאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **If it is due to that** reason, **there is no** conclusive **argument,** as one may say that for **the deaf-mute, whose** situation **has no rectification that is permitted** because she cannot perform *ḥalitza*, the *baraita* **teaches a prohibited rectification;** but for **the minor, whose** situation **has a permitted rectification,** in that she can perform *ḥalitza* after she reaches majority, the *baraita* **does not teach a prohibited rectification.**

*Bi'ah pesulah* is the halakhic category for a case in which the act of *bi'ah* that would normally constitute *yibbum* is rendered invalid by circumstances that make it forbidden. Here the circumstances are that he is quasi-definitely married to the co-wife, the *chereshet*'s sister, making the act of *bi'ah* with the *chereshet* the act of sleeping with his wife's sister. The exact contours of *bi'ah pesulah* are a matter of discussion in Rashi and Tosafot, but for present purposes the operative principle is clear.

### Biah Pesulah No Get

When *bi'ah pesulah* has occurred, the *Gemara* rules *de-Rabbanan* that it does not count as completed *yibbum*. A *get* alone is insufficient to release the woman; she still requires *chalitza*. Since *chalitza* is unavailable to the *chereshet*, the reverse scenario — where the *chereshet* is all-or-nothing and the *ketanah* is fifty percent — would leave the *chereshet* with no solution even *bedi'avad*. But the *braita* states that *bedi'avad* there is a solution. This proves that the reverse scenario is not the correct one: the *chereshet* must be the fifty percent and the *ketanah* the all-or-nothing.

### Rav Nechemiah View

At this point, Rav Nechemiah enters the picture. Rav Nechemiah had ruled that *bi'ah pesulah poteret* — that even a forbidden act of *bi'ah* is sufficient to permit a subsequent *get*, without requiring *chalitza*. If one adopts Rav Nechemiah's position, then the argument above collapses: even if the *chereshet* were all-or-nothing and the *bi'ah* with her were a *bi'ah pesulah*, she could still receive a *get* and be freed. One could therefore maintain the opposite assignment — *chereshet* as all-or-nothing, *ketanah* as fifty percent — and still explain the *braita*'s *bedi'avad* ruling. So the *braita*'s conclusion can no longer be taken as proof of Rav's position unless Rav Nechemiah is set aside.

### Braita Assumes Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ רַבָּנַן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, conclude from** this reasoning that this *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

To resolve this, the *Gemara* turns to the *seifa* of the *braita*: *mi she-nasui shtei yetomot, ketanah ve-chereshet* — a man married to a *ketanah* and a *chereshet*, who then dies, and the brother-in-law comes. The *braita* describes the following: *u-va yavam al ha-ketanah ve-chazar u-va al ha-chereshet* — first *bi'ah* with the *ketanah*, then *bi'ah* with the *chereshet* (or another brother performed the second *bi'ah*); and similarly the reverse scenario. In both cases, *ne'esru shteihen alav* — both women become forbidden to him; he must divorce both. *Keitzad takanatan? Chereshet yotzet be-get ve-ketanah tamtin ad she-tagdil ve-tachloz* — the *chereshet* receives a *get*, and the *ketanah* waits until she grows and performs *chalitza*.

The crucial question: why does the *ketanah* require *chalitza* rather than simply a *get*? If it were Rav Nechemiah's position — *bi'ah pesulah* works — then whichever *bi'ah* occurred second would have resolved the obligation, and a *get* for both women should suffice. The fact that the *braita* insists on *chalitza* for the *ketanah* can only be explained if we assume that: (a) the *bi'ah* with the *ketanah* was a *bi'ah pesulah*, and (b) *bi'ah pesulah* does not suffice. *Bi'ah pesulah* arises for the *ketanah* only if, at the time of the *bi'ah* with her, he was definitely quasi-married to her sister — i.e., the *chereshet* must be the fifty-percent marriage. The *braita* thus operates on Rav's assumptions, not Rav Nechemiah's. *Ela shema mina rabanan hi, shema mina* — conclude from this that the *braita* follows the position of the *Rabbanan*, and Rav's framework is confirmed: the *chereshet* is the quasi-fifty-percent marriage and the *ketanah* is the all-or-nothing *safek*.

## Mishna Cases

### Same Category Wives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָיָה נָשׂוּי לִשְׁתֵּי יְתוֹמוֹת קְטַנּוֹת, וּמֵת, וּבָא יָבָם עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַשְּׁנִיָּה, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַשְּׁנִיָּה — לֹא פָּסַל אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי חֵרְשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a man was married to two minor orphans and he died, and a *yavam* engaged in intercourse with the first** of them to consummate the levirate marriage, **and then engaged in intercourse with the second, or if his brother** who is also their *yavam* **engaged in intercourse with the second,** the *yavam* or his brother **did not disqualify the first** girl from staying married to him, as her levirate marriage was consummated. **Likewise,** if the two wives were **two female deaf-mutes,** the first wife may remain married to the *yavam*. Intercourse with the second wife, though prohibited, has no effect: If the marriage was of uncertain status, then either the levirate marriage was concluded when he engaged in intercourse with the first, or neither wife was really married to the first husband, and they are therefore not rival wives. If the initial marriage was partial, then since both wives have the same standing, the levirate marriage with the first wife fully realizes whatever degree of levirate marriage is available.

Having worked through the underlying principles at length, the *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishnah* itself, much of whose content has already been clarified in the preceding *sugya*. The *Mishnah* presents a series of cases based on the relationship between the two wives of the deceased brother — whether they are of the same legal category or of different categories, and what the result is when the surviving brother performs *yibbum* with one and then has relations with the other. The first cases are the symmetric ones: when both women are of the same type — both *ketanot* or both *cherashot* — so that each marriage carries equal weight. In that case, the first act of *yibbum* is definitive; it fully resolves the obligation as between equals.

### Minor First, Deaf Second

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְטַנָּה וְחֵרֶשֶׁת. בָּא יָבָם עַל הַקְּטַנָּה, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת — פָּסַל אֶת הַקְּטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** If one wife was **a minor and** the other **a deaf-mute,** and **the *yavam* engaged in intercourse with the minor and then engaged in intercourse with the deaf-mute, or if his brother engaged in intercourse with the deaf-mute,** then the *yavam* or his brother **disqualified the minor** from staying married due to the Sages' decree, lest it be confused with a situation where the intercourse with the deaf-mute was first.

*Ba yavam al ha-ketanah ve-chazar u-va al ha-chereshet* — the surviving brother first performed *bi'ah* with the *ketanah*, then with the *chereshet*. Or his brother performed the second *bi'ah*. Because the two marriages are of different qualities — and we do not know which is the weightier one — the second *bi'ah*, whichever marriage it strengthens, threatens to undermine the first. He is therefore in the position of having impaired his relationship with the first woman. *Pasal et ha-ketanah* — his relationship with the *ketanah* is ruined. Both women become forbidden to him, and he must divorce both and send them away.

### Deaf First, Minor Second

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא יָבָם עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַקְּטַנָּה, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַקְּטַנָּה — פָּסַל אֶת הַחֵרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** If **the *yavam* engaged in intercourse with the deaf-mute and then engaged in intercourse with the minor, or if his brother engaged in intercourse with the minor,** then the *yavam* or his brother **disqualified the deaf-mute** from staying married. The marriage to the deaf-mute creates a partial acquisition that does not exempt the second wife from levirate marriage, as she, as a minor, has a different standing. Accordingly, intercourse with the second wife also creates a partial acquisition and thereby both women are prohibited to the *yavam*, as it is prohibited to consummate levirate marriage with more than one wife.

The reverse case: *ba yavam al ha-chereshet ve-chazar u-va al ha-ketanah* — first *bi'ah* with the *chereshet*, then with the *ketanah* (or a brother with the *ketanah*). Same logic applies: because neither marriage can be confirmed as the weightier, each subsequent act of *bi'ah* risks overturning the prior one. *Pasal et ha-chereshet* — his relationship with the *chereshet* is similarly rendered invalid, and both women are forbidden to him.

### Clear Hierarchy Wives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּקַּחַת וְחֵרֶשֶׁת. בָּא יָבָם עַל הַפִּקַּחַת, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת — לֹא פָּסַל אֶת הַפִּקַּחַת.
>
> **Translation:** If one widow was halakhically **competent and** one widow was **a deaf-mute,** and **the *yavam* engaged in intercourse with the** halakhically **competent woman and then engaged in intercourse with the deaf-mute, or** if **his brother** then **engaged in intercourse with the deaf-mute,** the *yavam* or his brother **did not disqualify the** halakhically **competent woman** from staying married. Since the *yavam* consummated the levirate marriage with her first, the levirate bond was entirely dissolved and the intercourse with the deaf-mute, though forbidden, had no effect.

The *Mishnah* then considers the case where the two marriages are of *clearly* different halakhic weight — no genuine uncertainty about which is stronger. Two scenarios: a *pikachat* (hearing adult woman) and a *chereshet*, where the *pikachat*'s marriage is *de-oraita* and the *chereshet*'s is *derabbanan*; or a *gedolah* (adult) and a *ketanah*, where the *gedolah*'s marriage is *de-oraita* and the *ketanah*'s is *derabbanan*. In both pairings, one marriage is clearly and unambiguously stronger than the other.

### Stronger First

*Ba yavam al ha-pikachat ve-chazar u-va al ha-chereshet* — if he began with the hearing woman, performing a full *de-oraita yibbum*, and then had relations with the *chereshet*: *lo pasal et ha-pikachat* — the second act does not impair the first. Once a full *de-oraita yibbum* has been completed, nothing in the rabbinic domain can undo it. The same applies to *gedolah* before *ketanah*: *lo pasal et ha-gedolah*.

### Weaker First, Stronger S…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא יָבָם עַל הַחֵרֶשֶׁת וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַפִּקַּחַת, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַפִּקַּחַת — פָּסַל אֶת הַחֵרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** If **the *yavam* engaged in intercourse with the deaf-mute and then engaged in intercourse with the** halakhically **competent woman, or if his brother engaged in intercourse with the** halakhically **competent woman,** the *yavam* or his brother **disqualified the deaf-mute** from staying married. Consummation of the levirate marriage with the deaf-mute creates only a partial acquisition that does not fully dissolve the levirate bond.

*Ba yavam al ha-chereshet ve-chazar u-va al ha-pikachat* — if he began with the *chereshet* (the rabbinic marriage) and then had relations with the *pikachat*, or if his brother did: *pasal et ha-chereshet* — this does impair his relationship with the first woman. The second act was a stronger, *de-oraita* act of *yibbum*, which retroactively overshadows the weaker rabbinic relationship. Even if the *bi'ah* with the *pikachat* involved some element of *bi'ah pesulah* — since he was in some relationship with her co-wife — it certainly works *de-oraita* and now undermines the prior weaker relationship. The same logic applies symmetrically to *ketanah* and *gedolah*: beginning with the *ketanah* and then having relations with the *gedolah*, *pasal et ha-ketanah*.

## Mi'un Solution

### Rabbi Eliezer Mi'un

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּדוֹלָה וּקְטַנָּה. בָּא יָבָם עַל הַגְּדוֹלָה, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַקְּטַנָּה, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַקְּטַנָּה — לֹא פָּסַל אֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה. בָּא יָבָם עַל הַקְּטַנָּה, וְחָזַר וּבָא עַל הַגְּדוֹלָה, אוֹ שֶׁבָּא אָחִיו עַל הַגְּדוֹלָה — פָּסַל אֶת הַקְּטַנָּה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: מְלַמְּדִין הַקְּטַנָּה שֶׁתְּמָאֵן בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If the deceased brother had two wives, **an adult and a minor,** and **the *yavam* engaged in sexual intercourse with the adult, then engaged in intercourse with the minor, or if his brother engaged in intercourse with the minor,** the *yavam* or his brother **did not disqualify the adult** from staying married, as the consummation of the levirate marriage with the adult completely dissolves the levirate bond. If **the *yavam* engaged in intercourse with the minor, and then engaged in intercourse with the adult, or if his brother engaged in intercourse with the adult,** the *yavam* or his brother **disqualified the minor** from staying married. **Rabbi Elazar says:** The court **instructs the minor to refuse him** thereby annulling her marriage retroactively, and then the minor is permitted to marry any man.

*Rabbi Elazar omer: melamdin et ha-ketanah she-tima'en lo* — Rabbi Eliezer says: we instruct the *ketanah* to perform *mi'un*, to refuse the marriage. This is the solution that cuts through the entire tangle. Rather than navigating the maze of competing *yibbum* obligations with a *ketanah* who cannot perform *chalitza* until she grows up, and whose status introduces all the complications explored above, the rabbinic authorities teach the *ketanah* to exercise her right of *mi'un* — retroactive refusal of her minority marriage — which dissolves her marriage as if it never took place and removes her from the *yibbum* equation entirely.

### Halakha K'Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, and likewise** the *amora* **Rabbi Elazar** ben Pedat **said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar** ben Shammua, the *tanna* in the mishna.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: halakha ke-Rabbi Elazar* — Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: the *halakha* follows Rabbi Eliezer. And so too ruled Rabbi Elazar the *Amora*: *halakha ke-Rabbi Elazar ha-Tanna*. When there is a *ketanah* involved, we instruct her to perform *mi'un*.

### Why Mi'un in This Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּהָא קַמַּיְיתָא, בְּהָךְ קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא קַיֵּים מִצְוַת יִיבּוּם, אֲבָל בְּהָא, דְּאִיקַּיַּים מִצְוַת יִיבּוּם — אֵימָא תַּרְוַיְיהוּ לִפְּקוּ בְּגֵט,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: It is **necessary** to rule that the minor is instructed to refuse, thus annulling her original marriage, both in this case and in the earlier case (*Yevamot* 109a) of an adult woman who becomes the *yevama* of her husband's brother who is married to her minor sister. **For if this** halakhic ruling **was stated only in that earlier** case, I would have said: **It was in that** case **that Shmuel stated the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, due to** the fact that the yavam **did not fulfill the mitzva of levirate marriage** with the adult. In order for him to be permitted to do so, the Sages rule that the minor should be instructed to refuse him. **But for this** case, **where the mitzva of levirate marriage is fulfilled, say that they must both be released with a bill of divorce.**

But this ruling was stated in two different contexts — here, and also in an earlier passage (*109a*) involving two brothers married to two sisters. The *Gemara* asks: *utzricha* — why is it necessary to state this ruling in both places? Could one not have derived the rule for one case from the other? The *Gemara* explains the necessity. In the earlier case — two brothers married to two sisters, where the *ketanah* is peripheral to the *yibbum* obligation because the *achot ishto* dimension dominates — one might have said: the reason we tell the *ketanah* to do *mi'un* is *mishum de-lo kayma mitzvat yibbum*, because there is no genuine *mitzvat yibbum* at stake. The *ketanah* is in the picture only because of the complication of the sisters; she is not a proper candidate for *yibbum* in the ordinary sense. In such a case, we may encourage *mi'un* because the *yibbum* framework barely applies to her. But here — where *yibbum* is genuinely operative, and where he has already performed *bi'ah* with both women — one might have said we do not encourage *mi'un* after the fact; rather, we deal with the situation through *get* and *chalitza* as appropriate.

### Both in Yibum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָא, מִשּׁוּם דִּגְדוֹלָה רַמְיָא קַמֵּיהּ. אֲבָל אִידַּךְ — לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** And conversely, **if** the *tanna* **teaches us** the ruling only **in this** case, one would think: **It is because the adult happened before him** for levirate marriage, and since both the adult and the minor are equally candidates for levirate marriage, it makes sense to encourage the minor to refuse; **however,** in the **other** mishna, where the minor is already married to the *yavam*, making the adult *yevama* his wife's sister who is forbidden to him, she should **not** be encouraged to refuse. Therefore, it was **necessary** to state, also in this case, that she is nevertheless encouraged to refuse.

Conversely, if the ruling had been stated only in the current case, one might have reasoned differently. Here there are two women both genuinely within the *yibbum* framework, and the adult's obligation is clearly in play: *mishum de-gedolah ramya kamei* — since the stronger obligation is the adult *yevamah*, it makes sense to have the *ketanah* step aside through *mi'un* so that the *gedolah*'s *yibbum* can proceed cleanly. But in the earlier case involving sisters — where both women have a complex and entangled status — one might have thought that *mi'un* would not be encouraged. Since each case presents a different reason why one might have hesitated to rule like Rabbi Eliezer, and since in each case there is a reason one might have said Rabbi Eliezer does not apply, both rulings are necessary. *Tzricha*. When a *ketanah* is involved in a *yibbum* scenario, we instruct her to perform *mi'un*, and this is the operative *halakha* in both types of cases.
