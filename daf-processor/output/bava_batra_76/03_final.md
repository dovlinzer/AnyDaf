# Bava Batra 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Boat Acquisition Debate

### Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: סְפִינָה – רַב אָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁמָּשַׁךְ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – קָנָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיִּמְשׁוֹךְ אֶת כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss the mishna, which discusses the acquisition of a ship. **It was stated** that there was a dispute among *amora'im* with regard to the manner in which **a ship** is acquired. **Rav says: Once** the buyer **has pulled** the ship and moved it by **any amount,** he has **acquired** it. **And Shmuel says:** He does **not acquire** it **until he pulls the entire** ship to the extent that the end of the ship has at least reached the place previously occupied by its front.

The sugya on this daf represents a radical shift from the *aggadic* discussions and classic Bava Batra material that had occupied the preceding pages. We now enter a discussion that could have come from many other tractates in *shas* — a careful legal analysis of the mechanics of acquisition. The daf begins approximately fifteen lines from the bottom of 75b, immediately after the double-dot break.

### Rav's Minimal Movement

There is something immediately arresting about this. A boat is classified as *metaltelin*, and so in principle the classic modes of acquisition for movable property apply: *meshicha*, *hagbahah*, and as we will see, *mesirah*. One might wonder whether an unusually large vessel should be treated more like land and require a *shtar*, but the *gemara* does not take that path. Instead, the entire focus is a precise technical question: how far must the boat actually be moved?

Rav holds: *kivan shemashach kol shehu kanah* — once the buyer has displaced the boat even a small amount, even a single centimeter, the *kinyan* takes effect.

### Shmuel's Full Displacement

Shmuel disagrees: *lo kanah ad sheyimshoch es kulah* — the buyer does not acquire until he has pulled the entire boat completely out of its original position, so that no part of it remains where any part of it had been before.

### Boat Movement Example

To clarify: according to Rav, moving the boat even slightly — displacing it a negligible distance — is sufficient *meshicha*. According to Shmuel, the boat must be moved its full length, so that its new position shares no overlap with its original position. The boat, being an inanimate object, moves as a unified whole; every part of it shifts together whenever it moves at all.

### Animal Movement Tannaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי – כֵּיצַד בִּמְסִירָה? אֲחָזָהּ בְּטַלְפָּהּ, בִּשְׂעָרָהּ, בְּאוּכָּף שֶׁעָלֶיהָ, בִּשְׁלִיף שֶׁעָלֶיהָ, בִּפְרוּמְבְּיָא שֶׁבְּפִיהָ, בְּזוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארָהּ – קְנָאָהּ. כֵּיצַד בִּמְשִׁיכָה? קוֹרֵא לָהּ וְהִיא בָּאָה, אוֹ שֶׁהִכִּישָׁהּ בְּמַקֵּל וְרָצְתָה לְפָנָיו – כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָקְרָה יָד וָרֶגֶל, קְנָאָהּ. רַבִּי אַחַי וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי אַחָא אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁתְּהַלֵּךְ מְלֹא קוֹמָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this dispute **is parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: **How** is an animal acquired **through passing?** If the buyer **grasped it by its hoof,** or **by its hair,** or **by the saddle that is on it,** or **by the load that is on it,** or **by the bit [*perumbiyya*] in its mouth,** or **by the bell on its neck,** he has **acquired it. How** is an animal acquired **through pulling?** If **he calls it and it comes, or** if **he hits it with a stick and it runs before him, once it lifts a foreleg and a hind leg** from where it was standing, he has **acquired it. Rabbi Aḥai, and some say Rabbi Aḥa, says:** It is not enough if the animal lifts its hooves. Rather, one does not acquire it **until it walks its full length,** i.e., it moves both of its forelegs and both of its hindlegs.

The *gemara* asks whether this dispute mirrors an earlier Tannaitic debate. A *Tosefta* in Kiddushin enumerates how one performs *mesirah* for large animals: taking hold of its hoof (*achaza b'talphah*), its hair or tail (*besearah*), the saddle (*u'ukaf she'aleha*), the saddlebag (*beshalif she'aleha*), the bit in its mouth (*bafrombaya shebepiha*), or the bell around its neck (*bezog shebetzavara*) — any physical contact with the animal or its appurtenances constitutes *mesirah*. It is worth pausing here on the concept of *mesirah* itself. *Mesirah* — literally "handing over" — cannot be performed on a *hefker* object, because there must be a transferor who hands it over. According to one opinion in *Tosafos*, this requires that the seller or giver literally places the object in the buyer's hand; another opinion holds that it suffices for the transferor to instruct the buyer verbally to take hold, after which the buyer's act of seizing constitutes the *mesirah*. Either way, the critical distinction is that unlike *hagbahah* or *meshicha* — where the physical act in itself demonstrates ownership, as when one lifts something up or pulls it toward oneself — *mesirah* is not self-explanatory as a physical act. Holding a saddle or a hoof does not by itself demonstrate ownership. What gives the act its legal significance is the context of transfer: it is the handing-over that is symbolically meaningful, not merely the physical contact.

Returning to the Tannaitic debate on *meshicha* for animals: the *Tosefta* in Kiddushin asks *keitzad bimeshicha* — how does one perform *meshicha*? One method is calling the animal and having it come (*koreh lah vehia ba'ah*); another is striking it with a stick so it runs forward (*o shehikisha bamakel veratzta lefanav*). The key ruling is: *kivan she'akra yad varegel kena'ah* — once the animal has lifted a foreleg and a hind leg, the *kinyan* is complete. Rabbi Achai (some say Rabbi Acha) disagrees: *ad shetehalech melo komatah* — it must travel its full height, or presumably its full body length. This appears to map directly onto the dispute of Rav and Shmuel.

### Boat vs. Animal Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַב דְּאָמַר כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי אַחָא? אָמַר לְךָ רַב: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי אַחָא – עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא אֶלָּא בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים; דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּעָקְרָה יָד וָרֶגֶל – בְּדוּכְתַּהּ קָיְימָא; אֲבָל סְפִינָה, כֵּיוָן דְּנָדָה בַּהּ פּוּרְתָּא – נָדָה לַהּ כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shall we say** that **Rav, who** holds that a buyer can acquire the ship even by moving it only a minimal distance, **states** his ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna*** that it is enough for the animal to lift two legs; **and Shmuel, who** holds that the entire ship must be moved, **states** his ruling **in accordance** with the opinion of **Rabbi Aḥa** that the animal must move its full length? The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Rav** could have **said to you: I state** my ruling **even** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Aḥa,** since **Rabbi Aḥa states** his opinion **only with regard to animals, as although it lifted a foreleg and a hind leg, it stands in its place. But** in the case of **a ship, once a bit of it moves, all of it moves,** and therefore the buyer acquires it.

The *gemara* initially suggests that Rav follows the *tanna kamma* and Shmuel follows Rabbi Achai, but then rejects this framing. Each *amora* explains why the boat and the animal are not equivalent, even if they hold different positions regarding one of them.

Rav says: *amar lach Rav ana d'amri afilu l'Rabbi Acha* — I, who require only minimal displacement for a boat, could agree even to Rabbi Achai that an animal must be fully displaced. The reason: when an animal lifts a foreleg and a hind leg, *beduchta kaima* — it is essentially still where it was. The two legs that moved do not mean the body has shifted; the other legs remain planted, and a large portion of the animal's mass has not moved at all. Therefore a more substantial displacement is required. But a boat is different: *kivan denad purta nad kulah* — when a boat moves even a little, every single part of the boat has been displaced from its original position. The boat moves as a rigid whole. Therefore even minimal movement suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא – עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא אֶלָּא בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים, דְּכֵיוָן דְּמִיעַקְרָא יָד וָרֶגֶל – אִידַּךְ לְמִיעְקַר קָיְימָא; אֲבָל סְפִינָה, אִי מָשֵׁיךְ לַהּ כּוּלַּהּ – אִין, אִי לָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** could **say: I stated** my ruling **even in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna*. The first *tanna* states** his opinion **only with regard to animals, as once** an animal has **lifted a foreleg and a hind leg, the other** legs **stand** ready **to be lifted. But** with regard to **a ship, if he pulls the entire** ship, **yes,** he acquires it, but **if** he does **not** pull the whole ship, he does **not** acquire it.

Shmuel says the opposite: *ana d'amri afilu k'tanna kamma* — I, who require full displacement for a boat, would agree that for an animal even the lifting of a foreleg and hind leg suffices. Why? *De'keivan d'me'akrai yad varegel, idach me'akrai kai* — once the animal has begun to step, lifting one foreleg and one hind leg, the biological momentum of the living creature means it will continue to move; the step will complete itself. For a living being, beginning the motion is as good as completing it. But a boat is inanimate: *ei mashchei kulah in, ei lo lo* — if you moved the whole thing, yes; if not, no.

The underlying conceptual axis is thus clear: the dispute is about what counts as meaningful displacement for the purpose of *meshicha*, and the answer differs based on the nature of the object. An animal's life-force (*chayut*) means that initiating movement is effectively completing it. An inanimate boat moves uniformly but must be moved completely. These considerations then determine where each *amora* draws the line for each case.

## Debt Acquisition Intro

### What Are Otiot?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי – דְּתַנְיָא: סְפִינָה נִקְנֵית בִּמְשִׁיכָה. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: סְפִינָה וְאוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְשִׁיכָה וּבִשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another suggestion: **Let us say** that the dispute of Rav and Shmuel is **parallel to** the dispute between **these *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A ship is acquired by pulling. Rabbi Natan says: A ship and letters,** i.e., the content of a promissory note, **are acquired by pulling** the document **and by** means of **a bill** of sale.

Having addressed boat acquisition, the *gemara* now pivots to a far more abstract subject, introduced by a *baraita* in Kiddushin. The *baraita* states: *sefina nikneis bimeshicha* — a boat is acquired by *meshicha*. Rabbi Natan then adds: *sefina v'otiot niknot bimeshicha uv'shtar* — a boat and *otiot* are acquired by *meshicha* and *shtar*. The word *otiot*, literally "letters," requires explanation. The *gemara* will understand it to mean a debt — specifically, the legal claim represented by a promissory document. If Reuven holds a *shtar* documenting that Shimon owes him a thousand *zuz*, and Reuven wishes to sell that debt to a third party, the question is how such a transfer is effected.

### Abstract Debt Problem

This is a genuinely difficult conceptual problem. The money is owed personally to Reuven. What exactly is being transferred? One might propose transferring the liens — the encumbrances on Shimon's property that accompany the debt. *Tosafos* in fact raises this question. But the *gemara* does not pursue this avenue, and *Tosafos* offers two reasons why not. First, liens only exist in the context of a personal debt obligation; they cannot be detached and transferred independently, because they exist only as an extension of the underlying obligation. Second, and separately, one does not own enough of the encumbered property to effect a transfer of the lien — one's interest in the property is too abstract a quasi-ownership to be alienated. In any case, a debt can exist without any lien at all, so the mechanism of debt-sale must be conceptualized independently of liens.

There are two ways to conceptualize what is transferred when a debt is sold. One approach holds that the debt remains fundamentally personal — Shimon still technically owes Reuven — but a kind of direct-deposit arrangement is created, such that any payment flows immediately to the purchaser. Under this view, Reuven retains the theoretical ability to forgive the debt even after selling it, leaving the buyer without recourse against Shimon (though potentially with a claim against Reuven in *grama* or *garmi*). This is associated with the position of Shmuel. The second approach treats the debt more abstractly: there is an obligation that exists as a transferable entity, and it can be conveyed outright to a new creditor, who then stands in the original lender's place. This conceptual ambiguity about what a debt *is* underlies the subsequent halakhic debate about how to transfer it.

Because a debt is abstract, the *gemara* refers to it as *otiot* — letters, i.e., what is written in the document. The physical document itself — the parchment and ink — is just a piece of paper. As the *gemara* will note, one could use it as a bottle stopper. The *kinyan* of *meshicha* applied to the document would acquire only the paper. What the purchaser actually wants is not the paper but what it represents: the debt, expressed in its *otiot*. The question, then, is what mode of transfer is adequate to convey not the paper but the obligation it records.

### Physical vs. Symbolic Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹתִיּוֹת מַאן דְּכַר שְׁמַיְיהוּ? חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: סְפִינָה נִקְנֵית בִּמְשִׁיכָה, וְאוֹתִיּוֹת בִּמְסִירָה. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: סְפִינָה וְאוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְשִׁיכָה וּבִשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the *baraita*: **Letters** in promissory notes, **who mentioned** anything about **them?** Why would Rabbi Natan speak about promissory notes, which are not discussed by the first *tanna*? The Gemara answers: The *baraita* **is incomplete, and this** is what **it is teaching: A ship is acquired by pulling, and letters,** i.e., the content of a promissory note, **are acquired by** merely **transferring** the document, not through pulling. **Rabbi Natan says: A ship and letters are acquired by pulling and** also **by** means of **a bill** of sale.

The *gemara* returns to the *baraita* and notes that *otiot* seem to appear without introduction — Rabbi Natan is responding to a position the *tanna kamma* never stated. The *gemara* therefore reads the *baraita* as incomplete: *chisuri mechasra vehachi katani* — there are missing words, and the text must be reconstructed. The *tanna kamma* is understood to have stated a position about debts — that a debt is acquired by *mesirah* — and Rabbi Natan then responds to that position. This reading is justified because, as the *gemara* will show, there is another *baraita* that explicitly attributes the *mesirah* position to a Tannaitic authority, and we therefore know the opinion exists to be read into the *tanna kamma* here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁטָר לִסְפִינָה לְמָה לִי? מִטַּלְטְלֵי הִיא! אֶלָּא לָאו הָכִי קָתָנֵי: סְפִינָה נִקְנֵית בִּמְשִׁיכָה, וְאוֹתִיּוֹת בִּמְסִירָה. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: סְפִינָה בִּמְשִׁיכָה, וְאוֹתִיּוֹת בִּשְׁטָר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **a bill** of sale **for a ship?** A ship **is movable property,** which is acquired not by means of giving a bill of sale, but through other acts of acquisition. **Rather, is it not** correct to say that **this** is what the *baraita* **is teaching: A ship is acquired by pulling, and letters** of credit **by passing. Rabbi Natan says: A ship** is acquired **by pulling, and letters,** i.e. the contents of a promissory note, are acquired either through pulling or **by** means of **a bill** of sale.

After further consideration, the *gemara* realizes that even Rabbi Natan's position as stated needs re-reading: if the *shtar* element in *meshicha uv'shtar* is attributed to the *sefina*, that makes no sense — one does not use a *shtar* to acquire cattle, and a boat is treated analogously. The *shtar* must therefore pertain to the debt, not the boat. The *gemara* then rereads the *baraita* to yield: both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Natan agree that a *sefina* is acquired by *meshicha*; they debate only how to transfer a debt, where the *tanna kamma* says *mesirah* and Rabbi Natan says a *shtar* is required.

This leads to the key conceptual question: why is *mesirah* a better candidate than *meshicha* or *hagbahah* for transferring a debt? The answer lies in what each act signifies. *Meshicha* and *hagbahah* are self-contained physical demonstrations of control: I have pulled this toward me, I have lifted this up — the physical act itself, without any further context, shows that I control the object. That works well for acquiring a lost object or a *hefker* item. But when applied to a *shtar*, *meshicha* or *hagbahah* demonstrates only that I control the piece of paper, not that I have acquired the debt it records.

*Mesirah*, by contrast, is not self-sufficient as a physical act — it derives its significance from the context of transfer. Holding a saddle does not demonstrate ownership by itself; what makes it meaningful is that it occurs within an act of handing over. For the same reason, *mesirah* cannot be performed on a *hefker* item, since there is no transferor to confer context on the act. Applied to a *shtar*, then, *mesirah* says more than *meshicha*: the owner of the document is handing it over, and given what is written in it, that handover can symbolically represent the transfer of the underlying debt, not merely the transfer of the paper.

## Rabbi vs. Sages Debate

### Rabbi's Mesira Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי – דְּתַנְיָא: אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין כָּתַב וְלֹא מָסַר, בֵּין מָסַר וְלָא כָּתַב – לֹא קָנָה, עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב וְיִמְסוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to this interpretation, the first *tanna* and Rabbi Natan disagree **with regard to the dispute** between **these *tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Letters,** i.e., the contents of a promissory note, **are acquired by** merely **transferring** the document to the buyer; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: Whether one wrote** a bill of sale **but did not transfer** the promissory note to the buyer, or **whether he transferred** the promissory note **but did not write** a bill of sale, the buyer **does not acquire** the documents **until** the seller both **writes** a bill of sale **and transfers** the promissory note.

The *gemara* now identifies the debate about debt acquisition with a broader Tannaitic dispute. A *baraita* states: *otiot niknot bimesirah divrei Rabbi* — debts are acquired by *mesirah* alone, this is the position of Rabbi (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi).

The Sages say: *bein katav v'lo masar, bein masar v'lo katav, lo kanah* — whether one wrote a new transfer document without handing over the original, or handed over the original without writing a new document, the buyer does not acquire; *ad sheyichtov veyimsor* — both acts are required together.

### Why Both Acts Needed

The logic of each position is as follows. Rabbi holds that handing over the original *shtar* — the document stating that the debtor owes the money — is symbolically sufficient to convey the debt, since the owner is physically transferring the very instrument that represents the obligation. The Sages hold that this alone is not enough: for *mesirah* of a *shtar* to successfully transfer the debt rather than merely the paper, it must be accompanied by a second document explicitly stating that the creditor is transferring all his rights in the debt and in the debtor's encumbered property to the purchaser. Without this clarifying document, the symbolic act is insufficient.

To be precise about what the Sages require: two documents are involved. The original *shtar* — e.g., "Shimon owes Reuven a thousand *zuz*" — is physically handed over (*mesirah*), and simultaneously a new *shtar* is written stating the terms of the transfer. Rabbi requires only the handing over of the original. The Sages require both the *mesirah* of the original and the *ketivah* of the new one.

### Shtar's Limited Scope

Why does a new *shtar* alone not suffice to transfer the debt, without the accompanying *mesirah*? The technical answer is that a *shtar* as an instrument of *kinyan* has a narrowly defined scope: it operates for land and for acquiring or altering the status of persons (as in *kiddushin* or *gittin*). It does not operate for *metaltelin*, and a fortiori it cannot serve as the instrument to transfer an abstract debt. Therefore, the *shtar* written by the Sages is not functioning as a *shtar kinyan* — it is not the vehicle of transfer. The vehicle of transfer is the *mesirah* of the original document. What the new *shtar* does is serve as a clarifying instrument: it establishes the context that makes the *mesirah* meaningful, declaring explicitly that the handover is intended to convey the debt and not merely the paper. Alternatively, one might frame it symbolically: if the creditor writes a document stating he is transferring the debt but continues to hold onto the original *shtar*, the transfer lacks conviction; the physical handing over of the original, accompanying the written declaration, reinforces and completes the act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא – כְּרַבִּי? סְפִינָה נָמֵי תִּיקְּנֵי בִּמְסִירָה! דְּתַנְיָא: סְפִינָה נִקְנֵית בִּמְסִירָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא קָנָה
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with which** opinion **did you interpret** the opinion of the first *tanna* of the aforementioned *baraita*? If it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, then **let a ship be acquired also by passing,** not only through pulling, as stated in the following *baraita*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A ship is acquired by passing;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say:** The buyer **does not acquire** it

The *gemara* then proceeds to identify which position in the earlier *baraita* (the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Natan) corresponds to which position here (Rabbi and the Sages). If the *tanna kamma* who says *otiot bemesirah* is identified with Rabbi, one immediately faces a difficulty: elsewhere a *baraita* states explicitly that Rabbi holds *sefina nikneis bimesirah* — Rabbi says a boat is acquired by *mesirah*. So if this *tanna kamma* is Rabbi, why does he speak only of *meshicha* for the boat and not mention *mesirah*? And conversely, the Sages in that second *baraita* say a boat requires *meshicha* or *sechirat makom* (renting the space in which the boat sits) — but neither of those, as we will see, works in a *reshut harabim*, which leads to the *gemara*'s next major discussion.

---

## Contexts & Braitot

### Identifying Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁיִּמְשְׁכֶנָּה אוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּשְׂכּוֹר אֶת מְקוֹמָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **until he pulls it, or until he rents its place.** How, then, can the opinion of the first *tanna* of the *baraita* be ascribed to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi?

The *gemara* now undertakes to reconcile the various *braitot* and identify which Tannaitic authorities hold which positions. The *baraita* that has the *tanna kamma* saying *sefina bemeshicha* and Rabbi Natan saying *sefina v'otiot bemeshicha uv'shtar* presents an apparent difficulty when mapped onto the second *baraita* featuring Rabbi and the Sages. If the *tanna kamma* of the first *baraita* corresponds to Rabbi, why does he describe the boat as acquired only by *meshicha*, when Rabbi elsewhere says *mesirah* also works for a boat? The *gemara* will resolve this by reference to context.

### Rabbi on Boats

As the *gemara* establishes, Rabbi's position in the second *baraita* is: *sefina nikneis bemesirah divrei Rabbi* — Rabbi says a boat is acquired by *mesirah*. The Sages disagree: *lo kanah ad sheyimshachenah* — one does not acquire until one has dragged it, or alternatively, until one has rented the location where the boat is situated (*sechirat makom*). Rabbi thus accepts *mesirah* as a valid mode for boat acquisition; the Sages do not.

### Context Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, כָּאן בְּסִימְטָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult; here,** where Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi states that a ship is acquired through passing, he is referring to a ship situated **in the public domain.** Since a ship in the public domain cannot be acquired through pulling, which must be performed in a domain that is in one's possession, it is acquired through passing. By contrast, **there,** in the first *baraita*, the ship is situated **in an alleyway [*simta*],** which is not the public domain, as both parties have the right to keep their possessions there. A ship in this location must be acquired through pulling.

The resolution is: *kan birshut harabbim, kan besimta* — one *baraita* speaks of a boat in the *reshut harabim* (the public domain), and the other speaks of a boat in a *simta* (a semi-private alley). This distinction is the key to understanding the entire discussion. *Meshicha* works by demonstrating physical control over an object. But in the *reshut harabim* — a busy public thoroughfare — the act of dragging an object toward oneself does not meaningfully demonstrate control. An object left on the ground in a crowded public space is not securely in anyone's domain; dragging it a step or two does not establish dominion in any symbolically convincing sense. *Meshicha* therefore does not effect a *kinyan* in the *reshut harabim*. In a *simta*, by contrast, the semi-private character of the space means that pulling an object toward oneself does carry the significance of establishing control, and *meshicha* is valid there.

*Mesirah*, on the other hand, is not about physical control at all — it is about the symbolic act of transfer. Like *chalifin* (the *kinyan sudar*, where the buyer hands the seller a cloth or utensil and by that act acquires the purchased item), *mesirah* works through the signification of transfer rather than through a demonstration of physical dominion. For the same reason, *chalifin* works in the *reshut harabim* — its efficacy is independent of the location because it is not about controlling the space. Accordingly, *mesirah* functions precisely in the *reshut harabim*, where *meshicha* does not.

The first *baraita* (*tanna kamma*: *sefina bemeshicha*) therefore speaks of a case in the *simta*, where *meshicha* is valid. Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi, identified as the *tanna kamma*, is not denying that *mesirah* works — he is simply describing the *simta* context, where *meshicha* is the applicable mode. The second *baraita* (*divrei Rabbi: sefina bemesirah*) speaks of the *reshut harabim*, where *mesirah* is the relevant mode and *meshicha* is not available. There is no contradiction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתָּא לְהָא בָּתְרָיְיתָא – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים? אֵימָא סֵיפָא – וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיִּמְשְׁכֶנָּה. וְאִי בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, מִמַּאן אָגַר? וְתוּ, מְשִׁיכָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מִי קָנְיָא?! וְהָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מְסִירָה קוֹנָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וּבְחָצֵר שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם; מְשִׁיכָה קוֹנָה בְּסִימְטָא, וּבְחָצֵר שֶׁהִיא שֶׁל שְׁנֵיהֶם; וְהַגְבָּהָה קוֹנָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **To what** case **did you interpret that last** *baraita* to be referring? It was interpreted as referring **to the public domain.** If so, **say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **And the Rabbis say** that the buyer **does not acquire** it **until he pulls it** or until he rents its place. The Gemara asks: **But if** the ship is situated **in the public domain, from whom can he rent** the place? **And furthermore, does pulling in the public domain effect acquisition? But don't Abaye and Rava both say** with regard to the different methods of acquisition: **Passing effects acquisition in the public domain or in a courtyard that does not belong to either of** the parties; **pulling effects acquisition in an alleyway or in a courtyard that belongs to both of** the parties; **and lifting effects acquisition in every place,** even in the seller's domain.

One might ask: in the *simta*, if *meshicha* works, why can one not also use *mesirah*? This question generates a fascinating discussion among the *rishonim*. The *Rashbam* and several others hold that when a more robust mode of *kinyan* is available — *meshicha* in a *simta* — only that mode works; the weaker or more symbolic mode (*mesirah*) does not. Rabbeinu Tam sharply rejects this principle: are *kinyanim* like *mitzvot*, where one must do the most preferred form? If *mesirah* works, it works — the availability of *meshicha* does not negate *mesirah*. This debate is left open by the *gemara*, though the basic resolution of the textual contradiction stands.

A further problem emerges: if the second *baraita* is set in the *reshut harabim*, how do the Sages there say that *meshicha* and *sechirat makom* work? *Meshicha* does not function in the *reshut harabim*, as just established, and *sechirat makom* — renting the space in which the boat sits — is impossible in a *reshut harabim*, since one cannot rent out a public thoroughfare that one does not own.

### Sages on Boats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״עַד שֶׁיִּמְשְׁכֶנָּה״ נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר; וּמַאי ״עַד שֶׁיִּשְׂכּוֹר אֶת מְקוֹמָהּ״ דְּקָאָמַר – הָכִי קָאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיִּמְשְׁכֶנָּה מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לְסִימְטָא; וְאִם רְשׁוּת בְּעָלִים הִיא – לָא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיִּשְׂכּוֹר אֶת מְקוֹמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What** does the *baraita* mean **when it says: Until he pulls it, and what** does it mean **when it says: Until he rents its place? This** is what it **is saying:** The buyer does not acquire the ship **until he pulls it from the public domain into an alleyway. And if** the ship **is** located in **the domain** of some other **owner,** the buyer **does not acquire** it **until he rents its place** from the owner.

The Sages' position requires a contextual reinterpretation, provided by the next section of the *gemara*.

---

## Kinyanim Principles

### Context & Meaning

The underlying principle at work throughout this discussion is that the meaning and validity of a *kinyan* act is not intrinsic to the act itself but is determined by context. The same physical motion — dragging an object — is a valid *kinyan* in one context and entirely meaningless in another. This is not arbitrary: *kinyan* acts signify ownership and control, and for that signification to be operative, it must be intelligible in the setting where it occurs. Dragging a briefcase across Times Square does not demonstrate meaningful control in the way that pulling an object to oneself in a quiet alley does. *Mesirah*, because it signifies not physical control but the act of transfer, is independent of this spatial dimension and retains its meaning regardless of setting. This is also why *hagbahah* — lifting an object off the ground — works universally: the act of physically lifting something up places it entirely under one's bodily control, and that is unambiguous in any location.

---

## Contexts & Braitot (continued)

### Abaye & Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא – דְּאָמְרִי כְּרַבִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say** that **Abaye and Rava state** their opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, not that of the Rabbis? The *baraita* indicates that only Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi maintains that one can acquire ownership by means of passing in the public domain.

Abaye and Rava together articulate the rule precisely: *mesirah konah birshut harabim uvechatzer she'eino shel sheneihem* — *mesirah* effects acquisition in the *reshut harabim* and in a courtyard belonging to neither party. *Meshicha konah besimta uvechatzer shehi shel sheneihem* — *meshicha* effects acquisition in a *simta* or in a courtyard shared by both parties. In the latter settings, the buyer has some degree of right or access to the space, and the act of pulling the object toward himself in that semi-controlled environment carries the meaning of establishing dominion.

---

## Kinyanim Principles (continued)

### Hagbaha Universal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַגְבָּהָה קוֹנָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **and lifting effects acquisition in every place,** even in the seller's domain.

*Hagbahah* — lifting — is categorically different from both *meshicha* and *mesirah*: *hagbahah koneh bechol makom*, it effects acquisition everywhere. The act of lifting an object off the ground places it entirely under the lifter's bodily control in a way that is unambiguous regardless of setting. Even if the buyer is standing in the seller's courtyard, lifting the object demonstrates full physical control, and the *kinyan* takes effect. The seller's desire to transfer and the buyer's act of lifting together complete the transaction without any dependence on the character of the surrounding space.

---

## Contexts & Braitot (continued again)

### Sages' Solution

The *gemara* resolves this: *ani d'amar lo katav v'lo masar, lo kanah ad sheyichkov v'yimsor* — establishing both the principle and the specific case-by-case application: *kan birshut harabim* — the context of *reshut harabim* determines which *kinyan* is viable. This leads to clarification about the Sages' position and how the different contexts make their statements coherent.

---

## Kinyanim Principles (continued once more)

### Chalifin Alternative

This leads naturally to a final observation about *chalifin*. If a boat sits in the *reshut harabim* and the Sages hold that neither *mesirah* nor *meshicha* within that domain is valid, what can a buyer do if dragging the boat to a *simta* is not practicable — say, the boat is on the open sea? The answer is *chalifin*: the buyer hands the seller a cloth or utensil, and by that symbolic exchange the boat is acquired. *Chalifin* works universally because, like *mesirah*, its efficacy is not a function of physical control but of symbolic convention — it is, at its core, the legal formalization of the parties' mutual commitment to the transaction. Indeed, this makes *chalifin* conceptually the most abstract of the *kinyanim*: whereas *meshicha* and *hagbahah* are about demonstrating physical mastery over the object, and *mesirah* is about the symbolic handover of the object itself, *chalifin* entirely abstracts away from the object being sold. The buyer hands the seller something unrelated, and by that convention acquires the purchased item. It is, in essence, a formalized handshake. As such, it succeeds precisely where the physically grounded modes of acquisition cannot reach.
