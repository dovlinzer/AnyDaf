# Bava Metzia 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Picking Up Lost Object

### Rami Bar Chama Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ – קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama says: That is to say:** In a case of **one who** performs an act of acquisition by **lifting a found item on behalf of another, the other** person, i.e., the latter, **acquires** ownership of the item.

The Gemara at the eighth line of the daf opens with the statement of Rami Bar Chama: *hamagbia metzia lachaveiro kana chaveiro* — if one person picks up a lost object on behalf of another person, the other person acquires it. This teaching, introduced after an extended discussion about the *shtar* and how to divide it, returns us to the *mishna*'s ruling of *yachloku*.

### Zachin L'Adam Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ לֹא קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ, תֵּעָשֶׂה זוֹ כְּמִי שֶׁמּוּנַּחַת עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, וְזוֹ כְּמִי שֶׁמּוּנַּחַת עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, וְלֹא יִקְנֶה לֹא זֶה וְלֹא זֶה. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ – קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama explains his inference: **As, if it enters your mind** that if one lifts a found item for another **the other does not acquire** the item, this garment has not been acquired by either of the two litigants, as each prevents the other's acquisition. If that were the case, **this** part of the garment, held by one of them, **would be considered as though** it is still **lying on the ground, and that** part of the garment, held by the other one, would be considered **as though** it is still **lying on the ground, and neither this** one **nor that** one **acquires** it; if a third party takes it, it is his. **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** it that in a case of **one who** performs an act of acquisition by **lifting a found item on behalf of another, the other** person **acquires** the item?

At first glance, one might wonder where the *chiddush* lies. There is, after all, a well-established general principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* — one may act on behalf of another person for their benefit even without their presence. If someone receives a gift intended for Reuven, he may take possession of it on Reuven's behalf without any difficulty. But the case of a *metzia* is different in a critical way. When you pick up a lost object for Reuven, you are simultaneously excluding everyone else from being able to take it. You are actively disadvantaging third parties. One might therefore think that while you may do this for yourself, you are not empowered to do it on behalf of someone else at the expense of others. Rami Bar Chama derives from our *mishna* that the principle does in fact apply — *hamagbia metzia lachaveiro kana chaveiro* — and one may acquire a found object on another's behalf.

### Two People, Same Time

The inference works as follows. If *hamagbia metzia lachaveiro kana chaveiro* were not true — if you could not pick something up on another's behalf — consider the case where two people simultaneously pick up a lost object. Each person picked up only his half of the object; neither performed a complete act of *hagbahah* on the whole thing. As Rami Bar Chama frames it: *teiasei zu kemunachat agabei karka vezu kemunachat agabei karka velo yikneh lo zeh velo zeh* — each person's other half should be treated as if it were still lying on the ground, and neither should acquire anything. A third party could then come and take the object entirely. Yet the *mishna* implies that when two people pick up an object simultaneously, they each acquire half. How does this work? It must be that when I pick up my half, I am simultaneously picking it up on his behalf, and when he picks up his half, he is simultaneously picking it up on my behalf. Each person's act of *hagbahah* on his own half is combined with the other person's act on his behalf, so that from each person's perspective a complete picking-up has occurred. Naturally, neither person intends pure altruism — each wants the object for himself — but because the only way the *kinyan* will function is if each is understood to be acting for the other as well, we assume that is indeed what is occurring. From this, Rami Bar Chama concludes that *hamagbia metzia lachaveiro kana chaveiro* is operative.

## Rava's Migu Principle

### Rava's Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ – לֹא קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ. וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: מִגּוֹ דְּזָכֵי לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ זָכֵי נָמֵי לְחַבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This is not a proof, as **actually I** could **say to you** that in a case of **one who** performs an act of acquisition by **lifting a found item** solely **on behalf of another, the other** person **does not acquire** the item. **And here** in the mishna, **this is the reason** the two litigants acquire the garment: **Since** each of the litigants **acquires** part of the garment **for himself, he also acquires** the other part **for the other** one.

Rava challenges Rami Bar Chama's broad formulation. He argues: *leolam eima lach hamagbia metzia lachaveiro lo kana chaveiro* — one may never straightforwardly acquire a *metzia* solely on another person's behalf. The reason the simultaneous pickup works is not because of a general principle of *zachin l'adam shelo befanav* applied to found objects, but rather because of a more limited mechanism: *migo deizachi lenafshei zachinei nami lechavrei* — since you are acquiring for yourself, you can simultaneously acquire for another as well. This is a narrower claim. The *migu* principle allows one to piggyback another's acquisition onto one's own act of acquisition. But it does not establish that a person standing outside his own self-interest can acquire a *metzia* solely for someone else, where the concern about disadvantaging third parties is most acute.

### Theft vs. Metzia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּדַּע שֶׁאִילּוּ אָמַר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: "צֵא וּגְנוֹב לִי", וְגָנַב – פָּטוּר. וְשׁוּתָּפִין שֶׁגָּנְבוּ – חַיָּיבִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן: מִגּוֹ דְּזָכֵי לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ זָכֵי נָמֵי לְחַבְרֵיהּ. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava adds: **Know** that one who acquires an item for himself can acquire part of it for another, **as, if** someone **says to his agent: Go out and steal** an item **for me, and** the agent **stole** that item, the one who sent him is **exempt** from liability, due to the principle that there is no agency for sin; **but partners who stole** an item together are both **liable** even if only one of them actually lifted the item. **What is the reason** that they are both liable? Is it **not because we say** that **since** the one **who** lifted the item **acquires** part of it **for himself, he also acquires** the other part **for the other** one, his partner? The Gemara concludes: **Learn from it** that this principle is correct.

Rava marshals evidence that the *migu* distinction is real and operative. He raises the case of *ein shaliach lidvar aveirah*: if one instructs a *shaliach* to go steal an object from Reuven and the agent does so, the sender is exempt. Whatever acts of *kinyan* attach to the theft do not extend to the one who commissioned it, because the agent's act is illegal and cannot confer acquisition upon another. This illustrates that when someone is acting not for himself but purely on another's behalf — and in this case doing something unlawful — the acquisition does not transfer.

By contrast, the case of *shutfin sheganvu* — two partners who together steal a sofa, each carrying part of it out — is different. There, *veshutfin sheganvu chayavim*: both are liable. Again, neither partner lifted the entire object; each lifted only half. Yet both are considered to have stolen it. Why? *Migo de'zachie l'nafsheih zachie nami l'chavreih* — because each is acquiring for himself, he simultaneously acquires on behalf of the other. This mirrors the *metzia* case precisely: just as two partners stealing together creates mutual acquisition through the *migu* mechanism, so too two people simultaneously picking up a found object creates mutual acquisition. But Rava emphasizes: this does not prove that one person standing alone could pick up a *metzia* solely for the benefit of Reuven without any interest of his own, because the *shaliach* case shows that it doesn't work when a person acts purely on another's behalf. *Shema mina* — Rava concludes that the two situations must be kept distinct.

### Cheresh & Pikeach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ אָמְרִינַן מִגּוֹ: חֵרֵשׁ וּפִקֵּחַ שֶׁהִגְבִּיהוּ מְצִיאָה, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁקָּנָה חֵרֵשׁ – קָנָה פִּקֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in continuation of his statement: **Now that you said** that **we say: Since [*miggo*]** one acquires part of an item for himself he can also acquire the other part for another, another *halakha* can be derived: In a case of **a deaf-mute and** a mentally **competent** person **who lifted a found item** simultaneously, **since** the **deaf-mute acquires** his part of the item, the mentally **competent** person also **acquires** his portion.

Rava now develops the implications of the *migu* principle by presenting a striking case: *cheresh u'pikeach she'higbihu metziah* — a *cheresh* (a deaf-mute, who lacks legal standing *min haTorah*) and a *pikeach* (a fully competent person) simultaneously pick up a found object.

The halachic status of the *cheresh* requires brief explanation. Within the framework of the Gemara, a *cheresh* is not considered a *bar da'at* — a person with full legal agency — and therefore cannot effect a real *kinyan*. The Rabbis, however, instituted a special rule for the *cheresh* out of *darchei shalom* — for the sake of social peace. If a *cheresh* finds something and takes it, we treat it as if it is his, not because a genuine *kinyan* has occurred, but because to do otherwise would lead to injustice and conflict. Critically, this *takkanah* was instituted only for the *cheresh* himself — it allows him to acquire for himself; it does not give him the power to acquire on behalf of others.

Now in the case where *cheresh* and *pikeach* pick up an object together, neither has performed a complete act of *hagbahah*. For the *kinyan* to work, each must be understood as acting on behalf of the other. The Gemara initially proposes: *mitoch she'kanah cheresh kanah pikeach* — because the *cheresh* acquires for himself (through the Rabbinic *takkanah*), he can piggyback the *pikeach*'s acquisition onto his own via *migu*.

### Pikeach's Failure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא חֵרֵשׁ קָנָה – דְּקָא מַגְבַּהּ לֵיהּ בֶּן דַּעַת. אֶלָּא פִּקֵּחַ בְּמַאי קָנָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the **deaf-mute acquires** his portion of the found item, **as** a **mentally competent** person **lifted it for him;** since the mentally competent person acquired his own part, he also acquired the other part for the deaf-mute. **But how does** the **mentally competent** person **acquire** his part? He needs the deaf-mute to acquire it for him, and a deaf-mute cannot acquire an item for another.

The Gemara immediately objects. *Bishlama cheresh kanah* — it is understandable why the *cheresh* acquires. The *pikeach* picks up his half for himself and simultaneously for the *cheresh*, and since the *pikeach* is a full *ben da'at*, his act of *hagbahah* on the *cheresh*'s behalf works just as it would for any other person. *Ella pikeach bemai kani* — but how does the *pikeach* acquire? For the *pikeach* to have a complete *hagbahah*, he needs the *cheresh*'s pickup to work on his behalf. But the *cheresh*'s Rabbinic *kinyan* was instituted only for the *cheresh* himself; it cannot be extended to create a valid *kinyan* on behalf of the *pikeach*. So from the *pikeach*'s perspective, only half the object was properly picked up.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵימָא: חֵרֵשׁ – קָנָה, פִּקֵּחַ – לָא קָנָה. וּמַאי מִגּוֹ? מִגּוֹ דִּשְׁנֵי חֵרְשִׁין בְּעָלְמָא קָנוּ, הַאי נָמֵי קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, say** Rava's statement differently: The **deaf-mute acquires** his part, but the mentally **competent** person **does not acquire** his part. **And what** is the principle of ***miggo*** from which Rava derives this *halakha*? It is not the principle that one who acquires a found item for himself can acquire part of it for another as well, but rather: **Since [*miggo*] in general two deaf-mutes** who pick up an item simultaneously **acquire** it, in **this** case **too,** the deaf-mute **acquires** it, even though the mentally competent person does not acquire it.

The Gemara therefore revises: *cheresh koneh pikeach lo koneh* — the *cheresh* acquires and the *pikeach* does not. For the *cheresh*, both halves have been "picked up": his own half via the Rabbinic dispensation, and the second half because the *pikeach* — a *ben da'at* — can genuinely pick it up for him. For the *pikeach*, however, the *cheresh*'s act cannot create a valid *kinyan* on his behalf, so the *pikeach* receives nothing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ, הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּקָא מַגְבַּהּ לֵיהּ אַדַּעְתָּא דְּחַבְרֵיהּ. הַאי אַדַּעְתָּא דִּידֵיהּ קָא מַגְבַּהּ לֵיהּ, אִיהוּ לָא קָנֵי – לְאַחֲרִינֵי מַקְנֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is **this** derivation? Even **if you say** that in a case of **one who** performs an act of acquisition by **lifting a found item on behalf of another, the other** person **acquires** it, as Rami bar Ḥama says, **this statement** applies only **where he lifts it with the intention of** having **the other person** acquire the item. But in **this** case, the mentally competent person **lifted** the item **with the intention of** acquiring it for **himself;** he had no intention of acquiring it for the deaf-mute. If **he does not acquire** the item for himself, **does he acquire** it **for others?**

But then the Gemara objects to this formulation as well. The *migu* principle only works because both parties have a self-interest in the arrangement. The *pikeach* is fundamentally picking up his half for himself; we then say he is also doing so for the *cheresh* because the *cheresh* is reciprocally doing so for him. But *hai ada'ata didei ka'magbeilah* — the *pikeach* is acting out of self-interest. If we now say that the *cheresh*'s pickup cannot work for the *pikeach* and the *pikeach* will get nothing, why would the *pikeach* pick up the object for the *cheresh*? *Ee lo kanei leachrini aknei?* — If I, the *pikeach*, am not going to benefit, am I going to be a philanthropist and acquire it for the *cheresh* alone? Certainly not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵימָא: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁלֹּא קָנָה פִּקֵּחַ – לֹא קָנָה חֵרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, say** that **since** the mentally **competent** person **does not acquire** any part of the found item, the **deaf-mute does not acquire** it either.

The consequence cuts further. If the *pikeach* is unwilling to pick up the object on behalf of the *cheresh* since there is nothing in it for him, then the *cheresh*'s half is not fully acquired either. We therefore conclude: *mitoch she'lo kanah pikeach lo kanah cheresh* — because the *pikeach* does not acquire, the *cheresh* does not acquire either. Neither of them owns the object.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, מַאי שְׁנָא מִשְּׁנֵי חֵרְשִׁין דְּעָלְמָא – הָתָם תַּקִּינוּ לְהוּ רַבָּנַן דְּלָא אָתֵי לְאִנְּצוֹיֵי. הָכָא מֵימָר אָמַר: פִּקֵּחַ לָא קָנֵי, אֲנָא אֶקְנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say:** In **what** way **is** this case **different from** the **general** case of an item that was found by **two deaf-mutes,** in which they both acquire it? The answer is that **there,** in that case, **the Sages instituted** an ordinance **for them** that they both acquire the item **so that they will not come to quarrel** with others who want to take the item from them due to the fact that a deaf-mute lacks the halakhic ability to acquire the item. **Here,** in the case of the deaf-mute and the mentally competent person, the deaf-mute **says** to himself: If even the mentally **competent** person **does not acquire** the item, **can I acquire** it? Therefore, in that case, he will not quarrel if others take the item from him.

One might ask: how is this different from the case of two *charashim* who simultaneously pick up a *metzia*, where both are indeed considered to acquire? The answer is that the *rabanan* instituted their *takkanah* for *charashim* precisely so that they should not come to fight — *delo atei l'inyatuyei*. If two *charashim* are both treated by the Rabbinic dispensation as having individually valid acquisitions, fairness demands that we extend that dispensation to their joint pickup as well, just as we would for two *pikchin*. To allow a third party to grab the object from two *charashim* who jointly found it would be a glaring injustice that would breed conflict. By contrast, in the case of a *cheresh* and a *pikeach*, where the mechanism fails for the *pikeach*, the *cheresh* cannot reasonably claim injustice — the *pikeach* is in precisely the same position. There is no asymmetry that demands rectification, and therefore no need to extend the *takkanah* further.

## Rami Bar Chama's Source

### Tallit Case Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַדָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: דִּיּוּקֵיהּ דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵהֵיכָא? אִי נֵימָא מֵרֵישָׁא "שְׁנַיִם אוֹחֲזִין בְּטַלִּית", הָתָם הַאי קָאָמַר "כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי וַאֲנָא אַגְּבַּהְתַּהּ כּוּלַּהּ", וְהַאי אָמַר "כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי וַאֲנָא אַגְּבַּהְתַּהּ כּוּלַּהּ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Adda, said to Rav Ashi: From where** in the mishna **is Rami bar Ḥama's inference** drawn? **If we say** that he infers it **from the first clause** of the mishna, i.e., the case of **two** people **holding a garment,** isn't the case **there** one in which **this** one **says: All of it is mine, and I lifted the entire** garment; **and that** one **says: All of it is mine, and I lifted the entire** garment? How can the *halakha* where one acquires an item for another be inferred from that case?

The Gemara now turns to the question of exactly where in the *mishna* Rami Bar Chama finds the proof that two people picking up a *metzia* simultaneously both acquire. Rav Acha the son of Rav Ada raises the issue before Rav Ashi: *diyka de-Rami bar Chama me'heicha?* — from which clause of the *mishna* is Rami Bar Chama drawing his inference?

If it is from the opening clause — *shnayim ochazin be-talit*, two people holding onto a cloak — that would not establish simultaneous acquisition. Each claimant in that opening case is saying *kula sheli* — it is entirely mine — and claiming to have picked up the entire object himself. *Hai kula sheli ve'ana agbahti kula* — each says the whole thing is his and he picked it all up. There is nothing there that implies they picked it up at the same time in a shared fashion.

### Whole vs. Half Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא דְּקָתָנֵי "זֶה אוֹמֵר כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי וְזֶה אוֹמֵר כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי" הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא מִמִּשְׁנָה יְתֵירָה שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ – קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** he infers it **from that which is taught** later in the mishna: **This** one **says all of it is mine and that** one **says all of it is mine. Why do I** need **this** case **as well?** The first case, where each one says: I found it, is sufficient. **Rather, learn from** the **superfluous** case in the **mishna** that even if they lifted it simultaneously, they divide it and a third party has no right to take it, as in a case of **one who** performs an act of acquisition by **lifting a found item on behalf of another, the other** person **acquires** the item.

The Gemara therefore looks to the second clause, where both claimants say *kula sheli* — both claim the whole. One might wonder: why does the *mishna* need this case at all, given the first clause already addresses a dispute? *Ha tu lamma li?* — What is added? The answer suggested is that the second case hints at the scenario of simultaneous pickup — *ella mi'shniya tishma mina hamagbiya metzia le'chavero kana chaveiro*. The double language in the *mishna* suggests a teaching beyond the obvious, namely that even when both pick it up at the same moment, they both acquire. Exactly how the double language of the second case conveys this is not fully spelled out, but that is the inference Rami Bar Chama draws from it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אוֹקֵימְנָא רֵישָׁא בִּמְצִיאָה, וְסֵיפָא בְּמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** that clause in the mishna is not superfluous; **didn't we** already **establish** (2a) that **the first clause** is referring **to** a dispute over **a found item, and the latter clause** is referring **to** a case of **buying and selling,** where each party claims that he is the one who bought the item from its seller?

The Gemara, however, deflects this. We have already established that the first and second clauses of the *mishna* are meant to cover two different situations: *reisha bi'metzia ve'seifa be'mekach u'mimkar* — the first is a found object, the second is a purchase. There is thus nothing redundant in the second clause demanding an additional *drasha*; each case is independently necessary.

### Half Claim Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵסֵיפָא: "זֶה אוֹמֵר כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי וְזֶה אוֹמֵר חֶצְיָהּ שֶׁלִּי", הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא מִמִּשְׁנָה יְתֵירָה שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ – קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rami bar Ḥama infers his ruling **from the latter clause** of the mishna, i.e., the case where **this** one **says all of it is mine and that** one **says half of it is mine. Why do I** need **this** case **as well?** What does it add to the previous cases? **Rather, learn from** this **superfluous** case in the **mishna** that in a case of **one who** performs an act of acquisition by **lifting a found item on behalf of another, the other** person **acquires** the item.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּבִמְצִיאָה? דִּלְמָא בְּמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it inferred **that** this clause is referring **to** the case of **a found item? Perhaps** it is referring **to** a case of **buying and selling.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: אִי בְּמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא הַאי דְּקָאָמַר "חֶצְיָהּ שֶׁלִּי" לֶהֱוֵי כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה וְלִיפְּטַר. קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּהַאי אִיעָרוֹמֵי קָא מַעֲרִים. סָבַר: אִי אָמֵינָא "כּוּלָּהּ שֶׁלִּי", בָּעֵינָא אִשְׁתְּבוֹעֵי, אֵימָא הָכִי, דְּאֶהְוֵי כְּמֵשִׁיב אֲבֵידָה וְאִיפְּטַר!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: If** it were referring **to** a case of **buying and selling, what** would be the purpose **of stating** it, as it adds no novel ruling? One could answer that it **was necessary** to teach this additional case, as otherwise it might **enter your mind to say** that **the one who says: Half of it is mine, should be considered the equivalent of one returning a lost item;** he could have claimed that the garment was entirely his, and instead he conceded half of it to the other party **and** consequently **he should be exempt** from taking an oath. To counter this, the mishna **teaches us that** he is not exempt, as perhaps **this** person **is employing artifice.** Perhaps he is **thinking: If I say** that **all of it is mine I will need to take an oath. I will state this** claim, that half of it is mine, **as I will** thereby **be considered the equivalent of one returning a lost item, and I will be exempt** from taking an oath. Therefore, this clause is not superfluous; it teaches that this litigant is not considered the equivalent of one returning a lost item.

The Gemara then examines the clause of *zeh omer kula sheli ve'zeh omer chatzi sheli* — one claims the whole and the other claims only half. Again, *ha tu lamma li?* — why is this case necessary? Perhaps it teaches that even when two people simultaneously pick up an object, one claiming total ownership and the other claiming half, both acquire proportionately. The *mishna* then would imply simultaneous pickup and mutual acquisition.

The Gemara, however, offers a different reading of why this case is taught. The person claiming only half *could* have claimed the whole thing. Had he said *kula sheli*, he would have received half based on the dispute. By claiming only half, he receives a quarter. One might think he should be believed on his lesser claim and exempted from taking a *shvuah*, since it resembles the position of one who is *meshiv aveidah* — voluntarily returning something he could have kept. The Gemara responds: *ka mashma lan de'arumei ka'arem* — the *mishna* teaches that we suspect this is a clever stratagem. He may be reasoning: if I claim the whole, I will be obligated in a *shvuah* which I wish to avoid; if I claim only half, perhaps I will appear generous and escape the oath. The *mishna* therefore teaches that even in this configuration the *shvuah* is required. The case is thus included to address this strategic maneuvering around the oath, not to teach anything about simultaneous pickup.

### Riding Animal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא, הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם רוֹכְבִין עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה, הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא מִמִּשְׁנָה יְתֵירָה שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ – קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rami bar Ḥama infers his ruling **from this** clause: If **two** people **were sitting on an animal,** and each of them claims that it is his, each of them takes an oath and they divide the value of the animal. **Why do I** need **this** case **as well?** It teaches no novel *halakha*. **Rather, learn from** this **superfluous** clause in the **mishna** that in a case of **one who** performs an act of acquisition by **lifting a found item on behalf of another, the other** person **acquires** the item.

The Gemara next looks to the clause of *hayu shnayim rochvin al gabei behema* — two people riding on an animal. *Ha tu lamma li?* — why does the *mishna* need this case, given the *talit* case already covers simultaneous disputed possession? The suggestion is that this extra case is the source for Rami Bar Chama's principle — from the redundancy we learn that simultaneous acquisition by two parties is valid, which grounds the rule of *hamagbiya metzia le'chavero kana chaveiro*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא, הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּרוֹכֵב נָמֵי קָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps this** mishna **teaches us that** one **who sits** on an animal **also acquires** it, even though he has not caused the animal to move.

The Gemara objects: perhaps the riding case comes to teach an independent *halacha*, namely that *rochev nami kani* — riding an animal is a valid mode of *kinyan*. This would not be obvious, since riding is not the classic *kinyan* of *meshicha*. If the case is needed to establish that riding works as a *kinyan*, it teaches nothing about simultaneous pickup by two parties, and Rami Bar Chama cannot derive his principle from here.

### Agreement/Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָא, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהֵן מוֹדִין אוֹ שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן עֵדִים – חוֹלְקִין בְּלֹא שְׁבוּעָה. בְּמַאי? אִי בְּמִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר, צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר?! אֶלָּא לָאו בִּמְצִיאָה, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַמַּגְבִּיהַּ מְצִיאָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ קָנָה חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rami bar Ḥama inferred his ruling **from the last clause** in the mishna: **When they** each **admit** to the validity of the other's claim **or when they have witnesses** attesting to their claims, **they divide** it **without** taking **an oath. To what** case is the mishna referring? **If** it is referring **to** a case of **buying and selling,** does it **need to be said? Rather, is it not** referring **to a found item?** Accordingly, the reason they divide the item is that they knowingly lifted it together, and they intended to acquire it for both of them. **And learn from it** that in a case of **one who** performs an act of acquisition by **lifting a found item on behalf of another, the other** person **acquires** the item.

The Gemara therefore turns to the final clause of that case: *bizman shehen modin o sheyesh lahem edim cholkin belo shvuah* — when they agree or when there are witnesses, they divide without a *shvuah*. *Bemai* — what is the underlying scenario? If we are speaking of a case of purchase — *mekach u'mimkar* — then of course if they both agree or there are witnesses to the terms of the transaction, they divide it without an oath; that would be entirely self-evident. It must therefore be teaching a case of a *metzia*, where witnesses or the parties' own admission confirm that both picked it up simultaneously. And here the *chiddush* is real: one might have thought that even with witnesses or agreement, simultaneous pickup by two parties would not result in acquisition — perhaps the mechanism of mutual *hagbahah* on behalf of the other fails even when agreed upon. The *mishna* teaches that it does work: *shema mina hamagbiya metzia le'chavero kana chaveiro*. This is the clause from which Rami Bar Chama draws his principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר לָךְ: מִגּוֹ דְּזָכֵי לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ זָכֵי נָמֵי לְחַבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And Rava said to you** that this clause is based on a different principle: When one acquires an item, **since he acquires** it **for himself he can also acquire** part of it **for another** person.

Rava would accept that this is what the *mishna* is teaching — two people simultaneously picking up a *metzia* each acquire half — but would still insist that this does not demonstrate the broader principle that one person acting alone can acquire a *metzia* on someone else's behalf. What operates here is only *migo dezachi lenafshei zachi nami lechavrei* — the *migu* mechanism enables mutual acquisition when each party is acquiring for himself; it says nothing about purely altruistic acquisition on another's behalf at the expense of third parties.

## Riding vs. Leading

### Shmuel's Rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, אָמַר לִי רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁמַעִית מִינֵּיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל תַּרְתֵּי: רָכוּב וּמַנְהִיג, חַד קָנֵי וְחַד לָא קָנֵי. וְלָא יָדַעְנָא הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Rav Yehuda said to me:** **I heard two** *halakhot* **from Master Shmuel,** one *halakha* with regard to one **who sits** in a riding position on an animal, **and** the other *halakha* with regard to one **who leads** an animal. With regard to **one** case I heard that **he** thereby **acquires** the animal, **and** with regard to the other **one** I heard that **he does not acquire** the animal. **But I do not know** which *halakha* applies to **which of them.**

The Gemara now turns to the extended discussion of riding as a mode of *kinyan*, prompted by the *mishna*'s case of two people riding an animal. Rav Yosef reports in the name of Rav Yehuda, who received it from Shmuel: *rachuv umanhig — chad kani ve'chad lo kani* — of riding (*rachuv*) and leading (*manhig*), one works as a *kinyan* and one does not — and Rav Yehuda could not remember which Shmuel said was valid and which was not.

A terminological clarification is in order. *Rochev* refers to someone actively riding an animal in motion, which shades into *manhig*. *Rachuv* is the one who is merely seated on the animal, without actively driving it. Leading (*manhig*) is the classic case of *meshicha* — drawing the animal — which everyone agrees works as a *kinyan*. The puzzle is whether mere seated possession, *rachuv*, constitutes a valid act of acquisition.

### Simultaneous Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא רָכוּב לְחוֹדֵיהּ וּמַנְהִיג לְחוֹדֵיהּ – מַנְהִיג לְחוֹדֵיהּ מִי אִיכָּא מַאן דְּאָמַר לָא קָנֵי? אֶלָּא אִי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר דְּלָא קָנֵי – רָכוּב הוּא דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that this is referring to one **who sits** in a riding position **alone and** to one who **leads alone, is there anyone who says that** one **who leads** an animal **alone does not acquire** it? Pulling an item, or leading an animal, is a classic mode of acquisition (see *Kiddushin* 25b). **Rather, if** there is a case where **it could be said that** one **does not acquire** the animal, **it is** obviously in the case of one who **sits** in a riding position **that it could be said.** Therefore, why was Rav Yehuda uncertain?

This raises an immediate question: if Shmuel said one of these works and one does not, and Rav Yehuda cannot remember which is which, the scenario cannot be referring to each one independently. *Manhig lechuda mi ika man damar lo kani?* — who would ever say that leading an animal by itself fails as a *kinyan*? Leading is classic *meshicha* and universally recognized as effective. If we cannot imagine any authority doubting that *manhig* works, then Rav Yehuda could never be uncertain which Shmuel said was valid — obviously *manhig* works and *rachuv* might not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רָכוּב בִּמְקוֹם מַנְהִיג אִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ. מַאי: רָכוּב עָדִיף – דְּהָא תָּפֵיס בַּהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא מַנְהִיג עָדִיף – דְּאָזְלָא מֵחֲמָתֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, his dilemma** was with regard to a case where one **sits** in a riding position on the animal **while** another **leads** it. **What** is the *halakha*? Which of them acquires the animal? Does the one **sitting** in a riding position on the animal **take precedence, as** the animal **is in his grasp,** since his legs are grasping the sides of the animal, **or perhaps** the one **leading** the animal **takes precedence, as it walks because of him?**

The Gemara therefore concludes that the question Shmuel was addressing is *rachuv bimkom manhig* — what happens when both occur simultaneously, when one person is seated on the animal while another is leading it? On this reading, both modes are independently assumed to be valid, and the question is which one dominates when they compete. Does the *rachuv* prevail because he is holding onto the animal (*de'tafis bei*)? Or does the *manhig* prevail because the animal is moving on his account (*de'azal machmatei*)? This is a genuinely uncertain question, and it is Rav Yehuda's inability to remember Shmuel's answer that generates the discussion.

### Kilayim Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, אָמַר לִי רַב יְהוּדָה: נֶחְזֵי אֲנַן, דִּתְנַן: הַמַּנְהִיג סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, וְהַיּוֹשֵׁב בַּקָּרוֹן סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים. רַבִּי מֵאִיר פּוֹטֵר אֶת הַיּוֹשֵׁב בַּקָּרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Rav Yehuda said to me:** Although I do not remember what Shmuel said, **let us see** if we can analyze this **ourselves, as we learned** in a mishna concerning the prohibition against leading animals of diverse kinds (*Kilayim* 8:3): If two animals of diverse kinds, e.g., a horse and a donkey, are harnessed to the same wagon, **the one leading** the animals **incurs the forty** lashes for transgressing the Torah prohibition: "You shall not plow with an ox and a donkey together" (Deuteronomy 22:10), **and the one sitting in the wagon [*bakaron*]** also **incurs the forty** lashes. **Rabbi Meir deems the one sitting in the wagon exempt,** as he did not perform any action.

Rav Yosef reports that Rav Yehuda sought to resolve the question by appeal to a *mishna* in *kilayim*. The prohibition of *kilayim* forbids plowing with an ox and a donkey together. One who leads such a pair — *ha'manhig* — transgresses and receives lashes. What about one who sits in a wagon being pulled by the two of them — *ha'yoshev ba'karon*? The standard position is that he too receives lashes. Rabbi Meir, however, exempts the one sitting in the wagon, holding that merely sitting while the animals pull you does not constitute the prohibited act of driving *shor ve'chamor yachdav*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּאָפֵיךְ שְׁמוּאֵל וְתָנֵי: וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין אֶת הַיּוֹשֵׁב בַּקָּרוֹן, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ רָכוּב לְחוֹדֵיהּ לָא קָנֵי, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן רָכוּב בִּמְקוֹם מַנְהִיג.
>
> **Translation:** **And from** the fact **that** in his version of the mishna **Shmuel reversed** the opinions **and taught: And the Rabbis deem the one sitting in the wagon exempt,** it can be inferred that he agrees with this opinion that the one sitting in the wagon is considered to have not performed any action, as the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis in their disputes with Rabbi Meir. **Conclude from it** that one who **sits** on an animal **alone does not acquire** it, as sitting on an animal is not considered a significant action, **and all the more so** one who **sits** on an animal **while** another **leads** the animal does not acquire the animal.

Shmuel, Rav Yehuda reports, actually reversed the attributions in that *mishna*: *ve'tani va'Chachamim potrim et ha'yoshev ba'karon* — he taught that it was the *Chachamim*, not Rabbi Meir, who exempted the wagon-sitter. Shmuel evidently preferred this position and wished it to carry majority authority. The implication for our discussion is then: *shema mina rachuv lachudei lo kani ve'chol she'ken rachuv bimkom manhig* — from the fact that Shmuel ruled that sitting in a wagon is not even sufficient to constitute the *kilayim* violation, we learn that *rachuv* by itself lacks meaningful legal weight, and certainly it cannot prevail against *manhig*.

### Wagon vs. Saddle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: הָא זִמְנִין סַגִּיאִין אֲמַרְתְּ לַן: נֶחְזֵי אֲנַן, וְלָא אֲמַרְתְּ לַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Didn't you say to us many times** with regard to this *halakha*: **Let us see** if we can analyze this **ourselves,** followed by the proof from the aforementioned mishna? **And you did not say to us** that this statement was **in the name of Rav Yehuda.** Rav Yosef had an illness that caused him memory loss. Consequently, some of his later statements of *halakha* were inaccurate, and Abaye suspected that he attributed this statement to Rav Yehuda erroneously.

Abaye, however, challenged this reasoning — whether as a challenge directed at Rav Yehuda himself (as reported by Rav Yosef) or directly at Rav Yosef who initially presented the argument in his own name, the *gemara* preserves two versions of the exchange. The thrust of the objection is the same: *heichi pashit mar rachuv mi'yoshev?* — how can you derive the law of riding from the case of sitting in a wagon? *Yoshev lo tafis be'mosira; rachuv tafis be'mosira* — the person sitting in the wagon is not holding the reins, whereas the person riding in the saddle is holding the reins. These are fundamentally different cases. Even if sitting passively in a wagon lacks legal significance, one who is actively seated in the saddle and holding the reins might constitute a meaningful act of control, either for *kilayim* or for *kinyan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִבְרָא! וּדְכִירְנָא נָמֵי דַּאֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: הֵיכִי פָּשֵׁיט מָר רָכוּב מִיּוֹשֵׁב? יוֹשֵׁב לָא תָּפֵיס בְּמוֹסֵירָה, רָכוּב תָּפֵיס בְּמוֹסֵירָה! וַאֲמַר לִי רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מוֹסֵירָה לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: Indeed [*ivra*],** I remember that Rav Yehuda stated this proof, **and I also remember that I said to him** in response: **How can the Master resolve** the case of one who **sits** on an animal via proof **from** the case of one who **sits** in the wagon? One who **sits** in the wagon **does not hold the reins,** whereas one who **sits** on the animal **holds the reins. And** Rav Yehuda **said to me** in response: **Rav and Shmuel both say** that holding the **reins** of an ownerless animal **does not effect acquisition** of it. Consequently, there is no difference between sitting on an animal and sitting in a wagon drawn by an animal.

The response to Abaye's challenge comes in the name of Idi, who taught: *mosira lo kani* — holding the reins does not constitute a *kinyan*. If holding the reins by itself is legally insignificant as an independent act of acquisition, then the combination of seated riding and rein-holding adds nothing. Zero plus zero remains zero. Since sitting in the saddle is not meaningful (derived from the *kilayim* case) and holding the reins is independently not meaningful, their combination does not produce a valid *kinyan*. This is how Rav Yehuda's proof from the wagon case survives Abaye's objection.

### Mesira Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מוֹסֵירָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ – קָנָה. בִּמְצִיאָה וּבְנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר – לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It **was also stated** that **Rabbi Ḥelbo says** that **Rav Huna says:** With regard to holding the **reins** of an animal in order to acquire it, if he is attempting to acquire it **from another** person, he **acquires** the animal. But **with regard to** acquisition of **a found** animal, **or with regard to** acquisition of an animal that was **the property of a convert** who died without heirs, leaving his property ownerless, it **does not effect acquisition.**

The Gemara then clarifies the law of *mesira* more precisely. Rabbi Chelbo in the name of Rav Huna teaches: *mesira mi'chaveiro kana; be'metzia u've'nichsei ha'ger lo kana* — handing over the reins in a sale from one party to another constitutes a valid *kinyan*; but in the case of a *metzia* or the property of a *ger* who died without heirs (property that is effectively ownerless and free for anyone to take), taking hold of the reins does not effect acquisition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לְשׁוֹן מוֹסֵירָה? אָמַר רָבָא: אִידִי אַסְבְּרָא לִי כְּאָדָם הַמּוֹסֵר דָּבָר לַחֲבֵירוֹ. בִּשְׁלָמָא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ קָנֵי, דְּקָא מָסַר לֵיהּ חַבְרֵיהּ. אֶלָּא בִּמְצִיאָה וּבְנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר מַאן קָא מָסַר לֵיהּ דְּלִיקְנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of **the term reins [*moseira*]? Rava said: Idi explained to me** that they are used **like a person who transmits [*moser*] an item to another,** i.e., they are used to transfer the ownership of the animal. **Granted,** in a case where one takes the reins **from another,** this **effects acquisition** of the animal, **as the other** person **hands** them **to him. But** in a case **of a found** animal **or of** one that was **the property of a convert, who is handing him** the reins, enabling him **to acquire** the ownerless animal? Since there was no transaction, one cannot acquire the animal by merely holding the reins.

Rava explains the rationale in the name of Idi: *ke'adam ha'moser davar mi'chaveiro le'chaveiro* — the word *mesira* evokes the image of one person handing something over to another. The legal significance lies in the act of transfer — in the handing. *Bishlama mi'chaveiro kana, de'ka masar leih chavrei* — when buying from another person, there is an act of handing the reins over, and that deliberate transfer is what creates the *kinyan*. *Ela be'metzia u've'nichsei ha'ger man ka masar leih de'likni?* — but in the case of ownerless property, who is doing the handing? Nobody. There is no act of transfer; one is simply taking hold of something. And merely taking hold of reins, as Idi taught, is not a *kinyan*.

### Two Types Manhig

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם רוֹכְבִין עַל גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה וְכוּ׳. מַנִּי? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר – הַשְׁתָּא יוֹשֵׁב קָנֵי, רָכוּב מִיבְּעֵי?! אֶלָּא לָאו רַבָּנַן, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ רָכוּב קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the mishna: If **two** people **were sitting** in a riding position **on an animal,** or if one was sitting on it in a riding position and the other was leading it, they divide it after taking an oath. In accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? **If we say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, now,** in his opinion, even one who **sits** in a wagon **acquires** the animal that is pulling the wagon. **Is it necessary** to state that one who **sits** in a riding position on an animal acquires it? **Rather, is it not** the opinion of **the Rabbis? And learn from it** that one who **sits** in a riding position on an animal **acquires** it.

With all of this established, the Gemara returns to the *mishna*'s case of two people riding an animal and asks: *mani* — whose position does this *mishna* reflect? If it reflects Rabbi Meir's view — that sitting in a wagon constitutes a meaningful act such that one who sits while an ox and donkey pull him transgresses *kilayim* — then the *mishna* should have taught an even more striking case. *Hashta yoshev kana, rachuv miba'ya?* — if even sitting in a wagon effects a *kinyan*, the *mishna* need not teach us about riding; it could have taught the more novel case of mere sitting. The fact that the *mishna* specifically uses a case of riding suggests it is following the view of the *rabanan*, who exempt the wagon-sitter and thus consider passive sitting legally insignificant — and from that it would follow that riding *does* work as a *kinyan*, which is the *chiddush* the *mishna* intends.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמַנְהִיג בְּרַגְלָיו. אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ מַנְהִיג! תְּרֵי גַּוְונֵי מַנְהִיג, מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: רָכוּב עָדִיף, דְּהָא מַנְהִיג וְתָפֵיס בַּהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the one sitting on the animal also **leads,** i.e., drives it by squeezing or kicking it **with his legs.** The Gemara asks: **If so, this is** the same as **leading** the animal by pulling the reins, as the essential factor in both is that one causes the animal to move, so why does the mishna need to mention it? The Gemara answers: The *tanna* teaches **two types of leading,** both pulling the animal by the reins and driving it while sitting on it. **Lest you say** that one who **is sitting** in a riding position on the animal **takes precedence, as he is** both **leading** the animal **and** also **holding it** by the reins, the *tanna* **teaches us** that the claim of the one sitting in a riding position on the animal is not stronger than the claim of the one leading it by the reins.

The Gemara, however, resolves the question differently: *hacha b'mai askinan b'manhig b'raglav* — the *mishna*'s case of two people riding is not a case of passive seated possession at all. It refers to riders who are actively kicking with their legs, causing the animal to move. This is itself a form of *meshicha* — the classic act of *manhig* — performed from the saddle. The *kinyan* here is not *rachuv* in the static sense but rather an active driving of the animal, functionally equivalent to leading.

The Gemara then asks: *ei hachi, hainu manhig?* — if so, is this not simply *manhig* by another name? Why does the *mishna* treat it separately? The Gemara answers: *trei gavnei manhig* — there are two forms of *manhig*. One is leading from the ground by pulling the animal, and the other is leading from the saddle by kicking. Both are variants of *meshicha*. The reason the *mishna* includes the riding case alongside the leading case is to teach that *rachuv* of this active variety does not dominate or displace the other form of *manhig*. One might have thought — returning to Shmuel's original question — that since the rider is both driving the animal and physically atop it, holding it, his form of *manhig* is superior: *rachuv adif d'manhig ve'tafis bei*. The *mishna* teaches that this is not so: the two forms of *manhig* are equal, and therefore when two people are performing them simultaneously over the same animal, *yachaloku* — they divide the animal between them.
