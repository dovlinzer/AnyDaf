# Nazir 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Raglayim L'Davar

### External Causes Dismissed

The *Gemara* on daf 66 opens at the bottom of 65b, picking up from a *Mishnah* that, while having little direct connection to *Nazir*, introduced the phrase *raglayim l'davar* — the same phrase that appeared at the beginning of the *perek*. The *Mishnah* concerned a *zav*, a man who has experienced the abnormal genital discharge designated as *zivah* by the Torah. Before a man becomes a full *zav*, we check whether each sighting of the discharge might be attributable to some external cause — *ones* — such as exertion, illness, or certain foods. If so, that sighting is discounted. Once he has had two flows, however — making him a *zav* in the preliminary sense — any subsequent flows are relevant either because a third flow obligates him to bring a *korban* or because any flow during the seven clean days he must count will reset that count entirely. The principle of *raglayim l'davar* teaches that for these subsequent flows, we no longer concern ourselves with the possibility of external causes: since the man is already a *zav*, we assume the continued flow stems from his condition, not from something incidental.

### V'Hazav Et Zovo

The *Gemara* then asks: *minah hanei milei* — what is the scriptural source for this distinction? Rabbi Natan answers by citing the verse *v'hazav et zovo* — "and the one who flows, his flow" (Leviticus 15:3) — and notes that the verse concludes *l'zachar ul'nekeivah*, "whether male or female," drawing an analogy between the male *zav* and the female *zavah*. The difference between the two is instructive. For a woman, the operative distinction in the Torah is between *niddah* — menstrual bleeding occurring during the expected cycle — and *zavah* — bleeding outside the menstrual period. The question for her is entirely about *when* the blood flows, not *what* is flowing. For a man, by contrast, the distinction is between *shichvat zera*, a seminal emission, which is natural and its own category of *tumah*, and *zivah*, which is an abnormal discharge distinguishable from semen by its consistency, as described in the *Mishnah*. For a woman, external causes are entirely irrelevant — the *tumah* applies regardless. The verse *v'hazav et zovo l'zachar ul'nekeivah* is therefore read as establishing when the male *zav* is treated like the female: after the double language of *zovo* — implying two prior flows — external causes cease to matter for him as well. From the third sighting onward, he is analogized to the woman for whom *ones* is never a consideration.

### Rabbi Eliezer vs. Rabban…

A *baraita* is then cited that appears to complicate this picture. Rabbi Eliezer holds that even for the third flow — the one that would obligate a *korban* — we still check for external causes. According to him, it is only the fourth flow and beyond (those that could interrupt the seven clean days) for which we no longer check. The *tanna kamma*, by contrast, holds that from the third sighting onward, *ones* is already irrelevant.

### Et Particle Dispute

The *Gemara* traces this disagreement to a hermeneutical dispute over the word *et* in *v'hazav et zovo*. The *rabbanan* do not treat the particle *et* as expanding the scope of the verse, and so they read *v'hazav zovo* — two flows — as the threshold after which external causes are dismissed; the third flow is therefore already governed by the rule of *raglayim l'davar*. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, applies the standard exegetical principle associated with Rav Nachum Ish Gamzu and does *darish et* — reads the particle as adding to the count — yielding three flows as the threshold, so that only the fourth flow is beyond scrutiny. All authorities agree, in any event, that from a certain point onward, *ones* is simply not a consideration.

## Safek Regarding Emission

### Identity of Emission

The *Mishnah* then lists additional categories treated as *tamei*: *ones*, *safek*, and *shichvat zera*. Having addressed *ones*, the *Gemara* now turns to *safek*. Rava clarifies that the *safek* in the *Mishnah* does not refer to a case where one is uncertain whether anything came out at all. Such a doubt would not be governed by *raglayim l'davar* — the mere fact that a man is already a *zav* does not allow us to assume that a new flow occurred when there is no evidence of one. Rather, Rava explains: *vadai chaza safek machmat shichvat zera safek machmat re'iyah* — something definitely emerged, but its identity is unknown. The doubt is whether what came out was the result of a seminal emission or whether it was *zivah* flowing on its own.

### Zivah with Semen Rules

Why does this distinction matter? *Tosafot* clarifies by reference to a rule taught elsewhere: *zivah* that is mixed together with semen — *depeticha bei shichvat zera* — destroys only one day of the man's clean count, whereas *zivah* that flows on its own — *delo peticha bei shichvat zera* — destroys all seven days and requires him to start again from the beginning. The practical stakes are therefore significant. If a man is, say, on day three of his seven clean days, and something occurs: if it was a seminal emission, there is no interruption at all. If it was a mixed emission — *zivah* together with semen — he cannot count that one day but may continue counting the rest. If it was *zivah* alone, he must begin the entire count anew. The *safek* of the *Mishnah* addresses the case where one finds evidence of both substances — stains of each — and cannot determine whether they emerged together or separately.

### Strict Assumption

Rava concludes: *keivan shenizkak l'tumah, sfeko tamei* — since the man has already established himself as a *zav*, we invoke *raglayim l'davar* and adopt the stricter reading. We assume that the *zivah* came out on its own, not mixed with the semen, and the man must restart all seven days.

## Zav's Semen Status

### Semen Tumah by Masa

The *Mishnah* includes *shichvat zera* — semen — as *tamei*. The *Gemara* asks: *l'mai hilchata* — for what practical *halachah*? If the point is merely that touching the *zav*'s semen renders one *tamei*, that would hardly be remarkable: even the semen of any ordinary man who is *tahor* is itself an *av hatumah* and conveys *tumah* by contact. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the *Mishnah* must be establishing something more: *zaro shel zav metamei b'masa* — the semen of a *zav* conveys *tumah* even through carrying (*masa*), not merely through contact. This would place it in the same category as the *zivah* flow itself, which is *metamei b'masa*.

### Rabbi Eliezer vs. Yehosh…

The *Gemara* then identifies which *tanna* holds this position, by citing a *baraita*: Rabbi Eliezer says that the semen of a *zav* is *not metamei b'masa*; Rabbi Yehoshua says it *is metamei b'masa*. Rabbi Yehoshua's reasoning is not that semen inherently achieves this status — rather, he reasons that since the man is a *zav*, it is impossible that any penile emission would not contain within it at least some droplets of *zivah* — *tzikhtzukhei zivah*. It is those droplets, not the semen itself, that account for the stringent *tumah*.

### Droplets Explanation

This seems a perfectly serviceable reading of the *Mishnah*: our case involves a known *zav*, and *raglayim l'davar* allows us to assume that any emission will be contaminated with *zivah* — this is precisely why we treat even his semen strictly. The *Gemara*, however, pushes back: *afilu Rabbi Yehoshua lo amar ella mishum tzikhtzukhei zivah, aval b'inei lo amar* — even Rabbi Yehoshua does not say that pure semen alone, entirely free of any *zivah*, would be *metamei b'masa*. This is an abstract distinction — in practice there is indeed no way to separate the emissions — but the *Gemara* treats it as meaningful in principle, and so will not read the *Mishnah* as making a statement solely about the droplets.

### Rav Ada Bar Ahava

Instead, the *Gemara* cites Rav Ada bar Ahava: *lomar she'ein tolin bah* — the *Mishnah*'s reference to *shichvat zera* is teaching that once a man is a *zav*, we do not attribute a subsequent *zivah* flow to the seminal emission that preceded it. This interpretation connects back to the laws of *ones*: among the external causes that would ordinarily discount a *zivah* is a prior seminal emission. The reasoning is that the body may be weakened or affected by the ejaculation, and a flow following it closely may therefore be attributed to that cause rather than to the *zivah* condition itself. But once this man is already an established *zav*, the principle of *raglayim l'davar* operates: we no longer attribute the flow to the *ba'al keri* that preceded it. *She'ein tolin bah* — we do not chalk it up to the seminal emission. That is the law the *Mishnah* is teaching when it says *shichvat zera* is *tamei* — not that the semen conveys elevated *tumah*, but that being a *ba'al keri* no longer serves as a discounting factor for a man who is already a *zav*.

## Weakness Theory Rejected

### Pappa's Weakness Theory

The *Gemara* now steps back to examine the underlying rationale for why a seminal emission ordinarily exempts from *zivah*. Rav Pappa, reasoning before Rava, proposed that it is a straightforwardly physical explanation: the body is weakened by the ejaculation — *aiday khulsha hu d'chazei* — and any subsequent discharge is therefore attributable to that depleted physical state, much as a flow following illness or exertion would be attributed to those conditions.

### Ger Conversion Challenge

Rava challenged this with a *Mishnah*: *ger shenitgayer metamei miyad b'zivah* — a convert who converts and then experiences a *zivah* flow immediately becomes a *zav*, and we do not take into account anything that may have happened to his body immediately before his conversion. If the exemption of *ba'al keri* were genuinely based on the physical condition of the body, then a seminal emission occurring moments before immersion in the *mikveh* should matter just as much as one occurring after conversion — the body's state is unchanged by the conversion itself. *Tosafot*, in the name of the Ri, reconstructs Rava's argument as follows: *ein lecha choli gadol mizeh* — meaning that this case of the convert presents exactly the same physical reality, yet we disregard what occurred before his conversion. The contrast with the other causes listed in the *Mishnah* — illness, certain foods, exertion — is striking: those are real physical conditions that presumably affect the body regardless of one's legal status, yet for the *ba'al keri* exemption, a pre-conversion occurrence is treated as entirely irrelevant.

### Gezeirat HaKatuv

The *Gemara* concludes: *ela tanai hi* — it is a dispute among *tanna'im*. More precisely, the underlying conclusion is that the exemption of *zivah* following *ba'al keri* is not grounded in the physical state of the body at all, but is rather a *gezeirat hakatuv* — a Scriptural decree, derived from elsewhere in the *midrash halachah*, that this specific combination does not count as *zivah*. Because it is a legal decree tied to the person's obligations under the Torah, it applies only from the moment of conversion onward. Whatever occurred before the man was Jewish falls outside the scope of the decree entirely. This also resolves the question that now re-emerges about the *zav*: if the *ba'al keri* exemption is a *gezeirat hakatuv* rather than a description of physical reality, does it potentially continue to operate even after a man becomes a *zav*? Rav Ada bar Ahava's reading of the *Mishnah* addressed precisely this — once he is a *zav*, *raglayim l'davar* removes the basis for applying that exemption in the first place.

## Semen & Scripture

### Lo Yihyeh Tahor

The *Gemara* now turns to the scriptural grounding for the *Mishnah*'s statement that the semen of a *zav* is *tamei*. The *pasuk* in question is drawn from *Devarim* — entirely removed from the *parshiyot* of *Vayikra* that deal with *zav* — and reads: *ki yihyeh vecha ish asher lo yihyeh tahor mikreh layla*, "if there is a man among you who is not *tahor* because of an occurrence of the night." The *Gemara* reads this as referring to a man who is already not *tahor* — already a *zav* — and who then experiences a nocturnal emission, a *mikreh layla*. That emission, in his case, is to be treated as *zivah*.

### Duration of Tumah

On this basis, a *tannaitic* dispute is cited. One view holds that the semen of a *zav* conveys *tumah* for a full twenty-four hour period — *kol me'et le'et* — following the *zivah* flow. Rabbi Yose holds that it applies only for *yomo* — that same period of the day, roughly a twelve-hour segment.

### Verses Resolution

The *Gemara* identifies the source of this dispute in a contradiction raised by Shmuel between two expressions in the same passage. One phrase is *mikreh layla* — "an occurrence of the night" — implying that the *tumah* pertains only to the night of the emission. The other phrase, later in the verse, is *lifnot erev yirchatz bamayim* — "towards evening he shall bathe in the water" — implying that the *tumah* extends all the way until the following evening, a full twenty-four hours.

The view that holds *me'et le'et* derives from *lifnot erev*: the man experiences the event at night, and the semen's status as *zivah* persists until the next evening, a period of approximately twenty-four hours. The view of Rabbi Yose that holds *yomo* derives from *mikreh layla*: only that same nocturnal period carries the stringency. The *Gemara* then asks: for the view that holds *lifnot erev* — what does he do with *mikreh layla*? The *Gemara* answers: *orchah de'keri* — nocturnal emissions typically occur at night; that phrase simply reflects the normal circumstance, while the *tumah* itself extends until the following evening.

The upshot of this entire discussion is to establish from a *pasuk* that the semen of a *zav*, flowing in the wake of *zivah*, is treated as *zivah* itself — and is therefore *metamei b'masa*. This scriptural grounding supersedes the earlier discussion about whether the *tumah* is only because of the admixed droplets of *zivah*, or whether the semen itself achieves this status during the defined window following a *zivah* flow. The *Mishnah*'s statement that *shichvat zera* is *tamei* is thus substantiated on a principled, textual basis, not merely through the practical inference of *raglayim l'davar*.

## Closing Aggadot

### Samuel Nazir Status

The *Gemara* concludes the tractate with a series of *aggadic* passages. The final *Mishnah* states: *nazir hayah Shmuel k'divrei Rav Nehorai* — Samuel was a *nazir*, according to Rav Nehorai. The scriptural basis is the phrase spoken before Samuel's birth: *u'mora lo ya'aleh al rosho* — "and a razor shall not come upon his head" (1 Samuel 1:11). The verse says nothing about wine or contact with the dead, the other *nezirut* prohibitions, but the language of the razor is itself decisive. Moreover, the same word *mora* appears in the verse describing Shimshon: *nazir Elokim yiheyeh hana'ar min habaten* — "the youth shall be a *nazir* of God from the womb." The *Gemara* reasons by means of a *gezeirah shavah*: the same term *mora* used for Shimshon — who is explicitly called a *nazir* — appears also for Samuel, and therefore Samuel too was a *nazir*. One might note the irony that in both cases it is neither the father nor the subject himself who initiates the *nezirut*, but rather a mother or an angel speaking to a mother — recalling earlier discussions in the tractate about whether a father may impose *nezirut* on a son.

### Mora Meaning Dispute

Rabbi Yose challenges Rav Nehorai: *v'halo ein mora ela shel basar vadam* — surely *mora* means fear, not razor! The word, he argues, means that no human being shall instill fear in Samuel. Rav Nehorai deflects this reading by invoking the plain narrative of Shmuel itself: *vayomer Shmuel eich eileich v'shama Shaul va'haragani* — "Samuel said: how shall I go? If Saul hears, he will kill me!" Samuel was clearly afraid of a human being. The interpretation of *mora* as "fear of mortals" therefore cannot stand, and the reference must be to the razor. One might have thought the most straightforward counter would be to point out that *mora* with the meaning of razor is spelled with a *heh*, while *mora* meaning fear is spelled with an *alef* — but the *Gemara* pursues the narrative refutation instead.

### Rav Chiyya Teaching

Following the close of the *Mishnah*'s material, Rav said to his son Chiyya: *chatof u'varech* — seize the opportunity and make the *bracha*. Rashi explains that this refers to being the one to lead *birkat zimun* or to recite *kiddush* or similar communal blessings. Rav Huna similarly told his son Rabbah: *chatof u'varech*. The implicit teaching is that one should eagerly pursue the opportunity to recite a blessing on behalf of others.

### Amen Response Value

The *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: does this not imply that the one who recites the *bracha* has the greater merit? But a *baraita* teaches explicitly — *gadol ha'oneh Amen yoser min hamevarech* — responding *Amen* is greater than making the blessing. Rav Nehorai confirms this with an oath: *hashamayim kach hu* — by heaven, this is so.

He offers a vivid military illustration: *she'rei gulyarim misgarim b'milchama v'giborim notzchim* — the lighter front forces go out and engage first, but it is the mighty warriors who follow who ultimately prevail. So too in the structure of prayer: the one who recites the *bracha* goes first and initiates, but the one who answers *Amen* provides the force that carries it through. The image recalls a contemporary insight: a lone person acting publicly is easily dismissed, but the first follower who joins in is what transforms an individual gesture into a movement. The one who answers *Amen* is that first and decisive responder — lending weight and communal reality to the blessing that preceded it. It is also worth noting that the Sephardic custom of responding *hashamayim* when one is about to recite a blessing on behalf of the community — such as for *sefirat ha'omer* or *zimun* — resonates precisely with this passage, since *hashamayim* is itself the response of the *oneh* preceding the *bracha*.

### Mevarech vs. Oneh

The *Gemara* notes that this is in fact a *tannaitic* dispute: one view holds *echad hamevarech v'echad ha'oneh Amen bamashma* — whether one recites the *bracha* or answers *Amen*, both are equally reckoned. The *Gemara* seems to conclude that while the two are equal in ultimate weight, the counsel to *chatof u'varech* was nevertheless practical guidance — encouraging one to take initiative — even if the *oneh Amen* carries no lesser merit. The passage is presumably cited here in part because Rav Nehorai, the unusual *tanna* whose name appeared in the final *Mishnah*, also appears in this *baraita*.

### Peace Blessing

The tractate closes with the words of Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *talmidei chachamim marbim shalom ba'olam shene'emar v'chol banayich limudei Hashem v'rav shalom banayich* — Torah scholars increase peace in the world, as it is said: "And all your children shall be students of God, and great shall be the peace of your children." With these words, *masechet Nazir* reaches its conclusion.

*Hadran alach masechet Nazir.*
