# Berakhot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dovid's Midnight Practice

### Harp Wakes Dovid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדָוִד בְּפַלְגָא דְלֵילְיָא הֲוָה קָאֵי? מֵאוּרְתָּא הֲוָה קָאֵי! דִּכְתִיב: ״קִדַּמְתִּי בַנֶּשֶׁף וָאֲשַׁוֵּעָה״, וּמִמַּאי דְּהַאי ״נֶשֶׁף״ אוּרְתָּא הוּא? — דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּנֶשֶׁף בְּעֶרֶב יוֹם בְּאִישׁוֹן לַיְלָה וַאֲפֵלָה״! אָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר (דָּוִד): מֵעוֹלָם לֹא עָבַר עָלַי חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה בְּשֵׁינָה. רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: עַד חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה הָיָה מִתְנַמְנֵם כְּסוּס, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ הָיָה מִתְגַּבֵּר כַּאֲרִי. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: עַד חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה הָיָה עוֹסֵק בְּדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ בְּשִׁירוֹת וְתִשְׁבָּחוֹת. דָּוִד, סִימָנָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲסִידָא: כִּנּוֹר הָיָה תָּלוּי לְמַעְלָה מִמִּטָּתוֹ שֶׁל דָּוִד, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה, בָּא רוּחַ צְפוֹנִית וְנוֹשֶׁבֶת בּוֹ וּמְנַגֵּן מֵאֵלָיו, מִיָּד הָיָה עוֹמֵד וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה עַד שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Did David rise **at midnight?** He rose **in the evening.** As it is written: "I rise before the dawn and cry, I hope for Your word" (Psalms 119:147). And how do we know **that this *neshef* is the evening?** As it is written: "In the *neshef*, in the evening of the day, in the blackness of night and the darkness" (Proverbs 7:9). The Gemara suggests an alternative interpretation: **Rav Oshaya said** that **Rabbi Aḥa said: This is what David said: Midnight never passed me by in my sleep.** Sometimes he fulfilled the verse "I rise before the dawn and cry," but he always at least fulfilled the verse "At midnight I rise to give thanks." **Rabbi Zeira said: Until midnight** David **would doze like a horse,** but never sleep deeply. **From midnight on, he would gain the strength of a lion. Rav Ashi said: Until midnight, he would** engage in **the study of Torah, from midnight on,** he would engage in **songs and praise.** **David had a sign** to know when midnight was. **Rav Aḥa bar Bizna said** that **Rabbi Shimon Ḥasida said: A lyre hung over David's bed, and once midnight arrived, the northern wind would come and** cause **the lyre to play on its own.** David **would immediately rise** from his bed **and study Torah until the first rays of dawn.**

We pick up at the bottom of daf 3b, at the line beginning וְנוֹשֶׁבֶת בּוֹ. The Gemara has been discussing how Dovid HaMelech would rise at midnight to praise God, and it now explains the mechanism: a northern wind would blow through his harp — וּמְנַגֵּן מֵאֵלָיו — and the harp would play by itself at midnight. מִיָּד הָיָה עוֹמֵד וְעוֹסֵק בְּתוֹרָה — he would immediately rise and engage in Torah study. This is interesting because an earlier verse had framed his rising as לְהוֹדוֹת לָךְ, to praise God, yet here it is framed as learning Torah. Perhaps the learning was prior to the praise, but in any case the Gemara presents it as his midnight practice. עַד שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר — he continued until the morning star rose, until the first light approximately an hour before sunrise.

### Sages Seek Parnassah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, נִכְנְסוּ חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶצְלוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֲדוֹנֵינוּ הַמֶּלֶךְ, עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל צְרִיכִין פַּרְנָסָה. אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְהִתְפַּרְנְסוּ זֶה מִזֶּה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין הַקּוֹמֶץ מַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת הָאֲרִי, וְאֵין הַבּוֹר מִתְמַלֵּא מֵחוּלְיָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Once **dawn** arrived, **the Sages of Israel entered** to advise **him** with regard to the various concerns of the nation and the economy. **They said to him: Our master, the king, your nation requires sustenance.** **He said: Go and sustain one another,** provide each other with whatever is lacking. The Sages of Israel **responded to him** with a parable: **A single handful** of food **does not satisfy a lion, and a pit** will **not** be **filled** merely **from** the rain that falls directly into **its mouth.**

כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, once the morning star appeared, נִכְנְסוּ חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶצְלוֹ — the sages of Israel would come to him. These were his policy representatives, the people with whom he would discuss the affairs of the kingdom. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֲדוֹנֵינוּ הַמֶּלֶךְ, עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל צְרִיכִין פַּרְנָסָה — our master the king, the nation of Israel needs to make a livelihood; things are not going well financially.

אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְהִתְפַּרְנְסוּ זֶה מִזֶּה — go and sustain yourselves from one another, do business among yourselves. They responded with two images: אֵין הַקֹּמֶץ מַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת הָאַרְיֵה — the tiny handful does not satisfy the lion — and וְאֵין הַבּוֹר מִתְמַלֵּא מֵחֻלְיָתוֹ — nor is a pit filled from its own clods of earth.

Rashi explains the second image: if you already have a pit and you pull out a little clump of earth from within it and throw it back, you will not fill the pit. The metaphor is apt — you are already in the red, and whatever you take from one part of yourselves to fill the need, you are simply robbing from Peter to pay Paul. Tosafot offers related interpretations: Rabbeinu Tam suggests the image of an underground stream that cannot fill a well from below; the Ri makes the same essential point, that digging in one place to fill another merely creates a new hole. The common thrust is clear: our needs are far greater than what internal commerce can supply. Note too the respectful register of their reply — they do not tell the king he is wrong; they answer through metaphor, as befits addressing a monarch.

### Milchemet Reshut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וּפִשְׁטוּ יְדֵיכֶם בַּגְּדוּד. מִיָּד יוֹעֲצִים בַּאֲחִיתוֹפֶל, וְנִמְלָכִים בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין, וְשׁוֹאֲלִים בְּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: Go and extend your hands against a military band,** go out to war. **Immediately they seek advice from Ahitophel** to determine whether or not it was appropriate to go to war at that time and how they should conduct themselves, **and they consult the Sanhedrin** in order to receive the requisite license to wage a war under those circumstances. **And they ask the *Urim VeTummim*** whether or not they should go to war, and whether or not they would be successful.

אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וּפִשְׁטוּ יְדֵיכֶם בַּגְּדוּד — he said to them: go and extend your hands against a military band, go out to war. This is, frankly, a striking response. One might ask whether there is any halachic significance to the suggestion that Jews in a state of poverty simply attack others. For roughly two thousand years following the composition of the Gemara this was a moot question, but it raises serious issues. It is not cited as a normative halachic ruling.

What the Gemara does develop, and what the Rambam later formalizes, is the distinction between מִלְחֶמֶת מִצְוָה and מִלְחֶמֶת רְשׁוּת. *milchemet mitzvah* encompasses the conquest of the land of Israel, the war against the seven nations, the destruction of Amalek, and — as the Rambam makes explicit — wars of self-defense when Israel is attacked. *milchemet reshut*, by contrast, is war undertaken for territorial expansion. The campaign described here — going to war to improve the economy, to seize the wealth of others — falls squarely into the category of *milchemet reshut*. That category itself raises questions about what legitimizes such a war, and indeed the Gemara immediately proceeds to describe the process that must precede it.

### War Protocol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קְרָא? דִּכְתִיב ״וְאַחֲרֵי אֲחִיתֹפֶל בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע וְאֶבְיָתָר וְשַׂר צָבָא לַמֶּלֶךְ יוֹאָב״. אֲחִיתוֹפֶל — זֶה יוֹעֵץ, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וַעֲצַת אֲחִיתֹפֶל אֲשֶׁר יָעַץ בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם כַּאֲשֶׁר יִשְׁאַל אִישׁ בִּדְבַר הָאֱלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** Upon **what verse** is this *aggada* based? **As it is written: "And after Ahitophel was Benayahu son of Yehoyada and Evyatar, and the general of the king's army, Yoav"** (I Chronicles 27:34). **Ahitophel is the adviser** whose advice they sought first with regard to going to war, **and so it says: "Now the counsel of Ahitophel, which he counseled in those days, was as a man who inquires of the word of God;** so was the counsel of Ahitophel both with David and with Absalom" (II Samuel 16:23).

מִיָּד יוֹעֲצִין בַּאֲחִיתֹפֶל — immediately they take counsel with Achitofel. Then וְנִמְלָכִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין — they ask permission of the Sanhedrin. The distinction between יוֹעֲצִין and נִמְלָכִין is significant: the former is consultation for good policy; the latter is asking for permission or at least the imprimatur of that body. Then וְשׁוֹאֲלִים בָּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים — they consult the Urim veTumim, the divine instrument worn by the Kohen Gadol. One of the earliest appearances of the Urim veTumim in Tanach is precisely in the context of going to war — יְהוּדָה יַעֲלֶה — asking who goes up first, whether the campaign is right. And then, after all of this, the orders are handed to the general.

אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף מַאי קְרָא — Rav Yosef asks: what is the verse? וְאַחֲרֵי אֲחִיתֹפֶל בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע וְאֶבְיָתָר וְשַׂר צְבָא לַמֶּלֶךְ יוֹאָב. After Achitofel come Benayahu ben Yehoyada and Evyatar, and then the general of the king, Yoav. The Gemara identifies the players: אֲחִיתֹפֶל זֶה יוֹעֵץ — Achitofel is the advisor, as the verse attests that his counsel was like asking the word of God. Then at the top of daf 4a: בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע זֶה סַנְהֶדְרִין — Benayahu ben Yehoyada represents the Sanhedrin. וְאֶבְיָתָר אֵלּוּ אוּרִים וְתוּמִּים — and Evyatar, who was the Kohen, represents the Urim veTumim. The verse offered is: וּבְנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע עַל הַכְּרֵתִי וְעַל הַפְּלֵתִי. Why are they called Kreiti uPleiti? כְּרֵתִי שֶׁכּוֹרְתִים דִּבְרֵיהֶם — because their words are cut and definitive; פְּלֵתִי שֶׁמֻּפְלָאִים בִּדְבְרֵיהֶם — and their words are astounding. According to Chazal, the Urim veTumim functioned through the letters inscribed on Aharon's breastplate rearranging themselves to provide a divine answer.

### Rashi vs Tosafot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע זֶה סַנְהֶדְרִין, וְאֶבְיָתָר אֵלּוּ אוּרִים וְתוּמִּים. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: וּבְנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע עַל הַכְּרֵתִי וְעַל הַפְּלֵתִי. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָם כְּרֵתִי וּפְלֵתִי? כְּרֵתִי שֶׁכּוֹרְתִים דִּבְרֵיהֶם, פְּלֵתִי שֶׁמּוּפְלָאִים בְּדִבְרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Benayahu ben Yehoyada** corresponds to **the Sanhedrin,** and **Evyatar** corresponds to **the *Urim VeTummim*.** **And so it says** regarding Benayahu ben Yehoyada's position as head of the Sanhedrin: **"And Benayahu ben Yehoyada was over the *Kereti* and over the *Peleti*"** (II Samuel 20:23). **And why was** the Sanhedrin **called *Kereti UPeleti*?** It was called ***Kereti*** because **they were decisive [*koretim*] in their pronouncements.** It was called ***Peleti*** because **their pronouncements** and wisdom **were wondrous [*mufla'im*].**

Rashi and Tosafot read the verse differently. Rashi understands that Benayahu ben Yehoyada preceded the Kreiti uPleiti — that is, he came before the Urim veTumim, and the Kreiti uPleiti refers to the Urim veTumim itself. His identification of Benayahu as the head of the Sanhedrin is not derived from the verse but is more or less assumed. Tosafot objects: we have no need for a verse to establish that Evyatar was the Urim veTumim — he was the only Kohen left alive after Shaul's massacre of Nov ir haKohanim; that is obvious. What we do need a verse for is identifying Benayahu ben Yehoyada as head of the Sanhedrin. Therefore Tosafot reads the verse differently: עַל הַכְּרֵתִי וְעַל הַפְּלֵתִי means Benayahu was over the Kreiti uPleiti — he headed them. And the Kreiti uPleiti on this reading refers to the Sanhedrin itself, with the plural language שֶׁכּוֹרְתִים דִּבְרֵיהֶם שֶׁמֻּפְלָאִים בִּדְבְרֵיהֶם describing the body collectively. The Gemara also notes that although it quotes the verse as בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע, in Divrei HaYamim the name actually appears as יְהוֹיָדָע בֶּן בְּנָיָהוּ, and the Gemara equates the two.

### Sanhedrin's Role


One might have expected that asking permission from the Sanhedrin meant seeking their formal approval before undertaking a war of Reshut — the Sanhedrin representing the people, much as they lay their hands on the eglah arufah as the people's representatives. But Rashi, in his comment on וְנִמְלָכִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין, says: נוֹטְלִין מֵהֶם רְשׁוּת כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתְפַּלְלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם — they take permission from them so that the Sanhedrin will pray on their behalf. This is a striking Rashi. That you want the sages and the people praying for you is perfectly understandable, but that this is why you ask permission — that is unusual. You would normally assume that soliciting prayers happens after the decision is made, not as the process of authorization.

A further point sharpens this: the very next Rashi, on וְשׁוֹאֲלִים בָּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים, reads אִם יַצְלִיחוּ — asking whether they will succeed. So even the Urim veTumim is framed less as granting permission and more as seeking reassurance of success. The overall picture Rashi paints is of a king who has already decided, and who is gathering blessings, prayers, and divine assurance — not truly asking whether he may proceed. The formal permission structure may be less about granting authority and more about ensuring divine and communal support for what is already determined.

וְאַחַר כָּךְ שַׂר צְבָא הַמֶּלֶךְ — and only then are the orders given to Yoav, the general.

### Dovid Wakes Morning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַדָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מַאי קְרָא ״עוּרָה כְבוֹדִי עוּרָה הַנֵּבֶל וְכִנּוֹר אָעִירָה שָּׁחַר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Adda, and some say Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Idi, said:** From **what verse** is it derived that David's lyre would wake him at midnight? **"Awake, my glory; awake, harp and lyre; I will wake the dawn"** (Psalms 57:9). This means that the playing lyre has already woken, and now I must engage in Torah study until dawn.

אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַדָּא — Rav Yitzchak bar Adda asks: what is the verse showing that Dovid would rise so early — not merely at midnight to learn, but already involved in the affairs of kingship before sunrise? עוּרָה כְבוֹדִי עוּרָה הַנֶּבֶל וְכִנּוֹר אָעִירָה שָׁחַר — Bestir my honor, bestir the nevel and the kinnor; I will wake the morning. The culminating phrase is אָעִירָה הַשַּׁחַר — I will wake the morning. Rashi puts it beautifully: שְׁאָר מְלָכִים הַשַּׁחַר מְעוֹרְרָן — other kings need the morning to wake them, וַאֲנִי מְעוֹרֵר הַשַּׁחַר — I am up so early that I wake the morning.

## Kechatzos — Why Approx

### Harp as Alarm Clock

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: מֹשֶׁה לְעוֹלָם הֲוָה יָדַע, וְדָוִד נָמֵי הֲוָה יָדַע. וְכֵיוָן דְּדָוִד הֲוָה יָדַע, כִּנּוֹר לְמָה לֵיהּ? לְאִתְּעוֹרֵי מִשִּׁנְתֵּיהּ. וְכֵיוָן דְּמֹשֶׁה הֲוָה יָדַע, לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר ״כַּחֲצוֹת״? — מֹשֶׁה קָסָבַר: שֶׁמָּא יִטְעוּ אִצְטַגְנִינֵי פַּרְעֹה, וְיֹאמְרוּ ״מֹשֶׁה בַּדַּאי הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira** offered a different solution to the question of whether Moses and David knew exactly when it was midnight and **said:** Moses **certainly knew** when it was midnight, **and David also knew.** **If David knew,** then **why did he need the lyre?** The Gemara answers: He needed the lyre **to wake him from his sleep.** Similarly with regard to Moses, **since Moses knew** the precise moment of midnight, **why did he say: About midnight,** instead of: At midnight? Moses did so because he **maintained: Lest Pharaoh's astrologers err** and believe midnight to be earlier. Since no disaster would have occurred, **they would say: Moses is a liar.**

וְכֵיוָן דְּדָוִד הֲוָה יָדַע כִּנּוֹר לָמָּה לִי — if Dovid knew, then what was the harp for? לְאַתְּרוּיֵי מִשִּׁינְתֵּיהּ — to wake him from sleep. It was an alarm clock. He did not need the harp to tell him the time; he needed it to rouse him.

### Astrologers & Moshe

וְכֵיוָן דְּמֹשֶׁה הֲוָה יָדַע, לָמָּה לֵי לְמֵימַר כַּחֲצוֹת — if Moshe knew exactly when midnight was, why did he say approximately? מֹשֶׁה קָסָבַר שֶׁמָּא יִטְעוּ אִצְטַגְנִינֵי פַּרְעֹה — Moshe was concerned that Pharaoh's astrologers might make an error. This is why astrologers are specifically mentioned: in antiquity, time at night was reckoned by the stars, which is precisely what astrologers tracked. So perhaps they would misjudge the moment by a little, וְיֹאמְרוּ מֹשֶׁה בַּדַּאי הוּא — and they would say: Moshe is a liar. He said midnight and it did not come at exactly midnight.

### Eini Yodeia Maxim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר מָר: לַמֵּד לְשׁוֹנְךָ לוֹמַר אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ, שֶׁמָּא תִּתְבַּדֶּה וְתֵאָחֵז.
>
> **Translation:** Moses spoke in accordance with the principle **articulated by the Master: Accustom your tongue to say: I do not know, lest you become entangled in** a web of **deceit.**

דְּאָמַר מָר: לַמֵּד לְשׁוֹנְךָ לוֹמַר אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ — there is a teaching from Masechet Derech Eretz: train your tongue to say "I do not know," שֶׁמָּא תִּתְבַּדֶּה וְתֵאָחֵז — lest you fabricate and be caught in the error. The teaching addresses the human tendency to answer even when uncertain; you will be found out and your words will lose credibility.

What Moshe is doing goes even one step further. He actually knew the answer. But he was so concerned that people might perceive him as having been mistaken that he chose deliberate imprecision over correct precision. The logical inference is a *kal vachomer*: if someone who knows the answer should sometimes speak with reservation to avoid the appearance of error, how much more so should one who genuinely does not know resist the temptation to fake confidence.

### Rashi & Oral Torah


Rashi in his commentary on the Chumash chose this explanation — that Moshe said כַּחֲצוֹת so the astrologers would not call him a liar — and not the earlier answer of the Gemara that Moshe simply did not know. This is worth pausing over. Rashi so thoroughly integrates the world of *torah sheba'al peh* into the biblical narrative that readers of his Chumash commentary absorb particular Talmudic readings as if they were the plain meaning of the text. His commentary positions the learner seamlessly within the rabbinic universe. At the same time, one must remember that even when Rashi adopts a particular strand, the Gemara itself preserves a much wider range of interpretation, and what Rashi selects is one choice among many.

### Rav Ashi: Kaf Dimyon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בְּפַלְגָא אוּרְתָּא דִתְלֵיסַר נָגְהֵי אַרְבֵּסַר הֲוָה קָאֵי, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר מֹשֶׁה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, לְמָחָר כַּחֲצוֹת הַלַּיְלָה כִּי הָאִידָּנָא אֲנִי יוֹצֵא בְּתוֹךְ מִצְרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** This question is unfounded, as Moses **was standing at midnight of the thirteenth, leading into the fourteenth,** when he pronounced his prophecy, **and Moses told Israel** that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said** that **tomorrow,** at the exact time **like midnight tonight, I will go out into the midst of Egypt.** This indicates that the passage should not be understood to mean about midnight, an approximation; but rather, like midnight, as a comparison, likening midnight tomorrow to midnight tonight.

רַב אַשִׁי אָמַר: בְּפַלְגָא אוּרְחָא דִּתְלֵיסַר נָגֵי אַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה הֲוֵי קָאֵי — Rav Ashi offers yet another explanation. Here the *kaf* is not a *kaf* of approximation at all, but a *kaf* of comparison. When Moshe spoke at midnight of the 13th, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ מֹשֶׁה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — this is what he said: אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, לְמָחָר כַּחֲצוֹת הַלַּיְלָה כִּי הָאִידָּנָא — tomorrow, like midnight right now, at exactly this moment, אֲנִי יוֹצֵא בְּתוֹךְ מִצְרַיִם. The *kaf* there does not mean "approximately"; it means "like this very moment." Midnight of the 14th will be at precisely the same point of night as midnight of the 13th, when Moshe is speaking.

## Dovid as Posek Halacha

### Chasid Ani Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְדָוִד, שָׁמְרָה נַפְשִׁי כִּי חָסִיד אָנִי״. לֵוִי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק. חַד אָמַר: כָּךְ אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, לֹא חָסִיד אֲנִי? שֶׁכָּל מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב יְשֵׁנִים עַד שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת, וַאֲנִי ״חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה אָקוּם לְהוֹדוֹת לְךָ״. וְאִידַּךְ, כָּךְ אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, לֹא חָסִיד אֲנִי? שֶׁכָּל מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב יוֹשְׁבִים אֲגוּדּוֹת אֲגוּדּוֹת בִּכְבוֹדָם, וַאֲנִי יָדַי מְלוּכְלָכוֹת בְּדָם וּבְשָׁפִיר וּבְשִׁלְיָא כְּדֵי לְטַהֵר אִשָּׁה לְבַעֲלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further explores King David's character. It is said: "A prayer **of David…Keep my soul, for I am pious"** (Psalms 86:1–2). Levi and Rabbi Yitzḥak debated the meaning of this verse and how David's piety is manifest in the fact that he went beyond his fundamental obligations. **One said:** David's declaration of piety referred to his awakening during the night to pray, and **so said David before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, am I not pious? As all of the kings of the East and the West sleep until the third hour** of the day, **but** although I am a king like them, **"At midnight I rise to give thanks"** (Psalms 119:62). **And the other** Sage said: **David said the following before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, am I not pious? For all of the kings of the East and the West sit in groups** befitting **their honored** status, but I sit as a judge who issues rulings for the people. Women come with questions of ritual impurity and **my hands become soiled with** their **blood** as I labor to determine whether or not it is blood of impurity and she has menstruating woman status, **and** with **a fetus that miscarried** at a stage of development before it was clear whether or not it is considered a birth, **and** with **placenta,** which women sometimes discharge unrelated to the birth of a child. King David went to all this trouble **in order to render a woman ritually pure** and consequently permitted **to her husband.**

The Gemara turns now to a verse from Tehillim: שָׁמְרָה נַפְשִׁי כִּי חָסִיד אָנִי — protect my soul, for I am a chasid. לֵוִי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק disagree about what Dovid is saying here before God.

The first opinion: חַד אָמַר — one says that Dovid is telling God, לֹא חָסִיד אָנִי — am I not righteous? שֶׁכָּל מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב יְשֵׁנִים עַד שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת — all the kings of East and West sleep until three hours into the day, וַאֲנִי חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה אָקוּם לְהוֹדוֹת לָךְ — while I rise at midnight to praise You. He is pointing to his extraordinary dedication to divine service.

The second opinion interprets the claim differently. Dovid is not comparing when he wakes up, but what he does during his active hours. שֶׁכָּל מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב יוֹשְׁבִים אֲגוּדּוֹת אֲגוּדּוֹת בִּכְבוֹדָם — all other kings sit surrounded by their retinues in honor and glory, embodying their power and majesty. וַאֲנִי — what do I do?

### Dovid & Niddah Psak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא כָּל מָה שֶׁאֲנִי עוֹשֶׂה, אֲנִי נִמְלָךְ בִּמְפִיבֹשֶׁת רַבִּי, וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״מְפִיבֹשֶׁת רַבִּי, יָפֶה דַּנְתִּי? יָפֶה חִייַּבְתִּי? יָפֶה זִכִּיתִי? יָפֶה טִהַרְתִּי? יָפֶה טִמֵּאתִי?״, וְלֹא בּוֹשְׁתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **And not only** do I engage in activity considered to be beneath the station of a king, **but I consult my teacher, Mefivoshet,** son of King Saul's son, Jonathan, with regard to **everything that I do. I say to him: Mefivoshet, my teacher, did I decide properly? Did I convict properly? Did I acquit properly? Did I rule** ritually **pure properly? Did I rule** ritually **impure properly? And I was not embarrassed.** Forgoing royal dignity should make me worthy to be called pious.

יָדַי מְלוּכְלָכוֹת בְּדָם וּבְשָׁפִיר וּבְשִׁלְיָא — my hands are dirty with blood, embryo, and placenta, כְּדֵי לְטַהֵר אִשָּׁה לְבַעְלָהּ — in order to declare a woman pure for her husband. Rashi reminds us of much that was learned in Masechet Niddah: not all shades of blood render a woman a niddah, and a woman who miscarries may or may not be considered a yoledet depending on the state of the fetus and placenta, with corresponding implications for her tum'ah status. Dovid is saying that he dirties his hands with precisely this work — because his role is not merely that of king or poet, but of someone who cares for every individual and is immersed in the daily particulars of halacha.

### Dovid Talmid Chacham


This is a striking image, and one that tends to stay with anyone who has learned the Gemara. Here is Dovid HaMelech — not on his throne, not surrounded by his guard, but examining a placenta and inspecting blood. The contrast with the other kings is stark and very deliberately physical. But what is also fascinating is the picture the rabbis want to paint of Dovid: not merely the composer of Tehillim, not merely the king who follows God, but a talmid chacham, a posek halacha, someone to whom people bring she'alos and who issues rulings. Indeed there is something poignant about people bringing their most personal halachic questions to the king — this imagines a Dovid who participates in the intimate texture of every Jewish household.

One cannot help but note the resonance of "blood on his hands" with the verse in which Dovid is told he cannot build the *beis hamikdash* because his hands are full of blood. The Gemara here reframes that image: yes, his hands are full of blood — the blood of niddah examination, brought in service of taharat hamishpacha. And earlier in this very passage Dovid told the sages to go to war, which also leaves blood on his hands. The rabbis seem to be consciously taking the constellation of imagery around Dovid and blood and redirecting it toward an act of chesed and halachic service.

### Mefiboshet Consult

וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא, כָּל מַה שֶּׁאֲנִי עוֹשֶׂה, אֲנִי נִמְלָךְ בִּמְפִיבֹשֶׁת — not only that, but whatever I do in the halachic realm, I first consult Mefiboshet. The Gemara derives this from a wordplay on the name: מְפִיבֹשֶׁת רַבִּי — I say to him: "Mefiboshet my master" — יָפֶה דַנְתִּי? יָפֶה חִיַּבְתִּי? יָפֶה זִכִּיתִי? יָפֶה טִהַרְתִּי? יָפֶה טִמֵּאתִי? — Did I judge correctly? Was I right to obligate? To exempt? To declare pure? To declare impure? And ולֹא בֹשְׁתִּי — I am not embarrassed.

Notably, Dovid does not ask Mefiboshet before ruling but after. When someone comes to the king with a shaylah, the king must embody his role and give an answer; he cannot defer to someone else in the moment. But afterwards he checks his ruling, and if he was wrong, he can correct it and learn for the future.

Rashi explains the name itself: מְפִיבֹשֶׁת — בִּדְבְרֵי הֲלָכָה הַיּוֹצֵא מִפִּי הָיָה בּוֹשֵׁשׁ אוֹתִי — from the words that came out of his mouth there would be embarrassment for Dovid, שֶׁפְּעָמִים שֶׁהָיָה טוֹעֶה, וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ טָעִיתָ — because sometimes Dovid would be wrong and Mefiboshet would tell him so. The balance Rashi is striking: I was not so embarrassed as to refrain from asking, even though when he told me I had erred it was indeed embarrassing. The willingness to be exposed was the mark of Dovid's greatness.

### Neged Melachim Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מַאי קְרָא — ״וַאֲדַבְּרָה בְעֵדֹתֶיךָ נֶגֶד מְלָכִים וְלֹא אֵבוֹשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehoshua, son of Rav Idi, said: What verse** alludes to this? **"And I speak Your testimonies before kings and I will not be ashamed"** (Psalms 119:46). This verse alludes both to David's commitment to Torah, in contrast to the kings of the East and the West, as well as to the fact that he was not ashamed to discuss matters of Torah with Mefivoshet, a descendant of kings. David was not afraid to have his mistakes corrected by Mefivoshet.

אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מַאי קְרָא — what is the verse that supports this? וַאֲדַבְּרָה בְעֵדֹתֶיךָ נֶגֶד מְלָכִים וְלֹא אֵבוֹשׁ — I will speak of Your testimonies, Your laws, in the presence of kings and I will not be ashamed. This verse from the same chapter of Tehillim — the chapter containing חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה אָקוּם — speaks to two things simultaneously: that Dovid is not embarrassed to dirty his hands with the practical details of halacha rather than sit in royal splendor, and that he is not embarrassed to consult Mefiboshet and be corrected.

### Kilav vs. Mefiboshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: לֹא מְפִיבֹשֶׁת שְׁמוֹ אֶלָּא אִישׁ בּשֶׁת שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ מְפִיבֹשֶׁת? שֶׁהָיָה מְבַיֵּשׁ פְּנֵי דָּוִד בַּהֲלָכָה, לְפִיכָךְ זָכָה דָּוִד וְיָצָא מִמֶּנּוּ כִּלְאָב. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא ״כִּלְאָב״ שְׁמוֹ אֶלָּא ״דָּנִיאֵל״ שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ ״כִּלְאָב״ — שֶׁהָיָה מַכְלִים פְּנֵי מְפִיבֹשֶׁת בַּהֲלָכָה. וְעָלָיו אָמַר שְׁלֹמֹה בְּחָכְמָתוֹ ״בְּנִי אִם חָכַם לִבֶּךָ יִשְׂמַח לִבִּי גַם אָנִי״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״חֲכַם בְּנִי וְשַׂמַּח לִבִּי וְאָשִׁיבָה חֹרְפִי דָבָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *Tosefta* from a tannaitic tradition: **His name was not Mefivoshet, but rather Ish Boshet was his name. Why was** Ish Boshet **referred to as Mefivoshet? Because he would embarrass [*mevayesh*] David in matters of *halakha*.**  **Because** David was not embarrassed to admit his errors, **he merited that Kilav,** who, according to tradition, was exceedingly wise, **would descend from him.** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: His name was not Kilav; rather, his name was Daniel,** as it appears in a different list of David's descendants. **Why was he called Kilav? Because he would embarrass [*makhlim*] Mefivoshet,** the teacher or authority figure [av] **in matters of *halakha*.** **In his** book of **wisdom, Solomon said** about this wise son: **"My son, if your heart is wise, my heart will be glad, even mine"** (Proverbs 23:15), as David enjoyed witnessing his son Kilav develop into a Torah luminary to the extent that Kilav was able to respond to Mefivoshet. **And** Solomon **says** about Kilav: **"Be wise, my son, and make my heart glad, that I may respond to those who taunt me"** (Proverbs 27:11).

תָּנָא: לֹא מְפִיבֹשֶׁת שְׁמוֹ אֶלָּא אִישׁ בֹּשֶׁת שְׁמוֹ — his real name was not Mefiboshet but Ishboshet. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ מְפִיבֹשֶׁת — why was he called Mefiboshet? שֶׁהָיָה מְבַיֵּשׁ פְּנֵי דָוִד בַּהֲלָכָה — because he would embarrass Dovid in halacha. לְפִיכָךְ זָכָה דָוִד יָצָא מִמֶּנּוּ כִּלְאָב — and because Dovid was willing to subject himself to this, he merited that his son Kilav came forth from him.

וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא כִּלְאָב שְׁמוֹ אֶלָּא דָּנִיֵּאל שְׁמוֹ — Rabbi Yochanan says his real name was Daniel, as listed in Divrei HaYamim, and the Gemara reconciles the two names as referring to the same person. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ כִּלְאָב — why was he called Kilav? שֶׁהָיָה מַכְלִים פְּנֵי מְפִיבֹשֶׁת בַּהֲלָכָה — because he showed up Mefiboshet in halacha. The name encodes the story: כְּ-אָב — he shamed the "father," the halachic master, Mefiboshet. And the verse with which Shlomo praises this: בְּנִי אִם חָכַם לִבֶּךָ יִשְׂמַח לִבִּי גַּם אָנִי — my son, if your heart is wise, my heart too will rejoice — and further: חָכַם בְּנִי וְשַׂמַּח לִבִּי וְאָשִׁיבָה חוֹרְפִי דָבָר — my son has been wise, my heart has rejoiced, and I will respond to those who disgrace me.

That word חוֹרְפִי — those who disgrace me — is telling. The Gemara frames the dynamic between Dovid and Mefiboshet not merely as student and teacher but as something sharper: Mefiboshet appears to have delivered his corrections in a way that was genuinely humiliating, not simply constructive. That Dovid submitted to this and continued asking is all the more remarkable. And the sense of vindication when his son bested Mefiboshet suggests that the treatment was not entirely forgotten, even as Dovid's willingness to endure it is itself held up as the very reason he merited such a son.

### Dot Over Lulei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדָוִד מִי קָרֵי לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ ״חָסִיד״? וְהָכְתִיב ״לוּלֵא הֶאֱמַנְתִּי לִרְאוֹת בְּטוּב ה׳ בְּאֶרֶץ חַיִּים״, וְתָנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: לָמָּה נָקוּד עַל ״לוּלֵא״? — אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מוּבְטָח אֲנִי בְּךָ שֶׁאַתָּה מְשַׁלֵּם שָׂכָר טוֹב לַצַּדִּיקִים לֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא, אֲבָל אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם יֵשׁ לִי חֵלֶק בֵּינֵיהֶם אִם לָאו?! שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to David's statement, "Keep my soul, for I am pious," the Gemara asks: **Did David call himself pious? Isn't it written: "If I had not [*luleh***] **believed to look upon the goodness of the Lord in the land of the living"** (Psalms 27:13). The dots that appear over the word *luleh* in the text indicate doubt and uncertainty of his piety, and whether he was deserving of a place in the land of the living (see *Avot DeRabbi Natan* 34). **In the name of Rabbi Yosei, it was taught** in a *Tosefta*: **Why** do **dots** appear **over the word *luleh*,** as if there are some reservations? Because **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe. I** have every **confidence in You that You grant an excellent reward to the righteous in the World-to-Come** since God's ultimate goodness is manifest in the land of eternal life, **but** I still harbor uncertainty with regard to myself, and **I do not know whether or not I** definitely **have a portion among them.** In any case, apparently David was uncertain whether or not he deserved to receive a portion of God's reward for the righteous; how, then, could he characterize himself as pious? His concern does not prove anything, as King David knew that he was pious. He was simply concerned **lest a transgression** that he might commit in the future **will cause** him to lose his opportunity to look upon the goodness of the Lord in the land of the living.

וְדָוִד מִי קָרֵי לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ חָסִיד — but does Dovid call himself a chasid? The verse in Tehillim seems to contradict this: לוּלֵא הֶאֱמַנְתִּי לִרְאוֹת בְּטוּב **Shin-Kaf-Resh-Heh**' בְּאֶרֶץ חַיִּים — had I not believed I would see the goodness of God in the land of the living. The Gemara understands אֶרֶץ חַיִּים as referring to Olam Haba. And there is a tradition in the name of Rabbi Yosi: לָמָּה נָקוּד עַל לוּלֵא — why is there a dot over the word לוּלֵא? The dot signals qualification: yes, he said "had I not believed" — but perhaps sometimes his belief did falter. As the Yiddish expression captures: וַיִּשָּׁקֵהוּ מִיט פִּינְטְלֶעךְ — with dots, meaning with reservation. So the verse with the dot reads: had I not believed — with dots.

### Shema Yigrom HaChet

Why did his belief falter? מוּבְטָח אֲנִי בְּךָ שֶׁאַתָּה מְשַׁלֵּם שָׂכָר טוֹב לַצַּדִּיקִים לֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא — I have no doubt, God, that You reward the righteous in the world to come. וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם יֶשׁ לִי חֵלֶק בֵּינֵיהֶם אִם לָאו — but I do not know whether I will merit a share among them. Why not? שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא — perhaps sin will cause it to be lost.

This resolves the apparent contradiction. When Dovid says כִּי חָסִיד אָנִי, he is not being arrogant; he knows what he has achieved, and that knowledge is not immodest but accurate. Yet knowing what one has accomplished so far does not guarantee the future. The principle לֹא תַאֲמִין בְּעַצְמְךָ עַד יוֹם מוֹתְךָ — do not trust yourself until the day of your death — captures precisely this: you cannot be certain that even great accumulated merit will not be undone by future failure. Dovid holds both things simultaneously: a clear-eyed recognition of his own righteousness, and a persistent awareness of his own frailty. That combination is not contradiction but spiritual maturity.

### Yaakov's Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי, דְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: ״וְהִנֵּה אָנֹכִי עִמָּךְ וּשְׁמַרְתִּיךָ בְּכָל אֲשֶׁר תֵּלֵךְ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּירָא יַעֲקֹב מְאֹד״ אָמַר: שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא. כִּדְתַנְיָא ״עַד יַעֲבֹר עַמְּךָ ה׳, עַד יַעֲבֹר עַם זוּ קָנִיתָ״. ״עַד יַעֲבֹר עַמְּךָ ה׳״ — זוֹ בִּיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, ״עַד יַעֲבֹר עַם זוּ קָנִיתָ״ — זוֹ בִּיאָה שְׁנִיָּה. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: רְאוּיִם הָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לֵיעָשׂוֹת לָהֶם נֵס בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה לָהֶם בִּימֵי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן. אֶלָּא שֶׁגָּרַם הַחֵטְא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof that there is room for one to fear lest he commit a transgression in the future **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi, as Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi raised a contradiction** between two verses. **It is written** that God told Jacob in his vision of the ladder: **"Behold, I am with you and I guard you wherever you go"** (Genesis 28:15), yet when Jacob returned to Canaan and realized that Esau was coming to greet him, **it is written: "And Jacob became very afraid,** and he was pained" (Genesis 32:8). Why did Jacob not rely on God's promise? Jacob had concerns and **said** to himself: **Lest a transgression** that I might have committed after God made His promise to me **will cause** God to revoke His promise of protection. Apparently, at times, transgression does cause God's promise to go unfulfilled, **as it was taught** explicitly in a *baraita* with regard to the ostensibly redundant language in a verse in the Song of the Sea: **"Until Your people will cross, Lord, until the people You have acquired will cross.** You bring them in and plant them in the mountain of Your inheritance, the place, Lord, which You made for Your dwelling" (Exodus 15:16–17). The Gemara interprets homiletically that **until Your people will cross** refers to the **first entry** into Eretz Yisrael during the time of Joshua, while **until the people You have acquired pass over** refers to the **second entry** following the exile in Babylonia. **Based on** the juxtaposition of these two entries in this single verse, **the Sages said: Israel was worthy of having a miracle performed on its** behalf **in the time of Ezra** the scribe, just **as** one **was performed on their** behalf **in the time of Joshua bin Nun. However, transgression caused** the absence of a miracle.

רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי shows a parallel contradiction: the verse states וְהִנֵּה אָנֹכִי עִמְּךָ וּשְׁמַרְתִּיךָ בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר תֵּלֵךְ — God promised Yaakov He would be with him wherever he went. Yet when Yaakov returned, וַיִּירָא יַעֲקֹב מְאֹד — Yaakov was very afraid. How could he be afraid after an explicit divine promise?

The answer is the same: שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא. For Yaakov the formulation shifts slightly — שֶׁמָּא גָּרַם הַחֵטְא — perhaps sin has already caused the promise to be forfeited, whereas for Dovid the concern was about future sin. Yaakov feared that in the interval since the promise was given he might have sinned in ways that caused him to no longer deserve its fulfillment. The Gemara does not identify any specific sin; the concern is more of a generalized vigilance about the distance between divine promise and one's continuing worthiness of it. This is expressed in the verse אַשְׁרֵי אָדָם מְפַחֵד תָּמִיד — praised is the person who is always afraid — which the Gemara understands as referring to a constant awareness of the aveiros one may have committed or may yet commit.

### Second Entry & Sin


This brings the abstract concern of שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא into concrete historical relief. Yes, things are anticipated, promised, or anticipated by trajectory — and sometimes sin intervenes and the fulfillment is real but diminished.

### Rambam Nevuah Issue


This raises a significant question about the Rambam's treatment of nevuah. The Rambam states that a prophecy of good cannot be retracted — it is only a prophecy of doom that can be undone by teshuvah, as in the case of Yonah and Nineveh (עוֹד אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם וְנִינְוֵה נֶהְפָּכֶת). A negative prophecy that does not come to pass does not impugn the prophet, because the people may have done teshuvah and God had mercy. But if a prophet promises something good and it does not come about, that demonstrates he is a false prophet — because God never withdraws a promise of good.

How then does שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא work? The case of Yaakov received an explicit divine promise, and the case of the second entry involves what the Gemara treats as an anticipated divine blessing. Yet in both cases the full realization was diminished. There are various ways one could distinguish these cases — for instance, whether the promise was formally proclaimed as nevuah before the people — but the tension with the Rambam's principle is real and the Gemara's discussion here raises it squarely.

## Chatzos as Rabbinic (II)

### Chatzos Fits Neither

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים עַד חֲצוֹת: חֲכָמִים כְּמַאן סְבִירָא לְהוּ, אִי כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סְבִירָא לְהוּ — לֵימְרוּ כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וְאִי כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לְהוּ — לֵימְרוּ כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to explain what we learned in the mishna: **And the Rabbis say:** The time for the recitation of the evening *Shema* is **until midnight.** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **do they hold** in explaining the verse: "When you lie down"? **If** they explain this verse **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who says that "when you lie down" is the time when people customarily go to sleep, then **let** the Rabbis also **say** that the time for the recitation of *Shema* extends, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** until the end of the first watch. **And if** they explain this verse **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel** who says that "when you lie down" refers to the entire night, then **let** the Rabbis also **say** that one may recite the evening *Shema* until dawn, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel.**

וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים עַד חֲצוֹת — and the Chachamim say until midnight. The Gemara asks: what position are they holding? אִי כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סְבִירָא לְהוּ — if they hold like Rabbi Eliezer, who limits the time for evening Shema to the first few hours of the night (since that is when people typically go to sleep), then they should say a few hours into the night. ואִי כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לְהוּ — and if they hold like Rabban Gamliel, who says all night — because as Rashi explains, Rabban Gamliel understands that the mitzvah of Shema is tied to night itself rather than to the act of going to sleep, so the whole night qualifies — then they should say all night. Midnight fits neither position.

## Chatzos as Rabbinic (II)

### Fence Against Aveirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לְהוּ, וְהָא דְּקָאָמְרִי עַד חֲצוֹת, כְּדֵי לְהַרְחִיק אָדָם מִן הָעֲבֵירָה. כִּדְתַנְיָא, חֲכָמִים עָשׂוּ סְיָג לְדִבְרֵיהֶם, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא אָדָם בָּא מִן הַשָּׂדֶה בָּעֶרֶב, וְאוֹמֵר: ״אֵלֵךְ לְבֵיתִי וְאוֹכַל קִימְעָא וְאֶשְׁתֶּה קִימְעָא, וְאִישַׁן קִימְעָא, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אֶקְרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאֶתְפַּלֵּל״, וְחוֹטַפְתּוֹ שֵׁינָה וְנִמְצָא יָשֵׁן כָּל הַלַּיְלָה. אֲבָל אָדָם בָּא מִן הַשָּׂדֶה בָּעֶרֶב, נִכְנָס לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, אִם רָגִיל לִקְרוֹת — קוֹרֵא. וְאִם רָגִיל לִשְׁנוֹת — שׁוֹנֶה, וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִתְפַּלֵּל, וְאוֹכֵל פִּתּוֹ וּמְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the Rabbis **hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Gamliel,** and the fact **that they say until midnight is** in order **to distance a person from transgression. As it was taught** in a *baraita*, **the Rabbis created a "fence" for their pronouncements** with regard to the recitation of *Shema* **in order to prevent** a situation where **a person comes home from the field in the evening,** tired from his day's work, and knowing that he is permitted to recite *Shema* until dawn **says** to himself: **I will go home, eat a little, drink a little, sleep a little and then I will recite** *Shema* **and recite** the evening prayer. In the meantime, **he is overcome by sleep and ends up sleeping all night. However,** since one is concerned lest he fall asleep and fail to wake up before midnight in order to recite *Shema* at the appropriate time, **he will come from the field in the evening, enter the synagogue,** and until it is time to pray, he will immerse himself in Torah. **If he is accustomed to reading the Bible, he reads. If he is accustomed to learning** *mishnayot*, a more advanced level of study, **he learns. And** then **he recites** *Shema* **and prays** as he should. When he arrives home, **he eats his meal** with a contented heart **and recites a blessing.**

לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לְהוּ — in principle they hold like Rabban Gamliel that the time extends all night. וְהָא דְּקָאָמְרִי עַד חֲצוֹת, כְּדֵי לְהַרְחִיק אָדָם מִן הָעֲבֵירָה — the reason they said until midnight is to distance a person from transgression, as we learned in the baraita: חֲכָמִים עָשׂוּ סְיָג לְדִבְרֵיהֶם — the rabbis made a fence around their words.

### Worker & Sleep Trap

כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא אָדָם בָּא מִן הַשָּׂדֶה בָּעֶרֶב — a man should not come home from the field in the evening, וְיֹאמַר: אֵלֵךְ לְבֵיתִי — and say let me go home first, וְאֹכַל קִמְעָה, וְאֶשְׁתֶּה קִמְעָה, וְאִישַׁן קִמְעָה — I'll have a little to eat, a little to drink, a little nap, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אֶקְרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאֶתְפַּלֵּל — and then I'll daven. וְתַחְטְפֶנּוּ שֵׁינָה — and sleep will seize him, וְנִמְצָא יָשֵׁן כָּל הַלַּיְלָה — and he will sleep the whole night.

Instead: נִכְנַס לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת — before going home, go to shul. What is fascinating is the Gemara inserts: אִם רָגִיל לִקְרוֹת קוֹרֵא, אִם רָגִיל לִשְׁנוֹת שׁוֹנֶה — if you are accustomed to read, read; if accustomed to learn mishnah, learn. First do a little learning to make the transition from a hard day's work, to get yourself into the right mental state for davening. Then וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִתְפַּלֵּל — say Shema and daven. Then go home, eat your bread, and make a berachah. That is the proper sequence.

### Chayav Misah — Why?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָל הָעוֹבֵר עַל דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים חַיָּיב מִיתָה. מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי חַיָּיב מִיתָה, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא אוֹנֶס שֵׁינָה. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמַּאן דְּאָמַר ״תְּפִלַּת עַרְבִית רְשׁוּת״, קָא מַשְׁמַע לָן דְּחוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes with a warning: **Anyone who transgresses the pronouncements of the Sages is liable to** receive the **death** penalty. This is a startling conclusion. **What is different here that it is taught that he is liable to** receive the **death penalty?** There is no unique stringency apparent in the rabbinic restriction on the recitation of *Shema*. The Gemara offers two answers, explaining that the conclusion of the *baraita* essentially stems not from the magnitude of the transgression, but from concern that the "fence" created around this particular mitzva may be neglected. **If you wish, say** that one returning from work is quite anxious to go to sleep, and due to the risk that he will be **overcome by sleep,** he must be particularly vigilant in the recitation of *Shema*. **And if you wish, say** instead that strong language is employed here in order **to exclude** the opinion of **he who says that** although the morning prayer and the afternoon prayer are mandatory, **the evening prayer is optional.** Therefore, **it teaches us** that the evening prayer **is mandatory,** and anyone who transgresses the pronouncement of the Sages in this regard is liable to receive the death penalty.

וְכָל הָעוֹבֵר עַל דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים חַיָּב מִיתָה — anyone who transgresses this rabbinic ruling is liable to death. The Gemara asks: מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי חַיָּב מִיתָה — why here specifically does the language of חַיָּב מִיתָה appear, when elsewhere a violation of rabbinic enactment does not typically carry such language?

Two answers are given. The first: אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא אוֹנֶס שֵׁינָה — because here the compelling force of sleep makes the failure so likely that we need to impress upon you with maximum urgency that you must not put yourself in that position. The second: אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא לְאַפּוּקֵי מִמַּאן דְּאָמַר תְּפִלַּת עַרְבִית רְשׁוּת — to exclude the opinion that Maariv, the evening prayer, is only optional. There is a debate about whether the evening Shemoneh Esrei is truly obligatory. By using the language of חַיָּב מִיתָה, the baraita signals that both the Shema and the Shemoneh Esrei of Maariv are genuine obligations, not to be taken lightly.

One might note that even setting midnight as a deadline may not by itself create urgency in a world without electric lights, where nobody is routinely awake until midnight in any case. The real concern is not that people will casually push things off until after midnight; it is that they will intend to daven and then fall asleep. The fence of midnight and the language of חַיָּב מִיתָה are both aimed at that specific failure mode.

## Geulah Samuch Tefillah

### Order Supports Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר, קוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִתְפַּלֵּל. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵיזֶהוּ בֶּן הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא — זֶה הַסּוֹמֵךְ גְּאוּלָּה לִתְפִלָּה שֶׁל עַרְבִית. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אוֹמֵר: תְּפִלּוֹת בָּאֶמְצַע תִּקְּנוּם.
>
> **Translation:** In this *baraita*, **the Master said** that when one returns from work in the evening, he enters the synagogue, **recites *Shema*, and prays.** From this *baraita*, we see that at night, just as during the day, one first recites *Shema* and then prays. This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Who** is assured **of** a place in **the World-to-Come? It is** one **who juxtaposes** the blessing of **redemption,** recited after *Shema*, **to the evening prayer. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: The prayers were instituted** to be recited **between** the two recitations of *Shema*. According to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, one recites the morning *Shema*, then recites all of the prayers and only after the recitation of the evening prayer does he recite the evening *Shema*.

אָמַר מָר: קוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִתְפַּלֵּל — the baraita stated: you say Shema and then you daven. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — this supports Rabbi Yochanan, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵיזֶהוּ בֶּן עוֹלָם הַבָּא — who is a person who merits Olam Haba? זֶה הַסּוֹמֵךְ גְּאֻלָּה לִתְפִלָּה שֶׁל עַרְבִית — one who juxtaposes the geulah to the tefillah in the evening. You say Shema, culminating in גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל, and immediately go into Shemoneh Esrei, just as in the morning.

### Tefillot B'Emtza

אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: תְּפִלּוֹת בָּאֶמְצַע תִּקְּנוּם — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi holds that the Shemoneh Esrei prayers were instituted in the middle, meaning the structure is: Shema in the morning, then Shemoneh Esrei; in the evening, Shemoneh Esrei first, and then Shema. The Shema of each service is flanked on the outside, making morning Shema the first thing after waking and evening Shema the last thing before sleeping.

### Geulah at Night?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְּגִי? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא. דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר גְּאוּלָּה מֵאוּרְתָּא נָמֵי הָוֵי, אֶלָּא גְּאוּלָּה מְעַלַּיְיתָא לָא הָוְיָא אֶלָּא עַד צַפְרָא. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי סָבַר כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הָוְיָא אֶלָּא מִצַּפְרָא — לָא הָוְיָא גְּאוּלָּה מְעַלַּיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Although the practical difference between these two positions is clear, the Gemara seeks to determine: **With regard to what do they disagree? If you wish, say** that they disagree over the interpretation of **a verse; if you wish, say** instead that they disagree on a point of **logic.** **If you say** that they disagree on a point of **logic,** then the argument relates to the redemption recited after *Shema*, whose focus is the exodus from Egypt, the first redemption. The question is whether that redemption began at night, which would render it appropriate to juxtapose redemption to the blessing of the evening prayers as well, in prayer for immediate redemption. Or, perhaps, the redemption from Egypt only began during the day. **Rabbi Yoḥanan holds: Redemption occurred in the evening as well;** however, **the full-fledged redemption was only in the morning.** Since the redemption began in the evening, it is appropriate to juxtapose the blessing of redemption to the daily evening prayer. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi,** on the other hand, **holds: Since** full-fledged redemption **only occurred in the morning,** and the redemption of the previous evening **was not a full-fledged redemption,** there is no need to juxtapose the blessing of redemption to the evening prayer.

בְּמַאי קָא מִפַּלְּגִי — what is the underlying dispute? The Gemara offers two possible frameworks. The first is a matter of סְבָרָא, independent reasoning about the nature of the Exodus. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: גְּאוּלָּה מֵאוּרְתָּא נַמִּי הָוֵי — there was a genuine beginning of geulah at night. It was with מַכַּת בְּכוֹרוֹת, the plague of the firstborn at midnight, that the process of the Exodus began. אֶלָּא גְּאוּלָּה מְעַלְּיָיתָא לָא הָוִיָא אֶלָּא צַפְרָא — the full, consummate geulah did not occur until the morning at the Yam Suf, but the beginning was genuinely at night. Therefore when we say גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל in the evening Shema, it is a real mention of geulah, and it follows that we juxtapose it to Shemoneh Esrei just as in the morning.

רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הָוִיָא אֶלָּא צַפְרָא, לָאו גְּאוּלָּה מְעַלְּיָיתָא הִיא — since the true, complete geulah happened in the morning, what occurred at night was not a full-fledged geulah. Therefore although we do say the third paragraph of Shema and do say גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל at night, it does not carry the same weight, and we need not juxtapose it to Shemoneh Esrei. This debate is closely tied to the broader question addressed in a later mishnah in Berachot: is there a mitzvah to remember *yetziat mitzrayim* at night? And does the requirement to recite the third paragraph of Shema, which speaks of redemption, apply equally at night?

### Mekish Shechivah Kimah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ, דִּכְתִיב ״בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ״. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: מַקִּישׁ שְׁכִיבָה לְקִימָה, מָה קִימָה — קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאַחַר כָּךְ תְּפִלָּה, אַף שְׁכִיבָה נָמֵי — קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאַחַר כָּךְ תְּפִלָּה. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי סָבַר: מַקִּישׁ שְׁכִיבָה לְקִימָה: מָה קִימָה — קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע סָמוּךְ לְמִטָּתוֹ, אַף שְׁכִיבָה נָמֵי — קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע סָמוּךְ לְמִטָּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is not a difference over a point of logic, but over the interpretation of **a verse. Both derived their opinions from the same verse: "When you lie down, and when you rise."** Both interpreted that the juxtaposition in this verse of the recitation of *Shema* at night and the recitation of *Shema* in the morning draws a parallel between them. **Rabbi Yoḥanan holds:** The verse **juxtaposes lying down and rising. Just as when one rises, the recitation of** *Shema* **is followed by prayer,** as everyone agrees that in the morning one juxtaposes redemption to the morning prayer, **so too, when one lies down, the recitation of** *Shema* **is followed by prayer. And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi maintains:** The verse **juxtaposes lying down and rising** in a different sense. **Just as when one rises,** he recites *Shema* **adjacent to** rising from **his bed,** as the verse, when you rise, means when one awakens, **so too when one lies down, he recites** *Shema* **adjacent to** lying down in **his bed.** Therefore, the recitation of the evening *Shema* should be performed as close as possible to the moment when one actually lies down.

וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא — **if you wish, say** instead that they disagree over the interpretation of **a verse:** דִּשְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ — both Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi derive their positions from the same verse: בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ — when you lie down and when you rise. The verse connects lying down and rising up, and the question is the direction of the connection.

רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: מַקִּישׁ שְׁכִיבָה לְקִימָה — lying down is compared to rising. מַה קִימָה קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאַחַר כָּךְ תְּפִלָּה — just as when rising you say Shema and then daven, אַף שְׁכִיבָה נַמִּי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאַחַר כָּךְ תְּפִלָּה — so too when lying down you say Shema and then daven. The structural parallel runs in the same direction each time.

רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי סָבַר: מַקִּישׁ שְׁכִיבָה לְקִימָה in a different sense — מַה קִימָה קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע סָמוּךְ לְמִטָּתוֹ — just as rising, Shema is said right next to the bed, the first thing after getting up, אַף שְׁכִיבָה נַמִּי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע סָמוּךְ לְמִטָּתוֹ — so too lying down, Shema should be said right at the bedside, the very last act before sleep. On this reading, the parallelism of בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ is actually more naturally fulfilled by placing Shema at the temporal edges of sleep: the last thing at night, the first thing in the morning. This makes Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's position in some ways a more elegant reading of the verse, even though the practical result is that Shemoneh Esrei and Shema are not juxtaposed in the evening.

### Hashkiveinu Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיב מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא, בָּעֶרֶב מְבָרֵךְ שְׁתַּיִם לְפָנֶיהָ וּשְׁתַּיִם לְאַחֲרֶיהָ. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בָּעֵי לִסְמוֹךְ, הָא לָא קָא סָמֵךְ גְּאוּלָּה לִתְפִלָּה, דְּהָא בָּעֵי לְמֵימַר ״הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, it is a mitzva to recite *Shema* before the evening prayer. **Mar, son of Ravina, raises an objection** from a mishna: How can one do that? We learn in a later mishna: **In the evening, one recites two** blessings **prior to** the recitation of *Shema* **and two** blessings **afterward. And if you say** that **one must juxtapose** redemption to prayer, **doesn't he fail to juxtapose redemption to prayer, as he must recite: Help us lie down [*hashkivenu*],** the blessing recited after the blessing of redemption, which constitutes an interruption between redemption and prayer?

מֵיתִיב מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא — Mar Bereih deRavina raises an objection. We say two berachot before the evening Shema and two after it. The second berachah after Shema is הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ, Hashkiveinu, which intervenes between גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל and Shemoneh Esrei. אִי אָמְרַת... בָּעֵי לִסְמוֹךְ — if you hold there is a concept of semikat geulah litfillah in the evening, the Hashkiveinu berachah breaks the juxtaposition. הָא לָא קַסְמֵיךְ — so you are not actually joining them.

### Sefasai Tiftach Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן ״הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ״ — כִּגְּאוּלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, שַׁחֲרִית הֵיכִי מָצֵי סָמֵיךְ? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּתְּחִלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״ה׳ שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח״, וּלְבַסּוֹף הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״יִהְיוּ לְרָצוֹן אִמְרֵי פִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **They say** in response: **Since the Sages instituted** the practice of reciting: **Help us lie down, it is considered one extended blessing of redemption,** and therefore does not constitute an interruption. **As if you fail to say** that the sections added by the Sages are considered no less significant than the original prayers, **then can one juxtapose redemption to prayer** even **in the morning? Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Before** every prayer **one recites** the verse: **"Lord, open my lips, that my mouth may declare Your glory"** (Psalms 51:17) as a prelude to prayer? **Afterward, one recites** the verse: **"May the words of my mouth and the meditations of my heart be acceptable before You"** (Psalms 19:15). Doesn't the verse: Lord, open my lips, constitute an interruption between redemption and prayer?

הָכִי אָמְרִי: כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ, כְּגְּאוּלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא — they answer: Since the rabbis instituted Hashkiveinu as part of the evening liturgy, it is regarded as an extension of the geulah itself, a "long geulah." This answer is, as one must note, formally striking. The content of גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל and Hashkiveinu are quite different in theme: the former is about the historical redemption from Egypt, the latter about protection through the night. Yet the Gemara holds that once the rabbis instituted Hashkiveinu as part of the evening liturgy, it becomes absorbed into the liturgical unit of geulah and does not constitute an interruption.

### Shema as Parenthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָתָם כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן לְמֵימַר ״ה׳ שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח״ — כִּתְפִלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא. הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן לְמֵימַר ״הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ״ — כִּגְּאוּלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, there, since the Sages instituted** that one must **recite: Lord, open my lips, it is considered as an extended prayer** and not as an interruption. **Here, too,** with regard to the evening prayer, **since the Sages instituted to recite** the blessing **Help us lie down, it is considered as one extended** blessing of **redemption.**

אֶלָּא הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן לְמֵימַר ה׳ שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח, כְּתְפִלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא — since the rabbis ordained that this is how Shemoneh Esrei opens, it becomes part of Shemoneh Esrei itself. Indeed, the universally accepted practice is that Shemoneh Esrei begins with ה׳ שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח. הָכָא נַמִּי כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן לְמֵימַר הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ, כִּגְּאוּלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא — and so too Hashkiveinu, having been instituted by the rabbis, becomes part of the geulah literature and not an interruption of the semikhah.

What is philosophically interesting here is the way the rabbis' institutional act transforms the status of a berachah. The content does not change. But the fact that the rabbis required it as part of the liturgy reshapes its halachic and conceptual category. This is a broader principle that will recur as we continue through the masechet: when the Sages fix a text or practice as part of the liturgical sequence, they do not merely add it; they redefine the units of which the liturgy is composed.

### Concluding Reflection: Shema as Parenthesis Around Life — Love, Liturgy, and Tension Between Mitzvah and Prayer


The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and the interplay of Shema and Shemoneh Esrei throughout Maariv, illuminate a persistent tension in the masechet between two ways of understanding what we are doing. From one vantage point, Shema is a discrete mitzvah — an act of declaration tied to specific times, morning and evening, with its own berachot and its own requirements of intention and sequence. From another vantage point, Shema is part of an integrated liturgy that shapes the rhythm of the entire day, folding together confession, praise, memory, petition, and love into a structure that brackets human experience.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's position captures something profound about Shema as בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ: the first thing at waking, the last at sleeping. On this understanding, Shema is a parenthesis around our lives — a framing of each day within the love and covenant that define who we are. The most important thing, the declaration that opens and closes each day, is the relationship itself: the love for God proclaimed in the first paragraph, the commitment to carry that love into every moment of lying down and rising up. The question of whether that declaration is immediately followed by Shemoneh Esrei or not is, in this sense, secondary to what the declaration itself is doing: situating every day, from its first moment to its last, within the foundational reality of אֲהַבְתָּ אֵת **Shin-Kaf-Resh-Heh**' אֱלֹקֶיךָ.

The tension between these two understandings — Shema as mitzvah and Shema as the frame of life — is one that the masechet will continue to explore.
