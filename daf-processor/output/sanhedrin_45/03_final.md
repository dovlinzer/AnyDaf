# Sanhedrin 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Achan's Confession

### Achan Atonement Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן שֶׁכִּיפֵּר לוֹ וִידּוּיוֹ וְכוּ׳? תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁכִּיפֵּר לוֹ וִידּוּיוֹ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מֶה עֲכַרְתָּנוּ יַעְכֳּרְךָ ה׳ הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה״. הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה אַתָּה עָכוּר, וְאִי אַתָּה עָכוּר לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. וּכְתִיב: ״וּבְנֵי זֶרַח זִמְרִי וְאֵיתָן וְהֵימָן וְכַלְכֹּל וְדַרְדַּע כֻּלָּם חֲמִשָּׁה״. מַאי ״כּוּלָּם חֲמִשָּׁה״? כּוּלָּן חֲמִשָּׁה הֵן לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that Achan's **confession achieved atonement** for him? The Sages taught likewise in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that Achan's **confession achieved atonement** for him? **As it is stated: "And Joshua said: Why have you brought trouble on us? The Lord shall trouble you this day"** (Joshua 7:25). Joshua said to Achan: **On this day** of your judgment **you are troubled, but you will not be troubled in the World-to-Come. And** elsewhere **it is written: "And the sons of Zerah were Zimri and Ethan and Heman and Calcol and Dara, five of them in all"** (I Chronicles 2:6). **What** do the words **"five of them in all"** serve to teach? **All five** sons **are** destined **to** receive a share in **the World-to-Come.** According to the Sages, Zimri is Achan, son of Zerah, as will be explained. Since Achan has a share in the World-to-Come, his confession must have achieved atonement for him.

The daf begins at the bottom of 44b, picking up with a *baraita* that asks: *minayin shekiper lo viduyo* — how do we know that Achan's confession atoned for him? The source is the verse in which Yehoshua says to Achan: *ma achartanu ya'acharcha Hashem bayom hazeh* — "as you have troubled us, so shall God trouble you this day." The *drash* is that the word *hayom hazeh* — "today" — implies a limitation: today you are cursed, but not for *olam haba*. Because he confessed, Achan retains his portion in the World to Come.

A second verse confirms this. The sons of Zerach are listed: *Zimri, Eitan, Heiman, Calcol, and Dardah* — and they are counted as five. Since we know that Achan was *Achan ben Zerach*, he must appear somewhere in that list. The Gemara concludes that *Zimri* is a name for Achan. Just as the verse about Yaakov's sons — *vayihyu venei Yaakov shneim asar* — treats all twelve sons as equal in their standing, so too here: *ma kulam chamisha kulam chamisha l'olam haba* — just as all five brothers share equally in *olam haba*, so too does Achan.

### Zimri/Achan Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״זִמְרִי״, וּכְתִיב ״עָכָן״. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַד אָמַר, עָכָן שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ זִמְרִי? שֶׁעָשָׂה מַעֲשֵׂה זִמְרִי. וְחַד אָמַר, זִמְרִי שְׁמוֹ, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ עָכָן? שֶׁעִיכֵּן עֲוֹנוֹתֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** In one place, among the five sons of Zerah, **it is written: "Zimri,"** without any mention of Achan (I Chronicles 2:6), **and** in another place **it is written:** "And Joshua took **Achan,** son of Zerah" (Joshua 7:24). **Rav and Shmuel** both say that Zimri and Achan are one and the same, but they disagree about his real name. **One** of them **says: His** real **name was Achan. Why** then **was he called Zimri?** He was called Zimri **because he acted like Zimri,** i.e., just as Zimri engaged in sexual intercourse with a Midianite woman, Achan engaged in intercourse with a betrothed young woman. **And** the other **one says: His** real **name was Zimri. Why** then **was he called Achan?** He was called Achan **because he acted like a snake [*she'iken*]** and caused the punishment **of the sins of the Jewish people.**

This leads directly to the question of the dual naming. The verse is written as *Zimri* and yet elsewhere as *Achan*. Rav and Shmuel dispute the matter.

### Rav/Shmuel Dispute

One opinion holds that Achan was his true name, and he was called *Zimri* because *she'asa ma'aseh Zimri* — he acted like Zimri, committing a grievous sin that brought death upon the Jewish people. The other opinion holds that Zimri was his true name, and he came to be called Achan because *she'hichin avonoteihem shel Yisrael* — he set in motion, or caused to roll upon Israel, the punishment for their sins. Rashi explains the word *hichin* through the image of *ke'achna* — like a serpent — evoking the sense of one who causes consequences to come crashing down upon others.

## Execution Confession

### How to Confess

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לְהִתְוַודּוֹת כּוּ׳. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר כּוּ׳. לְנַקּוֹת עַצְמָן, וִינַקּוּ עַצְמָן! כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְהוֹצִיא לַעַז עַל בָּתֵּי דִינִין וְעַל הָעֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **if** the condemned man **does not know how to confess,** they say to him: Say simply: Let my death be an atonement for all my sins. **Rabbi Yehuda says** that if he knows that he was wrongfully convicted, he should say: Let my death be an atonement for all my sins except for this sin. The Sages counter that if such a confession is acceptable, anyone who is sentenced to execution would state it **to clear himself** in the eyes of the public. The Gemara challenges: **Let them clear themselves.** Why should the court intervene if the condemned man wishes to proclaim his innocence? The Gemara answers: They intervene **so as not to cast aspersions on the courts and on the witnesses** who testified against him.

The *Mishnah* teaches that if a condemned person does not know how to perform *viduy*, the court teaches him what to say. Rabbi Yehuda goes a step further: if the person knows that the witnesses against him were *mezuman* — properly warned and gathered — he may say, "Let my death be an atonement for all my sins, except for this one, of which I am innocent."

### Rabbis' Objection

The Rabbis, however, do not permit this formulation. At first glance, the objection seems odd — if only a truly innocent person would say such a thing, why not allow it? But the reasoning becomes clear: the concern is not primarily about the guilty borrowing the language of the innocent. Rather, we do not want to introduce any formulation that casts ill repute upon the *beis din*, making it appear that they are executing an innocent person. The Gemara states this explicitly: *kedei shelo l'hotzi la'az al batei dinim* — so as not to bring disrepute upon the courts — and adds *v'al ha'edim* — and upon the witnesses as well.

### Man Exonerates Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁיָּצָא לֵיהָרֵג. אָמַר: אִם יֵשׁ בִּי עָוֹן זֶה, לֹא תְּהֵא מִיתָתִי כַּפָּרָה לְכׇל עֲוֹנוֹתַי. וְאִם אֵין בִּי עָוֹן זֶה, תְּהֵא מִיתָתִי כַּפָּרָה לְכׇל עֲוֹנוֹתַי, וּבֵית דִּין וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל מְנוּקִּין, וְהָעֵדִים לֹא תְּהֵא לָהֶם מְחִילָה לְעוֹלָם. וּכְשֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ חֲכָמִים בַּדָּבָר, אָמְרוּ: לְהַחְזִירוֹ אִי אֶפְשָׁר, שֶׁכְּבָר נִגְזְרָה גְּזֵירָה. אֶלָּא יֵהָרֵג, וִיהֵא קוֹלָר תָּלוּי בְּצַוַּאר עֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: An incident** occurred **involving a person who was being taken out to be executed** after having been convicted by the court. **He said: If I committed this sin** for which I am being executed, **let my death not be an atonement for all my sins; but if I did not commit this sin** for which I am being put to death, **let my death be an atonement for all my sins. And the court** that convicted me **and all** the people of **Israel are clear** of responsibility, **but the witnesses** who testified falsely against me **will never be forgiven. And when the Sages heard this, they said:** It is **impossible to bring him back** to court and reconsider the verdict, **as the decree has already been decreed. Rather, he shall be executed, and the chain** of responsibility for his wrongful execution **hangs around the necks of the witnesses.**

A *baraita* then relates a striking case. A man being led out for execution declared: "If I am guilty of this sin, let my death not atone for any of my other sins. But if I am innocent of this sin, let my death atone for all my sins — the *beis din* and all of Israel are absolved, and the witnesses who bore false testimony against me should never receive atonement." The power of this statement lies in its structure: he is willing to forfeit atonement for his other sins in order to make his declaration of innocence. He does not blame the *beis din* — he absolves them entirely, placing the responsibility squarely on the witnesses. This sounds entirely sincere. And of course, once a person learns how to sound sincere, others may imitate the form without the substance.

### Cannot Reverse Verdict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא דְּקָא הָדְרִי בְּהוּ סָהֲדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that he should be executed? **Is it in his power** to have his sentence overturned just because he says he is innocent? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state that the condemned man is executed even **when the witnesses retracted** their testimony.

When the Sages heard this, they were shaken. Nevertheless, *amru l'hachziro ee efshar shekvar nigzar gzeira* — to reverse the ruling was impossible, for the verdict had already been rendered. Without new admissible evidence, a verdict cannot be reversed. So the decision was: *yeihareg* — he must be executed — *vekola taluy betzavar edim* — and let the weight of it hang around the necks of the witnesses.

Even though one might imagine that, at least in principle, permission should have been sought before making such a declaration, the fact is that even had he asked, we likely would not have permitted it — because even a statement that explicitly exonerates the *beis din* still reflects poorly on them if it becomes known that they executed someone who proclaimed his innocence in so compelling a manner. But he said it, and now what is to be done? The possibility that an innocent person is being executed cannot be acted upon once procedure has run its course.

### Testimony Irreversible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הָדְרִי בְּהוּ, מַאי הָוֵי? כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיד, שׁוּב אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וּמַגִּיד! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא יָהֲבִי טַעְמָא לְמִילְּתַיְיהוּ, כִּי הָהוּא מַעֲשֶׂה דְּבַעְיָא מֹכְסָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Even **if** the witnesses **retracted** their testimony, **what of it?** It is still clear that the condemned man is to be executed, as the *halakha* is that **once** a witness has **stated** his testimony, **he may not then state a revision** of that testimony. In other words, a witness's retraction of his testimony has no validity. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state that the condemned man is executed **even when** the witnesses retracted their testimony and **gave an explanation for** having lied in **their** initial **statement.** This is **like that incident involving Ba'aya the tax collector,** where it was discovered that witnesses had falsely accused the son of Rabbi Shimon ben Shataḥ in revenge for the son's having sentenced to death for sorcery the witnesses' relatives.

The Gemara then sharpens the point. One might think the whole discussion is obvious — of course we proceed, since his word alone cannot reverse a verdict. So the Gemara clarifies: *lo tzricha d'hadri behu sahadei* — the novel case is where the witnesses themselves have retracted their testimony. Even so, the principle of *keivan shehigid shuv eino chozer umagid* — once testimony has been given, it cannot be withdrawn — applies. A retraction of testimony is not admissible.

The Gemara pushes further: the case is even more extreme — *af al gav d'ka yahavi ta'ama l'milsaihu* — not only do the witnesses retract, but they provide a convincing explanation of why they testified falsely, and all the surrounding circumstances support the claim that their original testimony was fabricated. Even then, procedure binds us. This is a deeply uncomfortable conclusion. In monetary cases, a judge has considerably more latitude — *ein l'dayan ela mah she'einav ro'os* — but in capital cases, the formal rules govern even when the outcome seems manifestly unjust.

### Baya & Shimon Story

The Gemara here includes — in some versions in brackets, suggesting it may be a later insertion — the story of *Baya machsa*, the tax collector, and Shimon ben Shatach. The story, reconstructed largely from the Yerushalmi, runs as follows: an evil tax collector and a great *tzaddik* died on the same day. During the commotion of the funeral, their bodies were switched and each was buried in the other's grave. The *talmid* of the *tzaddik* was distraught, wondering what his *rebbi* could have done to deserve burial in the grave of a *rasha*. The *rebbi* appeared to him in a dream and explained that there had been one sin on his part and one exceptional *mitzvah* on the tax collector's part that had resulted in this reversal — but in *olam haba*, the *tzaddik* was in a good place and the tax collector was suffering in *gehinnom*, with the hinge of the *gehinnom* gate resting upon him.

The *talmid* asked how long the tax collector would remain there, and was told: until Shimon ben Shatach dies and takes his place. This led to the question of what Shimon ben Shatach had done wrong — the answer being that he had failed to act against a group of witches. This detail feeds directly into the next *mishnah*. Shimon ben Shatach then devised a plan to capture and hang all the witches at once.

The relatives of the executed witches, enraged, hired false witnesses to testify against Shimon ben Shatach's son, accusing him of a capital crime. The witnesses could not be refuted through the standard procedure, and so he was led out to be executed. At the last moment, the witnesses themselves admitted they had been paid to lie, and it was evident to everyone that the testimony was fabricated. And yet — they could not halt the execution. Shimon ben Shatach's son was killed. This is the paradigm case for the Gemara's ruling: even when it is all but certain that the witnesses lied, once testimony has been given, it cannot formally be retracted, and procedure takes its course.

This is, as the Gemara acknowledges, deeply troubling. The counter-consideration is equally sobering: once you allow discretion to interrupt procedure when things seem obviously wrong, what prevents that discretion from being catastrophically abused? The formal rules exist precisely because we cannot trust case-by-case intuitions, however compelling they appear in the moment. A similar logic applies to the case of *eidim zomemin*: if a pair of alibi witnesses comes forward to counter the original testimony — *imanu heyitem* — we formally credit the *mezimim* even if many others could theoretically provide the same alibi. We follow formal rules. It is hard to live with in capital cases, and it will be a theme to which the tractate returns.

## Clothing at Stoning

### Strip Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאִישׁ מְכַסִּין אוֹתוֹ פֶּרֶק אֶחָד מִלְּפָנָיו, וְאִשָּׁה שְׁנֵי פְּרָקִים – בֵּין מִלְּפָנֶיהָ בֵּין מִלְּאַחֲרֶיהָ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ עֶרְוָה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הָאִישׁ נִסְקָל עָרוֹם, וְאֵין הָאִשָּׁה נִסְקֶלֶת עֲרוּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that a man is stoned naked but a woman is not stoned naked. With regard to this matter **the Sages taught** a related *baraita*: **They cover a man's** genitals with **one piece** of cloth **in the front, and a woman** is covered with **two pieces** of cloth, **both in the front and in the back, because all of** that area **is nakedness,** which may not be viewed. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. But the Rabbis say: A man is stoned naked, but a woman is not stoned naked.**

The *Mishnah* rules that when the condemned person is four *amot* from the *beis hasekila*, he is stripped of his garments. Rabbi Yehuda holds that a man is covered only in front, and a woman is covered both in front and behind — the operative word being that in both cases they are essentially brought to the place of execution nearly naked. The Sages disagree: *haish niskal arom* — the man is stoned naked — *ve'ein haisha niskal aruma* — but the woman is not stripped at all.

The *baraita* specifies the practical details: for the man, *perek echad milfanav* — one expanse of cloth in front. For the woman, *shenei prakim bein milfanav bein machoreha* — two pieces of cloth, front and back. The reason offered for the additional covering of the woman, in Rabbi Yehuda's formulation, is *mipnei shekula erva* — because the entire body of a woman is considered *erva*, invoking the principles of *sear be'isha erva* and *tefach be'isha erva*. This creates an immediate paradox: if every part of her body is considered nakedness, why strip her at all? And yet Rabbi Yehuda does strip her, while adding more covering than he provides the man. It is a strange balancing act.

On the distinction between objective and subjective *erva*: the question of whether one may recite a *bracha* in the presence of an exposed body part concerns the objective, formal category of nakedness — and in that context, for men, essentially only the genitals are at issue. For women, there is a distinction between the formal-objective category and the subjective dimension — what is considered nakedness in the presence of men, which shifts the analysis considerably. These are matters for a separate discussion.

### Sexual Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְרָגְמוּ אֹתוֹ״. מַאי ״אוֹתוֹ״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning** behind the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who say that a man is stoned naked, but a woman is not? The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "And** all the congregation **shall stone him"** (Leviticus 24:14). **What** does the verse intend to teach when it emphasizes that they stone **"him"?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא אוֹתוֹ וְלֹא אוֹתָהּ, וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְהוֹצֵאתָ אֶת הָאִישׁ הַהוּא אוֹ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה הָהִיא״? אֶלָּא מַאי ״אוֹתוֹ״? אוֹתוֹ בְּלֹא כְּסוּתוֹ, הָא אוֹתָהּ בִּכְסוּתָהּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אוֹתוֹ בְּלֹא כְּסוּתוֹ, לָא שְׁנָא אִישׁ וְלָא שְׁנָא אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that this serves to teach that they stone only **him,** the man, **but not her,** i.e., women are not punished with stoning, there is a difficulty. **As isn't it written** explicitly: **"And you shall bring forth that man or that woman**…and stone them with stones until they die" (Deuteronomy 17:5)? **Rather, what** does the verse mean to teach when it stresses that they stone **"him"?** If he is a man, they stone just **him, without his clothes, but** if the condemned party is a woman, they stone **her with her clothing. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The emphasis on the word **"him"** teaches that they stone him alone, i.e., **without his clothes,** but as is the case with all other punishments stated in the Torah, **there is no difference** for **a man and no difference** for **a woman,** meaning the same *halakha* applies to both men and women.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבָּנַן חָיְשִׁי לְהִרְהוּרָא, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא חָיֵשׁ לְהִרְהוּרָא? וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שְׁמַעְנָא לְהוּ, דִּתְנַן: הַכֹּהֵן אוֹחֵז בִּבְגָדֶיהָ – אִם נִקְרְעוּ נִקְרְעוּ, וְאִם נִפְרְמוּ נִפְרְמוּ, עַד שֶׁמְּגַלֶּה אֶת לִבָּהּ וְסוֹתֵר אֶת שְׂעָרָהּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיָה לִבָּהּ נָאֶה לֹא הָיָה מְגַלֵּהוּ, וְאִם הָיָה שְׂעָרָהּ נָאֶה לֹא הָיָה סוֹתְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that the Rabbis are concerned** that the sight of a naked woman will arouse sexual **thoughts** among the onlookers, **and Rabbi Yehuda is not concerned** about such sexual **thoughts? But didn't we hear them** say just the **opposite, as we learned** in a mishna (*Sota* 7a) with regard to a *sota*, a woman suspected of adultery by her husband, and who was made to undergo the ordeal of the bitter waters: And **the priest grabs hold of her clothing** and pulls it, without concern about what happens to it. **If** the clothes **are torn, they are torn; if the stitches come apart, they come apart.** And he pulls her clothing **until he reveals her heart,** i.e., her chest. **And** then **he unbraids her hair. Rabbi Yehuda says: If her heart was attractive he would not reveal it, and if her hair was attractive he would not unbraid it.** This seems to indicate that it is Rabbi Yehuda who is concerned about the sexual thoughts of the onlookers.

The Gemara now asks: *mai taama d'rabbanan* — what is the Rabbis' reason for not stripping the woman? The Gemara answers from the word *oto* in the verse *veragmu oto* — "you shall stone him." One might read *oto* as excluding women from stoning altogether, but this cannot be right since another verse explicitly includes women: *vehotzeta et haish hahu o et haisha hahi*. Rather, *oto belo ksuto* — "him, without his garment" — means the man is to be stoned stripped. *Ve'ota bichsuta* — but "her," it implies, is to be stoned with her garments on.

Rabbi Yehuda reads *oto belo ksuto* as meaning nakedness is required — *lo shena ish velo shena isha* — regardless of male or female. The question then becomes: what is actually driving this disagreement about how to read the verse?

The Gemara makes the underlying motivation explicit. The Rabbis read it to exclude stripping the woman because *rabbanan chaishi l'hirhura* — they are concerned about sexual thoughts arising in the onlookers. Rabbi Yehuda, who speaks in terms of *kulo erva*, is by contrast *lo chaishi l'hirhura* — not concerned about *hirhur* among those present. It should be stated plainly: the concern the Gemara voices is entirely about men viewing a woman's body and experiencing sexual thoughts. The Gemara expresses no corresponding concern about the reverse. This asymmetry is built into the framing, and it should be acknowledged rather than explained away.

### Sotah Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, שֶׁמָּא תֵּצֵא מִבֵּית דִּין זַכָּאָה וְיִתְגָּרוּ בָּהּ פִּירְחֵי כְּהוּנָּה. הָכָא הָא מִקַּטְלָא. וְכִי תֵּימָא: אָתֵי לְאִיתְגָּרוֹיֵי בְּאַחְרָנְיָיתָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: גְּמִירִי, אֵין יֵצֶר הָרָע שׁוֹלֵט אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁעֵינָיו רוֹאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: There,** in the case of a *sota*, **this is the reason** that Rabbi Yehuda says that the priest does not reveal the woman's chest or unbraid her hair: **Perhaps** the *sota* **will leave the court** having been proven **innocent, and the young priests** in the Temple who saw her partially naked **will become provoked by** the sight of **her. Here,** in the case of a woman who is stoned, **she is killed** by being stoned, and there is no concern about the onlookers' becoming provoked after her death. The Gemara comments: **And if you would say** that the fact that she is killed is irrelevant to their having sexual thoughts because the onlookers **will be provoked with regard to other** women, this is not a concern, as **Rabba says:** It **is learned** as a tradition **that the evil inclination controls only that which** one's **eyes see.**

This creates an apparent contradiction. In the laws of *sotah*, the *kohen* holds the woman's garments and tears them — *im nikre'u nikre'u v'im nifremu nifremu* — until her heart, meaning her breast, is exposed, and her hair is undone. There, Rabbi Yehuda says: *im hayah libah naveh lo hayah megalehu* — if her breast was attractive, he would not expose it. *Im hayah se'arah naveh lo hayah soisro* — if her hair was attractive, he would not undo it. So in the case of *sotah*, Rabbi Yehuda is concerned about *hirhur*, and the Rabbis apparently are not. Here, their positions appear reversed.

### Death Changes Stakes

Rabbah resolves the contradiction for Rabbi Yehuda: *hasam hainu ta'ama shema taitzei m'beis din zaka'ah v'yisgaru bah pirchei kehuna* — there, in the *sotah* case, there was concern that she might be exonerated and leave the court, having already been publicly exposed. The young priests who had seen her body or hair would then pursue her, and that pursuit would be able to find its footing precisely because she had already been publicly displayed in that state. So Rabbi Yehuda's concern is not her dignity as such — it is the concrete consequence: that men who have seen her body will be provoked to pursue her. If she is attractive, and she is exonerated and goes out into the world, those men will act on what they saw. Here, at the execution, she will not go out. There is no subsequent action to be feared.

But then what of the concern that seeing one woman stripped will provoke lust toward other women? Rabbah answers: *ein yetzer hara sholet ela b'mah she'einav ro'os* — the *yetzer hara* — here meaning specifically the sexual drive — has power only over what a person is actually seeing. It does not transfer: a man who sees a woman in one context does not, by virtue of that encounter, go on to pursue someone else. The *yetzer hara sholet* is specifically the impulse that leads to action, and action is bounded by the present visual experience.

This is a significant distinction. The Gemara elsewhere is concerned that a man who sees something sexually provocative may experience *hirhur* that leads to nocturnal seminal emission — which is a concern even when no overt action results. But apparently that level of concern is not sufficient to alter execution procedure. It is only the *yetzer hara sholet* — the drive that leads to actual transgression — that we must prevent, and that drive is bounded by *mah she'einav ro'os*. This principle has ramifications for later discussions, including the question of *kol isha* — one hears on the radio but does not see — but those discussions belong elsewhere.

The resolution for the Rabbis runs parallel: in the *sotah* case, the Rabbis are not concerned about *hirhur* because the verse itself mandates the public exposure — *venivasseru kol hanashim velo taasina kizmizatchena* — "and all women will be chastened and not do as she has done." There, public shaming serves the purpose of the verse's own imperative. Here, at the execution, *ein lecha yissur gadol mizeh* — there is no greater rebuke than death itself. The execution teaches the lesson; no additional shaming is required.

### Dignity vs Pain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לֶיעְבֵּיד בַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ״ – בְּרוֹר לוֹ מִיתָה יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **two** forms of chastening, both stoning and humiliation, **should be done to her, Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The **verse states: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18), teaching that even with regard to a condemned prisoner, **select a good,** i.e., a compassionate, **death for him.** Therefore, when putting a woman to death by stoning, she should not be humiliated in the process.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא: דְּרַב נַחְמָן תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, וְהָכָה בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר בִּזְיוֹנֵי דְאִינִישׁ עֲדִיף לֵיהּ טְפֵי מִנְּיָחָא דְגוּפֵיהּ, וּמָר סָבַר נְיָחָא דְגוּפֵיהּ עֲדִיף מִבִּזְיוֹנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that whether one rules in accordance with the statement **of Rav Naḥman is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** and according to Rabbi Yehuda there is no mitzva to select a compassionate death. The Gemara refutes this: **No,** it may be **that everyone agrees with** the opinion **of Rav Naḥman, and here they disagree about this:** One **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds:** Minimizing **one's degradation is better for him than** seeing to **his physical comfort,** i.e., than minimizing his physical pain. Therefore, the Rabbis view the more compassionate death as one without degradation, even if wearing clothes will increase the pain of the one being executed, as the clothes will absorb the blow and prolong his death. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds that one's physical comfort is better** for him **than** minimizing **his degradation,** and therefore the one being executed prefers to be stoned unclothed, without any chance of the clothing prolonging his death, even though this increases his degradation.

This raises a final question: if not stripping the woman is appropriate, why then strip the man? Does *v'ahavta lere'acha kamocha* not apply equally? The Gemara insists it does. Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha says: *v'ahavta lere'acha kamocha — beror lo misah yafa* — you shall love your neighbor as yourself: choose for him a good death. This is a remarkable application. The person being executed is, at the very moment of execution for a grave sin, still the subject of *v'ahavta lere'acha kamocha*. The obligation to treat him as one would wish to be treated does not lapse. Even if there is value in a deterrent lesson for others, introducing gratuitous shame is foreclosed by this principle.

The Gemara therefore frames the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages as a disagreement not about whether *v'ahavta lere'acha kamocha* applies — everyone agrees it does — but about what a person actually prefers. *Mar savar bizyoni de'inish adif leh tefei minei nicha degufa* — one holds that a person cares more about avoiding shame than about avoiding physical pain, such that stripping him and causing the added discomfort of execution without garments is preferable to the shame of being exposed. *Umar savar nicha degufa adif mibizyonei* — the other holds that physical ease outweighs shame, so stripping him to allow a smoother, less friction-laden execution is the greater act of *mita yafa*.

The difference between men and women, then, is not that *v'ahavta lere'acha kamocha* applies only to one. It is that the calculation of *mita yafa* differs. For the man, the shame of stripping is not as acute — or the physical benefit of stripping outweighs the shame — and so stripping serves *mita yafa*. For the woman, according to the Sages, the shame overwhelms any physical benefit, and so *mita yafa* requires keeping her clothed. And beyond this, the verse *oto belo ksuto* provides an independent textual anchor.

## Stoning Procedure

### Platform Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית הַסְּקִילָה הָיָה גָּבוֹהַּ שְׁתֵּי קוֹמוֹת. אֶחָד מִן הָעֵדִים דּוֹחֲפוֹ עַל מׇתְנָיו. נֶהְפַּךְ עַל לִבּוֹ, הוֹפְכוֹ עַל מׇתְנָיו. וְאִם מֵת בָּהּ – יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The place of stoning** from which the condemned man is pushed to his death **is** a platform **twice the height** of an ordinary person. He is made to stand at the edge of the platform, and then **one of the witnesses** who testified against him **pushes him** down **by the hips,** so that he falls face up onto the ground. If **he turned over onto his chest,** with his face downward, the witness **turns him** over **onto his hips. And if he dies through this** fall to the ground, the obligation to stone the transgressor is **fulfilled.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָאו, הַשֵּׁנִי נוֹטֵל אֶת הָאֶבֶן וְנוֹתְנוֹ עַל לִבּוֹ. אִם מֵת בָּהּ – יָצָא, וְאִם לָאו – רְגִימָתוֹ בְּכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַד הָעֵדִים תִּהְיֶה בּוֹ בָרִאשׁוֹנָה לַהֲמִיתוֹ וְיַד כׇּל הָעָם בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the condemned man does **not** die from his fall, **the second** witness **takes the stone** that has been prepared for this task **and places,** i.e., casts, **it on his chest.** And **if he dies with** the casting of **this** first stone, the obligation to stone the transgressor **is fulfilled. And if** he does **not** die with the casting of this stone, then **his stoning** is completed **by all of the Jewish people,** i.e., by all the people who assembled for the execution, **as it is stated: "The hand of the witnesses shall be first upon him to put him to death, and afterward the hand of all the people"** (Deuteronomy 17:7).

The *Mishnah* states that the *beis hasekila* — the stoning platform — was two heights of a man. One of the witnesses would push the condemned off by the hips; if he flipped and landed on his back, the witness would push him again. *Ve'im mes bah yatza* — if this killed him, good. If not, the second witness would take the stone and place it on his chest. If that killed him, good; if not, *regimaso bechol Yisrael* — the entire community would participate in stoning him, as the verse states: *yad ha'edim tihyeh bo barishona lehamiso veyad kol ha'am ba'achrona*.

But why exactly two heights? A *baraita* adds that if the person's own height is included, we arrive at three heights total. And the Gemara asks: *umi bainan kulei hai* — do we really need all that height? After all, a pit capable of killing is defined as *asara tefachim* deep, far less than the height implied here. The resolution is: Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha says — again — *v'ahavta lere'acha kamocha: beror lo misah yafa*. The height is not merely sufficient to cause death; it is chosen to cause death quickly and certainly. Rashi says *sheyamus maher* — so that he will die quickly. A greater height ensures a faster death.

### Don't Deform Body

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: וְקוֹמָה שֶׁלּוֹ, הֲרֵי כָּאן שָׁלֹשׁ. וּמִי בָּעֵינַן כּוּלֵּי הַאי? וּרְמִינְהוּ: מָה בּוֹר שֶׁהוּא כְּדֵי לְהָמִית – עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, אַף כֹּל כְּדֵי לְהָמִית – עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: Adding together the height of the platform, which is twice the height of an ordinary person, **and** the condemned man's own **height,** it turns out that **there is** a height **here three** times the height of an ordinary person. The Gemara asks: **Do we** really **need all that** height to kill him? The Gemara **raises a contradiction** to the *baraita* from what was taught in a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 50b) when discussing the *halakhot* of damage caused by a pit: Why does the Torah specify a pit when one is liable for the damage caused by any type of excavation that he digs into the ground? This teaches that **just as a pit that is** of **sufficient** depth **to cause** one's **death** from falling into it is at least **ten handbreadths** deep, **so too, any** other excavations **that are** of **sufficient** depth **to cause** one's **death** may be no less than **ten handbreadths.** If a fall of ten handbreadths is sufficient to kill a person, why must the platform from which the condemned man is pushed be twice the height of an ordinary person?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ״, בְּרוֹר לוֹ מִיתָה יָפָה. אִי הָכִי, לַיגְבְּהֵיהּ טְפֵי! מִשּׁוּם דְּמִינַּוַּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** The **verse states: "You shall love your neighbor as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18), teaching that even with regard to a condemned man, **select a good,** i.e., a compassionate, **death for him.** Therefore, even though the one being executed is likely to die from a fall from a lesser height, a platform is built that is twice the height of an ordinary person in order to ensure a quick and relatively painless death. The Gemara challenges: **If so, they should raise** the platform even **higher.** The Gemara answers: This is not done, **because** if the condemned man were pushed from a higher platform, **he would become** seriously **disfigured,** and this would no longer be considered a compassionate form of death.

Then why not make it even higher — so that death on impact would be virtually instantaneous? The Gemara answers: *mishum deminuval* — because the body would become deformed. Even the condemned person would not want his body completely shattered and disfigured. This too falls under *v'ahavta lere'acha kamocha*: even a faster death is not worth purchasing at the cost of the body being destroyed in that manner. The height, therefore, is calibrated precisely — high enough for a quick death, not so high as to deform the body.

### Push vs Stone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד מִן הָעֵדִים דּוֹחֲפוֹ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִין שֶׁבִּדְחִיָּיה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יָרֹה״. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁבִּסְקִילָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״סָקוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **one of the witnesses** who had testified against the condemned party **pushes him** off the platform. Concerning this *halakha* **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that the punishment of stoning can be fulfilled **by pushing** the condemned party from a high place, so that he dies from his fall? **The verse states** with regard to those who crossed the boundaries that were set up around Mount Sinai and touched the mountain: "Take heed to yourselves, that you not go up into the mountain, or touch the border of it; whoever touches the mountain shall be put to death; no hand shall touch him, but he shall be stoned or **shall be thrown down"** (Exodus 19:12–13). **And from where** is it derived that this punishment can be fulfilled **with** actual **stoning? The verse states: "He shall be stoned."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁבִּסְקִילָה וּבִדְחִיָּיה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״סָקוֹל יִסָּקֵל אוֹ יָרֹה יִיָּרֶה״. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם מֵת בִּדְחִיָּיה יָצָא? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ יָרֹה יִיָּרֶה״. מִנַּיִין שֶׁאַף לְדוֹרוֹת כֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived that this punishment is sometimes fulfilled both **by stoning and by pushing,** i.e., if the transgressor did not die from his fall, he is then stoned? **The verse states: "He shall be stoned or shall be thrown down." And from where** is it derived **that if** the condemned man **died from the pushing,** the obligation to stone him **has been fulfilled,** and there is no further need to actually stone him? **The verse states: "Or shall be thrown down,"** with the term "or" indicating that only one of the two options must be fulfilled. **And from where** is it derived **that this** is the *halakha* not only at Mount Sinai, but **even** with regard **to** future **generations?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״סָקוֹל יִסָּקֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states: "He shall be stoned [*sakol yissakel* ],"** with the doubled verb form indicating that the *halakha* applies in all places and at all times.

A *baraita* derives the different stages of the execution procedure from the verses dealing with *Har Sinai*. *Yaroh yerah* — "he shall surely be shot" — is read as referring to pushing off the platform. *Sakol yisakel* — "he shall surely be stoned" — refers to actual stoning. The combination of the two terms teaches that both methods are to be used if necessary. The word *o* — "or" — teaches that if the pushing alone caused death, the second stage is not required. The fact that pushing comes first, rather than the more intuitive stoning, reflects the overarching desire to reduce the violence of the procedure and its character as a public mob event. The dual form *sakol yisakel* with its future-tense *yud* establishes that this procedure applies not only at *Har Sinai* but for all generations.

### Second Witness Stone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָאו, עֵד הַשֵּׁנִי נוֹטֵל אֶת הָאֶבֶן. נוֹטֵל? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר, אֶבֶן הָיְתָה שָׁם מַשּׂוֹי שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם, נוֹטְלָהּ וְנוֹתְנָהּ עַל לִבּוֹ. אִם מֵת בָּהּ – יָצָא. וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, תִּיקְשֵׁי לָךְ הִיא גּוּפַהּ: מַשּׂוֹי שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי אָדָם, נוֹטְלָהּ וְנוֹתְנָהּ עַל לִבּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **if** the condemned party does **not** die from his fall, **the second witness takes the stone** that had been prepared for this task and casts it on his chest. The Gemara asks: Does the witness **take** the stone by himself? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: A stone was there** with **a weight** that could be lifted only by **two men.** The witness **takes it and places,** i.e., casts, **it on** the condemned party's **chest. If he dies with** the casting of **this** stone, the obligation to stone the transgressor **is fulfilled.** This indicates that one individual could not lift the stone by himself. The Gemara answers: **And according to your reasoning,** the *baraita* **itself should pose a difficulty for you,** as it first states that the stone was of **a weight** that could be lifted only by **two men,** and then it says that the witness **takes it and places it on** the condemned party's **chest,** apparently acting alone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּמַדְלֵי לַהּ בַּהֲדֵי חַבְרֵיהּ, וְשָׁדֵי לַהּ אִיהוּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתֵיתֵי מֵרַזְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** both the mishna and the *baraita* should be explained as follows: The second witness **lifts** the stone **together with the other** witness, **but** then the second witness alone **casts it so that it should fall with force** upon the condemned party's chest, as two people working together cannot aim their throw with precision.

Returning to the *Mishnah*: if the push did not kill the condemned, the second witness takes the stone. The Mishnah's language of *notel* — taking — is relatively spare. A *baraita* of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar specifies that *even haytah sham masui shenei benei adam* — the stone was of a size requiring two people to lift. The *baraita* then says the witness *notlah venosnah al libo* — takes it and places it on his chest — which seems to imply one person. The Gemara resolves this: two people lift the stone together, and then one of them holds it while *veshadei lei ihu* — the other throws it down alone — *ki heichi detisei mizayar* — so that it comes down with force and velocity. Two people throwing together cannot generate the same directed force as one person casting alone.

This is, frankly, a more violent image than the simple *Mishnah* language suggests. The stone requires two men to lift it; it is not merely rested on the man's chest — it is hurled down upon him. The design serves *mita yafa*: swift and decisive. If even this does not kill him, *regamuhu kol Yisrael* — the community participates — but by that point the condemned is already lying on the ground, so this is far removed from the image of a mob chasing a person.

### People Never Stoned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לָאו, רְגִימָתוֹ כּוּ׳. וְהָתַנְיָא: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא שָׁנָה בָּהּ אָדָם! מִי קָאָמֵינָא דְּעָבֵיד? דְּאִי מִצְּרִיךְ קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And if** the condemned party does **not** die with the first stone, then **his stoning** is completed by all of the Jewish people. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Never did a person repeat** the stoning, as the condemned party always died with the first stone cast upon him? The Gemara answers: **Did we say that they acted** so? Rather, **we said that if** the condemned party did not die with the casting of the first stone and **it was necessary** to try again, the stoning is completed by all of the people who assembled for the execution.

A *baraita* then states: *me'olam lo shanah bah adam* — it never actually came to a second stoning by the community. The push and the witnesses' stone always sufficed. This sits alongside similar rulings in the tractate — *ir hanidachat*, *eidim zomemin* — where the Gemara notes *lo hayah velo nivra*: the case never occurred or was never implemented. Here too, the community stoning remained a theoretical final stage that was never reached in practice.

The Gemara clarifies: *mi kamina de'avid* — who said it ever happened? *De'mitzrich kamina* — the *Mishnah* is speaking hypothetically. It lays out the theoretical procedure should each stage fail, but in practice it never progressed to community participation.

### Bury Instruments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אֶבֶן הָיְתָה כּוּ׳. וְהָתַנְיָא: אַחַת אֶבֶן שֶׁנִּסְקָל בָּהּ, וְאַחַת עֵץ שֶׁנִּתְלֶה עָלָיו, וְאֶחָד סַיִיף שֶׁנֶּהֱרָג בּוֹ, וְאֶחָד סוּדָר שֶׁנֶּחְנָק בּוֹ – כּוּלָּן נִקְבָּרִין עִמּוֹ. לָא צְרִיכָא דִּמְתַקְּנִי וּמַיְיתִי אַחֲרִינֵי חִלּוּפַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita* that **a stone was** there, indicating that a special stone was kept in the place of stoning and used time and time again for this purpose. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The stone with which** the condemned party **was stoned and the tree on which** his corpse **was hung, or the sword with which he was killed, or the cloth with which he was strangled, all** of them **are buried** together **with him?** If so, the same stone could not have been used for every execution. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach **that** after the stones were buried with the transgressor, **they would prepare other** stones **and bring them** to the place of stoning **in their place.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִקְבָּרִין עִמּוֹ. וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין נִקְבָּרִין עִמּוֹ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מַאי ״עִמּוֹ״? עִמּוֹ בִּתְפִיסָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that all of the items used in the execution **are buried** together **with** the transgressor. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita* that **they are not buried** together **with** the transgressor? **Rav Pappa said: What** does the *baraita* mean when it says that these items are buried together **with him?** They are not actually buried together with him in the same grave; rather, they are buried **within his grasp,** i.e., in the area surrounding his grave.

A *baraita* teaches that all the instruments used in the execution — the stone by which he was stoned, the wood of the gallows, the sword, the *sudar* used for strangulation — *kulan nikbarim imo* — all are buried with the condemned. The principle behind this is striking: we do not maintain a standing collection of execution instruments, as though execution is a routine feature of communal life. The instruments are buried, the memory of the event is put away. It had to be done; it should not define who we are.

A second *baraita*, however, seems to say *ein nikbarim imo* — they are not buried with him. Rav Pappa resolves the apparent contradiction: *mai imo* — what does "with him" mean? Not in the same grave, but *b'arba amot shelo* — within his four *amot*, in his immediate area. The instruments are buried near him but not placed in his grave. He is not interred with the symbols of his execution upon him. They are removed from circulation, buried out of sight, but they do not accompany him into the grave itself.
