# Shabbat 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Liquids: Standards

### Common Uses Principle

The daf begins approximately fifteen lines into 78a, picking up in the middle of the *Mishnah* that catalogs the minimum amounts (*shiurim*) for which one becomes liable for carrying various liquids on *Shabbat*. Before proceeding to the final line of that *Mishnah*, it is worth recalling the elegant principle that *Abaye* articulated in the preceding discussion. He observed that when a substance has both a common use and an uncommon use, we follow the common use to determine the relevant *shiur* — even if that results in a more lenient (smaller) minimum. The rationale is that significance is calibrated by the primary function people actually have for an object. When there are multiple common uses, however, we adopt the strictest (smallest) amount, because even that small a quantity is rendered significant by the fact that it serves a recognized, ordinary purpose.

This principle, while clarifying, still leaves open a subtle question. Water, for instance, is far more commonly used for drinking than for preparing an eye compress. Yet the *Gemara* treats the compress application as a "common use" sufficient to define water's *shiur* for that purpose. The definition of "common use" is thus itself a contextual and perhaps even geographic judgment — the *Gemara* raises elsewhere whether the *shiur* might vary by region — and the broader question of how fixed or malleable these *shiurim* are in the face of changing circumstances remains a live one throughout this section of the *Gemara*.

### Revi'it Standard

With that framework in place, the *Mishnah* concludes: *veshear kol hamashkin berevi'it* — all other liquids go by a *revi'it* (a quarter-log, approximately three and a half ounces). This is the default measure for any liquid not specifically addressed by one of the earlier exceptions.

A *baraita* then expands on this: *dam vechol minei mashkin berevi'it* — blood and all other types of liquid go by a *revi'it*. Tosafot notes that the word *vekol* here should be read as *veshear*, meaning "and all other" liquids beyond those already discussed. The intent is not that every liquid whatsoever requires a *revi'it*, but that blood and the remaining unspecified liquids, having not been granted a smaller *shiur*, default to the standard *revi'it*.

### Blood Exceptions

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar dissents regarding blood. He holds that blood does not require a full *revi'it*; rather, the minimum is *kedei lichol ayin achat* — enough to apply to one eye. His reasoning is that blood was used medicinally for a condition called *barakit*, which Rashi identifies as a type of raised growth or mote on the eye. Since people would apply blood to one eye for this ailment, even so small an amount carries practical significance. (The cosmetic application, which one might assume would involve both eyes, is not the operative use here — and notably, the use need not be one that can be performed on *Shabbat* itself. An object's significance for the purposes of carrying liability is determined by its value and use during the week. Just as one may be liable for carrying a match even though striking it on *Shabbat* is forbidden, blood used medically during the week has its *shiur* defined by that use.)

The *Gemara* then asks which blood is specifically used for *barakit*: it is *dama detarnagola bara*, the blood of a wild rooster. Whether this means the *shiur* for all blood is thereby defined, or only for wild rooster blood specifically, remains somewhat unclear from the *Gemara*'s formulation.

Rabban Gamliel agrees with the measure of one eye's worth but arrives there by a different route. He says blood is applied for *erod*, which Rashi describes as white spots on the eye. The blood used for *erod* is *dama dekharshutin'a* — the blood of a bat. To remember which blood treats which condition, the *Gemara* offers a mnemonic: *gavva le-gavva* — "outside to outside, inside to inside." The wild rooster (*bara*, meaning "outside/wild") corresponds to *barakit*, a protrusion that is, as it were, external. The bat, which Rashi notes inhabits enclosed spaces within a city, corresponds to *erod*, the white spots within the inner part of the eye. Again, whether this *shiur* applies to all blood or only to these specific types is left without a fully explicit resolution.

### Removal vs. Setting Aside

The *baraita* then introduces a critical distinction: *bameh devarim amurim bemotzi* — when do these smaller *shiurim* apply? When one is the *motzi*, the person who simply carries out the liquid. *Aval be-matznia kol shehu chayav* — but in a case where the item was *matznia* (set aside as personally significant), even the smallest amount renders one liable.

Rabbi Shimon responds with the inverse formulation: *bameh devarim amurim be-matznia* — these smaller *shiurim* apply to the case where the item was set aside and given personal significance. *Aval bemotzi eino chayav ela bi-revi'it* — but the ordinary person who merely carries out (without having designated the object as significant) is not liable unless there is a full *revi'it*.

This restates the debate from the *Mishnah* itself. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that the smaller *shiurim* (for honey, milk, blood, and so forth) are the standard measures for ordinary carrying; if the person additionally designated the item as significant, liability descends further to *kol shehu*, any amount. Rabbi Shimon takes the opposite position: the smaller *shiurim* reflect the significance conferred by setting aside; absent such designation, all liquids — even honey, milk, and blood — revert to the default *revi'it*.

### Master-Apprentice Exception

The *Gemara* raises a difficulty with the *Tanna Kamma*'s formulation. The *baraita* reads *be-matznia kol shehu chayav* — "in a case where it was set aside." The *Gemara* notes that Tosafot and Rashi actually read this phrase differently from each other, but following Tosafot's reading: had the *baraita* said *ha-matznia*, it would mean "the one who set it aside" — that is, only the person who personally designated the item would be liable for carrying any amount. That would accord with the *Tanna Kamma*'s baseline view. But the *baraita* says *be-matznia*, meaning "in a case where it was set aside" — implying that anyone who carries it, even someone other than the person who designated it, would be liable for *kol shehu*.

The *Gemara* objects: is that not the rejected view of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who held that once one person confers significance upon an object, it becomes significant for everyone? The *Gemara* cites the example: I set aside my chewing gum because I intend to chew it later. According to the *Tanna Kamma*, that act of setting aside makes me liable for *kol shehu* when I subsequently carry it — but it does not affect someone else who carries it; for them, the standard *shiur* still applies. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar held the more expansive view: once I confer significance, it becomes universally significant.

*Amar Abaye*: the *baraita* is not speaking about the general case at all. It addresses a specific scenario — *betalmid she'amar lo rabbo*, a master who instructed his apprentice. When a master tells his apprentice to clear out a room, and the apprentice does so by carrying items into the public domain, the apprentice is liable for items that carry ordinary significance. For items that lack standard significance but which the master had specifically designated, the apprentice is likewise liable — because his role is entirely subordinate to the master's directives, and the master's designation of significance functionally constitutes significance for the apprentice as well. In all other cases, only the person who actually set aside the object is liable for *kol shehu* when carrying it; no one else's liability is affected by that designation.

The overall framework, then, is as follows. For foods, the standard is *kegrogeret*, the size of a pressed fig. For liquids, the default is a *revi'it*, with specific exceptions for items like honey, milk, and arguably blood, which have recognized uses in smaller quantities. If a person sets something aside as personally significant, the *shiur* drops to *kol shehu* — for that person. And in the unique relationship of master and apprentice, the master's act of setting aside extends that lowered *shiur* to the apprentice as well.

### Dirty Water & Plaster

The *baraita* includes one more point of agreement: *u-modim chachamim le-Rabbi Shimon bemotzi shofchin she-shi'uran bi-revi'it* — the Sages concede to Rabbi Shimon that dirty water (*shfochin*) has a *shiur* of a *revi'it* even in ordinary carrying. This invites a natural question: *shpochin le-mai chazu*? What does one do with dirty water? *Amar Rabbi Yirmiyah*: you use it to mix plaster — *le-gabel bahen et ha-tit*.

But this raises a further difficulty. A separate *baraita* establishes that the *shiur* for plaster (*tit*) is the amount needed to form the opening of a bellows (*pi kur*), the small channel used in smelting. That amount of finished plaster is far smaller than the quantity of plaster one could produce from a *revi'it* of water. If a small quantity of finished plaster has recognized significance, should not the proportionally smaller amount of water sufficient to produce that plaster also be significant — and therefore carry a *shiur* well below a *revi'it*?

The *Gemara* resolves this with *lo kashya*: there is no contradiction, because the two cases involve different states of material. *Ha d'migbal, ha d'lo migbal* — one speaks of plaster already mixed, the other of water not yet combined into plaster. *She'ein adam tore'ach le-gabel tit la'asot pi kur*: a person does not go to the trouble of mixing plaster merely to produce the tiny amount needed for a bellows opening. One would only undertake the labor of mixing if one had a meaningful quantity of plaster to work with. Therefore, the water that has not yet been made into plaster requires a full *revi'it* to be considered significant; it is only the already-prepared plaster, in its finished state, that carries significance in a smaller amount.

## Non-Food Shiurim

### Rope vs. Strap

The *Mishnah* now leaves the domain of liquids and foodstuffs and turns to various other items, each assigned its own *shiur* for carrying liability. *Hamotzi chevel* — if one carries out a rope — the measure is *kedei la'asot ozen le-kupa*, enough to fashion a handle for a box. *Gami* — a strap or elastic material — goes by the smaller measure of *kedei la'asot tlai le-nafa u-le-chvarah*, enough to make a hanging loop for a sieve, from which one would hang the sieve in the kitchen.

One might ask why rope is not also measured by the smaller sieve-loop standard, since a rope can obviously serve as a loop as well. The *Gemara* explains: *keivan de-charik be-mana, lo avdi inshi* — because rope chafes against the vessel. People do not use rope to hang a sieve precisely because of its abrasive quality. A box handle, which is used differently and does not require gentle material, is made from rope — and so that larger size is the operative *shiur* for rope, while the softer strap material continues to be measured by the sieve-loop.

A *baraita* adds several related materials. *Hutzin* — wicker — is measured by enough to make a handle (*ozen*) for a wicker basket (*sal ve-khfifa*). *Siv* — a fibrous material that grows between the bark and trunk of a palm tree — according to *acherim*, is measured by enough to place at the mouth of a small pitcher to filter wine. One gets a vivid sense of everyday material life from these *shiurim*: wicker baskets with wicker handles, palm fiber used as a natural wine strainer.

### Parchment Measures

*Klaf* — parchment — carries a *shiur* of enough to write the smallest *parasha* found in *tefillin*, which is the *parasha* of *Shema Yisrael*. This refers to the entire paragraph, not merely those opening words, since each of the four *parshiyot* of *tefillin* occupies its own compartment.

Parchment is considerably more expensive than paper, and one might intuitively assume that one becomes liable for a smaller amount of parchment, given its greater value. Rashi makes the important counterintuitive observation: because parchment is so valuable, people reserve it for significant uses — specifically, for writing sacred texts like *tefillin*. Trivial applications, such as writing a tax collector's receipt, are not done on parchment. Consequently, the recognized use of parchment is in a larger size, and that larger size becomes the *shiur*, even though the material itself is more expensive per unit.

### Various Materials

The *Mishnah* continues with a catalog of further items. *Dyo* — ink — requires enough to write *shtei otiyot*, two letters. As Rashi notes, two letters can already constitute an act of writing on *Shabbat* — one writes one letter on each of two separate items and combines them, or simply writes two letters that together form a meaningful mark.

*Kachol* — the blue cosmetic or medicinal powder applied to the eye — is measured by enough to apply to one eye. The *Gemara* will address why one eye and not two.

*Devek* — glue — is measured by enough to place on top of a board as a trap for birds: a glue trap laid out for pigeons.

*Zefet ve-gofrit* — tar and pitch — go by enough to form the seal over a certain type of vessel that contained quicksilver or mercury. A hole would then be made in that pitch seal through which the mercury could be controlled. Rashi's understanding is that the operative measure is the amount of pitch needed to cover the vessel's opening, with the hole being formed within that sealing layer. The language of the *Mishnah* — *kedei la'asot nekev*, "to make a hole" — requires some explanation, and Rashi's reading is that the pitch covers the vessel and the hole is then configured within it, rather than the pitch itself filling a pre-existing hole.

*Sha'avah* — wax — is measured by enough to place over the opening of a small hole to stop it up. The contrast with pitch is instructive: wax goes by enough to fill a hole, whereas pitch, per Rashi, goes by the amount needed to cover the vessel in which a hole is then made.

*Charasit* — ground pottery — goes by enough to form the opening of a bellows (*pi kur shel tzorfei zahav*), the channel used in gold smelting. Rabbi Yehuda says the *shiur* is enough to make a small tripod leg (*pitput*).

*Subin* — bran — is measured by the amount needed to place at the mouth of this same smelting bellows, though the precise function of bran in that context is not fully transparent.

### Plaster Uses

*Sid* — plaster — carries a *shiur* of enough to smooth (*lasud*) the skin of the smallest girl who uses it. The application in question is hair removal: plaster was applied to the skin and then pulled off, removing hair — a practice that continues in analogous form today with wax treatments.

Rabbi Yehuda says the *shiur* is enough to make *kilkel*, which refers to a preparation used for hair removal near the temples. Rabbi Nechemya says enough to smooth (*lasud*) *undefi*, also understood as relating to hair removal near the temple region. The *Gemara* will explore the precise application each authority has in mind.

### Wicker & Fibers

As noted above, a *baraita* supplements the *Mishnah*'s list with wicker (*hutzin*) and palm fiber (*siv*). The palm fiber entry — filtered wine through a small pitcher's mouth — is attributed to *acherim*, a designation that does not necessarily signal disagreement with any prior authority but simply introduces an additional view.

The *baraita* also addresses *revav* — a type of fat or schmaltz — measured by enough to place underneath a small wafer in the oven, so it does not stick, much as one would grease a pan today. The wafer's size is given as *ke-sela*, a Greek coin, and the *Gemara* confirms that this is equivalent to the size of a *grogeret* (a pressed fig), establishing that those two measures correspond to the same two-dimensional surface area.

Tosafot raises a pointed question: how can one use meat fat (*revav*) beneath bread? There is a prohibition against baking *fleishig* or *milchig* bread (*lechem*) lest one come to eat it together with the opposing category. Tosafot resolves this by observing that the prohibition applies to bread proper and does not extend to items that are distinctly not bread — small wafers or items with an obvious identifying feature (*siman*) that mark them as something other than ordinary bread. The Tosafot discussion also connects naturally to the broader *halachic* question of dairy-baked goods with distinctive features.

### Paper Measures

*Niyar* — paper — is measured by enough to write on it a *kesher mochsin*, a tax collector's receipt. The *Mishnah* adds that if one carries out such a receipt, one is likewise liable. The *Gemara* asks: *ve-kama kesher mochsin?* How large is the tax collector's receipt? *Shtei otiyot* — two letters. But the *Gemara* assumes that the two marks of the tax collector are a particular type of symbol, likely larger than ordinary letter-writing would require, distinct from the standard *shtei otiyot* used to measure ink liability.

A contradiction is then raised from another source: *ha-motzi niyar chalak im yesh bo kedei lichtov shtei otiyot chayav* — one who carries blank paper is liable if it contains enough space for two letters. This sounds like the standard two-letter measure, not the tax-collector-symbol measure. Why should paper go specifically by the larger tax collector standard?

### Two Letters Debate

*Amar Rav Sheshet*: when the *Mishnah* says *shtei otiyot*, it means the two letters of the tax collector's receipt (*shtei otiyot she-kesher mochsin*). This is the *Mishnah*'s only standard for paper liability. The blank-paper *baraita* therefore means the same thing: two tax-collector symbols, not two ordinary letters.

*Rava* disagrees. He holds that *shtei otiyot didan* — our standard two ordinary letters — is the operative measure, but with an important addition: *u-veit achiza*, plus the portion of the paper one must hold while writing. When writing two letters on a piece of paper, one requires not only the space for the letters themselves but also space to grip the paper. That combined area — two letters plus holding space — *dehaynu kesher mochsin*, is equivalent to the size of the tax collector's receipt. Thus for Rava, paper is used for ordinary writing as well; it simply happens that when one factors in the holding space, the resulting *shiur* coincides with the tax-collector measure.

This debate is not merely technical. According to Rav Sheshet, paper's recognized use is exclusively for tax receipts; ordinary writing on paper is not a standard enough use to confer significance. According to Rava, paper is used for ordinary writing too, and the two *shiurim* converge only because holding space must be counted. Rava's position is consistent with an acknowledgment that private writing on paper was a genuine practice of their time.

A further consideration relevant to this discussion: parchment (*klaf*) was typically usable only on one side, owing to the way it was processed. Whether paper was similarly limited is less clear, but the conventional use of paper for receipts may have been a single-sided, relatively standardized format — which would explain why one does not simply use the other side of erased paper for ordinary writing.

### Erased Paper & Docs

The *Gemara* raises a challenge (*meiteivei*): *ha-motzi niyar machuk u-shtar parua* — one who carries out erased paper or a paid-up debt document. Each of these has lost its primary use. What remains? The *Mishnah* teaches: *im yesh be-loveno shelo kedei lichtov shtei otiyot*, if there is enough white space remaining on it to write two letters, one is liable. Alternatively, *o ve-chulo kedei lachroch al pi tzluchit ketana*, if the whole piece is large enough to wrap around the opening of a small flask, one is also liable.

Erased paper retains value as a piece of wrapping material — placed over the mouth of a small perfume bottle (*tzluchit*) the way one might use a small patch of cloth. A paid-up debt document (*shtar parua*), once discharged, can similarly serve as wrapping material, or, if it retains unwritten margins or white space, that space can be used for writing two letters.

According to Rav Sheshet, the two-letter measure here again refers to the tax-collector symbols, and the resolution is straightforward. According to Rava, who says two ordinary letters plus holding space, the *Gemara* raises a subtle question: when one writes in the white margin of an already-existing *shtar*, one does not need extra space for holding — the rest of the *shtar* functions as the holding portion. Therefore *beit achiza lo tzarich*, one does not need to add a holding margin, and the *shiur* reverts to two bare letters. So in this specific context, the measure is simply two ordinary letters, smaller than the standard measure used for a blank piece of paper — a result the *Gemara* accepts as straightforward once the distinction between holding space and available-paper holding is clarified.

## Debt Documents

### Tax Receipt Rules

The *Gemara* now turns to a related set of rules: *ha-motzi kesher mochas ad shelo hera'o le-mochas chayav* — one who carries out a tax receipt before having shown it to the tax collector is liable, because the receipt retains full value as a functional document. *Mishe-hera'o le-mochas, patur* — once it has been shown to the collector and used for its purpose, it has no further use, and one is exempt. The analogy to a used ticket stub is apt: once shown at the gate, its function is spent.

The question of why one cannot fall back on the bottle-stopper use at this point is not immediately addressed, but it will become clear as the *Gemara* develops the broader discussion about the prohibition on retaining paid-up documents.

### Receipt Holding Debate

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *af mishe-hera'o le-mochas chayav* — even after having shown the receipt to the tax collector, one remains liable for carrying it. The reason given is *mitnei she-tzorech lo* — because one still has a use for it.

*Mai bainaihu?* — what is the practical difference between the Sages and Rabbi Yehuda? Three suggestions are offered. *Amar Abaye*: the difference concerns the *rahiti mochsa*, the runners or inspectors who circulate to verify payment after the initial checkpoint. Just as a transit passenger might need to retain a ticket even after boarding in case an inspector checks later, a person who has shown his receipt to the primary tax collector may still need it for a secondary inspector. The Sages hold that in such a case one can simply return to the original collector who will vouch for the payment; Rabbi Yehuda holds one must retain the receipt.

*Rava* says: the difference concerns a senior and a junior tax collector (*mochsa gadol u-mochsa katon*). One may pay the senior collector but then encounter the junior substitute who has no knowledge of the transaction. For that contingency, retaining the receipt has value.

*Rav Ashi* says: even with only a single tax collector who has no deputies and no runners, one still has a use for the receipt — to show at a different checkpoint on a different occasion, demonstrating to another tax collector that one is a reliably tax-paying citizen. Rashi notes that the underlying *mochsa* here refers not to general income taxation but to a toll or crossing fee — for a bridge or river crossing — which adds texture to why these scenarios of multiple collectors, inspectors, or future encounters are practically relevant.

### Debt Writ Verification

The *Gemara* now examines a parallel case — a *shtar chov*, a debt document — and the analysis runs considerably deeper. *Ha-motzi shtar chov ad shelo pera'o chayav* — carrying an unpaid debt document renders one liable, since it has clear monetary value. *Mishe-pera'o, patur* — once the debt has been paid and the document discharged, one is exempt.

Rabbi Yehuda again disagrees: *af mishe-pera'o chayav*, one remains liable even after payment, because one still has a use for the document.

### Paid-Up Doc Prohibition

*Amar Rav Yosef*: the crux of the debate is *issur li-shehot shtar parua* — whether there is a prohibition to retain a paid-up debt document at all. The concern is serious: a creditor who keeps a discharged *shtar* might attempt to collect again on the same debt. The debtor, having paid, may no longer have a receipt or may have forgotten the transaction, creating the danger of double collection. Even returning the *shtar* to the debtor carries risk — the debtor might lose it, it could be returned to the creditor, and the same danger arises. For this reason, the Sages hold that it is *assur li-shehot shtar parua*, prohibited to retain a paid-up document. Because it may not be held by anyone, it has no legitimate use and one is exempt from carrying it. Rashi adds that this also explains why the bottle-stopper use was never invoked in this context: precisely because the document may not be retained for any purpose, even wrapping a flask is unavailable as a justifying use.

Rabbi Yehuda holds *mutar le-shehot shtar parua* — it is permitted to retain a paid-up document. Perhaps the debtor holds it as evidence of payment, or it is returned to the debtor who takes responsibility for it. Since it may lawfully be retained, it has a use, and one remains liable for carrying it.

*Abaye* offers a different framing entirely: *de-chulei alma assur le-shehot shtar parua* — everyone agrees that one may not retain a paid-up document. The dispute between the Sages and Rabbi Yehuda concerns an entirely different question.

### Admission vs. Notarization

*Abaye* identifies the true axis of the debate as *modeh be-shtar she-ketavo tzarich le-kayemo* — whether one who admits that a debt document is genuine still requires that document to be verified (*kiyum*). This refers to the process of notarization: to collect on a debt document, the creditor must typically produce witnesses who recognize the signatures of the signing witnesses, establishing that the document is authentic and not forged. Without *kiyum*, the document cannot be used to collect.

The scenario Abaye has in mind is one where the signatures cannot be verified — perhaps the signing witnesses are unavailable or unrecognizable. In that case, what does it mean for the debtor to say "I paid"? To say "I paid" implies, at minimum, "there was a valid debt, and this document is genuine." That implicit admission (*hoda'ah*) that the *shtar* is authentic raises the question: does such an admission eliminate the need for formal *kiyum*?

The *Tanna Kamma* holds *modeh be-shtar she-ketavo tzarich le-kayemo* — even if the debtor admits the document was written, the creditor still requires formal *kiyum* before collecting. Consequently, if the signatures cannot be verified and the debtor claims payment, the document is effectively unenforceable. It has no monetary value and one is exempt for carrying it.

Rabbi Yehuda holds *modeh be-shtar she-ketavo ein tzarich le-kayemo* — if the debtor admits the document's authenticity, no formal *kiyum* is required. The debtor's own admission that this is a genuine obligation serves in place of notarization. Therefore, when the debtor says "I paid," the creditor in effect now has a verified document — verified by the debtor himself — and may potentially still collect if the claim of payment is rejected. The document therefore retains value, and carrying it renders one liable.

How does this connect to the language of "before and after payment"? Abaye explains that the *Mishnah*'s formulation *ad shelo pera'o* and *mishe-pera'o* does not literally mean before and after the money changed hands, but rather before and after the debtor has stated that he paid — *ad she-yomar lo parati* and *le-achar she-yomar parati*. Before the debtor makes any such statement, the document is straightforwardly valuable: the creditor holds a debt instrument against which there is no pending claim. After the debtor says he paid, the document's value depends entirely on whether the creditor can still enforce it — which turns on whether *kiyum* is still required. According to the *Tanna Kamma*, since *kiyum* is still required and in this scenario cannot be obtained, the document is now worthless. According to Rabbi Yehuda, since the debtor's very statement of payment implicitly notarized the document (*modeh be-shtar*), the document retains enforceable value, and one carrying it remains liable.

The discussion here reaches a point of considerable legal complexity, and the analysis will continue at the top of 79a.
