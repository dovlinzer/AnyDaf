# Kiddushin 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kinyanim Hierarchy

### Jumping Elephants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי: בַּחֲבִילֵי זְמוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the buyer can lift an elephant **by** using **bundles of vines.** He leads the elephant to them, and when the elephant stands on the bundles of vines this is considered lifting the elephant.

Two matters from the previous session warrant brief revisitation before proceeding to the *mishna*. The first concerns *Tosafos*' reading of the *Gemara*'s discussion of bundles of twigs. According to *Rashi*, the case involves walking on top of the twigs, such that one gets the elephant to move off the ground — a form of *hagbaha* that, notably, does not require actually lifting the object clear of the surface. *Tosafos*, by contrast, understands the case differently: one holds a twig with a leaf aloft and the elephant leaps for it. The difficulty, of course, is that elephants do not jump — a point confirmed by those who follow the writings of Natan Slifkin on the natural world. Whether *Tosafos* had ever observed an actual elephant is itself an open question. One proposed defense of *Tosafos* — that the discussion concerns an elephant on a trampoline — may be taken for what it is worth.

### Hierarchy Debate

The second point concerns the hierarchy of *kinyanim*. When the *Gemara* states that a particular *kinyan* works for a given type of object, the implication is that lower-ranked *kinyanim* do not. So when one says *hagbaha* works for *behemah dakah*, the point is that *meshichah* and *mesirah* do not. Understanding which *kinyan* is considered stronger is therefore crucial. When the *Gemara* states that *mesirah* works for *behemah gassah*, is that an exclusive statement — *mesirah* and not *meshichah* — as *Rashi* reads it? Or does it mean that even *mesirah* suffices, and certainly *meshichah*? The direction of the hierarchy matters. Once *halacha* accepts that multiple *kinyanim* work in a given case, one may in principle use whichever is available.

### R. Tam's Objection

A position attributed to the *Rashbam*, however, goes further: in certain cases where both *mesirah* and *meshichah* are available, one is obligated to perform *meshichah* — the stronger *kinyan* — and doing the weaker one would be insufficient. *Tosafos* records *Rabbeinu Tam*'s sharply worded objection to this. The relevant passage in *Tosafos* begins approximately ten lines before the end of that *Tosafos*, at the word *l'miknayei*. The *Tosafos* reads: since *meshichah*, which is the more distinguished *kinyan*, is available in a place such as a *simta*, it would be inappropriate — *lav orach d'ara* — to effect the acquisition through *mesirah*, which is the inferior *kinyan*. Then *Rabbeinu Tam* poses his famous challenge: *v'chi mitzvos hein* — are *meshichah* and *mesirah* commandments, such that performing one is *mevatlo* the other, and one is required to perform the *mitzvah min hamuvchar*, the most distinguished option? The point is incisive: *kinyanim* are not ritual acts whose relative merit carries normative weight. If both are ruled valid in a given case, why should one be compelled to choose the stronger one? The act either transfers ownership or it does not.

### Ownership Demonstration

The conceptual logic underlying the *Rashbam*'s position, however, is not without merit. The underlying purpose of a *kinyan* is to demonstrate *ba'alus* — ownership. If a stronger demonstration of ownership is available, then performing the weaker one in its presence may not constitute a meaningful act of ownership at all. *Mesirah*, according to *Tosafos*, is indeed a relatively weak form of demonstration: merely holding the reins is not the same as drawing an object toward oneself or lifting it. When the stronger forms of demonstrating ownership are available, *mesirah* in that context may be insufficiently meaningful — not because of any ritual hierarchy, but because relative to what could have been done, it does not signify genuine control. In isolation, *mesirah* can work; when better options are present, the act may not rise to the level of a meaningful expression of ownership.

This gives rise to a broader conceptual point. One might think of ownership as binary — either the transfer occurred or it did not — like a switch being flipped. But the *Rashbam*'s view suggests a more graduated picture, where the meaningfulness of the act of acquisition is itself context-dependent. Whether the *halacha* follows this graduated view or the binary one is part of what the debate between *Rabbeinu Tam* and the *Rashbam* is really about.

## Land with Money

### Achrayut Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נְכָסִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת – נִקְנִין בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה. שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת – אֵין נִקְנִין אֶלָּא בִּמְשִׁיכָה. נְכָסִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת נִקְנִין עִם נְכָסִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה,
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Property that serves as a guarantee,** i.e., land or other items that are fixed in the earth, **can be acquired by** means of giving **money, by** means of giving **a document, or by** means of taking **possession** of it. Property **that does not serve as a guarantee,** i.e., movable property, **can be acquired only by pulling. Property that does not serve as a guarantee can be acquired** along **with property that serves as a guarantee by** means of giving **money, by** means of giving **a document, or by** means of taking **possession** of them. The movable property is transferred to the buyer's possession when it is purchased together with the land, by means of an act of acquisition performed on the land.

The *Gemara* now moves from *metaltelin* — from animals in particular — to land. The *mishna* at the top of 26b reads: *nechasim sheyesh lahem achrayut niknim b'kesef b'shtar uv'chazakah* — property that bears liens is acquired through money, through a document, and through *chazakah*. The phrase *nechasim sheyesh lahem achrayut* is the older, more antiquated formulation for real estate. In later usage this becomes simply *karka*, but this *mishna* employs the earlier idiom, a detail that reflects the age and tightness of this collection of *mishnayot*. As noted when beginning *Masechet Kiddushin*, this series of *mishnayot* — organized around a single theme of degrees of *kinyanim*, moving through women, slaves, Hebrew slaves, *eved kena'ani*, and various categories of property — represents a very early and tightly organized collection.

*Nechasim sheyesh lahem achrayut* means land and real estate because real property serves as collateral: when a loan is made, a lien attaches to the lender's land. The *mishna* states that such property is acquired by *kesef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah*.

### Three Methods

There is, incidentally, a significant debate regarding what *kesef* means in this context. Normally one would assume it refers to payment of the full purchase price. But the parallel to *kiddushei isha* — where *kesef* is fulfilled symbolically by a single *perutah* — raises the question of whether paying the first *perutah* toward the purchase of land itself constitutes *kinyan kesef*. Two conceptual frameworks present themselves. One could say the full payment is technically required, but as soon as a single dollar is paid, we treat it as though the remainder has been paid and the seller has lent the buyer the balance — so the outstanding amount is a loan, not an unpaid purchase price. Alternatively, one could say that any partial payment effects the *kinyan* immediately. The analogous discussion in *Bava Kamma* regarding a merchant who has not yet received full payment — whether such an outstanding debt is a *halva'ah* subject to *shemitah* annulment or an unpaid commercial obligation — is equally relevant. The fundamental question here is whether the model of *kiddushei isha*, where the first *perutah* is sufficient, teaches that the same symbolic sufficiency applies to land, or whether land is fundamentally different and requires the full purchase price.

The *mishna* continues: *u'v'shtar* — with a document, which as the *Gemara* will explain means the seller writes *sadi mechurah lach* and hands over the document. And *uv'chazakah* — through taking possession, which will be discussed in its own right. The reason *karka* requires these distinct forms of *kinyan* rather than the intuitive forms used for *metaltelin* is conceptually clear. *Metaltelin* is movable: one can draw it toward oneself, lift it, carry it into one's home. Ownership of chattels is demonstrated by physical control — the object is on one's person, in one's house, in one's domain. The principle *kol mah shetachat yad adam harei hu shelo* — whatever is under a person's control is presumed to be his — explains why *meshichah*, *hagbaha*, *yad*, and *chatzer* all work for movables: they all express meaningful physical control.

Land, by contrast, cannot be lifted or drawn toward oneself. Simply standing on someone else's land does not demonstrate ownership. The *kinyanim* for land therefore focus on the act of exchange itself — *kesef* is transactional, *shtar* is transactional, and *chazakah*, while touching on something more like control, is still a momentary act rather than an ongoing state of possession. This is why the law of land requires these particular modes.

The *mishna* goes on: *nechasim she'ein lahem achrayut ein niknin ela b'meshichah* — chattel that bears no liens is acquired only through *meshichah*. Though *hagbaha* would obviously also work, and *mesirah* was applicable in certain cases involving animals, the general rule for inanimate movables is *meshichah*. Then: *nechasim she'ein lahem achrayut niknin im nechasim sheyesh lahem achrayut b'kesef b'shtar uv'chazakah* — chattel can be acquired together with land through the same modes that acquire land. The *Gemara* will address whether the chattel must be physically present on the land for this to work.

The *mishna* then adds a further dimension: *v'zokakin et hanechasim sheyesh lahem achrayut l'hishava aleihem* — the chattel can drag the land into the orbit of an oath. Normally, no oath is taken regarding land; oaths are taken only regarding chattel — as in *modeh b'miktzat* or disputes about objects entrusted for safekeeping. But through the mechanism of *gilgul shvuah*, once someone is obligated to take an oath regarding chattel, that oath can roll forward and encompass land as well. So the relationship between land and chattel is bidirectional: land can pull chattel along in the act of acquisition, and chattel can pull land along into the domain of oaths.

### Money Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּכֶסֶף מְנָלַן? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: אָמַר קְרָא: ״שָׂדוֹת בַּכֶּסֶף יִקְנוּ״. וְאֵימָא עַד דְּאִיכָּא שְׁטָר, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכָתוֹב בַּסֵּפֶר וְחָתוֹם״! אִי כְּתִיב ״יִקְנוּ״ לְבַסּוֹף – כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, הַשְׁתָּה דִּכְתִיב ״יִקְנוּ״ מֵעִיקָּרָא, כֶּסֶף – קָנֵי, שְׁטָר – רְאָיָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara inquires: **From where do we** derive that land can be acquired **by** means of **money? Ḥizkiyya said** that **the verse states: "They shall acquire fields with money"** (Jeremiah 32:44). The Gemara asks: **But** if the proof is from that verse, one can **say** that the acquisition is not valid **unless there is a document** as well, **as it is written** in the same verse: **"And write a document and sign"** (Jeremiah 32:44). The Gemara answers: **If it were written: They shall acquire** fields with money, **at the end** of the verse, it would be **as you said,** that one must also write a document so that he can acquire the land with money. **Now that it is written "they shall acquire" at the beginning** of the verse, this teaches that the **money** itself **effects acquisition** of the land, and the **document is merely a proof.**

The *Gemara* asks: *b'kesef m'nalan* — what is the source that land is acquired with money? *Chizkiyah* answers: *amar kra sadot b'kesef yiknu* — the verse says fields shall be purchased with money, a passage in *Yirmiyah*.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges: perhaps the verse requires both money *and* a document, since the verse continues with instructions to write a document, seal it, and have witnesses sign it. The *Gemara* responds with a grammatical argument: if the verb *yiknu* appeared at the end of the verse, one might read money and document together as a joint condition. But since *yiknu* appears immediately after *b'kesef* — *sadot b'kesef yiknu* — the acquisition is effected by the money alone. The subsequent instructions to write a document are, in the context of that verse, purely evidentiary: *shtar re'ayah b'alma hu* — the document is merely for proof. The document can, of course, also serve as the operative mode of acquisition in other circumstances, but in this verse it is not what effects the transaction.

A natural question arises: why not derive the rule from the *pesukim* regarding *Avraham*'s purchase of the cave of *Machpeilah*, where money is also paid for land? *Tosafos* explains that the *Gemara* prefers not to draw from that episode because it involves a transaction with a non-Jew, and the rules of *kinyan* for non-Jews may differ.

### Rav's Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין כּוֹתְבִין אֶת הַשְּׁטָר, אֲבָל בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁכּוֹתְבִין אֶת הַשְּׁטָר – לֹא קָנָה. וְאִי פָּרֵישׁ – פָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: They taught** that land can be acquired by means of money alone, i.e., without a document, **only in a place where** the custom is that **they do not write documents; but in a place where** the custom is that **they write documents one does not acquire** land until a document is given to him. **And if he specified** that he wishes to acquire the land from the time of the money transfer, then **he has specified** his wishes, and the land is acquired once the money is given.

*Rav* now introduces an important qualification: *lo shanu ela b'makom she'ein kotvin shtar* — the rule that money alone suffices applies only where it is not the local practice to write a document. In a place where writing a document invariably accompanies every land transaction — *makom shekotvin shtar* — money alone does not effect the *kinyan*.

*Rashi* explains this as a *gemar da'at* issue. If the universal practice in a given locale is that a document of sale always accompanies the transaction, then even if technically money could suffice, the parties' own expectations define when ownership is finalized. Since the seller does not consider the transaction complete until the document is transferred, there is no *gemar da'at* on the seller's part until that occurs — *lo samcha da'atei d'lokach ad d'nakat shtra*. One interpretation of this principle focuses on cases where the practice recognizes that the transaction is simply not concluded without a document, making it a formal condition. Another reading, suggested here, goes further: even if the document functions technically as *re'ayah*, if registering the transfer is so universally expected that no one considers ownership to have moved until that step is completed, then the *gemar da'at* is correspondingly delayed.

The *Gemara* adds: *v'ei parish parish* — but if the parties explicitly state that the money alone will effect the *kinyan*, it works. Money is fundamentally valid; the default expectation in a document-writing locale merely shifts the assumption, and that assumption can be overridden by explicit agreement.

### R. Idi Bar Avin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין כִּי זָבֵין אַרְעָא, אָמַר: אִי בָּעֵינָא בְּכַסְפָּא – אִיקְנֵי, אִי בָּעֵינָא בִּשְׁטָרָא – אִיקְנֵי. אִי בָּעֵינָא בְּכַסְפָּא – אִיקְנֵי, דְּאִי בָּעֵיתוּ לְמִיהְדַּר, לָא מָצִיתוּ הָדְרִיתוּ. וְאִי בָּעֵינָא בִּשְׁטָרָא – אִיקְנֵי, דְּאִי בָּעֵינָא לְמִיהְדַּר, הָדַרְנָא בִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This is **like that which Rav Idi bar Avin** would do. **When purchasing land,** Rav Idi bar Avin **would say: If I wish** to acquire it **by** means of **money, I will acquire** it in that manner, and **if I wish** to acquire it **by** means of **a document, I will acquire** it by that method. He would stipulate at the outset that he reserves the right to choose how the transaction will be finalized. The Gemara elaborates: **If I wish** to acquire it **by** means of **money, I will acquire** it in that way, **as, if you wish to retract** your participation in the sale **you cannot retract** it, because the money has already changed hands. **And if I wish** to acquire the land **by** means of **a document, I will acquire** it in that way, **as, if I wish to retract** my participation in the sale **I can retract** it provided that I have not received a document of purchase.

This practice reflects a broader principle about *kinyanim*: they are not rigid ritual acts but instruments of *gemar da'at*, and the parties' articulated intentions can shape which act carries operative weight. This is consistent with the concept of *kinyan situmta* — where the accepted commercial practice of a given community can itself constitute a valid *kinyan*, provided genuine *gemirus da'at* is present. In the diamond trade, for instance, the declaration *mazel u'bracha* is understood as the operative signal that the deal is concluded, enforceable before a *beit din*.

What is perhaps the larger *chidush* of *Rav Idi bar Avin*'s practice is the idea that one party can reserve the right to determine retroactively which act constituted the *maaseh kinyan*. This is a more unusual claim than simply preserving a right of retraction, since it concerns the characterization of which act, among acts that have already occurred, carries the legal weight of the transfer.

## Land with Document

### Document Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁטָר. מְנָלַן? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכָתוֹב בַּסֵּפֶר וְחָתוֹם וְהָעֵד עֵדִים״, וְהָאָמְרַתְּ שְׁטָר רְאָיָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא! אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא, ״וָאֶקַּח אֶת סֵפֶר הַמִּקְנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that land can be purchased **by** means of **a document.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **If we say** that it is **because it is written: "And write in a document and sign, and witnesses shall testify"** (Jeremiah 32:44), **but didn't you say** that the document mentioned in the verse **is merely a document of proof? Rather,** it is derived **from here: "And I took the deed of purchase"** (Jeremiah 32:11), an expression that indicates that the document itself effects the acquisition.

The *Gemara* turns to the second mode: *u'v'shtar m'nalan* — what is the source for acquisition of land by document? One might initially suggest deriving it from the instruction in the same verse of *Yirmiyah* to write a document and seal it — but the *Gemara* already established that in that context the document is merely evidentiary. The operative source is instead a different verse in the same passage: *va'ekach et sefer ha'miknah* — "and I took the book of purchase." The document is called the *sefer ha'miknah* — the instrument of acquisition — indicating that the document itself effects the transaction.

### Shmuel's Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטַר מַתָּנָה, אֲבָל בְּמֶכֶר לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן לוֹ דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said:** The Sages **taught** that the document itself effects acquisition **only** in the case **of a deed of a gift. But with regard to a sale, it does not effect acquisition until** the buyer **gives** the seller **money.** The document itself does not effect the acquisition.

*Shmuel* now introduces a parallel limitation to *Rav*'s ruling about money: *lo shanu ela b'shtar matanah* — the rule that a document alone suffices applies only to a gift. *Aval b'mecher lo kanah ad sheyiten damim* — but in a sale, one does not acquire until money actually changes hands.

The reasoning again tracks *gemar da'at*. A gift, by definition, involves no expectation of payment; the document alone fully expresses the donor's finalized intention. In a sale, however, the seller expects payment, and without receiving it, has not achieved the settled state of mind — the *gemar da'at* — that the transaction is concluded. The document alone, in the context of a sale, does not suffice.

### Hamnuna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הַמְנוּנָא: בִּשְׁטָר כֵּיצַד? כָּתַב לוֹ עַל הַנְּיָיר אוֹ עַל הַחֶרֶס, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶם שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, ״שָׂדִי מְכוּרָה לָךְ״, ״שָׂדִי נְתוּנָה לְךָ״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְכוּרָה וּנְתוּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna raises an objection** to this from a *baraita*: **How** is acquisition performed **by** means of **a document?** If he **wrote for him on paper or earthenware, even though** the paper or the earthenware **is not worth one *peruta*: My field is sold to you,** or: **My field is given to you** as a gift, **it is thereby sold or given.** This indicates that a document is sufficient to effect acquisition both in the case of a sale and in the case of a gift.

*Rav Hamnuna* challenges *Shmuel* from a *braita*: *b'shtar keitzad* — how is the acquisition by document accomplished? One writes on paper or even on a shard of pottery — *al haniyar o al hacheres* — the phrase *sadi mechurah lach* or *sadi netunah lach*, "my field is sold to you" or "my field is given to you," and *harei zu mechurah u'netunah* — it is thereby sold or given. Crucially, the *braita* treats *mechira* and *matanah* together, implying that a document alone works even in a sale — directly contradicting *Shmuel*.

### Desperate Seller

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְפָרֵק לַהּ: בְּמוֹכֵר שָׂדֵהוּ מִפְּנֵי רָעָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Hamnuna **raised the objection and he resolved it:** The *baraita* is referring to one **who sells his field due to its poor** quality. The seller wants to be rid of his field due to its decreasing value and would like to transfer ownership of it as quickly as possible. In this case writing a document is enough to complete the acquisition.

*Rav Hamnuna* himself resolves the challenge he raised: *b'mocher sadehu mipnei ra'atah* — the *braita* addresses the special case of someone who is desperate to sell his field because it is worthless or has been on the market for a very long time. In such a case, the seller has *gemar da'at* even without receiving payment — he is sufficiently eager to conclude the transaction that the document alone suffices. In the standard case, where the seller's field is desirable and commands a fair price, the seller is not reconciled to the transaction until he actually receives the money, and so the document alone is insufficient.

### R. Ashi Reinterp.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בְּמַתָּנָה בִּיקֵּשׁ לִיתְּנָהּ לוֹ, וְלָמָּה כָּתַב לוֹ לְשׁוֹן מֶכֶר – כְּדֵי לְיַפּוֹת אֶת כּוֹחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says:** It can be claimed that the entire *baraita* is referring to one case, that **of a gift** one **wished to give** another. The *baraita* does not deal with a sale at all. **And why does he write for him** a deed for a gift containing the **language of a sale? He does it in order to enhance his power.** If it turns out that there was a lien on this land, the beneficiary can collect the value of the field from the giver's other property, as though this land had been sold to him. In other words, by writing that it is a sale, the giver grants the beneficiary the acquisition power of a buyer, but since the transaction is actually a gift, the document itself completes the acquisition.

*Rav Ashi* offers a different reading of the *braita* entirely. He reinterprets the two phrases — *sadi netunah lach* and *sadi mechurah lach* — not as two alternative scenarios but as a single transaction. The seller is giving a gift, but writes the language of a sale in the document. Why would a donor describe his gift as a sale? Because if a creditor later seizes the field, and it was documented as a gift, the recipient has no claim against the donor — easy come, easy go. But if the document describes it as a sale, then the recipient acquires the rights of a purchaser: should a creditor collect the field, the recipient can demand compensation from the donor. So the donor writes *sadi netunah lach sadi mechurah lach* — this is a gift, but I am granting you the standing of a purchaser so that you may collect from me if the field is seized. Under this reading, the *braita* is discussing a gift throughout, and is consistent with *Shmuel*'s ruling that a document alone works for gifts but not for ordinary sales.

The upshot of this extended discussion is that in most practical cases, both *kesef* and *shtar* are required: *kesef* alone is insufficient where the practice is to write a document, and a *shtar* alone is insufficient in most sales where payment is expected. Each acts as the operative *kinyan*, but *gemar da'at* requires the other to accompany it in the typical case.

### Document Concept

A further conceptual point about the nature of a *shtar* deserves attention. One natural way to understand why a document effects a *kinyan* is that it creates *re'ayah* — written proof of ownership, and the ability to prove ownership effects the transfer. But the *braita* notes that the document can be written on a shard of pottery, which can be scraped and altered, and the *braita* does not require witnesses. A document lacking witnesses and written on an erasable surface is not reliable proof of anything. This points toward an alternative conceptual understanding: the *shtar* is not primarily a *re'ayah* but rather a written expression of *da'as* — a concretization of the seller's intent, the word made real. By writing *sadi mechurah lach* and handing over the written expression, the seller externalizes and crystallizes his *da'at* in a tangible act. It is not that the recipient can now prove ownership; it is that the act of writing and delivery itself constitutes the expression of finalized intent. This distinction — *shtar* as *re'ayah* versus *shtar* as a written expression of *da'as* — has significant implications for how one understands the entire institution of documentary *kinyanim* in *Gittin*, *Kiddushin*, and the transfer of property.

## Possession (Chazaka)

### Dwelling Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַחֲזָקָה. מְנָלַן? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּשְׁבוּ בְּעָרֵיכֶם אֲשֶׁר תְּפַשְׂתֶּם״, בַּמֶּה תְּפַשְׂתֶּם – בִּישִׁיבָה. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״וִירִשְׁתֶּם אֹתָהּ וִישַׁבְתֶּם בָּהּ״, בַּמֶּה יְרַשְׁתֶּם – בִּישִׁיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches that land can be acquired **by** means of taking **possession** of it. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Ḥizkiyya said** that **the verse states: "And dwell in your cities that you have taken"** (Jeremiah 40:10). **In what** manner **have you taken** these cities? They are taken **by dwelling,** which indicates that taking possession of a plot of land and dwelling there is an act demonstrating ownership, and it is itself a valid act of acquisition. A Sage from **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** a different proof: **"And you shall possess it and dwell there"** (Deuteronomy 11:31). **How have you possessed** it? You have done so **by dwelling** there. This teaches that land can be acquired through an act that demonstrates ownership.

The *Gemara* asks: *ub'chazakah m'nalan* — what is the source for acquisition of land through *chazakah*? *Chizkiyah* cites two verses. The first: *ushvu b'areichem asher tafastem bah* — dwell in the cities that you have seized. The question is posed: *bameh tafastem* — in what manner did you seize them? And the answer is *b'yeshivah* — through dwelling in them. The second source, from the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*, reads: *virishtem osah v'yashavtem bah* — you shall inherit it and dwell in it. *Bameh yerishtem* — in what manner do you take possession? *B'yeshivah* — by dwelling.

### Kedusha Rishona

This source carries historical significance beyond its legal function. The *Rambam* distinguishes between the first and second *kedushah* of *Eretz Yisrael*. *Kedushah rishona* was established *al yedei kibbush milchamah* — through military conquest, a forcible seizure in war. *Kedushah shniyah*, by contrast, was established when the returnees from Babylonian exile were granted permission by the Persian king to return; they did not conquer but simply settled in the cities. The *Rambam* characterizes the *kinyan* of *Eretz Yisrael* the second time as a *kinyan chazakah* — effected through the settling and dwelling in the land. This is precisely the mode derived from the verse cited by *Chizkiyah*.

The conceptual relationship between the verse's *yeshivah* — dwelling — and the specific acts of *chazakah* recognized by halacha (such as plowing, planting, or fencing) deserves further exploration, as dwelling over time suggests something different from the momentary act that *chazakah* typically denotes. That question will become clearer as the *Gemara* continues its discussion of what precisely constitutes an act of *chazakah* in the context of land acquisition.

## Chattels Kinyan

### Meshicha Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁאֵין לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת אֵין נִקְנִין אֶלָּא בִּמְשִׁיכָה. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִי תִמְכְּרוּ מִמְכָּר לַעֲמִיתֶךָ אוֹ קָנֹה מִיַּד עֲמִיתֶךָ״ – דָּבָר הַנִּקְנֶה מִיָּד לְיָד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that property **that does not serve as a guarantee can be acquired only by pulling.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **As it is written: "And if you sell any item to your neighbor or buy from your neighbor's hand"** (Leviticus 25:14). This verse speaks of **an item that is acquired from hand to hand,** i.e., by pulling.

The *mishna* states: *she'nechasim she'ein lahem achrayut ein niknin ela b'meshichah* — chattels without liens are acquired only through *meshichah*. As noted, *hagbaha* always works as well; the statement of *meshichah* does not exclude *hagbaha*, which is universally valid. The focus here is on inanimate movables generally, as distinct from the earlier discussion of animals where *mesirah* was potentially relevant.

### Hand-to-Hand

The *Gemara* asks: *m'nalan* — what is the source? The verse cited is: *v'chi timkeru mimkar l'amitecha o kano miyad amitecha* — when you sell a sale to your friend or purchase from the hand of your friend. Although the larger context of this verse in *Parashat Behar* concerns land — it is the *issur* of *ona'ah*, overcharging — *Chazal* understand it to refer to chattel, since the *issur* of *ona'ah* does not apply to land, only to movables. The verse describes the transfer as *miyad amitecha* — from the hand of your friend: *davar hanikna miyad l'yad* — something that transfers from hand to hand. This is the *meshichah*, the drawing of the object into one's possession, which constitutes the *kinyan* for chattels.

### R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה מָעוֹת קוֹנוֹת, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? תַּנָּא תַּקַּנְתָּא דְרַבָּנַן קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says** that **by Torah law** giving **money effects acquisition** but pulling does not, **what can be said?** Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that acquisition through pulling is a rabbinic decree, and by Torah law movable property can be acquired only by means of giving money. Why does the mishna not mention this mode of acquisition? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yoḥanan could answer that the ***tanna* teaches a rabbinic ordinance,** which reflects the accepted practice, but he does not find it necessary to mention a mode of acquisition that applies by Torah law.

The *Gemara* then notes the position of *Rabbi Yochanan* in *Bava Metzia*: *dvar Torah ma'os konos* — biblically, for chattels, it is money that effects acquisition, not physical taking of possession. On *Rabbi Yochanan*'s view, *meshichah* for chattels is only rabbinic. The verse cited therefore cannot prove a biblical basis for *meshichah* on his account. The *Gemara* resolves this simply: *fein, tana takanta d'Rabbanan katani* — fine, then the *mishna* is teaching the rabbinic rule, and no biblical verse is needed.

### Risk and Control

The conceptual reason underlying *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling — and more broadly, why the law of *meshichah* developed for chattels at all — illuminates the fundamental difference between land and movables. Consider a case where one party pays money for a shipment of wheat, but before taking physical possession, the wheat is destroyed. Even if the seller is not at fault and even if some form of *shemirah* obligation exists, the buyer has no interest in the object remaining under the seller's control. The seller does not want to be holding on to the buyer's property, and the buyer does not want his property exposed to risk under someone else's watch. Therefore, for *Rabbi Yochanan*, the Rabbis instituted that until *meshichah* is performed, the transaction is not complete — precisely to prevent the situation where money has changed hands but the object remains at risk in the seller's domain.

Land is categorically different. It cannot burn down and run away. It is always there to be taken possession of. As soon as money changes hands for land, there is no concern that the seller is holding the buyer's property at risk, because the land is simply there. This is why land does not require *meshichah* and why the *kinyanim* for land focus on acts of exchange — *kesef*, *shtar* — rather than acts of physical control. *Chazakah* is the one mode for land that partakes somewhat of the idea of control, and it will be discussed further. But the central contrast is: for movables, being in physical control is the clearest demonstration of ownership, and being in someone else's physical control is positively problematic; for land, physical control in that sense is simply not available or applicable.

### Partial Meshicha

A practical question follows from this analysis: does one need to perform *meshichah* on every individual item of chattel being acquired, or does partial *meshichah* suffice? If ten bushels of wheat are being purchased, must the buyer physically draw each bushel? If all ten bushels are in a single container and the buyer performs *meshichah* on the container, he may well have acquired the whole. But if they are separate and the buyer performs *meshichah* on half of them, the situation is less clear. If the remaining half is subsequently destroyed before *meshichah* is performed on it, can the buyer claim it is not yet his — that he bears no responsibility for the loss? The answer, it seems, is yes: if genuine *meshichah* was performed on only part of the chattel, and before *meshichah* is performed on the remainder a loss occurs, the buyer may argue that the unpossessed portion remains at the seller's risk. A box or container that is moved as a unit likely transfers everything within it, but distinct items require their own act of *meshichah*.

## Kinyan Agav

### Chattels w/ Land

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְכָסִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת נִקְנִין עִם נְכָסִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** Property **that does not serve as a guarantee can be acquired** along **with property that serves as a guarantee by** means of giving **money, by** means of giving **a document, or by** means of taking **possession** of them. The movable property is transferred to the buyer's possession when it is purchased together with the land, by means of an act of acquisition performed on the land.

### Walled Cities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַנָּלַן? אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתֵּן לָהֶם אֲבִיהֶם מַתָּנוֹת וְגוֹ׳ עִם עָרֵי מְצֻרוֹת בִּיהוּדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Ḥizkiyya said** that **the verse states: "And their father gave them great gifts, of silver, and of gold, and of precious things, with fortified cities in Judah"** (II Chronicles 21:3). This indicates that he gave them movable items together with the cities. He did not need to give the items to them directly, as he was able to transfer these gifts by means of the cities he gave them.

The *mishna* states that chattel can be acquired *agav* — incidentally together with — land, using the same modes of *kesef*, *shtar*, and *chazakah* that acquire the land. The Gemara sources this from the verse: *v'yiten lahem avihem matanos* — and their father gave them gifts — *im arei metzuros b'Yehudah* — together with walled cities in Yehudah. The gifts came along with the cities: by acquiring the walled cities, the recipients acquired the gifts together with them.

### Piled Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בָּעֵינַן צְבוּרִים, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages with regard to this matter of acquisition of movable property by way of land: Do **we require** that this movable property be actually **piled** on the land that is sold **or not?**

A major question is then raised: *be'inan tzuburin o lo* — must the chattel be physically piled on the land for *kinyan agav* to work? The conceptual stakes are clear. If the land and chattel are understood as a single package — like a furnished house — then taking possession of the land naturally extends to whatever is on it, since they form one bundle. If the chattel need not be on the land, then the *kinyan* of land is being treated as so weighty, so powerful an act of *gemar da'at*, that it can extend its reach to objects entirely elsewhere — which is conceptually more difficult to justify.

### Agav vs. Chatzer

Before addressing whether *tzivurin* is required, a prior question must be answered: if the chattel must be on the land anyway, why is *kinyan agav* needed at all? Why does the law not simply apply *kinyan chatzer* — the principle that taking possession of one's property acquires whatever is within it? The answer given by the *rishonim* is that *kinyan chatzer* requires a *chatzer mishtameres* — a protected, enclosed domain. A walled house or courtyard satisfies this; an open field of wheat does not. *Kinyan agav* therefore provides a mode of transferring chattel that is physically on unprotected land, a situation *kinyan chatzer* would not reach.

### R. Yosef Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: קַרְקַע כׇּל שֶׁהוּא חַיֶּיבֶת בַּפֵּאָה, וּבַבִּכּוּרִים, וְלִכְתּוֹב עָלֶיהָ פְּרוֹסְבּוּל, וְלִקְנוֹת עִמָּהּ נְכָסִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם אַחְרָיוּת. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בָּעֵינַן צְבוּרִים, כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַאי חֲזֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** a proof from the following mishna (*Pe'a* 3:6). **Rabbi Akiva says:** The owner of **any amount of land is obligated in *pe'a* and in first fruits, and** if the debtor possesses land of any area the creditor **can write a document that prevents the Sabbatical Year from abrogating an outstanding debt [*prosbol*] for it** so that his loans will not be canceled in the seventh year, **and** he **can acquire property that does not serve as a guarantee** along **with it. And if you say** that **we require** the movable property to be **piled** on the land, **for what is** land **of any size fit?** What can be piled on a tiny spot of land?

*Rav Yosef* introduces a *braita* of *Rabbi Akiva* to resolve whether *tzivurin* is required: *karka kol shehu chayevet b'peah* — even the smallest amount of land is subject to the obligation of *peah*. The *Yerushalmi* notes the practical minimum involves at least two stalks — one harvested and one left as *peah* — though even a single stalk with multiple ears might satisfy this if one ear is harvested and the rest left. The *braita* continues: even the smallest amount of land obligates *bikurim*, since the verse says *ha'adamah asher nasata li*. And then, crucially: *v'lichtov aleha pruzbul* — even the smallest amount of land owned by the borrower allows a *pruzbul* to be written.

The *pruzbul*, *Hillel*'s enactment, allows a creditor to collect a loan after *shemitah* by formally transferring the debt to the court. It is effective only when the borrower owns real property. One reason is that standard loans, which *Hillel* was trying to encourage, were typically secured by liens on land; *Hillel* therefore built his enactment around cases that mirror a standard secured loan. A second conceptual reason is that *shemitah*'s annulment of debts operates on money that is "out there" and uncollected — *asher yihiye lecha es achicha tishmet yadecha*. If the borrower owns land on which the creditor holds a lien, the money is not quite so "out there" — it is, in a sense, already accessible, already to some degree within the creditor's reach. This places a land-secured debt on a conceptual continuum closer to already-collected funds, which *shemitah* cannot annul. It does not fully get there on its own — hence the need for the *pruzbul* — but the presence of land moves it in that direction. An oral, unsecured debt is furthest from this; a debt secured by a lien on land is closer. The *pruzbul* bridges the remaining gap.

The *braita* then states: *v'liknos imah nechasim she'einam achrayus* — and with even the smallest amount of land one can transfer chattel *agav*. If *tzivurin* is required, this raises an immediate difficulty.

### Needle Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בָּעֵינַן צְבוּרִים בָּהּ, כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַאי חֲזֵי? תַּרְגְּומַאּ רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר בִּיסְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּעַץ בָּהּ מַחַט. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: קְבַסְתַּן! אִיכְּפַל תַּנָּא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן מַחַט? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאן לֵימָא לַן דְּלָא תְּלָה בָּהּ מַרְגָּנִיתָא דְּשָׁוְויָא אַלְפָּא זוּזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that to acquire movable property by way of land **we require** that the property be actually **piled upon it, for what is** land **of any size fit?** It is impossible to pile one hundred sheep and one hundred barrels on top of such a small plot of land. The Gemara rejects this argument: **Do you maintain** that **a *beit sela*** is referring to a place that is **actually** the size of **a *sela*** coin? No; rather **what** is the meaning of the term ***sela*?** It is referring to a place **that** is **very large** and that could hold the many gifts. If that is true, **why did they call it *sela*?** This name indicates **that** it was **hard as rock [*sela*].**

The difficulty is pointed: *ve'amarta be'inan tzivurin, kolshehu l'mai chazi* — if chattel must be piled on the land, what can possibly be placed on a plot of land so small as to be *kolshehu*, the minimum? *Rav Shmuel bar Bisna* answered before *Rav Yosef*: *kegon she'na'atz bah machat* — for instance, a needle stuck vertically into the ground. Transfer a needle on a tiny plot of land. *Rav Yosef* responded sharply: *kavastan* — you are causing distress. Would the *Tanna* go to the trouble of establishing this rule merely to teach how to transfer needles? If the entire purpose of *kolshehu* land is for a needle, the legal principle being taught seems trivial.

### R. Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּבְרֵי רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר בִּיסְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אָמַר לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּעַץ בָּהּ מַחַט. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קְבַסְתַּן! אִיכְּפַל תַּנָּא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן מַחַט? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאן לֵימָא לַן דְּלָא תְּלָה בָּהּ מַרְגָּנִיתָא דְּשָׁוְויָא אַלְפָּא זוּזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** [This passage is part of the prior section]

*Rav Sheshet* defended the position: *man leima lan d'lo tala bah marganita d'shavya alfa zuzei* — who says one did not hang a pearl worth a thousand *zuz* from that needle? The point is not the needle's intrinsic value but the legal principle: there is no minimum land requirement for *kinyan agav* beyond the minimal existence of *karka*; whatever the transferor wishes to pile on the land — even something dangling from a needle — may be transferred. The legal rule is significant regardless of whether the common case involves inexpensive objects.

## Sick Man Story

### Man's Situation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּמָדוֹנִי אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ מִטַּלְטְלִין הַרְבֵּה, וּבִיקֵּשׁ לִיתְּנָם בְּמַתָּנָה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה עַד שֶׁיַּקְנֵם עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע. מָה עָשָׂה? הָלַךְ וְלָקַח בֵּית סֶלַע סָמוּךְ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְאָמַר: ״צְפוֹנִי זֶה לִפְלוֹנִי, וְעִמּוֹ מֵאָה צֹאן וּמֵאָה חָבִיּוֹת״, וָמֵת, וְקִיְּימוּ אֶת דְּבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear,** as **Rabbi Elazar said:** There was **an incident involving a certain Madonite** [***Madoni*] who was in Jerusalem, as he had a great deal** of **movable property and wished to give it as a gift.** He was ill and did not have time for the recipient to acquire the property by pulling. The Sages **said to him:** One in this situation **has no remedy but to transfer them by means of land. What did he do? He went and acquired a *beit sela*,** apparently meaning land the size of a *sela* coin, **near Jerusalem and said: This northern** portion of the *beit sela* is given **to so-and-so, and with it one hundred sheep and one hundred barrels. And** the Madonite **died, and** the Sages **fulfilled his statement** and gave the gifts.

### Beit Rova

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁחָלָה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם – כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: בָּרִיא הָיָה – כְּרַבָּנַן, שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ מִטַּלְטְלִין הַרְבֵּה וּבִיקֵּשׁ לִיתְּנָם בְּמַתָּנָה, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה עַד שֶׁיַּקְנֵם עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע. מָה עָשָׂה? הָלַךְ וְלָקַח בֵּית רוֹבַע סָמוּךְ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְאָמַר: ״טֶפַח עַל טֶפַח לִפְלוֹנִי וְעִמּוֹ מֵאָה צֹאן וּמֵאָה חָבִיּוֹת״, וָמֵת, וְקִיְּימוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת דְּבָרָיו. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בָּעֵינַן צְבוּרִים, טֶפַח עַל טֶפַח לְמַאי חֲזֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from a different source, **as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** There was **an incident involving a certain person who became sick in Jerusalem,** and the assumption that he became sick is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who says that a person on his deathbed can transfer property only by means of an accepted standard act of acquisition. **And some say he was healthy,** and that assumption is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** that a person on his deathbed can transfer property by means of speech alone, whereas a healthy person requires an accepted act of acquisition. This man **had a great deal** of **movable property and he wished to give it** away **as a gift.** The Sages **said to him:** In this situation one **has no remedy but to transfer** movable property **by means of land. What did he do? He went and acquired** land the size of **a *beit rova* near Jerusalem and said: This square handbreadth** is given **to so-and-so, and with it one hundred sheep and one hundred barrels. And he died, and the Sages fulfilled his statement. And if you say** that **we require** that the property be **piled** on the land, **for what is a square handbreadth fit?** Is it possible to place all of these items in such a limited space?

*Rav Yehudah* in the name of *Rav* relates a similar case. A man who was ill in Yerushalayim — the *Gemara* will note that this may mean he was a *shechiv mera*, in which case one might wonder why a formal *kinyan* was necessary at all, since the words of a *shechiv mera* themselves effect a transfer. The *Gemara* resolves this: either the case follows *Rabbi Eliezer*'s view that even a *shechiv mera* requires a formal *kinyan*, or the man was not actually on his deathbed and was simply ill, such that the *halacha* of *shechiv mera* did not apply at all. Either way, a proper *kinyan* was needed. He too had much chattel and wished to give it as a gift. Told there was no straightforward remedy, he went and acquired a *beit rova* — a quarter-*kav* plot, measuring 104 and 6 square *amot*, perhaps approximately 200 by 200 feet. He then declared that a *tefach* by *tefach* of that land would go to a specific recipient, and with it, a hundred sheep and a hundred barrels of wine. He died, and the *chachamim* affirmed the transfer.

### Piling Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, לִדְמֵי. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מֵאָה צֹאן וּמֵאָה חָבִיּוֹת מַמָּשׁ, נַיקְנִינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲלִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **With what are we dealing here?** It is **with money,** i.e., he sought to give the value of the barrels and sheep, and money of this amount can be placed on a small plot of land. The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** that this incident involved money. **As, if it enters your mind** to say that it involved **an actual** group of **one hundred sheep and one hundred barrels, let him transfer them to** the recipient **through** an act of symbolic **exchange.** If the incident involved money, which cannot be transferred by symbolic exchange, he had no recourse but to acquire the land.

Both stories now create a difficulty for the *tzivurin* position. If chattel must be physically piled on the land, how can one pile a hundred sheep and a hundred barrels of wine on a *tefach* by *tefach*? The *Gemara* presses this: *v'ee amarta be'inan tzivurin, tefach al tefach l'mai chazi* — if piling is required, what is the point of a *tefach* by *tefach*?

The *Gemara* proposes: *hacha b'mai askinan b'damai* — the case must be dealing with money. He did not literally transfer a hundred sheep and a hundred barrels; rather, he piled the monetary equivalent of those assets on the small plot of land, and declared that the sum representing the value of the sheep and the wine would transfer to the recipient. This is admittedly a strained reading of the narrative, which explicitly mentions sheep and barrels. But the *Gemara* accepts it tentatively, noting it makes the *tzivurin* requirement coherent with the small plot described.

## Why Land Necessary

### Alt. Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי, לִדְמֵי? נַיקְנִינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ בִּמְשִׁיכָה! אֶלָּא: דְּלֵיתֵיהּ לִמְקַבֵּל מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against this argument: **Rather, what** will you say, that this is referring **to money,** which cannot be acquired through symbolic exchange? Even so, he still could have acted differently: **Let him transfer it** to the recipient **through pulling. Rather,** you are forced to say **that the recipient of this gift was not** present, and the man wanted to grant him possession of it without the recipient having to perform a physical act of acquisition.

### No Chatlipin

The *Gemara* immediately presses further. If the narrative is really about money being piled on land, this raises the question of why *karka* was necessary at all. Were there not other ways to transfer the assets?

The *Gemara* asks: *nikninayhu lei b'chalipin* — why not use *chalipin*, a *kinyan sudar*? A *kinyan sudar* operates universally and is a quick and flexible mode of acquisition. The answer is that money cannot be acquired through *chalipin*. *Chalipin* involves the exchange of objects that have intrinsic value as objects; money, as a medium of exchange rather than an object of value in itself, is not subject to acquisition by *chalipin*. Women are similarly excluded. So if the underlying assets were money, *chalipin* was not available.

### No Meshicha

The *Gemara* then asks: *nikninayhu lei b'meshichah* — why not have someone take the money through *meshichah* on behalf of the recipient? The answer is: *lo samcha da'atei* — the transferor did not feel confident doing so. Specifically: *savar shamit v'achil lehu* — he feared the third party who would physically take the money would simply keep it. If the recipient is not present to take physical possession himself, entrusting cash to a third party carries the risk that the intermediary will misappropriate it. So *meshichah* via a third party was not a realistic option.

### No Participation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיזְכִּינְהוּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ אַגַּב אַחֵר? לָא סָמְכָה דַּעְתֵּיהּ, סָבַר שָׁמֵיט וְאָכֵיל לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is there no other way to perform this acquisition? **But let him transfer it to him by means of another** person, i.e., another can pull the property on behalf of the recipient. The Gemara answers: The giver **did not rely** on that option, as he **feared** that the third party might **seize it and consume it** or use the property in some other manner. The giver wanted to be sure that the acquisition would be completed in full.

The *Gemara* continues: if the issue is that the recipient is not present, why not have a third party stand in and do *chalipin* on the recipient's behalf — *v'nikninayhu lei agav acher*? The answer is *lo samcha da'atei*: the third parties were not willing to participate. In *chalipin*, the facilitating party must hand over an object of their own to initiate the exchange; no one volunteered to do so. Perhaps they were concerned about this dying man's estate and did not want to be drawn into the transaction; or perhaps they simply had no confidence in the process. Whatever the reason, no one was willing to lend their object to effectuate the *chalipin*.

### Final Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי ״אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה״? הָכִי קָאָמַר: לְמַאי דְּלָא סָמְכָה דַּעְתֵּיהּ – אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה עַד שֶׁיַּקְנֵם עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what** then is the meaning of the statement: **He has no remedy?** Even if he did not want to use the option of a third party, it was certainly available to him. The Gemara explains that **this is what** Rav was **saying** and meant in his description of this incident: In accordance **with** his decision **that he does not rely** on another person and does not want to transfer property by means of anyone else, in this situation one **has no remedy but to transfer** movable property **by means of land.** In summary, no decisive proof has been cited as to whether or not it is possible to acquire movable property by means of land when the items are not piled upon the land.

The *Gemara* thus establishes that the fundamental reason *karka* was necessary is that the recipient was not present — *d'leisa l'mikabel matanah*. When the recipient is absent, *meshichah* cannot be performed directly, *chalipin* through a third party fails because no one will volunteer their object, and handing cash to an intermediary is too risky. *Karka*, however, can be transferred in ways that do not require the recipient's physical presence and do not depend on third-party cooperation in the same way. A *shtar*, for instance, can be written declaring the land given to the absent party and handed to another without the risk of the intermediary pocketing the document.

### Zachin Principle

The final resolution invokes the principle of *zachin l'adam shelo b'fanav* — one may acquire merit on behalf of another person even in their absence. Since receiving a gift is an unambiguous benefit — a *zechut* — for the recipient, a third party may accept it on his behalf without the recipient's explicit authorization. This principle underlies the viability of the *karka* transfer in these stories: even though the recipient was not present, the *chachamim* affirmed the transfer because the acquisition was a *zechut* for him and could therefore be effected without his presence. Whether this principle fully resolves all the questions raised — including the unresolved question of whether *tzivurin* is required — remains to be seen in the continuation of the *sugya*.
