# Zevachim 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Washing Hands & Feet

### Source Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָחֲצוּ אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו מִמֶּנּוּ אֶת יְדֵיהֶם וְאֶת רַגְלֵיהֶם. בְּבוֹאָם אֶל אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד יִרְחֲצוּ מַיִם וְלֹא יָמוּתוּ. אוֹ בְּגִשְׁתָּם אֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ לְשָׁרֵת לְהַקְטִיר אִשֶּׁה לַיהוָה.
>
> **Translation:** (Exodus 30:19–20) Aaron and his sons shall wash their hands and their feet from it. When they go into the Tent of Meeting, they shall wash with water, that they not die. Or when they come near to the altar to minister, to cause an offering by fire to smoke to the Lord.

The discussion opens at the very end of 19b and continues onto daf 20. The source text is Shemot 30:19–20: *verachatzu Aharon u-vanav mimenu et yedeihem ve-et ragleihem* — Aaron and his sons shall wash their hands and their feet from it — *bevo'am el Ohel Moed yirchatzu mayim velo yamutu*, when they come to the Tent of Meeting they shall wash with water and not die, *o begishtam el hamizbe'ach lesharet* — or when they approach the altar to serve — *lehaktir isheh laHashem*, to offer a fire-offering to Hashem. The earlier discussion on 19b turned on the two verbs describing the kohanim's entry: *bevo'am*, when they come, and *begishtam*, when they approach — whether the obligation of hand and foot sanctification is triggered by entering the sanctuary or specifically by drawing near to perform a service.

### Exegetical Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "לְהַקְטִיר אִשֶּׁה״ לְמָה לִּי?
>
> **Translation:** Why do I need the continuation of the verse: "To cause an offering by fire to smoke"?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי – עֲבוֹדָה דִּמְעַכְּבָא כַּפָּרָה, אֲבָל עֲבוֹדָה דְּלָא מְעַכְּבָא כַּפָּרָה – לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Lest you say that this matter, sanctification of the hands and feet, applies only to a rite that is indispensable for effecting atonement, e.g., sprinkling the blood on the altar; but a rite that is not indispensable for effecting atonement, e.g., placing the fats and sacrificial portions of the offering on the altar, does not require prior sanctification of the hands and feet, therefore this verse teaches us that even such rites require sanctification of the hands and feet.

The *gemara* opens daf 20 by asking: *lehaktir isheh lama li*? Why does the *pasuk* need to add the phrase "to offer a fire-offering to Hashem"? The verse would have been perfectly intelligible without it. The answer is that one might have thought the entire requirement of *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim* — hand and foot sanctification — applies only to an *avodah* that is *ma'akvet kaparah*, a service that is indispensable to achieving atonement, such as *zerikah* (blood dashing) or *kabalah* (blood reception). An *avodah* that is not *ma'akvet kaparah* one might have thought exempt. Therefore the *Torah* specifies *lehaktir isheh laHashem* — even the burning of the fire-offering, which is not *ma'akvet kaparah*, requires sanctification. This is the *ka mashma lan*.

### Rabbi vs R. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו בַּיּוֹם – אֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ בַּלַּיְלָה, בַּלַּיְלָה – צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ בַּיּוֹם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי; שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: לִינָה מוֹעֶלֶת בְּקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין לִינָה מוֹעֶלֶת בְּקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: If a priest sanctified his hands and feet during the day, he does not need to sanctify them that night, but if he sanctified them at night, he must sanctify them during the following day. This is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, as Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi would say: The disqualification of being left overnight is determinative with regard to sanctification of the hands and feet, like any sacrificial item that is sanctified. Accordingly, once night has passed, the priest must sanctify them again. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: The disqualification of being left overnight is not determinative with regard to sanctification of the hands and feet. Therefore, as long as the priest continues to serve, he need not sanctify them again.

The *gemara* now recalls a dispute introduced on 19b that will run through the rest of this *daf* and must be kept clearly in view. *Rabbi* — Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — holds that the water with which a kohen sanctifies his hands and feet becomes *nifsal be-linah*, invalidated by overnight passage. If a night passes, the kohen must sanctify again before resuming service. *Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon*, by contrast, holds that once a kohen has sanctified his hands and feet and entered the *mikdash*, he may continue serving for even ten consecutive days without repeat sanctification — the overnight does not disqualify. This dispute will animate everything that follows: the question of the *kiyor* water, the resolution attributed to Rabbi Yochanan, and the debate between Abaye and Rava over that resolution's proper attribution.

## Kiyor Overnight

### Ilfa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בָּעֵי אִילְפָא, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר אֵין לִינָה מוֹעֶלֶת בְּקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם – מֵי כִיּוֹר מַהוּ שֶׁיִּפָּסְלוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: הָנֵי לְמַאי – לְקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם, קִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם גּוּפַיְיהוּ לָא פָּסֵיל בְּהוּ לִינָה; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּקְדוּשׁ לְהוּ בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת – מִיפַּסְלִי?
>
> **Translation:** When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Ilfa raises a dilemma: According to the statement of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, who says that the disqualification of being left overnight is not determinative with regard to sanctification of the hands and feet, what is the halakha with regard to the water in the Basin? Is it disqualified by being left overnight? Do we say: For what purpose is this water? It is for the sanctification of the hands and feet, and since sanctification of the hands and feet itself is not disqualified by being left overnight, the halakha should be the same concerning the water. Or perhaps, since the water is sanctified in a service vessel, i.e., the Basin, it is disqualified by being left overnight, like all other items sanctified in service vessels.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael to Bavel, he taught in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that Ilfa had posed a dilemma: *ledivrei ha'omer ein linah mo'elet be-kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim, mei kiyor mahu she-yifsalu* — according to the one who says that *linah* does not invalidate *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim*, what is the law concerning water left overnight in the *kiyor*? Does that water become *pasul*, disqualified?

### First Explanation

The first line of analysis is as follows: water in the *kiyor* is there *lekiddush yadayim ve-raglayim*. But once that sanctification has been performed, there is no more water on the hands and feet — the hands and feet themselves have no residual water that could become *pasul be-linah*. Since there is nothing left on the kohen's person to be disqualified, perhaps *linah* does not apply. The *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim gufeih lo pasal be-linah* — the sanctification itself, considered in isolation, is not subject to overnight disqualification.

### Alternative View

The alternative position is that the *kiyor* has the status of a *kli sharet* — a service vessel — and any water consecrated within a service vessel that remains overnight is subject to overnight invalidation on those grounds. *Kevan dekadosh kadosh hu be-khli sharet mipsali*: because it has been sanctified within a service vessel, the water is disqualified when morning comes after a night.

### Ravin's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הֲדַר פָּשֵׁיט אִילְפָא: כְּמַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ כָּךְ מַחְלוֹקֶת בָּזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said: Rabbi Yirmeya says that Rabbi Ami says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Ilfa then resolved the dilemma: Just as there is a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, with regard to that issue, i.e., whether sanctification of the hands and feet is disqualified overnight, so too there is a dispute between the two with regard to this issue, i.e., the water in the Basin. Accordingly, Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds that the water is not disqualified by being left overnight.

A separate tradition arrives when Ravin comes from Eretz Yisrael to Bavel. He reports in the name of Rabbi Yirmiyah, in the name of Rav Ami, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, that Ilfa ultimately resolved his own dilemma: *kemachloket be-zo kach machloket be-zo* — just as we have the dispute concerning hand and foot sanctification between Rabbi and Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon, so too the same dispute applies to the question of the *kiyor* water. Rabbi holds that the water left overnight in the *kiyor* is *pasul*, and Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon holds it is not disqualified.

### Objection Raised

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר בִּיסְנָא: רַבִּי, אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן?! אֶלָּא הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן [מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאִילְפָא]: כִּיּוֹר שֶׁלֹּא שִׁקְּעוֹ מִבָּעֶרֶב – מְקַדֵּשׁ מִמֶּנּוּ לַעֲבוֹדַת לַיְלָה, וּלְמָחָר אֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Ravin continued: Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Bisna said before Rabbi Yirmeya: My teacher, do you say so? But this is what Rabbi Asi says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Ilfa: With regard to a Basin that they did not sink into its pit at night, which would have prevented the water from being disqualified by being left overnight, a priest sanctifies his hands and feet from it for the service of that night, and the next day he does not sanctify his hands and feet from it.

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Bisna objects before Rabbi Yochanan: you are answering the first question by introducing a second question that is itself unresolved. *Ata omer ken*? Is this really your intended resolution? The dilemma about the *kiyor* water remains exactly as difficult as the original question about *linah* in hand and foot sanctification.

### Rav Assi Teaching

The *gemara* then presents another teaching. Rav Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan who heard it from Ilfa himself, rules: *kiyor shelo shik'o ba-erev, mekadesh mimenu la-avodat layla, u-lemachar eino mekadesh* — a *kiyor* that was not lowered into the spring overnight may nonetheless be used to sanctify hands and feet for night service, but the following morning the water in it is no longer valid for sanctification. The practice alluded to here is one we will encounter shortly: submerging the *kiyor* into a spring at night so that its water mingles with fresh water, thereby avoiding overnight disqualification.

### Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: לְמָחָר אֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ – דְּלָא צְרִיךְ לְקַדּוֹשֵׁי; אוֹ דִלְמָא, אִיפְּסַלוּ בְּלִינָה? וְלָא פְּשִׁיט לַן, וּמָר קָא פָשֵׁיט לֵיהּ מִיפְשָׁיט.
>
> **Translation:** And we discussed it: When Ilfa said that the next day he does not sanctify his hands and feet from it, did he mean that he does not require sanctification the next day, since sanctification is not disqualified overnight? Or perhaps Ilfa meant that it is prohibited to sanctify one's hands and feet from the Basin since the water was disqualified by being left overnight? And Rabbi Asi did not resolve the issue for us. But you, Master, are resolving the issue by claiming that Ilfa ruled explicitly with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.

The *gemara* rejects this teaching as a sufficient resolution of the underlying question. The ruling that *lemachar eino mekadesh* — the next morning the *kiyor* cannot be used — does not tell us why. Is it because the water remaining overnight in the *kiyor* is *pasul be-linah*? Or is it because the *kiyor* that was not lowered simply no longer needs sanctification the next day? *Ve-la pasht lan* — we have not received an answer — and the *gemara* dismisses this attempted proof: we did not receive resolution, and you think this constitutes one?

## Ben Kattin's Device

### Twelve Spigots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בֶּן קָטִין עָשָׂה שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר דַּד לַכִּיּוֹר, אַף הוּא עָשָׂה מוּכְנִי לַכִּיּוֹר שֶׁלֹּא יִהְיוּ מֵימָיו נִפְסָלִין בְּלִינָה. מַאי, לָאו רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא? לָא, רַבִּי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to resolve the issue: Come and hear a proof from a mishna (Yoma 37a): The High Priest ben Katin made twelve spigots for the Basin so that several priests could sanctify their hands and feet at once. He also made a mechanism [*mukhani*] for sinking the Basin into water during the night so that its water would not be disqualified by being left overnight. What, is it not that this mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon? If so, he holds that water of the Basin is disqualified by being left overnight. The Gemara responds: No, it is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

At this point the *gemara* introduces a *ta shema* drawn from a historical detail about the Temple. Ben Kattin, who served as *kohen gadol*, redesigned the *kiyor* from a vessel with two spigots to one with twelve — *Ben Kattin asa shenaim asar dad la-kiyor* — so that twelve kohanim could sanctify their hands and feet simultaneously rather than waiting in line.

### Pulley System

More significantly for our discussion, Ben Kattin also created a pulley mechanism — *mukhni* — that allowed the *kiyor* to be lowered each evening into the spring beneath it, so that its water would mingle with the fresh spring water. The purpose was explicit: *shelo yehu meimav nifsalim be-linah* — so that the water of the *kiyor* would not become *pasul* through overnight standing. This device addressed a problem that had apparently gone without a technical solution for the entire period of the *Mishkan* and much of the *Bayit Rishon*.

### Attribution Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּרֵישָׁא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – סֵיפָא נָמֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But from the fact that the first clause of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, one should infer that the latter clause is also in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.

The *gemara* now asks: according to whom was this practice instituted? *Mai lav Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon?* One might have thought that since Ben Kattin went to the trouble of lowering the *kiyor*, he must have been concerned that the water would otherwise become *pasul* — which would suggest he followed the view of Rabbi, not Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon. Or one might argue the opposite: perhaps he follows Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *linah* does not disqualify, and the lowering was done for some other reason or as an added stringency.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: בָּא לוֹ אֵצֶל פָּרוֹ; וּפַר הָיָה עוֹמֵד בֵּין הָאוּלָם וּלְמִזְבֵּחַ, רֹאשׁוֹ לַדָּרוֹם וּפָנָיו לַמַּעֲרָב; וְהַכֹּהֵן עוֹמֵד בַּמִּזְרָח וּפָנָיו לַמַּעֲרָב. מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר בֵּין הָאוּלָם וְלַמִּזְבֵּחַ – צָפוֹן? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: As the first clause teaches (Yoma 35b): At one point during the Yom Kippur service, the High Priest would come and stand next to his bull, and his bull was standing between the Entrance Hall and the altar with its head to the south and its face to the west. And the priest stands to the east of the bull, and his face points to the west. This is where he would slaughter the bull. Whom have you heard who says that between the Entrance Hall and the altar is considered part of the north of the courtyard, where it is permitted to slaughter offerings of the most sacred order? It is Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – מִשּׁוּם חוּלְשָׁא דְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל; הָכָא נָמֵי, מִשּׁוּם חוּלְשָׁא דְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, what have you to say to explain why the bull is positioned specifically between the Entrance Hall and the altar? It is due to the weakness of the High Priest, so that he need not exert himself and walk long distances on Yom Kippur. Here too, the mishna may be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and due to the weakness of the High Priest the bull is positioned there.

The *gemara* concludes: *lo, Rabbi hi* — this practice reflects the position of Rabbi, who holds that overnight does disqualify the water, and the *mukhni* was therefore constructed to prevent that disqualification. However, the *gemara* then notes that the reasoning can be inverted: one could have assumed from the earlier clause that this is according to Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon, and inferred the same for the later clause. Neither inference constitutes definitive proof. The question remains open on this front, and the *gemara* moves to a different avenue of discussion.

## Temple Geography

### Bull's Position

The *gemara* transitions to a distinct but related topic arising from the Yom Kippur service. The *par* — the bull brought by the *kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur — stood between the *ulam* (the entrance hall) and the *mizbe'ach*, with its head to the south and its face to the west, while the *kohen* stood to its east facing west. The reason the bull's body ran north-south rather than east-west is that if it faced east, this would be considered *lo kavod* — undignified — since it would be oriented toward the entrance through which the populace entered.

### Definition Tzafon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ צָפוֹן? מִקִּיר מִזְבֵּחַ צְפוֹנִי וְעַד כּוֹתֶל עֲזָרָה צְפוֹנִי, וּכְנֶגֶד כׇּל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ כּוּלּוֹ צָפוֹן. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹסִיף: אַף בֵּין הָאוּלָם וְלַמִּזְבֵּחַ. רַבִּי מוֹסִיף: אַף מְקוֹם דְּרִיסַת רַגְלֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים וּמְקוֹם דְּרִיסַת רַגְלֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. אֲבָל מִן הַחֲלִיפוֹת וְלִפְנִים – הַכֹּל מוֹדִים שֶׁפָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** This is as it is taught in a *baraita*: What is the north? It is the area from the northern wall of the altar until the northern wall of the Temple courtyard. And opposite the entire altar is considered the north; this is the statement of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, adds even the area between the Entrance Hall and the altar to the area that is considered north. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi adds even the areas to the north in the place where the priests walk, and the areas to the north in the place where the Israelites walk. But all concede that the area from the Chamber of the Knives [*haḥalifot*] and inward, which is an area to the side, is unfit for slaughtering offerings of the most sacred order, as it is not visible from the altar.

The positioning of the *par* in this location raises the broader question: what is *tzafon* — the "north" — for purposes of the laws requiring that *kodshei kodashim* (offerings of the highest sanctity) be slaughtered in the northern section of the *azarah*? The north is clearly designated as such, but its precise boundaries — how far the designation extends — is a matter of dispute. A *baraita* frames the question: *eizehu tzafon?*

### Tannaitic Dispute

Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Yehuda defines *tzafon* as extending from the northern wall of the *mizbe'ach* to the northern wall of the *azarah*, and spanning the full width of the *mizbe'ach* — *mi-kir mizbe'ach tzefoni ve-ad kotel azarah tzefoni u-keneged kol ha-mizbe'ach kulo*. Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon extends this definition to include also the area between the *ulam* and the *mizbe'ach* — *af bein ha-ulam ve-la-mizbe'ach*. Rabbi (Yehudah HaNasi) extends it further still to include the area where the kohanim walk and the area where Israelites stand.

### Kohen Gadol Fatigue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא?! רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא – וְלָא רַבִּי?! [הַשְׁתָּא רַבִּי] אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹסֵיף, אַדְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא מוֹסֵיף?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: And must you understand that the first clause of the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, and not the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi? Now, does Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi add only to the statement of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, but not add to the statement of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon? The area deemed north according to Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, is included in the area deemed north by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. Accordingly, since the first clause of the mishna can also be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, perhaps the mishna offers no proof at all with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.

A second approach is attempted: if the position of the *par* between the *ulam* and the *mizbe'ach* reflects Rabbi's expansive definition of *tzafon*, one might ask why the bull is not simply positioned closer to the entrance gate, which would also qualify as *tzafon* under Rabbi's view, and would spare the *kohen gadol* the exertion of walking further into the *azarah*. The *gemara* answers that the placement of the *par* near the *ulam* rather than at the entrance is not a legal ruling about what constitutes *tzafon* — it is a practical accommodation to the physical state of the *kohen gadol*. On Yom Kippur the *kohen gadol* is fasting, running repeatedly between different areas of the *mikdash*, and has been awake all night. The bull is positioned close to where he is working so as to reduce his burden. *Mishum chulsha de-kohen gadol* — because of the weakness of the *kohen gadol* — the bull is brought to him, not as a legal statement about the boundaries of *tzafon*. This resolves neither the question of the *kiyor* water nor the dispute about *tzafon*'s precise extent, and the *gemara* moves on.

## Terumat HaDeshen

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו לִתְרוּמַת הַדֶּשֶׁן – לְמָחָר אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ, שֶׁכְּבָר קִידֵּשׁ מִתְּחִילַּת עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan says: If a priest sanctified his hands and feet in preparation for the removal of the ashes, the first service performed every day in the Temple, which was performed before dawn, then the next day, i.e., after dawn, he does not need to sanctify them again, as he already sanctified them at the beginning of the day's service.

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *kiddesh yadav ve-raglav le-terumat ha-deshen, lemachar eino tzarich le-kaddesh, she-kevar kiddesh mi-techilat avodah* — a kohen who sanctified his hands and feet for the *terumat ha-deshen*, the removal of the ash from the altar, does not need to re-sanctify the following day, because he already sanctified at the beginning of the service.

*Terumat ha-deshen* — the removal of the ashes — is the first service of each morning, performed by a kohen who comes before dawn and clears the residue of the overnight burnt offerings from the altar. It is the opening act of *Parashat Tzav*, and it carries a certain symbolic weight: before the new day's service can begin, the remnants of the previous day must be cleared. That this task — taking out, in a manner of speaking, what remains — should fall to a kohen and be treated as sacred service says something important about how even the clearing away of what is finished is elevated within the *mikdash*.

The question is: *lema'an mi* — according to whose opinion is Rabbi Yochanan making this ruling? The challenge is immediate. If this statement is according to Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon, who holds that a kohen need not re-sanctify even after ten days, then of course there is no need to sanctify the next day — that is obvious and requires no special ruling. The very modesty of Rabbi Yochanan's claim — that one need not sanctify again merely the *next day* — implies a more restrictive background position.

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאן? אִי לְרַבִּי, הָאָמַר: פָּסְלָה לִינָה! אִי לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הָאָמַר: אֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ אֲפִילּוּ מִיכָּן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In accordance with whose opinion is this statement? If it is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, doesn't he say that being left overnight disqualifies sanctification of the hands and feet? If so, one should be required to sanctify them again after dawn. And if it is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, doesn't he say that if the priest sanctified his hands and feet at the beginning of the service, he does not need to sanctify them again even if he continues to perform rites for the next ten days? Why, then, does Rabbi Yoḥanan state only that one who sanctified his hands and feet before the removal of the ashes is not required to do so after dawn?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם לְרַבִּי; וְלִינָה – דְּרַבָּנַן הִיא, וּמוֹדֵי דְּמִקְּרוֹת הַגֶּבֶר וְעַד צַפְרָא לָא פָּסְלָה לִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye says: Actually, the statement is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and the disqualification of sanctification of the hands and feet by being left overnight is by rabbinic law. And although the Sages mandate that a priest must sanctify his hands and feet every morning, they concede that during the period from the rooster's crow before dawn until morning, being left overnight does not disqualify his sanctification. Therefore, one who sanctifies his hands and feet at the rooster's crow may continue to perform rites after dawn.

Abaye resolves the tension by saying that Rabbi Yochanan is following the view of Rabbi — who holds that *linah* does disqualify — but that Rabbi agrees *linah de-rabanan hi*: the overnight disqualification of *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim* is rabbinic in nature. And Rabbi concedes one exception: *mi-kri'at ha-gever ve-ad tzafra lo pasla linah* — from the *kri'at ha-gever* (the pre-dawn call, whether by rooster or appointed caller) until the break of dawn, the *linah* does not disqualify. A kohen who sanctified during this liminal period before dawn may continue to perform service into the following day, because that brief transitional interval does not constitute the kind of overnight separation that triggers the rabbinic disqualification. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling, then, is addressed to a kohen who sanctified during that window: he does not need to sanctify again when the full day begins.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן [הִיא, וְרָאָה רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּבָרָיו] בִּתְחִילַּת עֲבוֹדָה, וְלֹא בְּסוֹף עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava says: Actually, the statement is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon. And Rabbi Yoḥanan saw the statement of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, as correct with regard to the beginning of the service, i.e., that a priest may serve during the day on the strength of sanctification performed before dawn. But he did not agree with regard to the end of the service. Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that if a priest sanctified his hands and feet before participating in the day's final rites, he must sanctify them again the next morning. According to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, one who sanctified his hands and feet before sunrise must do so again afterward.

Rava offers a competing interpretation. He maintains that Rabbi Yochanan's statement does follow Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon — but only in part. Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon's lenient view, says Rava, was acknowledged by Rabbi Yochanan for *techilat avodah* — the beginning of the service — but not for *sof avodah*, the end or continuation of the service. This is a striking position: Rabbi Yochanan, an *amora*, is essentially accepting Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon's ruling for one phase of service while declining to extend it to another.

### Chronology Problem

This raises a methodological puzzle that deserves explicit attention. Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi, the redactor of the *Mishnah*, is roughly contemporaneous with the late *Tannaim* and flourished around 200 of the common era. Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon is a *Tanna* of the same period or somewhat earlier. Rabbi Yochanan, who studies and teaches in Tiberias, belongs to the first generation of *Amoraim*, working approximately 250–300 CE. Abaye and Rava belong to a later Babylonian generation, around 300–400. The *Amoraim* as a matter of general principle do not dispute *Tannaim* directly — they interpret, harmonize, and explain, but they do not simply overrule.

Yet Rava's reading of Rabbi Yochanan places him in a position of accepting one dimension of Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon's ruling while disagreeing with another. Moreover, the Babylonian *Amoraim* (Abaye and Rava) are attempting to determine what an earlier Israeli *Amora* (Rabbi Yochanan) meant when addressing a ruling of still-earlier Israeli *Tannaim* (Rabbi and Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon). The entire conversation reflects the urgency with which the later authorities felt pressed to resolve these laws — as though the *mikdash* might be rebuilt at any moment and clarity was essential.

## Tamid Mishna

### Scene Description

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רָאוּהוּ אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים שֶׁיָּרַד – וְהֵם רָצוּ וּבָאוּ, מִיהֲרוּ וְקִידְּשׁוּ יְדֵיהֶם וְרַגְלֵיהֶם מִן הַכִּיּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection from a mishna (Tamid 28b) that describes the daily service: His priestly brethren, members of the patrilineal family, saw that the priest who removed the ashes descended from the altar with the coal pan in his hands, and they ran and arrived at the Basin. They hurried and sanctified their hands and their feet with water from the Basin.

The *gemara* next introduces a *Mishnah* from Masechet Tamid. The brethren kohanim — *echav ha-kohanim* — see the kohen who has descended from the altar after performing the *terumat ha-deshen*. Seeing this is a signal that the new day has begun. *Hem ratzu u-va'u miharu ve-kidshu yedeihem ve-ragleihem min ha-kiyor* — they run and come quickly and sanctify their hands and feet from the *kiyor*. The image is vivid: kohanim hurrying to the laver at the moment the day's service is inaugurated.

### Interpretive Ambiguity

The question is what this *Mishnah* proves. The kohanim rush to sanctify — which implies they had not yet done so, or that the overnight had intervened and they must begin again. On the face of it, this seems to support Rabbi's position that overnight passage requires re-sanctification. But the *gemara* pauses: is that the only reading?

### New Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְאַבָּיֵי דְּמוֹקֵים לַהּ כְּרַבִּי, וּמוֹדֵי רַבִּי דְּמִקְּרוֹת הַגֶּבֶר עַד צַפְרָא לָא פָּסְלָה לִינָה – הָא מַנִּי, רַבִּי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Assuming that these priests had all been serving in the Temple through the night, one can ask: Granted, according to Abaye, who interprets the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi concedes that during the period from the rooster's crow before dawn until morning, being left overnight does not disqualify sanctification, in accordance with whose opinion is this mishna? It is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that although they served through the night, the previous day's sanctification was disqualified and they must sanctify their hands and feet again in the morning, but they may do so before dawn.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּכָהֲנֵי חַדְתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Actually, the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, and here we are dealing with new priests, who did not serve in the Temple that night. Since they are beginning their service now, they must sanctify their hands and feet.

The *gemara* resolves: *le-olam ke-Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon, ve-hacha be-mai askinan? Be-Kohanei chadati* — in truth this *Mishnah* may be following the view of Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon, because the kohanim who are running to sanctify are entirely new kohanim — *kohanei chadashim* — who have never sanctified their hands and feet before and are coming to the *mikdash* for the first time. Even Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon agrees that a kohen sanctifying for the first time must do so *bevo'am*, when they enter. These are not kohanim who sanctified yesterday and need to decide whether to repeat — these are kohanim who have never yet sanctified for this service at all.

### Unresolved

The result is that this *Mishnah* proves nothing decisive for either side of the dispute. It is consistent with Rabbi's position and equally consistent with Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon's position. The *gemara* has not moved any closer to resolution, and both views remain viable.

## Yetziah Question

### Core Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: יְצִיאָה מַהוּ שֶׁתּוֹעִיל בְּקִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם? אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר לִינָה לָא פָּסְלָה – דְּלָא פָּרֵישׁ, אֲבָל יְצִיאָה דְּפָרֵישׁ – אַסּוֹחֵי מַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּבְיָדוֹ לַחֲזוֹר – לָא מַסַּח?
>
> **Translation:** A dilemma was raised before the Sages: What is the halakha with regard to leaving the Temple? Is it effective to disqualify sanctification of the hands and feet such that the priest must sanctify them again when he reenters the Temple? The Gemara elaborates: If you say that being left overnight does not disqualify it, perhaps that is only because he does not separate from the Temple; but when leaving the Temple, where the priest separates from the Temple, it is possible that the priest becomes distracted from the service. If so, he must sanctify his hands and feet again. Or perhaps, since it is in his power to return to the Temple at any point, he does not become distracted, in which case he does not need to sanctify them again.

The *gemara* now turns to a new and related question: *ibayya lehu, yetziah mahu she-to'il be-kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim?* — what is the legal effect of *yetziah*, leaving the *azarah*, on prior sanctification of hands and feet? A kohen who has sanctified his hands and feet inside the temple and then exits — does his sanctification remain valid when he returns, or is it nullified?

A brief preliminary note is important here. From *de-oraita* there is no concept of *tumat yadayim* — partial impurity affecting only the hands. A person is either entirely *tamei* or entirely *tahor*. The notion that hands can be independently impure is a rabbinic enactment — *takanat Chachamim* — that the Sages applied in the context of kohanim who handled *piggul* (sacrifices with invalid intent) or *notar* (leftover sacrifices), which the Rabbis declared to render hands *tamei*. It is against this backdrop that the entire system of *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim* is being discussed.

### Two Approaches

Two frameworks are proposed for understanding whether *yetziah* disqualifies prior sanctification. The first is that the problem, if there is one, is *prishut* — the physical separation itself: the kohen has left the domain of sanctified service, and the departure constitutes a break. The second is that the issue is *hasachat ha-da'at* — a distraction or diversion of attention: if the kohen leaves but only to briefly retrieve something and immediately return, his mind (*da'at*) remains focused on his service and he has not truly disengaged. *Keivan de-veyado lachzor lo mesiach* — since it is within his power to return, his attention has not been diverted.

### Ta Shema 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: קִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו וְנִטְמְאוּ – מַטְבִּילָן וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַדֵּשׁ. יָצְאוּ – הֲרֵי הֵן בִּקְדוּשָּׁתָן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Come and hear a proof from a baraita: If a priest sanctified his hands and feet and his hands then became impure, he may immerse them to purify them, and he does not need to sanctify them again. If he sanctified his hands and they then left the Temple, they retain their sanctity. Apparently, leaving the Temple does not disqualify sanctification.

### Rejection 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאוּ יָדָיו לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לַן, כִּי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לַן – יָצָא כׇּל גּוּפוֹ. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: We do not raise the dilemma with regard to a case where only his hands left the Temple. In such a case, he certainly does not need to sanctify them again. When we raise the dilemma, it is with regard to a case where his entire body left the Temple; what is the halakha in such a case?

### Ta Shema 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שֶׁלֹּא רָחוּץ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם – מְקַדֵּשׁ בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת בִּפְנִים. קִידֵּשׁ בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת בַּחוּץ אוֹ בִּכְלֵי חוֹל בִּפְנִים אוֹ שֶׁטָּבַל בְּמֵי מְעָרָה, וְעָבַד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה. טַעְמָא דְּקִידֵּשׁ בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת בַּחוּץ, הָא קִידֵּשׁ בִּפְנִים וְיָצָא – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Come and hear another proof from a baraita: One whose hands and feet are not washed must sanctify them with a service vessel inside the Temple. If he sanctified them with a service vessel outside the Temple, or with a non-sacred vessel inside, or if he immersed in cave water and performed the service, his service is disqualified. The Gemara infers: The reason his service is disqualified is that he sanctified his hands and feet with a service vessel outside, but if he sanctified them inside and then left, his service is valid.

### Rejection 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא ״קִידֵּשׁ בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת בַּחוּץ״ הֵיכִי דָּמֵי – כְּגוֹן דְּאַפֵּיק יָדָיו לְבַר וְקִידֵּשׁ; הָא קִידֵּשׁ בִּפְנִים וְיָצָא, דְּאַפֵּיק יְדֵיהּ לְבַר – כְּשֵׁירָה. אֲבָל יָצָא כׇּל גּוּפוֹ תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Perhaps one can say: What are the circumstances of the case where one sanctified them with a service vessel outside? It is a case where he removed only his hands and placed them outside the Temple and sanctified them there. Consequently, one can infer only that if he sanctified them inside and then left, i.e., specifically where he removed only his hand and placed it outside, his service is valid. But with regard to a case where his entire body left the Temple, you should raise the dilemma.

### Ta Shema 3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב זְבִיד לְרַב פָּפָּא, תָּא שְׁמַע: יָצָא חוּץ לִמְחִיצַת חוֹמַת הָעֲזָרָה – אִם לִשְׁהוֹת, טָעוּן טְבִילָה; אִם לְפִי שָׁעָה, טָעוּן קִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Zevid said to Rav Pappa: Come and hear a proof from another baraita: In a case where a priest left the boundary of the wall of the Temple courtyard, if he left long enough to remain there for a time, he requires immersion. If he left only for a moment, he requires sanctification of the hands and feet. Apparently, one who leaves even for a brief time requires sanctification.

### Rejection 3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּצָא לְהָסֵךְ רַגְלָיו וּלְהַטִּיל מַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said to him: Here we are dealing with a case where the priest left the Temple to cover his legs, a euphemism for defecating, or to urinate. It is only for this reason that he must sanctify his hands and feet when reentering the Temple.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא בְּהֶדְיָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ: כָּל הַמֵּיסֵךְ רַגְלָיו טָעוּן טְבִילָה, וְכׇל הַמֵּטִיל מַיִם טָעוּן קִידּוּשׁ יָדַיִם וְרַגְלַיִם! תָּנֵי וַהֲדַר מְפָרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But the mishna (Yoma 28a) already teaches it explicitly: Anyone who covers his legs requires immersion afterward; and anyone who urinates requires sanctification of the hands and feet with water from the Basin afterward. The Gemara responds: The redundancy is not difficult. The *tanna* teaches the principle and then explains it again.

Rav Zvid presents to Rav Pappa a third *ta shema*: *yatza chutz le-mechizat chomat ha-azarah, im lishhot ta'un tevilah, im le-fi sha'ah ta'un kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim* — if a kohen left outside the wall of the *azarah* for a significant stay (*lishhot*), he requires full immersion; if for just a brief moment (*le-fi sha'ah*), he requires only *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim*. This at least implies that a brief exit does not eliminate prior sanctification entirely — otherwise full immersion would be required in all cases.

Rav Pappa rejects this as well: *hacha be-mai askinan? Ke-gon she-yatza le-hasech raglav u-le-hatil mayim* — this source is dealing with a kohen who left the temple to use the bathroom. That is a specific case with its own rules, and it is in fact already taught in Yoma: *kol ha-mesi'ach raglav ta'un tevilah, ve-khol ha-matil mayim ta'un kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim* — one who goes to relieve himself completely requires immersion; one who urinates requires *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim*. The *braita* cited by Rav Zvid is simply restating and elaborating on this principle — *tani ve-hadar mefaresh* — and teaches nothing about the general case of a kohen who exits the *azarah* for an ordinary reason.

### Ta Shema 4

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: פָּרָה – רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר: מְקַדֵּשׁ בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת בִּפְנִים, וְיוֹצֵא. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בַּחוּץ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּכְלִי חוֹל, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקִידָּה שֶׁל חֶרֶס!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Come and hear a proof from the following dispute: With regard to the priest who performs the service of the red heifer, which takes place outside the Temple, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef says: In preparation, he must sanctify his hands and feet with a service vessel inside the Temple, as he must before any other service, and afterward he leaves the Temple to perform the service. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He may sanctify his hands even outside of the Temple, and even with a non-sacred vessel, and even with an earthenware bowl [*bimkeida*]. Apparently, leaving the Temple does not disqualify sanctification of the hands and feet.

### Pappa Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שָׁאנֵי פָּרָה – הוֹאִיל וְכׇל מַעֲשֶׂיהָ בַּחוּץ, לָא פָּסְלָה בָּהּ יְצִיאָה. אִי הָכִי, לְמָה לִי דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ? כְּעֵין עֲבוֹדָה פְּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said: The rite of the red heifer is different, since all of its acts are performed outside the Temple, unlike other rites. Therefore, leaving the Temple does not disqualify sanctification of the hands and feet with regard to it. The Gemara asks: If so, why do I require that the priest sanctify his hands and feet at all for the service of the red heifer? The Gemara responds: The service of the red heifer must be similar to the service of offerings performed inside the Temple.

Rav Pappa addresses this case: *shani Para, ho'il ve-khol ma'aseha ba-chutz, lo pasla bi-yetziah* — the *Para Aduma* is different, because the entire service takes place outside the *azarah*. Since the *avodah* itself is conducted outside, the act of exiting cannot disqualify the sanctification — there would be nothing left to perform if it did. This is a special case and cannot serve as a general proof about a kohen who exits during an otherwise interior service.

However, this very reasoning generates a counterquestion: *iy hachi, lama li de-mekadesh?* — if the service is entirely outside and exiting does not disqualify, why does the kohen need to perform *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim* at all for the *Para Aduma* service? The *gemara* answers: it is done *ke-eyn avodat pnim* — to mirror and approximate, as closely as possible, the procedures of the interior temple service. Even when the *avodah* takes place outside, the requirements of interior service are maintained where feasible.

### Suspended

Notwithstanding these four attempted proofs, the question of *yetziah* and its effect on prior *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim* remains entirely unresolved. Each *ta shema* has been dismissed without providing a definitive answer to the central dilemma — whether it is the physical act of separation from the *azarah* that matters, or whether it is *hasachat ha-da'at*, the diversion of the kohen's attention and intention, that is the operative factor. The *gemara* leaves the question suspended for continued study.
