# Horayot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hierarchical Honor

### Nasi Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁהַנָּשִׂיא נִכְנָס, כׇּל הָעָם עוֹמְדִים, וְאֵין יוֹשְׁבִים עַד שֶׁאוֹמֵר לָהֶם: שֵׁבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **When the *Nasi*** of the Sanhedrin **enters, all the people stand and they do not sit until he says to them: Sit.**

The final *daf* of *Masechet Horayot* — the smallest *masekhet* in *Shas* — opens on 13b with a *beraitah* that addresses the protocols of honor within the *Sanhedrin* and the *beit midrash*. The *masekhet* as a whole is not the place to look for an egalitarian ethos within *Chazal*; its focus has consistently been on people in positions of authority — the *beit din*, the *kohen gadol*, the *nasi*, the king — and on questions of precedence. The final *mishnayot* dealt with who takes priority over whom, arising initially from the fact that the *par* of the *kohen gadol* precedes the *par he'elam davar shel tzibbur*. That thread expanded into the broader principles of *tadir* and *kadosh mechavero*, and into questions of status involving *kohen*, *levi*, and *yisrael*, as well as degree of need. Yet the *mishnah* also introduced a powerful counterweight: Torah learning is the great leveler. A *talmid chacham* takes precedence over a *kohen gadol am ha'aretz*. With that principle established, the *Gemara* now examines how different levels of Torah-based authority are honored in practice.

*Tanu rabanan*: when the *nasi* — the head of the *Sanhedrin*, who in *Eretz Yisrael* merged the political and rabbinic leadership in a single office (unlike in *Bavel*, where the *reish galuta* held the political role separately) — enters the *beit midrash* or the *Sanhedrin*, *kol ha'am omdim*: everyone rises. And *ein yoshvim ad sheyomar lahem shvu*: no one sits until he explicitly gives them permission. The honor is total and the resumption of normal activity requires his direct authorization, much as a bailiff calls the room to order and a judge then dismisses them to be seated.

### Av Beit Din Pathway

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁאַב בֵּית דִּין נִכְנָס, עוֹשִׂים לוֹ שׁוּרָה אַחַת מִכָּאן וְשׁוּרָה אַחַת מִכָּאן, עַד שֶׁיֵּשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **When the deputy *Nasi*** of the Sanhedrin **enters,** the people **form for him one row from here,** on this side of the path that he takes, **and one row from there,** on the other side of it, in a display of deference, **until he sits in his place,** and then they may be seated.

When the *av beit din* enters — the second-ranking figure, functioning something like an executive director to the *nasi*'s presidency, with a more internally focused administrative role — the honor is calibrated accordingly. The assembly does not all rise simultaneously; rather, they form two rows on either side of his path, creating a human corridor as he walks to his place. Those who face him as he passes stand, and they sit when he takes his seat — but crucially, he need not explicitly grant them permission to sit. Two gradations are already visible: universal standing with an explicit release versus a pathway of standing that settles naturally.

### Chacham Wave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁחָכָם נִכְנָס, אֶחָד עוֹמֵד וְאֶחָד יוֹשֵׁב, עַד שֶׁיֵּשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **When the *Ḥakham*,** who is ranked third among the members of the Sanhedrin, **enters, one** person **stands** when he is within four cubits of the *Ḥakham*, **and another sits,** i.e., when one is no longer within four cubits of the *Ḥakham* he may sit. And all those whom the Ḥakham passes do this, **until he sits in his place.**

The third level is the *chacham* — the third in authority after the *nasi* and the *av beit din*, a role that may have been particularly associated with Torah scholarship rather than administrative function. When the *chacham* enters, the honor is rendered in yet a more modest form: *echad omed ve'echad yoshev ad sheyeishev bimkomo* — one person stands as he passes and then sits as the *chacham* moves on to the next person, producing what amounts to a wave of rising and sitting that travels through the room in his wake. The underlying obligation of *lifnei seiva takum vehadarta pnei zaken* applies to each individual as the *chacham* comes before him, but not so as to require the entire assembly to stand at once.

### Sons/Students Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי חֲכָמִים וְתַלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, בִּזְמַן שֶׁרַבִּים צְרִיכִים לָהֶם – מַפְסִיעִין עַל רָאשֵׁי הָעָם.
>
> **Translation:** **When the multitudes require their** services, i.e., they serve a public role, **sons of the Sages and Torah scholars may step over the heads of the people** seated on the ground in order to reach their places in the Sanhedrin.

The *beraitah* continues: *bnei chachamim vetalmidei chachamim* — the sons and students of the *chachamim* — when the public needs them, may step over the heads of the people (*mafsi'in al rashei ha'am*) in order to reach the front. The study hall setting apparently lacked clear aisles, and making one's way through a seated crowd would involve stepping over people. While this might appear somewhat disrespectful to those being stepped over, when these individuals are needed urgently at the front, it is permitted and preferable to causing even greater disruption by opening a full pathway.

### Returning Seats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצָא לְצוֹרֶךְ – יִכָּנֵס וְיֵשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If one of the Sages **left for** the **purpose** of relieving himself, when he is finished **he may enter and sit in his place** in the Sanhedrin, and he need not be concerned that he is imposing upon those assembled.

If someone had to leave the assembly — to attend to a personal need, that is, to use the facilities — upon returning he may make his way back to his place even if doing so causes some disruption. The protocols of the *Sanhedrin* or *beit midrash* thus accommodate ordinary human necessity.

### Sons with Fathers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁמְּמוּנִּים אֲבִיהֶם פַּרְנָס עַל הַצִּבּוּר, בִּזְמַן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם דַּעַת לִשְׁמוֹעַ – נִכְנָסִים וְיוֹשְׁבִים לִפְנֵי אֲבִיהֶם וַאֲחוֹרֵיהֶם כְּלַפֵּי הָעָם. בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם דַּעַת לִשְׁמוֹעַ – נִכְנָסִים וְיוֹשְׁבִים לִפְנֵי אֲבִיהֶם וּפְנֵיהֶם כְּלַפֵּי הַעָם.
>
> **Translation:** **When they have the wisdom to hear** and to study, **the sons of Torah scholars, whose fathers are appointed as leaders of the congregation, enter and sit before their fathers, and their backs** are directed **toward the people. When they do not have the wisdom to hear** and to study **they enter and sit before their fathers, and their faces** are directed **toward the people,** so everyone sees that they are seated there in deference to their fathers but not as students.

The *beraitah* then addresses *bnei talmidei chachamim* — the sons (two degrees removed from the *chachamim* themselves) — whose fathers have been appointed as community leaders (*parnas al hatzibbur*). If these sons are capable of following the learning (*yesh bahem da'at lishmoa*), they may enter and sit directly before their fathers, facing the front of the hall with their backs to the people, as ordinary participants do when receiving instruction. If they are not yet able to follow the substance of the learning (*ein bahem da'at lishmoa*), they may still enter and sit before their fathers, but now facing the community rather than the front — because their role is not really to receive the teaching but to share in their father's reflected honor. The analogy is familiar: a rabbi's children sitting with him at the front of a *shul*, not because they have a formal function, but because their father holds the position of authority.

It is notable that this provision speaks of *bnei talmidei chachamim* and not *bnei chachamim*; the *bnei chachamim* had already appeared in the earlier clause about stepping over people, and their access here is apparently conditional on their fathers holding an active communal appointment. The exact institutional setting — whether *Sanhedrin* or *beit midrash* — and the precise seating arrangements remain somewhat opaque, but the underlying logic is clear.

### Weddings Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר רַבִּי [צָדוֹק] אוֹמֵר: אַף בְּבֵית הַמִּשְׁתֶּה עוֹשִׂים אוֹתָם סְנִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says: Even at** a wedding **party one renders them attachments [*senifin*]** and seats them adjacent to their fathers.

Rabbi Eliezer berabbi Tzadok extends the principle: even at a *beit hamishteh*, a wedding celebration, the children are permitted to sit alongside their fathers in a position of honor as a *snif* — an attachment or appendage — to the honor afforded the father himself. The term *snif* is apt: in halachic discourse *snif le-hakel* denotes a supplementary basis for leniency, something that does not stand alone but bolsters the primary ruling. Here the children have no independent honor; they participate by virtue of their fathers' status.

## Bathroom Rule

### Urine vs Defecation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא לִקְטַנִּים, אֲבָל לִגְדוֹלִים – לָא, הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִבְדַּק נַפְשֵׁיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The Sages **said** this **only** with regard to one who leaves **for minor** bodily functions, i.e., to urinate. **But** with regard to one who leaves **for major** bodily functions, i.e., to defecate, **no,** he may not return to his place, because **he should have examined himself initially** so that he would not need to leave.

The *Gemara* now returns to analyze the provision that one who stepped out for a personal need may return to his place. Rav Papa draws a sharp distinction: the permission was stated only for urination (*ktanim*), but not for defecation (*gdolim*). Why? *Havei lei lemivdak nafshieh me-ikara* — he should have examined himself beforehand. The need to urinate can arise without warning and cannot always be anticipated, but one can and should check whether one needs to defecate before settling into one's place and subjecting the assembly to the disruption of his return passage.

### Planning Ahead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לְעוֹלָם יְלַמֵּד אָדָם עַצְמוֹ לְהַשְׁכִּים וּלְהַעֲרִיב, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִתְרַחֵק.
>
> **Translation:** His failure to do so constitutes negligence and he may not impose upon others when he returns, **as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A person should always accustom himself to** relieving himself **in the morning and in the evening so that he will not** need to **distance himself**  during the daylight hours to find an appropriate place.

This principle is grounded in a teaching of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: a person should always train himself to relieve himself early in the morning and in the evening, so that he does not need to go a great distance during the day when people are around — for at a time when others are present, modesty requires one to go far from populated areas. The practice of attending to this need at set times demonstrates that it is, to a meaningful degree, a matter of planning and self-discipline. The one who failed to do so before entering the assembly bears responsibility for the disruption his exit and return cause.

### Rava's Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָאִידָּנָא דַּחֲלַשׁא עָלְמָא – אֲפִילּוּ לִגְדוֹלִים נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Today, when the world is weak** and people are not as healthy as they once were, one may **even** return after he leaves **for major** bodily functions.

Rava, however, introduces a compassionate qualification: *ha-idna de-chalsha alma* — nowadays, human constitutions are weaker. One cannot always predict or control the need even when one tries to plan ahead. Therefore, Rava holds that in this era, one should be granted the leniency of returning to one's seat even after attending to *gdolim*. The physiological realities of later generations soften the strict standard Rav Papa had applied.

## Gamliel Status Story

### Equal Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּימֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל נִישְׁנֵית מִשְׁנָה זוֹ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל נָשִׂיא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר חָכָם, רַבִּי נָתָן אַב בֵּית דִּין. כִּי הֲוָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָתָם, הֲווֹ קָיְימִי כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִקַּמֵּיהּ. כִּי הֲווֹ עָיְילִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי נָתָן, הֲווֹ קָיְימִי כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִקַּמַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This mishna,** i.e., the preceding *baraita*, **was taught during the days of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** was the ***Nasi*, Rabbi Meir** was the ***Ḥakham*,** and **Rabbi Natan** was the **deputy *Nasi*. When Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was there, everyone would arise before him. When Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan would enter, everyone would arise before them.**

Rabbi Yochanan provides the historical context for the *beraitah* that opened this *daf*: this teaching — which differentiates the honor shown to the *nasi*, the *av beit din*, and the *chacham* — was issued in the days of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. At that time, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was the *nasi*, Rabbi Meir was the *chacham*, and Rabbi Natan was the *av beit din*. Before the ruling was established, when Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel entered the *Sanhedrin*, the entire assembly would rise; and when Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan entered, the entire assembly would likewise rise — with no distinction whatsoever.

### Honor Reform

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: לָא בָּעוּ לְמִיהְוֵי הֶיכֵּרָא בֵּין דִּילִי לְדִידְהוּ? תַּקֵּין הָא מַתְנִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: Shouldn't there be a conspicuous distinction between me and them** in terms of the manner in which deference is shown? Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **instituted** the provisions delineated in **this *baraita*** that distinguish between the *Nasi* and his subordinates with regard to the deference shown them.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel reflected: should there not be some visible distinction between the honor shown to him and the honor shown to them? He occupied a higher level of authority; the absence of any differentiation obscured the hierarchy of roles. He therefore enacted the *beraitah* we have been studying, establishing graduated levels of honor — universal standing for the *nasi*, a pathway for the *av beit din*, a wave for the *chacham*. Significantly, he issued this ruling on a day when Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan were not present. The *Gemara* notes this as though it were coincidental — though one may well wonder whether that is entirely so.

### Conspiracy Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יוֹמָא לָא הֲווֹ רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי נָתָן הָתָם, לִמְחַר כִּי אֲתוֹ חֲזוֹ דְּלָא קָמוּ מִקַּמַּיְיהוּ כְּדִרְגִילָא מִילְּתָא, אָמְרִי: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַרוּ לְהוּ: הָכִי תַּקֵּין רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְרַבִּי נָתָן: אֲנָא חָכָם וְאַתְּ אַב בֵּית דִּין, נְתַקֵּין מִילְּתָא כִּי לְדִידַן. מַאי נַעֲבֵיד לֵיהּ? נֵימָא לֵיהּ: גַּלִּי עוּקְצִים, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ. וְכֵיוָן דְּלָא גְּמִר, נֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״מִי יְמַלֵּל גְּבוּרוֹת ה׳ יַשְׁמִיעַ כׇּל תְּהִלָּתוֹ״, לְמִי נָאֶה לְמַלֵּל גְּבוּרוֹת ה׳ – מִי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְהַשְׁמִיעַ כׇּל תְּהִלּוֹתָיו. נְעַבְּרֵיהּ, וְהָוֵי אֲנָא אַב בֵּית דִּין וְאַתְּ נָשִׂיא.
>
> **Translation:** **That day,** when Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel instituted these provisions, **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan were not there. The following day when they came** to the study hall, **they saw that** the people **did not stand before them as the matter was typically** done. **They said: What is this?** The people **said to them: This** is what **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel instituted. Rabbi Meir said to Rabbi Natan: I am** the ***Ḥakham* and you are** the **deputy *Nasi*. Let us devise a matter** and do to him **as he did to us. What shall we do to him? Let us say to him: Reveal** to us tractate ***Okatzim*, which he does not** know. **And once** it is clear to all **that he did not learn,** he will not have anything to say. Then **we will say to him: "Who can express the mighty acts of the Lord, shall make all His praises heard?"** (Psalms 106:2), indicating: **For whom is it becoming to express the mighty acts of the Lord?** It is becoming for **one who is capable of making all His praises heard,** and not for one who does not know one of the tractates. **We will remove him** from his position as *Nasi*, **and I will be deputy *Nasi* and you** will be ***Nasi*.**

The next day, when Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan arrived at the *beit midrash*, they noticed that people were no longer rising for them in the way they had before. They inquired and were told: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel had established this new protocol in their absence. Rabbi Meir said to Rabbi Natan: I am the *chacham* and you are the *av beit din* — let us devise a response. Their plan was a kind of palace coup. They would ask Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel to teach *Masechet Uktzin* — the last and most obscure *masechet* in *Shas*, a tractate in *Taharot* that almost no one mastered. As the *nasi*, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel held a combined political and Torah role, but he was not necessarily on the same level of pure Torah expertise as the *av beit din* and the *chacham*. If he was unable to teach *Uktzin*, they could invoke the verse *mi yemallel gevurot Hashem yashmia kol tehillato* — who is fitting to speak of God's greatness? Only one who can make known all of His praise. One who cannot command the full breadth of Torah does not deserve the status he holds. *Venehavi*, they said: we will remove him and replace him — I will become the *av beit din* and you will become the *nasi*.

### Warning Signal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁמְעִינְהוּ רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בֶּן קֻדְשַׁי, אֲמַר: דִּלְמָא חַס וְשָׁלוֹם אָתְיָא מִלְּתָא לִידֵי כִּיסּוּפָא, אֲזַל יְתֵיב אֲחוֹרֵי עִילִּיתֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, פְּשַׁט, גְּרַס וּתְנָא, גְּרַס וּתְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov ben Korshei heard them** talking, and **said: Perhaps, Heaven forfend,** this **matter** will **come to** a situation of **humiliation** for Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. He did not wish to speak criticism or gossip about Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan, so **he went** and **sat behind the upper story** where **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** lived. **He explained** tractate *Okatzin*; **he studied** it aloud **and repeated** it, and **studied** it aloud **and repeated** it.

Rabbi Yaakov ben Korshai overheard this plan. Concerned that it would lead, *chas veshalom*, to the public humiliation of the *nasi*, he positioned himself outside the upper story of Rabban Gamliel's house and proceeded to study *Uktzin* aloud — explaining, repeating, reviewing — over and over again. Rabban Gamliel, hearing this unusual behavior, understood that something was afoot in the *beit midrash*. He could not know the precise details, but the signal was unmistakable: study *Uktzin* — something is coming.

### Uktzin Study

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: מַאי דְּקַמָּא? דִּלְמָא חַס וְשָׁלוֹם אִיכָּא בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא מִידֵּי, יְהַב דַּעְתֵּיהּ וְגַרְסַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **said** to himself: **What** is this **that** is transpiring **before us? Perhaps, Heaven forfend, there is something** transpiring in **the study hall.** He suspected that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan were planning something. **He concentrated and studied** tractate *Okatzin*.

Heeding the implicit warning, Rabban Gamliel set his mind to the tractate that night and thoroughly reviewed it.

### Challenge Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִמְחַר אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: נֵיתֵי מָר וְנִיתְנֵי בְּעוּקְצִין, פְּתַח וַאֲמַר. בָּתַר דְּאוֹקֵים, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי לָא גְּמִירְנָא, כַּסֵּיפְיתֻּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **The following** day Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan **said to him: Let the Master come and teach** a lesson **in** tractate ***Okatzim*. He began and stated** the lesson he had prepared. **After he completed** teaching the tractate, **he said to them: If I had not studied** the tractate, **you** would have **humiliated me.**

The next day, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan made their move: *neisei mar venisnei be-Uktzin* — let the master teach us *Uktzin*, we would like to learn this *masechet*. Rabban Gamliel opened and taught — he knew the texts, he could explicate them, and what his challengers had assumed to be an area of ignorance proved instead to be thoroughly prepared ground. Having completed his presentation, he said to them: had I not studied this last night, *kisfitun* — you would have shamed me. I know what you intended.

### Expulsion Notes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּקֵּיד וְאַפְּקִינְהוּ מִבֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. הֲווֹ כָּתְבִי קוּשְׁיָיתָא [בְּפִתְקָא] וְשָׁדוּ הָתָם. דַּהֲוָה מִיפְּרִיק – מִיפְּרִיק, דְּלָא הֲווֹ מִיפְּרִיק – כָּתְבִי פֵּירוּקֵי וְשָׁדוּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: תּוֹרָה מִבַּחוּץ וְאָנוּ מִבִּפְנִים?
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **commanded** those present **and they expelled** Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan **from the study hall** as punishment. Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan **would write difficulties on a scrap of paper [*pitka*] and would throw** them **there** into the study hall. Those difficulties **that were resolved were resolved;** as for those **that were not resolved,** Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan **wrote resolutions** on a scrap of paper **and threw** them into the study hall. **Rabbi Yosei said to** the Sages: How is it that the **Torah,** embodied in the preeminent Torah scholars, **is outside and we are inside?**

He then gave the order and they were expelled from the *beit midrash*. Yet the *Gemara* makes clear that these were the greatest *talmidei chachamim* of the generation, and the intellectual life of the study hall could not simply exclude them. They remained outside but continued to participate: they would write their questions and challenges on slips of paper and pass them in. When the assembled scholars could answer, they answered; when they could not, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan would write the answer on a slip and send it in as well. The pattern was not subtle — the most searching questions and the most satisfying answers were consistently coming from outside the room.

### Reinstatement Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן רַבָּן [שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן] גַּמְלִיאֵל: נִיעַיְּילִינְהוּ, מִיהוּ נִיקְנְסִינְהוּ דְּלָא נֵימְרוּ שְׁמַעְתָּא מִשְּׁמַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said to them: Let us admit them** into the study hall. **But we will penalize them** in **that we will not cite *halakha* in their names.**

Rabbi Yosei observed the situation and addressed the assembled scholars directly: *Torah mi-bachutz ve-anu mi-bifnim* — the Torah is outside and we are inside. Either we go out to them or they come in to us. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel relented: bring them back in. But he was not without conditions — *mihu niknasinehu*: we will fine them, we will punish them. The form of the punishment was specific: their teachings would no longer be transmitted in their names. *De-la nemru shamata mi-shmaiyehu* — we will teach what they taught, but we will not say who said it.

### Anonymous Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַסִּיקוּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר אֲחֵרִים, וּלְרַבִּי נָתָן יֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** They cited** statements **of Rabbi Meir** in the name of ***Aḥerim*,** meaning: Others, **and** they cited statements **of Rabbi Natan** in the name of ***yesh omerim*,** meaning: Some say.

The anonymous attributions *acherim omrim* — "others say" — were assigned to Rabbi Meir, and *yesh omrim* — "some say" — to Rabbi Natan. This is the Talmud's own explanation for why those formulae appear as they do in the halachic literature. Their Torah would be preserved and perpetuated, but the honor of named attribution was withdrawn.

### Dream Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחְווֹ לְהוּ בְּחֶלְמַיְיהוּ: זִילוּ פַּיְּיסוּהוּ [לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל], רַבִּי נָתָן אֲזַל, רַבִּי מֵאִיר לָא אֲזַל, אֲמַר: דִּבְרֵי חֲלוֹמוֹת לֹא מַעֲלִין וְלֹא מוֹרִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan **were shown** a message **in their dreams: Go, appease Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rabbi Natan went. Rabbi Meir did not go. He said** in his heart: **Matters of dreams are insignificant.**

A heavenly message came to Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan in a dream: go and appease Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rabbi Natan went. Rabbi Meir did not: *divrei chalomot lo maalin ve-lo moridin* — dreams neither elevate nor diminish; they carry no halachic weight and he would not be moved by one.

### Father Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲזַל רַבִּי נָתָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: נְהִי דְּאַהֲנִי לָךְ קַמְרָא דַּאֲבוּךְ לְמֶהֱוֵי אַב בֵּית דִּין, שַׁוִּינָיךְ נָמֵי נָשִׂיא?!
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabbi Natan went, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said to him: Although the** ornate **belt,** i.e., the importance, **of your father was effective** in enabling you **to become deputy *Nasi*,** as Rabbi Natan's father was the Babylonian Exilarch, **will it render you *Nasi* as well?**

When Rabbi Natan came to make peace, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel received him with a pointed remark: the *kamara* — the golden belt, the distinguished lineage of your father — may have been sufficient to earn you the role of *av beit din*. But did you imagine it would make you the *nasi* as well? The reference to the father is telling. The *av beit din* and *chacham* positions were based on Torah merit; the *nasi*-ship was an inherited office, held by Rabban Gamliel's family across generations. It is striking that Rabban Gamliel invokes the father's honor even against Rabbi Natan, whose claim to his position was precisely through Torah knowledge rather than lineage.

### Rebbi Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרֵיהּ, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: אִילּוּ הָיָה תְּמוּרָה לֹא הָיָה קָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** Years later, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught Rabban Shimon his son** that ***Aḥerim* say: If it was** considered **a substitute,** **it would not be sacrificed.**

The story continues into the next generation. Rebbi — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi — was teaching his son Rabbi Shimon, and in the course of his teaching he cited a ruling attributed to *acherim*: *acherim omrim ilu haya temurah lo haya kareiv* — others say, if it were a *temurah* it would not have been offered. The ruling concerns *maaser behemah*: when one counts the newborn animals of the year and designates every tenth as *maaser*, an error in counting may result in the ninth, tenth, and eleventh all being declared the tenth. The question is whether the eleventh — called "ten" when a true tenth had already been designated — has the status of an inherently consecrated animal or whether it is a form of *temurah*, an exchange. Since the *halacha* is that *temurah* of *maaser* is not offered, and since the eleventh *is* offered as a *shelamim*, Rabbi Meir infers that its sanctity is not derived from *temurah* but is inherent. This was taught, of course, as *acherim omrim* — "others say."

### Conspiracy Inquiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מִי הֵם הַלָּלוּ שֶׁמֵּימֵיהֶם אָנוּ שׁוֹתִים וּשְׁמוֹתָם אֵין אָנוּ מַזְכִּירִים?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's son **said to him: Who are these** Sages **whose water we drink but whose names we do not mention?**

Rabbi Shimon asked his father: who are these people whose waters we drink but whose names we do not mention?

### Institutional Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁבִּקְּשׁוּ לַעֲקוֹר כְּבוֹדְךָ וּכְבוֹד בֵּית אָבִיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi **said to him:** They are **people who sought to abolish your honor and the honor of your father's house.**

Rebbi replied: these are the people who sought to uproot your honor and the honor of your father's house — *bikshu la'akor kvodcha u-kvod beit avicha*. They attempted a coup against the *nesiyut*, which is precisely a hereditary position, and that history justifies the suppression of their names.

### Challenge Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״גַּם אַהֲבָתָם גַּם שִׂנְאָתָם גַּם קִנְאָתָם כְּבָר אָבָדָה״!
>
> **Translation:** His son **said to him,** citing the verse: **"Their love as well as their hatred and their envy is long ago perished"** (Ecclesiastes 9:6): That was long ago and they have already died.

Rabbi Shimon pressed further: but their love, their hatred, their jealousy — *gam ahavatam gam sinatam gam kinatam kvar avda* — all of that is past. Why preserve the punishment?

### Swords Remain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הָאוֹיֵב תַּמּוּ חֳרָבוֹת לָנֶצַח״.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi's response was striking: **"The enemy are come to an end; the wasted places are forever"** (Psalms 9:7).

Rebbi's response was striking: even when the enemy ceases, *charavot lanetzach* — the swords remain. Whether this means the damage they could have caused still stands as a warning, or that the threat to institutional authority must always be answered clearly regardless of the ultimate outcome, the principle is that the consequences must endure.

### Reassessment of Success

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִלֵּי הֵיכָא דְּאַהֲנוֹ מַעֲשַׂיְיהוּ, רַבָּנַן לָא אַהֲנוֹ מַעֲשַׂיְיהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Shimon **said to** his father: **These matters** apply in a case **where their actions were effective.** In the case of these **Sages, their actions were not effective.**

Rabbi Shimon pushed back: that reasoning applies when the actions succeeded in causing harm, but *rabanan lo ahanu ma'aseihem* — their actions did not actually succeed. If the coup failed, why perpetuate the punishment?

### Partial Restoration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אַתְנִי לֵיהּ, אָמְרוּ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אִילּוּ הָיָה תְּמוּרָה לֹא הָיָה קָרֵב. אָמַר רָבָא: אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי דְּעִנְוְותָנָא הוּא (תְּנָא), ״אָמְרוּ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר״. ״אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר״ לָא אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi **then taught him:** The Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Meir: If it was** considered **a substitute, it would not be sacrificed. Rava said: Even Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who is humble, taught:** The Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Meir.** But **he did not say** directly: **Rabbi Meir said.**

Rebbi softened his position. Rather than continuing to attribute the ruling to *acherim* anonymously, he began to say *amru mishum Rabbi Meir* — it was said in the name of Rabbi Meir — without saying *Rabbi Meir said*. The formulation creates a slight but deliberate distance; the attribution is made secondhand rather than directly. Rava observes that even Rebbi, who was known for his humility, maintained this subtle distinction rather than fully restoring Rabbi Meir's name. The implication is that even a humble person, when occupying an office, must do certain things not for personal honor but to protect the dignity and stability of the institution itself — and so Rebbi held that line.

## Sinai vs Mountains

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פְּלִיגוּ בַּהּ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבָּנַן, חַד אָמַר: סִינַי עֲדִיף, וְחַד אָמַר: עוֹקֵר הָרִים עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis disagreed with regard to this** matter. **One said: Sinai,** i.e., one who is extremely knowledgeable, **is preferable; and one said:** One who **uproots mountains,** i.e., one who is extremely incisive, **is preferable.**

Rabbi Yochanan records a dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the *rabbanan*: which type of Torah scholar deserves precedence? *Chad amar Sinai adif* — one holds that the scholar who is like Sinai, the repository of received knowledge, all the texts and traditions, is superior. *Ve-chad amar oker harim adif* — the other holds that the one who can uproot mountains, who possesses overwhelming analytical power, is superior. The metaphor is rich: receiving Torah from atop the mountain versus being capable of uprooting the mountain itself. Content mastery versus dialectical strength — which quality, when they cannot both be found in one person, deserves the greater honor?

### Yosef vs Rabbah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף סִינַי, רַבָּה עוֹקֵר הָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef** was **Sinai; Rabba** was one who **uproots mountains.**

The practical exemplars were Rav Yosef and Rabbah. Rav Yosef was *Sinai* — the great repository of tradition, the master of received knowledge. Rabbah was *oker harim* — the towering analyst, the one who could penetrate and restructure an argument. The question of which of them deserved the superior position was a live one in their *beit midrash*.

### Sinai Preferred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לְתַמָּן: אֵיזֶה מֵהֶם קוֹדֵם? שְׁלַחוּ לְהוּ: סִינַי עֲדִיף, דְּאָמַר מָר: הַכֹּל צְרִיכִין לְמָרֵי חִטַּיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **They sent** a message from Babylonia **to there,** Eretz Yisrael: **Which takes precedence? They sent** in response: **Sinai is preferable, as the Master said: Everyone requires the owner of the wheat,** i.e., one who is expert in the sources.

They sent the question to *Eretz Yisrael* and received the reply: *Sinai adif* — the master of knowledge content is to be preferred. The reasoning given is *de-amar mar hakol tzerichin lemarei chitaya* — everyone needs the master of the wheat. The metaphor integrates seamlessly with the way Torah learning is described elsewhere: the word *garas* — to study — shares its root with *geresh karmel*, the grinding of grain. The received texts are the wheat; analysis is the grinding that turns wheat into flour and flour into bread. But you cannot grind without wheat. Everyone depends on the one who holds the raw material. In an oral tradition, before the age of printed books and accessible libraries of *mishnayot* and *beraitot*, possessing the received corpus was the precondition for any analysis whatsoever. You cannot choose the brilliant analyst if the texts are not in the room.

### Office Succession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא קַבֵּיל רַב יוֹסֵף עֲלֵיהּ. מְלַךְ רַבָּה עֶשְׂרִין וְתַרְתֵּי שְׁנִין, וַהֲדַר מְלַךְ רַב יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **And even so, Rav Yosef did not accept upon himself** the appointment of head of the yeshiva. **Rabba reigned for twenty-two years, and then Rav Yosef reigned.**

Notwithstanding this ruling — which clearly favored Rav Yosef — Rav Yosef refused to accept the office of *rosh yeshiva*. Rabbah therefore served for twenty-two years. Only after Rabbah's tenure did Rav Yosef take the position.

### Rav Yosef Deference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל שְׁנֵי דִּמְלַךְ רַבָּה, רַב יוֹסֵף אֲפִילּוּ אוּמָּנָא לְבֵיתֵיהּ לָא (חֲלֵיף).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that in **all those years that Rabba presided,** Rav Yosef **did not even call a bloodletter to his home.** Rav Yosef did not assume even the slightest air of authority, in deference to Rabba, and would go to seek out the bloodletter rather than expecting that the bloodletter would accommodate him.

During the entirety of Rabbah's leadership, Rav Yosef showed total deference. This stands in instructive contrast to the story of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Natan. Rav Yosef would not even summon a bloodletter — a doctor — to make a house call to his home; he would go to the doctor instead. House calls were made to important people. As long as Rabbah was *rosh yeshiva*, Rav Yosef's posture was that Rabbah was the important person, not himself.

## Study Group Leader

### Teaching Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא וְרַבִּי זֵירָא וְרַבָּה בַּר מַתְנָה הֲווֹ יָתְבִי וַהֲווֹ צְרִיכִי רֵישָׁא, אָמְרִי: כֹּל דְּאָמַר מִלְּתָא וְלָא מִפְּרִיךְ, לֶהֱוֵי רֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Abaye, Rava, Rabbi Zeira, and Rabba bar Mattana were sitting** and studying in a group **and were in need** of **a head** for their group. **They said: Let anyone who will say a matter** that **is not refuted be** the **head.**

A brief but charming episode closes the *masechet*. Abaye, Rava, Rabbi Zeira, and Rabbah bar Matna were sitting together and needed someone to lead the study group — based on the *Tosafot ha-Rosh*'s explanation, the session required someone to present the material at the head while the others engaged it. They agreed on a criterion: whoever could teach something that none of the others could disprove (*lo mifrach*) would be the one to lead.

### Abaye Prevails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלְּהוּ אִיפְּרִיךְ, דְּאַבָּיֵי לָא אִיפְּרִיךְ. חַזְיֵיהּ רַבָּה לְאַבָּיֵי דִּגְבַהּ רֵישֵׁאּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נַחְמָנִי, פְּתַח וְאֵימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Everyone's** statements **were refuted,** and the statement **of Abaye was not refuted. Rabba saw that Abaye raised his head,** i.e., he noticed that his statement was not refuted. Rabba **said to him: Naḥmani,** calling Abaye by his name rather than by his nickname, **begin and say** your lecture.

Everyone's teaching was challenged and overturned — except Abaye's. Rabbah, seeing Abaye's success, turned to him with enthusiasm: *Nachmani petach ve-eima* — Nachmani, open up and teach. This is Abaye's given name; he was apparently called *Abaye* by Rabbah partly to avoid using the name *Nachmani* directly, since that was also the name of Rabbah's father, Rabbah bar Nachman, and using it in address to a student might have felt uncomfortable. Some explain *Abaye* as a softened form of *Abba*. Others offer the reading that *Abaye* is an acronym for *asher becha yerucham yatom* — in You, God, the orphan finds consolation — reflecting the fact that Abaye was orphaned. In any case, Rabbah's response here is notably generous: rather than resentment at being outperformed by a student, he encourages Abaye to take the lead.

### Zeira vs Masna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי זֵירָא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב מַתְנָה הֵי מִנַּיְיהוּ עֲדִיף? רַבִּי זֵירָא חָרִיף וּמַקְשֶׁה, וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב מַתְנָה מָתוּן וּמַסֵּיק, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Between **Rabbi Zeira and Rabba bar Rav Mattana, which of them is preferable? Rabbi Zeira is incisive and raises** pertinent **difficulties, and Rabba bar Rav Mattana is moderate** and not so incisive, **but** ultimately **he draws** the appropriate **conclusions. What** is the conclusion? Which is preferable?

The *Gemara* then poses a further question that it cannot resolve: between Rabbi Zeira and Rabbah bar Matna, who is superior? Rabbi Zeira was *charif u-makshe* — sharp and incisive, the great questioner, the master of the penetrating challenge, the hair-splitting query. Rabbah bar Matna was *masun u-masik* — deliberate and conclusive, not the fastest thinker in the room, but the one whose slower, more careful reasoning produced sound and reliable rulings. Both qualities are essential, and both scholars embody them distinctly.

### Teyku Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּיקוּ. הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, וּסְלִיקָא לַהּ מַסֶּכֶת הוֹרָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved. 

*Teiku* — the question stands unresolved. It is an entirely fitting conclusion. The entire *masechet* has circled around questions of hierarchy, of who deserves precedence, of what qualities of Torah leadership merit greater honor. The *Gemara* ends by posing precisely that question in its most intimate form — between two study partners in a *beit midrash* — and declining to answer. Some questions about the relative worth of different modes of Torah engagement belong, it seems, in the category of those the Talmud leaves standing.

*Hadran alach Masechet Horayot ve-silka lach Masechet Horayot.*
