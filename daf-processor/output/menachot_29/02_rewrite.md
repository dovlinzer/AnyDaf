# Menachot 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Letter Recognition

### Mezuzah & Mukaf Gvil

Today's daf is 30, though we are picking up at the very top of 29b, on the *mishnah* that speaks about *parshiyot* of the *mezuzah* as indispensable — that even a single letter can invalidate it. The *mishnah*'s statement that "even one letter" matters prompts the *gemara* to specify what that means in practice, since it is taken for granted that a missing letter renders the *mezuzah* invalid. The discussion turns to particular requirements: for instance, the rule of *mukaf gvil*, that each letter must be surrounded by parchment and cannot be touching neighboring letters.

On the question of a hole in the middle of the *heh*: the *gemara* rules that this is *kasher*, because *mukaf gvil* concerns the outer perimeter of the letter, not a void within its body. The inner legs of the *heh* may be adjacent to a hole without that rendering the letter invalid.

### Hole in Hei, Kotzo

A separate question arises when part of a letter is broken — not merely adjacent to a hole, but where the ink itself does not form a complete line. The *gemara* rules that the leg of the letter must be at least the size of the smallest letter, which is the *yud*. There is debate among the *rishonim* about how this applies to the *heh* specifically: the Rosh holds that the requirement of appearing like a *yud* applies to the large outer leg, while the inner leg need only be as small as a tiny dot. Many other *rishonim*, however, require that even the inner leg meet the minimum size of a *yud* — calibrated, presumably, to the same font as the rest of the writing.

The *gemara* in general does not lay out detailed specifications for what each letter must look like; rather, the standard is that it must resemble the familiar form of the letter, as we shall see shortly with the test of a child. Nevertheless, there are other formal criteria beyond mere appearance — such as *mukaf gvil* and minimum stroke length — and these are independent of whether the letter looks recognizable. If one consults the *Mishnah Berurah* in the *Hilchot Tefillin* section — *siman* 36 — one finds a letter-by-letter guide to what each letter must look like; any trained *sofer* will elaborate even further. But the *gemara* itself provides relatively few of these specifics.

### Testing Broken Letters

At four lines from the top of 29b, we encounter the following case. Rami bar Chamri — who is the father-in-law of Rami bar Dikuli — found that the leg of the *vav* in the word *va-yeharog* (within one of the four *parshiyot* of *tefillin*) had been broken, forming a hole. He brought the question before Rabbi Zeira, who said: go and bring a child who is neither too clever nor too simple — average intelligence. The reasoning is precise: one does not want a child so intelligent that he deduces what the word must be from context rather than from the letter's appearance, but one also does not want a child so limited that he cannot recognize letters at all. If the child reads it as *va-yeharog*, it is *kasher*; if instead he reads it as two *yuds* — treating the *vav* as having shortened into a *yud* — then it is *pasul*, because the *vav* has lost its distinctive length.

This test applies specifically to questions of ambiguity in a letter's shape: is this long enough to be a *vav*, or is it so short as to be a *yud*? There are other categories of defect, however, that constitute clear formal invalidities regardless of appearance. If a letter violates *mukaf gvil* by touching a neighboring letter, no child's reading can cure that — the disqualification is structural, not visual. The child-test is reserved for cases where the formal structure is intact but the proportions are uncertain.

### Formal vs. Appearance

This distinction — between formal invalidities and appearance-based issues — is a central organizing principle of the entire discussion. Some defects are categorical: touching a neighboring letter, or a letter that was never properly surrounded by parchment. Others are a matter of degree: is the stroke long enough? Does the shape still read as the intended letter? For the latter category, the child's reading provides an objective standard.

### Shulchan Aruch Rules

The *Shulchan Aruch* codifies this precisely. If one of the letters is broken — and the *Rama* specifies this refers to letters with simple straight strokes, such as a *vav* or *zayin*, where the ink has snapped rather than formed a clean line — then one brings a child who is neither too clever nor too simple and who knows how to read. If he reads it correctly, it is *kasher*; if not, it is *pasul*. The *Shulchan Aruch* adds that one need not cover the surrounding letters to prevent the child from using context, though that is the common practice.

The *Rama* then introduces an important caveat: even if the child reads the letter correctly, if we ourselves can see that the letter's form does not meet the standard — that it is objectively not recognizable as the proper letter — it is *pasul* regardless of the child's reading. This creates an instructive tension: on one hand, we use the child as an objective observer of appearance; on the other hand, expert judgment can override that reading when we know the form has categorically failed. A child might guess correctly from proximity or elimination without actually recognizing the letter, and in such a case, his reading carries no weight.

### Hole Conditions

The *Shulchan Aruch* adds one more crucial qualification: the leniency regarding a hole or broken ink applies only when the letter was originally written *kasher* and the defect occurred afterward. If, however, there was a hole in the parchment at the moment of writing, the letter was never *mukaf gvil* to begin with — the end of the ink was always touching void rather than *klaf*. The same applies if the leg of the *kuf* was bent toward the edge of the parchment from the outset. In such cases, the letter is *pasul* from the moment it was written, and no subsequent reading by a child can repair that original deficiency. One must therefore verify not only the present state of the letter but also that it was properly formed at the moment of writing.

## God & Crowns, Rabbi Akiva

### Moshe's Vision

Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: when Moshe ascended to the heavens to receive the Torah, he found the Holy One, blessed be He, sitting and tying crowns to the letters. Before proceeding, it is worth identifying what these crowns are. As we shall see later in Rava's teaching, the letters *shin*, *ayin*, *tet*, *nun*, *zayin*, *gimmel*, and *tzadi* — the *shatnez get* — each receive three-pointed crowns. These are not the simple *tagin* that appear on many letters, but distinctive triple-pronged ornaments. The *Tosafot* provides an illustration: the three points spread over the top of the letter, with one rising higher at the center and the two flanking ones coming close by, though not quite as spread as one might imagine. Various styles of *ksav* handle these slightly differently, but all converge on the essential three-pronged form — referred to as *karnin*.

Moshe, seeing God occupied with this labor, asked: *Ribbono shel Olam*, who is holding back Your hand? The phrase is a curious one, since God's hand is not being held back; He is actively at work. The sense of the question is evidently: why are You still here adding these crowns? Give the Torah already! What is keeping You?

God answered: there is one person who will exist at the end of many generations, Akiva ben Yosef by name, who will in the future interpret on every single *kotz* — every little jot and tittle — *tilei tillin shel halachot*, mountains upon mountains of laws. That, God says, is what is keeping Me: I must put on these little crowns so that he will have the material to interpret.

### Crowns & Creativity

This is striking on reflection, and it raises an honest difficulty. Do we actually find Rabbi Akiva interpreting the *tagin* anywhere in the literature? In fact, we do not — he is far more associated with deriving laws from extra *alephs*, *yuds*, and the word *et*, not from the crowns themselves. One might even ask: if the crowns appear on a fixed and predictable set of letters — every *shin*, every *nun*, every *gimmel* — what room is there for creative interpretation? The placement is not arbitrary; it can be predicted entirely. And this is precisely why we do not find Rabbi Akiva explicitly doing it.

The deeper meaning of this *aggadata* seems to be about the nature of *Torah she-ba'al peh* and the power of human creativity within the tradition. To some degree, the crowns function as a metaphor: God builds into the Torah a kind of excess, an invitation, and human beings — Rabbi Akiva above all — accept that invitation and generate from it *tilei tillin shel halachot*, even when those derivations are not strictly *peshat*, even when they go beyond what was originally intended in the simple sense. That is the meaning of the partnership: we are not merely recipients of a fixed text but active participants in an ongoing interpretive process. The crowns represent the space that God leaves open for human creativity.

Rav Moshe Feinstein, in the introduction to his *Igros Moshe*, focuses on precisely this *gemara* in articulating the authority of a *posek*. If a *posek* approaches his responsibility with integrity — examining all the sources, reasoning carefully, seeking truth — then his ruling is by definition correct. This is the implication of *Torah she-ba'al peh*: what Rabbi Akiva says ultimately becomes God's Torah. Its authority derives from the fact that it is a genuine, honest development from what God communicated to Moshe. That is the relationship between the written Torah and its oral interpretation.

### Moshe in Class

God told Moshe: turn around. Moshe went and sat at the back of the eighth row in Rabbi Akiva's classroom. And he did not know what they were talking about. The material seemed entirely foreign to him; the *halacha* being discussed bore no resemblance to what he had received. *Tashash kocho* — he felt weak, despondent, as if his own Torah had become unrecognizable.

### Halacha L'Moshe

Then, when they arrived at one particular ruling, Rabbi Akiva's students asked him: *Rebbi*, where do you derive this from? And Rabbi Akiva answered: *halacha l'Moshe mi-Sinai* — it is a tradition going all the way back to Moshe at Sinai. At that moment, *nityashevah da'ato* — Moshe's mind settled, his composure was restored. He was satisfied, because even this unfamiliar development was ultimately attributed back to him.

One might press the question: why was it specifically Rabbi Akiva who received this capacity and not Moshe himself? One answer from the *Meshech Chochma* frames the difference in terms of what each figure represents within the tradition — Moshe as the embodiment of the direct divine command, Rabbi Akiva as the great architect of *gezeirot* and *takanot*, the rabbinic safeguards that protect against transgression. Whether or not that reading is entirely convincing, the larger point of the *aggadata* seems clear: if Moshe Rabbeinu himself were to sit in a *daf yomi shiur* and listen to the *gemara* being discussed, he might not recognize what was being said — because that is what *Torah she-ba'al peh* is. It grows, it develops, it generates from itself *kotzei kotzim shel halachos* that were not in the original. And yet in the end, it all traces itself back to the Torah that Moshe gave. The source is his; the development is ours. And that is the relationship. *Nityashevah da'ato* — he made his peace with it, and perhaps more.

The image of Moshe being told to *chazor le'achorecha* — turn around, go to the back — carries a resonance beyond the simple narrative. It echoes the moment in *Parashat Ki Tissa* when Moshe asked to see God's face and was told *ve-achorai tir'ah* — you will see My back, not My face. There too, the full picture is withheld; one gets only a glimpse of how God acts in history. Here, similarly, Moshe gets to see the future without fully inhabiting it. He steps, as it were, out of human history — and when God tells him to look behind, he must reintegrate himself into the flow of time to witness what will become of what he began.

### Martyrdom & Justice

Moshe then returned before God and said: *Ribbono shel Olam*, You have a person of such quality — why are You giving the Torah through me? God replied: *shtok* — silence. *Kach ala be-machshava lefanai* — this is what arose in My thought. This was My plan.

Moshe then asked: show me his Torah — and show me his reward. Again God said: turn around. And Moshe saw the Romans weighing out the flesh of Rabbi Akiva's martyred corpse in the marketplace, as butchers weigh meat. Moshe said before God: *zu Torah ve-zu secharah*? This is the Torah and this is its reward? God answered: *shtok. Kach ala be-machshava lefanai*. This too arose in My thought.

### Theodicy

The parallel phrasing is precise and deliberate. Both times — why Moshe and not Rabbi Akiva, and why Rabbi Akiva's martyrdom — God gives the identical answer: silence, for this was My plan. The juxtaposition is not accidental. Both questions resist resolution within the framework of human understanding, and the *gemara* offers no resolution. It simply says: silence.

This is an extraordinarily powerful moment in Rabbinic literature, and it deserves to be named as such. So much of *chazal*'s discourse operates within a framework of *sachar ve-onesh* — reward and punishment, cause and effect, sin and consequence. The destruction of the Temple is explained by this sin, this catastrophe by that transgression. And into that framework, this *gemara* introduces something genuinely different: an honest acknowledgment that some things simply do not fit. We do not understand. And sometimes the most truthful theological response is to say so, rather than to impose a scheme of divine justice that makes no sense.

One thoughtful observation raised in this discussion: there is a striking contrast within the *gemara* itself between the beginning and the end. At the outset, every *kotz*, every tiny ornament on every letter, is freighted with meaning — Rabbi Akiva can derive *tilei tillin shel halachot* from the smallest marking. God's Torah is saturated with significance at every point. Then, when we shift from Torah to history — from the world of letters to the world of human lives — the same God says only: *shtok*. Silence. I have no explanation. The very same figure who insisted that every *kotz* had something to teach offers nothing when confronted with the death of the man who could read those *kotzin*.

If there is a larger message, it may be something like this: human beings are given the license, indeed the mandate, to make meaning within the Torah — to interpret, to derive, to bring order. That is *Torah she-ba'al peh*. But the world itself, the way God moves in history, the question of why the righteous suffer — that remains beyond the reach of our interpretive tools. We can make sense of the text; we cannot always make sense of the world. And that, perhaps, is precisely why the partnership of *Torah she-ba'al peh* matters so much.

## Shatnez Get & Symbolism

### Shatnez Get Letters

Rava teaches: the seven letters require three *ziyunin* — three ornamental crowns. And these are the letters of the mnemonic *shatnez get*: *shin*, *ayin*, *tet*, *nun*, *zayin*, *gimmel*, *tzadi*. We have already seen these crowns in the context of the *aggadata* about Moshe and the *tagin*; now we address the *halacha* directly.

### Chet Bump

Rav Ashi adds: I have seen these crowns in the very precise *sifrei* of the academy of Rav — *sifrei dukni*, careful and meticulous manuscripts. In these scrolls, they made a bump — *chatri* — at the top of the *chet*. Rashi understands this bump as akin to the *kotzo shel yud*: a small raised point at the top left of the letter, suggesting that something rises upward. *Tosafot*, however, interprets *chatri* as a camel hump — and on the basis of another *gemara*, *Tosafot* understands the *chet* as inherently structured as two *zayinin* connected by a camel hump at the top. Looking at any sampling of careful *ksav*, one can see that if the hump were removed, the *chet* would indeed resolve into two separate *zayinin*. The keyword here is *sifrei dukni* — the most precise scrolls. Not every *sofer* wrote with the same level of care, and the presence of the bump distinguishes the more meticulous scribal tradition.

These precise *sifrei* also *talu kara'ei de-heh* — suspended the leg of the *heh*, meaning the inner left leg of the *heh* does not touch the roof of the letter. If it did, the *heh* would become a *chet*, and the letter's identity would be destroyed. The suspension is therefore both halakhically necessary and symbolically significant.

### Hei Leg & Teshuvah

Rav Yehuda Nesi'a asked Rav Ami: what is the meaning of the verse *bitchu va-Hashem adei ad, ki ve-kah Hashem tzur olamim* — trust in God forever, for in *Kah* God is the rock of the ages? Rav Ami initially understood this as a question about the simple meaning of the verse — *pshat* — and responded that one should put one's faith in God, who is a refuge in both this world and the world to come. But Rav Yehuda Nesi'a clarified: that was not his question. His question was: why does the verse say *be-kah* — *with* the letters *Kah* — rather than simply *Kah*?

The answer comes from a *derashah* of Rav Yehuda bar Rav Ilai: *Kah* consists of a *yud* and a *heh*, and the verse means to say that God fashioned the two worlds through these two letters of His name. The question then becomes: which world corresponds to which letter? This is resolved through a *derashah* on the word *be-hibaram* in the phrase *eileh toldot hashamayim ve-ha'aretz be-hibaram* — these are the generations of the heavens and the earth when they were created. The *heh* in *be-hibaram* is written small, and by reading it as *be-hey bera'am* — God created them with the letter *heh* — we learn that this world was created through the *heh*. The future world was created through the *yud*.

### Door Metaphor

Why was this world created with the *heh*? Because the *heh* resembles an *achsaderah* — a portico or colonnade. The *heh* has three walls and is open at the bottom on one side, like the entrance court of a house. The lower opening represents the ease with which one can exit this world: *she-kol ha-rotzeh latzet yetze* — whoever wishes to leave may leave. The door is wide open. If someone wishes to waste his portion in this world, to descend into *gehennom*, the exit is not blocked. That opening in the *heh* represents the unobstructed path downward.

Yet why is the inner leg of the *heh* suspended — detached from the roof above? Because *de-hadar bi-teshuvah, mayilei* — one who returns through *teshuvah* can sneak back in through that narrow back entrance. The suspended leg creates a small gap at the top, a back door of return. It is small; it requires effort; but it is there.

And why does one not simply re-enter through the same wide door through which one left? *De-lo mistaya milta* — because that path will not succeed. One cannot simply retrace the steps of one's fall and expect that the same environment, the same scenarios, will yield a different result. *Teshuvah* requires approaching one's life from a genuinely new direction, climbing up through a harder way. And that is precisely why *Reish Lakish* cites the verse: *im laleitzim hu yalitz ve-la'anavim yiten chen* — if one is a mocker, God mocks him; if one is humble and genuinely striving, *mesayi'in oto* — God assists him. The need for divine assistance in *teshuvah* is not incidental; it reflects the difficulty of genuinely starting anew rather than returning through the same broken opening.

### Crown as Rope

And why does the *heh* have a small *taga* — a little crown at the top? Rabbi Berachya answers in God's voice: if this person is returning through *teshuvah*, *ani kosher lo kesher* — I will tie him a knot. The image is of someone scaling the wall to reach that small back entrance at the top of the *heh*, with God reaching out and throwing a rope to help him climb. One variant reading replaces *kesher* (knot) with *keter* (crown): I will give him a crown — acknowledgment, honor, the reward of having made the return. Either reading circles back to the *aggadata* with which this section of the *gemara* began: God tying crowns to letters, now understood as God extending assistance and dignity to those who do *teshuvah*.

### Yud Future World

Why was the world to come created through the *yud*? Because *ha-tzadikim she-bo mu'atin* — the righteous who inhabit it are few. The *yud* is the smallest letter; it represents a small population. And why is the head of the *yud* bent? Because *ha-tzadikim she-bo kfufim rasheihem* — the righteous who dwell there bow their heads. They are humble. But Rashi adds a further nuance: even among the righteous of the world to come, some are greater and some are lesser — *kevodo shel tzaddik merubeh me-shel chaveiro* — and so each one, seeing another whose achievements surpass his own, bows his head in something closer to embarrassment than arrogance. One has made it to *olam haba*, but there is still reason for humility, still a recognition of how much more another accomplished. So the very smallness and the bent posture of the *yud* encode both the rarity of the truly righteous and the humility that characterizes them even in their reward.

## Sefer Torah Errors

### Two vs. Three Errors

Rav Yosef notes that Rav made two teachings that are each contradicted by a *braita*. The first: Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav that a *Sefer Torah* containing two errors in every column — *daf* here meaning column, *amud*, not the full page — may be corrected. It is still worth fixing. However, if it has three errors in every column, it must be placed in *genizah*. The *Sefer Torah* is so thoroughly defective that even correcting it is not worthwhile; it should be retired.

### Baraita Contradiction

Against this, a *braita* is cited that applies a more lenient standard: three errors per column may still be corrected, while four errors per column requires *genizah*. This directly contradicts Rav, who placed the threshold at two versus three.

### Perfect Column

The same *braita* continues: if the *Sefer Torah* contains even one perfect column — a single column without errors — that column is sufficient to save the entire rest of the scroll. Even a scroll riddled with mistakes need not be sent to *genizah* if it contains this one unblemished section. The *gemara* will of course probe what "perfect" means in this context.

One may note a resonance between this ruling and the discussion of *tzaddikim* in the world to come: even a person whose life is filled with things requiring correction, if there is one beautiful area — one *daf* that is whole — that may be what defines him. The analogy is apt, even if the *gemara* does not make it explicit.

### Majority Correctness

Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel, in the name of Rav, adds a crucial qualification: these lenient thresholds — three errors per column permissible, one perfect column saving the rest — all apply only *ve-hu de-khtiv rubya de-sifra shapir*, when the majority of the *Sefer* is written correctly. If most of the scroll is defective, neither the three-per-column leniency nor the saving power of one good column applies.

This requirement interacts in a somewhat puzzling way with the per-column thresholds: if there are errors in *every* column, how can the majority of the scroll be written correctly? The *Rosh* addresses this, though the precise reconciliation is subtle. The most natural reading seems to be that one needs *both* conditions simultaneously — the majority of the scroll must be error-free, and within the minority of problematic columns, the error count must not exceed three per column. This makes the overall standard considerably stricter than either condition alone.

### Three Errors Allowed

A further question is raised: what if the one "perfect" column that saves the rest itself has three errors? After all, three is the permissible threshold — such a column is correctable, not disqualifying. Rav Yosef rules: since it remained under the threshold of four, and it is correctable, it retains its capacity to save the rest of the scroll. We are thus stricter in one direction — requiring a majority of the scroll to be correct — while simultaneously more lenient in another: the "perfect" column need not be completely flawless, only below the level that would require *genizah*.

### Missing vs. Extra

The discussion now turns to the distinction between two types of error. The lenient standard — that errors beyond the threshold require *genizah* — applies specifically when letters are *missing*, because correcting a missing letter requires scraping away the entire word and rewriting it in smaller letters. That is a major and visible intervention. Extra letters, by contrast, are a different matter: *aval yoteirot leit lan bah* — extra letters are not treated the same way. Scraping away a single extra letter is a far less radical correction than scraping an entire word. *Tosafot* clarifies that this does not mean extra letters need not be corrected — they must be — but simply that the scale of correction is much less damaging, and therefore the *Sefer Torah* does not fall under the same strictures.

### Leopard Pattern

Why is the missing-letter case so much more serious? Rav Kahana explains: because it *michezi k'menumar* — it looks like a leopard. Multiple scrapings across many columns produce a spotted, mottled appearance on the parchment, rendering the scroll visually unfit. A *Sefer Torah* that has undergone extensive scraping across column after column no longer presents with the dignity appropriate to a sacred scroll, and for that reason it must be retired to *genizah* rather than patched repeatedly.

It is worth noting that modern *sofrim* work with inks and parchments of such quality that scraping is nearly invisible — a skilled *sofer* can correct a word and leave no trace that anything was ever written or erased there. The *gemara*'s concern with the *menumar* appearance may reflect the technical conditions of its time, when scraping left more visible marks. The principle, however, remains the operative one.

### Igra's Case

A practical case: Igra, the father-in-law of Rav Abba, had extra letters in his *sefer*. He came before Rav Abba, his son-in-law, who told him not to worry. The stringent ruling — that accumulated errors may require *genizah* — applies specifically to missing letters, for the reasons just stated. But in Igra's case, the errors were extra letters, and even if those extra letters appeared throughout the scroll, *leit lan bah* — it is not a serious problem. We allow for corrections, and the corrections will not leave the scroll looking like a leopard.

*Tosafot* addresses a misreading that apparently circulated: some people mistakenly understood this *gemara* to mean that extra letters need not be corrected at all. That is not the ruling. Extra letters must be corrected; the point is only that such corrections do not render the scroll unfit even when they are numerous, because the visual damage they leave is minimal compared to the scraping required when letters are missing.

## Ending Sefer Torah

### Mid-Column Ending

The second teaching of Rav that is apparently contradicted by a *braita* concerns how a *Sefer Torah* should end. A *sofer* writing a *Sefer Torah* must plan meticulously in advance: certain passages — the *Shirat HaYam*, *Ha'azinu* — must begin in particular positions within a column, and the final words *le-einei kol Yisrael* must land at the bottom of the final column. This requires careful calculation from the outset. But what if the *sofer* failed to plan adequately and arrives at *le-einei kol Yisrael* at the top of a column rather than the bottom?

Rav teaches: when a *sofer* comes to the end, he may conclude even in the middle of a column — *gomeir va'afilu be'emtza ha-daf*. The scroll may end wherever it ends; there is no requirement to reach the bottom.

### Sofrim Contradiction

Against this, a *braita* from Masechet Sofrim is cited. It teaches: a *sofer* writing a *Sefer Torah* who comes to the end may *not* conclude in the middle of a column as he would in a *chumash*. Rather, he must compress his lines — *mekatzeir* — gradually shortening each line so that the text descends to the bottom of the column, ending there with a decorative mark, *kotz v'reish*. This requires advance planning; if one ends up too far from the bottom, no amount of compression will allow the text to reach it.

### Chumashim vs. Sefer

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: when Rav said one may end in the middle of a column, he was referring to *chumashim* — codices. Before the printing press, a *chumash* was a parchment manuscript bound as a book rather than rolled as a scroll. Such a *chumash* was not used for public reading in *shul* and operated under different formal requirements. The *Rambam* holds that in the absence of a proper *Sefer Torah*, one may read publicly even from a handwritten *chumash* of this type — it carries *kedushah* and bears certain scribal obligations — but it is distinct from a *Sefer Torah* in its formal requirements. When writing a *chumash*, one may end wherever the text ends. When writing a *Sefer Torah*, one must reach the bottom of the column.

The *gemara* then refines what "chumash" means in this context. The *Sefer Torah* is divided into five books — the *chamishai chumshei Torah* — and when one *Sefer* of the five concludes, one may end in the middle of a column. In practice, we do exactly this: *Bereishit* ends wherever it ends, four blank lines separate it from *Shemot*, and so on for each of the five books. Each book may conclude in the middle of a column. The requirement to reach the bottom applies only to the very end of the entire *Sefer Torah*.

### Line vs. Column

Rav Yehoshua bar Abba says in the name of Rav Giddel in the name of Rav: *le-einei kol Yisrael* — the very last words of the Torah — may be written in the middle of the column, *be'emtza ha-daf*. This seems to directly restore the original reading of Rav's ruling. A debate between the *Rabbanan* and Rav Ashi follows: the *Rabbanan* say one may end even in the middle of a line; Rav Ashi says the ending must be in the middle of a line *davka* — meaning one may not end in the middle of a line and call that sufficient, but neither must one end at the close of a line.

### Final Ruling

The *gemara* resolves: Rav's statement that *le-einei kol Yisrael* may appear *be'emtza ha-daf* was actually a teaching about *be'emtza shita* — the middle of a *line*, not the middle of a *column*. The two must be distinguished. The *Sefer Torah* must end at the bottom of the column — that is mandatory, and the *sofer* must plan far enough in advance to achieve it. But within that bottom position, *le-einei kol Yisrael* need not align perfectly with the end of the line; it may conclude mid-line.

The *halacha* is ruled in accordance with Rav Ashi: *be'emtza shita davka*. The final words of the *Sefer Torah* must come at the bottom of the column, but they need not reach the end of the line. The aesthetic requirement is vertical — reaching the column's foot — not horizontal. What happens when the *sofer* realizes he cannot reach the bottom without resorting to unusually large letters, or cannot fit without compression that distorts the text, remains a discussion for a subsequent *shiur*.
