# Yevamot 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Fathers: Smiting

### Two Fathers Setup

The daf opens in the middle of a case involving a child who does not know which of two men is his father — a situation arising when his mother remarried after leaving her first husband. Whatever the precise circumstances, it is certain that his father is a *kohen*; the uncertainty is only as to which of the two men that is. The Gemara on 101a explores the legal consequences of this ambiguity across several domains, beginning with the laws of smiting and cursing a parent.

### Exemption Principle

If such a child strikes or curses either of the two potential fathers, he is exempt from liability. The reason is straightforward: since it is unknown which man is actually his father, one cannot say with certainty that the particular individual he is striking or cursing is the one to whom he bears the filial obligation. The act, taken in isolation, lacks the definitive quality that the prohibition requires.

### Simultaneous Acts

A *baraita* then distinguishes cases based on how the acts are performed. If he cursed both men simultaneously — *killel shneihem bebat achat* — or struck them both in one act, he is liable. This case is actually the more intuitive of the two: in a single act targeting both men, he is definitively striking his father, even if he cannot identify which of the two that is. The larger *chiddush* is the sequential case: if he cursed one and then went back and cursed the other, or struck one and then struck the other, he is also liable according to the first opinion.

### Doubtful Warning

The sequential ruling turns on the principle of *hatra'at safek shemah hatra'ah* — that a doubtful warning counts as a valid warning for purposes of criminal liability. The scenario unfolds as follows: before the first act, witnesses warn him that this man might be his father. He disregards the warning and proceeds. Before the second act, they warn him again. He again disregards and proceeds. At that point, having completed both acts, he is liable, even though neither individual warning was directed at a certain transgression. The principle holds that the cumulative effect of two doubtful warnings — each acknowledged and disregarded — is sufficient to render him liable for the act that definitively constitutes the violation.

It is worth being precise about what *hatra'at safek* means here. The doubt is not about what the person will do next — every warning involves some uncertainty about that. Rather, the doubt is whether, even if he proceeds, his act will constitute a definite violation. The first opinion holds that such doubtful warnings, when combined to cover both potential fathers, are sufficient.

### Yehuda's View

*Rabbi Yehuda* takes a more restrictive position: *bebat achat chayav, bezeh achar zeh patur* — if he performed both acts simultaneously he is liable, but if he acted sequentially he is exempt. According to Rabbi Yehuda, *hatra'at safek* does not suffice. A sequential warning — "do not strike that man, he might be your father" — targets a single act that, at the moment of its commission, remains doubtfully a violation. The warning cannot aggregate across two separate acts to create retroactive certainty.

### Baraita Debate

The Gemara then raises a *baraita* suggesting that Rabbi Yehuda takes an even more lenient position — that even the simultaneous case renders him exempt. This creates an apparent contradiction with the view just attributed to him. The Gemara resolves this by invoking *trei tannai aliba de'Rabbi Yehuda*: there is a tannaitic dispute about what Rabbi Yehuda's actual position is, with some authorities reporting that he exempts even the simultaneous case.

### God/Father Analogy

To explain the reasoning of the more lenient version of Rabbi Yehuda's position, the Gemara cites *Rabbi Chanina*, who derives it from a textual analogy. The Torah uses the term *berachah* — a euphemism for cursing — in two contexts: cursing God (*ish ish ki yekallel elohav*) and cursing one's father (*umkallel aviv ve'imo mot yumat*). Just as in the case of cursing God there is no ambiguity about the object of the curse — there is only one God, and no question of which divine being is being cursed — so too by cursing one's father, there must be no ambiguity about who the father is. This requirement of clarity applies not merely to avoiding dual targets, but to the very identification of who the father is. Where that identification is genuinely uncertain, even a simultaneous act targeting both candidates fails to satisfy the standard established by the analogy to cursing God.

### Smiting-Cursing Link

The Gemara notes that *hukshah haka'ah liklalah* — smiting is compared to cursing. Therefore, the same requirement of clarity that applies to cursing applies equally to smiting. If the act of cursing requires unambiguous identification of the father as its object, so does the act of striking. This connection ensures that the analogy drawn by Rabbi Chanina governs both prohibitions.

## Kohanim Mishmarot

### Kohanim Setup

The Gemara now turns to the consequences when both potential fathers are *kohanim*. The *mishnah* establishes that the child in such a case goes up and performs the *avodah* in whichever *mishmar* he appears in, but does not receive a portion of the *korbanot*. The *mishmarot* are the rotating priestly watches that serve in the *Beit HaMikdash* by week; each *mishmar* will refuse to give him a *korban* portion on the grounds that he may belong to the other *mishmar*.

### Work Without Pay

This immediately raises a question: *lama oleh* — why would he bother performing the *avodah* if he receives no portion? The Gemara offers one answer: he is not working for pay. He works because he wants the privilege and the *mitzvah* of performing the *avodah* in the *Beit HaMikdash*. That said, this answer does not fully address the force of the question, because the language of the *mishnah* — *oleh*, he goes up — sounds not merely permissive but compulsory. It sounds as though he is being compelled.

### Family Honor Concern

The Gemara therefore reads the term *oleh* as indicating forced participation: *be'al korcho*, he is made to go up against his will. The reasoning is attributed in a long chain — *Rav Acha bar Chanina* said that *Abaye* said that *Rav Assi* said that *Rabbi Yochanan* — to the concern of *pgam mishpacha*, family disgrace.

The dynamic works as follows. Both potential paternal families are *kohanim*. When it is their *mishmar*'s week to serve, each family faces the same concern: if this young man is not seen performing the *avodah* alongside the rest of the *mishmar*, observers may assume he belongs to that family but is disqualified — a *pasul kohen*. Neither family wants the public to associate them with an unfit member. Each family therefore insists that he perform the *avodah* during their week, lest his absence create suspicion that falls on them. The result is that he ends up performing *avodah* in both *mishmarot*, even though he receives no *korban* portion from either.

### Tosafot Alternative

*Tosafot* raises a pointed objection to this reading, noting — with characteristic understatement, *teima ktzat*, one might wonder a bit — that it is not the straightforward reading of the word *oleh*. If you focus on the language carefully, the more natural reading is not that he is forced to go up, but that he has the right to compel them to allow him to go up: *be'al korcham de-vnei mishmeret*. Even if the *mishmar* refuses to give him a *korban* portion, they cannot legitimately bar him from performing the *avodah* itself. He is entitled to insist on participation. This reading inverts the direction of the compulsion — it is not the families forcing him, but he insisting on his right against the families.

### Beit Av Problem

The Gemara then complicates the picture further. Even granting that both potential fathers are from the same *mishmar* — the same weekly rotation — this does not resolve his inability to receive a *korban* portion. Within each *mishmar*, there are six *batei av*, each of which serves on a different day of the week, with all of them serving together on *Shabbat*. The *korban* portions are distributed daily based on which *beit av* is serving that day. If he does not know which *beit av* his father belongs to, he faces the same problem at a smaller scale: he goes to one *beit av* and they push him away, saying his father may be from the other one serving on a different day.

### Rav Papa's Fix

*Rav Papa* resolves this by rereading the *mishnah*'s condition. It is not sufficient that both potential fathers are from the same *mishmar*; for him to actually receive a *korban* portion, they must be from the same *mishmar* and the same *beit av*. Only then — *notel chelek echad* — does he work on that specific day and receive a definite entitlement to his portion. This significantly narrows the scope of the *mishnah*'s ruling, but it makes the internal logic precise.

## Chalitzah Overview

### Chapter Transition

The daf now moves into the next *perek*, which turns from the theoretical discussions of the preceding chapter to the practical mechanics of *chalitzah* — the ceremony in which the *yavam* who declines to perform *yibum* removes his sandal, enabling the *yevamah* to go free. The *perek* begins with the *mishnah*: *mitzvat chalitzah bishloshad dayanim* — the *mitzvah* of *chalitzah* requires three judges.

### Three Judges Rule

The source for three judges is the Torah's requirement that she come *l'einei hazkeinim* — before the elders. The word *zekeinim* implies a *beit din*. The *mishnah* adds: *v'afilu shlosha hedyotot* — even three commoners suffice. They need not hold *semikhah* or be formally ordained; they need not be *mumchin*, experts in the technical sense.

The Gemara asks: if even *hedyotot* suffice, why does the *mishnah* use the term *dayanim* at all? The answer is: *de'beinan shlosha she'yode'im lehakrot ke'ein dayanim* — we require three people who are literate enough to lead the recitation of the verses, functioning in the role of judges even if not possessing their formal qualifications. The practical need is clear: during the *chalitzah* ceremony, the *yavam* and *yevamah* must recite specified Biblical verses. Since many people were not literate, the judges would recite the text and have them repeat it. Accordingly, the judges must themselves be able to read and recite — they must be capable of *lehakrot*, of leading the recitation. The title *dayanim* in the *mishnah* thus sets a functional bar of literacy rather than a technical bar of ordination, reflecting the role these three individuals are serving rather than any personal credential they must possess.

### Shoe Requirements

The *mishnah* then addresses the type of shoe used in the ceremony. The ideal instrument is a *sandal* — a hard leather base with straps — which *Rashi* describes as cooked leather fashioned to be rigid. If she performed *chalitzah* with a regular shoe (*minal*), it is valid. If she used a soft slipper or sock (*anpilya*), it is invalid, since such a covering does not qualify as a shoe even after the fact. As for the *sandal* specifically: *besandal sheyesh lo akeiv kasher* — a sandal with a heel is valid; *veshein lo akeiv psula* — one without a heel is invalid.

### Leg Location Rule

The *mishnah* further specifies that the location on the leg matters: *min ha'arkuva ulemata chalitzah kesheirah* — from the knee downward the *chalitzah* is valid; *min ha'arkuva ulema'alah chalitzah psula* — from the knee upward it is invalid. *Rashi* understands this as addressing a case where the man has a partially amputated leg. The shoe must be worn on a portion of the limb that falls below the knee. If what remains of his leg is only above the knee, and the shoe is placed on that portion, it does not qualify as a shoe in the relevant sense. The *chalitzah* must involve the removal of a shoe from a part of the body that functions as the lower leg or foot.

### Shoe Variations

The *mishnah* continues with a series of cases. If the sandal does not belong to the *yavam* — *shein shelo* — or if it is made of wood rather than leather, or if it is his left sandal worn on his right foot, the *chalitzah* is nonetheless valid after the fact. If the sandal is oversized but he can still walk in it, or if it is undersized but covers the majority of his foot, it is likewise valid. These cases represent situations where the shoe is not ideal but remains sufficient to constitute the shoe that the Torah requires.

### Literacy Requirement

The Gemara's discussion of why the *mishnah* calls the participants *dayanim* leads, as noted above, to the conclusion that the essential qualification is the ability to lead the recitation of verses. This literacy requirement distinguishes the role from a purely passive one: the three individuals are not merely witnesses but active participants who enable the *yavam* and *yevamah* to fulfill their verbal obligations within the ceremony. The Gemara confirms this by reading the *mishnah*'s use of *dayanim* as shorthand for those who are *ke'ein dayanim* — functioning in the manner of judges — which carries with it this minimal literacy expectation.

## Three vs Five Debate

### Three Judges View

A *baraita* is then cited that articulates the standard position more explicitly: *mitzvat chalitzah bishloshad sheyodim lehakrot ke'ein dayanim* — the *mitzvah* of *chalitzah* requires three who know how to lead the recitation, functioning like judges. The *baraita* presents this as the normative rule, understood as a *lechatchila* standard that does not demand formally ordained judges.

### Five Judges View

*Rabbi Yehuda* dissents: *bachamisha* — five judges are required. This is a notably uncommon requirement; while the first *perek* of *Sanhedrin* does discuss courts of various sizes, a *beit din* of five is not a common or familiar institution.

### Word Count Analysis

The *baraita* derives the required numbers through a counting of the word *zekenim* in the relevant *pesukim*. *Zekenim* — elders — appears in the plural, indicating at minimum two. *Zekenim* without the definite article implies two; the additional occurrence of *ziknei* adds another two, bringing the total to four. Since a *beit din* cannot have an even number of judges — *ein beit din shakul* — one more is added.

### Odd Numbers Rule

The prohibition against an even-numbered court underlies the logic of both positions. The *tanna kamma* counts two occurrences of *zekenim* yielding two, adds one to avoid an even number, and arrives at three. *Rabbi Yehuda* counts additional occurrences yielding four, adds one to avoid an even number, and arrives at five. In both cases, the derivation is a counting exercise rather than a direct invocation of *beit din* as a formal legal institution — a point that carries significant implications, as we will see.

## Textual Derivations

### Zekenim Counts

The Gemara now examines what each *tanna* does with the words left over once the numerical derivation is complete. Since both positions use some occurrences of *zekenim* to count up their required number, the question arises: what does each position do with the remaining textual details? This produces a set of independent *derashot* from the *pesukim* of *chalitzah*.

### Hedyotot Allowed

According to the *tanna kamma*, one occurrence of *zekenim* is used not for counting but as a source permitting non-expert judges: it broadens the class of eligible participants to include *hedyotot*. Even though *zekenim* implies something like a *beit din*, the additional usage signals that the category is wider than formally ordained judges.

### Yisrael Derivation

*Rabbi Yehuda* derives the permission to use non-experts from a different source: the word *b'Yisrael* in the phrase *veyikarei shemo b'Yisrael*. Since it says "among Israel" without further qualification, this includes any member of Israel — even one without *semikhah*. For Rabbi Yehuda, the *zekenim* occurrences are thus entirely consumed by the numerical derivation, and the permission for *hedyotot* must come from elsewhere.

### Sight Requirement

The Gemara then addresses the requirement that the judges have sight. This is derived from the word *l'einei* — "before their eyes" — in the phrase *l'einei hazkenim*: *prat lesumim*, this excludes blind judges. The Gemara adds an important inference: the very fact that the Torah found it necessary to exclude the blind implies that these judges are not required to be *semuchim*. If they were required to be formally ordained members of a *sanhedrin*, blind individuals would already be excluded by the general rules governing ordained courts — the special exclusion in *chalitzah* would be superfluous. The necessity of the exclusion thus demonstrates that *chalitzah* does not require ordained judges, who are already disqualified from being blind.

### Direct Witness Rule

*Rava* offers an additional and more specific interpretation of *l'einei*: *tzrichei dayani lemichzi roka dekanfik mipuma deyevama* — the judges must actually see the spit exit from the mouth of the *yevamah*. This goes beyond merely possessing the faculty of sight; it demands that the judges directly witness the act of spitting at the moment it occurs. The source is the verse *veyorka befanav* — "and she shall spit before him" — which is governed by the earlier phrase *l'einei hazkenim*, meaning the *zekenim* themselves must witness the spitting as it happens.

This creates a textual accounting problem: if *l'einei* is used to teach Rava's requirement of directly witnessing the spit, it cannot simultaneously serve as the source for excluding blind judges. And if *l'einei* teaches the exclusion of the blind, where does Rava's requirement come from? The Gemara works through this tension carefully. The resolution is that *Rabbi Yehuda* uses *l'einei* exclusively for Rava's requirement — the judges must witness the spit — and derives the permission for *hedyotot* instead from *b'Yisrael*, as noted above. The *tanna kamma*, by contrast, uses one occurrence of *zekenim* to permit *hedyotot* and relies on *l'einei* for Rava's requirement as well, with the exclusion of the blind following as a natural implication.

### Non-Convert Requirement

Another derivation from *b'Yisrael*: *b'Beis Din shel Yisrael velo b'Beis Din shel geirim* — the *beit din* for *chalitzah* must consist of native-born Jews, not converts. This is taught in a *braita* attributed to *Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda*, whose ruling will become practically relevant later in the daf.

### Proclamation Duty

Finally, the phrase *veyikarei shemo b'Yisrael beis chalutz hana'al* — "and his name shall be called in Israel, the house of the one whose sandal was removed" — is read not merely as a future description but as an active directive. *Rabbi Tarfon* is cited as an example: when a *yevamah* came to perform *chalitzah* before him and those assembled with him, he instructed everyone present, *enu kulchem chalutz hana'al* — all of you should respond and say "the one whose sandal was removed." This proclamation is part of the role of those constituting the *beit din*: they represent *Yisrael* in publicly calling him by this designation. The verse is thus not merely a passive prediction but a mandate for active declaration.

## Halakhic Ruling

### Three Judges Ruling

*Rava* quotes *Rav Nachman*: the *halakhah* is *chalitzah bishloshad* — three judges. The basis for this ruling is that the *mishnah* in our tractate, which is a *stam mishna*, reflects the view of the *tanna kamma*. When a *stam mishna* exists, we follow it as the established *halakhah*.

### Mi'un Comparison

*Rava* then presses *Rav Nachman* with a question: if we rule three for *chalitzah* on the basis of a *stam mishna*, should we not likewise rule three for *mi'un* — the procedure by which a minor girl refuses a marriage arranged by her mother or brothers? There is a *stam mishna* in *Sanhedrin* that lists both *mi'un* and *chalitzah* together as requiring three. On the same logic, *mi'un* should also require three.

### Mi'un Debate

This is complicated by a *baraita* concerning *mi'un*. *Beit Shammai* holds that *mi'un* requires a *beit din* of *mumchin* — experts. The reasoning is intuitive: *mi'un* effectively dissolves what is treated as a marriage, and dissolving a marriage might be thought to require a court of greater authority. *Beit Hillel* disagrees: *mi'un* may be performed with or without a formal *beit din*. Yet both *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* agree — *v'eilu v'eilu modim she'tzrichim shlosha* — that three are still required. Thus the *baraita* seems to confirm three.

### Mi'un: Two Judges

Nevertheless, a further teaching — *Rav Yosef bar Minyumi* said in the name of *Rav Nachman* — rules that the *halakhah* follows the pair of *Rabbi Yehuda ben Yehuda* and *Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yosi*, who hold that even two suffice for *mi'un*. This appears to contradict the *stam mishna* pairing *mi'un* with *chalitzah* at three.

### Source Count Method

The Gemara attempts to justify this asymmetry through a counting of *stam* sources. For *chalitzah*, there are two independent *stam* sources requiring three: the *mishnah* in our tractate and the *mishnah* in *Sanhedrin*. For *mi'un*, there is only one — the *mishnah* in *Sanhedrin*. Therefore, the ruling for *chalitzah* is more firmly established. The Gemara, however, immediately pushes back: since when do we count *stam mishnayot* and give greater weight to two over one? A single *stam mishna* should suffice to establish the *halakhah*. Having two does not logically add force.

The Gemara then proposes a further count: even for *mi'un* there are two *stam* sources — the *mishnah* in *Sanhedrin* and a separate *mishnah* that discusses whether a judge who witnessed a *mi'un* may subsequently marry the woman, ruling that he may because he acted *b'Beis Din*, implying a court of three. For *chalitzah*, one might then count three *stam* sources. But again, the Gemara objects: why should three count for more than two? The differential between two and three sources does not explain why one *halakhah* overrides its *stam* and the other does not.

### Yehuda Concedes

The Gemara ultimately resolves the matter through a different argument. *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* explains: the reason we are confident that *chalitzah* is definitively three is because the *stam* ruling follows a *machloket* in which one party — *Rabbi Yehuda* — implicitly conceded.

The evidence comes from the *mishnah* in *Sanhedrin*. That *mishnah* lists *semichat zekenim* and *egla arufa* as requiring three, and attributes the alternative view of five to *Rabbi Yehuda*. The same *mishnah* then continues and rules that *chalitzah* and *mi'un* require three — and notably, *Rabbi Yehuda does not dispute this*. Since Rabbi Yehuda explicitly argued for five by *chalitzah* in the *baraita* we quoted earlier, his silence in the *mishnah* in *Sanhedrin* indicates that he retracted his position — *hadar bei Rabbi Yehuda*. He abandoned the view of five specifically with respect to *chalitzah*. This concession by the dissenting voice makes the ruling of three especially solid. No such concession exists for *mi'un*, which is why that ruling remains more open to challenge.

## Chalitzah Procedures

### Location Designation

*Rava* introduces a procedural requirement: *tzrichei dayani l'mikva duchtah* — the judges must designate a specific location for the *chalitzah* proceeding. They cannot conduct it in an ad hoc manner, convening wherever they happen to be. The source is the verse *v'altah yevamto hasha'arah el hazkeinim* — "and his sister-in-law shall go up to the gate, to the elders." The *sha'ar ha'ir*, the city gate, was historically where elders would sit in judgment. Even in times when there is no such physical gate serving that function, the requirement that she "come to" the judges implies that the judges must have a designated and established place.

### Five Judges Practice

The Gemara then brings examples of Amoraim who, in practice, assembled five judges for a *chalitzah*. *Avtolas* and *Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua* performed a *chalitzah* with five. The Gemara asks: like whom? Is this following *Rabbi Yehuda*? But we established that Rabbi Yehuda himself retracted his position. How can they be acting on a view he abandoned?

### Five for Publicity

The Gemara answers: *l'firsumei milta* — they assembled five not because of a technical halakhic requirement but in order to publicize the matter. The *chalitzah* has real consequences for the woman's status: a *chalutza* may not marry a *kohen*, and potential suitors should know she is now available. Assembling five rather than three serves the purpose of making the proceedings more public and well-known — a larger gathering ensures broader awareness. This is reinforced by the verse *veyikarei shemo b'Yisrael*: there is a built-in publicizing function to the ceremony. *Rashi* notes both dimensions: ensuring a *kohen* does not inadvertently marry her, and ensuring that eligible men know she is now free to remarry.

### Publicity Examples

*Rav Ashi* once arrived at the *yeshiva* of *Rav Kahana*. Rav Kahana said to him: *salik mar l'gaban l'miluyei bei chamisha* — master, come join us to complete our number of five. Here too the purpose was *pirsumei milsa*: Rav Kahana wanted five specifically to publicize the *chalitzah* proceeding, not because the law demanded it.

### Yehuda's Explicit

In a separate account, *Rav Kahana* recalled standing before *Rav Yehuda*, who told him to come stand on a particular woodpile — *sak l'tzikli d'kani* — to be counted among five, for the purpose of *l'firsumei milta*. This detail about the woodpile is also relevant to the *duchta* requirement: the "designated place" need not be a permanent or formal courtroom. Designating any specific spot — even a pile of wood — appears to satisfy the requirement. The designation itself is what matters, not the permanence or formality of the location. When those around him asked *Rav Yehuda* why he needed five, he replied explicitly: *ki heichi d'lifarsim milsa* — to publicize the matter. His reason was entirely one of publicity and not of legal necessity.

## Ger Status Issue

### Ger Exclusion

The Gemara records that *Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda* was in the presence of *Rav Yehuda* when the same invitation was extended — come stand on the woodpile and be counted among five, for the purpose of publicizing the *chalitzah*. Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda declined, citing the rule that he had studied: *b'Beis Din shel Yisrael velo b'Beis Din shel geirim* — the *beit din* for *chalitzah* must consist of native-born Jews, not converts.

### Textual Source

His refusal was grounded in the *pasuk*: *veyikarei shemo b'Yisrael* — the phrase *b'Yisrael* is read to require a *beit din shel Yisrael*, excluding a court composed of *geirim*. Since he himself was a *ger*, he could not participate. *V'ana geira ana* — and I am a convert. The *braita* that Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda himself taught had excluded *geirim* from the *beit din* of *chalitzah*, and he applied it scrupulously to himself.

### Praised Stringency

*Rav Yehuda* responded with high praise: *k'gon Shmuel bar Yehuda* — someone like Shmuel bar Yehuda, who is so meticulous and *frum* that he disqualifies himself even when invited to participate in an honor — such a person I would trust so implicitly that I would treat his testimony as determinative enough to extract money. In other words, Rav Yehuda held Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda's integrity and scrupulousness in such esteem that he considered his word effectively equivalent to full testimonial proof.

### Witness Reliability

The Gemara does not accept Rav Yehuda's hyperbole at face value. *Mafkina salka deitach?* — would you really extract money on his testimony alone? The Torah requires two witnesses: *hasvei shnei eidim amar Rachmana*. The Gemara therefore reinterprets: *mar'ana shtara apumei* — rather, what Rav Yehuda meant is that if Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda testified that a particular document was forged or invalid, Rav Yehuda would trust him sufficiently to disqualify the document on that basis. That is a narrower but still meaningful expression of trust.

### Ger Bar Remains

The final observation the Gemara draws from this exchange is striking. Recall that the assembled group already had its three valid judges — the technical requirement of *chalitzah* was already satisfied. Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda was being invited only to complete the number to five, which served merely the purpose of *pirsumei milta*. Yet he still declined. Even within the non-technical, publicity-oriented framework of adding a fourth or fifth person, he held that the *din* of *b'Yisrael* — which bars converts — applied. The conceptual framework of "five for publicity" did not operate in a legal vacuum; it was still understood as constituting a *beit din*, and a *beit din* requires non-*ger* status even for its non-essential members. The daf closes on this note, with the promise that tomorrow's discussion will continue exploring the role of a *ger* on a *beit din*.
