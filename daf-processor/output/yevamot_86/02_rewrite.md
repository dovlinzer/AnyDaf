# Yevamot 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Status of Women

### Women & Terumah

The mishnah on this amud opens with the question of who is entitled to eat *terumah*, and more specifically, which women may eat it by virtue of their spousal connection to a *kohen*. The mishnah establishes that a *bat Yisrael* who is merely betrothed (*me'ureset*) to a *kohen*, or who is pregnant by a *kohen* but not yet married, or who is a *shomeret yavam* awaiting *yibbum* with a *kohen* — in all these cases, she does not eat *terumah*. In each scenario, her connection to the *kohen* is real but still contingent: the marriage has not been fully concluded, the *yibbum* has not yet taken place, or she has conceived but not yet given birth to a living child. That uncertainty is sufficient to withhold the privilege of *terumah*.

The same rule, surprisingly, applies in the reverse direction: a *bat kohen* who is connected to a *Yisrael* — through betrothal, pregnancy, or *yibbum* — also may not eat *terumah*. This is somewhat counterintuitive. One might have assumed that since these tenuous connections are insufficient to grant a *bat Yisrael* access to *terumah*, they should equally be insufficient to strip a *bat kohen* of it. Yet the mishnah rules that any connection whatsoever to a *Yisrael* removes her from the category of those permitted to eat *terumah*.

### Safek Principle

The underlying logic, it seems, is the principle of *safek d'orayta l'chumra* — when there is doubt in a matter of Torah law, we rule stringently. For the *bat Yisrael*, the uncertainty is whether she will ultimately complete her connection to the *kohen* and thereby become entitled to eat *terumah*; since we are not yet certain, she does not eat. For the *bat kohen*, the uncertainty runs in the opposite direction: perhaps her connection to the *Yisrael* will be fully realized, and it will emerge that she ought not to have been eating *terumah*. We therefore forbid her in advance. In both cases, the doubt drives the stringent outcome.

### Women & Ma'aser

The same rules that govern *terumah* are applied by the mishnah to *ma'aser* as well. A *bat Yisrael* who is betrothed to, pregnant by, or a *shomeret yavam* of a *Levi* does not eat *ma'aser*. The same logic prevails: parallel to the rules of *terumah* for *kohanim*, the rules of *ma'aser* apply to *Levi'im*, and the same range of incomplete connections to a *Levi* is insufficient to grant access.

### Bat Kohen/Levi

The mishnah then states that a *bat Levi* connected to a *kohen* — and a *bat kohen* connected to a *Levi* — eats neither *terumah* nor *ma'aser*. This is one of the more unusual *halachot* in the mishnah. One can readily understand why a *bat Levi* betrothed to a *kohen* should not eat *terumah* — she is not yet a full member of the *kohen*'s household. But why should a *bat kohen* connected to a *Levi* be forbidden from eating *ma'aser*? As will emerge later in the *sugya*, a *kohen* is himself a species of *Levi*; if so, the *bat kohen* is already associated with the tribe that is entitled to *ma'aser*. The mishnah seems to be anticipating the possibility that she will fully become part of the *Levi*'s household, and we therefore restrict her in the interim — just as we restrict the *bat Levi* who might become part of the *kohen*'s household. The important underlying question of whether *zarim* — non-*Levi'im* — can eat *ma'aser rishon* at all will occupy the *gemara*'s attention in the next section.

## R. Meir & Zarim

### Non-Jews & Ma'aser

The *gemara* now raises an immediate difficulty with the mishnah. When a *bat Yisrael* marries a *Levi*, why should she be forbidden to eat *ma'aser* while she is only betrothed? After all, even a *zarah* — a complete outsider — can eat *ma'aser*; why would someone partially connected to a *Levi* be more restricted? The *gemara* answers, in the name of Rav Nachman citing Shmuel, that the mishnah follows the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who holds that *ma'aser rishon* is forbidden to *zarim*.

### Rabbi Meir's Source

This position is grounded in a *baraita*: "*terumah* goes to the *kohen* and *ma'aser rishon* goes to the *Levi* — these are the words of Rabbi Meir." The question arises: in what sense does the *baraita* imply that *zarim* are forbidden from eating *ma'aser rishon*? Rashi explains that there is a *hekesh* — a linguistic parallel — between *terumah* and *ma'aser*, drawn from the verse in the context of *ma'aser*: *ki et ma'asar Bnei Yisrael asher yarimu la-Hashem terumah* — the Torah applies the term *terumah* to *ma'aser*. Just as *terumah* is forbidden to *zarim*, so too *ma'aser rishon* is forbidden to *zarim*. This derivation is attributed in the *gemara* with the phrase *mishmeih d'Gemara* — transmitted in the name of the tradition rather than in the name of an identified individual. Rashi notes that this phrase indicates an unbroken chain of transmission whose links are not enumerated, and that it is used to assure us that the *amora* is not generating this interpretation himself but rather reporting a received tradition traced back to Rabbi Meir.

### R. Elazar Permits

The *baraita* continues: "Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah permits [it] to the *kohen*." The *gemara* notes that the word *matirah* — he permits — implies that there is someone who forbids. The *gemara* initially finds this strange: is there really any authority who would say that a *kohen* cannot eat *ma'aser*? The *gemara* points out that this is indeed Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's position in contrast to Rabbi Meir, yet it is perplexing because universally *kohanim* are themselves *Levi'im* — so on what basis would anyone restrict them?

### Gemara's Reworking

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by reinterpreting the *baraita*. Rather than reading *matirah l'kohen* as "he permits [the *kohen* to eat] *ma'aser*," the *gemara* proposes reading it as *notnah af l'kohen* — one may give *ma'aser* even to a *kohen*. In other words, the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah is not about whether a *kohen* is permitted to consume *ma'aser*, but rather about to whom one may hand it over in the first instance: Rabbi Meir holds that *ma'aser rishon* is given specifically to the *Levi*, while Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah holds that one may give it even to a *kohen* directly. This reinterpretation will also become significant later in the *sugya*.

## Ma'aser as Tevul

### Linguistic Parallel

Having established Rabbi Meir's position, the *gemara* inquires further: what exactly is the basis for applying the laws of *terumah* to *ma'aser rishon* via the *hekesh* in the verse? The verse *ki et ma'asar Bnei Yisrael asher yarimu la-Hashem terumah* uses the term *terumah* in connection with *ma'aser*, and from this Rabbi Meir derives that just as *terumah* is forbidden to *zarim*, so too *ma'aser rishon* is forbidden to *zarim* — *mah terumah asurah l'zarim af ma'aser rishon asur l'zarim*.

One might then push further: since *terumah* carries penalties of *mitah b'yedei shamayim* for intentional consumption by a *zar*, and *chomesh* — payment of principal plus a fifth — for inadvertent consumption, perhaps *ma'aser rishon* similarly carries these penalties. If the *hekesh* is broad, perhaps the full penal structure of *terumah* applies.

### Penalties Applied

The *gemara* raises this possibility directly: just as one who eats *terumah* illicitly incurs *mitah* and pays *chomesh*, so perhaps one who eats *ma'aser rishon* illicitly also incurs *mitah* and *chomesh*. The Torah, however, confines these penalties with the words *bo v'lo b'ma'aser* — "for *it*," meaning for *terumah*, but not for *ma'aser* — and *alav v'lo al ma'aser* — the *chomesh* applies to *terumah* specifically, not to *ma'aser rishon*. Thus, while *ma'aser rishon* shares with *terumah* the quality of being forbidden to *zarim*, it does not carry the same severe penalties.

### Alternative View

The *gemara* then considers the position of the *rabanan* — those who disagree with Rabbi Meir and permit *zarim* to eat *ma'aser rishon*. According to them, the *hekesh* between *terumah* and *ma'aser* does not teach that *zarim* are forbidden from eating *ma'aser rishon*. Instead it teaches something different: just as *terumah* renders produce *tovel* — untithed produce that is forbidden to be eaten until the *terumah* has been properly separated — so too *ma'aser rishon* renders produce *tovel* when it has not yet been separated. In other words, the *hekesh* from *terumah* teaches the *tovelet* status of *ma'aser rishon*, not its prohibition upon *zarim*.

## R. Yossi's Analysis

### R. Yossi's Baraita

A *baraita* of Rabbi Yossi is introduced at this point. Rabbi Yossi addresses the question of whether the severe penalty of *mitah b'yedei shamayim* applies only to produce from which no tithes whatsoever have been taken, or whether it extends even to produce from which some tithes have been taken but others remain. He poses the problem as follows: one might have thought that the *mitah* penalty applies only to *tevel* in its purest form — produce from which no separation at all has been made. But what if *terumah gedolah* has been taken but *ma'aser rishon* has not? Or if *ma'aser rishon* has been taken but *ma'aser sheni* has not? And further still, how do we know that even the failure to separate *ma'aser ani* leaves the produce with the status of *tevel* that triggers the death penalty?

### Ma'aser Ani Restriction

To extend the prohibition all the way down to *ma'aser ani*, Rabbi Yossi invokes the verse *lo tukhal le'ekhol bi-sh'arekha* — "you cannot eat it in your gates." He then draws a *gezerah shavah* from a parallel usage of *sh'arekha* in a verse that speaks of *ma'aser ani* — *v'akhlu bi-sh'arekha v'sav'u*, "and they shall eat in your gates and be satisfied." Since the word *sh'arekha* appears in both contexts, we learn that the prohibition of *lo tukhal le'ekhol bi-sh'arekha* extends to *ma'aser ani* as well. The produce remains *tevel* and is restricted so long as the *ma'aser ani* has not been properly set aside.

One might note that this appears in tension with the well-known rules of *masechet Demai*, which treats produce of uncertain tithing status leniently in most cases. The suggestion is that the obligations discussed in these *baraitot* operate at the biblical level for produce that is certainly *tevel*, while the rabbinic dispensations of *Demai* apply specifically where there is doubt whether the second and third tithes have been separated — a *chumra d'rabanan* that does not carry the biblical penalties.

### Death Penalty Source

The *gemara* then clarifies the interaction between Rabbi Yossi's derivation and Rabbi Meir's. If we had heard only from Rabbi Yossi's *pasuk*, we might have thought that the prohibition is only a *lav* — a negative commandment — but not one that carries the *mitah* penalty. The *pasuk* of Rabbi Meir — which creates the *hekesh* between *terumah* and *ma'aser rishon* and applies the term *terumah* to *ma'aser* — is therefore necessary to establish that the prohibition of *tevel* in its various stages carries the severity of *mitah* and not merely a *lav*. A *lishna achrina* — an alternative manuscript formulation — reverses the role of the two sources while reaching the same conclusion: *ma'aser rishon* acquires its *tovel* status from Rabbi Yossi's derivation, and from Rabbi Meir's *hekesh* we learn that the attendant prohibition reaches the level of *mitah*.

## Mishnah Problem

### Apparent Contradiction

The *gemara* now returns to the mishnah with a sharp challenge. Having explained that the mishnah follows Rabbi Meir — who holds that *ma'aser rishon* is forbidden to *zarim* — the *gemara* invites us to examine the latter clause of the mishnah: a *bat Levi* betrothed to a *kohen* and a *bat kohen* betrothed to a *Levi* eat neither *terumah* nor *ma'aser*. The problem here is glaring. The explanation that the mishnah follows Rabbi Meir was invoked to explain why a *bat Yisrael* betrothed to a *Levi* does not eat *ma'aser* — because Rabbi Meir forbids *zarim* from eating *ma'aser*, and she has the status of a *zarah*. But a *bat kohen* is herself a kind of *Levi'it*; why should she be forbidden from *ma'aser* when she is connected to a *Levi*? And symmetrically, a *bat Levi* connected to a *kohen* — why should she not eat *ma'aser* from the *Levi* side of her identity? The mishnah forbids both, and the explanation that we are following Rabbi Meir's view on *zarim* does not resolve this difficulty at all.

### Rav Sheshet's View

Rav Sheshet proposes a creative reinterpretation of the mishnah. When the mishnah says she does not eat (*eina ochelet*), he argues, it does not mean she is forbidden from eating *ma'aser* at all. Rather, it means she cannot authorize others to separate or collect *ma'aser* on her behalf — *eina notenet reshut litrom*. Normally, a woman's *shaliach* — her appointed agent — can go to the *goren* and take *terumah* or *ma'aser* on her behalf. The mishnah's ruling, according to Rav Sheshet, is that in these intermediate states of connection to a new household, she cannot send a *shaliach* to collect from her prospective husband's tithes.

### Married Women's Rights

This reinterpretation immediately raises a question: does a married woman actually have the right to send a *shaliach* to collect *terumah* on her behalf? If she does not have this right even when fully married, then Rav Sheshet's interpretation does not explain what she loses in the betrothed state. The *gemara* responds by citing a *baraita* that confirms she does indeed have this right when fully married. The verse states *va'achaltem oto bechol makom atem uveitchem* — "and you shall eat it in every place, you and your household" — and the *baraita* derives from *uveitchem* (your household) that a married *bat Yisrael* is empowered to authorize the taking of *terumah* on her husband's behalf. One might ask whether the verse is teaching that she is permitted to eat *terumah* rather than that she may authorize its collection. The *baraita* dismisses this reading: if the lenient *ma'aser* may be eaten by a married woman, surely *terumah* — which is more stringent — need not be taught anew. The verse must therefore be teaching the more novel point: that a married woman can serve as an authorizing agent for the taking of *terumah*.

### Mar brei de-Ravina

Mar brei de-Ravina offers an alternative explanation of the mishnah that differs from Rav Sheshet's. He reads the mishnah's *eina ochelet* to mean that she may not personally go to the *granot* — the threshing floors or storehouses — and collect *ma'aser* herself. Even if she is substantively entitled to eat *ma'aser*, she cannot personally appear at the place of distribution and take it. Her husband must go on her behalf.

The *gemara* probes the rationale for this restriction. One view is that it is *mishum yichud* — the concern about a woman being alone with men at the threshing floor is the reason she should not collect independently. Another view is that the concern is *mishum gerushah*: if she goes alone to collect, onlookers may not realize that she has been divorced and is no longer entitled to those tithes, leading to improper distribution. The *gemara* challenges this second rationale: if the concern is that a divorced *bat Levi* might show up and improperly collect, then a divorced *bat kohen* should equally be a concern when collecting *terumah* for her father. The *gemara* responds: this is indeed a *gezeirah mishum gerushah bat Yisrael* — a rabbinic precaution that we impose broadly so that people do not get into the habit of giving tithes to women who arrive at the *goren* alone, since sometimes that woman will turn out to be a *bat Yisrael* with no entitlement at all.

If this *gezeirah* applies broadly, the *gemara* continues, why does the mishnah speak specifically of a *me'ureset* — a betrothed woman? Would not the same concern apply to a married woman? The *gemara* answers: since the opening cases of the mishnah — where the question of actual eating rights was at issue — employed the language of betrothal, the mishnah uses consistent terminology throughout, even in the closing case, even though the ruling would apply equally to a fully married woman. This resolution, while analytically necessary, does produce an unusual reading of the mishnah, in which *eina ochelet* does not straightforwardly mean "she does not eat" but rather refers to a more limited practical restriction on personal collection at the *goren*.

## Kohen vs. Levi Dispute

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

A *baraita* is introduced that parallels the earlier one but attributes the positions to different *tanna'im*: "*terumah* goes to the *kohen* and *ma'aser rishon* goes to the *Levi* — these are the words of Rabbi Akiva." This closely parallels the language previously attributed to Rabbi Meir, and the *gemara* will need to sort out the relationship between these two positions.

### R. Elazar's Position

Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah states in this *baraita* that *ma'aser* goes to the *kohen* — or rather, as the *gemara* clarifies, even to the *kohen*. The *gemara* immediately challenges a literal reading: does *ma'aser* go to the *kohen* to the exclusion of the *Levi*? That cannot be. So the *gemara* corrects: read it as *af l'kohen* — *ma'aser rishon* may be given even to a *kohen*, not only to a *Levi*. This means that in Rabbi Akiva's view, *ma'aser rishon* properly goes to the *Levi* and may not go to a *kohen*; Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah, by contrast, permits giving *ma'aser rishon* even to a *kohen*. This is a sharper and more substantive disagreement than what was implied earlier in the *sugya*, and it carries significant consequences. It is also worth noting that Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah was himself a *kohen*.

### 24 Places Doctrine

The *gemara* explains that Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's position follows the principle articulated by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *b'esrim v'arba'ah mekomos nikra'u kohanim Levi'im* — in twenty-four places in the Torah and *Nevi'im*, *kohanim* are called *Levi'im*. This is one of those places. The verse governing the distribution of *ma'aser* addresses *ha-Levi'im*, and since *kohanim* are themselves called *Levi'im* in Scripture, the *kohen* too falls within the category of those to whom *ma'aser rishon* may be given. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi himself likely made this observation as a standalone *midrashic* point — that the *kohanim* are consistently referred to as *Levi'im* in twenty-four places — without necessarily knowing he was providing the conceptual basis for Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's position.

### Cemetery Argument

Rabbi Akiva's counter-argument is based on a different verse. The Torah states *achaltem oto bechol makom* — "you shall eat it in every place." Rabbi Akiva reads this to mean that only someone who can eat *ma'aser* in every place is entitled to receive it. A *kohen*, however, cannot eat in a cemetery — he is forbidden from contracting corpse-impurity in a graveyard. Therefore a *kohen* does not qualify as someone who can eat it *bechol makom*, and he is excluded from the class of recipients.

### Garden Collection

There is a historical incident that illuminates this dispute. Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah — himself a *kohen* — was in the habit of collecting *ma'aser rishon* from a certain garden. This was his practice in accordance with his own ruling that a *kohen* is entitled to receive *ma'aser*.

### Cemetery Blockade

Rabbi Akiva responded to this practice. The *gemara* initially appears to describe him leading Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah to the entrance of a cemetery, as if to say: you think you can eat *ma'aser* — go ahead and enter. But a more careful reading of the *gemara*'s language suggests something more pointed: Rabbi Akiva arranged that the only entrance to the storage area of that garden ran through a cemetery, effectively blocking all *kohanim* from accessing it. When the *gemara* asks why Rabbi Akiva would do this if even according to one opinion the *ma'aser* was to be redistributed to *kohanim*, the answer is that Rabbi Akiva was sending a message to Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah: if you are taking this as a legal entitlement (*torat chalukah*), that is unacceptable; if you understand it only as an emergency measure flowing from the historical *knas* on the *Levi'im*, that is another matter. Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah reportedly responded: "Akiva with his shepherd's sack — and I shall live." The wry remark reflects that Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah was wealthy, and he was speaking not for himself but for *kohanim* in general who have no fields of their own: where are we *kohanim* supposed to get our food?

## Levites' Punishment

### The Punishment Q.

The *gemara* now asks a foundational question: *mifnei mah kinsu Levi'im b'ma'aser?* — Why were the *Levi'im* punished by having *ma'aser rishon* taken away from them? The historical premise is that at some point, the *Levi'im* lost their statutory right to *ma'aser rishon*, and the *gemara* seeks to understand why this occurred and what became of it.

### Two Explanations

There is a dispute between Rabbi Yonatan and the *sav'ya* — the elders. One view holds that the *Levi'im* were punished because they did not make *aliyah* in the time of Ezra, when the Jewish people returned to the Land of Israel from Babylon. The other view holds that the redistribution of *ma'aser* was instituted so that *kohanim* would have something to rely upon during the periods when they were *teme'im* and therefore unable to eat *terumah*.

### Consensus Reached

The *gemara* challenges the second explanation. If the only reason the *Levi'im* lost their *ma'aser* was in order to feed *kohanim* when they were *teme'im*, why should the *Levi'im* bear this burden? The *gemara* concludes that in fact *kulei alma* — all opinions agree — that the *knas* was imposed because the *Levi'im* did not make *aliyah* in the time of Ezra. That is the core punishment; the *Levi'im* chose not to return to the Land, and so they forfeited the privilege that was theirs within the Land.

### Secondary Dispute

Having established that the punishment was for failing to make *aliyah*, the *gemara* identifies the actual point of dispute more precisely: the two opinions disagree about where the confiscated *ma'aser* was redirected. One opinion holds that it was distributed to the *ani'im* — the poor. If the *Levi'im* forfeited their *ma'aser*, it should simply go to those most in need. The other opinion holds that it was redirected to *kohanim* — specifically because *kohanim bimei tum'atan ani'im ninhu*: when *kohanim* become *teme'im* and lose access to *terumah*, they suddenly find themselves with nothing to eat, since unlike ordinary *Yisrael'im* they have no fields of their own. In their state of impurity they are effectively destitute, and the former *ma'aser* of the *Levi'im* was redirected to address their need.

This secondary dispute also illuminates Rabbi Akiva's behavior. According to the opinion that the *ma'aser* was redirected to the *ani'im*, Rabbi Akiva's blocking of Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's collection is entirely comprehensible — the *kohen* has no legitimate claim to that *ma'aser* at all. But according to the opinion that it was redirected to *kohanim*, Rabbi Akiva's message was more nuanced: you may collect it, but understand that you do so only as a consequence of the *knas* against the *Levi'im*, not as an expression of your inherent legal entitlement.

### Scriptural Evidence

The *gemara* asks: how do we know that the *Levi'im* in fact failed to make *aliyah* in Ezra's time? The answer comes from a *pasuk meforash* in the book of Ezra: *va'akbetzem el ha-nahar ha-ba el Ahava va-nachaneh sham yamim sheloshah va-avinan ba-am u-va-kohanim u-mibnei Levi lo matzati sham* — "And I gathered them at the river that flows to Ahava, and we camped there for three days, and I looked among the people and the *kohanim*, and I found none of the sons of Levi there." The verse is explicit: when Ezra assembled the returnees, no *Levi'im* were to be found among them.

### Rav Chisda's History

Rav Chisda adds a historical observation that flows from this situation: *batchilah lo hayu ma'amidim shotrim ela min ha-Levi'im* — originally, communal officials (*shotrim*) were drawn exclusively from the *Levi'im*, as indicated by the verse *ve-shotrim ha-Levi'im lifneichem*. But now, following the *Levi'im*'s failure to return in Ezra's time, officials are drawn from *Yisrael* instead. Rav Chisda cites a verse for this latter point, though the *Masoret ha-Shas* notes that the verse he cites does not appear verbatim in *Tanach*. The commentators suggest he may be loosely paraphrasing the verse in Devarim 1:13 — *havu lachem anashim chachamim u-nevonim vi-yedu'im le-shivteichem va-ashimem be-rasheichem* — though the correspondence is imprecise.

## Woman's Status Shifts

### Bat Yisrael Path

The second mishnah of the *daf* sets out the full range of possible status shifts for a woman over the course of multiple marriages. A *bat Yisrael* who marries a *kohen* eats *terumah*. If her husband dies and she has a son from him, she continues to eat *terumah* by virtue of that son. If she then marries a *Levi*, she eats *ma'aser*. If he too dies and she has a son from him, she can eat *ma'aser* by virtue of that son — but she has lost her access to *terumah*, since the *levi* son does not restore that privilege. If she then marries a *Yisrael*, she eats neither *terumah* nor *ma'aser*. If he dies and she has a son from him, she remains excluded from both.

### Sons' Effect

But the status conferred by a son persists even after the relevant marriage ends. If the son from the *Yisrael* dies, she regains her status from the *Levi* son — she may again eat *ma'aser*. If the son from the *Levi* dies, she regains her status from the *kohen* son — she may eat *terumah*. The sons function as status-anchors that outlast the marriages which produced them, and their deaths can restore her to progressively earlier entitlements. It is worth noting that this applies to a son of any age — even a minor child functions to determine the mother's eligibility.

### Bat Kohen Path

The mishnah then traces the corresponding path for a *bat kohen*. A *bat kohen* who marries a *Yisrael* does not eat *terumah*. If he dies with no son from him, she still does not eat *terumah*. If she then marries a *Levi*, she eats *ma'aser*. If he dies with no son from him, she continues to eat *ma'aser*. If she then marries a *kohen*, she eats *terumah*. The mishnah implies that her final marital connection carries priority: if her last husband was a *kohen*, and she has a son from that *kohen* as well as sons from earlier marriages, she eats *terumah* — the highest status — even though she also has a *ben Yisrael*.

### Reversion to Origins

As sons die, the woman's status reverts. If her *kohen* son dies, she can no longer eat *terumah*. If her *Levi* son dies, she can no longer eat *ma'aser*. If her *Yisrael* son dies, *chozeret le-veit aviha* — she returns to her father's house. *Beit aviha* here is understood as her tribal identity, not necessarily the physical home of her biological father; the term *beit av* refers broadly to her collective family and original tribal affiliation. Once all her sons from all her marriages have died, she reverts entirely to her original status as a *bat kohen*, and may again partake of the privileges of that station — eating as though she had never left her father's house. As was observed at the close of the shiur, this entire complex of shifting entitlements is the reason why the daughters of *kohanim* ideally marry *kohanim* — so that they never face these complications at all.
