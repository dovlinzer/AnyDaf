# Yevamot 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Status of Women

### Women & Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מְאוֹרֶסֶת לְכֹהֵן, מְעוּבֶּרֶת מִכֹּהֵן, שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם לְכֹהֵן, וְכֵן בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — לֹא תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה. בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל מְאוֹרֶסֶת לְלֵוִי, מְעוּבֶּרֶת מִלֵּוִי, שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם לְלֵוִי, וְכֵן בַּת לֵוִי לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — לֹא תֹּאכַל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If there is **an Israelite woman betrothed to a priest** or **pregnant from a priest,** and he died; and **a widow awaiting her *yavam*,** who is **a priest; and similarly,** the **daughter of a priest** who is betrothed, pregnant from, or is a widow waiting **for** her *yavam*, who is **an Israelite, she may not partake of *teruma*.** If there is **an Israelite woman betrothed to a Levite or pregnant from a Levite;** and **a widow awaiting her *yavam*, who is a Levite; and similarly** the **daughter of a Levite** who is betrothed, pregnant from, or a widow waiting **for** her *yavam*, who is **an Israelite, she may not partake of tithes.**

The mishnah on this amud opens with the question of who is entitled to eat *terumah*, and more specifically, which women may eat it by virtue of their spousal connection to a *kohen*. The mishnah establishes that a *bat Yisrael* who is merely betrothed (*me'ureset*) to a *kohen*, or who is pregnant by a *kohen* but not yet married, or who is a *shomeret yavam* awaiting *yibbum* with a *kohen* — in all these cases, she does not eat *terumah*. In each scenario, her connection to the *kohen* is real but still contingent: the marriage has not been fully concluded, the *yibbum* has not yet taken place, or she has conceived but not yet given birth to a living child. That uncertainty is sufficient to withhold the privilege of *terumah*.

The same rule, surprisingly, applies in the reverse direction: a *bat kohen* who is connected to a *Yisrael* — through betrothal, pregnancy, or *yibbum* — also may not eat *terumah*. This is somewhat counterintuitive. One might have assumed that since these tenuous connections are insufficient to grant a *bat Yisrael* access to *terumah*, they should equally be insufficient to strip a *bat kohen* of it. Yet the mishnah rules that any connection whatsoever to a *Yisrael* removes her from the category of those permitted to eat *terumah*.

### Safek Principle

The underlying logic, it seems, is the principle of *safek d'orayta l'chumra* — when there is doubt in a matter of Torah law, we rule stringently. For the *bat Yisrael*, the uncertainty is whether she will ultimately complete her connection to the *kohen* and thereby become entitled to eat *terumah*; since we are not yet certain, she does not eat. For the *bat kohen*, the uncertainty runs in the opposite direction: perhaps her connection to the *Yisrael* will be fully realized, and it will emerge that she ought not to have been eating *terumah*. We therefore forbid her in advance. In both cases, the doubt drives the stringent outcome.

### Women & Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּת לֵוִי מְאוֹרֶסֶת לְכֹהֵן, מְעוּבֶּרֶת מִכֹּהֵן, שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם לְכֹהֵן, וְכֵן בַּת כֹּהֵן לְלֵוִי — לֹא תֹאכַל לֹא בִּתְרוּמָה וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** If there is a **daughter of a Levite betrothed to a priest** or **pregnant from a priest;** and **a widow awaiting her *yavam*, who is a priest; and similarly a daughter of a priest** who is betrothed **to** or pregnant from a Levite, or is a widow waiting for her *yavam*, who is **a Levite, she may partake of neither *teruma* nor tithes.** This follows the *halakha* that betrothal, pregnancy, and waiting for a *yavam* disqualify the daughter of a priest from eating *teruma*, but they do not enable an Israelite woman to partake of *teruma*.

The same rules that govern *terumah* are applied by the mishnah to *ma'aser* as well. A *bat Yisrael* who is betrothed to, pregnant by, or a *shomeret yavam* of a *Levi* does not eat *ma'aser*. The same logic prevails: parallel to the rules of *terumah* for *kohanim*, the rules of *ma'aser* apply to *Levi'im*, and the same range of incomplete connections to a *Levi* is insufficient to grant access.

### Bat Kohen/Levi

The mishnah then states that a *bat Levi* connected to a *kohen* — and a *bat kohen* connected to a *Levi* — eats neither *terumah* nor *ma'aser*. This is one of the more unusual *halachot* in the mishnah. One can readily understand why a *bat Levi* betrothed to a *kohen* should not eat *terumah* — she is not yet a full member of the *kohen*'s household. But why should a *bat kohen* connected to a *Levi* be forbidden from eating *ma'aser*? As will emerge later in the *sugya*, a *kohen* is himself a species of *Levi*; if so, the *bat kohen* is already associated with the tribe that is entitled to *ma'aser*. The mishnah seems to be anticipating the possibility that she will fully become part of the *Levi*'s household, and we therefore restrict her in the interim — just as we restrict the *bat Levi* who might become part of the *kohen*'s household. The important underlying question of whether *zarim* — non-*Levi'im* — can eat *ma'aser rishon* at all will occupy the *gemara*'s attention in the next section.

## R. Meir & Zarim

### Non-Jews & Ma'aser

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְהֵא זָרָה, זָרָה מִי לָא אָכְלָה בְּמַעֲשֵׂר?! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן אָסוּר לְזָרִים. דְּתַנְיָא: תְּרוּמָה לְכֹהֵן, וּמַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן לְלֵוִי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that an Israelite woman betrothed to a Levite may not partake of tithes. The Gemara is puzzled by this ruling: **And let her** even **be** a complete **foreigner** who is not a Levite; **may a foreigner not partake of tithes?** In contradistinction to *teruma*, no special sanctity pertains to tithes; they are merely the possession of the Levite. What difference does it make, then, whether she is a Levite or not? **Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said** that the **first tithe is forbidden to foreigners,** i.e., non-Levites, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: ***Teruma* is for a priest and** the **first tithe is for a Levite;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**

This position is grounded in a *baraita*: "*terumah* goes to the *kohen* and *ma'aser rishon* goes to the *Levi* — these are the words of Rabbi Meir." The question arises: in what sense does the *baraita* imply that *zarim* are forbidden from eating *ma'aser rishon*? Rashi explains that there is a *hekesh* — a linguistic parallel — between *terumah* and *ma'aser*, drawn from the verse in the context of *ma'aser*: *ki et ma'asar Bnei Yisrael asher yarimu la-Hashem terumah* — the Torah applies the term *terumah* to *ma'aser*. Just as *terumah* is forbidden to *zarim*, so too *ma'aser rishon* is forbidden to *zarim*. This derivation is attributed in the *gemara* with the phrase *mishmeih d'Gemara* — transmitted in the name of the tradition rather than in the name of an identified individual. Rashi notes that this phrase indicates an unbroken chain of transmission whose links are not enumerated, and that it is used to assure us that the *amora* is not generating this interpretation himself but rather reporting a received tradition traced back to Rabbi Meir.

### Rabbi Meir's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּגְמָרָא: ״כִּי אֶת מַעְשַׂר בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר יָרִימוּ לַה׳ תְּרוּמָה״. מָה תְּרוּמָה אֲסוּרָה לְזָרִים, אַף מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן אָסוּר לְזָרִים. אִי מָה תְּרוּמָה חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִיתָה וָחוֹמֶשׁ — אַף מַעֲשֵׂר חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִיתָה וָחוֹמֶשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What is the reason** for **Rabbi Meir's** opinion? **Rav Aḥa, son of Rabba, said in the name of tradition** that the verse states: **"For the tithe of the children of Israel that they set apart as a *teruma* to the Lord,** I have given to the Levites for an inheritance" (Numbers 18:24). From the fact that this verse calls the tithe "*teruma*," we learn: **Just as *teruma* is forbidden to foreigners, so too** is the **first tithe forbidden to foreigners,** i.e., non-Levites. The Gemara asks: **If** so, is it true that **just as** with ***teruma*,** a foreigner who eats it **is liable** to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven **and** to pay the additional **fifth for it, so too,** with regard to **tithes,** a foreigner who eats it should be **liable for it** to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven **and** to pay the additional **fifth?**

### R. Elazar Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּמֵתוּ בוֹ כִּי יְחַלְּלֻהוּ״, ״וְיָסַף חֲמִישִׁיתוֹ עָלָיו״. ״בּוֹ״ — וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, ״עָלָיו״ — וְלֹא עַל מַעֲשֵׂר. וְרַבָּנַן, מָה תְּרוּמָה טוֹבֶלֶת, אַף מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי טוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "They will die through it if they profane it"** (Leviticus 22:9), and a different verse states: **"Then he shall add the fifth part thereof unto it"** (Leviticus 22:14). A close reading of these verses shows that the Torah is emphasizing that the death penalty comes **through it,** *teruma*, **and not through tithes,** and that a fifth must be added **to it, but not to tithes.** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Meir, how do they account for the comparison in the above verse? The Gemara answers: They would say it teaches that **just as** the requirement to separate ***teruma* produces** the status of forbidden **untithed produce, so too** the requirement to separate the **first tithe also produces** the status of forbidden **untithed produce.**

### Gemara's Reworking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּמָה לְכֹהֵן, וּמַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן לְלֵוִי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה מַתִּירוֹ לְכֹהֵן. מַתִּירוֹ?! מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא מַאן דְּאָסַר? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: נוֹתְנוֹ אַף לְכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** ***Teruma* is for a priest and** the **first tithe is for a Levite;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya permits it,** i.e., the first tithe, **to a priest,** as he too is from the tribe of Levi. The Gemara is puzzled by this last statement: It says: **Permits it.** Does this prove **by inference that there is one** *tanna* **that prohibits** a priest from partaking of tithes? But a priest is also a Levite and cannot be considered a foreigner. **Rather, say** that Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya meant that **one may give it even to a priest.** The tithe does not have to be handed to a Levite; one may choose to give it to a priest instead.

## Ma'aser as Tevul

### Linguistic Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל טֶבֶל שֶׁלֹּא הוּרַם מִמֶּנּוּ כׇּל עִיקָּר. הוּרַם מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה וְלֹא הוּרַם מִמֶּנּוּ מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, וַאֲפִילּוּ מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says:** One **might** have thought a person **should be liable only for untithed produce from which no** *terumot* or tithes **have been separated at all,** but if the **great *teruma* has been separated from it and the first tithe has not been separated from it;** or if the **first tithe** has been separated from it **and** the **second tithe has not; or even** if the **poor man's tithe,** which is merely given to the poor and has no sanctity, has not been separated, **from where** is it derived that such produce also has the status of untithed produce?

Having established Rabbi Meir's position, the *gemara* inquires further: what exactly is the basis for applying the laws of *terumah* to *ma'aser rishon* via the *hekesh* in the verse? The verse *ki et ma'asar Bnei Yisrael asher yarimu la-Hashem terumah* uses the term *terumah* in connection with *ma'aser*, and from this Rabbi Meir derives that just as *terumah* is forbidden to *zarim*, so too *ma'aser rishon* is forbidden to *zarim* — *mah terumah asurah l'zarim af ma'aser rishon asur l'zarim*.

One might then push further: since *terumah* carries penalties of *mitah b'yedei shamayim* for intentional consumption by a *zar*, and *chomesh* — payment of principal plus a fifth — for inadvertent consumption, perhaps *ma'aser rishon* similarly carries these penalties. If the *hekesh* is broad, perhaps the full penal structure of *terumah* applies.

### Penalties Applied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכוֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאָכְלוּ בִשְׁעָרֶיךָ וְשָׂבֵעוּ״. מָה ״שְׁעָרֶיךָ״ הָאָמוּר לְהַלָּן — מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, אַף ״שְׁעָרֶיךָ״ הָאָמוּר כָּאן — מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא תוּכַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states: "You may not eat within your gates** the tithe of your grain" (Deuteronomy 12:17), **and below,** with regard to the poor man's tithe, **it states: "That they may eat within your gates and be satisfied"** (Deuteronomy 26:12). **Just as "your gates" stated below** is referring to **the poor man's tithe, so too "your gates" stated here** is referring to **the poor man's tithe, and the Merciful One states** in the Torah **"you may not** eat," implying that it may be eaten only after separation.

The *gemara* raises this possibility directly: just as one who eats *terumah* illicitly incurs *mitah* and pays *chomesh*, so perhaps one who eats *ma'aser rishon* illicitly also incurs *mitah* and *chomesh*. The Torah, however, confines these penalties with the words *bo v'lo b'ma'aser* — "for *it*," meaning for *terumah*, but not for *ma'aser* — and *alav v'lo al ma'aser* — the *chomesh* applies to *terumah* specifically, not to *ma'aser rishon*. Thus, while *ma'aser rishon* shares with *terumah* the quality of being forbidden to *zarim*, it does not carry the same severe penalties.

### Alternative View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְלָאו, אֲבָל מִיתָה — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** we had learned it only **from there, I would say** that it merely teaches **a prohibition** against partaking of untithed produce of this type, **but** the **death** penalty is **not** warranted. The comparison to *teruma* consequently **teaches us** that eating this type of untithed produce is also punishable by death at the hand of Heaven.

The *gemara* then considers the position of the *rabanan* — those who disagree with Rabbi Meir and permit *zarim* to eat *ma'aser rishon*. According to them, the *hekesh* between *terumah* and *ma'aser* does not teach that *zarim* are forbidden from eating *ma'aser rishon*. Instead it teaches something different: just as *terumah* renders produce *tovel* — untithed produce that is forbidden to be eaten until the *terumah* has been properly separated — so too *ma'aser rishon* renders produce *tovel* when it has not yet been separated. In other words, the *hekesh* from *terumah* teaches the *tovelet* status of *ma'aser rishon*, not its prohibition upon *zarim*.

## R. Yossi's Analysis

### R. Yossi's Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן דְּטָבֵיל, מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי נָפְקָא! אִי מֵהַהִיא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְלָאו, אֲבָל מִיתָה — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **A different version** of this discussion presents it in the form of a question: Isn't it the case that the *halakha* that failure to separate the **first tithe creates** the status of **untithed produce is derived from** the *halakha* that **Rabbi Yosei** taught? If so, there is no need for the exposition of the verse referring to tithes as *teruma*. The Gemara answers: **If** the proof was **from that** source alone, **I would say** that it is only prohibited **by a prohibition but** the **death** penalty is **not** warranted. He therefore **teaches us** that all the stringencies of untithed produce are in force.

A *baraita* of Rabbi Yossi is introduced at this point. Rabbi Yossi addresses the question of whether the severe penalty of *mitah b'yedei shamayim* applies only to produce from which no tithes whatsoever have been taken, or whether it extends even to produce from which some tithes have been taken but others remain. He poses the problem as follows: one might have thought that the *mitah* penalty applies only to *tevel* in its purest form — produce from which no separation at all has been made. But what if *terumah gedolah* has been taken but *ma'aser rishon* has not? Or if *ma'aser rishon* has been taken but *ma'aser sheni* has not? And further still, how do we know that even the failure to separate *ma'aser ani* leaves the produce with the status of *tevel* that triggers the death penalty?

### Ma'aser Ani Restriction

To extend the prohibition all the way down to *ma'aser ani*, Rabbi Yossi invokes the verse *lo tukhal le'ekhol bi-sh'arekha* — "you cannot eat it in your gates." He then draws a *gezerah shavah* from a parallel usage of *sh'arekha* in a verse that speaks of *ma'aser ani* — *v'akhlu bi-sh'arekha v'sav'u*, "and they shall eat in your gates and be satisfied." Since the word *sh'arekha* appears in both contexts, we learn that the prohibition of *lo tukhal le'ekhol bi-sh'arekha* extends to *ma'aser ani* as well. The produce remains *tevel* and is restricted so long as the *ma'aser ani* has not been properly set aside.

One might note that this appears in tension with the well-known rules of *masechet Demai*, which treats produce of uncertain tithing status leniently in most cases. The suggestion is that the obligations discussed in these *baraitot* operate at the biblical level for produce that is certainly *tevel*, while the rabbinic dispensations of *Demai* apply specifically where there is doubt whether the second and third tithes have been separated — a *chumra d'rabanan* that does not carry the biblical penalties.

### Death Penalty Source

The *gemara* then clarifies the interaction between Rabbi Yossi's derivation and Rabbi Meir's. If we had heard only from Rabbi Yossi's *pasuk*, we might have thought that the prohibition is only a *lav* — a negative commandment — but not one that carries the *mitah* penalty. The *pasuk* of Rabbi Meir — which creates the *hekesh* between *terumah* and *ma'aser rishon* and applies the term *terumah* to *ma'aser* — is therefore necessary to establish that the prohibition of *tevel* in its various stages carries the severity of *mitah* and not merely a *lav*. A *lishna achrina* — an alternative manuscript formulation — reverses the role of the two sources while reaching the same conclusion: *ma'aser rishon* acquires its *tovel* status from Rabbi Yossi's derivation, and from Rabbi Meir's *hekesh* we learn that the attendant prohibition reaches the level of *mitah*.

## Mishnah Problem

### Apparent Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּהּ — כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בַּת לֵוִי מְאוֹרֶסֶת לְכֹהֵן, וּבַת כֹּהֵן לְלֵוִי — לֹא תֹּאכַל לֹא בִּתְרוּמָה וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, הָכָא מַאי זָרוּת אִיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **In what** manner **did you establish** the mishna? **In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** But if so, **say the latter clause:** The **daughter of a Levite betrothed to a priest and** the **daughter of a priest** betrothed **to a Levite may eat neither *teruma* nor tithe. Here, what foreignness is there** that prohibits her from partaking of the tithe?

The *gemara* now returns to the mishnah with a sharp challenge. Having explained that the mishnah follows Rabbi Meir — who holds that *ma'aser rishon* is forbidden to *zarim* — the *gemara* invites us to examine the latter clause of the mishnah: a *bat Levi* betrothed to a *kohen* and a *bat kohen* betrothed to a *Levi* eat neither *terumah* nor *ma'aser*. The problem here is glaring. The explanation that the mishnah follows Rabbi Meir was invoked to explain why a *bat Yisrael* betrothed to a *Levi* does not eat *ma'aser* — because Rabbi Meir forbids *zarim* from eating *ma'aser*, and she has the status of a *zarah*. But a *bat kohen* is herself a kind of *Levi'it*; why should she be forbidden from *ma'aser* when she is connected to a *Levi*? And symmetrically, a *bat Levi* connected to a *kohen* — why should she not eat *ma'aser* from the *Levi* side of her identity? The mishnah forbids both, and the explanation that we are following Rabbi Meir's view on *zarim* does not resolve this difficulty at all.

### Rav Sheshet's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַאי ״אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת״ דְּקָתָנֵי — אֵינָהּ נוֹתֶנֶת רְשׁוּת לִתְרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: What is** the meaning of: **She may not eat, that** the mishna **teaches?** It means that **she may not give permission** to others **to separate** the *teruma* from the tithe.

Rav Sheshet proposes a creative reinterpretation of the mishnah. When the mishnah says she does not eat (*eina ochelet*), he argues, it does not mean she is forbidden from eating *ma'aser* at all. Rather, it means she cannot authorize others to separate or collect *ma'aser* on her behalf — *eina notenet reshut litrom*. Normally, a woman's *shaliach* — her appointed agent — can go to the *goren* and take *terumah* or *ma'aser* on her behalf. The mishnah's ruling, according to Rav Sheshet, is that in these intermediate states of connection to a new household, she cannot send a *shaliach* to collect from her prospective husband's tithes.

### Married Women's Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְלָל דִּנְשׂוּאָה נוֹתֶנֶת רְשׁוּת? אִין, וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וַאֲכַלְתֶּם אוֹתוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם אַתֶּם וּבֵיתְכֶם״ — לִימֵּד עַל נְשׂוּאָה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנּוֹתֶנֶת רְשׁוּת לִתְרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is it to be concluded **by inference that a married** woman **may give permission** to separate *teruma* from the tithe? The Gemara answers: **Yes, and isn't it taught: "And you may eat it in any place, you and your households"** (Numbers 18:31)? This **teaches that an Israelite woman married** to a Levite **may give permission** to another **to separate** *teruma* from the Levite's tithe.

This reinterpretation immediately raises a question: does a married woman actually have the right to send a *shaliach* to collect *terumah* on her behalf? If she does not have this right even when fully married, then Rav Sheshet's interpretation does not explain what she loses in the betrothed state. The *gemara* responds by citing a *baraita* that confirms she does indeed have this right when fully married. The verse states *va'achaltem oto bechol makom atem uveitchem* — "and you shall eat it in every place, you and your household" — and the *baraita* derives from *uveitchem* (your household) that a married *bat Yisrael* is empowered to authorize the taking of *terumah* on her husband's behalf. One might ask whether the verse is teaching that she is permitted to eat *terumah* rather than that she may authorize its collection. The *baraita* dismisses this reading: if the lenient *ma'aser* may be eaten by a married woman, surely *terumah* — which is more stringent — need not be taught anew. The verse must therefore be teaching the more novel point: that a married woman can serve as an authorizing agent for the taking of *terumah*.

### Mar brei de-Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא תוּכַל לֶאֱכוֹל בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְאָכְלוּ בִשְׁעָרֶיךָ וְשָׂבֵעוּ״. מָה ״שְׁעָרֶיךָ״ הָאָמוּר לְהַלָּן — מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, אַף ״שְׁעָרֶיךָ״ הָאָמוּר כָּאן — מַעְשַׂר עָנִי, וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״לֹא תוּכַל״. וְאִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְלָאו, אֲבָל מִיתָה — לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** (This teaching appears in the source material above and carries forward into the analysis here.)

Mar brei de-Ravina offers an alternative explanation of the mishnah that differs from Rav Sheshet's. He reads the mishnah's *eina ochelet* to mean that she may not personally go to the *granot* — the threshing floors or storehouses — and collect *ma'aser* herself. Even if she is substantively entitled to eat *ma'aser*, she cannot personally appear at the place of distribution and take it. Her husband must go on her behalf.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבְנָא אָמַר: לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין חוֹלְקִין לָהּ מַעֲשֵׂר בְּבֵית הַגְּרָנוֹת. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם יִיחוּד, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם גְּרוּשָׁה, גְּרוּשָׁה בַּת לֵוִי מִי לָא אָכְלָה בְּמַעֲשֵׂר?!
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Rabbana, said:** The mishna is not teaching that the daughter of a Levite who was betrothed to a priest may not partake of tithe, but rather it is coming **to say that we do not distribute tithe to her in the granary.** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that the reason for the decree against distributing *teruma* to a woman in the granary is **due to** the prohibition (*Yevamot* 100a) against a woman **being alone** with a strange man in the granary, which is a secluded place, as this concern applies equally to the case here. **But according to the one who says** that the Sages prohibited this practice **due to** concern that the woman might be **a divorcée,** who is no longer entitled to *teruma*, this concern should not apply to the **daughter of a Levite. Does she not partake of tithe** on her own account, even after she is divorced?

The *gemara* probes the rationale for this restriction. One view is that it is *mishum yichud* — the concern about a woman being alone with men at the threshing floor is the reason she should not collect independently. Another view is that the concern is *mishum gerushah*: if she goes alone to collect, onlookers may not realize that she has been divorced and is no longer entitled to those tithes, leading to improper distribution. The *gemara* challenges this second rationale: if the concern is that a divorced *bat Levi* might show up and improperly collect, then a divorced *bat kohen* should equally be a concern when collecting *terumah* for her father. The *gemara* responds: this is indeed a *gezeirah mishum gerushah bat Yisrael* — a rabbinic precaution that we impose broadly so that people do not get into the habit of giving tithes to women who arrive at the *goren* alone, since sometimes that woman will turn out to be a *bat Yisrael* with no entitlement at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, גְּרוּשָׁה בַּת כֹּהֵן מִי לָא אָכְלָה בִּתְרוּמָה? אֶלָּא גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם גְּרוּשָׁה בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this argument: **And according to your reasoning** that rejects the explanation of Mar, son of Rabbana, with regard to the daughter of a Levite, **does a divorcée** who is the **daughter of a priest not partake of *teruma*?** Why should the daughter of a priest married to a priest not receive *teruma* in a granary? **Rather,** this is a rabbinic **decree** that was enacted primarily **due to** a priest's **divorcée** who is the **daughter of a non-priest,** as she may no longer partake of *teruma* after her divorce. They also applied this decree to the daughter of a priest divorced from a priest. For this reason, they also decreed against a Levite woman receiving a portion in the granary.

If this *gezeirah* applies broadly, the *gemara* continues, why does the mishnah speak specifically of a *me'ureset* — a betrothed woman? Would not the same concern apply to a married woman? The *gemara* answers: since the opening cases of the mishnah — where the question of actual eating rights was at issue — employed the language of betrothal, the mishnah uses consistent terminology throughout, even in the closing case, even though the ruling would apply equally to a fully married woman. This resolution, while analytically necessary, does produce an unusual reading of the mishnah, in which *eina ochelet* does not straightforwardly mean "she does not eat" but rather refers to a more limited practical restriction on personal collection at the *goren*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא מְאוֹרֶסֶת? אֲפִילּוּ נְשׂוּאָה נָמֵי! אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא מְאוֹרֶסֶת, תְּנָא נָמֵי סֵיפָא מְאוֹרֶסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, why specifically** one who was **betrothed; the same would hold true even** for **a married** woman **as well.** The Gemara answers: There is no difference between them in this regard, but **since** the *tanna* **taught** in the **first clause** of the mishna: **Betrothed, he also taught** in **the latter clause: Betrothed,** although the *halakha* in the latter clause does not apply exclusively to a betrothed woman.

## Kohen vs. Levi Dispute

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תְּרוּמָה לְכֹהֵן, וּמַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן לְלֵוִי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: לְכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: *Teruma* is** given **to a priest, and** the **first tithe is** given only **to a Levite;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says:** the first tithe **to a priest.**

A *baraita* is introduced that parallels the earlier one but attributes the positions to different *tanna'im*: "*terumah* goes to the *kohen* and *ma'aser rishon* goes to the *Levi* — these are the words of Rabbi Akiva." This closely parallels the language previously attributed to Rabbi Meir, and the *gemara* will need to sort out the relationship between these two positions.

### R. Elazar's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכֹהֵן וְלֹא לְלֵוִי? אֵימָא אַף לְכֹהֵן. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶל הַלְוִיִּם תְּדַבֵּר וְאָמַרְתָּ אֲלֵיהֶם״, בִּלְוִיִּם קָא מִשְׁתַּעֵי קְרָא. וְאִידַּךְ כִּדְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה מְקוֹמוֹת נִקְרְאוּ כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם, וְזֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶם: ״וְהַכֹּהֲנִים הַלְוִיִּם בְּנֵי צָדוֹק״.
>
> **Translation:** The first tithe is given **to a priest.** The Gemara is puzzled: **To a priest and not to a Levite?** But the Torah expressly states that the first tithe is for Levites. The Gemara answers: **Say** he means it can be given **also to a priest.** The Gemara clarifies: **What is the reason** for **Rabbi Akiva's** opinion? **As it is written: "You shall speak to the Levites, and you shall say to them"** (Numbers 18:26). Clearly, **the verse speaks of Levites,** not priests. **And the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Eliezer, maintains **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: In twenty-four places** in the Bible the **priests are called Levites. And this is one of those** verses: **"And the priests the Levites, the sons of Zadok"** (Ezekiel 44:15).

Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah states in this *baraita* that *ma'aser* goes to the *kohen* — or rather, as the *gemara* clarifies, even to the *kohen*. The *gemara* immediately challenges a literal reading: does *ma'aser* go to the *kohen* to the exclusion of the *Levi*? That cannot be. So the *gemara* corrects: read it as *af l'kohen* — *ma'aser rishon* may be given even to a *kohen*, not only to a *Levi*. This means that in Rabbi Akiva's view, *ma'aser rishon* properly goes to the *Levi* and may not go to a *kohen*; Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah, by contrast, permits giving *ma'aser rishon* even to a *kohen*. This is a sharper and more substantive disagreement than what was implied earlier in the *sugya*, and it carries significant consequences. It is also worth noting that Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah was himself a *kohen*.

### 24 Places Doctrine

The *gemara* explains that Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's position follows the principle articulated by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *b'esrim v'arba'ah mekomos nikra'u kohanim Levi'im* — in twenty-four places in the Torah and *Nevi'im*, *kohanim* are called *Levi'im*. This is one of those places. The verse governing the distribution of *ma'aser* addresses *ha-Levi'im*, and since *kohanim* are themselves called *Levi'im* in Scripture, the *kohen* too falls within the category of those to whom *ma'aser rishon* may be given. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi himself likely made this observation as a standalone *midrashic* point — that the *kohanim* are consistently referred to as *Levi'im* in twenty-four places — without necessarily knowing he was providing the conceptual basis for Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's position.

### Cemetery Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָכָא לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲכַלְתֶּם אוֹתוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם״, מִי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לְאוֹכְלוֹ בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. יָצָא כֹּהֵן, שֶׁאֵין יָכוֹל לְאוֹכְלוֹ בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת. וְאִידַּךְ? כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּבָעֵי — דְּלָא בָּעֵי חוֹמָה, וְאִי אָכֵיל לֵיהּ בְּטוּמְאַת הַגּוּף — לָא לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva** replies: **Here you cannot say** the verse is referring to priests, **as it is written: "And you may eat it in any place"** (Numbers 18:31), from which we learn that the tithe is given to **one who can eat it in any place.** This **excludes a priest, who cannot eat it in a cemetery,** as he is prohibited from entering such a place. Consequently, the verse cannot be referring to priests. **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Eliezer, how does he respond to this claim? He explains the verse as follows: He may eat it **anywhere that he wishes,** that is, in any city, **as it does not require** the **wall** of Jerusalem, like the second tithe. **And** we further learn from here that **if he eats it in** a state of **bodily impurity he is not flogged.** Consequently, we can say that tithe may be eaten by priests in any place.

Rabbi Akiva's counter-argument is based on a different verse. The Torah states *achaltem oto bechol makom* — "you shall eat it in every place." Rabbi Akiva reads this to mean that only someone who can eat *ma'aser* in every place is entitled to receive it. A *kohen*, however, cannot eat in a cemetery — he is forbidden from contracting corpse-impurity in a graveyard. Therefore a *kohen* does not qualify as someone who can eat it *bechol makom*, and he is excluded from the class of recipients.

### Garden Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא גִּינְּתָא דַּהֲוָה שָׁקֵיל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן מִינַּהּ. אֲזַל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אַהְדְּרֵיהּ לְפִתְחָא לְבֵי קִבְרֵי. אֲמַר: עֲקִיבָא בְּתַרְמִילוֹ, וַאֲנָא חָיֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain garden from which Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya,** a priest, **would take** the **first tithe,** in accordance with his opinion that priests are also entitled to this tithe. **Rabbi Akiva went,** closed up the garden, and **changed its entrance** so that it would be facing **toward the cemetery,** to prevent Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya from entering the garden. Rabbi Elazar **said** in the form of a lighthearted exaggeration: **Akiva,** a former shepherd, comes **with his satchel, but I have to live;** from where will I receive my livelihood if I cannot claim the first tithe? Rabbi Elazar was actually a very wealthy man and did not need the produce from this garden. However, his point was that Rabbi Akiva acted in order to stop him from receiving something that he felt was rightfully his.

There is a historical incident that illuminates this dispute. Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah — himself a *kohen* — was in the habit of collecting *ma'aser rishon* from a certain garden. This was his practice in accordance with his own ruling that a *kohen* is entitled to receive *ma'aser*.

### Cemetery Blockade

Rabbi Akiva responded to this practice. The *gemara* initially appears to describe him leading Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah to the entrance of a cemetery, as if to say: you think you can eat *ma'aser* — go ahead and enter. But a more careful reading of the *gemara*'s language suggests something more pointed: Rabbi Akiva arranged that the only entrance to the storage area of that garden ran through a cemetery, effectively blocking all *kohanim* from accessing it. When the *gemara* asks why Rabbi Akiva would do this if even according to one opinion the *ma'aser* was to be redistributed to *kohanim*, the answer is that Rabbi Akiva was sending a message to Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah: if you are taking this as a legal entitlement (*torat chalukah*), that is unacceptable; if you understand it only as an emergency measure flowing from the historical *knas* on the *Levi'im*, that is another matter. Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah reportedly responded: "Akiva with his shepherd's sack — and I shall live." The wry remark reflects that Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah was wealthy, and he was speaking not for himself but for *kohanim* in general who have no fields of their own: where are we *kohanim* supposed to get our food?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לַן דְּלָא סְלִיקוּ בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וָאֶקְבְּצֵם אֶל הַנָּהָר הַבָּא עַל אַהֲוָא וַנַּחֲנֶה שָׁם יָמִים שְׁלֹשָׁה וָאָבִינָה בָעָם וּבַכֹּהֲנִים וּמִבְּנֵי לֵוִי לֹא מָצָאתִי שָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the penalty imposed on Levites: **And from where do we** derive **that** the Levites **did not ascend in the days of Ezra? As it is written: "And I gathered them together to the river that runs to Ahava; and we encamped there** for **three days; and I viewed the people, and the priests, and found there none of the sons of Levi"** (Ezra 8:15).

## Levites' Punishment

### The Punishment Q.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, מִפְּנֵי מָה קָנְסוּ לְוִיִּם בְּמַעֲשֵׂר? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן וְסָבַיָּא. חַד אָמַר: שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא. וְחַד אָמַר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּסְמְכוּ כֹּהֲנִים עָלָיו בִּימֵי טוּמְאָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed about the following question: **For what reason did** the Sages **penalize** the **Levites with regard to** their **tithe,** by declaring that it may be given to priests as well? **Rabbi Yonatan and the Elders** who were with him **disagree** with regard to **this** matter. **One said** it was because **they did not ascend,** i.e., immigrate to the land of Israel, **in the days of Ezra. And one said** that it was not a penalty at all, but they gave the first tithe to the priests **so that they** could **rely on it during their days of impurity.** Because it is prohibited for priests to consume *teruma* while in a state of impurity, they would have had nothing to eat if they were dependent exclusively on *teruma*. It is permitted, however, to eat the tithe while impure.

The *gemara* now asks a foundational question: *mifnei mah kinsu Levi'im b'ma'aser?* — Why were the *Levi'im* punished by having *ma'aser rishon* taken away from them? The historical premise is that at some point, the *Levi'im* lost their statutory right to *ma'aser rishon*, and the *gemara* seeks to understand why this occurred and what became of it.

### Two Explanations

There is a dispute between Rabbi Yonatan and the *sav'ya* — the elders. One view holds that the *Levi'im* were punished because they did not make *aliyah* in the time of Ezra, when the Jewish people returned to the Land of Israel from Babylon. The other view holds that the redistribution of *ma'aser* was instituted so that *kohanim* would have something to rely upon during the periods when they were *teme'im* and therefore unable to eat *terumah*.

### Consensus Reached

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קַנְסִינְהוּ. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּסְמְכוּ עָלָיו כֹּהֲנִים בִּימֵי טוּמְאָתָן — מִשּׁוּם כֹּהֲנִים קַנְסִינְהוּ לַלְוִיִּם?! אֶלָּא כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא קְנָסָא שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר: קְנָסָא לַעֲנִיִּים, וּמָר סָבַר: כֹּהֲנִים בִּימֵי טוּמְאָתָן — עֲנִיִּים נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** it was because **they did not ascend,** we can understand that **due to that** reason **they penalized** the Levites by forcing them to share their tithe with the priests. **But according to the one who says** it was done **so that the priests** could **rely on it during their days of impurity,** should we **penalize the Levites for** the benefit of **priests? Rather, everyone agrees** that it was **a penalty** for the fact **that they did not ascend in the days of Ezra, and here they disagree about this:** One **Sage holds** that the **penalty** is that the tithe must be given **to the poor, and** one **Sage holds** that **priests are** classified as **poor in the days of their impurity.**

The *gemara* challenges the second explanation. If the only reason the *Levi'im* lost their *ma'aser* was in order to feed *kohanim* when they were *teme'im*, why should the *Levi'im* bear this burden? The *gemara* concludes that in fact *kulei alma* — all opinions agree — that the *knas* was imposed because the *Levi'im* did not make *aliyah* in the time of Ezra. That is the core punishment; the *Levi'im* chose not to return to the Land, and so they forfeited the privilege that was theirs within the Land.

### Secondary Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קְנָסָא לַעֲנִיִּים — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אַהְדְּרֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְפִתְחָא לְבֵי קִבְרֵי. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְכֹהֲנִים, אַמַּאי אַהְדְּרֵיהּ לְפִתְחָא לְבֵי קִבְרֵי? הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: אִי דְּקָא אָתֵית בְּתוֹרַת קְנָסָא — אִית לָךְ, וְאִי קָא אָתֵית בְּתוֹרַת חֲלוּקָּה — לֵית לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that the **penalty** imposed on the Levites is that the tithe must be given **to the poor, due to that** reason **Rabbi Akiva changed** the garden **entrance** so that it would be facing **toward the cemetery,** as Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya was a wealthy man. **But according to the one who says** the tithe was given **to the priests, why did he change** the **entrance** so that it would be **toward the cemetery?** The Gemara answers: **This is what he said to him,** i.e., this is what he meant: **If you come** to receive the tithe **by virtue of** the **penalty** imposed on the Levites, **you may have** it, **but if you come by the** standard *halakha* **of distribution,** demanding your share with the Levites, **you may not have** the tithe. If the owner of the garden chooses to give it to you, you may accept it, but you may not take it yourself.

Having established that the punishment was for failing to make *aliyah*, the *gemara* identifies the actual point of dispute more precisely: the two opinions disagree about where the confiscated *ma'aser* was redirected. One opinion holds that it was distributed to the *ani'im* — the poor. If the *Levi'im* forfeited their *ma'aser*, it should simply go to those most in need. The other opinion holds that it was redirected to *kohanim* — specifically because *kohanim bimei tum'atan ani'im ninhu*: when *kohanim* become *teme'im* and lose access to *terumah*, they suddenly find themselves with nothing to eat, since unlike ordinary *Yisrael'im* they have no fields of their own. In their state of impurity they are effectively destitute, and the former *ma'aser* of the *Levi'im* was redirected to address their need.

This secondary dispute also illuminates Rabbi Akiva's behavior. According to the opinion that the *ma'aser* was redirected to the *ani'im*, Rabbi Akiva's blocking of Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah's collection is entirely comprehensible — the *kohen* has no legitimate claim to that *ma'aser* at all. But according to the opinion that it was redirected to *kohanim*, Rabbi Akiva's message was more nuanced: you may collect it, but understand that you do so only as a consequence of the *knas* against the *Levi'im*, not as an expression of your inherent legal entitlement.

### Scriptural Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא לַן דְּלָא סְלִיקוּ בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וָאֶקְבְּצֵם אֶל הַנָּהָר הַבָּא עַל אַהֲוָא וַנַּחֲנֶה שָׁם יָמִים שְׁלֹשָׁה וָאָבִינָה בָעָם וּבַכֹּהֲנִים וּמִבְּנֵי לֵוִי לֹא מָצָאתִי שָׁם״. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּתְּחִלָּה לֹא הָיוּ מֵעֲמִידִים שׁוֹטְרִים אֶלָּא מִן הַלְוִיִּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשׁוֹטְרִים הַלְוִיִּם לִפְנֵיכֶם״, עַכְשָׁיו אֵין מַעֲמִידִין שׁוֹטְרִים אֶלָּא מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשׁוֹטְרִים הָרַבִּים בְּרָאשֵׁיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the penalty imposed on Levites: **And from where do we** derive **that** the Levites **did not ascend in the days of Ezra? As it is written: "And I gathered them together to the river that runs to Ahava; and we encamped there** for **three days; and I viewed the people, and the priests, and found there none of the sons of Levi"** (Ezra 8:15). With regard to this, **Rav Ḥisda said: Initially they would establish officers** over the people **only from** among **the Levites, as it states: "And the officers, the Levites, before you"** (II Chronicles 19:11), but **now they establish officers only from** among the **Israelites, as it is stated: And the officers of the many at your heads.** This indicates that officers were appointed from: The many, meaning the largest group, ordinary Israelites.

The *gemara* asks: how do we know that the *Levi'im* in fact failed to make *aliyah* in Ezra's time? The answer comes from a *pasuk meforash* in the book of Ezra: *va'akbetzem el ha-nahar ha-ba el Ahava va-nachaneh sham yamim sheloshah va-avinan ba-am u-va-kohanim u-mibnei Levi lo matzati sham* — "And I gathered them at the river that flows to Ahava, and we camped there for three days, and I looked among the people and the *kohanim*, and I found none of the sons of Levi there." The verse is explicit: when Ezra assembled the returnees, no *Levi'im* were to be found among them.

### Rav Chisda's History

Rav Chisda adds a historical observation that flows from this situation: *batchilah lo hayu ma'amidim shotrim ella min ha-Levi'im* — originally, communal officials (*shotrim*) were drawn exclusively from the *Levi'im*, as indicated by the verse *ve-shotrim ha-Levi'im lifneichem*. But now, following the *Levi'im*'s failure to return in Ezra's time, officials are drawn from *Yisrael* instead. Rav Chisda cites a verse for this latter point, though the *Masoret ha-Shas* notes that the verse he cites does not appear verbatim in *Tanach*. The commentators suggest he may be loosely paraphrasing the verse in Devarim 1:13 — *havu lachem anashim chachamim u-nevonim vi-yedu'im le-shivteichem va-ashimem be-rasheichem* — though the correspondence is imprecise.

## Woman's Status Shifts

### Bat Yisrael Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנִּיסֵּת לְכֹהֵן — תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה. מֵת וְלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ בֵּן — תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה. נִיסֵּת לְלֵוִי — תֹּאכַל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. מֵת וְלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ בֵּן — תֹּאכַל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. נִיסֵּת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — לֹא תֹּאכַל לֹא בִּתְרוּמָה וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. מֵת וְלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ בֵּן — לֹא תֹּאכַל לֹא בִּתְרוּמָה וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **An Israelite woman married to a priest may partake of *teruma*.** If the priest **died and she had a child from him, she may** continue to **partake of *teruma*.** If she subsequently **married a Levite,** she may no longer partake of *teruma* but **she may partake of** the first **tithe** on his account. If he, too, **died and she** had **a child from him, she may** continue to **partake of tithe** on account of the child. If she then **married an Israelite, she may partake of neither *teruma* nor tithe.** If her Israelite husband **died and she** had **a child from him, she** still **may partake of neither *teruma* nor tithe.**

The second mishnah of the *daf* sets out the full range of possible status shifts for a woman over the course of multiple marriages. A *bat Yisrael* who marries a *kohen* eats *terumah*. If her husband dies and she has a son from him, she continues to eat *terumah* by virtue of that son. If she then marries a *Levi*, she eats *ma'aser*. If he too dies and she has a son from him, she can eat *ma'aser* by virtue of that son — but she has lost her access to *terumah*, since the *levi* son does not restore that privilege. If she then marries a *Yisrael*, she eats neither *terumah* nor *ma'aser*. If he dies and she has a son from him, she remains excluded from both.

### Sons' Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת בְּנָהּ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל — תֹּאכַל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. מֵת בְּנָהּ מִלֵּוִי — תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה. מֵת בְּנָהּ מִכֹּהֵן — לֹא תֹּאכַל לֹא בִּתְרוּמָה וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** If **her child from the Israelite** also **died,** while her son from the Levite remained alive, **she may partake of tithe** on account of the Levite's child. If **her child from the Levite died,** leaving her with a son from the priest, **she may** once again **partake of *teruma*.** If **her child from the priest died** as well, **she may no** longer **partake of either *teruma* or tithe.**

But the status conferred by a son persists even after the relevant marriage ends. If the son from the *Yisrael* dies, she regains her status from the *Levi* son — she may again eat *ma'aser*. If the son from the *Levi* dies, she regains her status from the *kohen* son — she may eat *terumah*. The sons function as status-anchors that outlast the marriages which produced them, and their deaths can restore her to progressively earlier entitlements. It is worth noting that this applies to a son of any age — even a minor child functions to determine the mother's eligibility.

### Bat Kohen Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּת כֹּהֵן שֶׁנִּיסֵּת לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — לֹא תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה. מֵת וְלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ בֵּן — לֹא תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה. נִיסֵּת לְלֵוִי — תֹּאכַל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. מֵת וְלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ בֵּן — תֹּאכַל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. נִיסֵּת לְכֹהֵן — תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה. מֵת וְלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ בֵּן — תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The **daughter of a priest married to an Israelite may not partake of *teruma*.** If the Israelite **died and she** has **a son from him, she may not partake of *teruma*** as long as that son is alive. If she subsequently **married a Levite she may partake of tithe.** If he **died, and she** had **a son from him, she may** still **partake of tithe.** If she subsequently **married a priest, she may partake of *teruma*.** If the priest **died and she** had **a son from him, she may partake of *teruma*.**

The mishnah then traces the corresponding path for a *bat kohen*. A *bat kohen* who marries a *Yisrael* does not eat *terumah*. If he dies with no son from him, she still does not eat *terumah*. If she then marries a *Levi*, she eats *ma'aser*. If he dies with no son from him, she continues to eat *ma'aser*. If she then marries a *kohen*, she eats *terumah*. The mishnah implies that her final marital connection carries priority: if her last husband was a *kohen*, and she has a son from that *kohen* as well as sons from earlier marriages, she eats *terumah* — the highest status — even though she also has a *ben Yisrael*.

### Reversion to Origins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת בְּנָהּ מִכֹּהֵן — לֹא תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה. מֵת בְּנָהּ מִלֵּוִי — לֹא תֹּאכַל בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. מֵת בְּנָהּ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל — חוֹזֶרֶת לְבֵית אָבִיהָ, וְעַל זוֹ נֶאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁבָה אֶל בֵּית אָבִיהָ כִּנְעוּרֶיהָ מִלֶּחֶם אָבִיהָ תֹּאכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** If **her son from the priest** also **died, she may not partake of *teruma*,** but she may partake of tithe, as she has a son from a Levite. If **her son from the Levite died, she may no** longer **partake of tithe.** If **her son from the Israelite died, she returns to her father's house** and may once again partake of *teruma*. **And with regard to this** woman, it **is stated: "And she is returned unto her father's house, as in her youth; she may eat of her father's bread"** (Leviticus 22:13).

As sons die, the woman's status reverts. If her *kohen* son dies, she can no longer eat *terumah*. If her *Levi* son dies, she can no longer eat *ma'aser*. If her *Yisrael* son dies, *chozeret le-veit aviha* — she returns to her father's house. *Beit aviha* here is understood as her tribal identity, not necessarily the physical home of her biological father; the term *beit av* refers broadly to her collective family and original tribal affiliation. Once all her sons from all her marriages have died, she reverts entirely to her original status as a *bat kohen*, and may again partake of the privileges of that station — eating as though she had never left her father's house. As was observed at the close of the shiur, this entire complex of shifting entitlements is the reason why the daughters of *kohanim* ideally marry *kohanim* — so that they never face these complications at all.
