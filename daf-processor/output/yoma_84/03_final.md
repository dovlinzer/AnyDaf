# Yoma 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Daf Setup

### Greeting & Calendar

A brief question opens the session: is there a *mitzvah* of *marbin b'simcha* on *Adar Rishon*, when it is not yet the "true" Adar? The question is left pleasantly unresolved.

### Daf Navigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנְּשָׁכוֹ כֶּלֶב שׁוֹטֶה וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּכֶלֶב שׁוֹטֶה: פִּיו פָּתוּחַ, וְרִירוֹ נוֹטֵף, וְאׇזְנָיו סְרוּחוֹת, וּזְנָבוֹ מוּנָּח עַל יַרְכוֹתָיו, וּמְהַלֵּךְ בְּצִידֵּי דְּרָכִים. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף נוֹבֵחַ וְאֵין קוֹלוֹ נִשְׁמָע. מִמַּאי הָוֵי? רַב אָמַר: נָשִׁים כַּשְׁפָנִיּוֹת מְשַׂחֲקוֹת בּוֹ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: רוּחַ רָעָה שׁוֹרָה עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught that in the case of **one whom a mad dog bit,** one does not feed him the lobe of its liver. The Gemara clarifies the concept of the mad dog. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Five signs were said about a mad dog: Its mouth is** always **open; and its saliva drips; and its ears are floppy** and do not stand up; and **its tail rests on its legs; and it walks on the edges of roads. And some say it also barks and its voice is not heard.** The Gemara asks: **From where did** the dog become mad? **Rav said: Witches play with it** and practice their magic on it, causing it to become mad. **And Shmuel said: An evil spirit rests upon it.**

Today's *daf* is 84, and we pick up at the very bottom of 83b. The *Mishnah* there reads: *mishe-nashkho kelev shoteh* — someone who was bitten by a rabid dog. The *Mishnah* has moved on from Yom Kippur to the question of when one may eat non-kosher food for a health issue — and certainly when there is a risk to life. Within that framework, a debate arose concerning what constitutes a legitimate *refuah*: specifically, the practice of feeding the lobe of the liver of a rabid dog to a person who was bitten by such a dog. Matya ben Charash permits this; the *Chachamim* do not, holding that it is not a legitimate *refuah*.

## Rabid Dog Symptoms

### Mishnah Transition

The *Mishnah*'s transition from Yom Kippur to the broader category of health emergencies sets the stage for an extended *sugya* touching on medical knowledge, legitimate remedies, and the overarching principle of *pikuach nefesh*.

### Rabies Medical Facts

Rabies, as a modern medical reality, is almost invariably fatal once the disease fully develops. Historically, the number of people who actually contracted rabies and recovered is vanishingly small — essentially one confirmed case in the past several decades, where a treatment was devised in time. The critical point is that when someone is bitten by a rabid dog, what matters halachically and medically is that treatment must begin *before* the rabies develops. In the contemporary context, that means a vaccine — a series of injections administered promptly after exposure — which prevents the virus from taking hold. The shots are not pleasant, and must be given within a specific window of time.

### Five Rabid Dog Signs

The *Gemara* introduces a *Baraita*: *Tanu Rabanan, chamishah devarim ne'emru b'kelev shoteh* — five identifying signs were stated regarding a rabid dog. Its mouth is open (*pee patuach*); its spittle drips (*v'riro notef*); its ears droop (*v'oznav srukhot*); its tail hangs between its thighs (*v'znavo munach al yarkhotav*); and it walks at the sides of roads rather than the middle (*v'mehalech b'tzidei drakhim*). Some add a sixth: it barks, but its bark cannot be heard — a silent bark (*v'yesh omrim af noveach v'ein kolo nishmah*).

### Cause of Rabies

The *Gemara* then asks: *mimai havei* — what causes a dog to become rabid? Rav says: women who practice witchcraft (*nashim kashfaniyot mesakhakot bo*) — they use the dog to demonstrate or experiment with their spells. Shmuel says: an evil spirit (*ruach ra'ah*) seizes the dog.

### Witchcraft vs Spirit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ לְמִקְטְלֵיהּ בְּדָבָר הַנִּזְרָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between these** two opinions? The Gemara answers: **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to **killing it with an object that is thrown** from a distance like an arrow rather than with one's hands. If the dog is possessed by an evil spirit, one should avoid direct contact with it.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai beiniyu* — what practical difference does it make? The answer, at the top of 84a, is *l'miktelei b'davar hanizrak* — whether one must kill the dog from a distance, by projectile. If the cause is a *ruach ra'ah*, then even coming close to the dog — without being bitten — poses a danger, because the evil spirit may transfer. If the cause is witchcraft, there is apparently no such contagion from proximity alone; one could approach without that additional risk. The *baraita* rules in accordance with Shmuel: *keshe-horgo ein horgo ela b'davar hanizrak* — when killing the dog, one must do so only by means of a thrown projectile.

### Safety Protocols

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. כְּשֶׁהוֹרְגִין אוֹתוֹ — אֵין הוֹרְגִין אוֹתוֹ אֶלָּא בְּדָבָר הַנִּזְרָק. דְּחָיֵיף בֵּיהּ — מִסְתַּכַּן, דְּנָכֵית לֵיהּ — מָיֵית. דְּחָיֵיף בֵּיהּ — מִסְתַּכַּן: מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? נִישַׁלַּח מָאנֵיהּ וְנִירְהַיט. רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ חַף בֵּיהּ חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ בְּשׁוּקָא, שַׁלְּחִינְהוּ לְמָאנֵיהּ וְרָהֵיט. אֲמַר: קִיַּימְתִּי בְּעַצְמִי ״הַחׇכְמָה תְּחַיֶּה בְעָלֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This **was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel: When one kills** a mad dog, **he should kill it only with a thrown object.** Furthermore, **one who is rubbed by a mad dog will become dangerously** ill, while **one bitten** by the dog **will die.** The Gemara asks: **What is the remedy** for **one who is rubbed by mad dog** and **becomes dangerously ill?** The Gemara answers: **Let him take off his clothing and run.** The Gemara relates: **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, was rubbed by one of these** mad dogs **in the market,** whereupon **he took off his clothing and ran. He said: I have fulfilled** the verse: **"Wisdom preserves the lives of those who have it"** (Ecclesiastes 7:12).

This ruling brings to mind a reading of the *pasuk* in Parshat Yitro: *skol yisakel o yaroh yiyareh* — Rashi interprets *yaroh yiyareh* as casting a person off a cliff, consistent with the *halachic* procedure of *skilah*. But the plain sense of the verse is a practical instruction: if someone approaches Har Sinai unlawfully, you cannot go close to stop him, so you throw stones or shoot arrows. The parallel to the rabid dog is striking — in both cases, the danger of proximity necessitates acting from a distance.

The *Gemara* then states: *d'chaif bei mistaken* — if you rub up against the rabid dog, you are in danger; *d'nachit lei mayis* — if it bites you, you will die. If contact does occur, the instruction is: *nishlach manei v'nirat* — cast off your clothes and run.

## Rabid Dog Treatment

### Killing Method

The *Gemara* confirms the ruling of Shmuel through a *baraita*: when killing a rabid dog, one does so exclusively by thrown projectile (*b'davar hanizrak*), not by approaching it directly. This may even apply under certain circumstances on Shabbat or Yom Kippur, though that specific question — related to a *sugya* in Shabbat about whether *mazikin* that are not actively pursuing you require concern — is not the focus here.

### Clothes Removal

If one has rubbed up against the rabid dog, the *Gemara* instructs: cast off your clothes immediately and run. The reasoning appears to be that the contagion — or the *ruach ra'ah* — adheres to the clothing, so removing the garments and fleeing cuts off the danger.

### Rav Huna Story

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a story: Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua once rubbed up against a rabid dog in the marketplace. He immediately cast off his clothes and ran. He then said: *kiyamti b'atzmi* — I fulfilled in myself the verse, *hachochma techaye v'aleha* — wisdom will keep its owners alive (Kohelet 7:12). Just as we learned earlier that knowing to seek sweet figs could save someone seized by *bulmos*, here too practical wisdom — knowing exactly what to do when a rabid dog makes contact — was what preserved him.

### Hyena Leather Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּנָכֵית לֵיהּ מָיֵית: מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נֵיתֵי מַשְׁכָּא דְּאַפָּא דִּיכְרָא וְנִיכְתּוֹב עֲלֵיהּ: אֲנָא פְּלָנְיָא בַּר פְּלָנִיתָא אַמַּשְׁכָּא דְּאַפָּא דִּיכְרָא כָּתֵיבְנָא עֲלָךְ: ״כַּנְתִּי כַּנְתִּי קְלֵירוֹס״. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: ״קַנְדִּי קַנְדִּי קְלוֹרוֹס, יָהּ יָהּ ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אָמֵן אָמֵן סֶלָה״. וְנִשְׁלְחִינְהוּ לְמָאנֵיהּ וְלִקְבְּרִינְהוּ בֵּי קִבְרֵי עַד תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא, וְנַפְּקִינְהוּ וְנִקְלִינְהוּ בְּתַנּוּרָא וּנְבַדְּרִינְהוּ לְקִטְמֵיהּ אַפָּרָשַׁת דְּרָכִים. וְהָנָךְ תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא, כִּי שָׁתֵי מַיָּא — לָא לִישְׁתֵּי אֶלָּא בְּגוּבְתָּא דִנְחָשָׁא, דִּילְמָא חָזֵי בָּבוּאָה דְּשֵׁידָא וְלִיסְתַּכַּן. כִּי הָא דְּאַבָּא בַּר מָרְתָא הוּא אַבָּא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי, עֲבַדָא לֵיהּ אִימֵּיהּ גּוּבְתָּא דְּדַהֲבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the *baraita*: **One bitten** by a mad dog **will die.** The Gemara asks: **What is the remedy? Abaye said: Let him bring the skin of a male hyena and write on it: I, so-and-so, son of so-and-so, am writing** this spell **about you upon the skin of a male hyena: *Kanti kanti kelirus*. And some say** he should write: ***Kandi kandi keloros*.** He then writes names of God, ***Yah*, *Yah*, Lord of Hosts, amen amen Selah. And let him take off his clothes and bury them in a cemetery for twelve months** of **the year,** after which **he should take them out, and burn them in an oven, and scatter the ashes at a crossroads. And** during **those twelve months** of **the year,** when his clothes are buried, **when he drinks water, let him drink only from a copper tube** and not from a spring, **lest he see the** image of the **demon in the water and be endangered, like** the case of **Abba bar Marta, who is** also called **Abba bar Manyumi, whose mother made him a gold tube** for this purpose.

If the dog actually bites someone, the *Gemara* asks: *mai taknatei* — what is the remedy? Abaye provides an elaborate prescription. One should obtain the leather of a male hyena (*mashka d'apa d'dichra*) and write upon it an incantation: *ana Planya bar Planisa* — I, so-and-so, son of [one's mother's name] — *amashka d'apa dichra ksivna alach* — on the leather of a male hyena I am writing upon you. This is followed by the words *kanti kanti kliros*, or according to some versions, *kandi kandi kloros*, and then: *Ya Ya Hashem Tzeva'ot, Amen Amen Sela*.

After writing this incantation on the hyena leather, one casts off one's clothes, and buries everything (*v'likbrinhu*) in a cemetery — some versions specify at a crossroads. This remains buried for twelve months. At the end of the twelve months, one digs up the clothes, burns them in an oven (*v'naklinhu b'tanura*), and scatters the ashes at a crossroads (*aparsat drachim*). That apparently concludes the *refuah*.

### Twelve-Month Protection

During those twelve months of waiting, the person remains in a state of danger. What precaution must be taken? *Ki shati maya lo lishti ela b'guvta d'nachsha* — when drinking water, one should drink only through a copper tube. The reason: *dilma chazi bavuah d'shida* — lest one see the image of a demon in the water vessel, and come to danger. This protection appears to follow Shmuel's view that the cause of the dog's rabid state is a *ruach ra'ah*, since the concern here is also demonic in nature. There are well-known *aggadot* at the beginning of Berachot that deal similarly with perceiving *mazikin* and *shedim*, and with various types of metal vessels as protection.

### Golden Tube Story

The *Gemara* adds: *ki ha d'Abba bar Marta d'hu Abba bar Minyumi* — just as in the case of Abba bar Marta, who is Abba bar Minyumi: his mother made for him a golden tube through which to drink. Whether this was specifically in response to a rabid dog bite or more generally as protection against *shedim* is unclear, but the *Gemara* cites it as a parallel illustration of the principle.

## Remedy Legitimacy

### Matya's Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד אָמַר רַבִּי מַתְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן חָשׁ בְּצַפְדִּינָא. אֲזַל גַּבַּהּ דְּהַהִיא מַטְרוֹנִיתָא, עֲבַדָא לֵיהּ מִלְּתָא חַמְשָׁא וּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא. אֲמַר לַהּ: בְּשַׁבָּת מַאי? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לָא צְרִיכַתְּ. אִי מִצְטְרִיכְנָא מַאי? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אִישְׁתְּבַע לִי דְּלָא מְגַלֵּית, אִישְׁתְּבַע: לֵאלָהָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא מְגַלֵּינָא. נְפַק דַּרְשַׁהּ בְּפִירְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna said: **And furthermore, Rabbi Matya** ben Ḥarash **said:** With regard to one who suffers pain in his throat, one may place medicine inside his mouth on Shabbat, although administering a remedy is prohibited on Shabbat. The Gemara discusses a related incident: **Rabbi Yoḥanan suffered** from the illness ***tzefidna*,** which first affects the teeth and gums and then the intestines. **He went to a certain** gentile **matron [*matronita*]** who was a well-known healer. **She prepared a medicine for him on Thursday and Friday. He said to her: What** shall I do **on Shabbat,** when I cannot come to collect the medicine from you? **She said to him: You will not need** it. He asked her: **If I do need** it, **what** shall I do? **She said to him: Swear to me that you will not reveal** the remedy; then I will tell you, and you can prepare it yourself should you need it. **He swore: To the God of the Jews, I will not reveal it.** She told him the remedy. Rabbi Yoḥanan then **went out and taught it publicly,** revealing the secret of the remedy.

The *Gemara* now turns to the broader halachic question raised by these discussions. If *pikuach nefesh* permits one to violate Shabbat, Yom Kippur, and *kashrus*, who determines what counts as a legitimate *refuah*? Matya ben Charash articulates positions on three remedies: feeding the lobe of the liver of the rabid dog, a treatment for a throat ailment, and something related to jaundice.

### Sages' Objection

The *Chachamim* object. Their formulation — *b'elu ein bahem mishum refuah* — is that certain of these remedies are not genuine therapeutic interventions. Notably, the debate seems not to be about whether the underlying illnesses are serious, but about whether the proposed treatments are actually efficacious. This raises the sharper question: to what degree does *halacha* get to weigh in on whether a *refuah* is real?

### Medical Authority

This is a striking tension. We learned earlier in the *sugya* that if a *choleh* says he needs to eat and doctors say he does not, the *choleh*'s own assessment takes precedence. Here, however, when the *choleh* says he wants to use a certain *refuah* and the *Chachamim* say it is not a genuine remedy — there appears to be room to overrule the patient. The implication is that a claim of *pikuach nefesh* cannot automatically license any claimed *refuah*; the *refuah* itself must be assessed.

This has obvious contemporary resonance. In an era of holistic medicine and alternative treatments, what is a rabbi's role in saying that a given *refuah* is not legitimate? The question remains genuinely open.

### Rambam vs Geonim

There are two major scholarly frameworks for dealing with the *Gemara*'s medical recommendations more broadly. The Rambam takes the straightforward position: the rabbis were not scientists; they knew the science of their time, and one cannot expect their medical advice — or their astronomical knowledge — to transcend the limits of what was known in their era. If we know better now, we follow current knowledge, and this requires no apology. This is the intellectually honest position, though it raises a concern for some: if we say that *Chazal* could be mistaken about scientific matters, does that open the door to questioning their reliability more broadly?

The *frummer* approach is the one associated with the *Geonim*: rather than saying the rabbis got anything wrong, one says that nature has changed — *nishtanu ha-teva*. The world is different from what it was. This formulation allows one to accept that a given remedy no longer works without conceding rabbinic error. It is a protective move, keeping the honor of *Chazal* intact while acknowledging empirical reality.

Both positions have distinguished adherents. The Rambam's view is arguably more rigorous, while the *Geonim*'s formulation is more sociologically cautious. The observation that scientists today also get things wrong, while true, does not fully resolve the underlying tension — for many people, placing the rabbis within the category of "humans limited by the knowledge of their time" feels theologically fraught.

## Yochanan Scurvy Case

### Illness & Medicine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי עֲבַדָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי: מֵי שְׂאוֹר, שֶׁמֶן זַיִת, וָמֶלַח. רַב יֵימַר אֲמַר: שְׂאוֹר גּוּפֵיהּ, שֶׁמֶן זַיִת, וָמֶלַח. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מִשְׁחָא דְּגַדְפָּא דַאֲווֹזָא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲנָא עֲבַדִי לְכוּלְּהוּ וְלָא אִיתַּסַּאי, עַד דַּאֲמַר לִי הַהוּא טַיָּיעָא: אַיְיתִי קַשְׁיָיתָא דְזֵיתָא דְּלָא מְלוֹ תִּילְתָּא, וּקְלִינְהוּ בְּנוּרָא אַמָּרָא חַדְתָּא, וְאַדְבֵּיק בְּכַכֵּי דָרֵיה. עֲבַדִי הָכִי וְאִיתַּסַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** was the medicine that **she prepared for him? Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ami, said:** It was **water** in which **leaven** was steeped, **olive oil, and salt. Rav Yeimar said:** It was **leaven itself, olive oil, and salt. Rav Ashi said:** The remedy was **fat** from the bone marrow of **a goose's wing. Abaye said: I made all of these** medicines **and was not cured** from this ailment, **until a certain Arab told me** the remedy for it: **Take olive seeds that are less than one-third** ripe, **and burn them in a fire on top of a new hoe, and stick them along the row of gums. I did this and was cured.**

The *Gemara* now relates: *Rabbi Yochanan chash bitzapidna* — Rabbi Yochanan had *tzapidna*, which appears to be what we call scurvy, a disease associated with vitamin C deficiency and characterized, as the *Gemara* will shortly make clear, by bleeding gums that can progress to involve the throat and inner intestines. Rabbi Yochanan went to a *matronisa* — a non-Jewish woman of standing, apparently a herbalist or physician of sorts — who prepared a medicine for him on Thursday and Friday. Several recipes are later attributed to this treatment: one tradition attributes it to Rav Acha the son of Rav Ami, who says it consisted of *mei se'or* (yeast water), olive oil, and salt; Rav Yeimar says not the water from dough but the actual dough itself combined with olive oil and salt; Rav Ashi says it was the fat from the wing of a goose (*mishcha d'gadpa d'avaza*). Abaye adds that he personally tried all of those remedies and none healed him — until an Arab told him to take incompletely ripened olive pits, burn them on a new shovel, and apply the resulting paste directly to the gums. That, Abaye says, actually worked.

### Oath Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִישְׁתְּבַע לַהּ! לֵאלָהָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָא מְגַלֵּינָא. הָא לְעַמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל — מְגַלֵּינָא. וְהָא אִיכָּא חִלּוּל הַשֵּׁם! דִּמְגַלֵּי לַהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is surprised at this: **But he swore to her** that he would not reveal it. The Gemara answers that in his vow he declared: **I will not reveal it to the God of the Jews.** However, his words imply: **I will reveal it to His people, the Jews.** The Gemara asks: **Still, there is a desecration of God's name,** as the matron now thinks that a great man of Rabbi Yoḥanan's stature broke his vow. The Gemara answers: **He revealed** it **to her at the outset.** As soon as she revealed the remedy to him, he told her that his vow would not prevent him from publicizing the remedy.

Rabbi Yochanan, having received two days of treatment, asked the *matronisa*: what should he do on Shabbat, when he cannot travel to her? She replied that he would not need further treatment. He pressed: but what if he does? She was unwilling to divulge her formula — the remedy was her professional secret. She said: *ishtaba li d'lo maglis* — take an oath to me that you will not reveal this. He took an oath: *ishtaba leh l'elaha d'yisrael lo maglina* — by the God of Israel, I will not reveal it.

He then went to the public lecture that Shabbat and, rather than giving his regular *drashah* on the *parashah*, he disclosed the entire remedy to the community.

### Oath Solution

The *Gemara* asks: *v'ha ishtaba lah* — but did he not take an oath? How could he publicly reveal the remedy? The *Gemara* resolves this with a deft reading: *l'elaha d'yisrael lo maglina ha l'ama d'yisrael maglina* — his oath said he would not reveal it *to* the God of Israel; he revealed it *to* the people of Israel. He had constructed the wording of his oath in a way that afforded him this exit.

### Chilul Hashem

But the *Gemara* objects: *v'ika chillul Hashem* — even if technically he did not violate his oath, he is creating a *chillul Hashem*. From the outside, it looks to non-Jews as though he swore by God and then betrayed that oath anyway — both that he deceived the *matronisa* and that he does not respect the sanctity of an oath taken in God's name. This is *chillul Hashem* in the precise sense: God's name was invoked in an oath, and the appearance is that the oath was broken.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *d'magli lah mei-ikara* — after she disclosed the remedy and before he revealed it publicly, he explained to her exactly what his oath had meant. She therefore did not believe he violated the oath. Whether she believed instead that she had been cleverly maneuvered into disclosing a trade secret is another matter — the *Gemara* acknowledges this is a different kind of *chillul Hashem*, one of a lesser or different category. The story is, as noted, fascinating and somewhat unsettling. It raises questions that parallel modern debates about whistleblowing and the disclosure of classified information for the public good — how far one may go, and what costs one may impose on others, for the sake of communal welfare.

According to Artscroll, some *Amoraim* say the *matronisa* was so distressed that her exclusive remedy was now freely available that she took her own life; others say she converted to Judaism, having been so impressed that Rabbi Yochanan shared the cure without charging for it.

## Scurvy Treatments

### Remedy Options

As noted above, the *Gemara* records three competing traditions about what the *matronisa*'s medicine actually consisted of: Rav Acha the son of Rav Ami specifies yeast water, olive oil, and salt; Rav Yeimar specifies the dough itself (rather than the water runoff) combined with olive oil and salt; Rav Ashi specifies the fat of a goose wing. None of these obviously addresses a vitamin C deficiency.

### Olive Pit Cure

Abaye testifies in the first person: he tried all of these remedies without success, until an Arab advised him to take incompletely ripened olive pits — *kashyata d'zeita d'lo malu tilta* — burn them on a new shovel, apply the resulting substance to the gums (*b'kakei d'dari*), and that cured him.

### Scurvy Causes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי הֲוָה? מֵחַמִּימֵי חַמִּימֵי דְּחִיטֵּי, וּמִשִּׁיּוּרֵי כָּסָא דְהַרְסָנָא. וּמַאי סִימָנֵיהּ — כַּד רָמֵי מִידֵּי בְּכַכֵּיהּ וְאָתֵא דְּמָא מִבֵּי דָרֵי. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן כִּי חָשׁ בְּצַפְדִּינָא, עֲבַד הָכִי בְּשַׁבְּתָא וְאִיתַּסִּי. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? אֲמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: שָׁאנֵי צַפְדִּינָא הוֹאִיל וּמַתְחִיל בַּפֶּה, וְגוֹמֵר בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **From where** does **this** disease *tzefidna* come? It is from eating **wheat bread that is too hot and fish remains fried in oil. What is the sign of** this sickness? **When one puts something between his teeth, blood comes out from his gums. When Rabbi Yoḥanan suffered from *tzefidna*, he prepared this** medicine described above **on Shabbat and was cured.** The Gemara asks: **And how did Rabbi Yoḥanan prepare this** medicine on Shabbat for an ailment which affects only the gums but is not life-threatening? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: *Tzefidna* is different, since it does** indeed **begin in the mouth** and appears to be an illness of the teeth, **but it ends up in the intestines** and is dangerous.

The *Gemara* also identifies the causes of *tzapidna*: eating overly hot wheaten bread (*mechamimi chamimi d'cheiti*) and consuming leftover fish hash (*mishiyurei kasa d'harsana*). Whether this *tzapidna* corresponds precisely to what we call scurvy remains uncertain — the identification is based on the symptom pattern described.

### Scurvy Symptoms

The symptom the *Gemara* identifies is: *kad rami midi b'kakei v'ata dama* — when you put something between your teeth and blood comes out. Bleeding gums is the presenting sign. As the *Gemara* will now clarify, however, this is only the beginning — the disease progresses from the gums downward.

## Throat & Life Risk

### Scurvy Progression

The *Gemara* establishes that *tzapidna* is not merely a localized oral condition: *ho'il u-maschil b'peh v'gomer bivnei me'ayim* — it begins in the mouth and ends in the inner intestines. This progression through the throat and into the internal organs is what makes it potentially life-threatening.

### Throat Legitimacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי מַתְיָא בֶּן חָרָשׁ, דְּאָמַר: הַחוֹשֵׁשׁ בְּפִיו מְטִילִין לוֹ סַם בְּשַׁבָּת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר בְּזוֹ, וְלֹא בְּאַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: In accordance with whose** opinion did you do this? Was it not in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash, who said:** In the case of **one who suffers pain in his mouth, one puts medicine in his** mouth **on Shabbat,** which is a minority opinion? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** It is so, **but I say** the Sages agreed with him about taking medicine **in this** case alone, **but no other.** If so, with regard to medicine on Shabbat, the view of Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash is not a minority opinion.

Rav Chiya bar Abba raises a question to Rabbi Yochanan: when Rabbi Yochanan violated Shabbat to treat his *tzapidna*, was he acting in accordance with Rabbi Matya ben Charash — *k'Rabbi Masya ben Charash* — who holds that a throat ailment permits taking medicine on Shabbat? If so, is that not a position disputed by the *Chachamim*? How could Rabbi Yochanan simply rely on it?

### Debate Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: מִי שֶׁאֲחָזוֹ יֵרָקוֹן — מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ בְּשַׂר חֲמוֹר, מִי שֶׁנְּשָׁכוֹ כֶּלֶב שׁוֹטֶה — מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ מֵחֲצַר כָּבֵד שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהַחוֹשֵׁשׁ בְּפִיו — מְטִילִין לוֹ סַם בְּשַׁבָּת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מַתְיָא בֶּן חָרָשׁ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בְּאֵילּוּ אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם רְפוּאָה. ״בְּאֵלּוּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? מַאי לָאו, לְמַעוֹטֵי סַם?
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say that** this *baraita* **supports him:** With regard to **one who is seized with *yerakon*, one feeds him donkey meat** as medicine; with regard to **one whom a mad dog bit, one feeds him the lobe of its liver;** in the case of **one who has pain in his mouth, one puts medicine in his** mouth **on Shabbat;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash. And the Rabbis say: These have no** value **as a remedy.** The Rabbis used the term **these, to exclude what? What, is it not to exclude** this **medicine** for *tzefidna*, which the Rabbis agree is permitted on Shabbat?

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *shani omer d'zu v'lo acheres* — I say that the rabbis argue with Matya ben Charash on only one of his positions, not the other. Matya ben Charash made two claims: one regarding the liver of the rabid dog, and one regarding the throat. The *Chachamim* dispute only the rabid dog case; on the throat, they actually agree. Rabbi Yochanan's conduct on Shabbat was therefore not controversial — the treatment of a throat ailment that progresses internally is something all agree constitutes *pikuach nefesh*.

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי: מַקִּיזִין דָּם לִסְרוֹנְכֵי. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא. דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי שֶׁשָּׁמַע מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מַתְיָא בֶּן חָרָשׁ: מַקִּיזִין דָּם לִסְרוֹנְכֵי בְּשַׁבָּת, וּמִי שֶׁנְּשָׁכוֹ כֶּלֶב שׁוֹטֶה — מַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ מֵחֲצַר כָּבֵד שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהַחוֹשֵׁשׁ בְּפִיו מְטִילִין לוֹ סַם בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No, it excludes** a different remedy, which Rabbi Matya suggests: **Bloodletting** to heal the ailment ***serunkhi*** is permitted on Shabbat. The Gemara comments: **So too, this is reasonable** to say, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said three things that he heard in the name of Rabbi Matya ben Ḥarash: One** may **let blood for *serunkhi* on Shabbat; and** in the case of **one whom a mad dog bit, one feeds him the lobe of its liver; and** in the case of **one who has pain in his mouth, one puts medicine in his** mouth **on Shabbat.**

The *Gemara* seeks to identify more precisely which remedy the *Chachamim* concede to Matya ben Charash. It introduces a *baraita*: Rav bar Shmuel taught — *ubarah she-hericha ma'achilin otah ad she-tashuv nafshah* — a pregnant woman who smells food and craves it is fed until she is settled; *mi she-nashacho kelev shoteh ma'achilin oto mechatzar keved shelo* — someone bitten by a rabid dog is fed the lobe of the liver; *v'ha-choshesh b'piv matilin lo sam b'Shabbat* — and for a throat ailment, medicine is administered on Shabbat. This is the position of Matya ben Charash. And the *Chachamim* say: *b'zo v'lo b'acheret* — in this one, and not in the other.

The *Gemara* asks: *b'zu a-hei* — which case are they conceding? Surely not the pregnant woman — that one is obvious and requires no special concession. And we know they dispute the rabid dog. Therefore, *ela lav a-sam* — it must be that they are conceding the medicine for the throat ailment. *Shema mina* — this supports the reading that the *Chachamim* agree regarding the throat.

This also illuminates an important general principle: what kinds of illness does *halacha* recognize as potentially *pikuach nefesh*? While the primary answer is to follow the assessment of doctors, the *Gemara* also provides its own parameters. One of those parameters is that anything from the gums inward — any condition that begins in the mouth and can progress into the body — is treated as potentially life-threatening. This is an important category in *pikuach nefesh* analysis.

There is also the separate question of the rabbinic prohibition on taking medicine on Shabbat for minor ailments. The *Gemara* grounds this prohibition in a concern that one might come to grind medicines. The result is a taxonomy: *pikuach nefesh* overrides all prohibitions, including biblical ones; *choleh kol gufo* — a person whose whole body is ill, or who is severely affected even by a localized condition — permits rabbinic violations including *amira l'akum* and in many views even direct *d'Rabbanan* violations; the category of *mishush b'alma* — a minor, localized irritation — is where the prohibition on taking medicine applies. Many *poskim*, however, interpret *mishush b'alma* extremely narrowly and are prepared to classify most genuine illness within the *choleh kol gufo* category. A famous formulation attributed to Rav Soloveitchik captures this: if you truly need the medicine, you are a *choleh kol gufo* and it is permitted; if you do not truly need it, then it is not really medicine, and it is also permitted.

## Pikuach Nefesh

### Life Overrides Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת הוּא וְכוּ׳. לְמָה לִי תּוּ לְמֵימַר: ״וְכׇל סְפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת דּוֹחֶה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא סָפֵק שַׁבָּת זוֹ בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ סָפֵק שַׁבָּת אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that one with pain in his throat should be given medicine on Shabbat **because** it is a case of **uncertainty** concerning a **life-threatening** situation. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need to say **furthermore: And any** case of **uncertainty** concerning a **life-threatening** situation **overrides Shabbat? Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: They stated** this **not only** in a case where there is **uncertainty** with regard to **this Shabbat, but even** if the **uncertainty** is with regard to **a different** future **Shabbat.**

The *Mishnah* states the principle: *mipnei she-safek nefashot hu* — because it involves a risk to life — *v'chol safek nefashot docheh et ha-Shabbat* — and any doubt of life overrides Shabbat. The *Gemara* asks: why is it necessary to state this explicitly? It has been assumed throughout. Rav Yehuda says in the name of Rav: the statement is teaching something additional — *lo safek Shabbat zo bilvad amru ella afilu safek Shabbat acheret* — not only is the concern for this Shabbat sufficient to override it, but even concern for a future Shabbat is sufficient.

### Multi-Day Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן דְּאַמְדוּהּ לִתְמָנְיָא יוֹמֵי, וְיוֹמָא קַמָּא שַׁבְּתָא. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לִיעַכַּב עַד לְאוּרְתָּא כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נֵיחוּל עֲלֵיהּ תְּרֵי שַׁבָּתָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances** in which uncertainty would arise as to whether or not his life will be in danger in the future? They are a case **where** doctors **assess** that an ill person needs a certain treatment **for eight days, and the first day** of his illness **is Shabbat. Lest you say: He should wait until evening** and begin his treatment after Shabbat **so they will not need to desecrate two *Shabbatot* for his sake,** therefore **it teaches us** that one must immediately desecrate Shabbat for his sake. This is the *halakha*, despite the fact that an additional Shabbat will be desecrated as a result, because there is uncertainty about whether his life is in danger.

The practical case: suppose doctors determine that a patient requires treatment over eight consecutive days, and the first day is Shabbat. One might reason: let us wait until Saturday night to begin, thereby avoiding two Shabbat violations instead of one. Since there is no immediate mortal urgency — the patient is not going to die in the next few hours — perhaps delay is reasonable. The *Gemara* teaches: no. *Kamashmalan* — the teaching comes to tell us that if the person is in a *pikuach nefesh* situation, you act immediately. You do not make *cheshbonos* to push off treatment until after Shabbat, even when the urgency is not acute.

### Heated Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מְחַמִּין חַמִּין לַחוֹלֶה בְּשַׁבָּת, בֵּין לְהַשְׁקוֹתוֹ בֵּין לְהַבְרוֹתוֹ. וְלֹא שַׁבָּת זוֹ בִּלְבַד אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא לְשַׁבָּת אַחֶרֶת. וְאֵין אוֹמְרִים: נַמְתִּין לוֹ שֶׁמָּא יַבְרִיא, אֶלָּא מְחַמִּין לוֹ מִיָּד, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּפֵק נְפָשׁוֹת דּוֹחֶה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. וְלֹא סָפֵק שַׁבָּת זוֹ, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ סָפֵק שַׁבָּת אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: **One heats water for an ill person on Shabbat, whether to give him to drink or to wash him,** since it might help him recover. **And they did not say** it is permitted to desecrate **only** the **current Shabbat** for him, **but even a different,** future **Shabbat. And one must not say: Let us wait** and perform this labor **for him** after Shabbat, **perhaps he will get well** in the meantime. **Rather, one heats it for him immediately because any** case of **uncertainty** concerning a **life-threatening** situation **overrides Shabbat. And** this is so **not** only with regard to **uncertainty** whether his life is in danger **on the current Shabbat, but even** in a case of **uncertainty** with regard to danger **on a different Shabbat.**

A *baraita* reinforces this: *mechammin chammin l'choleh b'Shabbat* — one heats water for a sick person on Shabbat — *bein l'hashkoto* — whether to drink — *bein l'havruto* — whether to bathe him and restore his strength. And not only for this Shabbat, but for an ongoing course of treatment that extends to the next Shabbat as well.

### All Health Needs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ, לֹא עַל יְדֵי גּוֹיִם, וְלֹא עַל יְדֵי כּוּתִיִּים, אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי גְּדוֹלֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. וְאֵין אוֹמְרִין יֵעָשׂוּ דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ, לֹא עַל פִּי נָשִׁים, וְלֹא עַל פִּי כּוּתִיִּים, אֲבָל מִצְטָרְפִין לְדַעַת אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And these acts should not be performed by gentiles or Samaritans but** should be done **by the greatest of the Jewish people,** i.e., their scholars, who know how to act properly. **And one does not say: These actions** may **be performed** based on the advice **of women or Samaritans,** since they are not considered experts able to declare a person ill enough to override Shabbat. **However,** the opinions of **these** people **do combine with an additional opinion,** meaning that if there is a dispute, their opinions may be considered when coming to a decision.

The principle at work here is *osin kol tzorchei choleh b'Shabbat* — one performs all the health needs of the sick person on Shabbat. Once a person is in the status of *pikuach nefesh*, you do not evaluate each individual act by asking: will his life be at risk if I fail to do precisely this? You take care of all of his health needs. The question of whether the person will die if not bathed need not be posed — he is *pikuach nefesh*, and all that pertains to his care follows from that status. This is a critically important principle.

### Immediacy Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְפַקְּחִין פִּקּוּחַ נֶפֶשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְהַזָּרִיז הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לִיטּוֹל רְשׁוּת מִבֵּית דִּין. הָא כֵּיצַד? רָאָה תִּינוֹק שֶׁנָּפַל לַיָּם — פּוֹרֵשׂ מְצוּדָה וּמַעֲלֵהוּ, וְהַזָּרִיז הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לִיטּוֹל רְשׁוּת מִבֵּית דִּין. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא צָיֵיד כְּווֹרֵי. רָאָה תִּינוֹק שֶׁנָּפַל לְבוֹר — עוֹקֵר חוּלְיָא וּמַעֲלֵהוּ, וְהַזָּרִיז הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לִיטּוֹל רְשׁוּת מִבֵּית דִּין. אַף עַל גַּב דִּמְתַקֵּן דַּרְגָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One engages** in **saving a life on Shabbat, and one who is vigilant** to do so **is praiseworthy. And one need not take permission from a court** but hurries to act on his own. **How so?** If **one sees a child who fell into the sea, he spreads** a fisherman's **net and raises him** from the water. **And one who is vigilant** and acts quickly **is praiseworthy, and one need not seek permission from a court, although** in doing so **he catches fish** in the net as well. Similarly, if **one sees a child fall into a pit** and the child cannot get out, **he digs part of the ground out** around the edge of the pit to create a makeshift step **and raises him out. And one who is vigilant** and acts quickly **is praiseworthy, and one need not seek permission from a court, although in doing so he fashions a step.**

*V'ein mamtinim lo shema yavri* — one does not wait and say: perhaps he will recover on his own and not need us to heat the water. Rather, *ella mechammin lo miyad* — you heat it immediately. There is no hesitation, no waiting game, no calculation that perhaps the situation will resolve itself.

### No Delegation

*V'ein osin devarim halalu lo al yedei nochrim v'lo al yedei Kutim* — these things are not done through non-Jews or through minors — *ella al yedei gedolei Yisrael* — but by Jewish adults. One does not seek to minimize the Shabbat violation by handing the task off to someone else. Jewish adults perform the necessary acts directly.

### Tosafot Rationale

Why not delegate to a non-Jew, even when the non-Jew could perform the task equally well? Tosafot explains: *shema yitatzel ha-nochri v'lo ya'aseh v'yavo lidei sakana* — perhaps the non-Jew will be negligent and not act with sufficient urgency, and danger will result. The underlying logic is that the person who has genuinely internalized the principle of *pikuach nefesh* — who understands that Shabbat is being set aside precisely because this is an emergency — is the one who will act with the requisite sense of urgency. A non-Jew or a minor may not fully appreciate the gravity of the situation in the same way.

### Policy Message

There is, however, a second and perhaps more fundamental reason. Even in cases where the non-Jew would perform the task with equal urgency, seeking to delegate sends the wrong message. If people observe that one is trying to find workarounds — trying to get a non-Jew or a minor to do it — the implicit message conveyed is that *chilul Shabbat* for *pikuach nefesh* is somehow problematic, something to be minimized or avoided if possible. That impression, once planted, leads to hesitation in the future — to people calculating and delaying when they should be acting immediately. The sociological message must be unambiguous: there is no hesitation, no minimization, and no delegation. A Jewish adult acts without pause.

This same logic is applied by the Rosh in a related context. The text appears to suggest that the medical opinions of women should not be acted upon in isolation. The Rosh objects that there is no reason a woman's medical knowledge should be less reliable than a man's. He offers an alternative reading: the instruction is not to say "let it be done by women" — meaning, do not deliberately delegate to women in order to avoid the men having to act. That delegation would convey exactly the same wrong message as delegating to a non-Jew: it suggests that the men want to find someone else to take on the violation, implying that it is something improper. The Rosh's interpretation is understood by many authorities as the operative *halachic* position.

## Pregnancy Discussion

### Labor Stages

The discussion of *pikuach nefesh* naturally raises the question of pregnancy and labor on Shabbat. At what stage does the situation rise to the level of *pikuach nefesh*? The very first example in the *Baraita* we encountered was a pregnant woman who craves food — clearly that situation requires response. But the onset of labor involves a process with varying stages of urgency, and the question of when to act is not always straightforward. Early contractions may not yet represent the stage at which one is clearly in a *pikuach nefesh* situation; that assessment depends on the specifics.

### Dollar Test

A practical heuristic offered: if someone proposes paying for a cab so that the husband need not drive on Shabbat, and the husband would readily accept that offer, it suggests the situation has not yet reached the level of true urgency. If, on the other hand, he would decline — insisting on driving himself — that instinct reflects an internalized sense of genuine emergency, and he should indeed drive. The test probes not whether Shabbat technically permits the action, but whether the actor's own sense of urgency is calibrated correctly.

This heuristic, however, applies only to the ambiguous early stages of a process that may take considerable time to develop. It emphatically does not apply to sudden accidents or clearly acute emergencies.

### Accidents Rule

In the case of an immediate accident or acute emergency, the entire framework of calculation is inapplicable. One does not pause, does not assess, does not seek alternatives. The instruction throughout the *sugya* has been unequivocal: when there is an immediate danger, act without hesitation. The *zariz* — the zealous one — is the one who is praised.

## Non-Jews Pikuach

### Mishnah Position

The *Mishnah* states that if a building collapses and one knows the person buried underneath is a non-Jew, one does not violate Shabbat to rescue him. This is the *Mishnah*'s plain position, and it raises an obvious and serious question: is this really the operative *halacha*?

### HaMeiri View

The *HaMeiri* offers a radical reinterpretation. His view is that the *Mishnah* was speaking only about pagans of antiquity — people who were morally depraved, who had no ethical code, and whose societal framework was fundamentally hostile. In the context of civilized non-Jews who live by moral standards, *chilul Shabbat* to save their lives is not only permitted but required. This position reshapes the entire category and is obviously attractive from the standpoint of moral principle. It does, however, represent a substantial departure from the *Gemara*'s framing.

### Rav Moshe Approach

Rav Moshe Feinstein takes a different approach, one that achieves a similar practical result without altering the underlying categories. He argues that one may always be *mechallel Shabbat* to save the life of a non-Jew because failing to do so indirectly endangers Jewish lives — it generates *eivah* and hostility that translates into real risk. On this view, the act is technically permitted as *pikuach nefesh* for Jews, even though it operates indirectly. Rav Moshe was emphatic, however, that this reasoning applies even when one believes no one will find out about a failure to respond — because the concern about *eivah* and its eventual consequences always exists.

### Hatzalah Practice

The principle on which Hatzalah responds to calls on Shabbat from non-Jewish patients is broadly this framework of Rav Moshe. However, there are concerning reports that certain Hatzalah units — not, it is emphasized, the one in Riverdale — have at times made a *cheshbon* about the likelihood of their non-response becoming known, and have allowed that calculation to influence whether they respond to a given call. This represents precisely the kind of problematic reasoning that Rav Moshe sought to foreclose. The very act of making such a *cheshbon* is itself a distortion of the principle.

### Lieberman Story

A story is told of Professor Saul Lieberman, who was walking on Shabbat with a non-Jewish philosopher colleague, in the presence of some Jewish Theological Seminary students. The colleague asked: if that building were to fall on me right now, would you violate Shabbat to save my life? Lieberman said: of course. The colleague pressed: would you do it because it is the *halacha*, or because of *darchei shalom*? Lieberman replied: because it is the *halacha*. The colleague walked away satisfied. The students turned to Lieberman and asked what he meant — how was it the *halacha*? He replied: I told him that for *darchei shalom*.

### Opinion Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאָה שֶׁנִּנְעֲלָה דֶּלֶת בִּפְנֵי תִּינוֹק — שׁוֹבְרָהּ וּמוֹצִיאוֹ, וְהַזָּרִיז הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לִיטּוֹל רְשׁוּת מִבֵּית דִּין. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא מִיכַּוֵּין לְמִיתְבַּר בְּשִׁיפֵי. מְכַבִּין וּמַפְסִיקִין מִפְּנֵי הַדְּלֵיקָה בְּשַׁבָּת, וְהַזָּרִיז הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לִיטּוֹל רְשׁוּת מִבֵּית דִּין. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא מְמַכֵּיךְ מַכּוֹכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if **one sees that a door is locked before a child** and the child is scared and crying, **he breaks the door and takes the child out. And one who is vigilant** and acts quickly **is praiseworthy, and one need not seek permission from a court, although he intends to break** it **into boards** to be used later. Similarly, **one** may **extinguish a fire** by **placing a barrier** of metal or clay vessels filled with water **in front of** it **on Shabbat** when life is endangered. **And one who is vigilant** and acts quickly **is praiseworthy, and one need not seek permission from a court, although he leaves the coals,** which can be used for cooking after Shabbat.

The *Gemara* states that these emergency acts on Shabbat should not be performed based on the word of women, Samaritans, or non-Jews alone — *ella mitztarfin l'daas acheres* — but they may combine their testimony with another opinion. According to Rashi, this means that if a woman, *Kuti*, or non-Jew says the situation is *pikuach nefesh* and no one else corroborates it, one does not act on that basis alone; however, her testimony can supplement other evidence.

### Rosh on Women

The Rosh finds this reading troubling. He argues: why should a woman with genuine medical knowledge be considered less reliable than a man? There is no basis for discounting her expertise. He therefore offers an alternative interpretation that effectively says the opposite of Rashi: even if a woman's assessment goes against the prevailing male opinion, if she says it is *pikuach nefesh*, one listens to her. This reading is linguistically strained, but the Rosh presses it.

### Women Delegation

Alternatively — and this is the reading that many authorities adopt as operative *halacha* — the Rosh reads the passage as a sociological instruction rather than an evidentiary one. The text is saying: do not arrange for it to be done *by* women — meaning, do not deliberately designate women as the ones to perform the Shabbat violation. This would send the signal, as with non-Jews and minors, that the men are seeking to avoid the act themselves, implying it is something improper. The instruction is not about the credibility of women's medical opinions, but about the public message conveyed by deliberate delegation. Just as one should not say "let the non-Jew handle it" or "let the minor do it," one should not say "let the women take care of it" — because all of these strategies communicate hesitation and undermine the unconditional norm that must be internalized.

## Specific Cases

### Praise for Zeal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְפַקְּחִין פִּקּוּחַ נֶפֶשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְהַזָּרִיז הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לִיטּוֹל רְשׁוּת מִבֵּית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One engages** in **saving a life on Shabbat, and one who is vigilant** to do so **is praiseworthy. And one need not take permission from a court** but hurries to act on his own.

The *Gemara* closes with a *Tanu Rabanan* passage: *pikuach nefesh b'Shabbat, ha-zariz harei zeh meshubach* — in matters of *pikuach nefesh* on Shabbat, the zealous one is praised. The parallel in the Yerushalmi adds: the one who delays is as one who sheds blood. *V'ein tzarich litol reshut mi-Beit Din* — one does not need to go and ask a *Beit Din* or a rabbi for permission. Act.

### Ocean Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן יָם — מִשּׁוּם דְּאַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי אָזֵל לֵיהּ, אֲבָל בּוֹר, דְּקָא יָתֵיב — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** to teach these examples, since each one suggests an original idea. **As, had it taught us** the *halakha* of the child who fell into **the sea,** we would have said: He must act quickly in that case **because in the meantime,** if he delays, the child **will be swept away** by the waves and disappear, and therefore the rescuer need not seek permission; **but** in the case of a child who fell into **a pit, who remains** there and is in no further danger, one might **say** the rescuer need not hurry but should request permission from the court first. Therefore, the *baraita* explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to tell us that case, too.

*Ra'ah tinok she-nafal l'yam* — if one sees an infant fall into the ocean, *pores metsuda u-ma'alehu* — one spreads a net and lifts him out. The zealous one is praised; no rabbinic consultation is needed. And this applies *af al gav de-tzayid kveirei* — even though in spreading the net one incidentally catches fish. Do not pause to figure out a method of rescue that avoids the incidental violation. The child comes first.

### Well Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בּוֹר — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מִיבְּעִית, אֲבָל נִנְעֲלָה דֶּלֶת, אֶפְשָׁר דְּיָתֵיב בְּהַאי גִּיסָא וּמְשַׁבֵּישׁ לֵיהּ בְּאַמְגּוֹזֵי. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it had taught us the** case of the **pit,** one might have thought it is **because the child is scared** at being trapped; **but when a door is locked** before a child, **it is possible to sit on the other side** of the door **and amuse him with** the sound of **nuts** until Shabbat is over. Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach that in this case, too, one does not delay but acts immediately because a life is possibly in danger.

*Ra'ah tinok she-nafal l'bor* — if an infant falls into a well, *oker hulya u-ma'alehu* — one digs out a clump of dirt to create footing and bring the child up, even *af al gav de-metaken darga* — even though by doing so one is effectively constructing steps within the pit. Again, do not hesitate.

### Locked Room Case

If a child is locked in a room, *shovro u-motzi'o* — one breaks down the door and brings the child out. This is *pikuach nefesh* because the child may be frightened to the point of mortal danger. *V'ein tzalich litol reshut mi-Beit Din.* And this is so even *af al gav de-kamkhavein l'mitbar bi-shifei* — even if as one breaks down the door one intends to keep the wood chips as kindling for after Shabbat. The intent to benefit from the incidental result does not negate the permissibility of the act.

### Fire Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מְכַבִּין וּמַפְסִיקִין מִפְּנֵי הַדְּלֵיקָה בְּשַׁבָּת, וְהַזָּרִיז הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לִיטּוֹל רְשׁוּת מִבֵּית דִּין. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא מְמַכֵּיךְ מַכּוֹכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **one** may **extinguish a fire** by **placing a barrier** of metal or clay vessels filled with water **in front of** it **on Shabbat** when life is endangered. **And one who is vigilant** and acts quickly **is praiseworthy, and one need not seek permission from a court, although he leaves the coals,** which can be used for cooking after Shabbat.

*Mekhabin u-mafsikin mipnei ha-dleika b'Shabbat* — one extinguishes a fire on Shabbat and positions vessels as a firewall to contain it, without hesitation and without asking a *Beit Din*. This applies *af al gav de-kamakhreikh* — even though in doing so one creates hot embers that one might want to use for cooking after Shabbat.

### Hesitation Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לְמָה לִי? דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְחָצֵר אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in a *baraita* that **one** may **extinguish a fire** by **placing a barrier** in front of it on Shabbat. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **this?** What new point is taught by this additional case of a life-endangering situation? The Gemara answers: This *halakha* applies **even** if the fire is spreading **toward another courtyard.** Not only may this be done to save the lives of people in the courtyard on fire; it may also be done to prevent the fire from spreading to an adjacent courtyard.

The *Gemara* asks: why is it necessary to state all of these cases? Each teaches something that might not be obvious from the others. The ocean case teaches urgency: one might think, do not wait to consult — but perhaps the reason is simply that the baby will float away. The well case addresses a possible counterargument: the child in the well is not going anywhere (*ka yatvi*), so perhaps there is time to ask a *she'elah* first. The *Gemara* answers: no. The child is terrified at the bottom of a well, and that fear itself may be life-threatening. Act immediately.

### Locked Room Urgency

The locked room is the most challenging case. One might reason: *efshar d'yateiv b'hai gisa u'mishabesh lei b'amgozi* — perhaps someone can sit on the other side of the door and entertain the child with nuts, calming him until Shabbat ends. How genuine a *pikuach nefesh* is this? The *Gemara* answers: *tzericha* — it is necessary to state this case precisely because one might have thought it does not qualify. Nevertheless, it does. Even this scenario — a child in a locked room, theoretically manageable through distraction — constitutes *pikuach nefesh*, and one breaks down the door immediately, without calculation, without delay, and without asking anyone's permission.
