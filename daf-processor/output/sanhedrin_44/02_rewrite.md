# Sanhedrin 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Achan & Confession

### Daf Overview

The daf begins at the top of 44a with the *mishnah* that speaks about encouraging a condemned person to perform *viduy* — confession — before execution. The narrative of *Achan* serves as the biblical basis for this practice: he offered a confession before being put to death by *Yehoshua*. As we saw in the previous daf, *Yehoshua* engaged in *shichto bidvarim* — he encouraged, even enticed, *Achan* to confess, apparently under the impression that doing so might save him.

### Viduy & Atonement

This dynamic raises an important theological question about the nature of *viduy*. There is no doubt that a central dimension of the practice is the principle of *mita hi kappara* — that death itself serves as atonement. The *beis din*, in this framework, functions as God's agent. The act of execution is not merely a legal sanction but a vehicle for the condemned person's spiritual rectification. Encouraging confession, then, is an act of genuine concern for the person's soul.

### Public vs. Private

Yet there is simultaneously a public dimension to *viduy* that cannot be ignored. The *beis din* must also be seen to be acting correctly. One might compare — though emphatically not equate — the forced confessions extracted by totalitarian governments, which serve to exonerate the regime rather than benefit the accused. The concept of *shichto bidvarim* becomes more complex if the sole purpose of confession were the condemned person's personal *kaparah*: the public acknowledgment carries its own institutional weight, making clear to all observers that justice is being done transparently and legitimately.

### Confession & Hatra'ah

This public dimension parallels, in a certain sense, the requirement of *hatra'ah* — the formal warning given before a transgression. The formula *ad she-yatir atzmo la-mitah*, that the perpetrator must "release himself to death," *al menas ken*, on the condition of accepting the consequence, means that the perpetrator actively acknowledges and accepts the death penalty upon himself. This, too, addresses the deeper question of how a *beis din* can be justified in carrying out a violent act against another person. Part of the answer is *ki ha-mishpat l'Elokim* — the court represents divine justice. But part of the answer is also that the person himself, through his knowing and willing acceptance, has in a real sense consented to what follows. *Viduy* and *hatra'ah* thus function in parallel as structures through which both the perpetrator's inner acceptance and the court's outer legitimacy are established.

## Five Charamim

### Five Instances

The *gemara* at the bottom of 43b opens a broader inquiry into *Achan*'s sin. Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon, enumerates *chamisha* — five instances of *mo'al b'charamim*, of taking benefit from property that had been placed under a *cherem* (ban). Four of these occurred during the time of *Moshe*, when various battles were fought and property was explicitly declared *cherem*. One occurred in the time of *Yehoshua*, and that is *Achan*'s sin. The derivation of the number five comes from the verse in which God reveals *Achan*'s guilt: *anochi chatasi v'chazos v'chazos asisi* — "I have sinned, and done such and such." The doubled *chazos* yields four, plus the instance already established, arriving at five. *Tosafos* notes that one could arithmetically derive an even higher number from the language, but since there were not that many relevant wars, the count cannot exceed five.

### Delayed Punishment

This enumeration raises an immediate question: if *Achan* sinned during the period of *Moshe*, why was he not punished then? More precisely — and this is the correction that sharpens the question — why were the *Jewish people* not punished collectively during *Moshe*'s time? The deaths at the battle of *Ha-Ai* represent a form of collective punishment visited upon Israel for *Achan*'s transgression. Why did this collective consequence only materialize after the crossing of the *Yarden*?

### National Identity

Rabbi Yochanan, again in the name of Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon, explains: *lo anash al ha-nistarot ad she-avru Yisrael et ha-Yarden* — collective punishment was not applied for hidden sins until Israel crossed the *Yarden*. The *pasuk* in *Devarim* — *ha-nistarot l'Hashem Elokeinu veha-niglot lanu u-levaneinu* — "the hidden things belong to the Lord our God, and the revealed things belong to us and to our children" — carries an unusual scribal notation: dots appear over the words *lanu u-levaneinu* and over the *ayin* of *ad*. Rabbi Yehuda derives from this notation that the responsibility for *nistarot*, hidden sins, was subject to a temporal limit: it did not apply until the crossing of the *Yarden*.

This is a striking and difficult idea. What is the conceptual basis for it? It cannot simply be about fault, because the people cannot be faulted for what they did not know. The better framework is one of collective *identity* rather than individual blame. Before the crossing of the *Yarden*, Israel functioned as a people in a more individualist mode — what one person did privately was, in a sense, his own business. After crossing, they became a *nation*, a collective unity. And as a collective unity, they bear responsibility — not necessarily blame — even for what one of their members does in private. An analogy: if a child breaks a store window, the parent may not be at fault but is nonetheless responsible. The shift from *am* to *goy*, from a people to a nation, is what creates this new framework of corporate accountability.

### Preventable Sins

Rabbi Nechemia takes a sharply different position. He cannot accept the idea that people die for sins they could not have prevented or even known about. He therefore inverts the reading: *keshem she-lo anash al ha-nistarot, kach lo anash al ha-niglot* — just as there was no collective punishment for hidden things, there was equally no collective punishment for public things — until the crossing of the *Yarden*. For Rabbi Nechemia, the dotted letters indicate not that the threshold for hidden sins was lowered after crossing, but that *both* categories — hidden and revealed — were only subject to collective consequence once the national identity was established. And crucially, even then, collective responsibility applies only to *niglot*, to public, preventable sins — things that the community could have known about and acted upon. The more palatable moral intuition here is clear: we can accept communal responsibility when we had the means to do something about it.

### Unity vs. Blame

The contrast between the two positions thus maps onto two distinct conceptions of collective responsibility. Rabbi Yehuda holds a more metaphysical view: once Israel crosses the *Yarden* and becomes a nation, they are one unity and bear the consequences of that unity even for what is beyond their knowledge. Rabbi Nechemia insists on a more pragmatic, action-based standard: communal responsibility attaches only where the community could have intervened. Before the crossing, even the *niglot* were not subject to collective sanction. After, they are — but only those.

### Family Knowledge

Having resolved why *Yisrael* as a whole suffered at *Ha-Ai* even according to Rabbi Nechemia's framework, the *gemara* still needs to explain how *Achan*'s sin — which was, after all, conducted in secret — could qualify as a *niglah*, a revealed sin, triggering collective responsibility. The answer offered is: *mishum de-havu yad'ei bei ishto u-vanav* — because his wife and children knew. The knowledge of the family introduced a public dimension into what had otherwise been a hidden act. Since those who were aware could have come forward and did not, the sin acquired something of the character of a communal failure to act. This is admittedly a difficult answer, and the *gemara* strains to reach it. Questions arise naturally: how many people must know before a sin becomes effectively public? Does some person in a position of authority need to have been in a position to discover it? The *gemara* does not fully resolve these tensions, but the thrust of the reasoning is that once family members who could have disclosed the sin chose not to, the community's failure of oversight becomes operative.

## Achan's Sins

### Jewish Identity

The *gemara* cites the *pasuk*, *chata Yisrael* — "Israel has sinned" — as God's announcement to *Yehoshua* even before *Achan* is identified through the casting of lots. Rabbi Abba bar Zavda draws from these words a fundamental *hashkafic* principle: *af al pi she-chata, Yisrael hu* — even though he sinned, he is still *Yisrael*. The *pasuk* does not say "Israel sinned and is no longer Israel." It says *chata Yisrael* — "Israel sinned" — the name *Yisrael* remains attached to the sinner even after the transgression.

The classic illustration cited is the verse about *Reuven*: *vayishkav et Bilhah pilegesh aviv vayihyu b'nei Yaakov shneim asar* — even after *Reuven*'s grave sin, the count of *Yaakov*'s sons remained twelve. He was not excised from the tribal register. The analogy from folk wisdom: *asa d'kai beini chilfei, asa shemei* — a *hadas* (myrtle) growing among thorns is still called a *hadas*. One student offered a deeper reading: the *hadas* itself hasn't changed, only its surroundings. But here *Achan* himself sinned. The response is that perhaps the *neshama* — the soul, the *pintele Yid* — is like the *hadas*, while the outer conduct has become corrupted. The core Jewish identity remains intact.

This *aggadic* passage became the textual basis for a major *halachic* claim. *Rashi*, in a *teshuva* dealing with apostasy, invoked this *gemara* to argue that regardless of how severely a person sins — even if they convert to another religion — their Jewish status remains intact *al pi halacha*. Strictly speaking, *Rashi* was not innovating without precedent, since there is no source anywhere in *Shas* suggesting that sin can strip a person of Jewish identity. But the rhetorical and *halachic* force of citing this *gemara* was powerful. It is worth noting that some *Geonim* held that apostasy does trigger some form of status change, and the question of whether there are perhaps two dimensions to Jewish identity — one inalienable and one that can be affected by apostasy — was taken up by *Rav Aharon Lichtenstein* in his discussion of the famous Brother Daniel case in Israel.

### Five Books

God's indictment of *Achan* is conveyed to *Yehoshua* through a verse heavy with repetition: *v'gam avru et briti asher tziviti otam, gam lakchu min ha-cherem, gam ganvu, v'gam kichu'shu, v'gam samu vi-kleihem* — the word *gam* ("also" or "and also") appears five times. Rabbi Ila, in the name of Rabbi Yehuda bar Masparta, derives from this: *melamed she-avar Achan al chamisha chumshei Torah* — *Achan* transgressed all five books of the Torah. This reflects the *gemara*'s characteristic tendency to portray the paradigmatic *rasha* as having committed the full spectrum of possible transgressions.

### Brit Milah

Rabbi Ila further teaches that *Achan moshach be-orlaso hayah* — *Achan* even attempted to reverse his *bris milah* by stretching his foreskin. The textual link is *gezeirah shavah*: the verse here reads *v'gam avru et briti*, and a verse elsewhere reads *et briti heifar* — both use the word *briti*, which is a coded reference to *bris milah*. The *gemara* preempts the objection that this is obvious — since he violated all of the *taryag mitzvos*, of course he violated this one too. The answer: *mahu de-seima, be-mitzvah gufei lo pakar* — one might have thought that even a person who transgresses all other commandments would not do something that compromises his very bodily identity. Therefore the verse teaches us: even that he did.

### Na'arah Me'orasah

Rabbi Abba bar Zavda adds another transgression inferred from the indictment: *melamed she-ba Achan al na'arah ha-me'orasah* — *Achan* had intercourse with a betrothed maiden. The derivation is again a *gezeirah shavah*: the verse says *ki asah nevalah* about *Achan*, and a verse elsewhere uses the same phrase *ki asah nevalah* in the context of a *na'arah ha-me'orasah*. Once more the *gemara* raises the objection: this is obviously included in the general sin of violating all *taryag mitzvos*. The response — citing *Rashi* — is that intercourse with a *na'arah ha-me'orasah* is particularly egregious because it renders her *asur* to her husband, thus actively harming a third party. One might have thought *Achan* would not go quite that far. The verse teaches us: even that.

### Sekilah Punishment

*Ravina* offers a clarification: the specific mention of a *na'arah ha-me'orasah* is not simply about cataloguing *Achan*'s sins, but about explaining his mode of execution. *Dinei k'na'arah ha-me'orasah* — his judgment was like that of a *na'arah ha-me'orasah*. Just as a *na'arah ha-me'orasah* who sins receives *sekilah* (stoning), so too *Achan* received *sekilah*.

## Family Punishment

### Challenge to Justice

*Reish Galusa* posed a challenge to *Rav Huna*: the verse states that *Yehoshua* took *Achan ben Zerach* along with the silver, the cloak, the bar of gold, his sons, his daughters, his ox, his donkey, his flock, his tent, and all that was his — and had them all executed. The question is sharp: *im hu chata, banav u-venosav mah chatu?* — if *Achan* sinned, what did his sons and daughters sin? We are dealing here not with God's inscrutable justice but with a *beis din* carrying out a sentence. The principle of *lo yumsu avos al banim* — fathers shall not be put to death for their children, nor children for their fathers — is explicit in the Torah.

*Rav Huna* responds with a rhetorical counter: if you ask about the sons and daughters, the same question applies to *kol asher lo* — all his possessions. What did his possessions do wrong? The verse *v'chol asher immo* refers, in context, not to their being punished but to their being brought and present at his execution. The point of bringing all of them — family, property, and livestock — was *ela lirdosam*, to bear down upon the people, to send an unmistakable message. The execution was public precisely because it was meant to deter all of Israel. And it is in that same framework — *kol Yisrael lirdosam* — that the sons and daughters, and all his possessions, were brought to the scene.

### Public Deterrence

The answer, then, is that the killing of *Achan*'s children and the destruction of his property were not punishments in the retributive sense but public acts of institutional messaging. *Ela lirdosam* — to impress upon Israel the gravity of what had occurred. Just as the *sotah* ritual is performed publicly to serve as a deterrent to others, the totality of *Achan*'s destruction — family, property, animals — served a communal pedagogical function.

### Disproportionate Punishm…

The *Ramban* identifies a pattern that runs through this week's *parasha*: in several cases, the punishment appears to exceed what simple retributive justice would demand. The *zaken mamre* who rules against the *Sanhedrin* is put to death; *edim zomemim* are executed; the *ben sorer u-moreh* is put to death for what is, in strict terms, very minor misconduct; the *navi sheker* is executed. In each of these cases, the Torah appends the phrase *v'chol Yisrael yishme'u v'yira'u* — and all Israel will hear and fear. The *Ramban* points out that this phrase itself is the justification. The institutions being protected — the authority of interpretation (*zaken mamre*), the integrity of the court system (*edim zomemim*), the structure of parental authority (*ben sorer u-moreh*), the authority of divine communication (*navi sheker*) — are of such foundational importance that their protection warrants punishments that would otherwise be disproportionate.

### Institution Preservation

This principle, that innocent or minimally guilty lives may be taken for the sake of institutional preservation and public message, sits in profound tension with the broader ethos of *masechet Sanhedrin*, which strains throughout to minimize capital punishment. One might fairly ask: which tendency serves as the normative model and which is the exception to be explained? The tension is real, and the *gemara* does not resolve it. What can be said is that the *Ramban*'s principle applies specifically to cases where *v'chol Yisrael yishme'u v'yira'u* is explicitly invoked — suggesting that the justification for disproportionate punishment is anchored in the protection of irreplaceable communal structures, and that without that explicit biblical warrant, the ordinary rules of proportionality govern.

## Sreifa & Sekilah

### Two Punishments

The verse records that after *Achan* and his household were condemned, *v'yisrefu otam ba-esh v'yiskelu otam ba-avanim* — they were both burned and stoned. The question is immediate: *b'tarti?* — both? Once a person is dead from one form of execution, what is the point of a second? And further: which punishment applies — *sreifa* or *sekilah*?

### Different Penalties

*Ravina* resolves this: *ha-ra'uy li-sreifa, sreifa; ha-ra'uy li-skila, skila* — each category received the punishment appropriate to it. The *property* — the cloak, the silver, the gold — was burned, as material objects designated for destruction by fire. The *people* — *Achan* and his family — were stoned, since *sekilah* is the applicable mode of execution for *Achan*'s capital offense (as established earlier from the comparison to a *na'arah ha-me'orasah*). The two punishments were not applied simultaneously to the same person but were differentiated according to the category of the condemned.

## Confession & Challenge

### Achan's Theft

The *gemara* now turns to *Achan*'s own confession. He declares: *va-ere ba-shalal, aderet shinar achat tovah, u-matayim shekalim kesef* — I saw in the spoils a fine cloak from Shinar, and two hundred shekels of silver, as well as a bar of gold.

### Garment Type

*Rav* and *Shmuel* debate the nature of the garment. *Rav* identifies it as *itztala d'milta* — a cloak of silk. *Shmuel* describes it as *sarvla d'tzrifa* — a heavier garment of fine wool, or according to some readings, a dyed wool cloak.

### Pouring Out

The verse then says *va-yatzikum lifnei Hashem* — he poured out before God what he had stolen. The word *va-yatzikum* is unusual; *lehatzik* is the word for pouring a liquid, not for placing objects. *Rav Nachman* therefore re-reads it: *ba v'chavtam lifnei ha-Makom* — he went and slammed them down before God. The image is one of force and anger, not reverent presentation.

### Joshua's Challenge

*Rav Nachman* goes further: the subject of this action is not *Achan* but *Yehoshua*. It is *Yehoshua* who slammed the stolen goods before God and said: *Ribono shel Olam* — Master of the Universe — the verse says *vayaku meihem anshei ha-Ai kishloshim v'shisha ish* — thirty-six men of *Ha-Ai* struck them down. A *baraita* records the debate: Rabbi Yehuda says this means literally thirty-six people died. Rabbi Nechemia says — but the verse says *kishloshim v'shisha*, *like* thirty-six, not exactly thirty-six. Rather, it was one person, *Yair ben Menashe*, who was equivalent in worth to the majority of the *Sanhedrin*, making him count as thirty-six. In some measure, narrowing the number to one person, rather than thirty-six, modulates the starkness of the collective punishment — though one innocent life remains one innocent life.

*Yehoshua*'s challenge to God is itself given voice through a further verse: *Rav Nachman* cites the *pasuk*, *tachanunim yedaber rash v'ashir ya'aneh azut* — a poor man speaks with supplications, while a rich man answers harshly. *Tachanunim yedaber rash zeh Moshe* — *Moshe* is the poor man who spoke softly and supplicated before God. *V'ashir ya'aneh azut zeh Yehoshua* — *Yehoshua* is the rich man who answered God harshly. The *gemara* thus frames *Yehoshua*'s confrontation with God as something qualitatively more aggressive than *Moshe*'s characteristic mode of petition. This is also the *gemara*'s way of giving voice to what *Chazal* themselves find troubling: thirty-six people died — or even one equivalent — for one man's hidden sin. *Yehoshua*'s challenge is the vehicle through which that disquiet is expressed.

### Other Challenges

The *gemara* raises a challenge: if the *drasha* that *Yehoshua* "slammed" the items before God is what establishes his harshness, was *Pinchas* not guilty of the same? The verse about *Pinchas* reads *va-ya'amod Pinchas vayifaleil* — and *Pinchas* stood and *vayifaleil*. Rabbi Elazar notes: the verse does not use the *hitpaleil* form, which would mean he prayed, but the *piel* form *vayifaleil*, from the root *plilim*, meaning judges — implying he held God to account. *Pinchas* said: *Ribono shel Olam*, twenty-four thousand people died in the plague — as the verse records *vayihyu ha-meitim ba-magefah arba'ah v'esrim elef* — and held God to task for it. And *Moshe* himself, the supposed model of gentle supplication, also challenged God — as the verse *v'lu ho'alnu va-neshev b'ever ha-Yarden* shows: *Yehoshua* said had we only remained on the other side of the *Yarden*, we would have been better off. This is something stronger than challenging God's decision; it is a near-rejection of the entire enterprise of entering the land. The *gemara* parallels it to *Moshe*'s cry of *lamah hare'ota la'am hazeh* — why have You dealt harshly with this people? Such challenges, the *gemara* implies, are not unique to *Yehoshua*, nor are they outside the range of acceptable discourse with God.

## God's Response

### Joshua's Sin

God's response to *Yehoshua*'s prostration and lamentation is *kum lach* — get up, deal with this. *Darash Shila* interprets God's words to *Yehoshua* as containing an implicit rebuke: *shelcha kasha mi-shelahem* — what is yours, your responsibility, is harsher than theirs. The people died at *Ha-Ai* not only because of *Achan*'s sin but also because of something *Yehoshua* himself had done — or failed to do.

### Delayed Altar

The specific charge: *ani amarti, ve-hayah be-ovrechem et ha-Yarden takimu* — God had commanded that immediately upon crossing the *Yarden*, certain stones were to be erected. *Ve-atem richaktem shishim mil* — and you delayed sixty *mil* before erecting them, going all the way to *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eval*. *Yehoshua*'s sin, in this reading, was that he did not fulfill the commandment with the immediacy God intended.

### Tosafot Resolution

*Tosafos* raises an obvious difficulty: the Torah itself specifies that the stones were to be erected at *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eval*. How could *Yehoshua* have been expected to erect them immediately upon crossing the *Yarden*, when those mountains were sixty *mil* away? *Tosafos* resolves this through a debate in the *Yerushalmi*. One opinion (attributed to Rabbi Elazar) holds that the commandment was to erect the stones immediately upon crossing — and that *Yehoshua* was therefore supposed to build two small mounds of earth right there, symbolically calling one *Har Gerizim* and the other *Har Eval*: *de-asu shushi de-afra, ve-karu le-achat Har Gerizim u-le-achat Har Eval*. The second opinion (Rabbi Yehuda) holds that the commandment referred to the actual mountains, but that *Yehoshua* fulfilled it on the very day of the crossing — *shishim mil halchu be-oto ha-yom* — covering sixty *mil* in a single day. According to the *Yerushalmi*, then, either *Yehoshua* made symbolic mounds immediately, or he reached the real mountains the same day. In both cases he did what was required. *Tosafos* explains that *Darash Shila* here holds that the commandment was meant like Rabbi Elazar — immediate erection upon crossing — and that *Yehoshua* erred by going to the real *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eval* instead of constructing symbolic ones on the spot.

### No Sin, Contribution

*Rav* responded to *Darash Shila*'s position by having an *amora* publicly expound: *ka-asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe avdo, ken tzivah Moshe et Yehoshua, ve-chen asah Yehoshua, lo he-sir davar mi-kol asher tzivah Hashem et Moshe* — as God commanded *Moshe* His servant, so *Moshe* commanded *Yehoshua*, and so *Yehoshua* did; he did not deviate from anything God had commanded *Moshe*. *Rav* thus rejected the claim that *Yehoshua* had sinned in any direct sense. And yet the *gemara* preserves the force of God's words: *attah geramta lahem* — you contributed to this. Not through transgression, but through *gerama*, indirect causation. *Yehoshua* had placed the spoils of *Yericho* under *cherem* — and it was the existence of that *cherem* that created the opportunity and temptation for *Achan*'s sin. Nobody asked *Yehoshua* to impose that *cherem*. He created the prohibition that *Achan* then violated.

### Learned Lesson

The *gemara* closes this thread by noting that God's subsequent command about the city of *Ai* carries an implicit lesson. The verse regarding *Ai* says to do to it and its king as was done to *Yericho* — but the end of the verse, which is not fully quoted, specifies: except do not place the spoils under *cherem*. The ellipsis is pointed: the lesson is built into the instruction. The tragedy at *Ha-Ai* was in part the consequence of an overly strict *cherem* on *Yericho*'s spoils. With *Ai*, that mistake is corrected.

## Angel & Talmud Torah

### Angel Encounter

Before the events of *Ha-Ai*, while *Yehoshua* was encamped near *Yericho*, he encountered an *ish omed l'negdo* — a man standing opposite him. This figure declared: *lo, ki ani sar tzeva Hashem atah bati* — no, I am the commander of God's army, and I have come now. *Yehoshua* fell on his face to the ground and prostrated himself.

### Greeting at Night

The *gemara* asks: *heichi avid hachi?* — how could *Yehoshua* have done this? Rabbi Yochanan teaches that *asur lo le-adam she-yiten shalom le-chavero ba-lailah* — it is forbidden for a person to greet another at night. The concern is *chaishinan shema shed hu* — we worry that the figure may be a demon. And since *shalom* is one of God's names, one cannot invoke God's name on a demon. Prostrating before an unknown figure at night would be equally problematic.

### Angel Identity

The resolution is that the figure identified himself by name as *sar tzeva Hashem*. By announcing himself as an agent of God, he established that he was not a demon. The *gemara* considers the counter: but perhaps he was a demon who was lying? The response: *gemiri d'lo mafkei shem shamayim le-vatalah* — we have a received tradition that demons will not invoke God's name. One who says *ani sar tzeva Hashem* is therefore either a human being or an angel. The identity of a demon is ruled out.

### Two Failures

The angel then articulated two distinct failures. First: *emesh bitaltem tamid shel bein ha-arbayim* — at the close of day (*Rashi* notes that *emesh* here refers to just before nightfall), they had been so preoccupied with the encampment of *Yericho* that they neglected to bring the daily *korban tamid*. Second: *v'achshav bitaltem Talmud Torah* — now, in the nighttime, they were maintaining the siege when no military activity was needed. The night hours should have been devoted to Torah study, not to an encampment with no immediate tactical purpose.

### Talmud Torah Greater

*Yehoshua* then asked: *al eizeh mehen bata?* — which of these sins brought you here? The angel answered: *atah bati* — now, referring to the current violation: the *bittul Talmud Torah* was the greater sin. The neglect of Torah study was what prompted the angel's appearance.

### Joshua Corrects

*Yehoshua* immediately responded: *mi-yad vayalen Yehoshua ba-lailah ha-hu be-toch ha-emek* — that very night, *Yehoshua* went and spent the night in the valley. Rabbi Yochanan adds: *melamed she-lan be-umkah shel halachah* — this means he went into the depths of *halachah*, immersing himself and his people in profound Torah study through the night.

### Explicit Teaching

*Rav Shmuel bar Unya* in the name of *Rav* draws the explicit conclusion: *gadol Talmud Torah yoter me-hakravat temidim* — *Talmud Torah* is greater than bringing the daily sacrifices. The proof: *she-ne'emar atah bati* — the angel said "now I have come," pointing to the *bittul Talmud Torah* as the primary offense, even over the neglect of the *tamid*.

## Secrets & Shame

### Dispute Verse

*Abaye* asked *Rav Dimi*: how is the verse *al taitzei la-riv maher, pen mah ta'aseh be-acharitah be-hachlim otcha re'acha* — "do not rush into a dispute, lest you not know what to do at the end when your friend shames you" — applied in *Eretz Yisrael*? He suspected there was a particular *derasha* in the west attached to this verse.

### Yechezkel's Protest

*Rav Dimi* related the following *midrash*: when God told *Yechezkel* to go and say to Israel, *avichem ha-Emori ve-imchem Chittit* — your father is an *Emori* and your mother is a *Chittit*, meaning you have behaved as children of Canaanites — a certain spirit of judgment rose up and protested before God: *Ribono shel Olam, im yavo Avraham ve-Sarah ve-ya'amdu lefanecha* — if *Avraham* and *Sarah* were standing right here before You, and You said this to them, would You embarrass them? *Umachlim otam* — and shame them publicly?

### Secret Principle

The connection to the verse is then made explicit: *rivcha riv et re'acha* — if you have a complaint to press against *Bnei Yisrael*, fight it out with them, address it directly. But *ve-sod acher al tegal* — do not reveal another's secret. The "secret" here is the ancestral lineage that connects *Avraham* and *Sarah* to peoples who were not Jewish; bringing that into the argument to shame Israel is not a legitimate move. Even if there is a case to be made against *Bnei Yisrael*, that case should not be made by embarrassing their forebears.

### Angel's Names

The *gemara* then addresses the question of whether this figure — identified as *ruach paskonit*, which *Rashi* understands as *Gavriel* — has the standing to challenge God in this way. The answer is yes, and the *gemara* explains through three names. *Rav Yose be-Rabbi Chanina* teaches: this angel has three names: *Piscon* — *she-posek devarim klapei ma'alah*, he has the authority to cut and deliver sharp words before God on behalf of Israel; *Itmon* — *she-otem avonoseihem shel Yisrael*, he seals and closes up the sins of Israel, protecting them from full prosecution; and *Sigron* — *ki-van she-soger, shuv ein pote'ach*, once he closes a matter, no one can reopen it. The angel's license to intercede on Israel's behalf is thus established through the very nature of his office.

## Prayer & Intercession

### Prayer Before Calamity

Rabbi Elazar teaches: *le-olam yakdim adam tefillah le-tzarah* — a person should always anticipate calamity with prayer, placing his prayers before the trouble arrives rather than after.

### Abraham's Altar

The paradigmatic example is *Avraham*: *she-imalai hikdim Avraham tefillah le-tzarah bein Beis El u-vein ha-Ai* — because *Avraham* built a *mizbe'ach* and prayed between *Beis El* and *Ha-Ai* — the very location where Israel later suffered defeat — his prayer a millennium earlier provided protective merit. Had *Avraham* not prayed there, *lo nish'ayar mi-sonei Yisrael sarid u-palit* — not a single survivor of Israel's enemies (a reverential circumlocution for Israel itself in times of danger) would have remained. Far more than thirty-six — or even one — would have died at *Ha-Ai*.

### Prayer Exertion

*Reish Lakish* reads the verse differently: *kol ha-ma'amitz atzmo be-tefillah mi-lemata, ein lo tzarim mi-lema'alah* — whoever exerts himself in prayer below, in this world, will have no adversaries pressing against him from above. The verse *ha-ye'aroch shuva'cha velo ba-tzar* — set out your prayer, not in distress — becomes a statement about the timing and intensity of prayer: commit to it before adversity strikes.

### Divine Support

Rabbi Yochanan adds a final teaching: *le-olam yevakesh adam rachamim she-yehu kol ma'amitzos kocho* — a person should always pray that all heavenly forces strengthen him, *ve-al yehu lo tzarim mi-lema'alah* — and that no one on high stand against him. The goal of prayer is not only to avert specific catastrophes but to ensure that the entire heavenly apparatus is aligned in one's favor rather than arrayed in opposition.
