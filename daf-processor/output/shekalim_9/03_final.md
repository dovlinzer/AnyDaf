# Shekalim 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Log & Kos Measures

### Log De'Oraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי חָנָן. לוֹגָא דְאוֹרַיְתָא תֹמַנְיָא עַתִּיקָא דְּמּוּרִיֵסָא דְצִיפּוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥanan said, the *log* of the Torah is the old Sepphorean eighth of fish sauce.

The daf opens at the bottom of 79b, in the midst of a digression prompted by the mention of the baskets used for *terumat halishka* — the three annual removals of coins from the chamber where the *shkalim* were deposited. That discussion gave rise to a broader inquiry into the sizes of various measures not made explicit in certain *mishnayot*, including the size of the *kos* used for *kiddush* and the *arba kosot*. We now pick up with a further discussion of measures.

*Amar Rabbi Chanin*: the biblical *log* — the *log de'oraita* — is equivalent to the *tmunta atikta*, the old measure used for measuring fish brine in Tzipori. So now we know. This establishes the biblical *log* in terms of a contemporaneously recognizable standard.

### Log & Honey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹנָה. וַחֲכִמְנָא לֵיהּ. דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי הֲוָה מְכִילִין בָּהּ דְּבַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Jonah said, I know it. In the House of Shammai they were measuring honey with it.

*Amar Rabbi Yona*: *vachachamnala* — and I recognized it — that in the house of Rabbi Yannai they would use this same measure to measure honey. The word *mechilin*, to measure, is the same root used in the *Mechilta*, the Tannaitic work on Shemot — a term conveying the sense of gathering or collecting. The discussion here concerns volume, not weight, even though honey is considerably heavier than wine.

### Shminis Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. חֲצִי שְׁמִינִית תִּבֶּרְנִית.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated, half of the old Tiberian eighth.

A *baraita* teaches that *chatzi shminis tabernis hayeshana* — half of the ancient Tiberian *shminis* — is another measure relevant to this discussion. The *Korban HaEdah* understands this as equivalent to a *revi'it*, since half of a *shminis* (an eighth) yields a sixteenth, though the *shminis* itself, being an eighth, would need to be doubled to reach a *revi'it*. In any case, this represents another ancient measure that has a recognizable modern equivalent.

### Why 'Ancient'?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. חָדָא דִידָן הֲוָה. וְלָמָּה קָרִי לָהּ עַתִּיקָה. מִן בְּגִין דַּהֲוָה בְיוֹמָא. אִית דְּאָמְרֵי. דַּהֲוָה זְעִירָא וְרָבַת. וְאִית דְּאָמְרֵי. דְאַזְעִירָא אַזְעָרַת קוֹמֵי דַהֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, this one we used. Why did he call it the old one? Because it was in his days. Some are saying, it was small, then was enlarged. But some are saying, it was diminished more than before.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: this ancient Tiberian *shminis* — *didan hu* — is really one of ours; we know what a *shminis* is and we have that measure today. So why is it called *atika*, the ancient one? *Min begin de'hava beyomei* — because it exists in our days. And some say that the ancient one actually used to be smaller, and then it was made larger, so the one we currently call the Tiberian *shminis* is larger than the ancient standard, which is why we must specify the ancient one. And there is yet another view: *de'azla* — that it was then re-shrunk, but *velo azla kema de'hava* — it was not shrunk back to its original size. In either case, the current Tiberian *shminis* does not precisely match the ancient measure, and therefore the ancient one must be specified by name.

### Kos Dimensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הוּא שִׁיעוּרָן שֶׁלּ כּוֹס. רִבִּי יוֹסֵי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן פָּזִי וְרִבִּי יוּסֵי בַּר בֵּיבִין בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל. אֶצְבָּעַיִים עַל אֶצְבָּעַיִים עַל רוּם אֶצְבַּע וּמֶחֱצָה וּשְׁלִישׁ אֶצְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** What is the measure of a cup? Rebbi Yose in the name of Rebbi Yose ben Pazi, Rebbi Yose ben Bevin in the name of Samuel: Two fingers by two fingers high a finger and a half and a third of a finger.

What is the *shiur* of a *kos*? Rabbi Yose Berabbi Bun in the name of Rabbi Yose ben Pazai, and Rabbi Yose bar Bibi in the name of Rabbi Shmuel, states: *etzba'im al etzba'im rom etzba u'mechtza veshlish etzba* — two *etzba'ot* by two *etzba'ot* by one and a third plus one half, that is, 1.833 *etzba'ot*. This is a striking discrepancy: the *Bavli* in Pesachim gives the dimensions as two by two by 2.7, not two by two by 1.833. None of the commentators printed alongside the *Yerushalmi* even address this line, so it may be that they did not have it in their text, or that there is some other explanation — but it is clearly a different size than what the *Bavli* records.

## Wine & Tumah

### Dried Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנִן דְּקַיְסָרִן וְרִבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר בֵּיבִין בְּשֵׁם שְׁמוּאֵל. אַתְיָא דְּרִבִּי נָתָן כְּרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. כְּמוֹ דְרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אָמַר. בָּרְבִיעָא. כֵּן אָמַר רִבִּי נָתָן. בָּרְבִיעָא. לִכְשֶׁיּקְרַשׁ וִיהֵא בוּ כְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The rabbis of Caesarea in the name of Rebbi Yose bar Bevin in the name of Samuel: Rebbi Nathan follows Rebbi Simeon. Just as Rebbi Simeon says, a *quartarius*, so Rebbi Nathan says, a *quartarius*, when it jells it will have the volume of an olive.

If wine dries up — *yavesh* — a *revi'it* of wine, when congealed, will be the size of a *kazayit*. This appears in a number of places in the *Bavli* as well, where it is assumed that a congealed *revi'it* equals a *kazayit*. The relevance to *Shabbat* is presumably this: if one carries dried wine, the *shiur* for carrying is a *kazayit*, since the dried wine is not being mixed with water and therefore one goes by the full size of the *kos*, by the *revi'it*, and a dried-up *revi'it* of wine becomes a *kazayit*. These are the words of Rabbi Natan.

### Rabbi Natan

The sages of Keisarah — Rabbi Yose bar Bibi in the name of Shmuel — note that Rabbi Natan's position here follows that of Rabbi Shimon. Just as Rabbi Shimon holds that one is only *chayav* for carrying wine on *Shabbat* if one carries a *revi'it* — not a quarter of a *revi'it* of concentrate — so too Rabbi Natan holds that the *revi'it*, when it dries up and congeals, becomes a *kazayit*.

The question is not in dispute: a *kazayit* is understood to be the quantity that a *revi'it* becomes when it congeals. The debate concerns only the *Shabbat* carrying context. Why do we go by a *revi'it* rather than by what is normally used when dealing with concentrate — a quarter of a *revi'it*? Some *nosei kelim* explain that one only uses the quarter-*revi'it* size when dealing with the concentrate in liquid form, because in that form it is significant: water can easily be added to reconstitute it into a full *revi'it*. When it is in congealed form, that small a quantity loses its significance. However, the *Gemara* here is suggesting that Rabbi Natan may actually hold that even in liquid concentrate form one needs a full *revi'it*, and therefore in congealed form one needs a *kazayit* — and this is consistent with Rabbi Shimon's position that even liquid wine goes by a *revi'it*, not a quarter of a *revi'it*. A note from one of the students identifies the *Tosafot* in Pesachim as making a related point about a different standard finger-width used in Tzipori, which would affect the dimensional calculations — a detail worth following up.

## Neveilah Blood

### Mule Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי סִימוֹן בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. מַעֲשֶׂה בְפִירְדַת רִבִּי שֶׁמֵּתָה. וְטִהֲרוּ אֶת דָּמָהּ מִשּׁוּם נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Simon in the name of Rebbi Joshua ben Levi: It happened that a mule of Rebbi's household died and they declared its blood pure regarding the carcass.

The *Gemara* transitions from the question of a *revi'it* that congeals to a related topic involving blood. Rabbi Simon in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi reports: *maaseh be-pirdat Rabbi she-metah* — there is a story involving the mule of Rabbi (Rebbi), which died. *Ve-tiharu et damah mishum neveilah* — and they declared its blood not problematic on account of *neveilah*, because *neveilah* applies to the meat, not to the blood. This, however, raises a significant question. In various areas of *halakha*, blood is treated similarly to meat: would the blood of a *neveilah*, if there were enough of it to constitute the equivalent of a *kazayit* of meat, itself be *tamei*? The story says the blood was *tahor* — but was it *tahor* categorically, or only because there was not enough of it?

### Blood Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁאַל רִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אֶת רִבִּי סִימוֹן. עַד כַּמַּה. וְלֹא אַשְׁגַּח בֵּיהּ. וְשָׁאַל לֵיהּ רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי דְאָמַר לֵיהּ. עַד רְבִיעִית טָהוֹר. יוֹתֵר מִכֵּן טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Eleazar asked Rebbi Simon, how much? He did not take notice of him. He asked Rebbi Joshua ben Levi who told him, it is pure up to a *quartarius*. More than that is impure.

*Ve-sha'al Rabbi Eliezer et Rabbi Simon*: Rabbi Eliezer asked Rabbi Simon, *ad kama* — how much blood would have been considered acceptable? Is the blood categorically *tahor*, or is there a threshold amount? *Velo ashkhach beh* — and Rabbi Simon did not respond to him. He went instead to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi himself, who said: *ad revi'it tahor yoter mirevi'it tamei* — up to a *revi'it* it is *tahor*, more than that is *tamei*. The reasoning is that a *revi'it* of blood, when it congeals, becomes a *kazayit*, and just as a *kazayit* of meat is *tamei*, so too a *revi'it* of blood would be *tamei* — and the reason the blood in the story was *tahor* was that it was less than a *revi'it*. It is slightly puzzling that the text says *yoter mirevi'it tamei* rather than *revi'it v'yoter tamei*, but the *Gemara* does not pause on that formulation. The *mefarshim* clarify that biblically only the meat is *metamei*; rabbinically, blood becomes *tamei* once it reaches a *revi'it* in quantity.

### Bibi's Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְעִט בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רִבִּי זְרִיקָא. בְּגִין דִּשְׁאַל לָךְ אַתְּ בְּעַט בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ. בְּגִין דְלָא הֲוָת דַּעְתִּי בִּי בָּעִיטְנָא בֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** He was unfriendly to him. Rebbi Zeriqa asked him, because he asked you, you were unfriendly to him? He answered him, because my mind was not clear, I was unfriendly to him.

Rabbi Bibi was sitting and retelling this same story, when Rabbi Yitzchak bar Bisna — or bar Kahana — posed the same question: when they said the blood was *tahor*, did they mean up to a *revi'it* it is *tahor* and beyond that *tamei*, or was it categorically *tahor*? *Uva'at beh* — and he kicked him. The *Korban HaEdah* understands this as the questioner going to Rabbi Simon himself, who this time, rather than simply ignoring the question, responded with a kick.

### Paltar Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רִבִּי חָנָן. וְהָיוּ חַיֶּיךָ תְּלוּאִים לְךָ מִנֶּגדֶ. זֶה שֶׁלּוֹקֵחַ חִיטִּים לַשָּׁנָה. וּפָחַדְתָּ לַיְלָה וְיוֹמָם. זֶה שֶׁלּוֹקֵחַ מִן הַצִּידָקִי. וְלֹא תַאֲמִין בְּחַיֶּיךָ. זֶה שֶׁלּוֹקֵחַ מִן הַפַּלְטָר. וַאֲנָא סְמִיכְנָא אֲפַּלְטָר.
>
> **Translation:** As Rebbi Ḥanan said, *your life will hang far from you*, that is one who buys a year's supply of wheat, *you will be fearful night and day*, that is one who buys from the Saracen, *and you will not believe in your survival*, that is one who buys from the retail store, and I am dependent on retail stores.

Rav Zerika challenged whoever did the kicking: *begin dasha'al lakh at ba'at beh* — he asked you a legitimate question, why did you kick him? The reply: *begin dila hava da'ati bi* — because my mind was not settled, I was not in a good frame of mind, so I kicked him. And what caused this unsettled state? *Da'amar Rabbi Chanin*: the verse in Devarim, *vehayu chayekha teluyim lekha mineged* — your life will hang before you — refers to one who must purchase grain for an entire year without owning land, relying entirely on others for his food supply. *Ufachadeta laila veyomam* — and you will be afraid night and day — refers to one who purchases from the *sadki*, the retailer, buying week to week with no reserves. *Lo ta'amin bechayecha* — you will not trust your life — refers to one who buys *pas paltar*, already-baked bread from the baker, having no grain stores of his own whatsoever. *Ve-ana samichna a-paltar* — and I rely on the baker's bread — he was saying that this is his situation, one of perpetual anxiety about where his sustenance will come from.

This is a remarkable explanation. It does not fully account for why the pattern of refusing or kicking recurred, but it frames it as a reflection of an ongoing psychological state of insecurity. There is a broader historical context worth noting: in *Eretz Yisrael* under Roman rule there was a period of significant inflation, after which the Roman government subsidized bread, which gave rise to various leniencies regarding *pas paltar*. Whether that context illuminates this passage is unclear, but the connection between reliance on the baker's bread and a state of existential anxiety would have resonated sharply in that environment.

### Halakha Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָהוּ כְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** What about it?

Stepping back from the story, the *Gemara* asks: *u-ma kado* — so what is the actual *halakha*? Is the blood of a *neveilah* categorically *tahor*, or is it *tamei* above a certain threshold?

### BenPesora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵעִיד רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן פְּתוֹרָא עַל דַּם נְבֵלָה שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi Joshua ben Bathyra testified about blood of carcasses that it is pure."

A *baraita* — indeed a *mishnah* in Eduyot — states: *Heid Rabbi Yehoshua ben Pesora al dam neveilah she-hu tahor* — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Pesora testified that the blood of a *neveilah* is categorically *tahor*. This seems to establish clearly that it is not a question of quantity but of categorical status.

### Sheretz Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְטָהוֹר. לְטָהֵר מִן הַבָּשָׂר. הָא לְטַמֵּא אַתָּה מְטַמֵּא. תַּמָּן תַּנִּינָא. דַּם הַשֶּׁרֶץ כִּבְשָׂרוֹ מְטַמֵּא וְאֵין מַכְשִׁיר. וְאֵין לָנוּ כַּיּוֹצֵא בוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** What means pure? It is pure in the flesh, but for impurity it makes impure. There, we have stated "The blood of a crawling animal is like its flesh, it makes impure but does not prepare. Nothing else is like this," in the amount needed that its blood makes impure like its flesh.

An attempt is made to reinterpret the word *tahor* in that *mishnah*: perhaps *tahor* means only that the blood does not render food susceptible to *tumah* — it is not *machshir* — but that it is itself a source of *tumah*. This would be a remarkable re-reading of a simple declaration of *tahor*. The *Gemara* challenges this immediately. The *mishnah* in Machshirin states: *dam sheretz ki-bsaro* — the blood of a *sheretz* is like its flesh: *metamei ve-eino machshir* — it causes *tumah* exactly as the flesh of a *sheretz* does but does not render food susceptible. And then the *mishnah* continues: *ve-ein lanu ke-yotzei bo* — nothing else is like this. No other blood has the quality of being *metamei* but not *machshir*. Therefore, that quality cannot be ascribed to the blood of a *neveilah*, because the *mishnah* explicitly says only the blood of a *sheretz* occupies that singular category. Furthermore, the *shiur* for the blood of a *sheretz* to convey *tumah* is the same as for its flesh — the size of a lentil.

### Amoraim Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵי פְּלִיגִין בָּהּ תְּרֵין אֲמוֹרָאִין חַד אָמַר. טָמֵא. וְחַד אָמַר. טָהוֹר. מָאן דְּאָמַר טָמֵא. כְּרִבִּי יְהוּדָה. וּמָאן טָהוֹר. כְּרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן פְּתוֹרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yose said, two Amoraim disagree, one says impure, and one says pure. He who says "impure" follows Rebbi Jehudah; he of "pure" follows Rebbi Joshua ben Bathyra.

*Ve-amar Rav Yosef plig ba terei amora'ei* — Rav Yosef states that this question, whether blood of a *neveilah* is *metamei*, is actually a dispute between two *amora'im*: one says *tamei* and one says *tahor*. The one who says *tamei* follows Rabbi Yehuda, who cites a *mishnah* that records the debate between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel, where according to Beis Hillel the blood of a *neveilah* is *metamei*. The one who says *tahor* follows the *mishnah* of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Pesora, cited above. Thus the question of whether blood of a *neveilah* is fundamentally *tahor* or *tamei* is both a *machlokes tanna'im* and a *machlokes amora'im* mapping onto those respective Tannaitic positions.

### Advuma's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אֲבוּהָ דְּאִימָן אַחְתֵּיהּ דְּרִבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹרְיָינָא דְּנָשִׂיא הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Abuha of Iman the sister of Rebbi Jehudah was the instructor of the Patriarch.

*Amar lei Rav Advuma de-min Nachusa*: this works out well — *ve-yeus de-Rebbi Yehudah moreina de-bei nesi'ah havei* — because Rabbi Yehuda was effectively the *posek* for the house of the *nasi*, and we recall that the house of the *nasi* at that time was Rebbi's household — the household whose mule had died in the opening story. So when they declared the blood *tahor* because it was less than a certain amount, that was consistent with the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda: he holds that blood of a *neveilah* is indeed *tamei* rabbinically once it reaches a *revi'it* (at which point it congeals into a *kazayit*), and the blood in the story was *tahor* only because it fell below that threshold. This neatly resolves the narrative: the story was proceeding according to Rabbi Yehuda's *psak*, not according to the categorical *tahor* view of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Pesora.

It is worth noting that the *Yerushalmi* here reflects quite naturally on the fact that Rebbi — who was not only the political leader but the pre-eminent *talmid chacham* of his generation — nonetheless had a dedicated in-house *posek*. The *Bavli* regularly discusses the rabbis who served in this capacity for the *Reish Galusa*, whose household raised its own set of halakhic challenges. Here, however, it is more surprising: one imagines how much time Rebbi actually had for the *beis medrash* while running the entire political and communal apparatus. The assumption that he needed a *posek* for the day-to-day *she'eilot* of his own home raises genuine questions about the realities of his schedule.

## Beyond Suspicion

### Appearance Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּנֵּי רִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. קִיוֵוץ לֹא יִתְרוֹם מִפְּנֵי הַחֲשַׁד.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ismael stated, a curly head should not disburse because of the suspicion.

The *Gemara* returns to the *mishnah*, which expresses concern not that the one collecting *terumat halishka* might steal, but that if something later changes in his financial circumstances — whether he becomes richer or poorer — people will attribute it to theft from the *lishka*. Therefore, the system is designed so that he is beyond any possible suspicion. As one participant notes, this concern extends beyond the individual: it would cast aspersions on the entire *Beis HaMikdash* system and the integrity of the institution. The *mishnah*'s invocation of *ve-yissem neki'im*, however, is read at the individual level as well.

### Body Search

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. הַגִּיזְבְּרִין הָיוּ מְפַסְפְּסִין בְּקִילִּקִין. תַּנֵּי מְדַבְּרִין הָיוּ עִמּוֹ מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהוּא נִכְנַס עַד שָׁעָה שֶׁהוּא יוֹצֵא. וִימַלֵּא פּוּמֵיהּ מוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated, "the treasurers were rubbing with Cilician cloth." It was stated, "they were talking with him from the time he entered to the time he left." [And the suggestion:] should he not fill his mouth with water?

A *baraita* taught by Rabbi Yishmael: a *kvat* — possibly a person with long, flowing hair or locks — should not perform the removal, because of *mipnei hachashad*, the appearance of impropriety (coins could be concealed in such hair). Another *baraita* states that the *gizbarim* would conduct a thorough pat-down — *mepaspasin be-kilkelin*, inspecting his hair or garments — before and after entry. Furthermore, they would speak with him continuously — *medabberin hayu immo she-hu nichnas ad she-hu yotzei* — from the time he entered to the time he exited, to prevent him from swallowing any coins.

One might ask: why not simply fill his mouth with water before he enters, so that he could not swallow anything? This was indeed raised.

### Bracha Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי תַנְחוּמָא. מִפְּנֵי הַבְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Tanḥuma said, because of the benediction.

*Amar Rav Tanchuma*: *mipnei habracha* — because of the *bracha*. In passing, the *Gemara* reveals that when the designated official would lift the money out with the basket, he would recite a *bracha*, just as one recites a *bracha* on taking *terumah* of grain. This is a striking detail: the entire act of removing funds from the collective chamber has been ritualized as *terumat halishka*, with its own accompanying *bracha*. The mouth therefore could not be filled with water, as that would prevent the *bracha* from being recited.

The degree to which this act was ritualized is worth pausing on. What is functionally a transfer of funds from one room to another — coins collected in a chamber being moved to a operational account — was transformed into a solemn ritual complete with formal pronouncements, prescribed questions and answers, tarping procedures, and a *bracha*. This ritualization will become even more apparent as the *mishnah* continues.

### Clean Twice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹנָתָן. בַּתּוֹרָה וּבַנְּבִיאים וּבַכְּתובִים מָצָאנוּ שֶׁאָדָם צָרִיךְ לָצֵאת יְדֵי הַבִּרְיוֹת כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהוּא צָרִיךְ לָצֵאת יְדֵי הַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Samuel bar Naḥman in the name of Rebbi Jonathan: In the Torah, in Prophets, and in Hagiographs we find that a person had to be innocent in the eyes of people just as he has to be innocent in the eyes of the Omnipresent.

Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: in Torah, *Nevi'im*, and *Ketuvim* we find that a person must not only be innocent before God but must also be seen as beyond reproach by other people — *latzet yedei habriyot* — just as one must be clean before *HaMakom*.

### Three Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּתּוֹרָה מְנַיִין. דִּכְתִיב וִהְיִיתֶם נְקִיִים מֵיְיָ וּמִיִּשְׂרָאֵל. בַּנְּבִיאים מְנַיִין. דִּכְתִיב אֵל אֱלֹהִים יְי אֵל אֱלֹהִים יְי הוּא יֹוֹדֵעַ וְיִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא יֵדָע. בַּכְּתובִים מְנַיִין. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר וּמְצָא חֵן וְשֵׂכֶל טוֹב בְּעֵינֵי אֱלֹהִים וְאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** In the Torah from where? As it is written, *you have to be blameless before the Eternal and before Israel*. In Prophets from where? As it is written, *The Power, God, Eternal, the Power, God, Eternal, He knows, and Israel has to know*. In Hagiographs from where? As it is written, *find grace and understanding in the eyes of God and man.*

*BaTorah minayin*: from the verse *ve-yissem neki'im meHashem u-miYisrael* — you shall be clean both before God and before Israel. *BaNevi'im minayin*: the verse *El Elohim Hashem veYisrael hu yeda* — God knows and Israel will know — from the episode in *Ever HaYarden* where the tribes were suspected of building a *bamah* for rebellion, and they declared their innocence before both God and the people. *UvaKetuvim minayin*: *u-matzah chen vesechel tov be'einei Elohim ve'adam* — you will find favor in the eyes of both God and people.

### Cleanest Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּמְלִיאֵל זֵוּגָא שָׁאַל לְרִבִּי יוֹסֵי בֵּירִבִּי בּוּן. אֵי זֶהוּ הַמְחוּוָר שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן. אָמַר לֵיהּ. וִהְיִיתֶם נְקִיִים מֵיְיָ וּמִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Gamaliel the twin asked Rebbi Yose ben Rebbi Abun, which one is the clearest of all of these? He answered him, y*ou have to be blameless before the Eternal and before Israel*.

Gamliel Zuga asked Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Bun: *eizo hi hamchuvarta she-bekulan* — which of these three *psukim* most clearly and cleanly captures this standard? The answer: *ve-yissem neki'im meHashem u-miYisrael*, because the term *naki* — clean — most directly conveys the idea of being completely free from any suspicion or blemish, and it explicitly demands this cleanliness both before God and before the people of Israel.

## Terumat Ritual

### Gamliel Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* משנה: שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָיָה נִכְנָס וְשִׁקְלוֹ בֵּין אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו וְזוֹרְקוֹ לִפְנֵי הַתּוֹרֵם וְהַתּוֹרֵם מִתְכַּוֵּין וְדוֹחֲקוֹ לַקּוּפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: A member of the household of Rabban Gamliel was entering with his *sheqel* between his fingers and threw it before the disburser. The disburser was directing it and threw it into the box.

The *mishnah* describes an interesting practice of Beis Rabban Gamliel. *Shel Beis Rabban Gamliel hayah nichnas ve-shiklo beyado bein etzbe'otav* — Rabban Gamliel would time his entry so that his *shekel* was held between his fingers, *vezarko lifnei hatoram* — and he would throw it directly in front of the one performing the removal at precisely the moment of the *terumah* act — *ve-hatoram mitkaven vetochavo letoch hakuppah* — and the one performing the removal would have *kavanah* and insert it into the basket. The motivation was to ensure that his money would be among the funds actually used for the *korban*, and not left behind among the remainder.

One wonders how this was permitted, given that it might cause others to feel their money was left behind. But Rabban Gamliel was the *nasi*, and the prerogative of position apparently extended this far.

### Take Terumah?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין הַתּוֹרֵם תּוֹרֵם עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר לָהֶן אֶתְרוֹם. וְהֵן אוֹמְרִים לוֹ תְּרוֹם, תְּרוֹם, תְּרוֹם, שָׁלֹֹֹש פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The disburser only starts disbursing after he asked, "shall I disburse"? And they answer him, "disburse, disburse, disburse," three times.

The one performing the removal does not simply proceed: *ein hatoram torem ad she-hu omer lahem etrom* — he first asks those supervising, "Shall I take the *terumah*?" *Ve-hen omrim lo terom* — and they say, "Take it." This exchange is performed three times — *shalosh pe'amim* — before he proceeds.

### Tarping System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּרַם אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְחִיפָּה בִּקְטַבֳלִיּוֹת הַשְּׁנִיָיה וְחִיפָּה בִּקְטַבֳלִיּוֹת. הַשְּׁלִישִׁית לֹא הָיָה מְחַפֶּה. וְלָמָּה הָיָה מְחַפֶּה שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁכַּח וְיִתְרוֹם מִן הַדָּבָר הַתָּרוּם.
>
> **Translation:** He disburses the first time and covers it with cushions, the second time and covers it with cushions. The third time he was not covering. Why did he cover? That he should not forget and disburse from anything which already had been disbursed.

*Tarma harishona*: after the first removal — presumably at Pesach time — the money left behind was covered with a tarp, *chippah be-katavliot*. This ensured that when new funds accumulated and the second removal was made at Shavuot, the *terumah* would be taken only from the new money piled atop the tarp, not from the previously-addressed funds. The second removal was similarly covered. *Shlishit lo haya mechape* — the third removal required no covering, since no new funds would be arriving afterward.

The reason for the tarp is rooted in the *terumah* model: just as one cannot take *terumah* from a new heap by drawing from a pile on which *terumah* has already been taken — *ein tormin min hachadash al hachadash* only from untouched grain — so too here, the removal must come from fresh funds, not from funds already addressed by a prior removal.

*Lama haya mechape*: why cover it at all? *Shema yishkach veyitrom min hadavar hatarum* — lest he forget and take from what has already been taken, which would be invalid.

### Three Regions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּרַם אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה לְשֵׁם אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל הַשְּׁנִיָיה לְשֵׁם כְּרַכִים הַמּוּקָּפִין לָהּ וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁית לְשֵׁם בָּבֶל וּלְשֵׁם מָדַי וּלְשֵׁם מְדִינוֹת הָרְחוֹקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The first time he disbursed in the name of the Land of Israel, the second time in the name of nearby walled cities, and the third time in the name of Babylonia, in the name of Media, and in the name of distant countries.

*Tarama et harishona leshem Eretz Yisrael*: the first removal was designated for those who lived in Israel, whose funds arrived earliest. *Ve-hashniya leshem krachim hamukaffin la* — the second for the cities and lands surrounding Israel. The phrase is notable: *krachim hamukaffin* normally refers to cities surrounded by a wall (as in the laws of *Megilla*), but here *mukaffin la* means surrounding Israel — a creative reuse of familiar terminology. *Ve-hashlishit leshem Bavel ve-leshem Madai ve-leshem medinot hayam* — the third for Babylonia, Media, and all the distant lands, whose funds arrived latest.

### Grain Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָיָה כול׳. אִילּוּ שְׁנֵי כְרִיִים וְתָרַם מֵאֶחָד מֵהֶן עַל חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁמָּא לֹא פָטַר חֲבֵירוֹ. הַנַַּחַת רוּחַ הִיא לָהֶם שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא קָרְבָּן מִתְקָרֵב אֶלָּא מִשֶּׁלָּהֶן תְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** HALAKHAH: "A member of the household of Rabban Gamliel was," etc. If there were two heaps and he gave heave from one for the other, would that not remove the liability from the other. It gave them satisfaction that the sacrifice should be brought first from their money.

The *Gemara* addresses the practice of Rabban Gamliel through an analogy from *terumah* of grain. *Ilu hayu shney krayim ve-toram mi-echad mehen al chavero* — if one had two mounds of grain and took *terumah* from one on behalf of both, the *terumah* applies to both and the second mound is certainly covered. So why did Rabban Gamliel insist his specific coin be in the basket? If the removal is taken from the communal funds and covers all the funds including the remainder, his money is represented whether or not it was physically scooped. The *Gemara* answers: *nachas ruach hi lahem* — it gave them personal satisfaction — *shelo yehe korban mitkarev ela mishelahem techila* — they wanted their money to be the first used toward the purchase of a *korban*. Not that one would not be *yotze* otherwise — obviously everyone would be — but the emotional and symbolic significance of having one's own money go first mattered to them.

### Barayta Pronouncements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. תָּרַם אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה לְשֵׁם אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וּלְשֵׁם כָּל יִשְׂרָאֵל. שְׁנִיָיה לְשֵׁם כַּרַכִין הַמּוּקָּפִין וּלְשֵׁם כָּל יִשְׂרָאֵל. וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁית לְשֵׁם בָּבֶל וּלְשֵׁם מָדַיי וּלְשֵׁם מְדִינוּת הָרְחוֹקוֹת וּלְשֵׁם כָּל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: He disburses the first time in the name of the Land of Israel and in the name of all of Israel. The second time in the name of fortified cities and in the name of all of Israel. The third time in the name of Babylonia and the name of Media and in the name of all of Israel.

A *baraita* elaborates the formal proclamations made during each removal. For the first: *harei zo me'Eretz Yisrael al kol Yisrael* — this is from the funds of Israel, for the sake of all of Israel. The *Tosefta* is even more explicit, recording the full text of each pronouncement and noting that after the first removal the tarp was put down, *mipnei she'anshei Surya ba'im ve-shoklim aleha* — because the people of Syria would come and add their contributions on top of it, necessitating separation. For the second: *harei zo me'Ammon u-miMoav u-mikrachim hamukafin le-Eretz Yisrael* — from Ammon, Moab, and the surrounding lands — for all Israel. For the third: *harei zo miBavel u-miMadai u-mimedinos harchokos al kol Yisrael* — from Babylon, Media, and the distant lands — for all of Israel. The funds were acknowledged by origin, but the act was performed for all of *Klal Yisrael*.

## Shirayim Status

### Tarp Slip

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי שָׁמַט אֶת הַקַּטָבֳלִיּוֹת נַעֲשׂוּ כוּלָּן שְׁיֵרִיים.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: If he removed the covers, all was made remainder.

*Shamat et hakattabaliyot* — if the tarp slipped accidentally, *nasu kullan shirayim* — everything becomes mixed together and takes on the status of *shirayim*, remainder funds. This is the *Tosefta*'s understanding: the mixing was accidental. The consequence is that one cannot now take the second removal from the mixed pile, since it cannot be distinguished which funds are new and which were already addressed. Following the *terumah* model strictly — one cannot take *terumah* from a pile already subject to a prior removal — the entire mixed pile is treated as *shirayim* and unavailable for the next formal removal.

## Third Collection

### Gold Coins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. שְׁלִישִׁית הִיא הָיְתָה עֲשִׁירָה שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן. שֶׁהָיוּ בָהּ אִיצְטְלֵי שֶׁלְזָהָב וְדַרְכוֹנוֹת שֶׁלְזָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: The third was the richest since it contained golden stateres and golden darics.

*Shelishit hi hayta ha-ashira she-bekulan* — the third collection was the richest of all. *She-haya bo istariyot shel zahav* — it contained these gold *istariyot* (possibly a type of gold coin or, according to some, fine garments), *darkonot shel zahav* — and gold *darkonot*. As discussed earlier in the masechta, when contributions were being transported from great distances, it was practical to convert them into more valuable denominations, which is why the third collection — from Babylon and the distant lands — arrived in gold. Strictly speaking, converting to gold does not necessarily mean the total monetary value was greater; one is simply expressing the same wealth in higher-denomination coins. Nevertheless, there are independent reasons to think the third collection genuinely represented greater wealth. First, it had the longest window of collection, from Sukkot until Pesach — approximately half the year. Second, it is well attested that the financial situation of the Jewish community in Bavel was substantially stronger than in *Eretz Yisrael*. Third, the sheer geographic spread and likely population of the diaspora communities in Babylon and beyond may have exceeded that of *Eretz Yisrael* itself. Those who chose not to return from exile were in many cases economically integrated and prosperous. The third collection, therefore, was almost certainly wealthier not merely in coin type but in aggregate value — which would explain why it also had to last the longest, sustaining the *Beis HaMikdash* operations for the six months between Sukkot and Pesach, while the first collection only had to last the two months from Pesach to Shavuot.

## Shirayim Status (II)

### Basket Cycle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. נָטַל מִן הָרִאשׁוֹנָה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה נוֹטֵל מִן הַשְּׁנִיָיה. נָטַל מִן הַשְּׁנִיָיה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּשְּׁנִיָיה נוֹטֵל מִן הַשְּׁלִישִׁית. שָׁלְמָה שְׁלִישִׁית חוֹזֵר לַשְּׁנִיָיה. שָׁלְמָה שְׁנִיָיה חוֹזֵר לְרִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: If he took from the first box, even if there was in the first box he shall take from the second. If he took from the second box, even if there was in the second box he shall take from the third. If the third was used up, he returns to the second; if the second was used up he returns to the first.

A *baraita* details the cycling procedure among the three baskets used at each removal. *Natal min harishona af al pi sheyesh barishona natal min hashniya* — they took from basket one even though there was still some left in basket one, and moved to basket two; *natal min hashniya af al pi sheyesh bashniya natal min hashlishis* — and from basket two even though there was still some left, to basket three. This refers to the three baskets from the Pesach removal that needed to fund the *korbanos* until Shavuot. They would cycle through them: use basket one, then two, then three — *shalma shlishis chozer lishniya* — when basket three was exhausted, return to basket two — *shalma shniya chozer lishona* — when two was done, return to basket one.

### Exhausted Baskets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלְמוּ שְׁלָשְׁתָּן חוֹזֵר וְשׁוֹקֵל. רִבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר. חוֹזֵר לִשְׁיֵרִיים.
>
> **Translation:** If all three are emptied, one collects new *sheqalim*; Rebbi Meïr says, he has regress on the remainder.

*Shalmu shloshtan* — if all three baskets were exhausted before the next *terumah* was due, what then? *Chozer veshokel* — one returns and collects anew. The precise mechanics of this are not fully clarified: since contributions would be arriving continuously from outlying regions, there would likely be funds available, but the exact procedure of *chozer veshokel* — whether it means soliciting new contributions specifically or simply drawing on funds already received but not yet formally included — remains unclear.

### Meir: Use Shirayim

Rabbi Meir has a simpler solution: *chozer lishirayim* — return to the remainder funds, the *shirayim*. If the three baskets are exhausted, simply draw on the money left behind from the prior removal.

### Sanctified?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה רִבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר. מוֹעֲלִין בִּשְׁיֵרִיִים שֶׁמָּא יִצְרְכוּ לָהֶן בַּסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** Since Rebbi Meïr says, one commits *me`ilah* with the remainder, perhaps one needs them at the end.

Rabbi Meir continues: *mo'ilin b'shirayim* — *me'ilah* applies to the *shirayim*, because *shema yitztarchu lahem b'sof* — you may need to draw on those funds again. This hints at a fundamental disagreement about the status of the remainder funds. According to the *chakhamim* who dispute Rabbi Meir, the *shirayim* lose their *kedushah* after the *terumah* is taken — analogous to the *terumah* of grain model: after *terumah* is removed from *tevel*, the remaining grain becomes *chullin*. So too the remaining funds, according to the *chakhamim*, revert to a non-*kodesh* status, available for the *Beis HaMikdash*'s discretionary operating expenses but no longer subject to *me'ilah* as consecrated *korban* funds. Rabbi Meir rejects this: the remainder funds remain *kodesh*, *me'ilah* applies to them, and one may therefore draw on them if needed.

What is remarkable — as the lecturer notes in closing — is what the *chakhamim*'s position implies: they constructed this elaborate ritual called *terumat halishka*, modeled on the *terumah* of grain, with the consequence that after the *terumah* act the remaining funds are formally desanctified. They truly converted what was essentially a logistical transfer of communal money into a ritual with real halakhic consequences for the sanctity of the funds left behind.

## Third Collection (II)

### Pinchas Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְכֵן הָיָה רִבִּי פִינְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר אוֹמֵר. זְרִיזוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי נְקִיּוּת. נְקִיּוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי טַהֳרָה. טַהֳרָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי קְדוּשָּׁה. קְדוּשָּׁה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי עֲנָוָה. עֲנָוָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי יִרְאַת חֵטְא. יִרְאַת חֵטְא מְבִיאָה לִידֵי חֲסִידוּת. חֲסִידוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ. רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ מֵבִיא לִידֵי תְחִיַית הַמֵּתִים. תְּחִיַית הַמֵּתִים מְבִיאָה לִידֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ זָכוּר לַטּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** "And so did Rebbi Phineas ben Yair say, promptitude brings to cleanliness, cleanliness brings to purity, purity brings to holiness, holiness brings to meekness, meekness brings to fear of sin, fear of sin brings to piety, piety brings to the Holy Spirit, the Holy Spirit to the Resurrection of the Dead, the Resurrection of the Dead brings to Elijah, may his remembrance be a blessing."

The *Gemara* connects the foregoing discussion of Rabbi Meir to a famous statement attributed to him in the name of Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair — a statement whose structure forms the organizing principle of the *Sefer Mesillas Yesharim*. *V'chein hayah Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair omer* — this is what Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair used to say:

*Zerizus mevia l'idei neki'os* — alacrity brings to cleanliness. *Neki'os mevia l'idei taharah* — cleanliness brings to purity. *Taharah mevia l'idei kedushah* — purity brings to holiness. *Kedushah mevia l'idei anavah* — holiness brings to humility. *Anavah mevia l'idei yiras cheit* — humility brings to fear of sin. *Yiras cheit mevia l'idei chasidus* — fear of sin brings to *chasidus*, which denotes some combination of scrupulousness, generosity, and high piety. *Chasidus mevia l'idei Ruach HaKodesh* — that brings to divine inspiration. *Ruach HaKodesh mevia l'idei techiyas hameitim* — divine inspiration brings to resurrection of the dead. *Techiyas hameitim mevia l'idei Eliyahu zachor latov* — and resurrection brings about Elijah, who is bound up with the entire process of *mashiach* and final redemption.

The connection to Rabbi Meir's statement is admittedly tenuous. The suggestion is that Rabbi Meir, in insisting that the remainder funds remain *kodesh* so that they may be drawn upon if needed, exhibits a kind of careful *zerizus* — covering all bases, leaving nothing unguarded — and it is this quality of care and vigilance that serves as the opening link in Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair's chain.

### Pinchas Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* זְרִיזוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי נְקִיּוּת. דִּכְתִיב וְכִלָּה מִכַּפֵּר. נְקִיּוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי טַהֳרָה. דִּכְתִיב וְכִפֶּר עָלֶיהָ הַכֹּהֵן וְטָהֵרָה. טַהֳרָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי קְדוּשָּׁה. דִּכְתִיב וְטִהֲרָהּ וְקִידְּשָׁהּ. קְדוּשָּׁה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי עֲנָוָה. דִּכְתִיב כִּי כֹה אמַר רָם וְנִשָּׂא שׁוֹכֵן עַד וְקָדוֹשׁ שְׁמוֹ מָרוֹם… וְאֶת דַּכָּא וּשְׁפַל רוּחַ. עֲנָוָה מְבִיאָה לִידֵי יִרְאַת חֵטְא. דִּכְתִיב עֲנָוָה יִרְאַת ה׳. יִרְאַת חֵטְא מְבִיאָה לִידֵי חֲסִידוּת. דִּכְתִיב וְחֶסֶד ה׳ מֵעוֹלָם וְעַד עוֹלָם עַל יְרֵיאָיו. חֲסִידוּת מְבִיאָה לִידֵי רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ. דִּכְתִיב אָז דִּבַּרְתָּ בְחָזוֹן לַחֲסִידֶיךָ. רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ מֵבִיא לִידֵי תְחִיַית הַמֵּתִים. דִּכְתִיב וְנָתַתִּי רוּחִי בָכֶם וִחְיִיתֶם. תְּחִיַית הַמֵּתִים מְבִיאָה לִידֵי אֵלִיָּהוּ זָכוּר לַטּוֹב. דִּכְתֵיב אָז תָּבִין יִרְאַת ה׳ וְדַעַת עֶלְיוֹן תִּמְצָא.
>
> **Translation:** "Promptitude brings to cleanliness," as it is written, *he finishes, and he atones*. "Cleanliness brings to purity," as it is written, *the Cohen shall atone for her, then she will be pure*. "Purity brings to holiness," as it is written, *he shall purify it and sanctify it*. "Holiness brings to meekness," as it is written, *for so says the High and Elevated One, Who thrones eternally, His name is Holy, in sublimity … and the oppressed and of meek spirit.* "Meekness brings to fear of sin," as it is written, *the consequence of meekness is fear of the Eternal*. "Fear of sin brings to piety," as it is written, *the Eternal's piety is eternally on those who fear Him*. "Piety brings to the Holy Spirit," as it is written, *then You spoke in a vision to Your pious ones*. "The Holy Spirit brings to the Resurrection of the Dead," as it is written, *I shall give My Spirit into you and you will live*. "The Resurrection of the Dead brings to Elijah, may his remembrance be a blessing," as it is written, *then you will understand the fear of the Eternal, and the knowledge of God you will find*.

The *Gemara* supplies a scriptural proof for each link in the chain. *Zerizus mevia l'idei neki'os dechsiv ve-chilah mikapeir* — perhaps reading *ve-chilah* as *be-chulah*, doing something completely and fully, which leads to a cleansing. *Neki'os mevia l'idei taharah dechsiv ve-chiper aleha ha-kohen ve-taherah* — he atoned and she became pure. *Taharah mevia l'idei kedushah dechsiv ve-tiharo ve-kidsho* — this connection is transparent: *taharah* is a precondition for entering the *Beis HaMikdash* and encountering the holy. *Kedushah mevia l'idei anavah dechsiv ki cho amar ram ve-nisa shochein ad ve-kadosh shemo marom ve-kadosh eshkon ve-es daka ushfal ruach* — God, who is most exalted and holy, directs his presence toward the lowly and humble in spirit. So holiness — understood through God's own attribute of holiness — translates into a turn toward humility. *Anavah mevia l'idei yiras cheit dechsiv eikev anavah yiras Hashem* — as a result of humility comes fear of God. *Yiras cheit mevia l'idei chasidus dechsiv ve-chesed Hashem me'olam ve'ad olam al yere'av* — God's eternal kindness rests upon those who fear him; *yirat cheit* thus flows into *chasidus*, though here *chasidus* is expressed through God's *chesed* rather than the human quality directly. *Chasidus mevia l'idei Ruach HaKodesh dechsiv az dibarta ve-chazon la-chasidecha* — you have spoken in a vision to your pious ones, so the *chasid* merits *Ruach HaKodesh*. *Ruach HaKodesh mevia l'idei techiyas hameitim dechsiv ve-natati ruchi bachem ve-chiyitem* — I will put my spirit within you and you will live again, the vision of Yechezkel, so *Ruach HaKodesh* leads to resurrection. *Techiyas hameitim mevia l'idei Eliyahu dechsiv hinei anochi sholeach lachem et Eliyah ha-navi lifnei bo yom Hashem ha-gadol ve-ha-nora* — behold, I send you Elijah the prophet before the coming of the great and awesome day of God.

## Closing Mishnah

### Fixed in Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּאנָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי מֵאִיר. כָּל מִי שֶׁקָּבוּעַ בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמְדַבֵּר לְשׁוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ וְאוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹתָיו בְּטָהֳרָה וְקוֹרֵא קִרְיַת שְׁמַע בַּבֹּקֶר וּבָעֶרֶב יְהֵא מְבוּשָּׂר שֶׁבֶּן הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the name of Rebbi Meïr: Anybody permanently in the Land of Israel who speaks the holy language, eats its produce in purity, and recites the *Shema`* mornings and evenings is assured of his place in the World to Come.

The *perek* closes with another statement in the name of Rabbi Meir: *kol mi she-kavu'a be-Eretz Yisrael* — whoever is permanently settled in Israel, not merely visiting but truly fixed there, *u-medaber bi-lashon ha-kodesh* — and speaks in the holy tongue, *ve-ochel perotav be-tahara* — and eats his food in *tahara* — threading together the themes of *kedushah* and *tahara* — *ve-koreh Kriyat Shema ba-boker u-va-erev*, and recites *Kriyat Shema* morning and evening — *kemevaser she-ben olam ha-ba hu* — he is assured of being a son of the World to Come.

The connection to the preceding discussion is not immediately transparent, but the statement draws together the themes of the *perek*: the sanctity of the land, the purity with which one conducts oneself, the holy language, and the acknowledgment of God morning and night. With this the *perek* concludes: *hadran lach perek bi-shlosha prakim*.

What we have seen across this entire daf is the thoroughgoing ritualization of *terumat halishka* — what began as a practical accounting procedure for communal Temple funds was transformed into a solemn act with prescribed language, formal questioning, tarping protocols, a *bracha*, geographic designations, and real halakhic consequences for the status of funds left behind. The *chakhamim*'s insistence that the *shirayim* lose their *kedushah* after the act — modeled explicitly on *terumah* of grain — and Rabbi Meir's counter-position that they remain *kodesh* and subject to *me'ilah*, are not merely administrative disagreements. They reflect a fundamental question about the nature of this act: is it a ritual transformation, with the accompanying consequences that once the act is complete the remainder reverts to ordinary status, or is it an ongoing consecration that leaves the entire pool of funds in a perpetually holy state?
