# Yevamot 108 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Miun: Beis Shammai vs. Beis Hillel

### Dispute Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: בְּפָנָיו וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא, אָמְרוּ לָהֶן בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: וַהֲלֹא פִּישׁוֹן הַגַּמָּל מֵיאֲנָה אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו! אָמְרוּ לָהֶן בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְבֵית הִלֵּל: פִּישׁוֹן הַגַּמָּל בְּמִדָּה כְּפוּשָׁה מָדַד, לְפִיכָךְ מָדְדוּ לוֹ בְּמִדָּה כְּפוּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai say:** The refusal must take place specifically **in the presence of** the husband, but Beit Hillel say: Either in his presence or in his absence. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai: But** didn't the **wife of Pishon the camel driver refuse** him **in his absence? Beit Shammai said to Beit Hillel: Pishon the camel driver measured using a defective standard,** as he did not properly take care of the property she brought into the marriage, **and therefore the Sages measured him with a defective standard [*midda kefusha*].** The marriage in that case was annulled by the Sages and the refusal was not treated as a standard refusal.

The daf opens at the first wide line of 107b. *Beis Shammai* holds that *miun* — the right of a minor girl married off without her father to refuse the marriage — is valid only when performed in the husband's presence and before a *beis din*. *Beis Hillel*, by contrast, holds that *miun* is effective whether or not it is performed in his presence, and whether or not it takes place before a *beis din*.

### Camel Driver Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָא אָכֵיל פֵּירֵי, פְּשִׁיטָא נְשׂוּאָה הִיא. וְהָאָמְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי נְשׂוּאָה לָא מְמָאֲנָה! תְּרֵי קִיטְרֵי עֲבַדוּ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Since he was consuming the profits** from her property, **it is obvious that** it is speaking of a case where **she was married,** as a man is not entitled to the profits of the property of his betrothed. **But didn't Beit Shammai say that a married** minor girl **cannot perform refusal?** The Gemara answers in accordance with Beit Shammai's opinion: **They tied him in two knots,** i.e., the Sages punished Pishon in two ways: They permitted the refusal to take place in his absence, and they permitted it even though she was already married to him.

A *braita* records *Beis Hillel*'s counter-proof to *Beis Shammai*: the case of Pishon the camel driver, whose wife performed *miun* in his absence. Given that camel drivers are away for extended periods — a year at a time — it would be exceedingly difficult to insist that *miun* occur in the husband's presence. *Beis Shammai* responds that Pishon measured with a *mida kefusha* — an overturned or upended measure, an idiomatic expression for dealing improperly — and therefore was measured back against him in kind. He had mistreated her property, so they treated him in kind.

### Proportional Response

The phrase *mida kefusha*, as Rashi explains, conveys something like the bottom of the barrel — a reversal, an upending. The specific impropriety, the *gemara* clarifies, is *mi-deka achal perei*: he consumed the produce of her *nichsei milug*, essentially strip-mining her property out of concern that she might perform *miun* and walk away. The rabbis responded by making it easier for her to do precisely that.

### Two Violations Analysis

This raises an immediate difficulty. If he was eating the *peirot*, that implies the marriage had already reached the stage of *nisuin*. But *Beis Shammai* itself holds that a *nisuah* cannot perform *miun* — so there are in fact two violations present: the *miun* was performed outside his presence (*shelo befanav*), and it was performed after *nisuin*. The *gemara* responds: *trei kuteiri avdu bah* — they did him two knots, meaning they penalized him on both counts simultaneously, allowing her *miun* despite its violating both of *Beis Shammai*'s normal conditions.

This is a striking passage. *Tosafot* notes that the *Yerushalmi* asks pointedly: can it really be that simply because someone acts improperly we dissolve his marriage? *Tosafot* responds that since *kiddushei ketanah* is entirely rabbinic in origin, the rabbis retain the authority to uproot those *kiddushin* — *afkinu rabbanan l'kiddushei minei*. Yet even *Tosafot* acknowledges this is somewhat different from the standard cases of *hafka'at kiddushin*, where the *kiddushin* themselves were invalid from the outset. Here, *Beis Shammai* is granting special *miun* rights on a case-by-case basis in response to his improper conduct — a genuinely unusual application of rabbinic authority.

One participant raised an intriguing alternative reading of the *befanav* requirement: perhaps his presence is not a formal condition of the *miun* act itself — the way, say, the *get* must reach the woman or the *kiddushin* money must reach her hand — but rather a procedural protection of his rights, analogous to how testimony is not given in someone's absence. On this reading, the *befanav* requirement is less about the mechanics of the act and more about safeguarding his interests. This framing, however, would not resolve the separate problem the *gemara* raises about *nisuin*, though it offers an interesting way of conceptualizing the first requirement.

## Chalitza & Miun: Three Witness Rule

### Three-Witness Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הַחֲלִיצָה וְהַמֵּיאוּנִין בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַבָּה: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בֵּית הִלֵּל, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי בֵּית הִלֵּל אֶלָּא דְּלָא בָּעֵינַן מוּמְחִין, אֲבָל שְׁלֹשָׁה בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Sanhedrin* 2a): ***Ḥalitza* and refusals** take place **in the presence of three** judges. The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught this? Rabba said: It is Beit Shammai** who say that refusal must take place specifically before a court. **Abaye said: You** can **even say** that it is **Beit Hillel. Beit Hillel state only that we do not require expert judges** for a refusal, **but we do require three** upright people, who constitute a court of laymen.

The *gemara* transitions to a *mishna* from *Sanhedrin*: *ha-chalitza veha-miun bi-shlosha* — both *chalitza* and *miun* require three. The *gemara* asks: who is the author of this *mishna*? Its ascription of a three-member panel to *miun* reflects the position of *Beis Shammai*. Accordingly, Rabbah identifies the *mishna* as authored according to *Beis Shammai*.

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין. וְאֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ מוֹדִים שֶׁצָּרִיךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁירִין בִּשְׁנַיִם. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּאוֹתוֹ הַזּוּג.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Beit Shammai say: Before a court, and Beit Hillel say:** Either **before a court or not before a court, but both this** school **and that** school **concede that three** people **are required. Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, validate** a refusal in the presence **of two. Rav Yosef bar Manyumi said** that **Rav Naḥman said: The *halakha* is in accordance with that pair.**

Abaye disagrees. He maintains that the *mishna* can even be attributed to *Beis Hillel*. When *Beis Hillel* says *miun* does not require a *beis din*, that means it does not require *mumchin* — ordained, expert judges — but three witnesses are still needed. A *braita* makes this explicit: *Beis Shammai* requires a *beis din*; *Beis Hillel* permits *miun* before or without a formal *beis din*; yet both houses agree (*ve-elu ve-elu modim*) that three are required.

### Two-Witness Minority

Against this consensus, Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Elazar son of Rabbi Shimon hold that *miun* with only two suffices. And the ruling follows them: Rav Nachman, cited by Yosef bar Minyumi, states that the *halacha* is in accordance with that pair — in contrast to *chalitza*, where the view of fewer than three was explicitly rejected.

## Miun Eligibility Standards

### Multiple Miun Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: תְּמָאֵן וְכוּ׳. וְהָא מֵיאֲנָה חֲדָא זִימְנָא! אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עַד שֶׁתַּגְדִּיל וְתֹאמַר: ״רוֹצָה אֲנִי בַּמֵּיאוּנִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai say: She refuses** once. **And** then **she must wait until she reaches majority, and refuse, and marry.** The Gemara asks: **Didn't she** already **refuse once?** Why must she refuse again? **Shmuel said:** Beit Shammai's statement means: The refusal does not take effect **until she reaches majority and says: I wish** to uphold **my initial refusal,** in case she changed her mind in the interim.

The *mishna* asks whether a *ketana* can perform *miun* repeatedly. *Beis Hillel* permits it; *Beis Shammai* objects that allowing a minor to marry, perform *miun*, marry again, and perform *miun* again renders *benot Yisrael hefker* — treats Jewish women as disposable. The language of the *mishna* on this point is somewhat ambiguous: it speaks of her performing *miun*, then waiting until she grows up, or alternatively performing *miun* and then marrying immediately. This led to two readings.

### Reaffirmation Upon Maturation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר, תַּרְתֵּי קָתָנֵי: אוֹ שֶׁתְּמָאֵן וְתַגְדִּיל וְתֵיאָרֵס, אוֹ שֶׁתְּמָאֵן וְתִנָּשֵׂא לְאַלְתַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: Two** different possibilities **are taught** in Beit Shammai's statement: **Either she should refuse, and** then once she **has matured** she **should become betrothed; or she should refuse and marry immediately.** She should not refuse and then only become betrothed again. According to Beit Shammai, as a minor, she may not refuse again.

Shmuel reads the *mishna* as requiring that after performing *miun* as a minor, the girl must, upon reaching adulthood, reaffirm her *miun* — she must say *rotzah ani be-miyvunin ha-rishonim*, "I affirm my earlier refusal." This is a striking idea with no prior parallel: a kind of confirmation, ensuring that the act of *miun* performed during minority is endorsed by the adult woman. The *gemara* objects that the *mishna*'s language does not express this clearly — it should have said she "reaffirms" rather than she "does *miun*" — and acknowledges this as a difficulty (*kashya*).

Ulla offers an alternative reading: the *mishna* is presenting two options (*tarte katani*). Either she performs *miun* and waits until she becomes an adult before marrying again, or she performs *miun* and marries again immediately — but only on condition that this second marriage includes *nisuin* straightaway. Why would *Beis Shammai* require immediate *nisuin* in the second scenario? Because according to *Beis Shammai*, once *nisuin* has taken place, *miun* is no longer available. So if she wants to remarry as a minor, *Beis Shammai* insists that the marriage be consummated immediately with *nisuin*, thereby foreclosing the possibility of a second *miun*. The ו in the *mishna* is thus read as "or," presenting two alternatives — a reading also supported by a *braita* that phrases them disjunctively.

### Minor's Consent Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵי זוֹ הִיא קְטַנָּה שֶׁצְּרִיכָה לְמָאֵן — כֹּל שֶׁהִשִּׂיאוּהָ אִמָּהּ וְאַחֶיהָ לְדַעְתָּהּ. הִשִּׂיאוּהָ שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעְתָּהּ — אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה לְמָאֵן. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: כׇּל תִּינוֹקֶת שֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לִשְׁמוֹר קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ — אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה לְמָאֵן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Who is a minor girl who needs to perform refusal** in order to annul her marriage? **Any** minor **whose mother or brother married her off with her consent. If they married her off without her consent, she need not refuse** her husband at all and may leave her husband without a declaration of refusal. **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says: Any girl who** is so young that she **cannot keep her betrothal,** i.e., the money or document of betrothal, **safe does not need to refuse,** as the Sages instituted marriage only for a girl old enough to understand what she is doing.

The next *mishna* asks: which minor must perform *miun*? The answer: one whose mother and brothers married her off *l'da'atah* — with her knowledge and consent. This is a remarkable requirement. When a father marries off his daughter, her personal awareness and consent are irrelevant — she could be an infant. Here, by contrast, the entire rabbinic institution is conditioned on her participation. The *Rambam*, it should be noted, largely drops the reference to the mother and brothers in his formulation, framing this institution more directly as the girl marrying herself off, with the mother and brothers acting alongside her rather than independently.

The inverse case is likewise stated: if the mother and brothers married her off *shelo l'da'atah*, without her knowledge, she does not require *miun* — the marriage carries no binding force even at the rabbinic level.

### Capacity to Protect

Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos adds a further threshold: any girl who is too young to be able to watch over (*lishmor*) her *kiddushin* does not require *miun*. If she is so young that she would simply drop or lose the token of *kiddushin* — unable to grasp its value or preserve it — then the *kiddushin* cannot take effect even rabbinically. This parallels a similar principle found in the law of *get*, where the recipient must have sufficient awareness to protect her *get*. The conceptual thrust is striking: the minor is being configured not merely as a passive recipient of the marriage act but as an active participant in it — almost as an agent.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַעֲשֵׂה קְטַנָּה כְּלוּם, אֶלָּא כִּמְפוּתָּה. בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן — לֹא תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה, בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — תֹּאכַל בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: The act of a minor girl is nothing,** so that if a minor girl's mother or brothers marry her off, the marriage is essentially invalid. **Rather,** her status is **as though she were** a **seduced** unmarried woman. Therefore, a minor **daughter of a non-priest married to a priest may not eat *teruma*,** and **the** minor **daughter of a priest married to an Israelite may eat *teruma*.**

Rabbi Eliezer takes a radical position: *ein ma'aseh ketanah klum* — the acts of a minor are legally void. He does not deny that something occurs when a minor is married off by her mother and brothers with her consent, but he refuses to characterize it as *kiddushin d'rabbanan*. Instead, he likens her status to that of a *mefutah* — a seduced woman. The rabbis tolerate the couple living together, but they do not recognize the arrangement as any form of rabbinic marriage.

This position has a pointed internal logic. The *mishna* has been moving steadily toward treating the minor as the operative agent of her own marriage — her consent, her receipt of *kiddushin*, her capacity to protect it. Rabbi Eliezer accepts that trajectory and draws its conclusion: if she is the operative agent, her act cannot create binding *kiddushin*, because a *ketanah* has no legal capacity to perform binding acts. Therefore, what exists between them is not quasi-marriage but an unrecognized cohabitation. The practical consequences are total: the husband is not entitled to her earnings (*metziatah*), the produce of her property (*peirot*), or the annulment of her vows (*hafrasat nedarim*); he does not inherit her; and if he is a *kohen*, he does not become *tamei* for her. She is not his wife in any sense — except that if she wishes to terminate the arrangement, she requires *miun*. As for *trumah*: if she is a *bat Yisrael* married to a *kohen*, she may not eat *trumah*; if she is a *bat kohen* married to a *Yisrael*, she continues to eat *trumah*. In neither case is she treated as a married woman.

## Miun: Documentation & Expression

### Document Reform

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ כּוֹתְבִין גֵּט מֵיאוּן: ״לָא רָעֵינָא בֵּיהּ וְלָא צָבֵינָא בֵּיהּ וְלֵית אֲנָא בָּעֲיָא לְהִתְנַסְבָא לֵיהּ״. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דִּנְפִישׁ דִּיבּוּרָא, אָמְרִי: אָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בְּגִיטָּא. תַּקִּינוּ הָכִי: ״בְּיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי מֵיאֲנָה פְּלוֹנִית בַּת פְּלוֹנִי בְּאַנְפַּנָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said, and some say it was taught in a *baraita*: At first, they would write a bill of refusal in this manner: I do not desire him, I do not want him, and I do not wish to be married to him. Once they saw that the text was too long, the** Sages **said:** This document **may come to be confused with a bill of divorce** and perhaps a man will err and give a bill of divorce using the text of refusal. Therefore, **they decreed** that one should write **as follows: On such and such a day, so-and-so, the daughter of so-and-so, performed refusal in our presence,** and no more.

Rav Yehuda, and a *braita* corroborates this, reports that originally the document recording an act of *miun* was written in an elaborate formula: *lo ra'ina beih* — I do not desire him; *v'lo tzavina beih* — I do not want him; *v'leis ana beih l'hitnasva leih* — I do not wish to marry him. This lengthy formulation was ultimately shortened. The reason: *ati l'chlufi b'gita* — scribes would confuse it with a *get*. Both Rashi and *Tosafot* explain this not as a fear that onlookers would mistake the woman for a *gerushah*, but rather that an ignorant *sofer*, having been called to write a *get miun* in its long form, would later be called to write a *get gerushin* and, remembering only the length and formality of the first document, would write the wrong type. This explanation is admittedly puzzling — a *get* is typically a far longer and more formalized document — but that is how the *rishonim* read it.

## Miun: Documentation & Standard Formula

### Standard Formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ כּוֹתְבִין גֵּט מֵיאוּן: ״לָא רָעֵינָא בֵּיהּ וְלָא צָבֵינָא בֵּיהּ וְלֵית אֲנָא בָּעֲיָא לְהִתְנַסְבָא לֵיהּ״. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזוֹ דִּנְפִישׁ דִּיבּוּרָא, אָמְרִי: אָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בְּגִיטָּא. תַּקִּינוּ הָכִי: ״בְּיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי מֵיאֲנָה פְּלוֹנִית בַּת פְּלוֹנִי בְּאַנְפַּנָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said, and some say it was taught in a *baraita*: At first, they would write a bill of refusal in this manner: I do not desire him, I do not want him, and I do not wish to be married to him. Once they saw that the text was too long, the** Sages **said:** This document **may come to be confused with a bill of divorce** and perhaps a man will err and give a bill of divorce using the text of refusal. Therefore, **they decreed** that one should write **as follows: On such and such a day, so-and-so, the daughter of so-and-so, performed refusal in our presence,** and no more.

The shortened standard form retained only the essential: on such-and-such a day, *mi'anah plonit bat ploni* — so-and-so the daughter of so-and-so performed *miun* — *b'apana*, in our presence. Nothing more.

## Valid Miun Contexts

### Absence & Informality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵי זֶהוּ מֵיאוּן? אָמְרָה: ״אִי אֶפְשִׁי בִּפְלוֹנִי בַּעְלִי״, ״אִי אֶפְשִׁי בְּקִידּוּשִׁין שֶׁקִּידְּשׁוּנִי אִמִּי וְאַחַי״. יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ יוֹשֶׁבֶת בְּאַפִּרְיוֹן וְהוֹלֶכֶת מִבֵּית אָבִיהָ לְבֵית בַּעְלָהּ, וְאָמְרָה: ״אִי אֶפְשִׁי בִּפְלוֹנִי בַּעְלִי״ — זֶהוּ מֵיאוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: What** constitutes **a refusal?** If **she said: I do not want so-and-so as my husband,** or: **I do not want the betrothal in which my mother and brothers had me betrothed,** that is a refusal. **Rabbi Yehuda said more than that: Even if she is sitting in a bridal chair [*apiryon*] going from her father's house to her husband's house and said** along the way: **I do not want so-and-so as my husband, this** constitutes **a refusal.**

A *braita* then defines what constitutes an act of *miun*. The girl says either *ee efshi b'ploni ba'ali* — I do not want so-and-so my husband — or *ee efshi b'kiddushin she'kiddushuni imi va'achai* — I do not want the marriage that my mother and brothers put me into. Beyond this, Rabbi Yehuda adds a series of expanding cases demonstrating just how informally *miun* can be expressed.

### Carriage Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ יוֹשֶׁבֶת בְּאַפִּרְיוֹן וְהוֹלֶכֶת מִבֵּית אָבִיהָ לְבֵית בַּעְלָהּ, וְאָמְרָה: ״אִי אֶפְשִׁי בִּפְלוֹנִי בַּעְלִי״ — זֶהוּ מֵיאוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said** even **more than that: Even if she is sitting in a bridal chair [*apiryon*] going from her father's house to her husband's house and said** along the way: **I do not want so-and-so as my husband, this** constitutes **a refusal,** even though it is possible that she is merely complaining about the effort she is expending.

Even if she is sitting in the wedding carriage (*apiryon*), being conveyed from her father's house to her husband's house, and while still in transit she says *ee efshi b'ploni ba'ali* — this constitutes *miun*. One might have thought: if she truly does not want this marriage, she should stop the procession and leave the carriage. The ruling is that even her statement from within the carriage, without halting the ceremony, is sufficient.

### Serving Guests Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ הָיוּ אוֹרְחִין מְסוּבִּין בְּבֵית בַּעְלָהּ וְהִיא עוֹמֶדֶת וּמַשְׁקָה עֲלֵיהֶם, וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם: ״אִי אֶפְשִׁי בִּפְלוֹנִי בַּעְלִי״ — הֲרֵי הוּא מֵיאוּן. יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה: אֲפִילּוּ שִׁיגְּרָהּ בַּעְלָהּ אֵצֶל חֶנְוָנִי לְהָבִיא לוֹ חֵפֶץ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְאָמְרָה: ״אִי אֶפְשִׁי בִּפְלוֹנִי בַּעְלִי״ — אֵין לְךָ מֵיאוּן גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said** even **more than that: Even if guests are reclining at her husband's house and she is standing and serving them drinks** as hostess, **and** she **said to them: I do not want so-and-so as my husband, this** constitutes **a refusal,** even though it is possible that she is merely complaining about the effort she is expending. **Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda said more than that: Even if her husband sent her to a shopkeeper to bring him an article of his and she said: I do not want so-and-so as my husband, there is no greater refusal than this.**

More striking: even if she is already in her husband's home, guests are gathered, and she is standing and serving them drinks — and in the midst of doing so she says *ee efshi b'ploni ba'ali* — this too is *miun*. Rashi identifies the *chiddush* here as the absence of any *beis din*. But the broader force of the case is that she is, at that moment, visibly performing the role of a wife, yet her verbal declaration suffices to dissolve the marriage. The question of whether she is expressing genuine legal will or merely marital frustration appears to be deliberately sidelined.

### Storekeeper Complaint

Further still: if her husband sends her to the storekeeper to collect something on his behalf, and while there she says *ee efshi* in reference to her husband — complaining, as it were, to the shopkeeper about the man who sent her on this errand — *ein lach miun gadol mizeh*: there is no greater *miun* than this. It qualifies.

### Will-Centered Principle

This is a genuinely striking series of rulings. The formal requirements appear to dissolve almost entirely: no *beis din*, no solemnity, no procedural acknowledgment that a legal act is taking place. The context in each case might easily be read as mere venting or frustration rather than a considered legal declaration. Yet the *gemara* insists each qualifies.

One participant raised the question of how this squares with the ruling, agreed upon even by *Beis Hillel*, that three witnesses are required. Rashi notes the tension. It may be that Rabbi Yehuda here represents a minority view that no *beis din* is required at all — not merely that *mumchin* are unnecessary, but that three are not required either. But part of the *chiddush* is conceptual, not merely technical: even where three witnesses happen to be present (guests, bystanders at the store), they are not constituted as a *beis din* and the statement is not framed as a legal act. Yet it counts.

Another participant offered an interesting perspective: perhaps these rulings are not about prospective *miun* but about retrospective identification. That is, if at a later point one needs to determine whether she had performed *miun* during her minority — say, she is now an adult and her husband refuses to give a *get*, and it is in her interest to establish that she had already performed *miun* while a *ketanah* — the *gemara* is teaching that these earlier statements, made in contexts that seemed informal or incidental, can be identified *ex post facto* as valid acts of *miun*. This reading would resolve some of the tension, though it is not how the *rishonim* generally frame these cases.

What the cases collectively express is the principle embedded in the *mishna*'s requirement of *l'da'atah*: the institution of *kiddushei ketanah* without a father was constructed as fundamentally grounded in her will, and therefore its dissolution is also anchored entirely in her will. All of her actions as wife may point in one direction; her words point the other way. The *gemara*'s formulation — *ein lach miun gadol mizeh*, there is no greater *miun* than this — is not ironic but precisely literal: the more embedded in the wifely role she appears, the more forceful her verbal rejection becomes as an expression of autonomous will.

## Miun Via Alternative Acts

### Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: קְטַנָּה שֶׁלֹּא מֵיאֲנָה, וְעָמְדָה וְנִשֵּׂאת, מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה אָמְרוּ: נִישּׂוּאֶיהָ הֵן הֵן מֵיאוּנֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught:** In the case of **a minor girl who did not refuse** her husband, **but who went and married** someone else, **it was said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira: Her** new **marriage constitutes her refusal,** as she made her state of mind known, that she does not want him, and that is sufficient.

The *gemara* records that Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira holds: if a minor who was married without her father's involvement neither performs a formal *miun* but instead stands up and marries another man, her act of *nisuin* with the second man itself constitutes her *miun* from the first. The *mishna* states this regarding *nisuin*; a subsequent *braita* extends it to *kiddushin* as well: if she accepts *kiddushin* from a second man, that too is her *miun* from the first.

### Kiddushin vs. Nisuin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: נִתְקַדְּשָׁה, מַהוּ? תָּא שְׁמַע: קְטַנָּה שֶׁלֹּא מֵיאֲנָה וְעָמְדָה וְנִתְקַדְּשָׁה — מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה אָמְרוּ: קִידּוּשֶׁיהָ הֵן הֵן מֵיאוּנֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha* **if she was betrothed** to another man without performing refusal of the first husband? Is her acceptance of the betrothal sufficient to indicate that she refuses the first husband? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an answer from a *baraita*: If **a minor girl did not refuse** her husband **but went and became betrothed** to another man, then, as the Sages **said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira: Her betrothal constitutes her refusal.**

The *gemara* then poses a multi-layered question (*ibaya lehu*): Do the *rabbanan* dispute Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira at all? If they do, is their dispute limited to the *kiddushin* case, or does it extend even to *nisuin*? And if there is a dispute, do we rule like Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira — and if so, in which cases?

### Kiddushin as Miun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה אוֹ לָא? אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר פְּלִיגִי: בְּקִידּוּשִׁין, אוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בְּנִישּׂוּאִין? וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר פְּלִיגִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּנִישּׂוּאִין: הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ? וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ: בְּנִישּׂוּאִין אוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בְּקִידּוּשִׁין?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Do the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira or not?** And further, **if you say** that **they do disagree** with him, do they disagree with him **with regard to betrothal** alone, **or** do they **also** disagree with him **with regard to marriage? And if you say** that **they disagree even with regard to marriage, is the *halakha* in accordance with his opinion or is the *halakha* not in accordance with his opinion? And if you say that the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion, is** this only **with regard to marriage, or is** it **even with regard to betrothal?**

The resolution comes from Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira*. The word *halacha* itself implies that the *rabbanan* disagree — for one rules *halacha* against an opposing position. Both Rashi and *Tosafot* infer from context that this ruling covers both the *kiddushin* and *nisuin* scenarios: we rule like Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira in both directions, meaning that even mere *kiddushin* from a second man constitutes *miun* from the first, and we assume the *rabbanan* contest this in both cases.

A further question then arises: does it matter what the nature of her relationship with the first man was? Is the ruling that *kiddushin* from guy number two constitutes *miun* from guy number one applicable even where she had already reached *nisuin* with guy number one — a far more actualized form of marriage? The *gemara* seeks to resolve this from a specific case.

### Avdin's Daughters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה. הֲלָכָה — מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a tradition: **Come** and **hear: Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira** with regard to both marriage and betrothal. From the fact that he ruled the ***halakha*,** one may derive **by inference** that the Rabbis **disagree.**

The daughters-in-law of Avdin — the same Avdin, as one participant noted, who appears earlier in the *daf* in connection with the humiliation of Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi — had married Avdin's sons while minors, without paternal authorization. Both performed *miun* against their husbands. Rabbi sent a pair of *rabbanan* to investigate — likely either because he did not trust the report or because he wanted to assess whether the statements were genuine expressions of will or merely complaints.

When the two *rabbanan* approached, some women at the scene told the daughters-in-law that their husbands were coming. The girls, apparently confusing the approaching *rabbanan* for their husbands, responded: *le-havu gavreichu didchu lanan lav gavreinu* — let them be your husbands; we have no need of them. Rabbi ruled: *ein lecha miun gadol mi-zeh* — there is no greater *miun* than this.

The *gemara* attempts to use this case to resolve the earlier question: if we can identify these girls as having performed *miun* at the level of a mere verbal rejection addressed to bystanders, and if this occurred after *nisuin* with the first husband, it would demonstrate that even *nisuin* with guy number one does not require a more elevated form of *miun*. The *gemara* ultimately declines the proof, suggesting the marriages may have been at the stage of *kiddushin* only. The ruling, however, stands: *ve-halacha ke-Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira ve-afilu be-nisuin de-kama* — we rule like Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, and even if the relationship with the first man had reached *nisuin*, the standard for *miun* remains accessible and low. The *gemara* makes it very easy for a *ketanah* to exit this institution.

## Rabbi Eliezer's Model

### Consistency Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חוֹזְרַנִי עַל כׇּל צִדְדֵי חֲכָמִים וְלֹא מָצָאתִי אָדָם שֶׁהִשְׁוָה מִדּוֹתָיו בִּקְטַנָּה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. שֶׁעֲשָׂאָהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר כִּמְטַיֶּלֶת עִמּוֹ בֶּחָצֵר, וְעוֹמֶדֶת מֵחֵיקוֹ וְטוֹבֶלֶת וְאוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה לָעֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: I reviewed all the opinions of the Sages** concerning these matters, **and I did not find any person who applied a consistent standard with regard to a minor like Rabbi Elazar** did. **For Rabbi Elazar portrayed her as a girl walking with** her husband **in a courtyard, who stands up from his bosom** after he engaged in intercourse with her, **and immerses** herself to become ritually pure, **and partakes of *teruma* by evening** as if there were no marital bond between them and as if she, as the daughter of a priest, could continue to partake of *teruma*.

The *gemara* returns to Rabbi Eliezer's position. Shmuel, as transmitted by Rav Yehuda, articulates a pointed observation: *chozani kol tzaddei chachamim v'lo matzati adam she'hishveh midotav be-ketanah ke-Rabbi Eliezer* — I have examined all the angles of the sages' positions and found no one as internally consistent in his treatment of the *ketanah* as Rabbi Eliezer.

The consistency problem Rabbi Eliezer identified is this: the institution of *kiddushei ketanah* without a father has been progressively reconfigured so that the minor herself is the operative agent. Her consent, her receipt of *kiddushin*, her capacity to protect it — these are the conditions. If that is the case, then her act cannot create rabbinic *kiddushin*, because a *ketanah*'s legal acts are void. The institution therefore cannot be *kiddushin d'rabbanan*. Instead, Rabbi Eliezer characterizes the relationship as *k'mefutah*: the rabbis tolerate cohabitation without recognizing it as marriage.

### Non-Wife Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַעֲשֵׂה קְטַנָּה כְּלוּם, וְאֵין בַּעְלָהּ זַכַּאי לֹא בִּמְצִיאָתָהּ, וְלֹא בְּמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וְלֹא בַּהֲפָרַת נְדָרֶיהָ, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ, וְאֵין מִיטַּמֵּא לָהּ. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: אֵינָהּ כְּאִשְׁתּוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר, אֶלָּא שֶׁצְּרִיכָה מֵיאוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: The act of a minor girl is nothing,** and therefore her marriage is not valid. **And her husband has no rights to items she finds, nor to her earnings; nor** does he have the right **to annul her vows; he does not inherit her** assets if she dies; **and** if she dies **he may not become ritually impure on her account** if he is a priest, i.e., through his presence in the same room as her corpse. **The principle is:** She is not **his wife in any sense, except that she must perform refusal** in order to marry someone else.

The *gemara* then draws out the full range of legal consequences. The husband is not entitled to her earnings (*metziatah u-ma'aseh yadeha*), cannot annul her vows (*hafrasat nedarim*), does not inherit her (*eino yorshah*), and — if he is a *kohen* — does not become *tamei* for her (*eino mitamei lah*). The governing principle: *eino k'ishto le-chol davar* — she is not his wife in any regard. The one exception: *ella she-tzricha mi'un* — if she wishes to terminate the cohabitation, she still requires *miun*. This is described as being like someone taking a walk in a garden together, sharing a bed, with her thereafter going to the *mikvah* and eating *trumah* (if she is a *bat kohen*) — no marital status attaches, only a cohabitation that can be ended by a simple statement.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: בַּעְלָהּ זַכַּאי בִּמְצִיאָתָהּ, וּבְמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וּבַהֲפָרַת נְדָרֶיהָ, וְיוֹרְשָׁהּ, וּמִיטַּמֵּא לָהּ. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: הֲרֵי הִיא כְּאִשְׁתּוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר, אֶלָּא שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה בְּמֵיאוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** In the case of a minor whose mother or brother married her off, **her husband has rights to items she finds, and to her earnings; and** he has the right **to annul her vows; and he inherits her** assets if she dies; **and** if she dies **he must become ritually impure on her account** even if he is a priest. **The principle is: She is his wife in every sense, except that she can leave** him **by** means of **refusal** and does not require a bill of divorce.

Rabbi Yehoshua takes the diametrically opposite view. The husband is entitled to everything: her finds, her earnings, the right to annul her vows, inheritance rights, and if he is a *kohen*, he becomes *tamei* for her — the *gemara* earlier explained this is treated rabinnically like a *met mitzvah*, despite there being no *d'oraisa* basis. She is *k'ishto le-chol davar* — fully his wife in every respect. The one anomaly: *yotzah be-miun* — she can exit the marriage through *miun* alone, without requiring a *get*.

### Consistency Evaluation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִדִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. שֶׁרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִשְׁוָה מִדּוֹתָיו בִּקְטַנָּה, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ חָלַק. מַאי חָלַק? אִי אִשְׁתּוֹ הִיא — תִּיבְעֵי גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: The statement of Rabbi Eliezer appears** to be more correct **than the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua, as Rabbi Eliezer applied a consistent standard with regard to a minor, while Rabbi Yehoshua applied an inconsistent standard.** The Gemara asks: In **what** way is his standard **inconsistent?** The Gemara answers: **If she is his wife,** she **should require a bill of divorce** from him.

Rabbi — referring here to Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — evaluates the two positions: *nirein divrei Rabbi Eliezer mi-divrei Rabbi Yehoshua* — Rabbi Eliezer's position is more persuasive. The reason is precisely the consistency that Shmuel praised. Rabbi Eliezer *hishveh midotav be-ketanah*: he applied a uniform standard. If she is not a wife, she needs no *get* — and that is what he holds. Rabbi Yehoshua *chillek* — he made a distinction — treating her as a full wife in every respect yet exempting her from the requirement of a *get*. If she is truly his wife, *tibaye get* — she should need a *get*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Rejoinder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר נָמֵי: אִי לָאו אִשְׁתּוֹ הִיא — מֵיאוּן נָמֵי לָא תִּיבְעֵי! אֶלָּא בִּכְדִי תִּיפּוֹק?!
>
> **Translation:** **According to Rabbi Eliezer too,** there appears to be an inconsistency, as, **if she is not his wife, she should not be required to perform refusal either.** The Gemara answers: **But shall she leave with no** ritual at all? Some sort of act is required to indicate that their relationship is permanently severed. Rabbi Eliezer has a consistent standard, according to which the marriage of a minor has no substance and to dissolve it she need only indicate that she does not want her husband. Rabbi Yehoshua is inconsistent in treating the relationship as a marriage even though it can be dissolved easily.

The *gemara* then probes Rabbi Eliezer's own consistency. If she is not his wife at all, why does she require *miun*? The *gemara* responds: *ella b'kadi teipuk* — should she simply walk out with nothing? Even a cohabiting couple that decides to separate requires some formal acknowledgment of the breakup. Rabbi Eliezer's *miun*, on this reading, is not the severing of *kiddushin* but the formal acknowledgment that a live-in relationship has ended. It is not *aishut* at all — not rabbinic marriage — merely a relationship that must be terminated by some act. This, the *gemara* suggests, is an internally consistent position. And it also explains why *miun* is so easy to perform: if there is no real marriage, the act dissolving it need not be a formal legal instrument.

The practical *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Eliezer. We rule that *kiddushei ketanah* without a father does create a form of rabbinic *kiddushin* — a pseudo-*kiddushin*, as it were — from which *miun* is the mechanism of exit.

## Miun vs. Get Status

### Refusal Signals Consent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי עַכָּבָה שֶׁהִיא מִן הָאִישׁ וְעַכָּבָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מִן הָאִישׁ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תְּבָעוּהָ לִינָּשֵׂא, וְאָמְרָה: ״מֵחֲמַת פְּלוֹנִי בַּעְלִי״ — זוֹ הִיא עַכָּבָה שֶׁהִיא מִן הָאִישׁ. ״מֵחֲמַת בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁאֵינָם מְהוּגָּנִין לִי״ — זוֹ הִיא עַכָּבָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מִן הָאִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** If there is any obstruction in the matter due to the man, it is as if she were his wife. If there is any obstruction in the matter that is not due to the man, it is as if she were not his wife. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of an obstruction due to the man, and an obstruction that is not due to the man? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** If someone **proposed marriage to her and she said:** I do not wish to marry **on account of so-and-so, my husband, this is an obstruction that is due to the man.** When she declined the proposal, she made it clear that she views herself as his wife. But if she says: I do not want to marry **because the men** suggested to me **are not suitable for me, this is an obstruction that is not due to the man,** and she is not considered to be his wife.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's statement in the *mishna* — that if she holds back because of the man it is as though she is his wife, and if not, it is not — is cryptic. *Tosafot* offers a reading that places this not in the context of *miun* at all but rather in the context of the *mishna*'s earlier condition: *l'da'atah* — that the *kiddushin* must have been performed with her knowledge and consent. How do we determine after the fact whether she consented?

The answer: if a second suitor approaches her and proposes, and she refuses him saying *machmat ploni ba'ali* — "I cannot marry you, I am already married to so-and-so" — that refusal signals that she regards herself as married to guy number one. She is being *held back* because of him (*akavta de-minei de-ish*). This demonstrates that she consented to the original marriage. Conversely, if she refuses the second suitor by saying *machmat b'nei adam she'einam mehuganim li* — "I would consider marrying someone suitable, but not you" — this indicates she does not regard herself as bound by the first marriage, and therefore she had never truly consented to it.

On *Tosafot*'s reading, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's statement is about identifying consent to *kiddushin* number one, not about the mechanics of *miun*. What she says to a potential second husband is a retrospective signal of her attitude toward the first.

### Consent Indicator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי בַּר אָבִין וְרַב חֲנִינָא בַּר אָבִין דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: נָתַן לָהּ גֵּט — זוֹ הִיא עַכָּבָה שֶׁהִיא מִן הָאִישׁ, וְהוּא אָסוּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ, וְהִיא אֲסוּרָה בִּקְרוֹבָיו, וּפְסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה. מֵיאֲנָה בּוֹ — זוֹ הִיא עַכָּבָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מִן הָאִישׁ, וְהוּא מוּתָּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ, וְהִיא מוּתֶּרֶת בִּקְרוֹבָיו, וְלֹא פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye bar Avin and Rav Ḥanina bar Avin both say: If** the minor's husband **gave her a bill of divorce, this is an obstruction** that is **due to the man,** since in presenting the bill of divorce, the marriage is being treated as valid. Therefore, from then onward, **he is prohibited from** marrying **her close relatives, and she is prohibited from** marrying **his close relatives; and,** as a divorced woman, **she is disqualified from** marrying into **the priesthood.** However, **if she refuses him, this is an obstruction** that is **not due to the man.** Therefore, **he is permitted to** marry **her close relatives, and she is permitted to** marry **his close relatives, and she is not disqualified from the priesthood,** since her refusal annuls the marriage retroactively.

Abaye and Rav Chanina bar Avin both offer a different reading: *natan lah get* — if guy number one gave her a *get* — *zu hi akavta de-minei de-ish*: that shows he was holding her back. Conversely, if she performed *miun*, *zu hi akavta d'lav minei de-ish* — it was she who was determining the outcome, not him. The practical implication of this reading follows immediately.

### Miun After Get

The *mishna* states that if the husband gave his minor wife a bill of divorce but subsequently remarried her and she refused him, and then she married someone else, she is permitted to remarry the first husband when her marriage to the second is concluded. If he gave her a *get* and then remarried her and she subsequently performed *miun*, the *miun* redefines her status retroactively. Even though she had been his *gerushah* after the first *get*, the *miun* following the second marriage erases that status — she is treated as though she was never his *gerushah* at all. Therefore, even after she marries a second man and that marriage dissolves, she may return to guy number one. There is no prohibition of *machzir gerushato*, because the *miun* has effectively rendered the *gerushin* status null.

How does this work conceptually? Rashi's explanation is that the *miun* — by its nature — underlines that she is a *ketanah* and this entire institution is rabbinic. Once she performs *miun* the second time, it retroactively recharacterizes the entire relationship: she was never truly married to him, not even rabbinically. The first *get* disappears along with the marriage it purported to dissolve.

It should be noted that on a *d'oraisa* level, none of this creates any real prohibition in any case, since there was never a *d'oraisa* marriage to begin with. The concerns about *machzir gerushato* and about *gerushin* status for *kehunah* are entirely *mi-derabbanan*.

### Get After Miun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: גֵּט אַחַר מֵיאוּן — אֲסוּרָה לַחְזוֹר לוֹ, מֵיאוּן אַחַר גֵּט — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle** concerning a minor girl who refused her husband and then married several times: **If the bill of divorce followed the refusal** and she remarried, **she is forbidden to return to him. If the refusal followed the bill of divorce, she is permitted to return to him.** Since the refusal followed the bill of divorce it is clear that she was a minor and neither the marriage nor the divorce were valid by Torah law. However, when the ultimate separation is by means of a bill of divorce, there is no indication that she was a minor at the time and there is potential for confusion with an adult divorcée.

The converse: if the sequence is reversed — she performed *miun* first, then he remarried her, and then he gave her a *get* — her most recent status with him is that of a *gerushah*. The *miun* is now in the past; the *get* is the more recent and defining act. She is forbidden to return to him after marrying another man, because she is his *gerushah* and the prohibition of *machzir gerushato* applies. The rule is stated concisely: *get achar miun* — *asura lachzor lo*. *Miun achar get* — *muteret lachzor lo*. Whichever act came last defines her status.

## Multiple Partners Cases

### Three-Partner Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְמָאֶנֶת בָּאִישׁ וְנִשֵּׂאת לְאַחֵר וְגֵירְשָׁהּ, לְאַחֵר וּמֵיאֲנָה בּוֹ, לְאַחֵר וְגֵירְשָׁהּ, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁיּוֹצְאָה הֵימֶנּוּ בְּגֵט — אֲסוּרָה לַחְזוֹר לוֹ, בְּמֵיאוּן — מוּתֶּרֶת לַחְזוֹר לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If a minor girl **refuses** one **man and marries another, and he divorces her,** and then she marries **another** man **and refuses him, and** then she marries **another** man **and he divorces her, this is the principle** for this case: With regard to **anyone she leaves by means of a bill of divorce, it is prohibited for her to return to him.** With regard to anyone she leaves **by means of refusal, she is permitted to return to him.**

The *mishna* then presents a more complex scenario involving three successive husbands. She performs *miun* with guy number one; marries guy number two and receives a *get*; marries guy number three and performs *miun* with him. After all of this, can she return to any of them? The *gemara* works through the implications.

### Same-Partner Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא אָתֵי מֵיאוּן וּמְבַטֵּל גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that if the man gave his minor wife a bill of divorce but subsequently remarried her and she refused him, and then she married someone else, she is permitted to remarry the first husband when her marriage to the second is concluded. **Apparently, refusal comes and nullifies a bill of divorce.**

The *gemara* at first seems to assume a contradiction between the cases: in the first scenario, *miun* following *get* with the same man redefines her as a non-*gerushah*; in the three-partner scenario, *miun* with guy number three does not redefine her status with guy number two. The *gemara* initially suggests (*amru*) that the two halves of the *mishna* reflect a genuine dispute — *mi she-shanah zo lo shanah zo*.

Rava dissolves the apparent contradiction: there is no dispute at all. *Miun dide mevattel get dide* — his own *miun* can retroactively undermine his own earlier *get* — because the *miun* redefines the entire relationship with that specific person. *Miun de-chavrei lo mevattel git dide* — a different man's *miun* cannot undermine a previous man's *get* — because the relationships are distinct. The *miun* with guy number three says nothing about the nature of her relationship with guy number two. This is the obvious answer, and Rava offers it as such.

### Leverage Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר רָבָא: וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא? וְדִלְמָא: מֵיאוּן דִּידֵיהּ מְבַטֵּל גֵּט דִּידֵיהּ, מֵיאוּן דְּחַבְרֵיהּ לָא מְבַטֵּל גִּיטָּא דִידֵיהּ. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵיאוּן דְּחַבְרֵיהּ דְּלָא מְבַטֵּל גִּיטָּא דִידֵיהּ — אַיְּידֵי דְּמַכֶּרֶת בִּרְמִיזוֹתָיו וּקְרִיצוֹתָיו אָזֵל מְשַׁבֵּשׁ וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ, מֵיאוּן דִידֵיהּ נָמֵי לָא לִיבַטֵּל גִּיטָּא דִידֵיהּ — דְּאַיְּידֵי דְּמַכֶּרֶת בִּרְמִיזוֹתָיו וּקְרִיצוֹתָיו אָזֵיל מְשַׁבֵּשׁ וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the difficulty** here? **Perhaps** her **refusal of him nullifies his bill of divorce, while** her **refusal of the other man does not nullify** the original husband's **bill of divorce.** The Gemara asks: In **what** way **is** her **refusal of the other man different, that it does not nullify his bill of divorce?** Isn't it that **because she is familiar with the intimations and gestures [*keritzotav*]** of her first husband, **he will lead her astray and bring her back** to him, by causing her to refuse her new husband and then return to him? Consequently, it was decreed that she may not return to her first husband by refusing the second. But for this same reason **the refusal against** the first husband **himself also should not nullify his** own **bill of divorce, as, since she is familiar with his intimations and gestures, he will lead her astray and bring her back** to him after she has married another man.

Yet the *gemara* presses further. In principle, the *gemara* argues, any act of *miun* — even with a different man — announces that she is a *ketanah* and therefore her entire history of rabbinic marriage is questionable. On pure principle, even another man's *miun* could redefine an earlier *get*. So there must be a positive reason why we do not allow it. The *gemara* provides one: *eidi d'makaras bi-remi'zosav u've-kitzotzav azil meshabesh u-meisei lah* — because she has been intimate with guy number one and knows his manner and his signals, he can go and manipulate her, inducing her to perform *miun* with guy number two or three, which would then free her to return to him. Allowing a different man's *miun* to override her *get* from guy number one would give guy number one leverage to destroy her subsequent marriages.

### Failed Attempt Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כְּבָר שַׁבְּשַׁהּ וְלָא אִישְׁתַּבַּשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **But he already tried to** lead **her astray and she was not** led **astray.** In other words, he already remarried her after the divorce and she still refused him, which proves that he does not have sufficient influence to lead her astray.

This raises a parallel question: why is the same concern not present in the case where the same man's *miun* is allowed to override his earlier *get*? If she performed *miun* from him the second time around and we let her return to him after her subsequent marriage dissolves — has he not also been given the leverage to signal her and bring her back?

The *gemara*'s answer: *hacha tiv sheiv velo meshabesh* — in this case, he already tried and it did not work. The *miun* the second time around was *her* act of rejection. She wanted out, and he could not keep her in the marriage. Given that history, we are not concerned that he will subsequently be able to manipulate her into returning, because she has already demonstrated that she is not under his influence. By contrast, when he was the one to give the *get* — perhaps she wished to remain married but he rejected her — and she then marries someone else, he may retain emotional leverage over her and use it to encourage her to perform *miun* and come back to him.

The *gemara*'s resolution operates on the level of practical social concern — identifying where leverage exists and where it has been negated by prior events. The more formalist explanation would simply observe that a *miun* redefines only the specific relationship in which it occurs. But the *gemara* prefers to ground the rule in the concrete reality of power dynamics between the parties, and to explain both the rule and its limits in those terms.
