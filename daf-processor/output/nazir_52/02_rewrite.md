# Nazir 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Incomplete Animals & Bir…

### Missing Ant Limb

The *daf* for today is 52. We pick up at the bottom of 51b, where the *Gemara* has been engaged with the topic of *rekev* — the dust produced by a decomposed corpse — and the narrow circumstances under which it generates *tumah*: primarily, a naked body in a marble casket, along with any additional criteria the *Gemara* may impose. Three lines from the bottom of 51b, the *Gemara* introduces a question posed by Rava.

*Bei Rava* — Rava asked: if you have an ant (*nemalah*) that is missing a limb, yet remains viable, what is the *halacha*? This question arises from the concept of *biryah* — the principle that consuming an entire creature, even one smaller than a *kezayit*, carries the same legal weight as consuming a *kezayit* of a forbidden substance, because the integrity of a complete entity constitutes a qualitative substitute for the quantitative *shiur*. The question is whether this qualitative status holds when the ant is missing a leg but can still survive, or whether the *biryah* status requires, in the end, translation into a quantitative measure, such that any deficiency renders it incomplete.

More precisely, Rava's question is: *shiura gmarina lei* — do we ultimately translate the *biryah* into a fixed quantity (the volume of the whole ant), so that anything less than that full volume is insufficient? Or do we say *biryah gmarina lei* — that as long as the creature remains a viable, living entity, it retains its qualitative *biryah* status regardless of a missing limb?

### Biryah & Quantity

It should be noted that the law of *biryah* applies to prohibitions of eating, not to the *shiurim* for *tumah*. Nevertheless, the underlying conceptual question — whether *biryah* is an irreducibly qualitative standard or whether it ultimately resolves into a quantitative measure — is what Rava is probing.

The *Gemara* proceeds to seek a resolution. *Amar Rav Yehuda miDiskharta* — Rav Yehuda of Diskharta says: *ta shema*, come and hear.

### Sheretz & Lentil

The *Gemara* returns to the domain of *tumah*, specifically to the *sheretz* — a creeping creature whose carcass conveys impurity. Two *pesukim* are at play: one says *kol asher yiga bahem* ("whatever touches them"), suggesting contact with the entire creature; the other says *kol asher yipol mehem* ("whatever falls from them"), suggesting that even a part suffices. The *Gemara* resolves this tension: *bahem* implies the whole, *mehem* implies a part — but the resolution is that one must touch *miktzatan shehu kekulan*, a portion that is representative of the whole.

How is a part deemed representative of the whole? *Veshi'eru hachamim ke'adasha* — the sages established the measure as the size of a lentil bean. The reasoning: *shekein hachomet techilat briato ke'adasha* — the *chomet* (identified variously as a snail or a type of limbless lizard) is the size of a lentil bean at the very beginning of its formation. This smallest of the *shratzim*, at the moment of its initial creation, is the size of a lentil. Therefore, the minimum quantity that can represent an entire *sheretz* is the size of a lentil bean — a piece of a dead mouse of that size renders one *tamei*.

The *Gemara* then draws a conclusion: *shema mina shiura gemiri lah* — we see that it is a matter of *shiurim*, not of *biryah*. Even where one begins with the concept of something that represents the whole, one ultimately arrives at a fixed quantitative number. The conceptual point is significant: even though the *sheretz* analysis does not work precisely the same way as the *biryah* analysis for eating (which is case-by-case, ant by ant, rather than a universal lentil-bean standard), the *Gemara*'s direction is clear — we seek to quantify. The *biryah* status, rather than remaining an open-ended qualitative assessment, gets translated into a number: the volume of the complete creature.

### Tosafot on Full Creature

*Tosafot* reads the proof somewhat differently. Two lines into the narrow commentary at the top of the *daf*, *Tosafot* writes: *bishlama shiura gemiri midba'inan ke'adasha* — since we require the size of a lentil bean, we see it is about *shiur*. Why? Because when we derived the lentil-bean measure, we derived it from the *chomet* in its full form, with its legs and horns intact. Even though one could theoretically cut a small piece of the *chomet* that would leave it lacking a leg and still capable of surviving, we do not say the *shiur* is the minimum size at which a *chomet* could live. Rather, *ba'inan ke'adasha mamash keviyata* — we require the full size of a complete *chomet*. This shows that the operative standard is the full creature, not a reduced creature that might still be viable.

In contrast to *Tosafot*'s emphasis, one might stress the more conceptual point: the *Gemara* shows that we consistently seek to translate the *biryah* concept into a fixed quantity, and that once we do so, any deficiency from that quantity is disqualifying.

### Soul & Shiur

The *Gemara* is not yet finished. *Amar Rav Shemaya*: *ki ba'inan shiura* — when do we need a *shiur* in the case of the *chomet* and the lentil-bean measure? *Devalo hachi ke'adasha lo metamei dela nafla beh neshama* — because without the lentil-bean size, the *chomet* would not yet convey *tumah*, since a soul has not yet entered into it.

This is somewhat cryptic, but *Tosafot* explains as follows: even though it is true that an animal can be missing a limb and still survive, this is only the case after it has been fully born. If the baby *chomet* were to be born without a leg, it would be born smaller than the size of a lentil — too small to be viable at all. Therefore, the reason we do not consider a sub-lentil-size *chomet* — one that might theoretically be missing a leg — is that no such viable creature exists before the soul has entered into it. However, if a *chomet* or ant was already born, already living, and then lost a limb, it remains viable. Whether such a living creature with a missing limb retains its *biryah* status for the purposes of the prohibition of eating — that question, Rav Shemaya implies, remains open.

The deeper conceptual point is worth appreciating: normally we think of a quantitative *shiur* as what lends qualitative significance to an act. But here the *Gemara* is working from the opposite direction — *biryah* is a qualitative substitute for the quantitative significance of a *kezayit*, and the *Gemara* asks whether that qualitative status is always anchored to a fixed quantity, or whether it can float contextually depending on the individual creature in front of us.

## Backbone & Skull

### Intro Backbone

We turn now to a related but distinct domain: the backbone (*hashidra*) and the skull (*hagulgoles*) as sources of *tumah*. These are bone-based sources, and bone operates differently from flesh in the laws of *tumah*. A small piece of bone — the size of a barley grain — conveys *tumah* through touch, but to convey *tumat ohel* (impurity under a shared roof), one needs enough bone to represent the body in a more substantial way. Two primary *shiurim* govern *tumat ohel*: either *rov binyano* (majority of the body's structure) or *rov minyano* (majority of the body's bone count), and even without meeting those thresholds, a *rova* or *chatzi kav* of bones from such a significant collection will cause *tumat ohel*. The backbone and skull constitute a separate category — the question before us is whether you need both together or whether either one alone suffices.

### Backbone No Ribs

*Iba'ya lehu* — the *Gemara* raises the question: when the *mishna* says *shidra ve-gulgoles*, does it mean the backbone and the skull together, or the backbone or the skull, either one independently? This is not merely a linguistic question about the *vav* in Hebrew; it is a substantive *halachic* question.

*Amar Rava ta shema* — come and hear. *Shidra shegirer rov ila'in sheba, tehora* — a backbone from which most of the ribs have been broken off is *tahor*; it does not convey *tumat ohel*. *Uvakever, afilu meshuberes o meforekes temea mipnei hakever* — but if it is in a grave, even if it is broken or dismembered, it remains *tamei* because of the *kever* itself. This is a *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — the grave is treated as combining the fragments into a single unified object, even when they are broken, and rendering the contents *tamei*. The *chiddush* is twofold: first, that a backbone without most of its ribs is no longer deemed a backbone for purposes of *tumah*; second, that the grave overrides this and causes *tumah* nonetheless.

### Both or Either?

Now the *Gemara* asks what this teaches about our question. *Ta'ama mishum degirei* — the reason the backbone without ribs is *tahor* is because the ribs are broken off. *Ha lo girei tamei* — if not, it would be *tamei*. This implies that the backbone alone — with its ribs intact — suffices to cause *tumat ohel*, even without the skull.

But the *Gemara* deflects: *halo katani* — did we actually say that? Perhaps the passage is only telling us what happens when the ribs are broken. *Dechi girei tahara itztreicha lach* — what the passage needed to tell us is that when the ribs are broken, it is *tahor*. Whether this means the backbone alone (with ribs) is enough, or whether the skull is also needed, is not determined by this ruling. Perhaps even if you need both the backbone and the skull, once the backbone loses most of its ribs it is no longer halachically a backbone, and so the *tumah* fails — but that does not tell us whether an intact backbone alone suffices.

The discussion then turns to the story of Dustai the doctor (*Todos HaRofei*).

### Dustai Story

*Rabi Yehuda omer: shisha devarim Rabbi Akiva metamei ve-Chachamim mitaherin* — there were six matters in which Rabbi Akiva held something to be *tamei* and the *chachamim* held it *tahor* with respect to *tumat meis*, and Rabbi Akiva eventually reversed himself on most of them. The occasion for identifying these six was a famous episode:

Boxes filled with bones were brought to the *beit knesset* of the metalworkers — the craftsmen did not want to bring them inside, lest the *kohanim* present become *tamei*, so they placed the boxes in the open air of the courtyard. This episode itself illustrates an important feature of the *beit knesset* in the *Tannaic* period: it served as a communal gathering space (*beit am*), not merely as a place of prayer — which is why the doctors and rabbis convened there. *Venichnas Todos HaRofei vekhol harofim imo* — Todos the doctor came with all the other physicians, and they declared: *ein kan shidra mimeit echad* — there is not here a backbone coming from a single corpse.

The point of the doctors' testimony was that even if the bones collectively constituted a quantity sufficient for *tumah*, they came from multiple bodies. This is directly relevant to the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *chachamim* about whether bones from multiple corpses can be combined.

### Single Bone Suffices

The *Gemara* initially reads this episode as resolving our question about backbone and skull: *ta'ama deleka shidra michada* — the reason it was *tahor* is that there was no backbone from a single body. *Ha ika o shidra o gulgoles michada* — if there had been a single backbone or a single skull from one body, *denazir megale'ach alei* — it would have been sufficient for a *nazir* to shave, and would have caused *tumat ohel*.

This would seem to confirm: *shema mina o shidra o gulgoles* — it is either one or the other that suffices, not both together.

But the *Gemara* again deflects with the formula of *lo miba'ya ka'amar*: not only was there no backbone-and-skull together from one corpse, there was not even a backbone alone from one corpse, nor a skull alone. The case was simply one of total absence. We cannot infer from the doctors' statement what would have been ruled had there been an intact backbone — whether that alone would have sufficed, or whether the skull would also have been required.

The simple reading of all these *braitot* supports the "either one" reading, but the *Gemara* consistently rebuts each proof, maintaining that the question remains open.

## Multiple Bodies

### Six Cases Setup

The *Gemara* now quotes the passage that lists the six matters in dispute. *U-ma hen shisha devarim she-Rabbi Akiva metamei ve-Chachamim metaherin* — what are the six things that Rabbi Akiva declared *tamei* and the *chachamim* declared *tahor*?

### Limbs & Blood

The first: *al ever min ha-mes she-ba mi-shnei mesim* — a limb assembled from parts of two separate corpses (e.g., the top of a finger from one body and the bottom from another). This is the most striking case — a limb would seem to derive its significance from its integrity as an entity, and combining parts from two bodies would seem to undercut that integrity entirely. Yet Rabbi Akiva held it *tamei*.

The second: *al ever min ha-chai she-ba mi-shnei bnei adam* — a limb from a living person composed from two separate living individuals.

The third: *al chatzi kav atzamot she-ba mi-shnei mesim* — a half-*kav* of bones from two separate corpses. Rabbi Akiva held that such bones, even from two different bodies, combine to cause *tumah*; the *chachamim* disagreed.

The fourth: *al reviet dam ha-ba'ah mi-shnayim* — a *reviet* (approximately three to four ounces, the same measure used for *kiddush*) of blood from two corpses. Here *Tosafot* notes a subtle distinction: the standard for a *nazir*'s obligation to shave is *chatzi kav* of bones and *chatzi log* of blood, while the standard for *tumat meis* more generally is *reviet kav* of bones and *reviet log* of blood. The *braita* mentions *chatzi kav* of bones (the *nazir* standard) but then mentions *reviet* of blood (the *tumat meis* standard) — a slight asymmetry worth noting.

### Split Barley Bone

The fifth: *al etzem k'se'orah she-nechelak li-shnayim* — a bone the size of a barley grain that has been split into two pieces. This case is notably different from the others: it does not involve bones from two separate bodies, but rather a single bone divided into two. It also differs in type — this is a case of *tumat maga* (impurity through contact) rather than *tumat ohel*. One might understand the ruling: a barley-grain-sized bone is already the minimum for contact-based *tumah*, and dividing it into two might cause each piece to fall below any meaningful threshold, even if they are placed back together.

### Count Clarification

This raises the question of enumeration: listing both backbone-and-skull and the above cases would produce seven, not six. The *Gemara* offers several answers.

First: *kol heicha de-palgi alei rabbim* — when we count six, we mean six cases in which *rabbim*, many sages, argue against Rabbi Akiva. *L'afukei etzem ka'siora* — excluding the split barley bone, because there it is only Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri who disagrees with Rabbi Akiva, not the *chachamim* collectively. The core dispute of Rabbi Akiva with the *chachamim* is about combining from two separate bodies — and the barley-bone case, involving division of a single bone, does not fit that pattern.

Second alternative: *ki tani eiver min hamet eiver min hachai lo katani* — when we count six cases related to corpses, *ever min ha-chai* (a limb from a living person) was not being counted, since it is not a matter of *tumat meis* per se.

Third alternative: perhaps *ever min ha-mes* and *ever min ha-chai* are treated as a single combined case, since they are closely related.

Fourth alternative: *ki tani kol heicha denazir migale'ach al achila* — we are counting cases that involve *tumat ohel* and *nazir megale'ach*, which excludes *etzem ka'siora* since that is only *tumat maga*.

Fifth: *ki tani kol heicha de-hadar beih* — the six refer to those cases on which Rabbi Akiva ultimately reversed himself. Though seven are listed, he reversed himself on only six — not on the *reviet* of blood. That brings us to the next discussion.

### Akiva's Reversal

*Ve-hadar bo Rabbi Akiva* — Rabbi Akiva reversed himself on most of these positions. But on the *reviet* of blood from two bodies, he did not reverse himself. One can appreciate why: blood from different bodies is naturally indistinguishable. Todos the doctor could look at bones and determine that they came from different individuals, but blood — before blood typing was possible — would have appeared as a single homogeneous mass. Rabbi Akiva therefore held firm.

*De'amar lei Rebbi le-Bar Kappara: lo tish'na reviet dam be-chazara* — Rebbi told Bar Kappara not to include *reviet dam* in the list of reversals. *She-hi limuda she-Rabbi Akiva beyado* — Rabbi Akiva kept his teaching firmly in hand. *Ve-od hamikra mesaye'o* — and furthermore, a *pasuk* supports him: *ve-al kol nafshot met lo yavo* — the word *nafshot* is in the plural, and given that *ki hadam hu hanafesh* — blood is the life — the plural implies that blood from multiple persons of the dead (in the plural) still generates *tumah*. This *drasha* grounds Rabbi Akiva's position in a *pasuk*, which is also why the *braita* mentions *reviet dam* (the *tumat ohel* measure) rather than *chatzi log* (the *nazir* measure): Rabbi Akiva's *limud* begins with *reviet* as the primary quantity.

### Shimon Testimony

*Rabi Shimon omer: ad yamav haya metame* — Rabbi Shimon testified that his entire life, Rabbi Akiva held his position on the blood. *Im mishemet hazar bo ani lo yodea* — if after Rabbi Akiva died he reversed himself, Rabbi Shimon says he does not know.

The *Gemara* then cites a *braita*: *hushcharu shinav mipnei taniyotav* — Rabbi Shimon's teeth turned black from the many fasts he undertook in repentance for having made this irreverent remark (implying that Rabbi Akiva might reverse himself post mortem). The humor of the remark was not lost on the *Gemara* itself, which also repeated it — and apparently both the *Gemara* and the audience understood the joke. Nonetheless, Rabbi Shimon recognized it as disrespectful toward his *rebbi* and took it seriously enough to fast in penance.

## Majority Bones

### Majority Debate

The *Gemara* now introduces a dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* regarding the *rov atzamot* requirement for *tumat ohel*. *Beit Shammai omrim: rov atzamot min ha'atzamim o mishnayim u'mishlosha* — Beit Shammai holds that the requisite *chatzi kav* (or *rova*) of bones may come from as few as two or three bones; they need not come from a broad representation of the body's full skeletal structure. *Beit Hillel omrim: rova min hageviya, mi-rov binyano o mi-rov minyano* — Beit Hillel requires that the bones represent the majority of the body's structure (*rov binyan*) or the majority of its bone count (*rov minyan*).

### Yehoshua's Proof

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua: yachol ani la'asot divrei Beit Shammai ve-divrei Beit Hillel ke-echad* — Rabbi Yehoshua claimed he could reconcile the two positions. The apparent contradiction between "two or three bones" and "*rov binyan* or *rov minyan*" is not a genuine dispute, he argued.

### Anatomy Proof

Rabbi Yehoshua explains: when Beit Shammai said "two or three bones," they specifically meant *mishtei shokaim ve-yerech echad o mishtei yerechayim ve-shok echad* — either two calves and one thigh, or two thighs and one calf. This combination of three bones — taken from the lower limbs — constitutes *rov govho shel adam*, the majority of a person's height. And this, Rabbi Yehoshua argues, is precisely what Beit Hillel means by *rov binyan*: the structural majority of the body.

The anatomical reasoning is that the thighs and calves together account for more than half of a person's standing height. (The pelvis sits slightly higher than the midpoint of the body, so where the thigh attaches is approximately at the midpoint; the combined leg bones below that represent the majority of the height.) Furthermore, not only do the thigh-and-calf bones represent the majority of the body's height, but taking a majority of those four bones (two thighs and two calves) — i.e., any three of the four — represents the majority of the body's primary structural bones. This is what Beit Shammai meant: not that any random two or three bones suffice, but that these specific two or three from the legs are the bones that constitute *rov binyan*.

*Tosfot* notes here that the *Gemara* in Ohalot assumes *rmach evarim* — 248 limbs — in a person, citing the principle that *kuf kaf hey hem rov minyan*, meaning 125 bones constitute a majority. This figure of 248 *evarim* (which likely here means bones rather than full limbs, since 248 discrete limbs would be anatomically impossible) underlies the *rov minyan* calculation.

As for *rov minyan*, Beit Hillel's position is that the majority of the bone count may actually be found concentrated in the small bones of the hands and feet — the *mifarkei yadayim ve-raglayim*. Thus one could have *rov minyan* without having *rov binyan*, if all the tiny bones of the extremities were present. Rabbi Yehoshua's reconciliation is that Beit Shammai was articulating *rov binyan* in anatomical terms, while Beit Hillel was stating the principle more broadly (encompassing both *rov binyan* and *rov minyan*) — but both agree that some form of body-representation is required.

### Single Bone Sammai

The *Gemara* now quotes the line that is directly relevant to the backbone-and-skull question: *Shammai omer afilu etzem min ha-shidra o min ha-gulgoles* — Shammai himself (not Beit Shammai collectively, but Shammai) held that even a single bone from the backbone or the skull suffices to cause *tumat ohel*.

This is the leniency of Shammai in the context of bone *tumah*: whereas Beit Shammai required two or three specific bones (*rov binyan*), Shammai went further and said even one bone from the skull or backbone is enough. What then is the opposing position? Presumably, Beit Hillel or the *chachamim* would require the whole backbone or skull. But Shammai says even one bone from either of those structures is enough — an extremely stringent position as to what qualifies, even if the single bone is minimal in size.

### Resolution Ambiguity

The *Gemara* attempts to infer what the opposing *Rabbinic* position must be: *u-lishmeih d'ta'ama d'Beit Shammai d'machmir, hanei Rabbanan ad d'ika shidra ve-gulgoles* — if the *rabbinic* position were simply requiring both together (the whole backbone and the whole skull), we could read the debate that way. But the *Gemara* pushes back: one cannot determine from Shammai's extreme stringency (accepting even one bone from either structure) what exactly the opposing position demands. The *Rabbanan* only disagree about the single bone; where Shammai says one bone is enough, they say you need the whole thing. But do they mean the whole skull *or* the whole backbone (either one), or the whole skull *and* the whole backbone (both together)? This ambiguity remains unresolved. The evidence consistently leans toward "either one" — all the cases discussed, including the Todos the doctor episode, speak only of finding a single backbone, with no mention of needing a skull as well. But the *Gemara* repeatedly declines to accept these as conclusive proofs.

### Rambam Rules

At this point, one may consult the *Rambam* in *Hilchos Tumat Meis*, Perek 8. The *Rambam* rules: *hashidra ve-hagulgoles, rov binyano ve-rov minyano* — these are the bones that convey *tumah* for a corpse. *Shidra sheshleimah harei hi ke-meis shalem* — a complete backbone is treated like a whole corpse. *Gulgoles sheshleimah harei hi ke-meis shalem* — a complete skull is likewise treated as a whole corpse. The *Rambam* thus rules that either one alone suffices — which is clearly where all the evidence in the *Gemara* was pointing, even if the *Gemara* itself declined to resolve the question with formal proof.

## Partial Backbone Question

### Quarter Kav Question

The *Gemara* now raises a new question posed by Rami bar Chama. *Ba'ei Rami bar Chama: rov atzamot min ha-shidra ve-ha-gulgoles mai?* — what is the law if one does not have a whole backbone or skull, but has a quantity of bones derived from the backbone or skull?

To understand this question properly, one must recall the framework. For *tumat ohel*, bones convey *tumah* either when they constitute *rov binyan* or *rov minyan* of the body (or a *chatzi kav* / *rova kav* coming from such a significant collection), or when one has an entire backbone or skull. Now Rami bar Chama asks: what if one has only a partial backbone — not the whole thing — but enough of it to constitute the relevant *shiur* of bones? Since the backbone and skull are themselves *significant* bone structures (indeed, they are treated as equivalent to a whole corpse when complete), perhaps a *shiur* derived from them should carry special weight.

More specifically, Rami bar Chama frames the question in terms of *nazir megale'ach*: when is a *chatzi kav* of bones required? *Heicha de-ika mish'ar evarav* — normally when the bones come from the general skeletal remains of the body — its ordinary bones. But since the backbone and skull are of heightened significance (*de-chamiri*), such that even a single one causes *tumat ohel*, perhaps *afilu rova atzamot* — even a *rova kav* (quarter-*kav*) of bones derived from the backbone or skull — should be sufficient for a *nazir* to shave, equivalent in weight to the *chatzi kav* required from ordinary bones.

In other words, Rami bar Chama assumes as his premise that a *shiur* of bones from the backbone or skull does convey *tumah* even if the backbone or skull is not whole. His question is only whether the *shiur* in such a case is reduced to a *rova* (due to the enhanced significance of these bones) rather than the standard *chatzi kav*.

### Rava's Proof

*Amar Rava ta shema* — come and hear. The *mishna* that governs the *nazir* discusses *chatzi kav atzamot* and separately lists *shidra ve-gulgoles*. If a *rova atzamot* from the backbone or skull were also a distinct operative measure for *nazir megale'ach*, it should have been mentioned in the *mishna* alongside these other categories. Its absence suggests that no such reduced *shiur* exists: the only quantity-based measure is the *chatzi kav*, and the only backbone/skull-based measure is the complete structure. A partial backbone or skull generating *tumah* at a reduced *shiur* finds no place in the *mishna*.

### Alternative Explanation

The *Gemara* then raises a complication. Rava himself is reported to have stated in another context: *lo nitzrecha ela le-shidra ve-gulgoles she-ein bahem rova atzamot* — that a ruling was necessary specifically for a case of backbone or skull that does not contain a *rova atzamot*. The implication is that even without the requisite *rova* quantity, the intact backbone or skull alone causes *tumah*. Conversely, if the backbone or skull were large enough to contain a *rova*, then the *rova* itself would be the operative measure — not the completeness of the structure.

(This statement is formulated in the *Gemara* as referring to *rova atzamot*, not *chatzi kav atzamot* — which is intriguing, since it seems to align with Rami bar Chama's suggestion that by backbone and skull, the *shiur* is a *rova* rather than a *chatzi kav*.)

This creates an internal tension: Rava used the *mishna* to refute the idea of a reduced *shiur*, yet his own statement in another context seems to presuppose exactly that reduced *shiur*. The resolution proposed is that Rava's earlier statement was made before he had fully considered Rami bar Chama's question; after hearing the challenge, he reconsidered and took the position that the *mishna's* silence on the *rova* measure from backbone/skull is dispositive.

### Unresolved Status

Ultimately, the question is left unresolved. The *Gemara* cannot confirm whether a partial backbone or skull generates *tumah* as a quantity measure, and if so, what that quantity is. Rava's proof from the *mishna* — that the absence of a *rova*-from-backbone category implies no such category exists — stands as the strongest argument. But Rava's own earlier statement, which the *Gemara* here attributes to a pre-reconsideration position, muddles the picture. The sugya thus concludes in an unresolved state, to be continued.
