# Bava Metzia 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomer Liability Review

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּעֲבִיר חָבִית מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם וּשְׁבָרָהּ, בֵּין שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם בֵּין שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – יִשָּׁבַע. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: זֶה וְזֶה יִשָּׁבַע, וְתָמֵיהַּ אֲנִי אִם יְכוֹלִין זֶה וָזֶה לִישָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who was transporting a barrel from one place to another and he broke it, whether** he was **an unpaid bailee or a paid bailee,** if **he takes an oath** that he was not negligent he is exempt from payment. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** Both **this** one, an unpaid bailee, **and that** one, a paid bailee, must **take an oath** to exempt themselves from payment, **but I wonder whether** both **this** one **and that** one **can take an oath.** In other words, this is the *halakha* that I heard from my teachers, but I do not understand their ruling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּעֲבִיר חָבִית לַחֲבֵירוֹ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם וּשְׁבָרָהּ, בֵּין שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם בֵּין שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – יִשָּׁבַע, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם – יִשָּׁבַע, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר – יְשַׁלֵּם. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: זֶה וְזֶה יִשָּׁבַע, וְתָמֵיהַּ אֲנִי אִם יְכוֹלִין זֶה וָזֶה לִישָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who was transporting a barrel for another** person **from one place to another and he broke it, whether** he was **an unpaid bailee or a paid bailee,** if **he takes an oath** that he was not negligent, he is exempt from payment. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: An unpaid bailee takes an oath** and does not pay, but **a paid bailee pays. Rabbi Eliezer says: This** one **and that** one **take an oath, and I wonder whether** both **this one and that one can take an oath.**

The daf opens with a summary of the previous *mishna*, which dealt with a *shomer* — whether a *shomer sachar* or a *shomer chinam* — who was transporting a barrel of wine, and it slipped and broke. The question is whether such a *shomer* is liable. The *tanna kamma* holds that the *shomer* takes an oath that he was not negligent, and on that basis is exempt. The *gemara* then identifies a range of positions: this first view reflects the position of Rabbi Meir, a middle position belongs to Rabbi Yehuda, and a dissenting position is attributed to Rabbi Elazar (with the *gersa* "Rav Elazar" possibly being more accurate).

### Rabbi Yehuda's Position

Rabbi Yehuda's position is perhaps the most logically appealing of the three. He distinguishes between the two categories of *shomer*: a *shomer chinam* swears and is exempt, while a *shomer sachar* pays. The reasoning is that when a barrel slips and breaks, the incident falls into a middle category of culpability — not full *pshia* (negligence), but not a pure *ones* (unavoidable accident) either. Accidents do not simply happen in a vacuum; there are things a careful person could have done differently. At the same time, we do not necessarily classify a slipped barrel as outright negligence. A *shomer sachar*, who accepts liability for *gneiva va'aveida* (theft and loss) — a middle tier of responsibility that is less than gross negligence but more than complete blamelessness — therefore pays. A *shomer chinam*, whose liability extends only to full negligence, is exempt.

### Rabbi Elazar's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם מִדְרוֹן נָמֵי, הָא תִּינַח הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא רְאָיָה, הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא רְאָיָה – נַיְתֵי רְאָיָה וְיִפָּטֵר. דְּתַנְיָא, אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵין רֹאֶה שְׁבֻעַת ה׳ תִּהְיֶה בֵּין שְׁנֵיהֶם״ הָא יֵשׁ רוֹאֶה – יָבִיא רְאָיָה וְיִפָּטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And even on an inclined plane, this works out well where there is no evidence. But where there is evidence, let him bring evidence and be exempt. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Isi ben Yehuda says,** with regard to the verse: "And it died or was hurt or driven away **without an eyewitness, an oath of the Lord shall be between them"** (Exodus 22:9–10), that one can infer from here that if **there is an eyewitness,** let **him bring evidence and be exempt.**

Rabbi Elazar's position begins from a different premise. He says: I know the tradition follows Rabbi Meir, but I do not understand it. A *shomer sachar* should obviously be liable — and in fact, even a *shomer chinam* should be liable on level ground, since tripping on a flat surface is straightforwardly negligent. Only if one was descending an incline could a genuine claim of *ones* be made. Furthermore, even in that case, the exemption via oath is only appropriate when there is no possibility of producing witnesses. Rabbi Elazar cites the position of Isi ben Yehuda: the verse *shevuat Hashem tihiyeh bein shneihem* — "an oath of God shall be between the two of them" — applies only when there is no witness (*ein ro'eh*); if there is a witness (*yesh ro'eh*), then the *shomer* must produce proof rather than rely on an oath. Rabbi Elazar concludes: despite the difficulties, this is the received tradition (*kabbalat rabotai*), though it remains difficult to justify logically.

### Tosafot on Adam Hamazik

At this point, Tosafot raises a fundamental question that the *gemara* itself does not explicitly address — though it is very much a *Bava Kamma* question. The question runs as follows: according to Rabbi Yehuda's middle position, a *shomer chinam* is exempt because the incident falls below the threshold of full negligence. But why should the *shomer chinam* not be liable as a *mazik* — someone who caused damage — independent of his status as a *shomer*? He broke someone else's barrel with his own hands. And the principle in *Bava Kamma* is *adam mu'ad le'olam* — a person is always liable for his acts of damage, regardless of intent or fault. So why does his status as *shomer chinam* exempt him?

This question leads Tosafot to a sweeping conclusion: *adam mu'ad le'olam* cannot mean what it appears to mean — namely, absolute liability regardless of fault. In reality, Tosafot argues, there is a minimum threshold of fault even for liability as an *adam hamazik*. That threshold corresponds roughly to the level associated with *aveidah* — the type of inattentiveness that leads to an object being lost — which represents a fairly significant degree of negligence. Only at or above that level does one incur liability as a *mazik*. Below that threshold, even an *adam hamazik* is exempt.

### Ramban's Categorization

The Ramban takes a fundamentally different approach and rejects Tosafot's reframing of *adam mu'ad le'olam*. He maintains that the principle means exactly what it says: a person is always liable for acts of damage. However, he argues that there are cases where liability is excluded not because the actor was insufficiently negligent, but because of other legal considerations — for example, when the one who suffered the damage brought it upon himself by placing himself in the way of harm. In such cases, the *nizak*'s own culpability displaces the actor's liability. This is a categorically different ground for exemption, not a redefinition of the negligence standard.

More directly relevant to our case, the Ramban (as explained by the *acharonim*) holds that a *shomer* carrying someone's barrel in the course of his duties simply cannot be categorized simultaneously as an *adam hamazik*. The two legal frameworks are mutually exclusive. His fundamental legal classification is as a *shomer*, and it is under the laws of *shomer* — in *Shulchan Aruch* — that his liability is determined. One cannot double-categorize him. This answer is conceptually elegant: it is not that he lacks sufficient negligence to be a *mazik*, but that the category of *adam hamazik* does not apply to someone who was doing exactly what he was engaged to do.

## Oath as Takana

### Takana for Workers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁבוּעָה זוֹ תַּקָּנַת חֲכָמִים הִיא, שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן – אֵין לְךָ אָדָם הַמַּעֲבִיר חָבִית לַחֲבֵירוֹ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara previously cited Rabbi Elazar's answer to the apparent contradiction between the statements of Rabbi Meir, which was followed by an analysis of the various opinions of the *tanna'im*. The Gemara presents a different interpretation. **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** There is no difficulty with the oath proposed by Rabbi Meir, as **this oath is a decree of the Sages** for the betterment of the world. The reason for this oath is **that if you do not say so,** that an unpaid bailee who broke the barrel in transport can exempt himself by means of an oath, **you will have no person who will** be willing to **transport a barrel for another from one place to another,** due to the fear that it might break and he will have to pay.

Rabbi Yochanan argues that Rabbi Meir does, in fact, consider tripping and breaking to constitute negligence. Fundamentally, both a *shomer sachar* and a *shomer chinam* ought to be liable. Yet the *halacha* exempts them upon taking an oath. How? Because the oath is not a matter of *ikar hadin* but a *takanas chachamim* — a rabbinic enactment.

### Economic Rationale

The reason for this *takana* is explicit in the *gemara*: *she-im atah omer ken, ein lecha adam hamavir chavis l'chavero mimakom l'makom* — if one were to hold workers liable every time a barrel slipped and broke, no one would ever agree to carry barrels for hire. The economic and social concern is that workers would simply refuse such employment if they bore the full risk of ordinary accidents. One might ask: why not let the market adjust, allowing owners to offer higher wages or to explicitly grant exemptions? The implication of the *gemara* is that workers, often too poor to negotiate effectively, would be forced into unjust arrangements, and *Chazal* therefore stepped in with a systematic protection. This also retroactively explains Rabbi Elazar's position: he understands perfectly well that there is negligence here and liability should follow, but *this is the halacha* — because it was enacted as a special *takana*.

### Oath Content

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי מִשְׁתְּבַע? אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּכַוּוֹנָה שְׁבַרְתִּיהָ. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְמֵימַר: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם יִשָּׁבַע, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר יְשַׁלֵּם, הַאי כְּדִינֵיהּ וְהַאי כְּדִינֵיהּ. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְמֵימַר: אִין, גְּמָרָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וּמִיהוּ, תָּמֵיהַּ אֲנִי אִם יְכוֹלִים זֶה וָזֶה לִישָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How** exactly **does he take an oath? Rava said** that the phrasing is: I take **an oath that I broke it unintentionally. And Rabbi Yehuda came to say: An unpaid bailee takes an oath,** while **a paid bailee pays, this one in accordance with his law and that one in accordance with his law. And Rabbi Elazar came to say: Yes,** there is **a tradition in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. But I wonder whether** both **this** one **and that** one **can take an oath.**

Given that the *takana* proceeds from an assumption of negligence, the question naturally arises: what precisely does the *shomer* swear? It cannot be "I was not negligent," because the entire framework assumes he likely was. Rava clarifies: *shevua shelo bekavana shevartia* — the oath is "I did not break it intentionally." The point is not to establish blamelessness but to exclude willful destruction. Yes, you may have been careless; yes, it was an accident — but it was not deliberate. That is what the oath establishes.

### Rava's Clarification

Rava's clarification is therefore more than a technical detail about oath language; it reflects the entire logic of the *takana*. The protection Rabbi Yochanan institutes is specifically for workers who suffer ordinary accidents, not those who wilfully destroy property and then seek to exploit the worker-protection mechanism. Rabbi Yehuda's position is then restated in contrast: he does not accept the *takana* framework and instead assigns liability at each *shomer*'s appropriate level — *shomer chinam* swears and is exempt, *shomer sachar* pays — with no recourse to a special rabbinic enactment. Rabbi Elazar then accepts the tradition as going like Rabbi Meir but remains troubled: a *shomer sachar* should certainly pay, a *shomer chinam* on level ground was negligent, and even where negligence is unclear, witnesses should be required. The tradition stands nonetheless.

## Isi ben Yehuda Cases

### Barrel on Beam Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה קָא מְעַבַּר חָבִיתָא דְחַמְרָא בְּרִיסְתְּקָא דְמָחוֹזָא, וְתַבְרַהּ בְּזִיזָא דְמָחוֹזָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רִיסְתְּקָא דְמָחוֹזָא שְׁכִיחִי בַּהּ אִינָשֵׁי, זִיל, אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה וְאִיפְּטַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who transported a barrel of wine in the market [*beristeka*] of Meḥoza and broke it on one of the protrusions** of a wall **in Meḥoza.** The case **came before Rava,** who **said to him:** With regard to **the market of Meḥoza, people are generally found there. Go** and **bring evidence** in your favor **and** you will **be exempt.**

The *gemara* turns to a series of concrete cases that bring these principles to life. In the first, a man was carrying a barrel of wine through a street in Mechuza (*ristaka d'Mechuza*) and broke it on a protruding beam (*beziza*). He came before Rava. Now, the street in Mechuza is a public thoroughfare — people are regularly passing through. Rava therefore tells him: *zil aisi raya v'ipater* — go bring proof and you will be exempt. The implication is that he cannot simply take an oath; given that people were present in the street, witnesses are presumed available.

### Isi ben Yehuda Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ: כְּמַאן כְּאִיסִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין כְּאִיסִי, וּסְבִירָא לַן כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef,** Rava's **son, said to him: In accordance with whose** opinion did you issue this ruling? **In accordance with** the opinion of **Isi** ben Yehuda? Rava **said to him: Yes,** I ruled **in accordance with** the ruling of **Isi, and we hold in accordance with his** opinion.

Rav Yosef the son of Rav Yosef asked his father: who are you ruling like — like Isi? Meaning, you are requiring the man to produce witnesses rather than permitting him to rely on an oath. Rav Yosef confirmed: yes, like Isi ben Yehuda, and we rule that way. This is significant: the ruling of Isi ben Yehuda — that an oath is insufficient when witnesses may be available — is operative *halacha*, at least as applied by Rava and confirmed by Rav Yosef. One pressing question this raises is what "available" means in practice. If witnesses were present but the claimant has no way of locating them, is the oath nonetheless unavailable to him? The *rishonim* grapple with this. There is also the further question of whether Isi's rule applies only to *shevuot hashomrim* — oaths in guardianship cases, which is the context Isi was originally discussing — or whether it extends to other categories of oath as well.

### Wine to Vinegar Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: זִיל זְבֵין לִי אַרְבַּע מְאָה דַּנֵּי חַמְרָא. אֲזַל זְבַן לֵיהּ. לְסוֹף אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זְבִינֵי לָךְ אַרְבַּע מְאָה דַּנֵּי חַמְרָא וּתְקִיפוּ לְהוּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַרְבַּע מְאָה דַּנֵּי חַמְרָא תְּקִיפִי קָלָא אִית לַהּ לְמִילְּתָא, זִיל אַיְיתִי רְאָיָה דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא כִּי מְזַבְּנַתְּ לְהוּ חַמְרָא מְעַלְּיָא הֲוָה, וְאִיפְּטַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident. There was **a certain man who said to another: Go** and **buy for me four hundred pitchers of wine.** The second man **went and bought** them **for him. Ultimately, he came before** the first man **and said to him: I bought four hundred pitchers of wine for you, but they fermented.** The case **came before Rava,** who **said to** the second man: If **four hundred pitchers of wine** had **fermented, this matter** would generate **publicity,** i.e., people would have heard of this occurrence. Consequently, **go** and **bring proof that initially, when you purchased** the pitchers, **the wine was good, and you will be exempt.**

The second case involves a *shaliach* — an agent — who was sent to purchase 400 barrels of wine. He returned and reported that he had bought them, but they had all turned to vinegar. The sender was suspicious: perhaps the agent had actually purchased 400 barrels of vinegar at half price and pocketed the difference. The agent came before Rava. Rava's response was notable: *arba me'ah dany chamra d'takifi, qola it lehu l'milta* — if 400 barrels of wine turned to vinegar, people would be talking about it. Such an event would generate rumor and discussion. Therefore, the agent must go and bring proof — evidence that at the time of purchase the wine was indeed wine — and only then is he exempt.

This case is striking in several respects. First, the "evidence" Rava envisions is not direct testimony but rather the presence of general rumor — circumstantial support rather than proof in the strict sense. Second, and more fundamentally, this is not a *shomrim* case at all: the agent is a *shaliach*, not a guardian. Yet Rava applies something resembling Isi ben Yehuda's principle — that where evidence may be obtainable, an oath is insufficient — in a context well beyond its original formulation. This suggests that Isi's rule has broader application. Questions naturally arise about what constitutes adequate *raya* in such cases: must it be two formal witnesses, or is any form of corroborating evidence sufficient? The *gemara* leaves considerable room for discussion.

### Agent Proof Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ: כְּמַאן כְּאִיסִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין כְּאִיסִי, וּסְבִירָא לַן כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef,** Rava's **son, said to him: In accordance with whose** opinion did you issue this ruling? **According to** that of **Isi** ben Yehuda? Rava **said to him: Yes,** I ruled **in accordance with** the ruling of **Isi, and we hold in accordance with his** opinion.

Rav Yosef the son of Rava responds with the same exchange: who are you ruling like — Isi? And Rava confirms: yes, like Isi, and we hold accordingly. The parallel structure of the two rulings — one involving a *shomer* in a public street, one involving a *shaliach* in a commercial transaction — underscores the breadth with which the Isi principle is being applied.

## Half vs Full Negligence

### Agra: Half Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְקֵין רַב חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף בְּסִיכְרָא: הָנֵי דְּדָרוּ בְּאַגְרָא וְאִיתְּבַר – נְשַׁלֵּם פַּלְגָא. מַאי טַעְמָא – נְפִישׁ לְחַד וְזוּטַר לִתְרֵי, קָרוֹב לְאוֹנֶס וְקָרוֹב לִפְשִׁיעָה. בְּדִיגְלָא – מְשַׁלֵּם כּוּלַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef issued a decree in** the city of **Sikhra,** where he was the presiding Sage: With regard to **those who carry** loads **on a pole [*be'agra*] and** the item **breaks, they must pay half. What is the reason?** Such a pole is used to carry loads that are too **much for one** individual to carry **and** too **little for two.** Consequently, this breakage is **close to an accident and** equally **close to negligence,** and therefore they compromise with a payment of half liability. If he carried it **with a *digla*,** a wooden tool designed for double loads, **he pays in full,** as the use of such a tool indicates that he was carrying a load beyond the capacity of a single individual to bear and therefore acted negligently.

Rav Chiyya bar Yosef encountered cases in the city of Sichra involving workers who had been carrying loads on an *agra* — a type of carrying-beam or pole — and the load broke. He ruled that they pay half. The *agra* was configured such that fully loading it exceeds what one person should comfortably carry, yet the load is not quite large enough to clearly require two separate trips. The *gemara* characterizes this situation as *karov le'ones ve'karov li'pshia* — somewhere between an unavoidable accident and negligence. Because it is genuinely unclear on which side of the line the conduct falls, the worker pays half.

This ruling raises an interesting question. If the conduct is in the middle range — neither pure *ones* nor full *pshia* — that is precisely the category of the *shomer sachar*, who pays in full. Why, then, is the payment only half? The Rosh reframes the analysis slightly: rather than placing the conduct in an intermediate category between the liability tiers, the Rosh treats it as a case where we are genuinely uncertain whether the worker crossed the threshold of *pshia* at all — whether he is just over or just under the line. Since the outcome is uncertain, we split the payment. This has the character of a *Sumchos*-type ruling: where liability is genuinely in doubt, the parties divide. The practical difficulty, of course, is that the judgment of whether the load was "too much for one person" may only be clear in retrospect — after the accident has already occurred.

### Dilga: Full Payment

In contrast, where workers carried a load on a *dilga* — a carrying-frame that by its nature is designed for two people — and one person did the job alone, the ruling is *meshalelem kula*: full payment. Here the negligence is unambiguous. Taking on a two-person load as a single carrier is straightforwardly *pshia*, and no partial payment is appropriate.

## Workers and Wages Story

### Broken Barrel Seized

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָן תְּבַרוּ לֵיהּ הָנְהוּ שָׁקוֹלָאֵי חָבִיתָא דְחַמְרָא, שְׁקַל לִגְלִימַיְיהוּ. אֲתוֹ אֲמַרוּ לְרַב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לְהוּ גְּלִימַיְיהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ דִּינָא הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an incident involving **Rabba bar bar Ḥanan: Certain porters broke his barrel of wine** after he had hired them to transport the barrels. **He took their cloaks** as payment for the lost wine. **They came and told Rav.** Rav **said** to Rabba bar bar Ḥanan: **Give them their cloaks.** Rabba bar bar Ḥanan **said to him:** Is **this the *halakha*?**

The *gemara* then presents one of its most frequently cited narratives, which serves as a fitting conclusion to the *perek*. Rabba bar bar Chana's porters (*shakulai*) broke a barrel of wine while working for him. Assuming they fell into the category of *shomer sachar* — and perhaps ruling like Rabbi Yehuda — Rabba bar bar Chana treated the incident as one for which they were liable. He therefore seized their cloaks (*shakal glimaihu*) as security against the debt. The workers went and complained to Rav.

### Beyond Strict Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, ״לְמַעַן תֵּלֵךְ בְּדֶרֶךְ טוֹבִים״. יְהַיב לְהוּ גְּלִימַיְיהוּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: עַנְיֵי אֲנַן, וְטָרְחִינַן כּוּלֵּהּ יוֹמָא, וְכָפֵינַן, וְלֵית לַן מִידֵּי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל הַב אַגְרַיְיהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ דִּינָא הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, ״וְאׇרְחוֹת צַדִּיקִים תִּשְׁמֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to him: Yes,** as it is written: **"That you may walk in the way of good men"** (Proverbs 2:20). Rabba bar bar Ḥanan **gave them their cloaks.** The porters **said to** Rav: **We are poor people and we toiled all day and we are hungry and we have nothing.** Rav **said to** Rabba bar bar Ḥanan: **Go** and **give** them **their wages.** Rabba bar bar Ḥanan **said to him:** Is **this the *halakha*?** Rav **said to him: Yes,** as it is written: **"And keep the paths of the righteous"** (Proverbs 2:20).

Rav told Rabba bar bar Chana: give them back their cloaks. Rabba bar bar Chana protested — is that really the *din*? They broke my wine, they owe me, and I am entitled to hold their property until they pay. Rav responded: *lemaan telech be-derech tovim* — so that you may walk in the ways of good people. This already sounds less like strict *din* and more like a moral exhortation. He gave back the cloaks. The workers then said: *anei anan* — we are poor. We worked the entire day and have nothing to show for it; we are hungry and have no means. Rav then turned to Rabba bar bar Chana and said: go pay them their wages — *zil hav lehu agraihu*. Again Rabba bar bar Chana asked: is that the *din*? And Rav said: yes — *ve'orchot tzaddikim tishmor* — you shall observe the paths of the righteous.

### Lifnim Mishurat Hadin

This narrative generates considerable discussion among the *rishonim* and is widely cited in the context of *lifnim mishurat hadin* — going beyond the letter of the law. One striking feature, noted by Tosafot, is that the *gemara* here does not actually use the phrase *lifnim mishurat hadin*. Instead, Rav invokes the language of *derekh tovim* and *orchot tzaddikim* — the path of the good and the ways of the righteous. Tosafot suggests this reflects a distinction: *lifnim mishurat hadin* in its more common application means declining to claim a technical exemption that the law makes available — analogous to the case of *zaken ve'eino lefi kevodo*, where a dignitary need not bend down to retrieve a lost object but should not insist on the exemption. That is a lower-level expectation. Here, by contrast, Rav is asking Rabba bar bar Chana to forgo what is genuinely owed to him under *din* — a higher-level demand rooted in *tzidkus*.

There is also something worth noting in the use of the word *dina*. Rabba bar bar Chana twice asks *dina hachi?* — is this the law? — which suggests he understood Rav's instructions as a legal ruling rather than a mere moral encouragement. The *gemara*'s language blurs the boundary between strict obligation and supererogatory righteousness in a way that is characteristically provocative.

It is also important to situate this story within the broader halachic context of the *perek*. What Rav effectively arrives at here — workers break a barrel, employer is told to pay them and let it go — is structurally close to the outcome of Rabbi Yochanan's *takana*. Under that *takana*, workers take an oath that they did not break the barrel intentionally, and they are thereby exempt and entitled to receive their wages. The story of Rabba bar bar Chana arrives at a functionally similar result, but through the language of righteousness and going beyond the letter of the law. This broader phenomenon of protecting workers' interests — whether through a formal *takana* or through the ethical demands placed on an employer — is the animating concern of the *perek* as a whole.

## Hiring Workers Rules

### Work Hours by Custom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׂוֹכֵר אֶת הַפּוֹעֲלִים וְאָמַר לָהֶם לְהַשְׁכִּים וּלְהַעֲרִיב, מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׁכִּים וְשֶׁלֹּא לְהַעֲרִיב – אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְכוֹפָן. מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לָזוּן – יָזוּן, לְסַפֵּק בִּמְתִיקָה – יְסַפֵּק, הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who hires laborers and tells them to rise** exceptionally **early and to continue** working until exceptionally **late,** if this is in **a locale where** laborers **are not accustomed to rising** so **early or to continuing** to work until so **late,** the employer is **not permitted to compel them** to do so. In **a locale where** employers **are accustomed to feeding** their laborers, the employer **must feed** them. If they are in a locale where an employer is accustomed **to providing** their laborers **with sweet** foods, **he must provide** such food. **Everything is in accordance with the regional custom** in these matters.

With *hadran alach*, the *gemara* concludes the sixth *perek* and opens the seventh: *Hassocher et haPoalim* — the hiring of workers. The *mishna* opens with a case in which an employer told his workers to come early and stay late (*lehashkim u'leha'ariv*). It rules that in a place where the practice is not to work such extended hours (*makom she'nahagu shelo lehashkim veshelo leha'ariv*), the employer cannot compel them to do so. The principle is *minhag hamedina* — local custom governs the terms of employment.

### Tosafot on Stipulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא דִּטְפָא לְהוּ אַאַגְרַיְיהוּ. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַאי דִּטְפַאי לְכוּ אַאַגְרַיְיכוּ אַדַּעְתָּא דְּמַקְדְּמִיתוּ וּמְחַשְּׁכִיתוּ בַּהֲדַאי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הַאי דִּטְפֵת לַן – אַדַּעְתָּא דְּעָבְדִינַן לָךְ עֲבִידְתָּא שַׁפִּירְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's statement that an employer may not compel his laborers to rise exceptionally early and continue working until exceptionally late, the Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** By what right can he force them to do so? The Gemara explains: **No,** the mishna's ruling is **necessary** only in a case **where** the employer **increased their wages** beyond the standard in that place. **Lest you say** that the employer could **say to** the laborers: The fact **that I increased your wages** was **with the understanding that you would rise early and continue** working **late for me,** the mishna therefore **teaches us that** the laborers can **say** in response **to** the employer: The fact **that you increased our** wages was **with the understanding that we** would **perform quality work for you,** not that we would work longer hours.

The *gemara* initially presents this ruling as obvious (*pshita*), and this requires explanation: if they explicitly agreed to come early and stay late, what is obvious about voiding that agreement? Tosafot, citing the Ri, explains that the *mishna* is specifically addressing a case where no stipulation was made at the time of hiring (*b'sheshacharan stama*), and only afterward did the employer announce his expectations. In that case, the workers can refuse, because the terms of the hiring are already fixed by *minhag hamedina*, and a subsequent unilateral demand carries no force. But if the early and late hours were stipulated at the outset (*hitna me'ikara*), then of course the stipulation governs — *hakol lefi tena'o*. The *gemara* signals agreement with this reading by treating the case as *pshita*, which would make no sense if explicit prior stipulation were at issue. This reading also leaves open the question of what happens when an employer offers higher wages contingent on extended hours; the *gemara* addresses that ambiguity as well, noting that workers may credibly respond that the higher wage was for better quality work, not longer hours — unless the connection was made explicit.

### Feeding Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לָזוּן – יָזוּן, לְסַפֵּק בִּמְתִיקָה – יְסַפֵּק, הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** In **a locale where** employers **are accustomed to feeding** their laborers, the employer **must feed** them. If they are in a locale where an employer is accustomed **to providing** their laborers **with sweet** foods, **he must provide** such food. **Everything is in accordance with the regional custom** in these matters.

The *mishna* continues: *makom she'nahagu lazun yazun* — where it is the practice to feed workers, the employer must feed them. And *lesapek bemetika yaspek* — where it is the practice to provide something sweet alongside the bread (Rashi understands this as something to dip into), that too is required. The governing principle in all these matters is *hakol ke'minhag hamedina* — everything follows local custom, not only whether feeding occurs but what quality and composition of food is considered adequate.

### Rabbi Yochanan Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן מַתְיָא שֶׁאָמַר לִבְנוֹ: צֵא שְׂכוֹר לָנוּ פּוֹעֲלִין. הָלַךְ וּפָסַק לָהֶם מְזוֹנוֹת. וּכְשֶׁבָּא אֵצֶל אָבִיו, אָמַר לוֹ: בְּנִי, אֲפִילּוּ אִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה לָהֶם כִּסְעוּדַת שְׁלֹמֹה בִּשְׁעָתוֹ – לֹא יָצָאתָ יְדֵי חוֹבָתְךָ עִמָּהֶן, שֶׁהֵן בְּנֵי אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב. אֶלָּא עַד שֶׁלֹּא יַתְחִילוּ בִּמְלָאכָה צֵא וֶאֱמוֹר לָהֶם: עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לָכֶם עָלַי אֶלָּא פַּת וְקִטְנִית בִּלְבָד. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָיָה צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר, הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** There was **an incident involving Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya, who said to his son: Go out** and **hire laborers for us.** His son **went,** hired them, **and pledged** to provide **sustenance for them** as a term of their employment, without specifying the details. **And when he came** back **to his father** and reported what he had done, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya **said to him: My son, even if you were to prepare a feast for them like that of** King **Solomon in his time, you would not have fulfilled your obligation to them, as they are the descendants of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Rather, before they begin** engaging **in** their **labor, go out and say to them:** The stipulation that food will be provided is **on the condition that you have** the right to claim **from me only** a meal of **bread and legumes,** which is the typical meal given to laborers. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Matya's son **did not need to state** this condition, as the principle is that **everything is in accordance with the regional custom.**

The *mishna* then cites a celebrated story: Rabbi Yochanan ben Matya told his son to go hire workers for the day and to agree to provide them food. The son went and made the commitment. When he returned, his father told him: my son, even if you were to feed them like a feast of King Solomon in his prime, you would not fulfill your obligation — *she'hen bnei Avraham Yitzchak veYaakov*, for they are descendants of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and are deserving of treatment befitting royalty. Therefore, before the work begins — because the contractual commitment becomes binding only once the work starts — go and say to them: *al menat she'ein lachem alai ela pat ve'kitnit bilvad*, the only thing I am obligating myself to provide is bread and legumes, nothing more.

The difficulty this story poses is that the *mishna* had just said that feeding obligations follow *minhag hamedina*. Why then did Rabbi Yochanan ben Matya's son's commitment to "feed them" expand beyond the local custom? One possibility is that by saying he would feed them without qualification, he opened the door to their claiming he meant the maximum — feeding them as befitting their full dignity. His father's correction was therefore to go back before the work began and specify a minimal standard. What remains unclear is why an unqualified commitment to feed should override the limiting principle of *minhag hamedina* in the first place.

### Rabban Shimon Position

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel addresses exactly this difficulty. He says: *lo hayah tzarich lomar* — it was not necessary to say any of this — *hakol ke'minhag hamedina*. The entire concern of Rabbi Yochanan ben Matya was unnecessary, because the scope of the feeding obligation was always defined by *minhag hamedina* and no explicit clarification was required. His position reasserts the primacy and sufficiency of local custom as the default framework governing all terms of employment.

## Sunrise to Nightfall

### Reish Lakish's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: פּוֹעֵל בִּכְנִיסָתוֹ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, בִּיצִיאָתוֹ מִשֶּׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תִּזְרַח הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ יֵאָסֵפוּן וְאֶל מְעוֹנֹתָם יִרְבָּצוּן. יֵצֵא אָדָם לְפׇעֳלוֹ וְלַעֲבֹדָתוֹ עֲדֵי עָרֶב״.
>
> **Translation:** **A laborer's entrance** into the city from the field at the end of a day's work is **at his own** expense, i.e., he must work until the very end of the day before returning home, and he is not paid for his travel time. In contrast, **his departure** to work is **at** his **employer's** expense, i.e., he may travel after sunrise, which is time that he is paid for. The source for this is **as it is stated: "The sun rises; they slink away and couch in their dens; man goes forth to his work and to his labor until the evening"** (Psalms 104:22–23). This verse indicates that people set out to work only from sunrise, but they work until the very end of the day.

Reish Lakish formulates a rule about work hours: *poel bichnisato mishelo biytziato mishel ba'al habayis* — when the worker comes to work, it is on his own time; when he leaves, it is on the *ba'al habayis*'s time. The *gemara* will interpret this as establishing the workday as running from sunrise (*netz*) to nightfall (*tzeis*). The implication of "his own time" for travel to work means the worker leaves his home at sunrise; "the employer's time" for the return trip means the worker does not leave his workplace until after *tzeis hakochavim*, and the return journey thereafter falls on the worker's own account.

### Travel Practicalities

This interpretation produces a counterintuitive result: the worker leaves home at sunrise, arrives at the job site somewhat after sunrise, and returns home after it is already dark. One of the *ba'alei hatosafot* attempts to read the *gemara* in the reverse direction — that the worker departs while it is still somewhat light (during *alot hashachar*) and arrives at work right around sunrise, then leaves work in time to arrive home by *tzeis* — which would be the more practically sensible arrangement. Tosafot acknowledges that this reading is more logical, particularly since it allows the worker to travel while there is daylight both ways. Nevertheless, Tosafot and Rashi ultimately prefer the more straightforward textual reading, even while acknowledging its difficulty.

### Scriptural Basis

The basis for Reish Lakish's rule is a verse from Psalm 104. *Tizrach hashemesh ye'asefun* — when the sun rises, the nocturnal animals gather in and return to their lairs (*ve'el me'onatam yirbatzun*). *Yetze adam lefo'olo* — then a person goes out to his labor, *vela'avodaso adey arev* — and to his work until evening. The verse thus presents the workday as the mirror image of the nocturnal animals' domain: when the sun rises, people go to work; when evening falls, their work ends.

### Workday Span

The practical reading of the verse, then, is that *yetziato* — going out to work — coincides with sunrise, and the labor continues *adey arev* — until nightfall. The worker's journey to work is on the employer's time (the worker begins "leaving" at sunrise), and the return journey after *tzeis* is on the worker's own time. This establishes the span of the contractual workday while allocating the travel times accordingly.

## Determining Work Hours

### New City Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיחְזֵי הֵיכִי נְהִיגִי – בְּעִיר חֲדָשָׁה. וְנִיחְזֵי מֵהֵיכָא קָא אָתוּ – בְּנָקוֹטָאֵי. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּאָמַר לְהוּ – דְּאָגְרִיתוּ לִי כְּפוֹעֵל דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if employment practices are in accordance with regional custom, how can a source be cited from a verse? **Let us see how** they **are accustomed** to working in that place. The Gemara answers: The statement of Reish Lakish is **with regard to a new city,** which does not yet have an accepted practice. The Gemara asks: Even so, **let us see from which city** the laborers originally **came,** and let them follow the customs of that city. The Gemara answers: This is a case **of an eclectic** group of laborers, who came from many different cities. The Gemara offers an alternative answer: **If you wish, say** instead **that** the employer **said to** the laborer: **I am hiring you as a laborer by Torah law.** According to this explanation, Reish Lakish is detailing the *halakha* by Torah law.

The *gemara* then considers a practical question: what is the baseline when there is no established *minhag*? If a new city has been settled by people from diverse origins, no single custom dominates. In that case, either the scriptural parameters derived from Psalm 104 provide the default — the workday runs from sunrise to nightfall — or the employer must explicitly stipulate the terms at hiring, stating that workers are engaged *ke'po'alei de'Oraisa*, according to the Torah's framework. The *gemara* acknowledges that a cosmopolitan city with no uniform practice creates genuine ambiguity and requires either explicit agreement or resort to the scriptural baseline.

### Rava's Homily

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי זֵירָא וְאָמְרִי לַהּ, תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״תָּשֶׁת חֹשֶׁךְ וִיהִי לָיְלָה בּוֹ תִרְמֹשׂ כׇּל חַיְתוֹ יָעַר״. ״תָּשֶׁת חֹשֶׁךְ וִיהִי לָיְלָה״ – זֶה הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה שֶׁדּוֹמֶה לְלַיְלָה. ״בּוֹ תִרְמֹשׂ כׇּל חַיְתוֹ יָעַר״ – אֵלּוּ רְשָׁעִים שֶׁבּוֹ, שֶׁדּוֹמִין לְחַיָּה שֶׁבַּיַּעַר. ״תִּזְרַח הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ יֵאָסֵפוּן וְאֶל מְעוֹנֹתָם יִרְבָּצוּן״. ״תִּזְרַח הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״ – לַצַּדִּיקִים, ״יֵאָסֵפוּן״ – רְשָׁעִים לְגֵיהִנָּם, ״וְאֶל מְעוֹנֹתָם יִרְבָּצוּן״ – אֵין לָךְ כׇּל צַדִּיק וְצַדִּיק שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מָדוֹר לְפִי כְבוֹדוֹ. ״יֵצֵא אָדָם לְפׇעֳלוֹ״ – יֵצְאוּ צַדִּיקִים לְקַבֵּל שְׂכָרָן, ״וְלַעֲבֹדָתוֹ עֲדֵי עָרֶב״ – בְּמִי שֶׁהִשְׁלִים עֲבוֹדָתוֹ עֲדֵי עָרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the aforementioned psalm, the Gemara notes: **Rabbi Zeira interpreted** a verse **homiletically, and some say** that **Rav Yosef taught** in a *baraita*: **What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "You make darkness and it is night, in which all the beasts of the forest creep forth"** (Psalms 104:20)? **"You make darkness and it is night"; this** is referring to **this world, which resembles nighttime. "In which all the beasts of the forest creep forth"; these are the wicked in** this world, **who resemble a beast of the forest,** as the wicked have great influence in this world. With regard to the World-to-Come, the verse states: **"The sun rises; they slink away and couch in their dens"** (Psalms 104:22). **"The sun rises" for the righteous,** and **"they slink away,"** i.e., **the wicked** go **to Gehenna.** As for the phrase: **"And couch in their dens,"** it is interpreted as follows: **You will not have a single righteous person who will not have his** own **residence** in the World-to-Come, as **befitting his dignity.** With regard to the phrase: **"Man goes forth to his work,"** it indicates that **the righteous go forth to receive their reward.** Finally, the phrase: **"And to his labor until the evening"** (Psalms 104:22), teaches that the verse is referring **to one who has completed his labor by the evening,** i.e., before the evening of his lifetime, his death.

Rava (and some say it was Rav Yosef who taught this) offers a homiletical reading (*darash*) of the same verses from Psalm 104. *Tashet choshech vihi layla* — God sets down darkness and there is night — this, says Rava, represents *olam hazeh*, this world, which is like night compared to the world to come. *Bo sirmos kol chaiso ya'ar* — then all the animals of the forest creep about — these are the *resha'im*, the wicked, who have their dominion in this world just as wild animals prowl in the night. *Tizrach hashemesh ye'asefun* — when the sun rises, they will be gathered in — this refers to the world to come: *ye'asefun resha'im legehinnom*, the wicked will be gathered into *Gehinnom*. *Ve'el me'onatam yirbatzun* — and to their dwelling places they shall lie down — now read as referring to the *tzaddikim*: every righteous person has a dwelling place in the world to come commensurate with his level of achievement, *ein lecha kol tzaddik ve'tzaddik she'ein lo mador lefi kevodo*. *Yetze adam lefo'olo* — a person goes out to his labor — the *tzaddikim* will go out to receive their reward (*lekabel secharam*) for all the work they did in this world through observance of *mitzvos*. *Vela'avodaso adey arev* — and to his labor until evening — this refers to one who completed his labor until evening, meaning until the end of his life. The reward is deferred; it comes not in this world but in the next.

The homily is striking precisely because it addresses one of the most persistent theological challenges: the righteous suffer and the wicked prosper. Rather than deflecting the problem, Rava's *drasha* confronts it directly. This world is night — of course the nocturnal animals prowl here. The *tzaddikim* will receive their reward when the sun rises in the world to come.

## Policeman Story

### Identifying Thieves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַשְׁכַּח לְהָהוּא פַּרְהַגְוָנָא דְּקָא תָפֵיס גַּנָּבֵי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵיכִי יָכְלַתְּ לְהוּ, לָאו כְּחֵיוָתָא מְתִילִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״בּוֹ תִרְמֹשׂ כׇּל חַיְתוֹ יָעַר״? אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי מֵהַאי קְרָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״יֶאֱרֹב בַּמִּסְתָּר כְּאַרְיֵה בְסֻכֹּה״. דִּלְמָא שָׁקְלַתְּ צַדִּיקֵי וְשָׁבְקַתְּ רַשִּׁיעֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a story that involves rising early. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, found a certain officer [*parhagavna*] whose** responsibility was to **arrest thieves. He said to** the officer: **How are you able** to arrest **them? Aren't they likened to beasts, as it is written:** "You make darkness and it is night, **in which all the beasts of the forest creep forth"** (Psalms 104:20)? **There are** those **who say that he said to him** a proof **from this verse: "He lies in wait in a secret place as a lion in his lair,** he lies in wait to catch the poor; he catches the poor when he draws him up in his net" (Psalms 10:9). Since the wicked are so devious, **perhaps you apprehend the righteous and leave the wicked** alone?

The *gemara* now introduces a lengthy and ethically charged narrative involving Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon. He encountered a royal official (*parhagona*) whose job was to seize thieves and criminals. Rabbi Elazar asked him: how are you able to identify them? How do you know you are catching the right people and not seizing the innocent while the guilty go free? Some versions of the text record that Rabbi Elazar cited a verse — *ye'erov be'mistar k'aryeh be'sukka*, they lie in wait like a lion in his lair — as his opening point of contact with the official.

### New Policeman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמַאי אֶעֱבֵיד? הַרְמָנָא דְמַלְכָּא הוּא. אֲמַר: תָּא אַגְמְרָךְ הֵיכִי תַּעֲבֵיד. עוּל בְּאַרְבַּע שָׁעֵי לְחָנוּתָא, כִּי חָזֵית אִינִישׁ דְּקָא שָׁתֵי חַמְרָא וְקָא נָקֵיט כָּסָא בִּידֵיהּ וְקָא מְנַמְנֵם, שְׁאוֹל עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The officer **said to him: But what should I do? It is the king's edict [*harmana*]** that I must arrest thieves, and I am performing my job to the best of my ability. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **said** to him: **Come** and **I will instruct you how you should do** it. **At the fourth hour** of the day **enter the tavern. When you see someone drinking wine, holding** his **cup in his hand, and dozing, inquire about his** background.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן הוּא וְנָיֵים – אקַדּוֹמֵי קַדֵּים לְגִרְסֵיהּ. אִי פּוֹעֵל הוּא – קָדֵים קָא עָבֵיד עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ, וְאִי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ בְּלֵילְיָא – רַדּוֹדֵי רַדֵּיד. וְאִי לָא – גַּנָּבָא הוּא וְתִפְסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **If he is a Torah scholar and is dozing,** assume that he rose **early** in the morning **for his studies. If he is** a daytime **laborer,** assume that he rose **early** and **performed his work. And if his work is at night** and no one heard him working, it is possible that this is because **he draws** copper wires, which is a form of labor that does not produce noise. **And if** he is **none** of these, **he is a thief, and** you should **arrest him,** as it can be assumed that he was awake the previous night because he was stealing, and that is why he is now dozing off.

This advice was reported to the palace. The response was immediate and pointed: *karyna d'igarta ihu leihavei parvanka* — the one who read the letter should be the one to execute it. The man who devised the system should implement it. Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon was appointed as the king's policeman, and he proceeded to seize Jewish criminals.

### Ethical Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתְּמַע מִילְּתָא בֵּי מַלְכָּא, אֲמַרוּ: קַרְיָינָא דְאִיגַּרְתָּא אִיהוּ לֶיהֱוֵי פַּרְוַנְקָא. אַתְיוּהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְקָא תָפֵיס גַּנָּבֵי וְאָזֵיל. שְׁלַח לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה חוֹמֶץ בֶּן יַיִן! עַד מָתַי אַתָּה מוֹסֵר עַמּוֹ שֶׁל אֱלֹהֵינוּ לַהֲרִיגָה?
>
> **Translation:** This **matter** of the advice of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **was heard** in **the king's palace.** The king's ministers **said: Let the reader of the letter be its messenger [*parvanka*],** i.e., since Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, offered this advice, he should be the one to implement it. **They brought Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** to the authorities who appointed him to this task, **and he proceeded to arrest thieves. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa sent** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, the following message: You are **vinegar, son of wine,** i.e., you are wicked in comparison to your father, the righteous Rabbi Shimon, just as vinegar is spoiled wine. **Until when will you inform on the nation of our God to** be sentenced to **execution** by a gentile king's court?

When Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha heard what was happening, he sent Rabbi Elazar a pointed rebuke: *chometz ben yayin* — vinegar, son of wine — a sharp epithet suggesting degeneration from a distinguished lineage. Then the substantive challenge: *ad masai ata moser amo shel eloheinu la'hariga?* — How long will you continue to hand over the people of our God to be killed? The concern is not merely abstract. The *gemara* is alive to the reality that those caught by the king's agents faced not merely imprisonment but death. Rabbi Elazar was enabling the execution of Jews — a profound moral and halachic problem touching on the prohibition of *mesira*, informing on Jews to the authorities.

### Thorn Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: קוֹצִים אֲנִי מְכַלֶּה מִן הַכֶּרֶם. שְׁלַח לֵיהּ: יָבֹא בַּעַל הַכֶּרֶם וִיכַלֶּה אֶת קוֹצָיו. יוֹמָא חַד פְּגַע בֵּיהּ הָהוּא כּוֹבֵס, קַרְיֵיהּ ״חוֹמֶץ בֶּן יַיִן״. אָמַר: מִדַּחֲצִיף כּוּלֵּי הַאי – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ רַשִּׁיעָא הוּא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: תִּפְסוּהּ. תַּפְסוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **sent** a message back to **him: I am** merely **eradicating thorns from the vineyard,** i.e., I am removing the wicked from the Jewish people. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa **sent** back **to him: Let the Owner of the vineyard,** i.e., God, **come and eradicate His** own **thorns.** It is not your place to do this. The Gemara relates: **One day, a certain laundryman met** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **and called him vinegar, son of wine.** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **said: From** the fact **that** this man **acted so insolently** by vilifying a Torah scholar, one can **conclude** that **he is a wicked person. He told** the authorities: **Arrest** that man. **They arrested him** and condemned him to death.

Rabbi Elazar responded by sending back his own message: *kotzim ani mechaleh min hakerem* — I am removing thorns from the vineyard. He framed his work as an act of purification — clearing the community of criminals who were corrupting it. The image is that of a gardener tending God's vineyard, the Jewish people, by excising the elements that defile and endanger it.

### Vineyard Owner's Role

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha replied with the decisive counter: *yavo ba'al hakerem vi'yechaleh et kotzav* — let the owner of the vineyard come and remove his own thorns. That is God's role, not yours. Even if the metaphor of thorn-removal is accepted, the authority to carry it out belongs to the Master of the vineyard. A human agent — however distinguished, however well-intentioned — does not have the standing to assume that role. On that profound challenge, the *gemara* pauses, and we are left with the cliffhanger of what becomes of Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon and the moral weight of his choices.
