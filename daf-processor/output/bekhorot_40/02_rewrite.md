# Bekhorot 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overview & Setup

### Opening

We begin today on 40b. Today's *daf* is 41, though we are one *amud* behind. It is, however, a relatively compact *daf*, and we proceed accordingly through the *mishnah*'s catalogue of physical conditions that constitute a *mum* in animals.

### Doubled Ear

The first *mishnah* of 40b introduces the case of a doubled ear: *ozen hagedi hayeta kefulah* — the ear of a kid goat was folded over. The *gemara* will clarify the precise mechanism, but the *mishnah* establishes the governing principle. *Amru chachamim*: *bizman shehu etzem echad mum* — if the folding is caused by a single piece of cartilage (Rashi understands *etzem* here to mean cartilage, since ears contain no true bone), that constitutes a *mum*. There is a textual variant in how the contrasting case is expressed: some versions read *ve'im eino etzem echad* — if it is not one cartilage but two separate ones, as though a distinct flap protrudes alongside the normal ear — that is *eino mum*, not a blemish.

This is, on its face, counterintuitive. One might expect the opposite ruling: a wholly separate cartilaginous protrusion seems more obviously abnormal than a single folded one. Rashi himself acknowledges the difficulty here, writing *ta'ama lo isparish* — the reason is not explained. What he does say is that it is at least less prominent than an extra limb and therefore not as visible. This candid admission captures a broader challenge running through this entire *perek*: we encounter many specific rulings without a consistently articulated underlying principle about what degree of deformity, and what type of anatomical abnormality, crosses the threshold into *mum*.

### Goat Tail

Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel adds the case of the goat's tail: *zanav hagedi shehu domeh leshel chazir* — if the tail resembles that of a pig, *veshe'ein bo shalosh chulyot* — or if it lacks three vertebrae, *harei zeh mum*. An unusual tail configuration, whether in form or in the number of its vertebrae, renders the animal blemished and therefore unsuitable as a *korban*. These distinctions underscore how much practical expertise in animal anatomy was woven into the *halacha* of *kodshim* — knowledge that would be indispensable were *korbanot* to be restored.

### Yerushalmi Question

One might ask why the *Yerushalmi* is largely absent from this tractate. The *gemara* throughout *Bekhorot* reflects relatively thin tradition on many of these points, and indeed there is no extant *Yerushalmi* on *Kodashim* at all. The *Rambam* in his *hakdama* to his *Peirush Hamishnayot* asserts that a *Yerushalmi* on *Kodashim* once existed, but no one has possessed or even seen a manuscript of it. Roughly a century ago, what purported to be a discovered *Yerushalmi Kodashim* appeared and was used to resolve hundreds of otherwise difficult *Rambam*s — but it was subsequently exposed as a forgery. It is also worth noting that there may have been something uncomfortable about deeply engaging *Kodashim* in *Eretz Yisrael*, where the *Beit Hamikdash* had so recently stood. The discussions in Bavel, at greater remove, may have felt less fraught.

## Physical Deformities

### Swollen Mouth/Legs

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *piv balum veraglav mevulamot* — the mouth or legs are *balum*. Rashi offers two readings of the term: either narrowed and constricted, or, alternatively, swollen and puffed up. The distinction between causes is halakhically decisive. If the condition is *machmat haruach* — due to some natural cause such as infection — *eino mum*, it is not a blemish. If it is *machmat ha'etzem* — due to the bone itself being thick or deformed — *harei zeh mum*. Some texts read *machmat atzmo* rather than *machmat ha'etzem*, meaning it is constitutionally that way, naturally deformed, as opposed to being caused by an external process. Either way, the distinction tracks whether the condition is structural and permanent or the result of a passing organic process.

### Ear Analysis

The *baraita* then addresses doubled ears: *ozno kefulot*. The ruling turns on *chas chasiyot* — the cartilage. If it is one cartilage, *harei zeh mum*; if there are two separate cartilages, *eino mum*. As we have seen, Rashi declines to explain the underlying logic of this ruling, and the difficulty stands. The asymmetry — that a single folded cartilage is worse than a separately protruding one — remains unresolved at the level of stated reasoning.

### Vertebrae Count

Rav Huna addresses the tail-vertebrae question. The *mishnah* states that if the tail lacks three vertebrae, it is a *mum*. Rav Huna extrapolates: *be-gedi shtayim harei zeh mum* — for a goat, even two vertebrae is already a *mum*, meaning three is the minimum normal count; *be-taleh shalosh harei zeh mum* — for a sheep, the threshold is one higher, so three is already deficient, and four is the minimum.

### Goat vs. Sheep

A *baraita* is then cited that appears to contradict Rav Huna. According to the *baraita*: *be-gedi achat harei zeh mum* — for a goat, even a single vertebra is a *mum*, meaning two is the normal minimum; *be-taleh shtayim harei zeh mum* — for a sheep, two is already a *mum*, so three is the minimum. This is one degree more stringent than Rav Huna's figures. The *gemara* declares *tiyuvta de-Rav Huna tiyuvta* — a refutation.

The *gemara* then explains how Rav Huna arrived at his error: *Rav Huna matnitin ateiya* — the *mishnah* misled him. The *mishnah* opens by discussing a goat (*gedi*) — the folded ear, the pig-like tail — and when it then states that a tail lacking three vertebrae is a *mum*, Rav Huna assumed the subject was still a goat: *reisha be-gedi ve-seifa nami be-gedi*. In fact, the *mishnah* shifts subject: *reisha be-gedi ve-seifa be-taleh* — the opening cases concern a goat, but the vertebrae ruling concerns a sheep, which is the default animal. Without an explicit marker, the *mishnah* reverts to the more common subject. It was an understandable error — not obvious at all — but a genuine one.

## Growth & Defects

### Yavelet & Growth

Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos introduces several cases. *Yavelet be-einav* — if the animal has a *yavelet*, some kind of protruding growth, in its eye, that is a *mum*. *Ve-shenifgam etzem yado ve-raglo* — if the bone of the foreleg or hind leg was nicked (as opposed to broken, which was addressed previously). *Ve-shenifrak atzmo shel piv* — if the bone of the mouth was detached or loosened, which Rashi understands as referring to the gums. This last case is worth noting: one might expect gum damage to be a greater *mum* than tooth damage, but since the gums are almost entirely hidden by the lips, the standard is adjusted — mere injury is insufficient, the damage must rise to actual detachment to qualify.

### Disproportionate Limbs

Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos continues: *eino achat gedola ve-achat ketana* — one eye is large and the other small; *ozno achat gedola ve-achat ketana* — similarly for the ears. These are *mum* only *be-mareh* — if the disproportion is visually apparent — *ve-lo be-mida* — one does not take out a measuring instrument and compare precise dimensions. Visible disproportion is the operative standard.

Rabbi Yehuda adds the case of the testicles: *shtei beitzim, achat gedola ki-shtayim she-be-chavertah* — if one testicle is twice the size of the other, that too is a *mum*. The Sages, however, did not agree with Rabbi Yehuda, since this condition is considerably less visible.

### Yavelet Debate

The *gemara* opens its analysis by noting that the *mishnah*'s ruling that *yavelet* in the eye constitutes a *mum* implies that a *yavelet* generally is a *mum*. Against this, the *gemara* cites the *mishnah* we will encounter shortly — the middle category of animals that are neither acceptable as a *korban* nor subject to *shechita* on the basis of a *mum*: *ve'elu she-shochtim aleihem lo ba-mikdash ve-lo ba-medinah*. That list includes *baal garav u-vaal yavelet* — animals with scabs or a *yavelet*. So according to that *mishnah*, a *yavelet* is only a middle-category disqualification, not a full *mum*. Yet the *Torah* itself in *Emor* explicitly lists *garav* and *yavelet* as blemishes. How can the *mishnah* treat them as merely middle-category conditions?

The *gemara* attempts a first resolution: *ha be-gufa ha be-eina* — the distinction is between the body and the eye. On the eye, far more visible and disfiguring, it is a full *mum* — this is our current *mishnah* and is consistent with the *Torah*'s *pasuk*. On the rest of the body, it is only middle-category — that is the later *mishnah*. The *gemara* then challenges this: *kra stam ksiv* — the *pasuk* makes no such distinction between body and eye. Why should we read it so narrowly?

### Bone Analysis

The *gemara* therefore proposes a second resolution: *ha de-is bei etzem ha de-leis bei etzem* — the distinction is whether the growth contains bone. If it contains bone, it constitutes a genuine structural deformity — that is the *pasuk*'s case, a full *mum* wherever it appears. If it is merely a skin protrusion without bone, the *gemara* calls it *tiltul* — a mere flap — and in that case a distinction between eye and body applies. On the eye even a flap is sufficiently disfiguring to constitute a full *mum*; on the body, a flap alone does not.

The *gemara* presses further: if a *yavelet* without bone is merely a skin flap, is it even meaningful to speak of it as problematic at all for the body? *Harei tiltul be-alma hu* — it is just a trivial protrusion. This raises the deeper question animating much of this *perek*: how do we define the threshold of disfigurement? Not every visual deviation constitutes a *mum*. Blemishes must be of a certain character and significance. A flap of skin without bone may not rise to the level of the conceptual category even if it is aesthetically imperfect.

The distinction matters practically as well. An animal in the middle category — disqualified as a *korban* but not subject to *shechita* on the basis of a *mum* — must simply wait until it develops an actual *mum*, at which point it may be slaughtered. There is an economic cost to that ruling, and one is reminded of the debate recorded earlier between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, where Rabbi Akiva exclaimed: *Ad matai ata mechaleh mamonam shel Yisrael* — how long will you go on destroying the wealth of Israel? That attitude is a genuine strand in the tradition. At the same time, the *gemara* is also trying to be conceptually honest about what a *mum* actually is — and something so trivial as a skin flap may simply not qualify, whatever the practical consequences.

### Skin Flaps

The *gemara* then poses the question: *tiltula be-adam mahu* — what is the status of a skin flap in a human being? The answer comes from a *mishnah* where Rabbi Eliezer rules: *ba'alei hatiltulin pesulin be-adam u-ksherim be-behemah* — people with skin flaps are disqualified for Temple service as *kohanim*, yet the same condition in an animal is entirely unproblematic from the standpoint of *mum*. This illustrates an important graduated structure. The *kohen* standard is the most demanding: it is not simply about the absence of *mum* but about conformity to the standard of *shaveh be-zaro shel Aharon* — being typical among the descendants of Aaron. Even a condition too minor to constitute a *mum* in an animal can disqualify a *kohen* from service. The *korban* standard for animals is intermediate. The baseline *mum* standard that permits *shechita* is the most lenient. Three distinct thresholds apply to three distinct contexts.

## Eye Blemishes

### Black vs. White

The *gemara* returns to the *yavelet* question, having established that the key distinction is between the eye and the body. It now asks: can both the *Torah*'s case (a *yavelet* with bone, a full *mum*) and our *mishnah*'s case (a *yavelet* without bone, a middle-category condition) both concern the eye? *Ela idi ve-idi be-einav* — yes, both are in the eye. *Ve-lo kashya*: the difference is *ha be-shachor ha be-lavan* — whether the protrusion is in the black of the eye (the iris) or the white (the sclera). A growth in the black of the eye is more disfiguring and constitutes a full *mum*; a growth in the white is less serious.

But this immediately raises its own difficulty: *ve-ha ein mumim be-lavan* — there are, as a general principle, no blemishes in the white of the eye. This is a striking ruling. Intuitively, a growth on the white of the eye is visibly abnormal. Why should it be categorically exempt? The *gemara* suggests that the eye's *mum* category is anchored to the specific language in the *Torah* — *tavlul be-eino* — and is defined narrowly around the iris rather than the entire eye.

### Hair Growth

Reish Lakish introduces a different resolution that turns on an entirely different variable: *ha de-it bei se'ar ha de-leit bei se'ar* — whether hair grows from the flap. A protrusion from which hair grows is more dramatically disfiguring and constitutes a full *mum*; one without hair does not. There is also a *girsa* question here. Rashi's text reads that the two cases both occur in the white of the eye, meaning that Rashi's version at least partially retreats from the categorical rule that the white of the eye can never bear a *mum*. Tosafot's text reads *be-shachor* throughout, keeping both cases in the black of the eye — consistent with the principle that the white is never subject to *mumim* — and distinguishes them only by whether hair is present. The anatomical picture Reish Lakish paints is admittedly difficult to visualize: a protrusion from the eye from which hair grows. This is, however, apparently a real phenomenon — certain tumors of the eye do produce plaque-like or wart-like growths, and ocular growths of various kinds do occur in cattle, including melanomas, squamous cell carcinomas, and papillomas.

### Eye Size Standards

A *baraita* is cited bearing on the *mishnah*'s ruling about disproportionate eyes: *gedola ke-shel egel* — if the eye is as large as that of a cow (when we are dealing with a sheep), that is a blemish; *ketana ke-shel avaz* — or if it is as small as that of a duck, equally a blemish. It is worth noting that this *baraita* appears to interpret the *mishnah* somewhat differently than its plain sense. The *mishnah*'s language — *eino achat gedola ve-achat ketana* — seems to speak of disproportion between the two eyes of a single animal. The *baraita*, however, frames the question as deviation from an external standard: how large, or how small, must an eye be — in absolute terms — before it qualifies as a *mum*? These may reflect two distinct readings of what the operative defect actually is.

### Veterinary Examples

The discussion of ocular growths has practical veterinary correlates. Ocular melanoma is apparently one of the most common tumors in bovines, presenting as a small circular white elevation on the surface of the eye. Squamous cell carcinoma produces a hard raised growth on the eyelid rather than the eye surface itself. Papillomas produce wart-like growths. The question the *gemara* wrestles with — whether a protrusion from the eye constitutes a *mum*, and whether its location in the black or white, and whether it contains hard tissue — maps in interesting ways onto these real pathological categories, even if the *gemara*'s conceptual vocabulary is not organized around modern pathology.

## Tail Blemishes

### Calf Tail Length

A new *mishnah* introduces the case of the calf's tail: *zenav ha-egel she-eino magia la-arkov* — if the tail does not reach the *arkov*, a *mum*. The Sages add an observation about normal development: *kol zman she-hem magdilot hen nimtachot* — as the calf grows, the tail continues to lengthen and stretch. Rashi reads the Sages not as disagreeing with the opening ruling but as affirming it: the normal developmental trajectory begins with the tail reaching the knee joint, and continues to grow beyond it. Any deviation from this norm constitutes a blemish.

### Knee Joint

*Eizehu arkov she-amru* — what is the *arkov* to which the *mishnah* refers? Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos explains: *ba-arkov shebe-emtza ha-yerech* — the joint in the middle of the thigh, which corresponds to what we call the knee. He distinguishes this from the lower joint — what would anatomically correspond to the ankle in humans but sits higher on the animal's leg — which is not the *arkov* in question.

### Camel Anatomy

A *baraita* further specifies: *kaf ha-elyon ve-lo kaf ha-tachton* — the upper joint, not the lower. *Ve-kenegdo be-gamal nikar* — and the corresponding joint is particularly visible and identifiable in camels. Those who recall the discussion in *Chullin* may remember a visual illustration of precisely this feature of camel anatomy, which makes the joint structure of the leg unusually apparent.

## Summary & Middle

### Wrap-up Mishnah

The *mishnah* now offers a summary statement: *al elu mumim shochtin et ha-bechor* — on the basis of all the *mumim* enumerated, one may slaughter the *bechor*. And: *u-fsulei ha-mukdashin nifdin aleihem* — consecrated animals (*kodshim*) that have acquired one of these blemishes are redeemed on their basis. The *mishnah* explicitly equates the *mum* standard for the *bechor* with that for other *korbanot*.

This equation is itself notable. At the opening of the *perek*, when the *gemara* sought to define *mum*, it focused primarily on the *psukim* concerning *bechor* and largely passed over the far more detailed passages concerning other *korbanot*. The summary *mishnah* now treats them as identical. Why the *gemara* chose to anchor its definitional work to the *bechor psukim* rather than the more expansive *korbanot psukim* remains a question worth sitting with.

### Wrap-up Analysis

The *gemara* immediately asks: *ha tu lamma li* — why is this summary *mishnah* necessary? *Ha tana lei reisha* — the *mishnah* already opened with *al elu mumim shochtin et ha-bechor*. The *gemara* responds: *seifa istreich* — the second clause is what requires the statement, namely *u-fsulei ha-mukdashin nifdin aleihem*, the rule about redemption of *kodshim*. But the *gemara* challenges even this: *ha nami peshita* — is that not obvious? If a *mum* is sufficient to permit *shechita* of a *bechor* notwithstanding its *kedusha*, then certainly a *mum* on other *kodshim* is sufficient for redemption, which is an even more lenient act.

The *gemara* concludes that the summary *mishnah* serves a different function: *ella eidi de-tana shalosh hosif* — since the *mishnah* earlier included three additional *mumim* added by Ila, about which the Sages said *lo shamanu*, we have no tradition, and the subsequent *mishnayot* included rulings in the names of individual authorities (Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos prominently), there was reason to wonder whether these carried full consensus weight. The wrap-up *mishnah* therefore comes to teach that all of these rulings — notwithstanding that some were introduced individually rather than as received *halacha* — represent the consensus position, with the exception of those matters where explicit debate is recorded.

## Middle Category

### Transitory Blemish

We now arrive at one of the most conceptually interesting sections of the *perek*: the middle category. *Ve-elu she-ein shochtin aleihem lo ba-mikdash ve-lo ba-medinah* — these are animals one may neither sacrifice in the Temple nor slaughter outside it on the basis of a *mum*. They cannot enter the *Beit Hamikdash* because they are not *tamim*; yet they cannot be slaughtered outside it because their condition does not constitute a *mum* proper. The paradigmatic case is *chavarur* — white spots — and *ha-mayim she-ein kevuim* — water in the eye that is not fixed, meaning the condition is transitory. This generalizes: any *mum over*, any blemish that may heal, falls into this middle category. The animal cannot be brought as a *korban* while the blemish is present, but neither may it be slaughtered on the basis of that blemish, since it is not a permanent *mum*. The owner is simply stuck, waiting for either the condition to resolve or a permanent *mum* to appear.

### Molar Damage

*Ve-ha-chitin ha-pnimiyot she-nifgemu aval lo ne'ekru* — the roots of the inner molars were nicked but not fully uprooted. Since inner molars are not visible under normal conditions, this is a striking ruling: it suggests that for the purpose of bringing a *korban*, the standard may not simply be the absence of a *mum* (which requires visibility) but perhaps a broader category of *tamim* — wholeness — that encompasses internal structural integrity. Alternatively, there may be specific circumstances under which this condition becomes visible, and the *halacha* is calibrated accordingly.

### Age/Sickness/Sin

The *mishnah* continues with categories that are not physical deformities at all. *Ha-zaken ve-ha-choleh ve-ha-mezuham* — the old, the sick, and the smelly. These are inappropriate as *korbanot* but are not *mumim*. Similarly: *she-ne'evda bo aveirah* — an animal used in bestiality; *she-hemit adam* — an animal that killed a person; *al pi eid echad o al pi ha-baalim* — and those cases established only through one witness or through the owner's own admission. In the last category, the reason the animal survives to be addressed is precisely because there are not two witnesses: with two witnesses, the animal would be stoned and *assur be-hana'ah* — forbidden for any benefit — so the question of *korban* would not arise. With only one witness or the owner's admission, no formal legal status of *shor ha-niskal* attaches, and the animal remains physically available — yet it is excluded from *korban*.

### Tumtum & Gender

Finally: *ve-tumtum ve-androgynos* — these too are neither brought as *korbanot* nor slaughtered outside on the basis of a *mum*. The *tumtum* has covered genitalia — we cannot determine its sex. The *androgynos* has both male and female genitalia. Rabbi Yishmael takes a more stringent view: *ein lecha mum gadol mi-zeh* — if the *androgynos* is considered male, there is no greater blemish than this. The Sages take an even more lenient position: *eino bechor ella nigzaz ve-ne'evad* — it is not even a *bechor*, it may be shorn and worked, because it is not established as male.

## Androgynous Status

### Transgender Discussion

The question of the *androgynos* is not merely an ancient taxonomic puzzle. The possibility that halakhic tradition makes room for a third gender category is one that contemporary discussions of gender identity have noted with interest. The *gemara*'s sustained engagement with whether *androgynos* represents a doubtful case of maleness, a definite male with a *mum*, or an altogether distinct third category has clear resonances with modern conversations about how to classify people who do not fit binary sex categories — including, in practical terms, halakhic questions about individuals who have undergone gender-affirming procedures. The parallel is real even if the categories do not map neatly.

## Scriptural Sources

### Flock Offering

The *gemara* now provides scriptural grounding for the middle category's exclusions. *Minah hanei milei? De-tanya Rabbanan* — where is this derived? The Torah says regarding *korbanot*: *min ha-tzon, u-min ha-kvasim, u-min ha-izim yikareivu* — from the flock, from the sheep, from the goats. The repeated *min* — "from," a limiting preposition — is read as excluding subsets. The three repetitions exclude three categories: old, sick, and smelly.

### Three Limitations

Each exclusion is independently necessary. Had only one been stated, we might have excluded only old, whose deterioration is irreversible, but not sick, who may recover. Had we excluded both old and sick, we might still not have excluded smelly, since that animal is not physically weakened. Conversely, if smelly had been derived first, we would not have known to exclude old and sick, which are physically deteriorated but not repugnant. Or, had we derived smelly on the basis of its disgust factor, we might not have excluded old and sick, which do not evoke the same reaction. Each category has a distinctive character — one does not recover, one is unusual, one is offensive — and all three must be explicitly excluded.

### Bestiality Source

The *gemara* derives the exclusion of animals involved in *aveirot* from the opening verse of the *olah* offering: *adam ki yakriv mikem korban min ha-behemah min ha-bakar u-min ha-tzon* — the three limiting *min*s here exclude, respectively: *roveia ve-nirva* — animals involved in bestiality, whether the active or passive party; *ha-ne'evad* — an animal worshipped as an object of *avodah zarah*; *ha-muktza* — an animal set aside for idolatry even if not actually worshipped; and *ha-nogei'ach* — an animal that gored a person to death.

### One Witness Rule

As noted above, the case of one witness or the owner's admission applies precisely because formal two-witness testimony would trigger the *shor ha-niskal* status and prohibition of benefit. With only one witness, no such formal status attaches and the animal remains halakhically available — but it is still excluded from *korban*, since its history is known to the owner even if legally unproven.

## Warts & Scabs

### Garav Contradiction

A difficulty: the *mishnah* treats *garav* and *yavelet* as middle-category conditions, not full *mumim*. Yet the *Torah* explicitly lists *garav* and *yallefes* as blemishes. The *gemara* asks: *ve-garav lo? ve-chazazit lo?* — surely these are Torah-level blemishes? *Ve-hachasiv garav de-Oraita* — the *Torah* says so explicitly.

### Three Garav Types

The resolution for *chazazit* is somewhat simpler: what the *Torah* calls *yallefes*, the *gemara* equates with *chazazit mitzrit* — the Egyptian variety of *chazazit*, based on the *baraita*: *yallefes zu chazazit mitzrit*. The *mishnah*'s *chazazit*, by contrast, refers to a less severe form — *chazazit de-alma* — and is thus distinguishable. For *garav* the problem is harder: both the *Torah* and the *mishnah* use the same word. The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between dry and moist forms: *ha be-lach ha be-yavesh* — the *Torah*'s *garav*, which is a full *mum*, is dry; the *mishnah*'s *garav*, which is only middle-category, is moist, and therefore capable of healing.

### Healing Capacity

The *gemara* challenges even this: a verse from *Devarim* invokes *garav* in the context of afflictions that *asher lo tuchal le-hirafei* — cannot be healed. And the same verse mentions *cheres*, which the *baraita* identifies as the dry wart. If *garav* appears alongside the incurable *cheres* in that context, perhaps even the moist form is incurable?

The *gemara*'s response is to identify not two but three distinct types of *garav*. The first — *yavesh bein mi-bifnim bein mi-bachutz*, dry both internally and externally — is the *Torah*'s case, a full *mum*, incurable. This is alternatively called *cheres* because it is as hard and dry as pottery. The second — *lach mi-bachutz u-mi-bifnim*, moist both internally and externally — is the *mishnah*'s middle-category case, since it can heal. The third — the one referenced in the *Devarim* verse about incurability — is *yavesh mi-bifnim ve-lach mi-bachutz*: dry internally and moist externally, or hard at the core though moist on the surface. This third type also cannot heal, and therefore also constitutes a full *mum* subject to *shechita*. The scriptural support for its moistness on the outside comes from the phrase *ava'buot poreach* — sprouting blisters — which implies external discharge. Since the *gemara* understands that a growth hard at the core cannot be healed despite external moisture, it too qualifies as a permanent *mum*.

## Philosophy of Perfection

### Inclusion Question

A deeper question underlies the entire *perek*: is the standard of perfection demanded of *korbanot* one that reflects what is pleasing to God, or is it a concession to human aesthetic sensibility? The question is genuinely troubling, particularly because physical perfection is defined here in terms of external appearance. The religious tradition at its highest tends to emphasize the opposite: *ki ha-adam yireh la-einayim ve-Hashem yireh la-levav* — human beings look at the exterior, but God looks at the heart. For those with experience of disability, difference, or special needs, a system in which physical imperfection categorically excludes from the sacred can feel not merely counterintuitive but morally dissonant.

### Concession Answer

The most honest framing may be that this is indeed a concession to human nature. The *Beit Hamikdash* itself — its gold, its silver, its carefully ordered aesthetic — is not strictly necessary if God is everywhere and transcends all materiality. Yet the *Torah* commands extraordinary investment in beauty and form precisely because people are moved by the material, the visible, the aesthetically elevated. For some, the austere intimacy of a *shtiebel* suffices; others need the grandeur of formal architecture and decorum to cultivate a sense of awe. The demand for unblemished animals may serve a parallel function: presenting what is imperfect as an offering risks communicating that the offering is trivial or that the One receiving it is indifferent to quality. The demand for *tamim* maintains the seriousness of the act. That said, this concession carries a genuine cost, and it is not incidental that the *nevi'im* expended considerable effort insisting that *korbanot* are not in themselves what God ultimately desires — that the ethical treatment of one's fellow human being matters more. The critique of overemphasis on sacrifice is itself part of the tradition that surrounds this very institution.

### Prophetic Critique

The tension is irreducible. The system demands physical perfection; the prophets warn against making that demand into the substance of religion. Both responses are authentically within the tradition. And there is an economic dimension too, as Rabbi Akiva made explicit: elaborate extension of the *mum* category imposes real losses on real people. How much those practical human costs should influence the halakhic boundary-drawing is itself a recurring question throughout this *perek*, one that does not resolve cleanly in either direction.

## Androgynous Status…

### Yishmael Position

The *gemara* now returns to the *androgynos* debate in detail. Rabbi Yishmael's position — *ein lecha mum gadol mi-zeh* — is that there is no greater blemish. If the animal is male, then possessing female genitalia is a severe deformity. His position is analyzed through the lens of *Abaye*'s ruling: *o charutz o shavur* — the *Torah*'s list of blemishes includes *charutz* (a crevice) and *shavur* (broken). *Charutz dumya de-shavur* — just as *shavur* refers to a break at a bone, so too *charutz* must occur at a place of bone. Since the female genitalia of the *androgynos* are not in a location of bone, they would not technically fall under the *Torah* category of *charutz* — and Rabbi Yishmael, according to the *gemara*, simply does not accept this restriction. He holds that *charutz* applies to any crevice-like deformity regardless of whether bone is present.

### Rabbanan Position

The Sages, by contrast, hold *eino bechor ella nigzaz ve-ne'evad* — it is not a *bechor* at all, may be shorn, may be worked. Their reasoning is that the *androgynos* is not established as male, and therefore the *kedushah* of *bechor* never attaches. This is a more permissive ruling in practical terms — the owner is not bound by the restrictions of *bechor* at all — but it rests on a more radical conceptual claim: the *androgynos* is not simply a doubtful male but something categorically distinct.

### Gendered Body

*Rava* adds a further consideration. Even without the analogy of *charutz* to *shavur*, one could not say that female genitalia in a place of flesh (without bone) constitutes a *mum*. Why? *Ei salka da'atach havei muma* — if it were a *mum*, then *garav*, which is listed alongside *charutz* in the *Torah*, would have to be explicable without reference to the specific anatomical location. But the *gemara* demonstrates that we derive from the juxtaposition of *garav* and *charutz* that a crevice in a place of flesh does not constitute a *mum* — otherwise the Torah would have had no need to separately list *garav*. We could have inferred *garav* from *charutz* alone if location were irrelevant. The fact that *garav* must be listed separately proves that *charutz* in a location without bone does not independently qualify. Therefore female genitalia, occurring where there is no bone, does not fall under the category of *charutz*. The underlying conceptual move here reflects a longstanding way of thinking about the female body as defined by the absence of male anatomical features — an analytic framework that is both reflected in the *gemara* and worth noting critically.

## Separate Gender

### Doubt vs. Category

*Rava* poses a pointed question about Rabbi Yishmael's position: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Yishmael* — what precisely is his reasoning? More specifically, is it *misha peshita lei* — obvious to him that the *androgynos* is definitively male? Or *o dilma isfukei mesafka lei* — is he uncertain whether it is male or female, and says that either way you may eat it: if female, it is not a *bechor*; if male, *harei mumo imo*, it has its blemish with it and may be slaughtered? The practical difference is significant. If Rabbi Yishmael holds it is definitively male, then an owner who shears or works this animal — rather than giving it to a *kohen* — has violated the prohibition against using a *bechor*. If Rabbi Yishmael holds it is doubtful, the owner might argue that since we cannot establish it is a *bechor*, he cannot be penalized for treating it as ordinary. Similarly, the question of whether the animal must be handed to a *kohen* turns entirely on whether it is certainly or only possibly a *bechor*.

### Zachar Exclusion

A *baraita* is cited: *zachar ve-lo nekevah* — the *olah* must be male, not female. The verse then says *zachar* a second time, and since the first mention already established maleness, the second must add something. *Le-hotzi tumtum ve-androgynos* — to exclude the *tumtum* and the *androgynos*, which are not unambiguously male. The *gemara* analyzes which opinion in the *mishnah* this *baraita* represents. If it is the first *tanna* — who treats the *androgynos* as a *safek* — then we would not need a *pasuk* to exclude a doubtful case, since the *Torah* need not legislate about legal doubts; it addresses factual uncertainties that humans may encounter, not questions of how we categorize an entity whose classification is itself the halakhic question. For this point, Tosafot offer a careful distinction: the *Torah* may instruct what to do when the *facts* are uncertain (as sometimes happens in practice), but it will not tell you how to resolve a *legal* question about how we define a category — that is for the *chachamim* to determine.

### Tosafot Principle

This principle is worth pausing on. Tosafot argue that if the *androgynos*'s status were merely a matter of factual doubt — we are uncertain whether it is male or female — then a *pasuk* could indeed instruct us how to behave under that uncertainty, as the *Torah* sometimes does for other doubtful cases. But if the question is a *legal* one — how should we categorize the *androgynos* as a matter of *halacha*? — then the *Torah* is not going to preempt the rabbinic determination by addressing it directly. The *Torah* leaves legal classification questions to the *chachamim*. Therefore, the fact that a *pasuk* was needed to exclude the *androgynos* implies that its exclusion required a positive scriptural act, which in turn implies it would otherwise have been included — which implies it was considered definitively male (with a *mum*), not merely doubtfully so. This points toward Rabbi Yishmael's position as the one the *baraita* represents.

### Third Gender

The *gemara* ultimately leaves open whether the latter Sages (*Rabbanan batrai*) — who hold the *androgynos* is not a *bechor* at all — are saying it is a separate gender or a doubtful case. The argument about needing two mentions of *zachar* is addressed: even by *bechor*, the *Torah* says *zachar* twice — *hazachar takdish* and *asher yihiyeh lecha ha-zecharim la-Hashem*. If the *androgynos* is a separate third gender, one mention of *zachar* might only exclude the female, while the second mention is needed to exclude this middle category as well. This accounts for why both by *bechor* and by other *korbanot* two exclusions were needed, without necessarily implying that the *androgynos* is simply a doubtful male. The upshot is that we end with three possible characterizations: the *androgynos* as a *safek*, as a definite male with a *mum* (Rabbi Yishmael), and as a categorically separate third gender. And notably, the view that withholds judgment — treating it as neither definitively male nor definitively female — has the interesting consequence of not calling it a *mum* at all. That framing treats the *androgynos* not as defective but as simply other, which carries its own kind of dignity. These discussions will continue in the next *amud*.
