# Niddah 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Women's Testimony

### Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הַנִּבְדָּקוֹת נִבְדָּקוֹת עַל פִּי נָשִׁים, וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹסֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מוֹסֵר לְאִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **All** girls who **are examined** to determine whether or not their signs indicating puberty have appeared **are examined based on** the testimony of **women. And likewise, Rabbi Eliezer** would follow this *halakha* in practice and **give** the girls **to his wife** to examine, **and Rabbi Yishmael** would similarly **give** the girls **to his mother** to examine.

The *sugya* opens with a *baraita*: *kol hanivdakot nivdakot al pi nashim* — all girls who require examination are examined on the basis of women's testimony. The context is the determination of whether a girl has reached adulthood through the appearance of two pubic hairs, a question with direct bearing on the halachic issues we have been discussing throughout this section of the masechta: *miun*, *chalitza*, and related matters of personal status.

### Named Authorities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לִפְנֵי הַפֶּרֶק וּלְאַחַר הַפֶּרֶק — נָשִׁים בּוֹדְקוֹת אוֹתָן, תּוֹךְ הַפֶּרֶק — אֵין נָשִׁים בּוֹדְקוֹת אוֹתָן, שֶׁאֵין מַשִּׂיאִין סְפֵקוֹת עַל פִּי נָשִׁים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף תּוֹךְ הַפֶּרֶק נָשִׁים בּוֹדְקוֹת אוֹתָן, וְנֶאֱמֶנֶת אִשָּׁה לְהַחְמִיר אֲבָל לֹא לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says: Before the** minimum **age** at which pubic hair is considered a sign, i.e., eleven years and one day, when any hair that is found is considered to be merely a mole and not a sign, **and** similarly **after the age** of majority, twelve years and one day, when a female is presumed to have grown pubic hair like most girls her age, **women may examine them** and their testimony is accepted. But **during the time** between eleven years and one day and twelve years and one day, **women may not examine them.** The reason is **that one does not resolve** cases of **uncertainty based on** the testimony of **women. Rabbi Shimon says: Even during the time** between eleven years and one day and twelve years and one day, **women may examine them. And a woman is deemed credible,** and her testimony is accepted, **to be stringent but not to be lenient.**

Three named authorities are introduced, each reflecting a somewhat different orientation toward women's testimony in this context.

Rabbi Eliezer would entrust the examination to his wife whenever a halachic question arose about whether a girl was an adult. Rabbi Yishmael would entrust it to his mother. These details are telling: neither authority speaks of two male witnesses, nor of any formal requirement resembling classical *edut*. The examination was treated as a matter of factual assessment delegated to a trusted woman.

Rabbi Yehuda, however, introduces a more structured limitation: *mifnei haperek ul'achar haperek nashim bodkot otan* — before and after the critical period, women may examine; *toch haperek*, during that period, they may not — *she'ein masi'in sefeikot al pi nashim*, because one does not resolve genuine doubts on the basis of women's testimony alone.

### General Principle

Before proceeding to the details of Rabbi Yehuda's position, it is worth pausing on the underlying question: what precisely is the hesitancy about relying on women's testimony here? One might initially assume it is simply a matter of credibility or accuracy. But that framing is difficult to sustain. The examination does not require technical expertise — there is no elaborate standard being applied, and in later contexts we will see only a modest criterion of hair length. Moreover, in other halachic domains, women are routinely believed: the principle of *eid echad ne'eman b'issurin* applies to women as well, and questions about *niddah* status, *kashrut*, and similar matters are regularly resolved on the basis of a single woman's report.

The more precise answer is that this case sits at the boundary between *issur* — permitted or forbidden — and *maaseh edut*, formal personal-status testimony. When a woman is declared to be an adult, the consequences are not merely about a prohibition being lifted or applied; they reshape her entire legal personality: her capacity to enter into transactions, the validity of acts performed in her name, whether she can do *miun*, whether *chalitza* applies to her. These are precisely the categories associated with *ein davar she'be'ervah pachot mishnayim* — matters of personal status requiring formal testimony. Whether one ultimately locates the issue there or in the lesser standard of *ne'emanus* appropriate when the matter is *avida l'igluyei* (easily verifiable), the tension between these two frameworks is the animating question of the entire discussion.

## Rabbi Yehuda's Position

### Three Periods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לִפְנֵי הַפֶּרֶק בָּעֵי בְּדִיקָה, דְּאִי מִשְׁתַּכְחִי לְאַחַר הַפֶּרֶק — שׁוּמָא נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Granted,** one can understand that **before the** minimum **age** there is **a requirement** for **examination,** so **that if** they **find** hair **after the** minimum **age** which they already saw before the minimum age, these hairs **are** evidently a mere **mole,** not a sign of maturity.

The *Gemara* unpacks Rabbi Yehuda's tripartite division. "Before" the *perek* means before eleven years and a day; "after" means after twelve years and a day; "during" refers to the twelfth year itself, the interval between eleven and a day and twelve and a day. The obvious question the *Gemara* immediately presses is: why does any of this matter? During the twelfth year, even if hairs are present, they are halachically irrelevant — so why would we need women to examine at all?

The answer, and the key to Rabbi Yehuda's entire position, will emerge from the interplay between the three periods and the mechanism of *chazakah d'Rabba*.

### Checking Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְאַחַר הַפֶּרֶק, לְמָה לִי בְּדִיקָה? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: קְטַנָּה שֶׁהִגִּיעָה לִכְלַל שְׁנוֹתֶיהָ אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה בְּדִיקָה, חֲזָקָה הֵבִיאָה סִימָנִין! כִּי אָמַר רָבָא חֲזָקָה לְמֵיאוּן, אֲבָל לַחֲלִיצָה בָּעֲיָא בְּדִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But why do I** need a girl to undergo **an examination after the age** of majority? **Didn't Rava say: A minor girl who reached her full years,** i.e., her age of majority, twelve years and one day, **does not require an examination,** as there is **a presumption** that **she has developed signs** indicating puberty? The Gemara explains: **When Rava said** that one relies on this **presumption,** he was referring specifically **to** her inability from now on to perform **refusal. But with regard to *ḥalitza*,** she **requires an examination** to determine that she has reached maturity.

The *Gemara* asks: *bishlama lifnei haperek ba'ei bdika* — it makes sense to check before the *perek*, because if hairs are found now and later reappear after the *perek*, they can be dismissed as *shuma* — growth from a blemish or mole that predated the age of halachic significance. The prior examination by women thus serves a prospective purpose: it creates a record that enables the hair to be discounted later. Without that prior documentation, one might be forced to treat hairs found at age twelve and a day as genuine *simanim*. With it, one can argue they were there all along and carry no weight.

### Chazakah d'Rabba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹךְ הַפֶּרֶק — אֵין נָשִׁים בּוֹדְקוֹת אוֹתָן, קָסָבַר: תּוֹךְ הַפֶּרֶק כִּלְאַחַר הַפֶּרֶק (דָּמֵי). וּלְאַחַר הַפֶּרֶק, דְּאִיכָּא חֲזָקָה דְּרָבָא — סָמְכִינַן אַנָּשִׁים וּבָדְקִי. תּוֹךְ הַפֶּרֶק, דְּלֵיכָּא חֲזָקָה דְּרָבָא — לָא סָמְכִינַן אַנָּשִׁים וְלָא בָּדְקִי נָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* stated that according to Rabbi Yehuda, women are deemed credible to examine girls who are either younger than eleven years and one day or older than twelve years and one day. But **during the time** between eleven years and one day and twelve years and one day, **women may not examine them.** The Gemara explains that Rabbi Yehuda **holds** that the period **during** this **time** is considered like **after the age** of majority. In other words, the signs indicating puberty that a girl develops during this time are considered to be full-fledged signs, which means that she has reached majority even before the age of twelve and one day. Accordingly, the testimony of women is not accepted for this period, as her change of status enables her to perform *ḥalitza* with her husband's brother. **But after the** minimum **age** of twelve years and one day, **when there is the presumption of Rava** that a girl has already developed signs indicating puberty, one **may rely on women and** have them **examine** the girl. **During** this **age,** between eleven and twelve, **when there is no presumption of Rava** that she has already developed signs indicating puberty, one **may not rely on women, and** therefore **women may not examine** the girl.

The *Gemara* then asks: *ela l'achar haperek lama li bdika* — what is the point of examining after twelve years and a day? The *Gemara* had already taught in a prior discussion that *chazakah d'Rabba* applies — once a girl reaches the appropriate age, there is a presumption that she has developed the requisite *simanim*, and she need not be checked at all.

The *Gemara* clarifies: Rava's *chazakah* is not applied uniformly. It suffices for rabbinic purposes or to achieve stringency — specifically, to prevent her from performing *miun*, since if we presume she is an adult she can no longer exercise that option. But for Torah-level purposes, and specifically where the consequence would be *lenient* — such as permitting *chalitza* — one does not rely on the *chazakah d'Rabba* alone. In those cases, actual examination is required.

This leads directly to the logic underlying Rabbi Yehuda's position regarding women's testimony after the *perek*: their testimony is accepted there because it is *reinforced* by *chazakah d'Rabba*. The combination of the presumption and the women's testimony together provides a sufficient basis for reliance — even, in Rabbi Yehuda's view, for more significant halachic conclusions. Without the *chazakah d'Rabba* to back it up, the testimony of women alone would not be enough.

### During perek Issue

This brings us to the core of the restriction: *toch haperek d'leika chazakah d'Rabba, lo somchinan a'nashim*. During the twelfth year, there is no *chazakah d'Rabba* available to reinforce women's testimony. Whether hairs at that age make a girl an adult at all is itself the subject of the dispute in the *Gemara* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that the twelfth year is treated like *after* the *perek*, meaning hairs during that year would confer adult status. But even so, without the *chazakah d'Rabba* that becomes available only at twelve and a day, the testimony of women stands unreinforced, and a genuine *safek* remains. It is precisely in this zone of unresolved doubt, absent any supporting presumption, that Rabbi Yehuda refuses to rely on women's testimony.

## Rabbi Shimon Position

### Shimon Permits Checking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף תּוֹךְ הַפֶּרֶק, נָשִׁים בּוֹדְקוֹת אוֹתָן. קָסָבַר: תּוֹךְ הַפֶּרֶק כְּלִפְנֵי הַפֶּרֶק, וּבָעֲיָא בְּדִיקָה, דְּאִי מִשְׁתַּכְחִי לְאַחַר הַפֶּרֶק — שׁוּמָא נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **Rabbi Shimon says** that **even during** this **age,** between eleven years and one day and twelve years and one day, **women may examine** girls to determine if they have grown pubic hair. This is because Rabbi Shimon **holds** that the period **during** this **age** is considered **like before the** minimum **age,** which means that the girl is a minor based on her age, regardless of the results of the examination. **And** the only reason she **requires an examination** is **that if** they **find** hair **after the** minimum **age,** which they had already seen before the minimum age, these hairs **are** deemed a mere **mole,** not a sign of maturity.

Rabbi Shimon's position is stated as follows: *af toch haperek nashim bodkot otan* — even during the *perek*, women may examine. This seems at first glance to be a broader permission than Rabbi Yehuda's. But the *Gemara* immediately qualifies it: *ne'emenet isha l'hachmir v'lo l'hakel* — a woman is believed to produce a stringent result, not a lenient one.

The *Gemara* then explains Rabbi Shimon's underlying view: he holds that *toch haperek k'lifnei haperek* — the twelfth year is treated like the period *before* the *perek*, not after. That means two hairs found during that year do not confer adult status. Why, then, examine at all? Because documentation of those hairs during the twelfth year serves the same purpose as examination before eleven and a day: if those hairs are later present after twelve and a day, one can point to the earlier examination and say they are *shuma*, reducing or eliminating the halachic significance of what appears to be pubic hair at the relevant moment.

### Stringent Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? ״גְּדוֹלָה הִיא״ — שֶׁלֹּא תְּמָאֵן, ״קְטַנָּה הִיא״ — שֶׁלֹּא תַּחְלוֹץ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **How so,** i.e., how is a woman's testimony accepted with regard to the girl she has examined as a stringency but not a leniency? If the woman said that the girl is **a female who reached majority,** her claim is accepted to the extent **that she does not perform refusal.** The refusal of a husband, which applies in the case of a girl whose father had died and who was married off by her mother or brother, can be performed by the girl only until she reaches majority. If the woman said that this girl is **a minor,** her testimony is accepted to the extent **that she does not perform *ḥalitza*.**

The practical meaning of Rabbi Shimon's rule that women may be believed only to produce stringency is spelled out clearly. If women testify that the girl is an adult (*gedola hi*), we accept that testimony *shelo temaein* — we do not permit her to perform *miun*, since *miun* is available only to minors. If they testify she is a minor (*ketana hi*), we accept that *shelo tachlotz* — we do not permit her to perform *chalitza*, since *chalitza* requires adult status. In both cases, the testimony is used to foreclose an option rather than to enable one. What women's testimony cannot do, according to Rabbi Shimon, is establish adult status so as to *permit* her to perform *chalitza*, or establish minor status so as to *permit* her to perform *miun*.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֶאֱמֶנֶת אִשָּׁה לְהַחְמִיר, אֲבָל לֹא לְהָקֵל. הַאי מַאן קָתָנֵי לַהּ? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְאַתּוֹךְ הַפֶּרֶק. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וּלְאַחַר הַפֶּרֶק, וְלֵית לֵיהּ חֲזָקָה דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches: **And a woman is deemed credible** to testify **to be stringent** with regard to the girl who she examined **but not to be lenient.** The Gemara asks: **Who taught this** *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** it is referring to **during** the **age** between eleven years and one day and twelve and one day. **And if you wish, say** instead that the *tanna* who taught this ruling was **Rabbi Shimon,** and the *baraita* is referring to an examination conducted **after the age** of majority. **And** Rabbi Shimon **does not** accept **the presumption of Rava** that a girl of this age has already developed signs indicating puberty.

The *Gemara* then draws the two positions together and observes that they are more alike than different. The concluding formulation — *ne'emenet isha l'hachmir v'lo l'hakel* — applies, according to the *Gemara*, either to Rabbi Yehuda's position regarding the *toch haperek* period, or to Rabbi Shimon's position regarding the period *after* the *perek*. Rabbi Shimon, on this reading, does not accept *chazakah d'Rabba*, and therefore even after twelve and a day he will not use women's testimony to produce a lenient outcome. Rabbi Yehuda accepts *chazakah d'Rabba* and therefore is willing to rely on women's testimony after twelve and a day in combination with that presumption. The fundamental agreement between them is this: neither authority will rely on women's testimony in isolation to produce leniency; the disagreement is about when *chazakah d'Rabba* is available and whether it can bridge the gap.

## Practical Application

### Tosafot/Rambam

How does the *halacha* emerge from this debate? Tosafot, at the end of the long *dibur hamaschil* of *iba'is eima*, states the matter plainly: *u'ma'asim b'chol yom d'somchinan a'bedikas nashim l'achar haperek* — in practice every day we rely on the examination of women after the *perek*. Tosafot further records that Rabbi ruled that the *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Shimon. The Rambam, similarly, rules straightforwardly that we rely on women's testimony for this purpose.

Tosafot also notes a separate question about whether the *Gemara*'s language implies that checking before the *perek* is obligatory. Tosafot rejects that reading; while it may be advisable *lechatchila* to check beforehand, no authority holds it to be strictly required.

### Both Required?

A further question follows from the analysis: is the accepted practice based on relying on women's testimony *independently*, in the manner of the *Tanna Kamma*, or is it grounded specifically on the combination of *chazakah d'Rabba* with women's examination, in the manner of Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning as explained by the *Gemara*? The practical stakes are significant. If reliance on women is independent, then women's testimony would suffice across a range of circumstances — including the earlier stages and different categories of examination. If it is grounded in *chazakah d'Rabba*, then women's testimony is authoritative only after twelve and a day, when the *chazakah* is operative, and only for the specific question of pubic hair. The conclusion of both Tosafot and the Rambam is that we do rely on women's examination, but the precise theoretical basis — whether it is independent *ne'emanus* or *ne'emanus* reinforced by *chazakah d'Rabba* — bears on how far that reliance extends.

## Mishnah Conclusion

### Rhetorical Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' כׇּל כְּלִי חֶרֶס שֶׁהוּא מַכְנִיס — מוֹצִיא, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁמּוֹצִיא וְאֵינוֹ מַכְנִיס. (וְכׇּל אֵבֶר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ צִפּוֹרֶן — יֵשׁ בּוֹ עֶצֶם, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ עֶצֶם וְאֵין בּוֹ צִפּוֹרֶן. וְכׇּל הַמִּטַּמֵּא מִדְרָס — מִטַּמֵּא טְמֵא מֵת, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁמִּטַּמֵּא טְמֵא מֵת — וְאֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא מִדְרָס.)
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Similar to** the order of the appearance of the signs of puberty in a girl, where it is impossible for the upper sign to appear before the lower sign, there is an analogous principle with regard to the mutual dependency of two items: **Any earthenware vessel** with a hole **that enables entry** of liquid into the vessel certainly **enables exit** of liquid through that hole, and it thereby ceases to be a vessel fit for sanctification of the waters mixed with the ashes of the red heifer. **And there are** holes **that enable exit** of liquids from the earthenware vessels **but do not enable entry** of liquids from outside the vessel, and therefore it remains a vessel. Likewise, **in any limb** of the body **where there is a nail, there is** certainly **a bone in it** as well. If it is the limb of a corpse, it transmits ritual impurity through contact, movement, and in a tent, even if its size is less than that of an olive-bulk. **And there are** limbs **in which there is a bone but** yet **there is not a nail in it.** That limb does not transmit impurity in a tent if its size is less than that of an olive-bulk. Similarly, **any** item **that becomes ritually impure** with impurity of a *zav* imparted by **treading,** e.g., a vessel designated for sitting, **becomes ritually impure** with **impurity imparted by a corpse. And there are** vessels **that become ritually impure** with **impurity imparted by a corpse but do not become ritually impure** with impurity of a *zav* imparted by **treading.**

The *Mishnah* we have been studying states at its outset that it is impossible for the upper *siman* — breast development — to appear before the lower *siman* of pubic hair, and then restates this impossibility at its conclusion. The *Gemara* asks: *ha tu lama li* — why repeat what was already stated at the beginning? Since the anonymous voice of the *Mishnah* already rules against the lone opinion of Rabbi Meir who held that the upper *siman* could appear first, the principle *yachid v'rabbim, halacha k'rabbim* — in a dispute between an individual and the majority, we rule with the majority — would seem to make the restatement superfluous.

The *Gemara* responds: one might have thought that Rabbi Meir's position is particularly compelling, since *psukim* appear to support it. The restatement comes to teach explicitly that we nonetheless rule like the *rabbanan*, notwithstanding the textual support Rabbi Meir might claim.

### Structural Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִסְתְּמַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן — פְּשִׁיטָא! יָחִיד וְרַבִּים — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּים. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּקָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ קְרָאֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִתְנֵי ״כַּיּוֹצֵא בוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** It is necessary for the *tanna* to state that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis despite the fact that they are the majority, **lest you say that the rationale for** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir is** more **reasonable.** One might have thought this, **as the verses** cited above **support it:** "Your breasts were fashioned, and your hair was grown," and: "When they from Egypt bruised your breasts for the sprouting forth of your young womanhood." Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **And if you wish, say** instead that the *tanna* repeated the claim that the upper sign cannot precede the lower one **because** he **wants to teach** a case in the next mishna which is **similar to** this one, i.e., this summary provides a transition to the *halakha* brought in the following mishna.

But the *Gemara* offers an alternative answer: *iba'is eima*, the restatement is not required for halachic purposes at all. Its function is literary and structural — *mishum d'ka'bei l'masnei kiyotzei bo* — because the *Mishnah* wishes to introduce the next *mishnah* with an identical structural formula. This is one of several places in *Shas* where consecutive *mishnayos* are connected not by shared subject matter but by shared logical form. The pattern being established is: A can exist without B, but B cannot exist without A. Pubic hair can appear without breast development, but breast development cannot appear without pubic hair. Having stated it this way, the *Mishnah* naturally opens onto a series of other cases exhibiting the same asymmetric structure — cases that have nothing to do with *niddah* but share this logical skeleton.

## Parallel Mishnayos

### Pottery Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל כְּלִי חֶרֶס שֶׁהוּא מַכְנִיס — מוֹצִיא, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁמּוֹצִיא וְאֵינוֹ מַכְנִיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Any earthenware vessel** with a hole **that enables entry** of liquid into the vessel certainly **enables exit** of liquid through that hole, and it thereby ceases to be a vessel fit for sanctification of the waters mixed with the ashes of the red heifer. **And there are** holes **that enable exit** of liquids from the earthenware vessels **but do not enable entry** of liquids from outside the vessel, and therefore it remains a vessel.

The next *mishnah* introduces the first of several parallel cases: *kol kli cheres shemachnis motzi* — any pottery vessel whose hole is large enough for water to enter is certainly large enough for water to exit. *V'yeish shemotzi v'eino machnis* — but some vessels have holes through which water exits but does not enter.

The underlying physical reality is straightforward. Water exiting a vessel is assisted by gravity and by the water pressure of the liquid inside pressing downward and outward against the hole. Water entering a vessel from outside must overcome that same pressure without the aid of gravity. Consequently, a smaller hole will allow exit without allowing entry. The hole required for entry is therefore larger than the hole required for exit — and anything large enough to admit water is certainly large enough to let it out.

The halachic stakes of this distinction concern the status of the vessel. A hole sufficient to invalidate a pottery vessel as a vessel for most purposes is one large enough for water to enter. A hole through which water merely exits but does not enter does not, as a general matter, compromise the vessel's standing.

### Parah Adumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְנִיס — פָּסוּל לְמֵי חַטָּאת, וּפָסוּל מִשּׁוּם גִּסְטְרָא. מוֹצִיא — כָּשֵׁר לְמֵי חַטָּאת, וּפָסוּל מִשּׁוּם גִּסְטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** A vessel that contains a hole that is large enough to enable liquid to **enter** is no longer considered a vessel and is therefore **unfit to** contain the **water of purification. And** it is also **disqualified as a shard [*gastera*]** of a vessel. A shard still has some utility and is therefore susceptible to ritual impurity. By contrast, an earthenware vessel that contains a small hole **that enables** only the **exit** of liquids is **fit for** the **water of purification, but** is **disqualified as a shard** of a vessel.

The *Gemara* then introduces two specific contexts in which these distinctions apply differently. First, *machnis pasul l'mei chatas*: a hole large enough for water to enter disqualifies a pottery vessel from being used to hold the water mixed with the ashes of the *parah adumah*. The process of mixing the *mei chatas* requires a proper vessel; a vessel with an entry-sized hole has lost that status.

A second and more delicate case involves the *gistra* — a pottery shard that was designated as a vessel. A broken piece of pottery that retains a concave shape can, if designated for a purpose such as holding oil, acquire the status of a vessel. But because this status is fragile — the *gistra* is barely a vessel to begin with — even a hole through which water merely exits, though insufficient to invalidate a standard pottery vessel, is sufficient to strip the *gistra* of its vessel-status. The principle stated by the *Gemara* is that one does not say *havei gistera l'gistera* — you do not use a shard to patch a shard. Once a *gistra* develops a defect, there is no salvaging it. Standard vessels can absorb a small imperfection; this marginal vessel cannot.

The practical upshot: a hole large enough for water to enter disqualifies all vessels from *mei chatas* use and strips the *gistra* of its status. A hole large enough only for water to exit leaves standard pottery vessels unaffected but still disqualifies the *gistra*.

### Testing Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד בּוֹדְקִין כְּלִי חֶרֶס לֵידַע אִם נִיקַּב בְּכוֹנֵס מַשְׁקֶה אִם לָאו? יָבִיא עֲרֵיבָה מְלֵאָה מַיִם, וְנוֹתֵן קְדֵרָה לְתוֹכָהּ. אִם כְּנָסָהּ — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁכּוֹנֵס מַשְׁקֶה, וְאִם לָאו — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁמּוֹצִיא מַשְׁקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **How does one test** a broken **earthenware vessel to know if it was pierced** with a hole **that enables liquid to enter** it **or not? One brings a tub filled with water and places** the broken **pot into it. If** the water from the tub **enters** the pot, **it is known that** the pot contains a hole **that enables liquid to enter. And if** the water does **not** enter the pot, **it is known that** the vessel contains only a small hole **that** merely **enables** the **exit** of **liquids.**

The *Gemara* raises a question that turns out to be less simple than expected: *keitzad bodkin kli cheres leida im nikav b'kinus mashkeh im lav* — how does one actually test whether the hole in a pottery vessel is large enough for water to enter?

The first method proposed: fill a large trough with water, place the suspect vessel inside it so the hole is submerged, and see whether water enters. If it does, the hole is entry-sized.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כּוֹפֵף אׇזְנֵי קְדֵרָה לְתוֹכָהּ וּמֵצִיף עָלֶיהָ מַיִם, וְאִם כּוֹנֵס — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁכּוֹנֵס מַשְׁקֶה, וְאִם לָאו — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁמּוֹצִיא מַשְׁקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the method for determining whether an earthenware vessel contains a hole that allows liquid to enter is as follows: One takes the **handles of the pot and turns it over,**placing it upside down **in** an empty tub, **and** he then **covers** the pot with **water. If** water **enters** the pot, **it is known that** it contains a hole **that enables liquid to enter, and if** the water does **not** enter the pot, **it is known that** the vessel contains a small hole **that enables** only the **exit** of **liquids.**

Rabbi Yehuda objects. When you push the vessel, floor first, into a body of water, the water exerts considerable pressure against the hole — more than it normally would. Water entering under that pressure does not prove that the hole is genuinely entry-sized under ordinary conditions. The test is therefore distorted. Rabbi Yehuda's preferred method is the reverse: invert the vessel so that the floor faces upward, place it inside the larger trough, and flood the trough with water so that the water level is above the upended floor. Now a thin, gravity-limited layer of water sits above the hole. If water penetrates under those conditions, the hole is genuinely large enough to admit water without extraordinary pressure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ שׁוֹפְתָהּ עַל גַּבֵּי הָאוּר, אִם הָאוּר מַעֲמִידָהּ — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁמּוֹצִיא מַשְׁקֶה, וְאִם לָאו — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁמַּכְנִיס מַשְׁקֶה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף לֹא שׁוֹפְתָהּ עַל גַּבֵּי הָאוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָאוּר מַעֲמִידָהּ, אֶלָּא שׁוֹפְתָהּ עַל גַּבֵּי הָרֶמֶץ. אִם רֶמֶץ מַעֲמִידָהּ — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁמּוֹצִיא מַשְׁקֶה, וְאִם לָאו — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁכּוֹנֵס מַשְׁקֶה. הָיָה טוֹרֵד טִיפָּה אַחַר טִיפָּה — בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁכּוֹנֵס מַשְׁקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** one can determine the size of the hole by the following method: One **places** the pot, with liquid in it, **on the fire. If the fire holds** the liquid back and does not allow it to exit the vessel, then **it is known** that the vessel contains a small hole **that enables** only the **exit** of **liquids. And if** the fire does **not** hold the liquid back and does not prevent it from exiting the vessel, then **it is known** that it contains a hole **that enables liquid to enter. Rabbi Yosei says: One should not place** the pot with liquid in it **on the fire.** This is not a reliable test for determining the size of the hole, as it is possible that the hole is actually large enough to enable liquid to enter, but nevertheless **the fire prevents** the liquid from exiting. **Rather, one places** the pot with liquid in it **on hot ash. If the hot ash holds** the liquid back and does not allow it to exit the vessel, then **it is known** that the vessel contains a small hole **that enables** only the **exit** of **liquids. But if** the hot ash does **not** hold the liquid back and does not prevent it from exiting the vessel, **it is known** that it contains a hole **that enables liquid to enter.** Another manner of testing is to fill the vessel with liquid. If it **drips** one **drop after** another **drop, it is known** that it contains a hole **that enables liquid to enter.**

A third approach uses heat. The vessel, filled with water, is placed over a fire. The heat, it is observed, has the effect of sealing or resisting outflow from the hole. Therefore: if even with the fire burning beneath it the water inside escapes, the hole is substantial — it is large enough that water would also enter from outside under normal conditions. If the fire successfully arrests the outflow, the hole is smaller, belonging to the *motzi* category.

Rabbi Yossi disputes this final approach. He argues that fire is too powerful — it will arrest the outflow even through a large hole, so proving nothing. Instead, he recommends using *remetz*, hot embers or smoldering ash, which generate heat but less dramatically. Under the embers: if outflow is arrested, the hole is small; if outflow continues despite the heat, the hole is large enough to admit water.

A final observation: *haya toreif tipah achar tipah* — if water drips out in a continuous stream, drop after drop, that too constitutes evidence that the hole is entry-sized, since such rapid exit implies a substantial aperture.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין תַּנָּא קַמָּא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר עוּלָּא: כִּינּוּס עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק אִיכָּא בֵינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** difference **is there between** the method of testing stated by **the first *tanna*,** placing the vessel in a tub of water, and that **of Rabbi Yehuda,** placing the vessel upside down into the tub and then covering it with water? **Ulla said:** The difference **between** their opinions is whether liquid that **enters** through a hole **with difficulty,** i.e., as the result of force, is considered entering. According to Rabbi Yehuda, placing the vessel directly into a tub of water constitutes the use of force to a certain degree, and he maintains that if water enters the vessel in such a case, this does not count as liquid entering the vessel. Therefore, he rejects the testing method of the first *tanna*.

The *Gemara* concludes by identifying the crux of the dispute between the first method and Rabbi Yehuda's method: *koness al yedei ha'dechak ika beineiyahu* — the question is whether water that enters only under the pressure of submersion counts as *kinus*, entry, for halachic purposes. The first opinion holds that pressure-entry is still entry. Rabbi Yehuda holds that only unpressured entry is probative.

## Extra Limbs

### Both Bone and Nail

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל אֵבֶר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ צִפּוֹרֶן — יֵשׁ בּוֹ עֶצֶם, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ עֶצֶם וְאֵין בּוֹ צִפּוֹרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **In any limb** of the body **where there is a nail, there is** certainly **a bone in it** as well. If it is the limb of a corpse, it transmits ritual impurity through contact, movement, and in a tent, even if its size is less than that of an olive-bulk. **And there are** limbs **in which there is a bone but** yet **there is not a nail in it.** That limb does not transmit impurity in a tent if its size is less than that of an olive-bulk.

The *mishnah* turns to a structurally parallel case: *kol eiver sheyeish bo tziporen yeish bo etzem* — every limb that has a fingernail also has a bone. *V'yeish sheyeish bo etzem v'ein bo tziporen* — but some have a bone without a fingernail. The context is extra digits — supernumerary fingers or toes on a person's body — and the question is whether such an appendage constitutes a halachic *eiver*, a limb, for the purposes of *tumah*.

The general rule is that primary fingers and toes do not require an additional criterion; their status as limbs is inherent. But extra, supernumerary digits are treated more stringently: they qualify as *eiver min hachai* or *eiver min hameit* only if they possess both bone and nail.

### Bone Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ בּוֹ צִפּוֹרֶן — מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא וּבְאֹהֶל; יֵשׁ בּוֹ עֶצֶם וְאֵין בּוֹ צִפּוֹרֶן — מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** A limb **in which there is a nail** and which therefore certainly contains a bone has the status of a full-fledged limb. Therefore, it **transmits impurity** through **contact, movement, and in a tent,** even if its size is less than that of an olive-bulk. By contrast, if **there is a bone in** the limb **but there is no nail,** it **transmits impurity** through **contact and movement** even if its size is less than that of an olive-bulk, **but** it does **not transmit impurity in a tent** unless its size is that of an olive-bulk.

If the extra digit has a bone but no fingernail, it does not qualify as a full *eiver*. The consequence: *metamei bemaga uvemasa v'eino metamei be'ohel*. It transmits *tumah* through contact and through carrying, but it does not transmit *tumah* through *ohel* — being under the same roof. The reason is that *ohel tumah* from a limb requires either a full limb or a *kezayit* of flesh. An extra digit lacking a nail does not meet the criteria for a full limb, and without sufficient flesh it cannot independently generate *tumah* through a tent.

### No Nail Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּכְשֶׁאֵינָהּ נִסְפֶּרֶת עַל גַּב הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And** this is the *halakha*, that it must contain both bone and a nail for it to be considered a limb, only in a case **where** this finger **cannot be counted along the back of the hand,** i.e., the extra finger is not aligned with the others. But if it is aligned with the other fingers then it is considered like any other limb and imparts impurity in a tent, whether or not it contains a nail.

Rabba bar Bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan introduces a qualification: this requirement of a nail applies specifically when the extra digit is *not* aligned with the other fingers — when it grows at an angle and would not naturally be included if one were counting fingers. If, however, the extra digit is aligned with the other fingers and would naturally be counted among them, then even without a fingernail it possesses the status of a full *eiver*. The misalignment, not merely the absence of a nail, is what triggers the more demanding standard.

## Seats vs. Vessels

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמִּטַּמֵּא מִדְרָס — מִטַּמֵּא טְמֵא מֵת, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁמִּטַּמֵּא טְמֵא מֵת — וְאֵינוֹ מִטַּמֵּא מִדְרָס.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **any** item **that becomes ritually impure** with impurity of a *zav* imparted by **treading,** e.g., a vessel designated for sitting, **becomes ritually impure** with **impurity imparted by a corpse. And there are** vessels **that become ritually impure** with **impurity imparted by a corpse but do not become ritually impure** with impurity of a *zav* imparted by **treading.**

The third parallel case: *kol hametamei midras metamei tamei met* — anything that can become *tamei* through *midras*, the sitting of a *zav* or *zavah*, can also become *tamei* through contact with a dead body. *V'yeish shemetamei tamei met v'eino metamei midras* — but some things can become *tamei* from a dead body without being susceptible to *midras tumah*. Both are *av hatumah* — primary sources of *tumah* — but the conditions under which each applies differ.

### Measuring Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ שֶׁמִּטַּמֵּא טְמֵא מֵת וְאֵין מִטַּמֵּא מִדְרָס. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי סְאָה וְתַרְקַב. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְהַיּוֹשֵׁב עַל הַכְּלִי״, יָכוֹל כָּפָה סְאָה וְיָשַׁב עָלֶיהָ, אוֹ תַּרְקַב וְיָשַׁב עָלָיו, יְהֵא טָמֵא?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And there are** vessels **that become ritually impure** with **impurity imparted by a corpse but do not become ritually impure** with impurity of a *zav* imparted by **treading.** The Gemara asks: **What is added** by this statement? The Gemara answers: This serves **to add** a measuring vessel, e.g., the measure of **a *se'a* or a half-*se'a* [*vetarkav*].** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the impurity of the treading of a *zav*: **"And he who sits on** any **object** whereon the *zav* sits shall wash his clothes and bathe himself in water, and is impure until the evening" (Leviticus 15:6). One **might** have thought that if a *zav* **turned over** a vessel used to measure **a *se'a* and sat on it, or** if he turned over a vessel used to measure **a half-*se'a* and sat on it,** that vessel **should be** rendered **impure** as a seat upon which a *zav* sat.

The *Gemara* asks what this inclusion captures and answers: *le'asuyei sa'ah v'tarkav* — a measuring vessel. The *Gemara* immediately notes that this point was already made in a prior context — in a discussion about the *Sanhedrin* where the same distinction was drawn and Rav Yehuda already offered *mamzer* as the example. Is there a redundancy?

### Primary Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲשֶׁר יֵשֵׁב עָלָיו הַזָּב״ — מִי שֶׁמְיוּחָד לִישִׁיבָה, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאוֹמְרִים לוֹ: עֲמוֹד וְנַעֲשֶׂה מְלַאכְתֵּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And he who sits on any object **whereon the *zav* sits"** (Leviticus 15:6). The wording of the verse indicates that it is speaking of an object **that is designated for sitting,** i.e., upon which people generally sit, **excluding such** a vessel, with regard to **which we say to** someone sitting on it: **Stand up and** allow us to use it to **do our work,** i.e., to measure. This is not defined as a vessel used for sitting, as it serves another function.

The principle is that designation matters. A vessel that people will immediately reclaim for its primary function whenever someone tries to use it as a seat — *amad v'na'amod melachtenu*, "get up, we need to use that for measuring" — can never genuinely be designated as a seat. Because it cannot sustain the designation, it cannot become *tamei* as a *mishkav u'moshav* of a *zav* or *zavah*. It will, however, become *tamei* through contact with a dead body like any other object. The *Gemara* notes that a simpler example could have been given — a pin, say, which is clearly not a seat — but this case is more instructive precisely because the measuring vessel *can* physically function as a seat yet still fails the halachic standard, due entirely to the impossibility of its serving as one's *primary* designation.

## Judges Standards

### Judge Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' כׇּל הָרָאוּי לָדוּן דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת — רָאוּי לָדוּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁרָאוּי לָדוּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת וְאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לָדוּן דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any** person **who is fit to adjudicate** cases of **capital law is fit to adjudicate** cases of **monetary law, and there are** those **who are fit to adjudicate** cases of **monetary law but are unfit to adjudicate** cases of **capital law.**

The final *mishnah* on the daf introduces another case of asymmetric qualification: *kol hara'uy ladun dinei nefashot ra'uy ladun dinei mamonot* — anyone fit to judge capital cases is fit to judge monetary cases. *V'yeish shera'uy ladun dinei mamonot v'eino ra'uy ladun dinei nefashot* — but some are fit for monetary cases and not for capital ones.

### Mamzer Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר. תְּנֵינָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא: הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין לָדוּן דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, וְאֵין הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרִין לָדוּן דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ, לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר. חֲדָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי גֵּר, וַחֲדָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַמְזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda said:** The statement of the mishna that some are fit to adjudicate cases of monetary law but are unfit to adjudicate cases of capital law serves **to add** the case of **a *mamzer*.** Although he may not adjudicate cases of capital law, nevertheless he may adjudicate cases of monetary law. The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary to teach this here? **We** already **learned** this on **another occasion,** in a mishna (*Sanhedrin* 32a): **All are fit to judge** cases of **monetary law, but not all are fit to judge** cases of **capital law. And we discussed it,** and asked **what is added** by the phrase: All are fit to judge. **And Rav Yehuda said** in response that this serves **to add** the case of **a *mamzer*.** The Gemara answers: **One** mishna serves **to add** the case of **a convert, and one** other mishna serves **to add** the case of **a *mamzer*.**

The *Gemara* asks what this inclusion captures and answers: *le'asuyei mamzer* — a *mamzer*, a person born of a union that renders him of disqualified lineage. The *Gemara* immediately notes that this point was already made in a prior context — in a discussion about the *Sanhedrin* where the same distinction was drawn and Rav Yehuda already offered *mamzer* as the example. Is there a redundancy?

The *Gemara* resolves this by saying that the two sources are excluding two *different* categories. One excludes a *mamzer*; the other excludes a *ger*, a convert.

### Ger Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גֵּר — מִשּׁוּם דְּרָאוּי לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל, אֲבָל מַמְזֵר דְּאֵין רָאוּי לָבֹא בַּקָּהָל, אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מַמְזֵר — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָאָתֵי מִטִּפָּה כְּשֵׁרָה, אֲבָל גֵּר דְּקָאָתֵי מִטִּפָּה פְּסוּלָה — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And** both additions **are necessary. As, if** the *mishnayot* had **taught us** only that **a convert** is fit to judge cases of monetary law, one might have said that the *halakha* is lenient in the case of a convert **because** he is **fit to enter into the congregation,** i.e., marry a Jewish woman. **But** with regard to **a *mamzer*, who is unfit to enter into the congregation,** one might **say** that **he** is **not** fit to judge cases of monetary law. **And if** the *mishnayot* had **taught us** only that **a *mamzer*** is fit to judge cases of monetary law, one might have said that the *halakha* is lenient in the case of a *mamzer* **because he comes from a fit drop** of semen, i.e., his father is Jewish. **But** with regard to **a convert, who comes from an unfit drop,** as he was born a gentile, one might **say** that he is **not** fit to judge cases of monetary law. Therefore, it is **necessary** to teach the *halakhot* of both a convert and a *mamzer*.

The *ger* is also excluded from serving as a judge in capital cases, despite being in every other respect a full member of the Jewish people. This is a striking ruling. In many domains — certainly in the famous formulation at the end of *Horayot* — a *mamzer talmid chacham* takes precedence over a *kohen gadol am ha'aretz*, and questions of *yichus* recede before Torah scholarship. Yet in this specific context of capital adjudication, both the *mamzer* and the *ger* are excluded.

The *Gemara* explains why both exclusions must be stated independently. If only the exclusion of the *ger* had been stated, one might have reasoned: the *ger* is excluded because he is *ra'uy lavo ba'kahal* — able to marry into the community without restriction — and yet is excluded, perhaps because his non-Jewish lineage creates some disqualification. But one might have thought that the *mamzer*, whose inability to marry into the congregation, *ein ra'uy lavo ba'kahal*, makes his communal standing more precarious, is excluded *a fortiori*. Conversely, if only the *mamzer* had been stated, one might have thought his exclusion stems from the fact that he was at least conceived by Jews — *kasi mitipa keshera* — even if through a forbidden union. The *ger*, who *kasi mitipa pesula* — comes from an entirely non-Jewish lineage — might then have been thought excluded for an even stronger reason, making the explicit statement unnecessary. Or the reverse: precisely because the *ger* is in some sense more fully integrated, one might not have extended the *mamzer*'s exclusion to him. *Tzericha* — both exclusions are needed, and each carries its own independent reasoning.
