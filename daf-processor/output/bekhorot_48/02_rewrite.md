# Bekhorot 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Unknown Firstborn Twins

### Two Male Sons—Five Sela'…

The *mishnah* on amud 1 opens with a case of genuine uncertainty about the identity of the firstborn. Earlier in the tractate, the confusion arose from a mix-up between wives — a *kohenet* or *leviyah* whose son is exempt from *pidyon haben* — but now the scenario is different: a man whose wife had never previously given birth delivers twins, and no one knows which son emerged first.

The *mishnah* states: *mi shelo bikra ishto veyalda shenei zacharin* — his wife had never borne children and now delivered two male sons. The implicit fact is that the birth order is unknown. Nevertheless, the father gives five *sela'im* to the *kohen*. The reasoning is straightforward: there certainly is a firstborn son; the uncertainty is merely about which twin it is. And so *pidyon haben* is performed. As an aside, none of the complexities regarding the double portion of inheritance or the writing of a *harsha'ah* apply here, since those issues arose from a different kind of confusion. Here we are squarely back to *pidyon*.

### Death Within 30 Days

The *mishnah* then introduces a complication: *mes echad mehen betoch sheloshim yom* — one of the twins died, God forbid, within thirty days. Since the obligation to redeem only takes effect on day thirty, the father may argue to the *kohen* that perhaps it was his firstborn son who died. He is therefore exempt.

This is a striking point worth pausing on. One might expect that even under conditions of doubt — a *safek* — the father would be pressed to perform the *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben*. Yet the *mishnah* rules him exempt. This reflects a fundamental framing: the obligation of *pidyon haben* hinges not merely on the ritual dimension but on a civil liability, the enforceable debt of five *sela'im* owed to the *kohen*. And a civil obligation can only be enforced when it can be proven. Since the father can credibly deflect the claim, he is released. This is, as we will see, quite a pervasive feature of how the *gemara* understands this entire topic.

### Father Dies—Sons Obligat…

The next scenario: *mes ha'av vehabbanim kayamim* — the father dies while both sons survive. Now the question of self-redemption arises, since a son not redeemed by his father must redeem himself. But which son is obligated? Neither can be proven to be the *bechor*.

If one approaches this as a question of *mitzvah* performance alone — I am in doubt, therefore I ought to fulfill the *mitzvah* — the answer would seem obvious: each son should redeem himself as a matter of *safek mitzvah*. But the *mishnah* treats this as a civil matter. Under civil framing, I cannot prove that *you* are the *bechor*, and you cannot prove that *he* is the *bechor*. The *kohen* approaches one brother, who directs him to the other; he approaches the other, who directs him back. Each individual simply deflects the claim. A civil case can only be brought against one person at a time, and neither can be held individually.

### Rebbi Meir vs. Rebbi Yeh…

*Rebbi Meir* addresses the case where the father has died and the sons are debating whether to pay before or after dividing the estate. He says: *im nasnu ad shelo chalku nasnu* — if they gave the *pidyon* before dividing the estate, it stands; the money has already been given. *Ve'im lav, peturim* — but if the estate has since been divided and payment has not yet been made, they are exempt. The *gemara* will explore why division should matter, but Rebbi Meir's logic presumably rests on the idea that before division there exists some enforceable claim on the estate as a whole, even if no individual can be specifically targeted. After division, each brother stands alone with his portion, and each can deflect the claim.

*Rebbi Yehuda* takes the position: *nitchayvu nechasim* — the property itself becomes obligated. Even after the estate is divided, one may still collect from the property. At first glance this seems almost self-evident, but as we will see, the *gemara* is far from convinced that it is.

### Mixed-Gender Twins

The *mishnah* closes this section with the case of *zachar unekeivah* — a male and female born together. Here, *ein kan lakohen klum* — there is nothing at all for the *kohen*. Unlike the case of two males, where the obligation definitely exists and the only question is its identity, here there may be no obligation whatsoever. If the girl emerged first, she is the *bechor* and there is no *pidyon haben* obligation at all. The very existence of the debt is in question, not merely its assignment. Once again we observe how thoroughly the *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben* is intertwined with and dependent upon the underlying civil liability of the five *sela'im*.

## Father's Obligation Timi…

### Post-Day-30 Death Scenar…

The *gemara* opens by interrogating the timing of the father's death: *de'meis ha'av eimas* — when exactly did he die? The *gemara* proposes: *ileima de'meis achar sheloshim yom* — suppose he died after thirty days. By that point the obligation had already crystallized: on day thirty or shortly thereafter, the father became obligated in the five *sela'im*, and presumably a *shi'bud* — a lien — had already attached to his estate. The father then dies. Should it not follow that the *kohen* can collect from the estate regardless of whether the sons subsequently divide it?

Consider the analogy: if someone borrowed a hundred dollars from me and then died, and his sons divided the estate, my *shi'bud* persists. I can collect from either son's portion. The division of the estate does not dissolve a pre-existing lien. So if the father became obligated at day thirty, why does Rebbi Meir say division matters? *Ve'ishtabdu lehu nechasim* — the estate was already encumbered!

This leads the *gemara* to the alternative scenario: *ela de'meis betoch sheloshim yom* — rather, the father died within thirty days, before the obligation ever fell on him. In that case, the obligation arises for the first time with the sons themselves. There never was a lien on the father's estate because he was never obligated. If so, says the *gemara*, why should the pre-division state of the estate make any difference? Even before division, there is no lien: *de'azil legabei hai umadchei leh ude'azil legabei hai umadchei leh* — the *kohen* goes to one brother, is turned away, goes to the other, is turned away again. There is no lien because the obligation first came to rest on the sons and no individual son can be held. And therefore: *ki lo chelku nami* — even without dividing the estate, why should the *kohen* be able to collect? Each brother could push him off regardless.

### Liens Through Persons On…

This leads to a critical *gemara* principle, articulated by Rava in his rejection of the proposed reading. The principle is stated thus: *nichsei devar inish inun arevin beh* — a person's property stands as guarantor for him. This sounds like it empowers the creditor to go directly to the property. But Rava reads it in precisely the opposite direction: the property is the guarantor *for the person*, meaning the lien can only be exercised *through* the person's individual obligation. One cannot bypass the obligated individual and go directly to encumbered property. The property is not an independent source of liability; it is a conduit for the person's personal debt.

This is a counterintuitive principle, certainly by the standards of secular commercial law. In *halakha*, the paradigm is that you cannot proceed against a guarantor — an *arev* — without first establishing the claim against the principal debtor. The same logic governs liens: I cannot declare that I have a lien on this property and proceed to collect without first being able to name and confront the specific person who owes me the money. The *shi'bud* is not free-floating; it is tethered to the interpersonal obligation. And the ruling — *de-lo yidpeih min ha-arev techilah*, that you may not go directly to the guarantor first — is the accepted *halakha*. So too, if I cannot identify the obligated person and bring him before a court, I cannot exercise the lien.

### Two Yosef ben Shimons Pa…

Before Rava's rejection is explained, the *gemara* first floats a possible reading of the *mishnah* that would validate collecting from an undivided estate even where only the sons are obligated. Rabbi Yirmiya proposes: *zot omeret* — from the fact that collection is permitted pre-division, one can derive a principle through the case of two people in the same city who share the name Yosef ben Shimon.

The scenario is this: I have a promissory note stating that Yosef ben Shimon owes me money. When I attempt to collect, I discover — or he informs me — that there is another Yosef ben Shimon in town. Each claims the debt belongs to the other, and I cannot prove otherwise. Ordinarily, I would be unable to collect from either. But suppose these two Yosef ben Shimons were unwise enough to purchase a field together in partnership. Now I have an opening. *Ba'al chov goveh mehem* — the creditor may collect from the shared property. The reasoning: *de'amar leh ei bedidach masikna, manta didach kashakilna* — if you are the one who owes me money, I am taking your share; *ve'ei bechaverach masikna, manta dechaverach kashakilna* — and if it is the other one, I am taking his share. The two partners can then sort out among themselves who must compensate whom. The creditor knows that whoever holds the debt is a part-owner of this property, and so he collects from the property directly, leaving the internal accounting to the debtors.

This, Rabbi Yirmiya suggests, is precisely the structure of the *mishnah* before the estate is divided: the brothers are equal partners, one of them is definitely obligated, and the *kohen* can collect from the shared estate using the same logic. This would explain why Rebbi Meir allows collection pre-division even where the obligation falls on the sons, not the father.

### Loan Sale & Waiver Examp…

Rava, however, rejects this reading and insists that the lien-through-person principle forecloses the Yosef ben Shimon solution. To illustrate the depth of this principle, consider the following: if one person owes another money, the creditor can sell that loan to a third party. The sale is effectively a sale of the creditor's lien on the debtor's estate — that is the concrete property right being transferred. But suppose the original creditor, after selling the loan, then proceeds to be *mochel* — to waive — the underlying debt to the original debtor. The third-party purchaser of the loan is left with nothing. Why? Because the lien does not exist independently of the interpersonal obligation. The lien operates only through the creditor's personal claim against the debtor, and that is a relationship that is uniquely between those two parties. Once the original creditor dissolves that personal obligation — even if someone else has nominally purchased the right to collect — the lien collapses, because it was never anything other than an expression of that personal debt. There is no free-standing property right that persists once the underlying obligation is extinguished.

This is a striking feature of *halakhic* commercial law with significant practical implications, and the *gemara* acknowledges that workarounds must be constructed. But the baseline rule stands: you cannot go straight to the property, you must go through the person, and if you cannot identify or confront that person, you are stuck. The Yosef ben Shimon scenario therefore cannot serve as a model for our *mishnah*.

## Small Estate & Division

### Rava's Resolution

Having rejected the reading in which the father dies within thirty days, the *gemara* — through Rava — now establishes the correct framing of the *mishnah*: *le'olam she-meis achar sheloshim yom* — in fact, the father died after thirty days. The obligation therefore attached to the father, a lien came into existence on his estate, and the father subsequently died leaving two sons. Now the *kohen's* right to collect is clearly grounded: there was an enforceable debt with a lien, and it runs against the estate. The question Rebbi Meir is addressing is therefore not whether a lien exists — it does — but what happens to that lien when the estate is divided.

### Small Estate—Five Sela'i…

If the estate is large — *ve'i ika nechasim tuva* — then of course the *kohen* simply collects the five *sela'im* via the lien; there is no difficulty. *Ve'hacha be-mai askinan* — what case is the *mishnah* actually discussing? *Kegon d'leika ela chamesh sela'im* — the entire estate is worth exactly five *sela'im*. The father owed five *sela'im*, the estate is worth five *sela'im*, and the *kohen* has a lien on it. Before division, the *kohen* can take the entire estate. But what happens when the sons divide it and each brother walks away with two and a half *sela'im* worth of property?

### Half-Heir, Half-Purchaser

At this point the *gemara* invokes the ruling of Rav Assi: *u've-chulei alma is le-hu de-Rav Assi* — everyone in this debate operates within the framework of Rav Assi's principle. Rav Assi holds: *ha-achin she-chalku, mechtza yorshin u-mechtza lekuchot* — brothers who divide an estate are considered half heirs and half purchasers. That is, when two brothers split shared property — say, the north half goes to one and the south half to the other — the portion each receives is not fully his by right of inheritance. Half of it is treated as his natural inheritance, and the other half is treated as if he purchased it from his brother. This distinction matters enormously for what a creditor can collect and from whom.

The question of whether division retroactively defines each brother's share as always having been his — the issue of *yesh bereira* — bears on this, though Rav Assi's resolution is the operative one: split the difference, half and half.

### Torah Debt vs. Written L…

The *gemara* then layers on a second shared assumption: *u've-chulei alma, milveh ha-ketuvah ba-Torah lav ke-chetuvah bi-shtar damya* — everyone agrees that a debt mandated by the Torah does not carry the status of a debt written in a formal document, a *shtar*. This requires explanation. When a person borrows money and the transaction is memorialized in a *shtar*, that document creates a *shi'bud* — a lien — on all of the borrower's real property. A creditor holding a *shtar* can collect not only from the borrower directly but from property the borrower subsequently sold, because the lien attached at the moment the document was executed.

But what about an obligation that arises not from a contractual document but from the Torah itself — such as a tortfeasor's obligation to pay for damages, or a father's obligation to redeem his firstborn son? Is the fact that the Torah mandates this payment equivalent to a written *shtar*, generating a lien on real property? The *gemara* assumes that everyone in this dispute holds: no, it is not equivalent. A Torah-mandated obligation, without an accompanying document, does not by itself create a lien on property.

### Rav Pappa on Heirs & Buy…

The third shared assumption comes from Rav Pappa: *milveh al peh goveh min ha-yorshin ve-einah goveh min ha-lekuchot* — a verbal loan, not memorialized in a *shtar*, may be collected from the borrower's heirs but not from purchasers of his property. The logic is that heirs stand in the place of their father — they are a continuation of him, and so the personal obligation runs through them. But since there is no *shtar* creating a lien on the property, a third-party purchaser who acquired the property takes it free and clear of the creditor's claim.

Now line up all three assumptions. The father owed five *sela'im* as a Torah-mandated obligation — which, per assumption two, is not equivalent to a *shtar*. He died. The obligation can pass to his sons as heirs — per Rav Pappa, even without a *shtar*, heirs are collectible from. But now the sons divide the estate, and per Rav Assi, half of what each son holds is treated as if he purchased it from his brother, not as inheritance. Since there is no *shtar*, there is no lien on the purchased portion. Therefore, the *kohen* can only collect from each son's half-as-heir — that is, half of each son's two and a half *sela'im*, yielding only half of the total five *sela'im*.

## Full Five vs. Half Five

### Rebbi Meir: Five Only

With this framework in place, the *gemara* now articulates the actual dispute. *Uv'ha b'chamesh v'lo b'chatzi chamesh kamiflagi* — they are debating the phrase *chamesh v'lo b'chatzi chamesh*: five, and not half of five. Rebbi Meir holds that the five *sela'im* must be collectible as a unit. This reflects not merely a quantitative requirement but a formal, ritual dimension of the *pidyon haben* payment: the five *sela'im* function as an indivisible unit of redemption. If the only amount collectible from the divided estate is two and a half *sela'im* — half of five — then that is insufficient, and no payment at all is due.

*Tosafot* notes that Rebbi Meir's position does not necessarily mean a father in ordinary circumstances must hand over exactly five *sela'im* in a single transaction. If a father wishes to pay in installments, or to distribute the five *sela'im* among different *kohanim*, that may be permissible, since in those scenarios there exists an underlying enforceable debt of five *sela'im*. The issue here is that the total collectible amount, after applying all three assumptions, is only two and a half — a debt of that magnitude never existed. And since the civil liability never amounted to five, the *mitzvah* cannot be fulfilled.

### Rebbi Yehuda: Half Suffi…

*Rebbi Yehuda* appears to say the opposite: *chamesh v'afillu chatzi chamesh* — five, and even half of five suffices. The property is obligated — *nitchayvu nechasim* — even post-division, and one may collect whatever is available.

But this framing immediately raises a difficulty in the *gemara*.

### Baraita: Twenty Zuz Requ…

The *gemara* challenges the reading of Rebbi Yehuda from a *baraita*: *Rebbi Yehuda omer ha-achim she-chalku* — Rebbi Yehuda says that when the brothers have divided the estate, *im yesh asara zuz l'zeh v'asara zuz l'zeh, chayavin* — if each has ten *zuz* (recall that a *sela* is four *zuz*, so ten *zuz* is two and a half *sela'im*, i.e., half of the five), they are obligated. *V'im lav, peturim* — if not, they are exempt.

At first this seems to confirm that Rebbi Yehuda allows collection of half the redemption amount — ten *zuz* from each, twenty total, equals five *sela'im* spread across two brothers each paying half. But the *gemara* presses: if Rebbi Yehuda truly permits collecting less than the full five *sela'im*, why set the threshold at ten *zuz* from each? *Afillu pachot mi-asara nami* — even less than ten *zuz* should suffice, since he has abandoned the requirement of a full five. The fact that he specifies a minimum of ten from each means he requires a total of twenty *zuz* — five full *sela'im* — to be collectible. *Alma chamesh v'lo chatzi chamesh sevira lei* — it is clear, then, that even Rebbi Yehuda demands the full five.

### Full Five: Hidden Debate…

The *gemara* now reformulates the dispute cleanly: *ela d'chulei alma chamesh v'lo chatzi chamesh* — in fact, both Rebbi Meir and Rebbi Yehuda agree that the full five *sela'im* must be collectable. The debate is not about whether half suffices. Rather: *uv'ha b'Rav Assi u'Rav Pappa kamiflagi* — they are disputing the applications of Rav Assi's ruling and Rav Pappa's ruling, which together determine whether the full five *sela'im* can in fact be collected from the divided estate.

### Rebbi Yehuda's Hidden As…

Rebbi Meir accepts all three assumptions laid out above — no lien from a Torah obligation, heirs collectible but purchasers not, and post-division each brother is half heir and half purchaser — and therefore concludes that only half of five is collectible per brother, which means the full five cannot be extracted, which means the *kohen* gets nothing.

Rebbi Yehuda, who insists the property *is* obligated — *nitchayvu nechasim* — and collection is possible, must therefore reject at least one of those assumptions. Either he holds that a Torah-mandated obligation *does* create a lien equivalent to a *shtar*, in which case the purchased half of each brother's share is also encumbered and the full five can be extracted; or he rejects Rav Assi's half-and-half principle and holds that after division each brother is considered a full heir to his portion, again allowing collection of the full amount. By rejecting whichever assumption he rejects, Rebbi Yehuda is able to say that a full lien — *nishta'abdu nechasim* — persists on the entire estate even after division, and the *kohen* may collect the complete five *sela'im*.

## Alt Sugya via R. Yehuda

### Textual Variant: Rebbi Y…

The *gemara* notes: *ve'ika de'matni la a'seifa* — some versions of this sugya are taught starting not from Rebbi Meir's clause in the *mishnah* but from Rebbi Yehuda's. The analytical content is substantially the same, but the entry point differs. Instead of beginning with Rebbi Meir's statement that division renders the sons exempt and then asking why, the alternative version takes Rebbi Yehuda's statement — that the property becomes obligated, *nitchayvu nechasim* — as its starting point and interrogates that.

### Two Yosef—Purchase Eleme…

Starting from Rebbi Yehuda: when did the father die? If after thirty days — *ilei d'mita achar shloshim* — then the lien exists on the estate and of course the *kohen* can collect; that is unremarkable. And if after division, Rebbi Meir would presumably say the sons are exempt — but why? *Nishta'abdu nechasai!* The lien already existed! So the father must have died within thirty days — *ela betoch shloshim* — meaning the obligation first arose with the sons themselves.

But then: *ki silku amai michayeiv Rebbi Yehuda* — if they have divided the estate and there was never a lien from the father, how can Rebbi Yehuda say they are obligated? *Leizeil legabei hai u'lidchiyei u'legabei hai u'lidchiyei* — the *kohen* goes to one, is pushed off, goes to the other, is pushed off. How does Rebbi Yehuda justify collection?

*Amar Rebbi Yochanan: zot omeret* — Rabbi Yochanan says this implies the Yosef ben Shimon principle. In this version, however, the case is slightly different: *velakach echad mehen sadeh mechaveiro* — one of the brothers purchased a field from the other. Not simply a shared purchase as in the earlier version, but an actual sale between them. The creditor — the *kohen* — now approaches the purchasing brother and says: *ei de'didach maseikna, manta didach shakilna* — if you owe me the money, I take this field from you; *ve'ei de'chaverach maseikna, mesha'abda lei kamai d'idach* — and if your brother owes me, I have a lien that I can exercise against property he sold to you. Either way, I collect from this field. You two sort it out internally.

This is the mechanism by which Rebbi Yehuda, starting from the within-thirty-days scenario, could justify collection after division: because division functions like a purchase, each brother may find himself holding property either directly owed by him or subject to a lien through his brother's obligation.

### Rava Rejects Direct Prop…

Rava, however, rejects this analysis as well. *Michtei nechezei, nichsei de'var inish inun arvun beih* — the same principle applies: you may only approach the property through the person. Since the obligation cannot be assigned to any particular individual — each brother deflects it to the other — the creditor has no mechanism by which to exercise a lien, regardless of what passed between the brothers in dividing the estate. The lien, as always, can only be exercised through the personal obligation of a specific, identified debtor. *Ve'chol k'lishna kama* — and the rest of the sugya proceeds exactly as in the first version.

### Ritual Hinges on Civil L…

The conclusion, in both versions of the sugya, is that the correct reading of the *mishnah* requires the father to have died after thirty days, giving rise to a genuine lien on the estate. The subsequent dispute between Rebbi Meir and Rebbi Yehuda concerns whether that lien survives division in a form sufficient to collect the entire five *sela'im*, with the answer turning on which of the layered assumptions — Rav Assi's half-heir/half-purchaser principle, the non-equivalence of a Torah obligation to a *shtar*, and Rav Pappa's rule about heirs versus purchasers — one accepts or rejects.

The deeper conceptual point the *gemara* has established is this: the ritual obligation of *pidyon haben* is not free-standing. It derives from, and is entirely conditioned by, the civil obligation of the five *sela'im*. If the civil debt cannot be enforced — whether because no individual can be identified as the debtor, or because the lien cannot be exercised after division — then the *mitzvah* itself is unenforceable. At the same time, the obligation is not purely civil either: even Rebbi Yehuda, who insists that what is collectible may be collected, requires the full five *sela'im* to be available. A civil debt of two and a half *sela'im* would still be a debt and would still be owed, but the *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben* would not be triggered. The ritual framing imposes its own formal requirement — *chamesh v'lo chatzi chamesh* — even as its very existence depends on the underlying civil liability being enforceable.
