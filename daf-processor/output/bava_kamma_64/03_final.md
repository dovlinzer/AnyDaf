# Bava Kamma 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Deriving Kefel from Klal

### Opening Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵאָמֵר ״שׁוֹר״ וּ״גְנֵיבָה״ – וְהַכֹּל בַּכְּלָל! אִילּוּ כָּךְ, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, אַף כֹּל קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Let** the verse **state** only **"ox" and "theft,"** and **all** items **would be included.** Why was it necessary to also mention "donkey" and "sheep"? **Had** the verse been written in **this** manner **I would have said: Just as** the item mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined as** an item that **is sacrificed on the altar, so too everything that is sacrificed on the altar** is subject to double payment, but other items are not.

The discussion on daf 64 concludes the series of *drashot* that opened *Meruba* — spanning the first several *dappim* — concerning how we know that *kefel* applies to all objects of theft. The *Gemara* has been working through two distinct passages in the *Torah*, each of which provides independent grounding for the law of double payment.

### Two Passages

The first passage is the direct case of *gneiva*: *ki yignov ish shor o seh utvacho o mecharo chamisha vakar yeshalem tachat hashor ve'arba tzon tachat haseh* — if a man steals an ox or a sheep and slaughters or sells it, he pays five times for the ox and four times for the sheep. This is the primary *pasuk* establishing the *chiyuv* of a *ganav* and the payments of four and five. The *pasuk* continues: *im himatzei timatzei beyado hagneiva mishor ad chamor ad seh chayim shnayim yeshalem* — if the stolen animal is found alive in his possession, from ox to donkey to sheep, he pays double. This establishes both *kefel* for the case where the animal was not slaughtered or sold, and introduces the phrase *chayim* — alive — alongside the list *shor*, *chamor*, *seh*.

The second passage arises in the context of a *shomer*. If an object was entrusted to a watchman and stolen, and the *ganav* is found, he pays double — a reference back to the general principle. If the *ganav* is not found and the *shomer* takes an oath and then is later discovered to have claimed falsely that the object was stolen, he too pays double. The preceding *dappim* focused primarily on this second passage to establish the scope of *kefel* across different categories of objects. Now the *Gemara* returns to the first passage — *mishor ad chamor ad seh chayim* — to derive the same scope from there.

### Why List Animals?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה יֵשׁ לְךָ לְהָבִיא – שֶׂה, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״שֶׂה״ – הֲרֵי שֶׂה אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״גְּנֵיבָה״? לְרַבּוֹת כָּל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **What** else **is there for you to include** in this category? **Sheep,** which, like oxen, can be sacrificed on the altar. **When** the verse **states "sheep," a sheep is thereby stated** explicitly. Therefore, the detail of "ox" cannot be written to include specifically items that can be sacrificed on the altar, e.g., a sheep. **How** then **do I realize** the meaning of the word **"theft,"** i.e., what is the category of items derived from the detail and generalization? It must be that it serves **to include all** types of **items** in the requirement of double payment.

A natural question arises: why does the *pasuk* enumerate *shor*, *chamor*, and *seh*, and why does it choose the formulation *mishor ad chamor ad seh chayim*? The purpose of this list, as the *Gemara* will establish, is to enable an expansion through the hermeneutical structure of *klal u-prat u-klal* — or alternatively, as a *ribui* — to extend the liability of *kefel* beyond these specific animals to all movable property with intrinsic value, while simultaneously excluding *karka*, *shtarot*, and other categories.

### Klal U-Prat Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: יֵאָמֵר ״שׁוֹר״ וּ״גְנֵיבָה״. מִי כְּתִיב ״שׁוֹר״ וּ״גְנֵיבָה״? ״גְּנֵיבָה״ וָ״שׁוֹר״ הוּא דִּכְתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita* above: **Let** the verse **state** only **"ox" and "theft."** The Gemara objects: **Are "ox" and "theft" written** in the verse in this order, as a generalization and a detail? No, **it is "theft" and** then **"ox" that is written.** Consequently, the verse contains a generalization and a detail, in which case the generalization is understood to refer only to that which is specified in the detail.

The *Gemara* opens with a pointed question from a *braita*: why not simply say *shor* and *gneiva*, using just those two terms to form a *klal u-prat* structure? The *Gemara* immediately clarifies: the *pasuk* actually reads *hagneiva mishor* — *gneiva* precedes *shor*. This order matters considerably. If the broader term *gneiva* appeared before the specific term *shor*, that would constitute a *klal u-prat*, limiting liability to the *prat* alone — only an ox. If *shor* appeared first and *gneiva* second, with the broader term at the end, one might read it as a *prat u-klal*, which would expand to include everything.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא ״אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר״ קָאָמַר – אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״שׁוֹר״ וּ״גְנֵיבָה״; וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ ״מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ״?! הָוֵה ״שׁוֹר״ פְּרָט וּ״גְנֵיבָה״ כְּלָל; פְּרָט וּכְלָל – נַעֲשָׂה כְּלָל מוֹסִיף עַל הַפְּרָט, וְאִיתְרַבּוּ לְהוּ כֹּל מִילֵּי!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that the *baraita* **is saying** that this would be the case **had** the verse **stated** the words in this manner, i.e.: **Had** the verse **stated "ox" and** then **"theft,"** all items would have been included; **how can you** then proceed to **say: Just as** the item mentioned in **the detail,** "ox," is clearly **defined as** an item that can be sacrificed on the altar, so too everything that can be sacrificed on the altar is subject to double payment? **"Ox" is a detail and "theft" is a generalization,** and according to the rules of hermeneutics, in such a case **the generalization adds to the detail, and all items are included,** not just items that resemble the detailed item.

The *Gemara* then considers the *braita*'s hypothetical: *ilu ne'emar shor ve-gneiva* — had it been written with *shor* first and then *gneiva*, which would make *gneiva* the closing *klal*. The *Gemara* asks: would that not have served as a *klal prat u-klal*, expanding the scope? No, the *Gemara* responds — if you have only two terms, you either have a *klal u-prat*, which limits to the *prat*, or a *prat u-klal*, which expands to everything without limit. The intermediate structure of *klal prat u-klal* requires three elements, not two. The *braita*'s argument assumes the existence of such a three-part structure somewhere in the *pasuk*, and the challenge to the *braita* is: where is the second *klal* that would allow that structure to operate?

### Rav's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תַּנָּא אַ״חַיִּים״ קָא סָמֵיךְ לֵיהּ, וּכְלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava stated** an explanation of the *baraita*: When **the *tanna*** suggested: Let the verse say "ox" and "theft," and similar suggestions, **he was relying on** the word **"alive"** at the end of the phrase. He considered this word an additional generalization after the detailed items, and therefore **he was saying** that the phrase is to be treated as **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization.** Consequently, it should have been sufficient for the verse to state merely "theft," followed by "ox," followed by "alive." Why does the Torah specify further items?

The *Gemara* works toward identifying the second *klal*. One candidate is the word *chayim* — living things — at the end of the *pasuk*. Another candidate is *shnayim yeshalem*, which carries a broadly inclusive connotation. The *Gemara* ultimately presents the answer in the name of Rava: *tanna hachayim kasameich* — the *tanna* relies on the word *chayim* to serve as the final *klal*. The structure is thus: *gneiva* is the opening *klal*, the listed animals are the *pratim*, and *chayim* is the closing *klal* — a complete *klal u-prat u-klal*.

### Chayim as Klal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לָא דָּמֵי כְּלָלָא בָּתְרָא לִכְלָלָא קַמָּא! הָא תַּנָּא – דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הוּא, דִּכְהַאי גַּוְונָא דָּרֵישׁ כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **But the latter generalization is not similar to the first generalization.** The first generalization, "theft," is so general that it includes all items, whereas the latter generalization, "alive," includes only animals. Consequently, the rule of a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization should not apply. The Gemara responds: **This *tanna*,** the author of this *baraita*, **is of the school of Rabbi Yishmael, who expounds generalizations and details in a case like this.** Even if the first generalization and the latter generalization are dissimilar, he interprets the verse as a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization.

The *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty with this construction. In a standard *klal prat u-klal*, the final *klal* should be at least as broad as the opening *klal*, or the expansion will be understood as limited to things similar to the *prat*. Here, the opening *klal* — *gneiva* — is maximally broad, encompassing any theft. The final *klal* — *chayim* — is explicitly narrower, referring only to living creatures. This asymmetry seems to undermine the structure.

The *Gemara* answers: *ha tanna de-vei Rabbi Yishmael hu* — this is the school of Rabbi Yishmael, which holds that even where the final *klal* is smaller than the opening *klal*, the *klal prat u-klal* structure still operates as an expansion. The hermeneutical assumption of this school is that two *klalim* bounding *pratim* always broaden the scope beyond the literal *pratim*, regardless of whether the two *klalim* are identical in scope.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ: ״אִם הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא״ לְמָה לִי? יֵאָמֵר ״שׁוֹר״ וּ״גְנֵיבָה״ וְ״חַיִּים״ – וְהַכֹּל בַּכְּלָל!
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues his explanation of the *baraita*: **And this is what** the *tanna* found **difficult** at the end of the *baraita*: **Why do I** need the beginning of the verse to state: **"If** the theft **shall be found [*himmatze timmatze*],"** with the verb repeated? Rava now explains the entire *baraita*, from the beginning: **Let** the verse **say "ox" and "theft" and "alive," and all** items would be **included,** in accordance with the rules of a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization. Why did the verse include other details?

The *Gemara* then reconstructs the *braita*'s internal question and answer. The *braita* asks: why is *himatzei timatzei* needed? If we already have *gneiva*, *shor*, *chamor*, *seh*, and *chayim* constituting a *klal prat u-klal*, why do we need the additional phrase *himatzei timatzei*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילּוּ כֵּן, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר הַקָּרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, אַף כֹּל הַקָּרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ. מָה יֵשׁ לְהָבִיא – ״שֶׂה״, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״שֶׂה״ – הֲרֵי שֶׂה אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״גְּנֵיבָה״? לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers this by saying: **Had** the verse been written in **this** manner, **I would have said: Just as** the item mentioned in **the detail,** "ox," is clearly **defined as an item that is sacrificed on the altar, so too everything that is sacrificed on the altar** is subject to double payment, but not other items. **What** else **is there** for you **to include** in this category? **A sheep. When** the verse **states "sheep," a sheep is thereby stated** explicitly. Therefore, the detail of "ox" cannot be written to include specifically items that can be sacrificed on the altar, e.g., a sheep. **How** then **do I realize** the meaning of the word **"theft,"** i.e., what is the category of items derived from the detail and generalization? It must be that it serves **to include all** types of **items** in the requirement of double payment.

The *Gemara* traces the incremental logic of the *braita*. Had the *pasuk* said only *gneiva* and *shor*, with *chayim* as the *klal*, the *prat* — *shor* — would suggest limiting liability to animals offered as sacrifices, *davar ha-karev gabbei mizbe'ach*. The explicit addition of *seh* then expands beyond that. Had the text said *gneiva*, *shor*, and *seh*, I would have expanded to things sanctified by the firstborn, *davar ha-kadosh bivechora* — but not more. The addition of *chamor* then expands again. With *shor*, *seh*, and *chamor* all present as *pratim*, I would have abstracted to *baalei chayim* — animals generally — but not beyond. Thus even with *chayim* functioning as a *klal*, the scope would have remained confined to living creatures. It is precisely here that *himatzei timatzei* is needed — to push beyond animals entirely.

### Two Kelalim Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: וְהָא שְׁנֵי כְלָלוֹת דִּסְמִיכִי אַהֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ! אָמַר רָבִינָא, כִּדְאָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא שְׁנֵי כְלָלוֹת הַסְּמוּכִים זֶה לָזֶה – הַטֵּל פְּרָט בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְדוּנֵם בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **state** an objection: **But these** repeated verbs **are two generalizations that are adjacent to each other** in the verse, and the details follow both of the generalizations. Consequently, it is not a case of a generalization, a detail, and a generalization, but rather of a generalization, a generalization, and a detail. **Ravina said:** It is **as they say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: **Any place** in the Torah **where you find two generalizations adjacent to each other, place the detail between them and** then **treat them as a generalization, and a detail,** and a generalization.

The *Gemara* now observes that *himatzei timatzei* is itself a doubled formulation — two words, each carrying the force of a *klal*. This raises a further question: *shnei klalos smichi ahadadi ninhu* — two *klalim* standing adjacent to each other do not function like a normal *klal prat u-klal*. They are simply two broad terms sitting together.

Ravina answers in the name of the sages of *Eretz Yisrael*: *kol makom she'atah motzei shnei klalos hasmouchin zeh lazeh hatel prat beineihem* — wherever you find two *klalim* adjacent to one another, insert the *pratim* between them and treat the result as a *klal prat u-klal*. The two words *himatzei* and *timatzei* function as the two flanking *klalim*, and the listed animals serve as the *pratim* that stand between them.

### Placing Shor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁדִי ״שׁוֹר״ בֵּין ״הִמָּצֵא״ לְ״תִמָּצֵא״ – לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אִי לְאֵתוֹיֵי בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים, מֵ״חַיִּים״ נָפְקָא! אֶלָּא לְאֵתוֹיֵי דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים, וּדְרוֹשׁ הָכִי: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר הַמִּטַּלְטֵל וְגוּפוֹ מָמוֹן, אַף כֹּל דָּבָר הַמִּטַּלְטֵל וְגוּפוֹ מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara applies this principle to the verse: **Place** the first detail, **"ox," between "*himmatze*" and "*timmatze*,"** and treat the verse as a generalization, a detail, and a generalization, so that other items that resemble the detail are included. **To include what? If** it serves **to include animals,** this is not necessary, as animals **are derived from** the word **"alive"** later in the verse. **Rather,** it serves **to include an item that is not an animal,** i.e., inanimate objects, **and this is how you should expound** the verse: **Just as** the items mentioned in **the detail** are clearly **defined as movable property and** are items that have **intrinsic monetary** value, **so too anything that is movable property and has intrinsic monetary** value is included, to the exclusion of land and financial documents.

The *Gemara* now works through each *prat* in turn, asking what work each one does when inserted between *himatzei* and *timatzei*. *Shadi shor bein himatzei letimatzei* — insert *shor* between the two *klalim*. What does this include? It cannot be coming to include *baalei chayim* — animals — since that is already derived from the explicit word *chayim*. Rather, it must be including something that is not a living creature. The *Gemara* therefore derives from *shor* the category of *davar hamitaltel v'gufo mammon* — movable property with intrinsic monetary value.

### Level of Abstraction

This formulation accomplishes the core work of exclusion as well. Land — *karka* — is immovable. Slaves — *avadim* — are legally equated with land. Documents — *shtarot* — have no intrinsic monetary value; their worth is representational. All three are thereby excluded.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ שְׁדִי ״חֲמוֹר״ בֵּין ״הִמָּצֵא״ לְ״תִמָּצֵא״ – לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אִי לְאֵתוֹיֵי דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים, מִשּׁוֹר נָפְקָא! אֶלָּא לְאֵתוֹיֵי דָּבָר מְסוּיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** following this method, **place** the next detail, **"donkey," between *himmatze* and *timmatze*,** and treat it as a case of a generalization, a detail, and a generalization, so that other items that resemble the detail are included. **To include what? If** it serves **to include an item that is not an animal,** this is not necessary, as it already was **derived from** the word **"ox." Rather,** it serves **to include an object that is** clearly **delineated,** to the exclusion of objects that are not clearly delineated in size or quantity.

The *Gemara* continues inserting *chamor* between the two *klalim*. Since movable property with intrinsic value is already covered by *shor*, *chamor* must be coming to include something further — *davar mesuyam*, a defined or specified thing. The precise meaning of *davar mesuyam* is somewhat obscure; it was discussed at length in the preceding *daf*, and the conclusion there was that its exact scope remains unclear. The *Gemara* continues similarly with *seh*, but runs into difficulty: after *shor* and *chamor* have done their work, it becomes hard to identify what further expansion *seh* could be adding. This difficulty motivates the *Gemara*'s pivot to an alternative interpretive framework.

## Exclusions & Ribui

### Ribui vs Klal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, ״שֶׂה״ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט וְרִיבָּה הוּא, כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** if this is the correct way to analyze the verse, **why do I** need the verse to state **"sheep"?** There is nothing left to include. **Rather,** this whole approach must be rejected, and a new method of analysis applied, as follows: It is not treated as a case of a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization; rather, **it is** treated as a verse that **amplified, restricted, and amplified, as the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught.**

At this point the *Gemara* introduces an alternative approach associated with *tanna de-vei Rabbi Yishmael*: instead of reading *himatzei timatzei* as a *klal prat u-klal* structure, read it as *ribui u-mi'ut v'ribui* — expansion, limitation, expansion. This framework operates differently from *klal prat u-klal*. Where *klal prat u-klal* begins by asking what the *pratim* tell us and then expanding from them, *ribui mi'ut v'ribui* begins from the assumption that everything is included, and uses the *pratim* as exclusions rather than as templates for expansion. Under this reading, *himatzei timatzei* constitutes a *ribui* — a broadly inclusive statement — and the listed animals serve as *mi'utim*, each one excluding a category.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״בַּמַּיִם״ ״בַּמַּיִם״ שְׁתֵּי פְּעָמִים – אֵין זֶה כְּלָל וּפְרָט, אֶלָּא רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט וְרִיבָּה; רִיבָּה הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught,** concerning the verse: "These may you eat of all that are in the waters: Whatever has fins and scales in the waters, in the seas, and in the rivers" (Leviticus 11:9), as follows: The verse states: **"In the waters," "in the waters," two times** and only afterward lists the details of seas and rivers. **This is not** expounded as **a generalization, and a detail,** and a generalization, because the detail does not appear between the generalizations. **Rather,** the verse **amplified, and** then **restricted, and** then **amplified,** and thereby **amplified** the general category "waters" to include **everything** except for "seas" and "rivers," the specific matter excluded in the restriction. Here too, since the two generalizations are written before the details, it is expounded as an amplification, a restriction and an amplification.

### Three Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי – רַבִּי כֹּל מִילֵּי? אִי הָכִי, כֹּל הָנֵי פְּרָטֵי לְמָה לִי? חַד לְמַעוֹטֵי קַרְקַע, וְחַד לְמַעוֹטֵי עֲבָדִים, וְחַד לְמַעוֹטֵי שְׁטָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **What do** the generalizations *himmatze* and *timmatze* **include?** Do they **include all items? If so, why do I** need **all these details** of ox, sheep, and donkey? **One** detail serves **to exclude land, and one** serves **to exclude** Canaanite **slaves, and one** serves **to exclude** financial **documents.**

Under the *ribui* approach, one begins with the universe of all objects potentially subject to *kefel*, and then asks what the listed *pratim* exclude. Each of the three explicit animals in the list — *shor*, *chamor*, and *seh* — is understood to exclude one category: *chad l'mi'utei karka* — one excludes land; *chad l'mi'utei avadim* — one excludes slaves; *ve'chad l'mi'utei shtarot* — one excludes documents. The result is identical in practical terms to the *klal prat u-klal* approach: all movable property with intrinsic value is subject to *kefel*, while land, slaves, and documents are exempt.

### Restore to Original

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״גְּנֵיבָה״ וְ״חַיִּים״ – לְכִדְרַב, דְּאָמַר: אַחֲיַיהּ לְקֶרֶן כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב.
>
> **Translation:** The words **"theft" and "alive [*ḥayyim*]"** do not serve to exclude further items from double payment, but **to** indicate the *halakha* taught by **Rav, who says:** If one stole an item, and it deteriorated or its value decreased, he must **revive [*aḥayah*] the principal** to the value it had **as** of the time **when he stole** it, i.e., he must repay the owner according to the value of the item at the time it was stolen.

That still leaves two words unused: *gneiva* and *chayim*. Under the *ribui* framework, these are no longer functioning as *klalim*. The *Gemara* invokes Rav's teaching to give them work: *d'amar Rav achyia l'keren k'ein sheganav* — restore the principal to the condition it was in at the time of the theft. The word *gneiva* teaches that the object must be restored as it was when stolen, and *chayim* teaches that it must be restored as a living thing — that is, in its living state as of the moment it was taken. Both words together express the principle that the thief's obligation with respect to the principal is calibrated to the moment of the theft, not any later point.

The principle of *achyia l'keren k'ein sheganav* — restoring the principal to the state it was in when stolen — is thus extracted from these two remaining words under the *ribui* interpretation. If the animal was in a certain condition when taken, the thief bears responsibility to restore it to that condition. The implications of this principle — particularly how it interacts with changes in the animal's condition or market value between the time of theft and the time of adjudication — will be taken up in detail in the final section of the *daf*.

## Ganav vs To'ein

### Option 1: Two Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַד בְּגַנָּב וְחַד בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, וְגַנָּב עַצְמוֹ נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״אִם יִמָּצֵא הַגַּנָּב״, הַאי ״אִם הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § Until this point, the Gemara has been discussing the source of the obligation of a thief to pay double payment according to the *baraita* (63b) that maintains that both verses (Exodus 22:6–7) are referring to a bailee who falsely claims that a deposit was stolen from him. The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that **one** of the two verses **is** speaking **about a thief and one** verse **is** speaking **about** a bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole from him, **and he derives** that **a thief himself** must pay double payment **from** the phrase **"if the thief shall be found"** (Exodus 22:6), **what does he derive from this** verse: **"If** the theft **shall be found"** (Exodus 22:3)?

The *Gemara* returns to a question that has been present throughout the discussion: how to read the relationship between the two halves of the passage about the *shomer*. One possibility is that one clause addresses the *ganav* himself — the actual thief who pays *kefel* — and the other addresses the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, the *shomer* who falsely claims that the object was stolen and thereby incurs *kefel* liability. On this reading, the two clauses divide the case between them.

### Option 2: Both To'ein

The alternative possibility is that both clauses concern the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, and that *himatzei timatzei* derives the scope of liability — that is, the categories of objects for which *kefel* applies — specifically in the context of a *ganav* himself, not the *shomer*. The *Gemara* explores what each view entails for the derivation of the rule of *modeh bi-knas patur*.

### Modeh Bi-Knas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרָבָא בַּר אֲהִילַאי, דְּאָמַר רָבָא בַּר אֲהִילַאי: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב, דְּאָמַר: מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר? דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא״ – אִם הִמָּצֵא בְּעֵדִים, תִּמָּצֵא בְּדַיָּינִים; פְּרָט לְמַרְשִׁיעַ אֶת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He requires** this verse **for** the *halakha* **of Rava bar Ahilai, as Rava bar Ahilai says: What is the reason** for the *halakha* **of Rav, who says:** One who **admits** he is liable **to** pay **a fine is exempt,** even if **afterward witnesses come** and testify to his liability? **As it is written: "If** the theft **shall be found [*himmatze timmatze*],"** which indicates that **if it is found [*himmatze*] through witnesses** that he is liable to pay the fine of double payment, **it will be found [*timmatze*]** that the fine will be enforced **by judges.** This **excludes** from double payment the case of **one who incriminates himself** by admitting his liability before any witnesses testify concerning him, and the same is applied to all cases that involve fines.

Rava bar Ahilai raises the well-known principle: *modeh bi-knas ve'achar kach ba'u edim patur* — one who admits liability for a fine and is subsequently corroborated by witnesses is nonetheless exempt from paying. The general rule that a *knas* cannot be imposed on the basis of one's own admission is familiar; what Rav extends is that even if witnesses later appear, once a person has admitted, the exemption persists. The question is where this principle is derived from.

### Asher Yarshi'un

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, דְּהַאי ״אִם הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא״ מַפֵּיק לֵיהּ לְגַנָּב עַצְמוֹ, מַרְשִׁיעַ עַצְמוֹ מְנָלַן? מֵ״אֲשֶׁר יַרְשִׁיעֻן אֱלֹהִים״ – וְלֹא הַמַּרְשִׁיעַ אֶת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that **both** verses are speaking **about** a bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, **who applies this** verse: **"If** the theft **shall be found," to the thief himself, from where do we** derive that **one who incriminates himself** is exempt from paying double payment or any other fine? The Gemara answers: He derives it **from** the verse: **"The one whom the judges convict** shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:8), which indicates that only one who is convicted by the judges on the basis of testimony must pay double, **but not one who incriminates himself.**

The derivation proceeds from the phrase *asher yarshi'un elohim yeshalem shnayim l'rei'eihu* — the one whom the judges find guilty shall pay double. The key word is *yarshi'un* — whom the judges declare wicked. This implies that the obligation to pay *kefel* is activated specifically by a judicial finding of guilt. If the person has already declared himself guilty through his own admission — *ha'marshi'a et atzmo* — the condition for the judicial imposition of the *knas* is not met. The payment follows only from the judges' determination, not from self-incrimination.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַד בְּגַנָּב וְחַד בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, דְּאַיְיתִי לֵיהּ מֵ״הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא״ לְמַרְשִׁיעַ אֶת עַצְמוֹ, הַאי ״אֲשֶׁר יַרְשִׁיעֻן״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its line of questioning: **And according to the one who says** that **one** verse is speaking **about a thief** himself **and one** verse is speaking **about** a bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, **who derives from: "If** the theft **shall be found" that one who incriminates himself** is exempt from payment of the fine, **what does he expound from this** verse: **"The one whom the judges convict** shall pay double to his neighbor"?

Under the view that one clause concerns the *ganav* and the other the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, *asher yarshi'un* establishes the first stage: that a *ganav*'s own admission does not trigger the obligation. The phrase *himatzei timatzei* then adds the further *chiddush*: even if witnesses subsequently arrive after the admission has already been made, the exemption remains in force. The discovery must be independent of the admission to generate liability.

### Parsha Nishnita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל – דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: כׇּל פָּרָשָׁה שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרָה וְנִשְׁנֵית, לֹא נִשְׁנֵית אֶלָּא לְדָבָר שֶׁנִּתְחַדֵּשׁ בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This may be explained **in accordance with** what **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught. As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: Any passage that was stated** in the Torah **and was** then **repeated, was repeated only for** the sake of **a matter that was introduced** for the first time in the repeated passage. That is, sometimes the Torah repeats an entire passage just for the sake of a single new detail. The single new detail derived from this passage is the *halakha* that one who admits that he is liable to pay a fine is exempt from paying the fine, which is derived from the words: "If the theft shall be found."

The *Gemara* invokes another principle of Rabbi Yishmael's school: *kol parsha she'ne'emra ve'nishnit lo nishnit ela bishvil davar she'nitchadesh bah* — any *parsha* that is stated and then repeated was repeated only on account of a single *chiddush* it introduces. It is therefore unnecessary to derive independent lessons from every detail in a repeated passage. Under the view that one clause covers the *ganav* and the other the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, the *chiddush* of *himatzei timatzei* is the admission rule, and the remaining details — *shor*, *chamor*, *seh*, *chayim* — do not each need to be accounted for; they are present simply because the passage is restated in full.

### Kefel Without Shvu'a

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא גַּנָּב עַצְמוֹ בִּשְׁבוּעָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** since the two cases of a thief and a bailee who falsely claims the deposit was stolen are juxtaposed, as their obligations to pay double payment are expressed in consecutive verses, **say** that **a thief himself** is not obligated to pay double payment unless he falsely confirms his innocence **with an oath,** just as a bailee is liable to pay double payment only under these circumstances.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ; דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: ״שְׁנַיִם יְשַׁלֵּם״ – שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה? אָמַרְתָּ לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This **cannot enter your mind, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Ya'akov says** that the phrase "if the theft shall be found…**he shall pay double"** indicates that a thief pays double even **without** taking **an oath.** Rabbi Ya'akov elaborates: Do **you say** the verse obligates every thief, even **without** his taking **an oath? Or** perhaps **it is** speaking **only** of a thief who falsely confirms his innocence **with an oath? You** must **say: This** second possibility **was not** the Torah's intent.

A final question closes this section. If one were to say that a *ganav* only pays *kefel* in conjunction with a *shvu'a* — as the *to'ein ta'anat ganav* pays *kefel* only after swearing — why would the *Torah* need to state explicitly that a *ganav* pays *kefel*? Abaye points out that this would be derivable by a *kal v'chomer*: if a person who originally received the object legitimately — a *shomer*, who started with permission — and then swore falsely pays double, certainly a *ganav* who took the object in violation of the owner's rights from the outset would pay double if he too took a *shvu'a*. The fact that the *Torah* states *kefel* explicitly for the *ganav* must therefore be to teach that the *ganav* pays *kefel* even without a *shvu'a* — *she'lo b'shvu'a*. Rabbi Yaakov makes this explicit: *chayim shnayim yeshalem she'lo b'shvu'a*. The *Torah*'s statement of *kefel* by the *ganav* is not superfluous; it establishes that the oath is not a prerequisite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״שְׁנַיִם יְשַׁלֵּם״ בְּגַנָּב, וְלֵיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב – וּמָה טוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, דִּבְהֶיתֵּירָא אֲתָא לִידֵיהּ, אָמַר קְרָא לִישַׁלֵּם תְּרֵי; גַּנָּב עַצְמוֹ, דִּבְאִיסּוּרָא אֲתָא לִידֵיהּ, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: If this was the Torah's intent, **let the Merciful One not write "he shall pay double" with regard to a thief** at all, **and let it be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the case of a bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, as follows: If, in the case of a bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, **for whom the** deposit initially **came into his possession in a permitted** fashion, **the verse states that he must pay double,** then in the case of **a thief himself, for whom** the stolen object **came into his possession in a prohibited manner** from the start, is it **not** clear **all the more so** that he must pay double payment?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״שְׁנַיִם יְשַׁלֵּם״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּגַנָּב עַצְמוֹ, לְמָה לִי? דַּאֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** then **why do I** need the words **"he shall pay double," which the Merciful One wrote concerning a thief himself?** It must be that the verse adds to the requirement of double payment for a thief and indicates **that** he must pay double **even without** having taken **an oath.**

## B'Yado Definition

### Literal vs Broad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״אִם הִמָּצֵא״ – לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״יָדוֹ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא יָדוֹ, גַּגּוֹ חֲצֵירוֹ וְקַרְפֵּיפוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא״ מִכׇּל מָקוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does this** verse, which states **"if** the theft **shall be found"** and concludes "he shall pay double," **come for this** purpose? **Isn't it required for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "If the theft shall be found in his possession." From the term "in **his possession [*yado*],"** **I have** derived **only** a case where the stolen item is found in **his hand [*yado*]. From where** do I derive that the same *halakha* applies if it is found on **his roof,** in **his yard, or** in **his enclosure [*vekarpeifo*]? The verse states** the repetitive phrase **"if** the theft **shall be found [*himmatze timmatze*],"** to indicate that the same *halakha* applies **in any case,** i.e., in any location that the stolen item is found.

The *pasuk* states *himatzei timatzei beyado hagneiva* — the theft is found in his hand. Taken literally, this might suggest that *kefel* liability attaches only when the stolen object is literally in the *ganav*'s hand. The *Gemara* begins from this narrow reading: *ein li ella yado* — I have learned only from his hand. *Himatzei timatzei* then serves to expand the concept of possession.

### Yard & Enclosure

The expansion is explicit in a *braita*: *ein li ella yado, gago, chatzeiro, ve'kapifo minayan?* — if I have learned only from his hand, how do I know the same applies when the object enters his roof, his courtyard, or his enclosure? *Talmud lomar im himatzei timatzei mikol makom* — the doubled verb *himatzei timatzei* teaches that the object being found in any form of his possession suffices, not only literally in his hand. Taking possession in any of the standard forms — through one's yard, roof, or enclosure — is sufficient to establish that the *ganav* has taken the stolen item.

### Dual Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא אוֹ ״הִמָּצֵא הִמָּצֵא״ אוֹ ״תִּמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא״; מִדְּשַׁנִּי קְרָא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If so,** if this is all the phrase is coming to teach, **let the verse state** the identical word twice: ***Himmatze himmatze*, or: *Timmatze timmatze*. From** the fact that **the verse varied** its wording, one may **conclude two** *halakhot* **from it:** That one is liable to pay double payment regardless of where the stolen item was found, and that a thief pays double payment even if he did not take an oath.

*Himatzei timatzei* thus serves a dual function in the passage. On one level it expands the concept of *b'yado* — possession — beyond the literal hand to encompass any domain the *ganav* controls. On another level it contributes, as developed throughout this section, to the scope of objects subject to *kefel*, whether through the *klal prat u-klal* structure or through the *ribui* framework. The *Gemara* notes that the doubled verb form — *himatzei* and *timatzei* — signals that more than one *halacha* is being conveyed simultaneously, and multiple *derashot* are therefore legitimately extracted from it.

## Principal vs Fines

### Rav's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַב: קֶרֶן – כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב. תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה – כִּשְׁעַת הַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to **the** matter **itself. Rav says:** When a thief makes his payments, the **principal** is paid **according to** the value as of the time **when he stole** it, whereas the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** are calculated **according to** the value at **the time of standing trial.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? אָמַר קְרָא: ״גְּנֵיבָה״ וְ״חַיִּים״. אַמַּאי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״חַיִּים״ בִּגְנֵיבָה? אַחֲיַיהּ לְקֶרֶן כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason,** i.e., the source, for this ruling **of Rav?** The Gemara answers: **The verse states:** "If the **theft** shall be found in his possession **alive,** whether it is an ox, or a donkey, or a sheep, he shall pay double" (Exodus 22:3). **Why is the Merciful One saying "alive [*ḥayyim*]" in** the context of this **theft?** This juxtaposition serves to teach: When making payment, **revive [*aḥayah*] the principal** to the value it had **as** of the time **when he stole** it.

The *Gemara* now turns to a *sugya* that marks the beginning of *Meruba* as it is traditionally studied in *yeshiva* — starting from daf 68. *Gufa*: Rav states the rule that becomes the organizing framework for the entire discussion that follows. *Keren ke'ein sheganav* — the principal is assessed according to the value of the stolen object at the time of the theft. The act that creates the *ganav*'s liability for the principal is the act of stealing itself, and therefore the value of what was taken at that moment determines what must be returned.

Rav's rule continues: *tashlumei kefel ve'tashlumei arba'ah ve'chamishah k'sha'at ha'amadah badin* — the payments of double and of four and five times are assessed at the time the *ganav* stands in court. This is conceptually coherent: *kefel* and *arba'ah ve'chamishah* are judicial penalties, not payments for the act of taking. The court imposes them, and the moment of imposition — when the *ganav* stands before the *dayanim* — is when the relevant value is assessed. Rav's rule thus draws a principled distinction: the principal is tied to the moment of transgression, while the fines are tied to the moment of adjudication.

### Rav Sheshet's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אָמֵינָא, כִּי נָיֵים וְשָׁכֵיב רַב אָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. דְּתַנְיָא: כְּחוּשָׁה וְהִשְׁמִינָהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: I say** that **when Rav was sleepy and lying down** to rest, **he said this *halakha*.** Rav Sheshet meant that this is a careless ruling, as it is contrary to a *baraita*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If the animal was stolen when it was **lean and** the thief **fattened it, he pays** the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment according to** the value of the animal as of the time **when he stole** it. This shows that the double payment and the fourfold and fivefold payment are applied in accordance with the value of the item at the time of the theft, not its value at the time of trial, as claimed by Rav.

Rav Sheshet challenges Rav's position sharply — *amina ki nayim*, I would say Rav was dozing when he said this — citing a *braita*: *kechushah vehishminah meshallem tashlumei kefel ve'tashlumei arba'ah ve'chamishah ke'ein sheganav* — if the animal was thin when stolen and the *ganav* fattened it, he pays the double and the four and five *ke'ein sheganav*, based on the value when stolen, not based on the enhanced value at the time of the court. This appears to contradict Rav's rule that the fines are assessed at the time of *ha'amadah badin*, since here the *braita* reverts to the value at the time of theft.

### No Benefit to Ganav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי, מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אֲנָא פַּטֵּימְנָא וְאַתְּ שָׁקְלַתְּ?!״
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: This *baraita* is not a refutation of Rav's opinion, **because** the thief **could say to** the owner of the animal: **I fattened** the animal myself, **and will you take** for yourself the added value from its fattening? In this particular case, Rav would agree that all payments are in accordance with the value of the item at the time of the theft.

The *Gemara* answers that this is a special case. The *ganav* can say to the owner: *ana patimna ve'at shaklat* — I fattened it and you are going to take it? The increase in value is attributable to the *ganav*'s own investment and effort. It would be inequitable to require the *ganav* to pay double or four and five times on a value that he himself created. The *braita*'s rule is therefore not inconsistent with Rav: when the increase in value results from the *ganav*'s own actions, that added value is not included in the assessment of the fines.

### Symmetry of Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁמֵינָה וְהִכְחִישָׁהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **Come** and **hear** another *baraita* that apparently contradicts Rav's ruling. If the animal was stolen when it was **fat and the thief caused it to become lean, he pays** the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment according to** the value of the animal as of the time **when he stole** it. The double and fourfold and fivefold payments are not paid in accordance with the value at the time of the trial, as claimed by Rav.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי – מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: מָה לִי קַטְלַהּ כּוּלַּהּ, מָה לִי קַטְלַהּ פַּלְגָא. כִּי קָאָמַר רַב – בְּיוּקְרָא וְזוּלָא הוּא דְּקָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There too,** there is a reason for this exception, **because we say of** the thief: **What is it to me** if he **killed** the animal **entirely,** and **what is it to me** if **he killed it partially?** By causing the animal to become lean, the thief is considered to have started to put it to death. Consequently, when he ultimately kills the animal, he pays the fourfold or fivefold payment according to its value when he first began to weaken it, i.e., at the time of the theft. **When Rav says** his ruling, **he is saying** it only **with regard to** cases of **appreciation** in value **and depreciation** in value, not to changes in the animal's physical state.

The converse case is likewise addressed: if the animal was fat when stolen and the *ganav* thinned it — *shmenah vehichishiah* — the *braita* states the same rule: *ke'ein sheganav*. Here one might expect the opposite result — if the *ganav* decreased the value, why should he benefit by paying fines based on the lower current value rather than the original higher value? The *Gemara* answers with a parallel logic: *mah li kullah katlah mah li palga katlah* — whether you destroyed it entirely or partially, the diminution in value is attributable to the *ganav*'s own actions. He should not be able to reduce his fine liability through damage he himself caused. The principle thus has a symmetry: value the *ganav* added does not increase the fines he must pay; value the *ganav* destroyed does not decrease them.

### Wine Barrel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא שָׁוְיָא זוּזָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי – קֶרֶן כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב; לֵימָא פְּלִיגָא דְּרַב אַדְּרַבָּה? דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: הַאי מַאן דִּגְזַל חָבִיתָא דְחַמְרָא מֵחַבְרֵיהּ, מֵעִיקָּרָא שָׁוְיָא זוּזָא וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי, תַּבְרַהּ אוֹ שַׁתְיַיהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם אַרְבְּעָ[ה]. אִיתְּבַר מִמֵּילָא – מְשַׁלֵּם זוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances** of the case referred to by Rav? **If we say that initially** the stolen item **was worth one dinar and in the end it was worth four dinars,** and Rav teaches that the thief pays the **principal according to** the value of the animal as of the time **when he stole** it, which is one dinar, **shall we say that Rav disagrees with this** ruling **of Rabba? As Rabba says:** With regard to **this one who robbed another of a barrel of wine,** if it was **initially worth one dinar and ultimately it was worth four dinars,** and after its appreciation the robber **broke** the barrel **or drank** the wine, **he pays four** dinars. If the barrel **broke by itself, he pays one dinar.** Rabba holds that even the principal is paid according to the time when he broke the barrel.

Rava introduces a case that probes the boundary between the principal and the fines under conditions of market change. A *ganav* stole a barrel of wine worth one *zuz*. By the time the case reaches court, the wine is worth four *zuzim*. The question is whether the principal — the *keren* — is assessed at one (the value at theft) or at four (the value at the time of standing in court).

### Active Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: כִּי קָאָמַר רַב – כְּגוֹן דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָ[ה] וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁוְיָא זוּזָא; קֶרֶן – כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּנַב, תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה – כִּשְׁעַת הַעֲמָדָה בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response that Rav agrees with Rabba with regard to the above case. **When Rav says** his *halakha* he is referring to a case where, **for example,** the item was **initially worth four** dinars **and ultimately was worth one dinar.** In this situation, Rav maintains that the thief does not profit from the decrease in the value of the principal. Rather, he pays the **principal according to** its value as of the time **when he stole** it, **and** the **double payment and** the **fourfold or fivefold payment** are paid **according to** the value at **the time of standing trial.**

Rava rules: *tavrah sha'at* — if the *ganav* actively destroyed the barrel when it was worth four, he pays four. *Itbar mimeila* — if it broke on its own, he pays one. The reasoning is that when the *ganav* actively destroys an object worth four, he incurs a new and independent act of liability at that moment. It is no longer merely the original *gneiva* that is being assessed; the act of destruction at the higher value constitutes a fresh *chiyuv* for that higher value. The principal is assessed accordingly. Where the barrel breaks on its own, no new act of liability is incurred, and the principal reverts to the value at the time of theft.

One might ask why the *ganav*'s active destruction raises the *keren* to the higher value rather than simply adding a *chiyuv* for *nezek*. The answer is that this is not a case of *shinui koneh* — the *ganav* did not acquire the barrel through transformation. The object still belongs to the owner, and the obligation to return it persists. But by actively destroying it at a point when it is worth four, the *ganav* generates a liability for four — not as a *shomer* who was negligent, but as someone who committed a fresh act of damage to the owner's property.

### Liability Breakdown

The distinction that emerges is between passive changes in value and active intervention. The three components of payment — *keren*, *kefel*, and *arba'ah ve'chamishah* — are each calibrated to a different moment and a different type of event. The *keren* is assessed at the time of theft, because that is when the transgression creating the principal obligation occurred. The *kefel* and *arba'ah ve'chamishah* are assessed at the time of *ha'amadah badin*, because these are penalties imposed by the court at the moment of adjudication. Neither category is adjusted for value changes attributable to the *ganav*'s own actions — whether increases or decreases. But if the *ganav* actively destroys the object when it carries a higher value, the *keren* itself is recalculated upward because a new independent act of liability has been committed.

### Ganav vs Shomer

A question arises from the comparison with a *shomer*. If a *shomer* holds an object given to him when it was worth one, and the value rises to four, and through negligence the object is destroyed when it is worth four, the *shomer* pays four. His liability is rooted in the ongoing obligation of *shmira* — he was responsible for the object when it was worth four, he was negligent, and an object worth four was lost. By contrast, if a *ganav* holds the same object and it is destroyed *mimeila* — on its own, without any active intervention — even through what would constitute negligence in a *shomer*, he pays only the value at the time of theft. This is not a leniency for the *ganav* in all respects; it reflects a difference in the nature of the liability. The *shomer*'s obligation is an ongoing custodial responsibility; damage during that custody triggers liability based on current value. The *ganav*'s obligation, by contrast, is generated by the original act of theft and is assessed at that moment. He never accepted the obligations of *shmira*, and therefore his liability for passive deterioration or passive destruction is calibrated to the original moment of taking.

The converse illustrates the same principle from the other direction. A *shomer* whose object is destroyed by *oness* — unavoidable accident — is exempt, because his liability is conditioned on negligence. A *ganav* whose object is destroyed by *oness* is *chayav*, because his liability is unconditional, rooted in the act of taking regardless of what subsequently occurs. The *ganav* pays not because of negligence or because of ongoing responsibility, but because he stole. That original act carries the obligation forward regardless of how the loss ultimately occurs.

### Final Synthesis

The full picture that emerges from this *sugya* is as follows. The *keren* is assessed *ke'ein sheganav* — at the value of the stolen object at the moment of theft, because that act is what generates the principal obligation. The *kefel* and *arba'ah ve'chamishah* are assessed *k'sha'at ha'amadah badin* — at the value at the time the *ganav* stands in court, because these are court-imposed penalties activated at the moment of adjudication. Neither category is inflated by value the *ganav* himself added, nor reduced by value the *ganav* himself destroyed. Where the *ganav* actively destroys the object at a point when it carries a higher market value, the *keren* is assessed at the higher value — not because the rule of *ke'ein sheganav* has been abandoned, but because the act of active destruction constitutes a new independent act of liability assessed at the time it occurs.
