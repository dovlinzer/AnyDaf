# Bava Kamma 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Deriving Kefel from Klal

### Opening Context

The discussion on daf 64 concludes the series of *drashot* that opened *Meruba* — spanning the first several *dappim* — concerning how we know that *kefel* applies to all objects of theft. The *Gemara* has been working through two distinct passages in the *Torah*, each of which provides independent grounding for the law of double payment.

### Two Passages

The first passage is the direct case of *gneiva*: *ki yignov ish shor o seh utvacho o mecharo chamisha vakar yeshalem tachat hashor ve'arba tzon tachat haseh* — if a man steals an ox or a sheep and slaughters or sells it, he pays five times for the ox and four times for the sheep. This is the primary *pasuk* establishing the *chiyuv* of a *ganav* and the payments of four and five. The *pasuk* continues: *im himatzei timatzei beyado hagneiva mishor ad chamor ad seh chayim shnayim yeshalem* — if the stolen animal is found alive in his possession, from ox to donkey to sheep, he pays double. This establishes both *kefel* for the case where the animal was not slaughtered or sold, and introduces the phrase *chayim* — alive — alongside the list *shor*, *chamor*, *seh*.

The second passage arises in the context of a *shomer*. If an object was entrusted to a watchman and stolen, and the *ganav* is found, he pays double — a reference back to the general principle. If the *ganav* is not found and the *shomer* takes an oath and then is later discovered to have claimed falsely that the object was stolen, he too pays double. The preceding *dappim* focused primarily on this second passage to establish the scope of *kefel* across different categories of objects. Now the *Gemara* returns to the first passage — *mishor ad chamor ad seh chayim* — to derive the same scope from there.

### Why List Animals?

A natural question arises: why does the *pasuk* enumerate *shor*, *chamor*, and *seh*, and why does it choose the formulation *mishor ad chamor ad seh chayim*? The purpose of this list, as the *Gemara* will establish, is to enable an expansion through the hermeneutical structure of *klal u-prat u-klal* — or alternatively, as a *ribui* — to extend the liability of *kefel* beyond these specific animals to all movable property with intrinsic value, while simultaneously excluding *karka*, *shtarot*, and other categories.

### Klal U-Prat Question

The *Gemara* opens with a pointed question from a *braita*: why not simply say *shor* and *gneiva*, using just those two terms to form a *klal u-prat* structure? The *Gemara* immediately clarifies: the *pasuk* actually reads *hagneiva mishor* — *gneiva* precedes *shor*. This order matters considerably. If the broader term *gneiva* appeared before the specific term *shor*, that would constitute a *klal u-prat*, limiting liability to the *prat* alone — only an ox. If *shor* appeared first and *gneiva* second, with the broader term at the end, one might read it as a *prat u-klal*, which would expand to include everything.

The *Gemara* then considers the *braita*'s hypothetical: *ilu ne'emar shor ve-gneiva* — had it been written with *shor* first and then *gneiva*, which would make *gneiva* the closing *klal*. The *Gemara* asks: would that not have served as a *klal prat u-klal*, expanding the scope? No, the *Gemara* responds — if you have only two terms, you either have a *klal u-prat*, which limits to the *prat*, or a *prat u-klal*, which expands to everything without limit. The intermediate structure of *klal prat u-klal* requires three elements, not two. The *braita*'s argument assumes the existence of such a three-part structure somewhere in the *pasuk*, and the challenge to the *braita* is: where is the second *klal* that would allow that structure to operate?

### Rav's Interpretation

The *Gemara* works toward identifying the second *klal*. One candidate is the word *chayim* — living things — at the end of the *pasuk*. Another candidate is *shnayim yeshalem*, which carries a broadly inclusive connotation. The *Gemara* ultimately presents the answer in the name of Rava: *tanna hachayim kasameich* — the *tanna* relies on the word *chayim* to serve as the final *klal*. The structure is thus: *gneiva* is the opening *klal*, the listed animals are the *pratim*, and *chayim* is the closing *klal* — a complete *klal u-prat u-klal*.

### Chayim as Klal

The *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty with this construction. In a standard *klal prat u-klal*, the final *klal* should be at least as broad as the opening *klal*, or the expansion will be understood as limited to things similar to the *prat*. Here, the opening *klal* — *gneiva* — is maximally broad, encompassing any theft. The final *klal* — *chayim* — is explicitly narrower, referring only to living creatures. This asymmetry seems to undermine the structure.

The *Gemara* answers: *ha tanna de-vei Rabbi Yishmael hu* — this is the school of Rabbi Yishmael, which holds that even where the final *klal* is smaller than the opening *klal*, the *klal prat u-klal* structure still operates as an expansion. The hermeneutical assumption of this school is that two *klalim* bounding *pratim* always broaden the scope beyond the literal *pratim*, regardless of whether the two *klalim* are identical in scope.

The *Gemara* then reconstructs the *braita*'s internal question and answer. The *braita* asks: why is *himatzei timatzei* needed? If we already have *gneiva*, *shor*, *chamor*, *seh*, and *chayim* constituting a *klal prat u-klal*, why do we need the additional phrase *himatzei timatzei*?

The *Gemara* traces the incremental logic of the *braita*. Had the *pasuk* said only *gneiva* and *shor*, with *chayim* as the *klal*, the *prat* — *shor* — would suggest limiting liability to animals offered as sacrifices, *davar ha-karev gabbei mizbe'ach*. The explicit addition of *seh* then expands beyond that. Had the text said *gneiva*, *shor*, and *seh*, I would have expanded to things sanctified by the firstborn, *davar ha-kadosh bivechora* — but not more. The addition of *chamor* then expands again. With *shor*, *seh*, and *chamor* all present as *pratim*, I would have abstracted to *baalei chayim* — animals generally — but not beyond. Thus even with *chayim* functioning as a *klal*, the scope would have remained confined to living creatures. It is precisely here that *himatzei timatzei* is needed — to push beyond animals entirely.

### Two Kelalim Structure

The *Gemara* now observes that *himatzei timatzei* is itself a doubled formulation — two words, each carrying the force of a *klal*. This raises a further question: *shnei klalos smichi ahadadi ninhu* — two *klalim* standing adjacent to each other do not function like a normal *klal prat u-klal*. They are simply two broad terms sitting together.

Ravina answers in the name of the sages of *Eretz Yisrael*: *kol makom she'atah motzei shnei klalos hasmouchin zeh lazeh hatel prat beineihem* — wherever you find two *klalim* adjacent to one another, insert the *pratim* between them and treat the result as a *klal prat u-klal*. The two words *himatzei* and *timatzei* function as the two flanking *klalim*, and the listed animals serve as the *pratim* that stand between them.

### Placing Shor

The *Gemara* now works through each *prat* in turn, asking what work each one does when inserted between *himatzei* and *timatzei*. *Shadi shor bein himatzei letimatzei* — insert *shor* between the two *klalim*. What does this include? It cannot be coming to include *baalei chayim* — animals — since that is already derived from the explicit word *chayim*. Rather, it must be including something that is not a living creature. The *Gemara* therefore derives from *shor* the category of *davar hamitaltel v'gufo mammon* — movable property with intrinsic monetary value.

### Level of Abstraction

The level of abstraction derived from *shor* as a *prat* is stated explicitly: *mah prat meforash davar hamitaltel v'gufo mammon, af kol davar hamitaltel v'gufo mammon* — just as an ox is movable and has intrinsic value, so too any object that is movable and has intrinsic value falls within the scope of *kefel*. This formulation accomplishes the core work of exclusion as well. Land — *karka* — is immovable. Slaves — *avadim* — are legally equated with land. Documents — *shtarot* — have no intrinsic monetary value; their worth is representational. All three are thereby excluded.

The *Gemara* continues inserting *chamor* between the two *klalim*. Since movable property with intrinsic value is already covered by *shor*, *chamor* must be coming to include something further — *davar mesuyam*, a defined or specified thing. The precise meaning of *davar mesuyam* is somewhat obscure; it was discussed at length in the preceding *daf*, and the conclusion there was that its exact scope remains unclear. The *Gemara* continues similarly with *seh*, but runs into difficulty: after *shor* and *chamor* have done their work, it becomes hard to identify what further expansion *seh* could be adding. This difficulty motivates the *Gemara*'s pivot to an alternative interpretive framework.

## Exclusions & Ribui

### Ribui vs Klal

At this point the *Gemara* introduces an alternative approach associated with *tanna de-vei Rabbi Yishmael*: instead of reading *himatzei timatzei* as a *klal prat u-klal* structure, read it as *ribui u-mi'ut v'ribui* — expansion, limitation, expansion. This framework operates differently from *klal prat u-klal*. Where *klal prat u-klal* begins by asking what the *pratim* tell us and then expanding from them, *ribui mi'ut v'ribui* begins from the assumption that everything is included, and uses the *pratim* as exclusions rather than as templates for expansion. Under this reading, *himatzei timatzei* constitutes a *ribui* — a broadly inclusive statement — and the listed animals serve as *mi'utim*, each one excluding a category.

### Three Exclusions

Under the *ribui* approach, one begins with the universe of all objects potentially subject to *kefel*, and then asks what the listed *pratim* exclude. Each of the three explicit animals in the list — *shor*, *chamor*, and *seh* — is understood to exclude one category: *chad l'mi'utei karka* — one excludes land; *chad l'mi'utei avadim* — one excludes slaves; *ve'chad l'mi'utei shtarot* — one excludes documents. The result is identical in practical terms to the *klal prat u-klal* approach: all movable property with intrinsic value is subject to *kefel*, while land, slaves, and documents are exempt.

That still leaves two words unused: *gneiva* and *chayim*. Under the *ribui* framework, these are no longer functioning as *klalim*. The *Gemara* invokes Rav's teaching to give them work: *d'amar Rav achyia l'keren k'ein sheganav* — restore the principal to the condition it was in at the time of the theft. The word *gneiva* teaches that the object must be restored as it was when stolen, and *chayim* teaches that it must be restored as a living thing — that is, in its living state as of the moment it was taken. Both words together express the principle that the thief's obligation with respect to the principal is calibrated to the moment of the theft, not any later point.

### Restore to Original

The principle of *achyia l'keren k'ein sheganav* — restoring the principal to the state it was in when stolen — is thus extracted from these two remaining words under the *ribui* interpretation. If the animal was in a certain condition when taken, the thief bears responsibility to restore it to that condition. The implications of this principle — particularly how it interacts with changes in the animal's condition or market value between the time of theft and the time of adjudication — will be taken up in detail in the final section of the *daf*.

## Ganav vs To'ein

### Option 1: Two Cases

The *Gemara* returns to a question that has been present throughout the discussion: how to read the relationship between the two halves of the passage about the *shomer*. One possibility is that one clause addresses the *ganav* himself — the actual thief who pays *kefel* — and the other addresses the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, the *shomer* who falsely claims that the object was stolen and thereby incurs *kefel* liability. On this reading, the two clauses divide the case between them.

### Option 2: Both To'ein

The alternative possibility is that both clauses concern the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, and that *himatzei timatzei* derives the scope of liability — that is, the categories of objects for which *kefel* applies — specifically in the context of a *ganav* himself, not the *shomer*. The *Gemara* explores what each view entails for the derivation of the rule of *modeh bi-knas patur*.

### Modeh Bi-Knas

Rava bar Ahilai raises the well-known principle: *modeh bi-knas ve'achar kach ba'u edim patur* — one who admits liability for a fine and is subsequently corroborated by witnesses is nonetheless exempt from paying. The general rule that a *knas* cannot be imposed on the basis of one's own admission is familiar; what Rav extends is that even if witnesses later appear, once a person has admitted, the exemption persists. The question is where this principle is derived from.

### Asher Yarshi'un

The derivation proceeds from the phrase *asher yarshi'un elohim yeshalem shnayim l'rei'eihu* — the one whom the judges find guilty shall pay double. The key word is *yarshi'un* — whom the judges declare wicked. This implies that the obligation to pay *kefel* is activated specifically by a judicial finding of guilt. If the person has already declared himself guilty through his own admission — *ha'marshi'a et atzmo* — the condition for the judicial imposition of the *knas* is not met. The payment follows only from the judges' determination, not from self-incrimination.

Under the view that one clause concerns the *ganav* and the other the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, *asher yarshi'un* establishes the first stage: that a *ganav*'s own admission does not trigger the obligation. The phrase *himatzei timatzei* then adds the further *chiddush*: even if witnesses subsequently arrive after the admission has already been made, the exemption remains in force. The discovery must be independent of the admission to generate liability.

Under the opposing view — that *himatzei timatzei* is used to define the scope of the *ganav*'s *kefel* obligation, not the admission rule — the rule of *modeh bi-knas* is derived solely from *asher yarshi'un*. On this reading, *asher yarshi'un* alone establishes both stages: that self-admission is insufficient, and that witnesses arriving after an admission do not override the exemption. The holder of this view may also hold that if witnesses come after the admission, the person is in fact *chayav* — that only the first stage (admission alone being insufficient) is accepted, while the second stage (perpetual exemption even after witnesses) is not.

### Parsha Nishnita

The *Gemara* invokes another principle of Rabbi Yishmael's school: *kol parsha she'ne'emra ve'nishnit lo nishnit ela bishvil davar she'nitchadesh bah* — any *parsha* that is stated and then repeated was repeated only on account of a single *chiddush* it introduces. It is therefore unnecessary to derive independent lessons from every detail in a repeated passage. Under the view that one clause covers the *ganav* and the other the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, the *chiddush* of *himatzei timatzei* is the admission rule, and the remaining details — *shor*, *chamor*, *seh*, *chayim* — do not each need to be accounted for; they are present simply because the passage is restated in full.

### Kefel Without Shvu'a

A final question closes this section. If one were to say that a *ganav* only pays *kefel* in conjunction with a *shvu'a* — as the *to'ein ta'anat ganav* pays *kefel* only after swearing — why would the *Torah* need to state explicitly that a *ganav* pays *kefel*? Abaye points out that this would be derivable by a *kal v'chomer*: if a person who originally received the object legitimately — a *shomer*, who started with permission — and then swore falsely pays double, certainly a *ganav* who took the object in violation of the owner's rights from the outset would pay double if he too took a *shvu'a*. The fact that the *Torah* states *kefel* explicitly for the *ganav* must therefore be to teach that the *ganav* pays *kefel* even without a *shvu'a* — *she'lo b'shvu'a*. Rabbi Yaakov makes this explicit: *chayim shnayim yeshalem she'lo b'shvu'a*. The *Torah*'s statement of *kefel* by the *ganav* is not superfluous; it establishes that the oath is not a prerequisite.

## B'Yado Definition

### Literal vs Broad

The *pasuk* states *himatzei timatzei beyado hagneiva* — the theft is found in his hand. Taken literally, this might suggest that *kefel* liability attaches only when the stolen object is literally in the *ganav*'s hand. The *Gemara* begins from this narrow reading: *ein li ella yado* — I have learned only from his hand. *Himatzei timatzei* then serves to expand the concept of possession.

### Yard & Enclosure

The expansion is explicit in a *braita*: *ein li ella yado, gago, chatzeiro, ve'kapifo minayan?* — if I have learned only from his hand, how do I know the same applies when the object enters his roof, his courtyard, or his enclosure? *Talmud lomar im himatzei timatzei mikol makom* — the doubled verb *himatzei timatzei* teaches that the object being found in any form of his possession suffices, not only literally in his hand. Taking possession in any of the standard forms — through one's yard, roof, or enclosure — is sufficient to establish that the *ganav* has taken the stolen item.

### Dual Purpose

*Himatzei timatzei* thus serves a dual function in the passage. On one level it expands the concept of *b'yado* — possession — beyond the literal hand to encompass any domain the *ganav* controls. On another level it contributes, as developed throughout this section, to the scope of objects subject to *kefel*, whether through the *klal prat u-klal* structure or through the *ribui* framework. The *Gemara* notes that the doubled verb form — *himatzei* and *timatzei* — signals that more than one *halacha* is being conveyed simultaneously, and multiple *derashot* are therefore legitimately extracted from it.

## Principal vs Fines

### Rav's Principle

The *Gemara* now turns to a *sugya* that marks the beginning of *Meruba* as it is traditionally studied in *yeshiva* — starting from daf 68. *Gufa*: Rav states the rule that becomes the organizing framework for the entire discussion that follows. *Keren ke'ein sheganav* — the principal is assessed according to the value of the stolen object at the time of the theft. The act that creates the *ganav*'s liability for the principal is the act of stealing itself, and therefore the value of what was taken at that moment determines what must be returned.

### Fines by Trial

Rav's rule continues: *tashlumei kefel ve'tashlumei arba'ah ve'chamishah k'sha'at ha'amadah badin* — the payments of double and of four and five times are assessed at the time the *ganav* stands in court. This is conceptually coherent: *kefel* and *arba'ah ve'chamishah* are judicial penalties, not payments for the act of taking. The court imposes them, and the moment of imposition — when the *ganav* stands before the *dayanim* — is when the relevant value is assessed. Rav's rule thus draws a principled distinction: the principal is tied to the moment of transgression, while the fines are tied to the moment of adjudication.

### Rav Sheshet's Challenge

Rav Sheshet challenges Rav's position sharply — *amina ki nayim*, I would say Rav was dozing when he said this — citing a *braita*: *kechushah vehishminah meshallem tashlumei kefel ve'tashlumei arba'ah ve'chamishah ke'ein sheganav* — if the animal was thin when stolen and the *ganav* fattened it, he pays the double and the four and five *ke'ein sheganav*, based on the value when stolen, not based on the enhanced value at the time of the court. This appears to contradict Rav's rule that the fines are assessed at the time of *ha'amadah badin*, since here the *braita* reverts to the value at the time of theft.

### No Benefit to Ganav

The *Gemara* answers that this is a special case. The *ganav* can say to the owner: *ana patimna ve'at shaklat* — I fattened it and you are going to take it? The increase in value is attributable to the *ganav*'s own investment and effort. It would be inequitable to require the *ganav* to pay double or four and five times on a value that he himself created. The *braita*'s rule is therefore not inconsistent with Rav: when the increase in value results from the *ganav*'s own actions, that added value is not included in the assessment of the fines.

### Symmetry of Loss

The converse case is likewise addressed: if the animal was fat when stolen and the *ganav* thinned it — *shmenah vehichishiah* — the *braita* states the same rule: *ke'ein sheganav*. Here one might expect the opposite result — if the *ganav* decreased the value, why should he benefit by paying fines based on the lower current value rather than the original higher value? The *Gemara* answers with a parallel logic: *mah li kullah katlah mah li palga katlah* — whether you destroyed it entirely or partially, the diminution in value is attributable to the *ganav*'s own actions. He should not be able to reduce his fine liability through damage he himself caused. The principle thus has a symmetry: value the *ganav* added does not increase the fines he must pay; value the *ganav* destroyed does not decrease them.

### Wine Barrel Case

Rava introduces a case that probes the boundary between the principal and the fines under conditions of market change. A *ganav* stole a barrel of wine worth one *zuz*. By the time the case reaches court, the wine is worth four *zuzim*. The question is whether the principal — the *keren* — is assessed at one (the value at theft) or at four (the value at the time of standing in court).

### Active Destruction

Rava rules: *tavrah sha'at* — if the *ganav* actively destroyed the barrel when it was worth four, he pays four. *Itbar mimeila* — if it broke on its own, he pays one. The reasoning is that when the *ganav* actively destroys an object worth four, he incurs a new and independent act of liability at that moment. It is no longer merely the original *gneiva* that is being assessed; the act of destruction at the higher value constitutes a fresh *chiyuv* for that higher value. The principal is assessed accordingly. Where the barrel breaks on its own, no new act of liability is incurred, and the principal reverts to the value at the time of theft.

### Active Destruction

One might ask why the *ganav*'s active destruction raises the *keren* to the higher value rather than simply adding a *chiyuv* for *nezek*. The answer is that this is not a case of *shinui koneh* — the *ganav* did not acquire the barrel through transformation. The object still belongs to the owner, and the obligation to return it persists. But by actively destroying it at a point when it is worth four, the *ganav* generates a liability for four — not as a *shomer* who was negligent, but as someone who committed a fresh act of damage to the owner's property.

### Liability Breakdown

The distinction that emerges is between passive changes in value and active intervention. The three components of payment — *keren*, *kefel*, and *arba'ah ve'chamishah* — are each calibrated to a different moment and a different type of event. The *keren* is assessed at the time of theft, because that is when the transgression creating the principal obligation occurred. The *kefel* and *arba'ah ve'chamishah* are assessed at the time of *ha'amadah badin*, because these are penalties imposed by the court at the moment of adjudication. Neither category is adjusted for value changes attributable to the *ganav*'s own actions — whether increases or decreases. But if the *ganav* actively destroys the object when it carries a higher value, the *keren* itself is recalculated upward because a new independent act of liability has been committed.

### Ganav vs Shomer

A question arises from the comparison with a *shomer*. If a *shomer* holds an object given to him when it was worth one, and the value rises to four, and through negligence the object is destroyed when it is worth four, the *shomer* pays four. His liability is rooted in the ongoing obligation of *shmira* — he was responsible for the object when it was worth four, he was negligent, and an object worth four was lost. By contrast, if a *ganav* holds the same object and it is destroyed *mimeila* — on its own, without any active intervention — even through what would constitute negligence in a *shomer*, he pays only the value at the time of theft. This is not a leniency for the *ganav* in all respects; it reflects a difference in the nature of the liability. The *shomer*'s obligation is an ongoing custodial responsibility; damage during that custody triggers liability based on current value. The *ganav*'s obligation, by contrast, is generated by the original act of theft and is assessed at that moment. He never accepted the obligations of *shmira*, and therefore his liability for passive deterioration or passive destruction is calibrated to the original moment of taking.

The converse illustrates the same principle from the other direction. A *shomer* whose object is destroyed by *oness* — unavoidable accident — is exempt, because his liability is conditioned on negligence. A *ganav* whose object is destroyed by *oness* is *chayav*, because his liability is unconditional, rooted in the act of taking regardless of what subsequently occurs. The *ganav* pays not because of negligence or because of ongoing responsibility, but because he stole. That original act carries the obligation forward regardless of how the loss ultimately occurs.

### Final Synthesis

The full picture that emerges from this *sugya* is as follows. The *keren* is assessed *ke'ein sheganav* — at the value of the stolen object at the moment of theft, because that act is what generates the principal obligation. The *kefel* and *arba'ah ve'chamishah* are assessed *k'sha'at ha'amadah badin* — at the value at the time the *ganav* stands in court, because these are court-imposed penalties activated at the moment of adjudication. Neither category is inflated by value the *ganav* himself added, nor reduced by value the *ganav* himself destroyed. Where the *ganav* actively destroys the object at a point when it carries a higher market value, the *keren* is assessed at the higher value — not because the rule of *ke'ein sheganav* has been abandoned, but because the act of active destruction constitutes a new independent act of liability assessed at the time it occurs.
