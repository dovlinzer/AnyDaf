# Yevamot 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Androgynus & Teruma

### Androgynus feeds wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּמָה בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: שְׁתֵּי קוּפּוֹת, אַחַת שֶׁל חוּלִּין וְאַחַת שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה, וְלִפְנֵיהֶם שְׁתֵּי סְאִין, אַחַת שֶׁל חוּלִּין וְאַחַת שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה, וְנָפְלוּ אֵלּוּ בְּתוֹךְ אֵלּוּ — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מוּתָּרִים, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: תְּרוּמָה לְתוֹךְ תְּרוּמָה נָפְלָה, וְחוּלִּין בְּתוֹךְ חוּלִּין נָפְלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA. The Gemara raises a question with regard to Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion: **Does Rabbi Yoḥanan hold that *teruma* in the present** applies **by Torah** law? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: There were **two** large **baskets, one** filled with **non-sacred** produce **and** the other **one** filled with ***teruma*, and before them** were **two** one-***se'a*** vessels, **one** filled with **non-sacred** produce **and** the other **one** filled with ***teruma*. And these,** the contents of each of the *se'a* vessels, **fell into those,** each of the baskets. It is possible that the *teruma* fell into the non-sacred produce, and it is prohibited for non-priests to eat a mixture of *teruma* and non-sacred produce. Nevertheless, the produce found in the basket containing the non-sacred produce **is permitted, as I say** that **the *teruma* fell into the *teruma* and the non-sacred** produce **fell into the non-sacred** produce.

Our discussion picks up near the end of a lengthy analysis of the *androgynus* — the hermaphrodite — and the question of whether such a person can cause his wife to eat *teruma*. As established in earlier sessions, the *mishna* records that according to Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon, an *androgynus* can indeed feed his wife *teruma*. Rabbi Yochanan extended this further, maintaining that the *androgynus* can even feed his wife *matnot kehuna* — those portions of *kodesh mamash*, the genuinely sacred priestly portions, including parts of the sacrificial offerings. Resh Lakish disagreed, holding that the *androgynus* cannot confer eligibility to those more elevated forms of *kodesh*.

### Teruma status debate

The *gemara* now frames the underlying dispute: Rabbi Yochanan's position implies that *teruma bizman hazeh* — *teruma* in the present era — retains the force of *de-oraita* law. For if *teruma* today were merely *de-rabbanan*, the *mishna*'s ruling that the *androgynus* can feed his wife *teruma* would not necessarily tell us anything about his ability to confer eligibility to more elevated forms of sanctity. The *gemara* therefore asks pointedly: *ve-savar Rabbi Yochanan Teruma bizman hazeh de-oraita?* — does Rabbi Yochanan truly hold that *teruma* today is biblical in force?

### Mixed baskets case

In support of this challenge, the *gemara* cites a *baraita*: there are two baskets — one of *chullin* and one of *teruma* — and above or adjacent to them are two *se'in*, one of *chullin* and one of *teruma*. The *se'in* tip over and fall into the baskets. We are now uncertain which *se'ah* fell into which basket. The *baraita* rules that everything is permitted, because we say: the *teruma* fell into the *teruma* basket and the *chullin* fell into the *chullin* basket — *teruma le-toch Teruma nafla, ve-chullin le-toch chullin nafla*.

### Rov requirement dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: וְהוּא שֶׁרַבּוּ חוּלִּין עַל הַתְּרוּמָה, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא רַבּוּ חוּלִּין עַל הַתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish said** that **this** is only the case **if the non-sacred** produce in the basket **was greater** in quantity **than the *teruma*** in the one-*se'a* vessel, so that even if the *teruma* fell into the non-sacred produce, it is nullified. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The mixture is permitted **even if the non-sacred** produce **was not greater** in quantity **than the *teruma*,** as one may rely on the assumption that each type of produce fell into its own type.

On this *baraita*, Resh Lakish added a qualification: *ve-hu she-rabbu chullin al ha-Teruma* — this ruling applies only when the *chullin* already in the basket is greater in quantity than the *se'ah* of *teruma* above it. His reasoning is that even if the *teruma* had fallen into the *chullin* basket, it would have been *batel be-rov* — nullified by a majority — since for a prohibition that is *de-rabbanan* we do not require sixty parts; a simple majority suffices. This is clear evidence that Resh Lakish holds *teruma bizman hazeh* is *de-rabbanan*.

Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, said: *af al pi she-lo rabbu chullin al ha-Teruma* — even if there is not a majority of *chullin* over *teruma*, we still permit everything, relying entirely on the possibility that each *se'ah* fell into its corresponding basket. Notably, this makes Rabbi Yochanan *more* lenient than Resh Lakish in this particular case — a striking result.

### Kashe on Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, קָסָבַר בִּדְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי רִבּוּיָא הוּא דְּבָעֵינַן. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קַשְׁיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to Reish Lakish,** who maintains that *teruma* nowadays applies by rabbinic law, this is logical, as **he maintains** that even **with regard to** *teruma* that is in effect only **by rabbinic** law, **we also require** the permitted portion of the mixture to be **greater** in quantity so that it can nullify the forbidden part. **But according to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** it is **difficult** because if he maintains that even nowadays *teruma* is mandated by Torah law, how can one disregard the concern and permit the mixture based merely on the assumption that the events occurred in a way that preserves the produce in its permitted state?

The *gemara* presses the difficulty: *bishlama* Resh Lakish's position is coherent — he holds *teruma* is *de-rabbanan*, and therefore a *rov* is sufficient to render the mixture permitted. But *ella le-Rabbi Yochanan kashya*: if Rabbi Yochanan holds *teruma bizman hazeh* is *de-oraita*, how can he permit the mixture without any *rov*? A *de-oraita* prohibition surely cannot be nullified by a bare majority, let alone without one. The *gemara* is stuck.

## Seder Olam proof

### Baraita is Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי — רַבָּנַן הִיא, וַאֲנָא דְּאָמְרִי כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rabbi Yoḥanan can say: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita* taught? **It** is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who maintain that *teruma* nowadays is in effect only by rabbinic law. **But I,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **said** what I said **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** that a priest who is a hermaphrodite enables his wife to eat *teruma*, and Rabbi Yosei himself maintains that even nowadays the *halakha* of *teruma* is in effect by Torah law. Accordingly, I argued that a hermaphrodite enables his wife to eat not only *teruma*, but even the breast and thigh of peace-offerings.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by asking: *ha mani* — whose position does this *baraita* represent? The answer: *Rabbanan hi* — it reflects the position of those who disagree with Rabbi Yose. And Rabbi Yochanan declares: *ve-ana de-amri ke-Rabbi Yose* — I am speaking in accordance with the view of Rabbi Yose. The *baraita* about the baskets is therefore irrelevant to Rabbi Yochanan's position, as it does not accord with the opinion he is invoking.

### Yerusha & Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא בְּסֵדֶר עוֹלָם: ״אֲשֶׁר יָרְשׁוּ אֲבוֹתֶיךָ וִירִשְׁתָּהּ״, יְרוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה — יֵשׁ לָהֶן, וּשְׁלִישִׁית — אֵין לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught in** a *baraita* in the anthology called ***Seder Olam*,** with regard to a verse that speaks of the Jewish people's return to Eretz Yisrael following their exile: "And the Lord your God will bring you into the land **that your fathers inherited, and you shall inherit it"** (Deuteronomy 30:5). These two expressions of inheritance teach that **they had a first inheritance** of Eretz Yisrael in the days of Joshua **and a second** inheritance at the time of the return from the Babylonian exile. That is to say, since the sanctity of the land had lapsed when the First Temple was destroyed and the Jewish people exiled to Babylonia, a second sanctification was necessary when they returned to their land. **But they will not have a third** inheritance. In other words, it will never be necessary to sanctify the land for a third time, as the second sanctification was permanent.

The *gemara* now demonstrates that Rabbi Yose does indeed hold *teruma bizman hazeh* is *de-oraita*, citing a *baraita* from *Seder Olam*: *asher yarshu avotecha ve-irshtah* — "which your fathers inherited and you shall inherit it." The *Seder Olam* derives from this verse that a first *yerusha* and a second *yerusha* carry enduring *kedusha*, but a third *yerusha* is unnecessary because the *kedusha* was never lost. As Rashi explains, the first *yerusha* refers to the conquest of Yehoshua bin Nun; the second to the return led by Ezra. The exile of *Galut Bavel* did eliminate the original *kedusha* of *Eretz Yisrael*, but Ezra's return re-consecrated the land. The subsequent Roman exile — *Galut Titus* — does not eliminate that second consecration, and therefore the *kedusha* of *Eretz Yisrael* remains intact, which in turn means that *teruma* retains its *de-oraita* force. This is the position of Rabbi Yose.

### Seder Olam nature

It is worth pausing on the nature of *Seder Olam* itself. It is not entirely clear whether this citation refers to the same *Seder Olam* commonly invoked as a work of historical chronology, though Chaim Milikowsky, who has written extensively on *Seder Olam*, argues that the text is extremely early — so the identification is at least plausible. The work attempts to fill in historical gaps not fully addressed in the *Chumash*, tracing events through the period of *Beit Sheni*. It is best understood as a kind of chronicle, organized something like a collection of *baraitot* — attributed statements and discussions — rather than flowing narrative prose. Whether the attribution of *Seder Olam* as a whole, or just this particular passage within it, belongs to Rabbi Yose is a question that admits no definitive answer here.

### Seder Olam explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאן תְּנָא סֵדֶר עוֹלָם — רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught *Seder Olam*?** It is **Rabbi Yosei.** Since Rabbi Yosei maintains that the second sanctification of Eretz Yisrael never lapsed even after the destruction of the Second Temple, he also holds that *teruma* nowadays is in effect by Torah law.

In any case, the *gemara* identifies the *tanna* of *Seder Olam* as Rabbi Yose: *ve-amar Rabbi Yochanan man tana Seder Olam — Rabbi Yose*. This closes the loop: Rabbi Yochanan is following Rabbi Yose, who holds that the *kedusha* established by Ezra was never nullified by *Galut Titus*, and therefore *teruma* today carries *de-oraita* status. The *baraita* concerning the baskets, which assumes *teruma* is *de-rabbanan*, is the position of the *Rabbanan* who dispute Rabbi Yose — and Rabbi Yochanan accordingly dismisses it as inapplicable to his ruling.

## Rov & Derabbanan

### Rov question again

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בִּדְרַבָּנַן לָא בָּעֵינַן רִבּוּיָא? וְהָא תְּנַן: מִקְוֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה מְכֻוּוֹנוֹת, נָתַן סְאָה וְנָטַל סְאָה — כָּשֵׁר. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר שֵׁילָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד רוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection to Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion: **But** does **Rabbi Yoḥanan maintain** that **with regard to** a mixture containing an item that is forbidden **by rabbinic** law, **we do not require** the permitted portion of the mixture to be **greater** in quantity so that it can nullify the prohibited part? **Didn't we learn** otherwise in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 7:2): With regard to **a ritual bath that contains exactly forty *se'a*** of water, the minimal measure of a fit ritual bath, and **one added** to it **a *se'a*** of liquid other than water, **and** then **removed** from it **a *se'a*** of the mixture, the ritual bath remains **fit,** as the liquid that was added was nullified in the forty *se'a* of water, so that when one *se'a* of the mixture was removed there still remained the requisite forty *se'a* of fit water. **And Rabbi Yehuda bar Sheila said** that **Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that this *halakha* applies **up to the greater part** of the ritual bath.

The *gemara* now poses a follow-up question. Even granting that the *baraita* of the baskets reflects the *Rabbanan* rather than Rabbi Yose — fine. But Rabbi Yochanan still rules leniently in that *baraita*'s case: he does not require a *rov* of *chullin* over *teruma* in order to permit the mixture. If we are operating on the assumption that *teruma* is *de-rabbanan*, should he not at minimum require a *rov*? Why does Rabbi Yochanan permit without one?

### Mikveh drawn water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, דְּנִשְׁתַּיֵּיר רוּבּוֹ! לָא, דְּלָא נִשְׁקוֹל רוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **What, is it not that its greater part must remain,** i.e., that most of the original forty *se'a* of water must remain in the ritual bath? In other words, up to nineteen *se'a* of other liquids may be added to the ritual bath, with an equal quantity of the mixture then removed from it, and these other liquids are nullified by the ritual bath water. But if twenty *se'a* are removed, so that fit water no longer constitutes the majority, the ritual bath is no longer fit for use. Since certain liquids are unfit for a ritual bath only by rabbinic law, this shows that even with respect to rabbinic prohibitions the permitted portion of a mixture must be greater in quantity, so that it can nullify the prohibited part. The Gemara refutes this proof: **No,** it means **that its greater part must not be removed,** but if the water and other liquids are present in equal proportion, the ritual bath remains fit.

To resolve this, the *gemara* invokes a *mishna* in *Mikvaot*: a *mikveh* containing exactly forty *se'ah* — the minimum for a valid *mikveh* — into which one pours a *se'ah* of something problematic, such as fruit juice, and then removes a *se'ah* of the now-mixed water. Despite the fact that this process dilutes the proportion of valid *mikveh* water, the *mishna* rules: *kosher*. On this *mishna*, Rabbi Yochanan said — via the transmission of Rabbi Yehuda bar Shila citing Rav Assi — *ad rubbo*: this process may continue until the majority of the *mikveh* is no longer valid water. The reason Rabbi Yochanan permits up to that point — but not beyond — is that *mayim she'uvim*, drawn water, is a *de-rabbanan* problem, and therefore a *rov* of valid water is sufficient to maintain the *mikveh*'s status. This demonstrates that Rabbi Yochanan does hold that for *de-rabbanan* prohibitions, a *rov* suffices — but he does not require *more* than a *rov*, and in fact he permits up to but not exceeding that threshold.

### Tosafot kashya

*Tosafot* raises a significant difficulty on this point. The *mishna* in *Mikvaot* is not necessarily speaking about *mayim she'uvim* — drawn water — at all. It may be referring to fruit juice or similar liquids that invalidate a *mikveh* on a *de-oraita* level. If so, how can Rabbi Yochanan permit the *mikveh* to continue functioning when up to half its content consists of something that is *de-oraita* problematic? Rov should not suffice in a case of biblical prohibition. *Tosafot* acknowledges this as a genuine difficulty and offers answers that are not entirely satisfying. The question itself, however, is an important one for understanding the scope of Rabbi Yochanan's principle, and one should keep it in mind when applying his ruling more broadly.

For practical purposes, this issue does not arise in contemporary *mikveh* construction, as one does not pour fruit juice into a *mikveh*. But analytically, *Tosafot*'s challenge remains.

### Safek status vs event

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** an alternative explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion: **It is different here,** with regard to the case of two baskets, one of which contains *teruma*, **since it may be said: As I say** that the *teruma* fell into the *teruma* and the non-sacred produce fell into the non-sacred produce, it is therefore assumed that that is what actually took place, and therefore the permitted portion need not be the greater part of the mixture.

The *gemara* offers a second answer — *ve-iba'it eima* — which it presents as a fully acceptable resolution. In the case of the two baskets, Rabbi Yochanan is not relying on a *rov* at all. Rather, he is relying on the principle of *she'ani omer* — we can say that the *teruma* fell into the *teruma* basket and the *chullin* fell into the *chullin* basket. So long as this outcome is a genuine possibility, and there exist two matching pairs — two baskets, two *se'in* — we are entitled to invoke this reasoning without needing to establish a probabilistic majority.

This raises a natural question: why is this principle of *she'ani omer* not applied to the *androgynus* himself? If the *androgynus* is a *safek* as to his gender, could one not similarly say *she'ani omer* — perhaps he is male? The answer lies in a crucial distinction: in the case of the baskets, the *safek* concerns an *event* — which *se'ah* fell where — and it is a plausible and equal possibility that each fell into the right place. In the case of the *androgynus*, the *safek* is not about an event but about *status* — what kind of being he is. A *safek* about an ongoing ontological status is categorically different from a *safek* about a discrete occurrence, and the principle of *she'ani omer* does not extend from one to the other.

## Androgynus status

### Noseh vs Nisha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס נוֹשֵׂא! תְּנִי ״אִם נָשָׂא״. וְהָא ״נוֹשֵׂא״ קָתָנֵי! וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ: מַאי ״אֲבָל לֹא נִישָּׂא״? אֶלָּא מַאי ״נִישָּׂא״ — דִּיעֲבַד, ״נוֹשֵׂא״ נָמֵי — דִּיעֲבַד!
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in the mishna: **A hermaphrodite may marry** a woman. This formulation indicates that he may do so *ab initio*, as he is considered a full-fledged male. Accordingly, a hermaphrodite who is a priest should enable his wife to eat the breast and thigh of peace-offerings, as is maintained by Rabbi Yoḥanan, and against the opinion of Reish Lakish. The Gemara rejects this argument and suggests that the text be emended: **Teach** in the mishna: **If he married.** That is to say, if he married a woman, the marriage is valid and a bill of divorce is required, as it is possible that he is a male. But even if he married, he does not enable his wife to eat *teruma*, as he may be a female. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But doesn't** the mishna teach that **he may marry** a woman, which indicates that he may do so *ab initio*? The Gemara rejects this argument: **And according to your reasoning, what is** the meaning of the next clause: **But he may not be married** to a man? This teaching is certainly referring to a case after the fact, as, if he is a full-fledged male there would be no reason to say that he may not be married to a man *ab initio*. **Rather, what is** the meaning of: He may not **be married** to a man? It means that even **after the fact** the marriage is not valid. This being the case, when the mishna states that a hermaphrodite **may marry** a woman, it is **also** speaking **after the fact.**

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishna* itself to probe the precise status of the *androgynus*. The *mishna* states: *androgynus noseh* — an *androgynus* marries — *aval lo nisa* — but is not wedded. The *gemara* investigates whether *noseh* is intended *lechatchila* — as an initial, affirmative ruling — or only *be-di'avad* — retroactively valid after the fact. The language of *noseh* would seem to suggest an active, prospective permission, implying that the *androgynus* has a sufficiently clear male status to marry *lechatchila*. The *gemara* therefore tests whether *noseh* should be read as *im nasa* — if he married, it is valid — treating it as a *be-di'avad* formulation rather than a *lechatchila* one.

### Lechatchila vs Di'avad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי, לָא: ״נוֹשֵׂא״ — לְכַתְּחִלָּה מַשְׁמַע, ״אֲבָל לֹא נִישָּׂא״ — דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** It may be **said** in response: **No;** the words: **May marry, indicate** that a hermaphrodite may marry a woman ***ab initio*,** whereas the words: **But he may not be married,** mean that his marriage to a man is **not** valid **even after the fact.** Accordingly, the difficulty raised against Reish Lakish remains.

The *gemara* asks: but the *mishna* says *noseh* — the active form. If you reinterpret that as *di'avad*, what then do you do with *lo nisa* — the passive, "is not wedded"? That formulation seems itself to be *be-di'avad*: even if someone married him in the feminine role, it is not valid. This would yield a reading where *noseh* is *lechatchila* and *lo nisa* is *di'avad*, or alternatively, both are *di'avad* — meaning *noseh be-di'avad* and even *di'avad* he cannot be wedded. Perhaps the *mishna* operates on a *lo zu af zu* principle: even the more lenient retroactive case is not valid. Ultimately the *gemara* leaves this reading under discussion.

### Sekila & gender

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו סְקִילָה כַּזָּכָר, מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סַפּוֹקֵי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this understanding: **But from** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches** in **the latter clause: Rabbi Eliezer says** that if a man had intercourse with **a hermaphrodite,** he **is liable to** receive the punishment of **stoning on his account as if** he had relations with **a male,** this proves **by inference that the first *tanna* is uncertain** as to whether or not a hermaphrodite is considered a full-fledged male, and this presents a difficulty to Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion.

The *mishna* continues with Rabbi Eliezer's ruling: *chayavin alav sekila ke-zachar* — one who has relations with a married *androgynus* incurs *sekila*, stoning, just as with a male. Rabbi Eliezer's formulation strongly implies that the *androgynus* is treated as fully male. The *gemara* infers from the fact that Rabbi Eliezer found it necessary to state this explicitly that the *tanna kamma* is apparently uncertain — *mispaka lei* — regarding the *androgynus*'s status.

### Sekila mishnei mekomot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין לְמָר בֵּין לְמָר מִפְשָׁט פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ סְקִילָה מִשְּׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת. דְּמָר סָבַר: חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו סְקִילָה מִשְּׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת, וּמָר סָבַר: כְּזָכָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: No, it **is obvious both to this Master and to that Master** that a hermaphrodite is deemed a full-fledged male. The practical difference **between them** relates to the question of whether one is liable to receive the punishment of **stoning** for intercourse with him at only one place or **at two places. As one Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that **one is liable to** be punished with **stoning on** a hermaphrodite's **account** for intercourse **at two places,** whether he penetrated him anally, in the manner of homosexual intercourse, or through his female organ. Since the hermaphrodite is deemed a male, one is liable to be punished with stoning for intercourse at either place. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds** that one is liable to be punished with stoning for relations with a hermaphrodite only if he penetrated him anally, **as if** he were **a male.**

The *gemara* rejects this inference. In fact, *bein le-mar bein le-mar mipshat pshita lei* — everyone agrees that the *androgynus* is definitively male; the dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer is not about his fundamental status. Rather, the disagreement is *sekila mishnei mekomot* — whether *sekila* applies depending on the nature of the sexual act. The *tanna kamma* holds that regardless of which form of relations one has with the *androgynus*, *sekila* applies. Rabbi Eliezer holds *ke-zachar* — specifically in the masculine mode, i.e., *bimkom zachrut*. If one has relations through the feminine aspect of the *androgynus*, Rabbi Eliezer would not impose *sekila* for that act. This, and not any uncertainty about maleness, is the substance of their disagreement.

## Androgynus category

### Third category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: לֵיתַהּ לְמַתְנִיתִין מִקַּמֵּי בָּרַיְיתָא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — בְּרִיָּה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ הוּא, וְלֹא הִכְרִיעוּ בּוֹ חֲכָמִים אִם זָכָר אִם נְקֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** **The mishna** here, which states that according to Rabbi Yosei a priest who is a hermaphrodite enables his wife to eat *teruma*, **is not** to be relied upon **in the presence of a *baraita*** that teaches otherwise. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: A hermaphrodite is a creature unto himself, and the Sages did not determine whether he is a male or a female.** He is consequently prohibited from marrying a woman, and if he does so he does not enable her to eat *teruma*.

Rav now intervenes with a striking challenge. He declares: *leita matnita kemei baraita* — this *mishna* does not stand in light of a *baraita* I can bring. The *baraita* cites Rabbi Yose: *androgynus beriya bifnei atzma hu, lo hichri'u bo chachamim* — the *androgynus* is a creature unto itself, a third independent category; the *chachamim* have not determined it to be male or female. This directly contradicts the thrust of our *mishna*, which treats the *androgynus* as essentially male. After the extended discussion in which Rabbi Yose was portrayed as holding that the *androgynus* is male, Rav now produces a *baraita* in which Rabbi Yose himself declares the opposite.

### Mishna vs Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה לֵיתַהּ לְבָרַיְיתָא מִקַּמֵּי מַתְנִיתִין! מִדְּשַׁבְקֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לְבַר זוּגֵיהּ — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הֲדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary,** say that **the *baraita* is not** to be relied upon **in the presence of the mishna** here, as *baraitot* are generally considered less authoritative than *mishnayot*. The Gemara answers: **From** the fact **that Rabbi Yosei left his colleague,** Rabbi Shimon, as the mishna's ruling is attributed to both Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon whereas the teaching of the *baraita* is reported only in the name of Rabbi Yosei, **learn from this** that Rabbi Yosei **retracted** his original opinion that he had maintained together with Rabbi Shimon and reached a different conclusion.

The *gemara* pushes back: *aderaba leita le-baraita dekamei matnitin* — on the contrary, why should the *baraita* take precedence over the *mishna*? This question reflects a broader principle that deserves attention: in many places in the *gemara*, it is entirely clear that a *mishna* and a *baraita* are considered *shkulim* — completely equal in authority. The fact that a ruling was included in the *mishna* rather than left as a *baraita* does not automatically grant it superiority. One cannot simply say "it is in the *mishna*" as a trump card.

### Oral canonization

This brings us to an important dimension of the *mishna*'s character. The *mishna* was not, in the first instance, a written document. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi canonized a certain collection of oral material as the authoritative code — a set text that was recited orally and transmitted accordingly. It remained in that form for centuries. The *Geonim* in their *yeshivot* still used oral *tannaim* to recite the *mishna*, even as manuscript fragments were beginning to circulate. The earliest manuscript evidence of the *mishna* as a written text dates to approximately the 10th century, with one fragment from *Chullin* traceable to the 8th. The nature of the *Seder Olam* and other early compilations similarly reflects a world of oral, canonized texts.

The *Tosefta* exists precisely to preserve the *baraitot* that were not incorporated into the *mishna* — another canonized but secondary collection that supplements and parallels the *mishna* without superseding it. Not every *baraita* appears in the *Tosefta*, but everything in the *Tosefta* is a *baraita*, and both carry the same fundamental standing as transmitted *tannaitic* teaching.

### Changed position

The *gemara* resolves the conflict between *mishna* and *baraita* by offering a decisive inference: *mideshavkei Rabbi Yose le-var zugya shma mina hadar bei* — from the fact that Rabbi Yose in the *mishna* formulated his ruling together with Rabbi Shimon, whereas in the *baraita* he speaks alone, we infer that he changed his mind. In the *mishna*, Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon together implied that the *androgynus* is male. In the *baraita*, Rabbi Yose appears independently and declares the *androgynus* to be a *beriya bifnei atzmah*. His separation from Rabbi Shimon suggests he revised his position.

*Tosafot* raises a counterpoint: perhaps Rabbi Yose held his own view originally and then temporarily joined Rabbi Shimon, before reverting to his independent position. *Tosafot* acknowledges they have no clean answer to this and flag it as a genuine difficulty.

## Mishna authority

### Shmuel preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לֵיתַהּ לְבָרַיְיתָא מִקַּמֵּי מַתְנִיתִין. אַדְּרַבָּה, לֵיתָא לְמַתְנִיתִין מִקַּמֵּי בָּרַיְיתָא, דְּהָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּחָיֵישׁ לִיחִידָאָה! הָנֵי מִילֵּי, כִּי לָא מִתְעַקְרָא מַתְנִיתִין, אֲבָל כִּי מִתְעַקְרָא מַתְנִיתִין — לָא חָיֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said** the reverse: **The *baraita* is not** to be relied upon **in the presence of the mishna** here. The Gemara asks: **On the contrary,** say that **the mishna** here **is not** to be relied upon **in the presence of the *baraita*, as we** have **heard that Shmuel takes into consideration** even **an individual** dissenting opinion appearing in a *baraita*. The Gemara answers: **This applies only when the mishna** itself is **not** thereby **uprooted,** as the *baraita* merely adds to it. **But when the mishna is uprooted** by a contrary statement taught in a *baraita*, **he does not take** it **into consideration.**

The *gemara* now records Shmuel's general ruling on the relationship between *mishna* and *baraita*: *leita le-baraita dekamei matnitin* — the *baraita* does not stand before the *mishna*. Shmuel grants the *mishna* authority over a contradicting *baraita*. This is grounded in Shmuel's broader principle: he is generally *choshesh* — concerned — for a *da'at yachid*, a minority individual opinion, and is inclined to rule *le-chumra* in accordance with it.

### Yachida in Mishna

However, the *gemara* qualifies this principle. Shmuel's stringency toward a *da'at yachid* applies specifically when that individual opinion appears *in the mishna*. When the *da'at yachid* appears only in a *baraita* and contradicts a *mishna* that records the more general, lenient ruling, Shmuel does not follow the *yachid*. In such a case, the combined weight of the *mishna*'s general position and its canonical status overcomes the stringent individual view preserved only in a *baraita*.

## Mishna authority…

### Halacha ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס וּבְהַרְכָּבָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּקוֹשִׁי וּבְקִידּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages of **the school of Rav said in the name of Rav** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** both **with regard to** the *halakha* of **a hermaphrodite and with regard to** the *halakha* of **grafting. And Shmuel says:** The *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion **with regard to** the laws of **protracted labor and forfeiture.**

From this framework flows the practical *halacha*: we follow Rabbi Yose on the question of the *androgynus* and on the question of *harkava* — grafting. The resolution of the conflict between *mishna* and *baraita* on the *androgynus*, and Shmuel's rule about the authority of the *mishna* over a contradicting *baraita*, lead to this conclusion.

## Rabbi Yose rulings

### Androgynus & graft

The school of Rav (*bei Rav*) states in the name of Rav: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yose be-androgynus u-ve-harkava* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yose with regard to the *androgynus* and with regard to grafting. It should be noted that *bei Rav* — the school or *beit midrash* of Rav — does not always transmit Rav's own positions exclusively; there is considerable cross-pollination between the academies of Sura and Nehardea, and a transmission *mishmo de-Rav* in this context signals the chain of transmission rather than a rigid institutional allegiance.

### Birth & field

Shmuel offers parallel rulings in two other domains: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yose be-koshi u-ve-kidush* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yose in the case of *koshi* (the woman experiencing difficulty in childbirth) and in the case of *kidush* (the mixed or hybrid field, referring to *kilei ha-kerem*, the prohibition of mixed plantings in a vineyard, *de-kadesh amra Torah*).

## Grafting timing

### Shmitta restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: אֵין נוֹטְעִין וְאֵין מַבְרִיכִין וְאֵין מַרְכִּיבִין עֶרֶב שְׁבִיעִית פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה. וְאִם נָטַע וְהִבְרִיךְ וְהִרְכִּיב — יַעֲקוֹר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַרְכָּבָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים — שׁוּב אֵינָהּ קוֹלֶטֶת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: שְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The *halakha* of **a hermaphrodite** is **that which we** just **said,** that he is considered a creature unto himself (Rabbeinu Ḥananel). The *halakha* of **grafting** is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 2:6): **One may not plant, or sink** the shoot of a vine into the ground, **or graft** a shoot onto a tree on the **eve of the Sabbatical Year less than thirty days before Rosh HaShana,** lest it take root in the seventh year. **And if he planted or sank or grafted, he must uproot** it. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Any graft that does not take root in three days will never take root,** and therefore it is prohibited to plant only for three days before the start of the Sabbatical Year. **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say** that it takes **two weeks** for a plant to take root.

The *mishna* concerning *harkava* establishes the following rule: one may not plant, clear land for agricultural use (*mavrichin* — understood here as bending and inserting a vine into the ground), or graft in the *Elul* immediately preceding the *shmitta* year if one is doing so fewer than thirty days before Rosh Hashanah. This period of thirty days constitutes *tosefet shvi'it* — the additional pre-*shmitta* period in which such activities are forbidden. If one did plant, clear, or graft during this prohibited window, one must uproot what was done: *ya'akor*.

### Graft takeover time

The *mishna* records a dispute about how long it takes for a graft to "take" — to bond with and draw sustenance from the host plant. Rabbi Yehuda holds that a graft takes only three days to take hold; if it does not take within three days, it will not take at all. Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, hold that a graft requires two weeks — *shtei shavuot* — to fully take hold. This same dispute appears in the tractate of Rosh Hashanah (10b).

### Rav Nachman formula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שְׁלֹשִׁים — צָרִיךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים ושְׁלֹשִׁים. לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שְׁלֹשָׁה — צָרִיךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה ושְׁלֹשִׁים. לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת — צָרִיךְ שְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת וּשְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: According to** the first *tanna*, **who says thirty** days, he means that **thirty** days are **required** for the tree to take root **and** another **thirty** days are required for the addition to the Sabbatical Year, as the prohibitions of the Sabbatical Year apply already during the last thirty days of the sixth year. It is consequently prohibited to plant sixty days before Rosh HaShana. And similarly, **according to** Rabbi Yehuda, **who says three** days, he means that **three** days **are required** for the tree to take root **and** another **thirty** days are required for the addition to the Sabbatical Year. And so too, **according to** Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon, **who say two weeks,** they mean that **two weeks are required** for the tree to take root **and** another **thirty** days are required for the addition to the Sabbatical Year. Rav rules on this matter in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei.

Rav Nachman, citing Rabba bar Avuha, derives a practical ruling from this dispute. The thirty-day prohibition of *tosefet shvi'it* is measured against the point at which the grafting process is fully complete. Accordingly, one must count backward not merely thirty days but thirty days *plus* the time it takes for the graft to take hold. According to the view that a graft takes three days: one must graft no fewer than thirty-three days before Rosh Hashanah. According to the view of Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon that it takes two weeks: one must graft no fewer than thirty plus fourteen days — forty-four days — before Rosh Hashanah. The thirty-day figure in the *mishna* thus represents the fixed *tosefet shvi'it* window, to which the grafting period must be added.

## Difficult birth

### Zava status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּקוֹשִׁי וְקִידּוּשׁ. קוֹשִׁי — דִּתְנַן: כַּמָּה יְהֵא קִישּׁוּיָהּ — רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אוֹ אַרְבָּעִים אוֹ חֲמִשִּׁים יוֹם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּיהּ חׇדְשָׁהּ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: אֵין קִישּׁוּי יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said** that the *halakha* is ruled in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei **with regard to protracted labor and proscription** as well. The Gemara explains: What is the case of **protracted labor? As we learned** in a mishna (*Nidda* 36b): Ordinarily a woman who experiences a flow of blood on three consecutive days during a time of the month when she does not expect to experience menstrual bleeding is rendered ritually impure as a *zava*. However, if she experiences such bleeding while she is in protracted labor, the bleeding is attributed to her labor, and she is governed by the *halakhot* of a woman after childbirth. The *tanna'im* disagree about the question: **How long is** a woman's period of **protracted labor?** For what period of time prior to her giving birth is the bleeding attributed to her labor? **Rabbi Meir says:** It is up to **forty or fifty days** before she delivers. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Her month suffices for her,** i.e., it is from the beginning of the month in which she gives birth. **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say: Protracted labor does not last for more than two weeks.** Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Yosei in this case.

The ruling that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yose in *koshi* directs us to a *mishna* that addresses the following situation. A woman who sees blood outside her normal menstrual cycle is subject to the laws of *zivah*. If she sees such blood on three consecutive days, she becomes a *zava* and must count seven clean days before immersing. These *halachot* are *de-oraita* in character, though less practically operative today.

### Pregnant woman labor

A pregnant woman in labor, however, presents a distinct case. Blood that appears as a result of contractions — *koshi*, the difficulty of labor — is not counted as *zivah* bleeding, even if it is continuous. The blood is attributed to the birthing process rather than to the condition of *zov*. Moreover, this exemption applies even if the blood is continuous from its onset until the moment of birth: a woman who bleeds continuously from the onset of labor contractions until delivery is not classified as a *yoledet be-zov* — one who gives birth while in a state of *zivah* — because the blood is subsumed under the birthing process.

The question then becomes: how far back before the actual birth can this reasoning extend? If a pregnant woman in, say, her sixth month begins to bleed continuously all the way until delivery, does the entire period of bleeding count as *koshi*? This cannot extend indefinitely, because attributing months of continuous bleeding to labor contractions defies biological reality.

### Bleeding duration limits

The *mishna* records three positions on this question. Rabbi Meir holds that either forty or fifty days before the birth is the outer limit: if the bleeding began continuously that far back and continued until delivery, we attribute the entire period to *koshi* and she is not a *zava*. Rabbi Yehuda says: *daya chodsha* — a month suffices; Rashi understands this as measured from Rosh Chodesh of the month in which she gives birth, though the plain meaning could equally indicate a calendar month before delivery. Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon hold: *ein kishuy yoter mi-shtei shabbatot* — *koshi* can extend no more than two weeks. Beyond two weeks before the birth, bleeding is no longer attributed to labor contractions, and the woman would be subject to the ordinary *halachot* of *zivah*.
