# Zevachim 59 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mizbeach Location

### Fire & Four Amot

The daf opens with an unresolved question carried over from the bottom of 58b: where precisely was the *mizbeach* located within the *azarah*? Was it entirely in the north, entirely in the south, or split evenly between the two halves? The half-and-half arrangement has considerable intuitive appeal, yet the Gemara has been examining a complicating datum — the fire used to light the *ketoret*. Twice daily, when the *kohen* brought fire into the *kodesh* for the *ketoret* offering, and when he carried it out on *Shabbat* for the burning of the *levonah*, that fire was positioned in the southwest corner of the altar's top, four *amot* removed from the edge. The question is what this placement implies about the altar's overall location: if the entire *mizbeach* was in the north, it means the fire could be no more than four *amot* from the southern ledge; if the entire *mizbeach* was in the south, it means the fire had to be at least four *amot* from that edge. The counting of that edge itself is also in dispute. The Gemara did not reach a conclusion on this basis, so it turns to other evidence.

### R. Yosi HaGlili

Rav Shravya introduces a different attribution and a different proof. The position that the *mizbeach* was entirely in the north, he says, belongs to Rabbi Yosi HaGlili — and the evidence comes not from the placement of the fire but from a *braita* that addresses the location of the *kiyor*, the laver.

### Kiyor Placement

Two verses create an apparent tension. One states: *venatata et hakiyor bein ohel moed uvein hamizbeach* — the *kiyor* is to be placed between the *ohel moed* and the *mizbeach*. A second verse states: *ve-et mizbeach ha'olah petach ohel moed* — the altar of the *olah* stands at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. From the juxtaposition, Rabbi Yosi HaGlili derives that while the *kiyor* must be in the space between the *mizbeach* and the *ohel moed*, it cannot itself stand *petach ohel moed*, because that position belongs exclusively to the *mizbeach*. The verse implies that only the *mizbeach* faces the doorway — not the *kiyor*.

This has a compelling logic beyond the mere textual inference. The entire zone designated as *petach ohel moed* represents a kind of access — an approach toward the *shechinah* residing within. One would not want anything interrupting that line of sight, even symbolically and metaphorically. At the same time, the verse distinguishes between the *ohel moed* as a structure and the *petach ohel moed* as its doorway, so the prohibition applies specifically to the opening, not the building's full extent.

To visualize this: the *heichal* is ten *amot* wide at its opening and the *ulam* is twenty *amot* wide. The *mizbeach* is thirty-two *amot* across at its base. If the *mizbeach* is centered, it extends eleven *amot* beyond the *ulam*'s walls on each side. Placing the *kiyor* somewhere between the *mizbeach* and the *ohel moed*, away from the direct line of the door, seems perfectly achievable — there is ample space in the *azarah* for such a placement.

### Mizbeach Petach

The Gemara cites the essential framing of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's *braita*: *mizbeach petach ohel moed velo kiyor petach ohel moed*. Where, then, was the *kiyor* placed? *Bein ha'ulam velamizbeach* — between the *ulam* and the *mizbeach* — *mashuch kima klafei hadarom*, drawn slightly southward. The Gemara's assumption is that "drawn southward" means drawn below the southernmost edge of the *mizbeach*. This raises an obvious question: why does it need to be pulled that far? There is clearly space between the *mizbeach* and the *ohel moed* without interfering with the doorway, regardless of whether the altar is positioned all in the north, all in the south, or split between the two.

### North Clear Only

The Gemara works through the possibilities systematically. If the entire *mizbeach* is in the south, there is obviously room in the southern portion of the *azarah* to place the *kiyor* between the wall of the *heichal* and the *mizbeach* without blocking the door. The same holds even if one considers the *kedushat ha'ulam* and the *kedushat ha'heichal* to be a single unified sanctity — so that the *kiyor* cannot stand opposite the door of the *ulam* either — because there are still six *amot* of wall on each side of the *ulam*'s opening, and room enough to position the *kiyor* there without interference.

Similarly, if the *mizbeach* straddles the midline, numerous positions remain available. The Gemara considers every combination: *kulei mizbeach bedarom kaei*, *chetzyo batzafon vechetzyo bedarom*, *kedushat heichal ve-ulam chada hi* — and in each case concludes that there would be room to place the *kiyor* appropriately without pulling it all the way below the southern edge of the *mizbeach*.

The only configuration that forces the *kiyor* to be drawn southward beyond the *mizbeach*'s southern edge is if the *mizbeach* is entirely in the north and there is a prohibition against placing anything else in the north at all. In that case, there is simply nowhere to put the *kiyor* in the northern zone, and it must be relocated entirely to the south. The Gemara concludes: *lav mishum dekasavar kulei mizbeach batzafon* — the reason the *kiyor* must be pulled south is that Rabbi Yosi HaGlili holds the entire *mizbeach* is in the north, and the north must remain clear.

But clear of what? The verse says *al yerech hamizbeach tzafona* — one slaughters to the north of the *mizbeach*. From the word *tzafona* the Gemara derives: *shetzafon panui miklum* — the north must be free of everything. One might ask: if the only valid location for *shechita* is directly to the north of the *mizbeach* itself, why does the space between the *ulam* and the *mizbeach* also have to remain clear? That is a genuine difficulty. Yet the Gemara does assume that this inner gap must also be free.

It is worth noting an important precision raised in discussion: the prohibition derived from the verse is not that the *kiyor* cannot stand *in front of the door*, but rather that it cannot interpose between the door and the *mizbeach*. If the entire *mizbeach* is in the north, the *kiyor* could technically be positioned just below the *mizbeach*'s southern edge and still be in front of the door without violating the rule — because it would not be standing between the door and the *mizbeach*. Practically speaking, one would still want to move it further south to avoid obstruction, but there is a halachic distinction worth preserving.

### Tosfot's Distinction

Tosafot engages with a complication. The verse says the north must be clear — *panui miklum* — yet in practice the north was not at all empty. There were tables, hooks, and posts there — the entire apparatus for *shechita*. If the *tzafona* verse demands total clearance, how can all that equipment be present?

Tosafot resolves this by clarifying the scope of the requirement. When the Torah demands that the north be clear, the purpose is to preserve adequate space for the *shechita* of *kodshei kodashim*. Since all *kodshei kodashim* must be slaughtered in the north and that is the only zone available for such sacrifices, the Torah insists that extraneous objects not crowd it out. But items that are themselves required *for* the *shechita* — tables, hooks, posts — are not extraneous at all. They serve the very act the verse is protecting space for, and therefore *tzorechei shechita* may be present. The prohibition applies to objects with no function in the slaughter process, like the *kiyor*.

On a related point, Tosafot notes that even if the *mizbeach* of Moshe was a portable vessel — a *kli* — rather than a fixed structure, and was itself in the north, that does not contradict the rule. The principle that the north must be clear of vessels refers to vessels other than the *mizbeach* itself. *Danin kli mikli ve-ein danin kli mibinyan* — one draws an analogy between a vessel and a vessel, not between a vessel and a permanent structure. The *mizbeach* in the *beit hamikdash* was a permanent structure and its presence in the north defines rather than violates the requirement.

### R. Eliezer ben Yaakov

Having established Rabbi Yosi HaGlili's position — that the entire *mizbeach* is in the north — the Gemara asks: who holds the opposing view that the *mizbeach* is entirely in the south? That is Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, as derived from a *braita*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov reads the word *tzafona* not merely as requiring clearance of vessels but as requiring clearance of everything — *afilu min hamizbeach*. The north must be free even of the *mizbeach* itself. Therefore the entire altar must be in the south. From a functional standpoint this actually has some appeal: if *kodshei kodashim* are slaughtered in the north, placing the *mizbeach* entirely in the south maximizes the space available for that slaughter rather than the *mizbeach* consuming part of that zone.

## Damaged Altar

### Rav's Rule

The Gemara now shifts to a related but distinct topic. Rav states: *mizbeach shenifgam kol hakodashim shenishchatu sham psulin* — if the *mizbeach* was chipped or damaged, any sacrifices whose *shechita* took place from the moment of the damage onward are invalid. The *shechita* itself is rendered *pasul*. The question of retroactivity — if the chip is discovered midday, whether earlier slaughterings of that day are presumed invalid — follows the usual rules of *chazakah*, and the Gemara does not pursue it here.

### Lost Verse

What is striking about Rav's statement is his candid admission: *mikra hu bi'yadeinu ve'shachachnuhu* — we have a verse that proves this, but we have forgotten it. The *halacha* has been retained in memory while the scriptural source that generated it has slipped away. There is something symbolically resonant about this. In the world of *kodashim*, we have held onto the practical rulings, the operational memory, while the living textual experience behind those rulings has become harder to access — much like the famous parable of successive generations losing first the words, then the melody, then the dance, until all that remains is the story about the story.

### Rav Kahana's Source

When Rav Kahana traveled to Eretz Yisrael — going back, as it were, toward the source — he found Rav Shimon the son of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, who transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yosi the missing derivation.

### Ve-Zavachta Alav

The verse is: *ve-zavachta alav et olotecha ve-et shelammecha* — you shall slaughter upon it your *olot* and your *shelamim*. The Gemara asks: *ve-chi alav hu zove'ach?* Does one literally slaughter on the altar? This verse is, of course, cited elsewhere in our *masechta* as a basis for the ruling that *shechita* may be performed on the top surface of the altar — a hyper-literal reading. But the Gemara here recovers the more contextual reading: *alav* means in reference to it, in correspondence with it, in the context of its standing whole before you. The *shechita* is performed with the altar as its frame of reference. From this follows: *ella keshe'hu shalem ve-lo keshe'hu chaser* — the slaughter must occur when the altar is complete, not when it is deficient. An incomplete altar is, in a meaningful sense, not fully an altar, and the *shechita* performed in its shadow lacks what it requires.

### R. Yochanan Dichuy

Rabbi Yochanan extends the ruling further. He states: *echad zeh ve-echad zeh psulin* — both an animal slaughtered while the altar was chipped, and an animal that was merely sanctified during that period and slaughtered afterward once the altar was repaired, are invalid. The mechanism is the principle of *dichuy ba'alei chayim*: even a living animal can be "pushed away" from its sanctified status. If during the period when the *mizbeach* was chipped a sanctified animal existed and could not be offered, it acquired a stigma of rejection — *dichuy* — that persists even after the altar is repaired and the animal remains alive.

Practically, this would mean that whenever the *mizbeach* sustained damage, some form of notice would have to be given to everyone who had sanctified an animal prior to that moment, informing them that their *korban* is now invalid — much like the *eruv* notice in a community today.

### Rav vs. R. Yochanan

The Gemara identifies the root of the dispute: Rav holds *ba'alei chayim einam nidchin* — a living creature is not subject to *dichuy*. Because something living is dynamic rather than static in its identity, a period of disqualification does not permanently stigmatize it. As long as the altar is repaired before the *shechita* takes place, the animal may still be offered. Rabbi Yochanan holds *ba'alei chayim nidchin* — even a living sanctified animal can be pushed away by a period of disqualification.

## Damaged Altar (II)

### Before Altar Built

A *braita* is introduced that appears to support Rabbi Yochanan: all animals sanctified before the *mizbeach* was built, and offered after it was built, are *psulin*. This seems to confirm that sanctification followed by a period of inability to offer — even because no altar yet existed — permanently invalidates the animal.

### Emendation

The Gemara presses on the *braita*'s language. *Ad shelo nivneh hamizbeach* — before the altar was built — would represent *dichuy mei'ikara*, a case where the animal was never fit to begin with, which is potentially a less severe form of rejection than *nir'ah ve-nidcheh*, where the animal was once suitable and then disqualified. If the *braita* means animals sanctified before the Second Temple's altar was constructed, that is merely *dichuy mei'ikara*, and it is not obvious that even Rabbi Yochanan holds this invalidates them.

The Gemara therefore reads the *braita* differently: it must refer not to *ad shelo nivneh* — before it was built — but to *ad shelo nifgam* — before it was chipped. Animals sanctified before the altar was chipped and then the altar was chipped: that is *nir'ah ve-nidcheh*, the animal was once fit and was then pushed away. With this reading the *braita* supports Rabbi Yochanan's position that *ba'alei chayim nidchin*. However, since the Gemara has had to substantially emend the *braita*'s text, it concludes that the *braita* can no longer serve as a clean proof for either side — once editing begins, the other side can propose its own reading.

## Golden Altar Exception

### Mizbeach She-Ne'ekar

A further challenge to Rav's ruling: Rav Gidal teaches in the name of Rav himself that if the golden altar — the inner *mizbeach* used for the *ketoret* — was uprooted, one may still burn *ketoret* on the spot where it had stood. The floor of the *kodesh*, in other words, retains a sanctity sufficient to permit offering *ketoret* even without the physical altar present. This seems to undercut the very premise that the altar must be whole and present for a valid offering.

### Blood vs. Incense

Rava resolves the tension by invoking a distinction he draws in a different context: *modeh haya Rabbi Yehuda be-damim* — in matters of blood, different rules apply. The same principle operates here. When Rav rules that the *mizbeach* must be present and whole for a valid offering, he is speaking of the blood-related service — the *shechita* and the *zerikat ha-dam* on the outer altar — where the physical reality of the *mizbeach* is constitutive of the act. The *ketoret*, by contrast, is not blood-dependent; in that realm one can perhaps rely on the sanctity of the space rather than the physical object. Hence *modeh Rav be-damim*: Rav himself would agree that when it comes to blood, the *mizbeach* is indispensable, while for *ketoret* the floor may suffice.

## Temple Dedication

### Bayom Hahu Kidesh

The Gemara now turns to the source of Rava's invocation of Rabbi Yehuda's distinction between blood and *ketoret*, which emerges from a *braita* about Shlomo's dedication of the Temple. The verse states: *bayom hahu kidesh hamelech et toch ha'chatzer hapnimit ki mizbeach hanechoshet asher lifnei Hashem katan me-hachil* — on that day the king sanctified the inner courtyard, because the copper altar was too small to contain all the sacrifices he was bringing. Shlomo offered twenty-two thousand *shelamim* on the day of dedication. The *braita* records a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi about what this verse means and what Shlomo actually did.

Rabbi Yehuda reads the verse as written: Shlomo sanctified the courtyard itself — the ground of the *azarah* — to serve as a sacrificial surface, because even the new altar he had built was too small to handle the volume of offerings. On Rabbi Yehuda's reading, *mizbeach hanechoshet* refers not to Moshe's original copper altar but to its replacement, the new altar of Shlomo, which also fell short.

### R. Yosi Rebuttal

Rabbi Yosi challenges this reading by doing the arithmetic. He notes that at a different occasion the verse records Shlomo offering one thousand *olot* on the original copper altar of Moshe. Now at the dedication he offers twenty-two thousand *shelamim* — twenty-two times as many. Rabbi Yosi then computes the usable surface of each altar. The altar of Moshe is described in the Torah as five *amot* by five *amot*. Taking that literally, and subtracting an *amah* on each side for the corners and an *amah* on each side for the *sovev*, the usable space at the top is one *amah* by one *amah* — a single square *amah*. The new altar of Shlomo, by contrast, was thirty-two *amot* at its base, twenty-eight at its second ledge, and at its top — after subtracting similarly — approximately twenty by twenty *amot* according to Rashi, giving four hundred square *amot* of usable space. That is four hundred times the usable surface of Moshe's altar, far more than the twenty-two-fold increase in offerings. There was no need to sanctify the courtyard floor.

For Rabbi Yosi, then, the verse means that Shlomo sanctified the new altar — *toch ha'chatzer* refers to the new *mizbeach* that stood within the courtyard — and *mizbeach hanechoshet* refers literally to the original copper altar of Moshe, which genuinely was too small, necessitating the construction and dedication of the new one.

### R. Yehuda Too Small

The Gemara asks: given Rabbi Yosi's arithmetic, why does Rabbi Yehuda insist the new altar was not big enough? The answer is that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi have a deeper, prior dispute about the dimensions of Moshe's altar — a dispute that fundamentally changes the ratio.

### Dimensions Debate

The Torah describes the altar of Moshe as *chamesh amot orech ve-chamesh amot rochav* — five *amot* long and five *amot* wide — and *shalosh amot komaso* — three *amot* high — and adds the word *ravu'a*, square. Rabbi Yosi reads these numbers literally: five by five by three. The usable top surface is one square *amah* after accounting for the corners and the *sovev*, so the new altar of Shlomo at four hundred square *amot* is indeed four hundred times more capable.

### Measured from Center

Rabbi Yehuda invokes a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *ravu'a* appears here regarding Moshe's altar and also in the book of Yechezkel regarding the future altar. In Yechezkel, the measurements are taken *me'emtzi'ato* — from the center outward. The verse in Yechezkel specifies twelve *amot* in each direction; the additional phrase *al arba'at reva'av* — across all four sides — teaches that the measurement emanates from the center point, going twelve *amot* in each of the four directions, producing a twenty-four *amah* altar. Rabbi Yehuda applies this same principle to the word *ravu'a* by Moshe's altar: *chamesh amot* means five *amot* measured from the center in each direction, producing an altar ten *amot* by ten *amot*. After subtracting the corners and the *sovev*, the usable space is six by six — thirty-six square *amot* — compared to the one square *amah* of Rabbi Yosi's reading. Shlomo's new altar at four hundred square *amot* is approximately eleven times larger, not four hundred times. Since one thousand offerings were brought on a single square *amah* and twenty-two thousand offerings were needed, eleven times the capacity is insufficient. Hence Rabbi Yehuda's conclusion stands: the new altar was too small, and Shlomo had to sanctify the courtyard floor itself.

### Ravu'a Height Double

Rabbi Yosi now responds. He agrees that the *gezeirah shavah* from *ravu'a* in Yechezkel teaches something — but says it teaches something about the *height*, not the width. The *braita* records: Rabbi Yosi says the word *ravu'a* appearing here corresponds to the golden altar of the *mishkan*, where the dimensions are one *amah* by one *amah* by two *amot* — the height is double the other two dimensions. *Mah lehalon govah pi shnayim ke-arko* — just as there the height is twice the length and width, *af kan pi shnayim ke-arko* — so too here, the height of Moshe's altar is twice its five *amot*, making it ten *amot* high. The three stated in the verse does not represent the total height but the height of a particular section above the *sovev*.

### Exposure Concern

Rabbi Yehuda objects to this reading with a practical challenge: *efshar kohen omed al gabei mizbeach va'avodah beyado ve-chol ha'am ro'im oto mibachutz?* — is it possible that the *kohen* stands atop a ten-*amah* altar performing the service, and all the people outside can see him? The *kla'im* — the curtains surrounding the *chatzer* — are only five *amot* high. A *kohen* standing ten *amot* off the ground would be towering some fifteen feet in the air, fully visible to everyone outside. The entire purpose of the enclosure is to preserve the intimate character of the *avodah*, to prevent it from becoming a public spectacle. A ten-*amah* altar reduces those curtains to irrelevance. Rabbi Yehuda's altar — wide but only three *amot* high — keeps the *kohen* largely within the enclosure, with perhaps only the very top of his head visible above the curtains.

### Curtains Fifteen Amot

Rabbi Yosi responds: the same method that expanded the height of the altar from three to ten *amot* can be applied to the curtains. The verse describing the *mishkan* says that the *kla'im* surround both the *mishkan* and the *mizbeach* — and since the *mishkan* itself was ten *amot* high, the curtains surrounding it must have been at least ten *amot*. Furthermore, a verse explicitly mentions *kla'im chamesh esreh amah al hakatef* — curtains fifteen *amot* along the shoulder. Rabbi Yosi reads this not as a measurement of width but of height: the curtains are fifteen *amot* tall. When the same verse elsewhere says *chamesh amot*, that refers to the portion that rises five *amot* above the top of whatever they surround — five *amot* above the ten-*amah* *mizbeach*, giving fifteen *amot* total. Similarly, the three *amot* in the Torah's description of the altar's height means three *amot* above the *sovev*, the walkway partway up the altar's face.

On this reading, Rabbi Yosi has transformed three stated *amot* into ten, and five stated *amot* of curtain height into fifteen. Rabbi Yehuda, for his part, has transformed five stated *amot* of altar width into ten. Both positions involve departures from a plain reading, but in opposite directions — Rabbi Yehuda makes the altar wide, Rabbi Yosi makes it tall. These strikingly different proportions have direct implications for the capacity calculations that determine whether Shlomo needed to sanctify the courtyard floor, which in turn determines whether blood service could be performed on sanctified ground rather than on a physical *mizbeach* — the thread that connects this entire discussion back to the damaged altar and the distinction between blood and *ketoret* with which the sugya began.
