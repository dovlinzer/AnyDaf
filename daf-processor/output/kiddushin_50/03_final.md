# Kiddushin 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Status Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲנִי כֹּהֵן״ וְנִמְצָא לֵוִי, ״לֵוִי״ וְנִמְצָא כֹּהֵן, ״נָתִין״ וְנִמְצָא מַמְזֵר. ״מַמְזֵר״ וְנִמְצָא נָתִין, ״בֶּן עִיר״ וְנִמְצָא בֶּן כְּרַךְ, ״בֶּן כְּרַךְ״ וְנִמְצָא בֶּן עִיר. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁבֵּיתִי קָרוֹב לַמֶּרְחָץ״ וְנִמְצָא רָחוֹק, ״רָחוֹק״ וְנִמְצָא קָרוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one said to a woman: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that I am a priest, and he was found** to be **a Levite;** or if he said: **A Levite, and he was found** to be **a priest;** or if he said: Be betrothed to me on the condition that I am **a Gibeonite,** a people prohibited by rabbinic law from marrying into the congregation, i.e., from marrying a Jew of fit lineage, **and he was found** to be **a *mamzer*,** who is prohibited by Torah law to marry into the congregation; or he said: **A *mamzer*, and he was found** to be **a Gibeonite;** or if he said: Be betrothed to me on the condition that I am **a resident of** a small **town, and he was found** to be **a resident of** a large **city;** or he said: **A resident of a city, and he was found** to be **a resident of a town;** or if he said: Be betrothed to me **on the condition that my house is close to the bathhouse, and it was found** to be **far;** or he said: **Far** from the bathhouse, **and it was found** to be **close,** she is not betrothed.

### Kohen/Levi Cases

Daf 50 opens at the very bottom of 49b with a new *mishnah*. The *mishnah* presents a series of cases in which a man betroths a woman by stating a condition regarding his personal status — *al menat she'ani Kohen venimtsa Levi*, on the condition that I am a *kohen* and he is discovered to be a *levi* — or the reverse, on the condition that I am a *levi* and he turns out to be a *kohen*.

### Natin/Mamzer Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בַּת אוֹ שִׁפְחָה מְגוּדֶּלֶת״ וְאֵין לוֹ, אוֹ ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לוֹ״ וְיֵשׁ לוֹ. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּנִים״ וְיֵשׁ לוֹ, אוֹ עַל מְנָת שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ וְאֵין לוֹ. וּבְכוּלָּם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרָה: ״בְּלִבִּי הָיָה לְהִתְקַדֵּשׁ לוֹ״ – אַף עַל פִּי כֵן אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְכֵן הִיא שֶׁהִטְעַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Or if he said that she is betrothed to him **on the condition that he has a grown daughter or a maidservant, and he does not have** one, **or on the condition that he does not have** one **and he has** one; or **on the condition that he has no sons, and he has** sons, **or on the condition that he has** sons **and he does not have** sons, then she is not betrothed. **And in all these** cases, **despite** the fact **that she** later **stated: I intended to become betrothed to him nevertheless,** whether or not he fulfilled the condition, **she is not betrothed. And similarly,** if it was **she who misled him** by making the betrothal conditional upon a statement of hers that turned out to be incorrect, the betrothal will not take effect.

The *mishnah* continues: *natin venimtsa mamzer*, on the condition that I am a *natin* — a Gibeonite, a lower rank of personal status — and it turns out he is a *mamzer*; or *mamzer venimtsa natin*, he declared himself a *mamzer* and turned out to be a *natin*. In each pair, the first-mentioned status is the more preferred or higher-ranking one. The pattern holds throughout: whether the condition stated was better than the reality, or worse, the *kiddushin* does not take effect.

### Town vs. City

The *mishnah* goes on: *ben ir venimtsa ben krach*, he said he lived in a town and it turned out he lived in a city, or *ben krach venimtsa ben ir*, a city-dweller who turns out to live in a town. Since the town is listed first — as the preferable condition — the *mishnah* is implicitly expressing that it is better to live in a town than in a city. Rashi explains: *yeshivat krachim kasha* — city living is difficult. The city is a place of markets, noise, high rents, and crowds. Of course, precisely those features lead many people to prefer city life, which makes this an interesting reflection of the *mishnah*'s social assumptions.

### Bathhouse Proximity

The *mishnah* continues with conditions relating to amenities: *al menat sheyesh li bayit karov lemerchats venimtsa rachok*, on the condition that my house is close to the bathhouse, and it turns out to be distant — or the reverse, distant and it turns out to be close. Either way, the condition was not fulfilled as stated.

### Household Help

Similarly: *al menat sheyesh li bat o shifcha megudelet*, on the condition that I have a daughter or a maidservant who is an adult — reading the straightforward *pshat*, someone who can assist with the household (though the *gemara* earlier had offered a different reading of this phrase) — and he does not, or he says he does not have such help and it turns out he does.

### Sons Condition

The *mishnah* further addresses: *she'ein li banim*, on the condition that I have no sons. This is a preferred status from the wife's perspective, since it means there will be no other heirs — specifically sons — competing with her future children for the inheritance. Again, whether he stated he has sons and does not, or stated he has none and does, the condition is unfulfilled and the *kiddushin* does not take effect.

Strikingly, the *mishnah* does not explicitly conclude with *eina mekudeshet* — she is not betrothed. That ruling is apparently presumed from the previous *mishnah*. It is also worth noting that Rabbi Shimon, who argued in the previous *mishnah* that fulfillment of a condition in the better-than-promised direction should be valid, does not dispute this *mishnah* — because as the *gemara* explained, in matters of personal status, "better" is not necessarily better. Perhaps one does not want a spouse of a higher status than anticipated. That is how the *gemara* resolved the matter there.

### Internal Intent Invalid

The *mishnah* then adds a critical new principle: *af al pi she'amra belibi haya lehitkadesh lo af al pi chen eina mekudeshet* — even if the woman says, "In my heart I had already decided to marry him regardless; I didn't care whether he was a *kohen* or a *levi*, I love him and the condition was irrelevant to me" — she is still not married. Once a condition is articulated, it becomes the legally binding framework of the *kiddushin*. The *mishnah* adds that *vechen hi shehit'athu* — the same applies in reverse, if she is the one who misrepresented a stated condition.

It is worth noting that the *mishnah* frames the issue in terms of deception — *leta'u* — but the *gemara* consistently does not focus on the wrongfulness of deception as such. The operative point is not the moral dimension of misleading the other party; it is that something was stated as a condition, and that condition was not met. The primary innovation here is that even an explicit disclaimer of internal indifference — "I didn't care about that condition" — has no legal effect. What is legally binding is what was said. This leads directly into one of the most consequential principles in the entire *Shas*.

## Devarim Shebalev

### Aliyah Sale Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּזַבֵּין לְנִכְסֵיהּ אַדַּעְתָּא לְמִיסַּק לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּבְעִידָּנָא דְּזַבֵּין לָא אֲמַר וְלָא מִידֵּי. אָמַר רָבָא: הָוֵי דְּבָרִים שֶׁבַּלֵּב, וּדְבָרִים שֶׁבַּלֵּב אֵינָם דְּבָרִים. מְנָא לֵיהּ לְרָבָא הָא? אִילֵּימָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן:
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who sold his property with the intention of ascending to Eretz Yisrael, but at the time that he sold** the property **he did not say anything** with regard to his intention. Ultimately, he did not ascend to Eretz Yisrael, and he wished to renege on the sale. **Rava said:** Since he did not explicitly state that he was selling his property on the condition that he ascend to Eretz Yisrael, **that is** an unspoken **matter that** remained **in the heart, and** unspoken **matters that** remain **in the heart are not** significant **matters.** The Gemara asks: **From where does Rava** learn **this** principle? **If we say** it is **from that which we learn** in a *baraita*:

The *gemara* introduces the foundational discussion of the principle of *devarim shebelev einam devarim* — that unarticulated intentions have no halakhic weight. The case that opens the discussion: *hahu gavra dezavin lenichsei ada'ata lemisak le'Ara deYisrael* — a man sold his property with the intent of making *aliyah*. Rashi emphasizes that the property in question was his home. The man had a plan — to sell everything and move to Eretz Yisrael — but critically, *u-ve-idna de-zavin lo amar velo midi*, when he actually sold the property he said nothing to anyone about this plan. It did not work out and he did not make *aliyah*, and he is now effectively homeless. Can he reverse the sale?

### Rava's Principle

*Amar Rava: havei devarim she-ba-lev*. Rava rules that the unarticulated intent — the assumption that the sale was contingent on his *aliyah* plans — constitutes *devarim she-ba-lev*, and *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*: things in the heart, things that are never articulated, carry no halakhic weight. The sale stands.

The *gemara* then asks: *minalei le-Rava ha?* — where does Rava derive this principle? The natural candidate is our own *mishnah*, which prompted the discussion, but the *gemara* works through several possible sources to determine which most cleanly establishes the principle.

### Sacrifice Forced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יַקְרִיב אֹתוֹ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ. יָכוֹל בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לִרְצֹנוֹ״. הָא כֵּיצַד? – כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״רוֹצֶה אֲנִי״. וְאַמַּאי? הָא בְּלִבֵּיהּ לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן: דְּבָרִים שֶׁבַּלֵּב אֵינָן דְּבָרִים? וְדִילְמָא שָׁנֵי הָתָם, דַּאֲנַן סָהֲדִי דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ בְּכַפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one who pledges to bring a burnt-offering, the verse states: "If his offering be a burnt-offering of the herd, he shall offer it a male without blemish; he shall bring it to the door of the Tent of Meeting, according to his will, before the Lord" (Leviticus 1:3). The seemingly superfluous words **"he shall offer it" teaches that they coerce him** to bring the offering. I **might** have thought that it can be offered entirely **against his will,** by taking it from his possession and sacrificing it; therefore, **the verse states: "According to his will."** **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? **They coerce him until he says: I want** to bring the offering. The Gemara asks: **But why** should this be effective; **but in his heart it is not satisfactory for him** to bring the offering, and it is not according to his will. **Rather, is it not because we say:** Unspoken **matters that** remain **in the heart are not** significant **matters,** and his intention is rendered irrelevant by his explicit statement? The Gemara rejects this: **But perhaps there it is different, since** it **is clear to us that it is satisfactory for him to** achieve **atonement,** despite his earlier statement to the contrary.

The *gemara* proposes a source from the top of 50a. In the beginning of *Vayikra*, the *pasuk* states *yakriv oto* — he shall bring it — which is read as a superfluous phrase alongside *zachar tamim yakrivenu*. The *mishnah* derives from this: *melamed she-kofin oto* — you may compel a person to bring a *korban* if he made a *neder*. But the *pasuk* also states *lirtzono* — it must be of his own will. How can one be both compelled and willing? *Kofin oto ad she-yomar rotzeh ani* — you compel him until he says, "I consent." He says the words verbally, even as it is evident he did not wish to do this. The *gemara* notes the apparent difficulty: *libeih lo nicha lei* — in his heart he clearly does not want to. Yet we accept his verbal declaration. Does this not prove *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*?

### Challenge to Principle

The *gemara* pushes back: *ve-dilma shani hatam de-anan sahadei de-nicha lei be-khaparah* — perhaps there it is different, because we can say that in truth he wants the atonement. Even if it took some arm-twisting, deep down he actually desires what he is ultimately being led to do. He wants to be reconciled with his obligations, and in that sense his verbal statement does align with his real desires, once we factor in what a person truly wants.

This points to a deeper issue. The principle of *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* is not merely an evidentiary claim — that we cannot verify what someone was thinking. It is a substantive legal principle: even if we somehow know with certainty what the person was thinking, it has no halakhic effect. But the *gemara* here is suggesting that in the sacrifice case, perhaps what the person says actually does reflect what he really wants — so it is not a pure case of discounting the inner state.

### Get & Slave Freedom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִסֵּיפָא: וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בְּגִיטֵּי נָשִׁים וְשִׁחְרוּרֵי עֲבָדִים, כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״רוֹצֶה אֲנִי״. וְאַמַּאי? הָא בְּלִבֵּיהּ לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן דְּבָרִים שֶׁבַּלֵּב אֵינָן דְּבָרִים? וְדִילְמָא שָׁנֵי הָתָם, מִשּׁוּם דְּמִצְוָה לִשְׁמוֹעַ דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara derives a proof **from the latter clause** of that same *baraita*: **And similarly, you find with bills of divorce of women and** bills **of manumission of slaves** that when the court rules that a man must divorce his wife or free his slave and he does not want to, **they coerce him until he says: I want** to divorce my wife, or: I want to free my slave. **But why** should this be effective; **but in his heart it is not satisfactory for him** to divorce her or to free him. **Rather, is it not because we say** that unspoken **matters that** remain **in the heart are not** significant **matters?** The Gemara rejects this proof as well: **But perhaps there it is different, because it is a mitzva to listen** to the **statements of the Sages,** and the assumption is that when he is required to divorce his wife or free his slave, his true desire is to perform the mitzva.

The *gemara* then turns to the continuation of that *mishnah*: *ve-khen ata motzei be-gitei nashim u-ve-shichrurei avadim — kofin oto ad she-yomar rotzeh ani*. The same principle applies to a *get* and to the freeing of a slave: when *beis din* determines that a *get* must be given or a slave freed, the man is compelled until he says *rotzeh ani*. This is directly relevant to the *agunah* problem — if *beis din* were to apply compulsion and obtain a verbal declaration of consent, it would be valid, something that cannot practically occur in certain jurisdictions but is theoretically operative in Israel. The principle is that once he says *rotzeh ani*, his internal reluctance is legally irrelevant.

### Yetzer Hatov Defense

Yet the *gemara* deflects this proof as well: *ve-dilma shani hasam mishum de-mitzvah lishmo'a divrei chachamim* — perhaps there, too, it is different. Since *beis din* is compelling him, we can say that deep down he wants to do the right thing; his *yetzer hatov* wants to comply with the ruling of *chachamim*, and it is only his *yetzer hara* that resists. The Rambam develops this idea at length — that every person fundamentally desires to fulfill his religious obligations, and *beis din*'s compulsion is simply helping the *yetzer hatov* overcome the *yetzer hara*. Under this reading, what the person ultimately says truly reflects his genuine inner will, and the case thus does not cleanly prove *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*.

### Woman Assumed Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, מֵהָכָא: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה וְאָמַר: כְּסָבוּר הָיִיתִי שֶׁהִיא כֹּהֶנֶת וַהֲרֵי הִיא לְוִיָּה, לְוִיָּה וְנִמְצֵאת כֹּהֶנֶת, עֲנִיָּיה וַהֲרֵי הִיא עֲשִׁירָה, עֲשִׁירָה וַהֲרֵי הִיא עֲנִיָּיה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁלֹּא הִטְעַתּוּ. וְאַמַּאי? הָא קָאָמַר כְּסָבוּר הָיִיתִי! אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן דְּבָרִים שֶׁבַּלֵּב אֵינָן דְּבָרִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: דִּלְמָא שָׁנֵי הָתָם, דִּלְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef says:** The proof is **from here** (64a): In the case of **one who betroths a woman and he said: I thought that she was** the daughter of **a priest, and she is** in fact the daughter of **a Levite;** or I thought **she** was the daughter of **a Levite, and she is found** to be the daughter of **a priest;** I thought **she** was **poor, and she is wealthy;** or I thought **she** was **wealthy, and she is poor, she is betrothed** despite his mistaken assumption, **because she did not mislead him. But why** is she betrothed; **but he said: I thought** that she had a different characteristic, and he betrothed her with that in mind? **Rather,** it is **because we say** that unspoken **matters that** remain **in the heart are not** significant **matters. Abaye said to him: Perhaps it is different there, since** the ruling there is that she requires a bill of divorce only **as a stringency,** and they are not definitively betrothed.

Rav Yosef then proposes a different source: *ha-mekadesh es ha-isha ve-amar kasavur hayiti she-hi kohenes ve-harei hi levaya* — a man betroths a woman and says after the fact, "I thought she was the daughter of a *kohen*" — the *mishnah* calls her a *kohenes* rather than *bat kohen* — "and it turns out she is the daughter of a *levi*." Or the reverse: he thought she was a *levaya* and she was a *kohenes*, or poor and she is wealthy, or wealthy and she is poor. In all these cases, *mekudeshes mipnei she-lo hit'ahu* — the *kiddushin* is valid because she did not deceive him. Again, the *mishnah* emphasizes deception, but the *gemara*'s consistent focus is on the distinction between what was articulated and what was merely assumed. He thought she was a *kohenes* — but he said nothing. His assumption is entirely internal and legally irrelevant. *Kasavur hayiti* — "I thought" — does not constitute a condition. This seems to be a strong proof for *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*.

### Stringency Objection

Abaye, however, objects: *dilma shani hasam de-lechumra* — perhaps there it is different, because there we are discounting the inner intent in the direction of *chumra*, of stringency. We refuse to treat her as unmarried based on his claim of what he was thinking — because we want to play it safe, not because inner intentions are always irrelevant. To truly prove *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*, one would need a case where inner intent is discounted even *le-kula* — even in the direction of leniency. Discounting intent in the stringent direction could simply be a matter of caution, not a statement about the irrelevance of inner states.

### Woman's Indifference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, מֵהָכָא: בְּכוּלָּם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרָה ״בְּלִבִּי הָיָה לְהִתְקַדֵּשׁ לוֹ״ – אַף עַל פִּי כֵן אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְאַמַּאי? הָא קָאָמְרָה ״בְּלִבִּי הָיָה״! וְדִילְמָא שָׁנֵי הָתָם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּאַתְנִיה לָאו כֹּל כְּמִינַּהּ דְּעָקְרָא לֵהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that the proof is **from here,** from the mishna: **And in all these** cases, **despite** the fact **that she** later **stated: I intended to become betrothed to him nevertheless, she is not betrothed. But why** should her betrothal not take effect at all; **but she said: I intended** to become betrothed? This clause of the mishna teaches that unspoken matters that remain in the heart are not significant. The Gemara rejects this proof: **But perhaps it is different there, as, since he stipulated** explicitly that a certain condition was true, **it is not in her power to uproot his condition** through thoughts alone.

Abaye therefore offers his own proof — from the *mishnah* itself. *Bakulam af al pi she'amra belibi hayah le-hiskadesh lo af al pi chen einah mekudeshes* — in all these cases, even if the woman says she didn't care about the condition, she is not married. Here, she is saying "I would have married him regardless" — and we discount that to rule *lekula*, that she is not married. We are ignoring her stated inner indifference and declaring the *kiddushin* void. This is discounting *devarim she-ba-lev* in the lenient direction. Surely this proves the principle?

### Stated Negates Internal

The *gemara* responds: *ve-dilma shani hasam de-kivan de-asni lav kol kaminei de-akei lei letna'ei* — perhaps there it is different, because since a condition was explicitly stated, she cannot use her inner thoughts to negate it. *Kivan de-asni* — since he articulated a condition, she cannot come and say "in my heart I didn't care about that." Inner intent cannot override an explicit verbal stipulation. This is a narrower principle than what Rava wants to establish: not that *devarim she-ba-lev* are never legally relevant, but that *devarim she-ba-lev* cannot negate something that was explicitly stated.

The question, then, is how to prove the broader principle — that even in cases where nothing was said at all (as in the man who sold his property planning *aliyah* without articulating that intent), the unarticulated intent is legally void.

### Shaliach & Windowsill

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין: עוֹבָדָא הֲוָה בֵּי רַב חִסְדָּא, וְרַב חִסְדָּא בֵּי רַב הוּנָא, וּפַשְׁטוּהָ מֵהָא: הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: ״הָבֵא לִי מִן הַחַלּוֹן אוֹ מִן הַדְּלוֹסְקָמָא״, וְהֵבִיא לוֹ. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמַר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת: ״לֹא הָיָה לִי בְּלִבִּי אֶלָּא עַל זֶה״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהֵבִיא לוֹ מִזֶּה – בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מָעַל. וְאַמַּאי? הָא קָאָמַר ״בְּלִבִּי״! אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן דְּבָרִים שֶׁבַּלֵּב אֵינָן דְּבָרִים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin says: There was an incident** of this kind in **Rav Ḥisda's study hall, and Rav Ḥisda** brought the case to **Rav Huna's study hall, and they resolved it from this** mishna (*Me'ila* 21a): In the case of **one who says to his agent: Bring me** such and such an item **from the window** ledge **or from the box [*hadeluskema*],** forgetting that the item in question was consecrated property and any use of it would constitute misuse of consecrated property, **and** the agent **brought it to him,** then **although** at that point **the owner said: My intention was** that you bring the item **only from this** other place, **once he brought** the item **to him from that** place that he had mentioned, once the agent uses it **the owner** is liable for **having misused** consecrated property. **But why** should he be responsible; **but he said: My intention** was for the other place, so the agent did not fulfill his mission. **Rather, is it not because we say** that unspoken **matters that** remain **in the heart are not** significant **matters?**

The *gemara* reports that Rav Chiya bar Avin brought this question before Rav Chisda, who brought it before Rav Huna, and they found support from the following *me'ilah* case. If a person says to his *shaliach*, "Bring me the money from the windowsill" — *havi li min hachalon* — and from a certain box — *umin hadeluskema* — and the *shaliach* does so and spends the money, and it turns out the money was *hekdesh*, the *ba'al habayis* has committed *me'ilah*. The principle of *shlichus* applies in *me'ilah* — the *shaliach* acted on his behalf, so the *ba'al habayis* bears the liability. Now, the *ba'al habayis* claims: *lo haya li belibi ella al ze* — "I only meant the money on this side of the windowsill, not the other side." The *shaliach* took from the other side. Does this matter?

### Shaliach Proof

*Keivan shehevi lo mize ba'al habayis ma'al* — it does not matter. Even though the *ba'al habayis* is articulating what he says he was thinking, the *shlichus* was fulfilled and he has committed *me'ilah*. This is an ideal proof for *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* for several reasons. First, there is nothing here that contradicts what was said — there was no explicit statement about which side of the windowsill; the inner thought is not being deployed to negate anything articulated. Second, the question of *kula* versus *chumra* is not straightforwardly applicable here — either the *ba'al habayis* brings the *korban* or the *shaliach* does. Third, we are completely discounting what the *ba'al habayis* says he was thinking, even if we believe him entirely. *Ella lav mishum de-amrinan devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim.*

### Korban Escape Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא שָׁנֵי הָתָם דְּלִמִיפְטַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ מִקׇּרְבָּן קָאָתֵי. הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵימַר מֵזִיד הֲוָה! לָא עֲבִיד אִינִישׁ דִּמְשַׁוֵּי נַפְשֵׁיהּ רַשִּׁיעָא. הֲוָה לֵיהּ לוֹמַר ״נִזְכַּרְתִּי״, דִּתְנַן: נִזְכַּר בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְלֹא נִזְכַּר שָׁלִיחַ – שָׁלִיחַ מָעַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **But perhaps it is different there,** since it is suspected **that he is coming to exempt himself from** bringing **an offering** for his misuse by claiming that he intended a different item. Since there is cause to question the truth of his statement that he had intended that the agent bring the item from the other place, his claim is not accepted. This cannot serve as a proof that in general, unspoken matters that remain in the heart are not significant. The Gemara responds: If all he wanted to do was exempt himself from the obligation to bring an offering, **he could have said** that the misuse **was intentional,** as one who misuses consecrated property intentionally is not obligated to bring an offering. Therefore, there is no cause to question the truth of his statement that he had intended that the agent bring the item from the other place. The Gemara counters: **It is not common** for **a person to place himself** in the category of **a wicked person** by claiming to have committed a transgression intentionally. Therefore, once again, there is cause to question the truth of his statement that he had intended that the agent bring the item from the other place. The Gemara continues to ask: To exempt himself from the obligation to bring an offering, **he could have said:** After the agent left **I remembered** that it was consecrated property. Such a claim would also have rendered him exempt, **as we learned** in that same mishna (*Me'ila* 21a): If one sent an agent to bring a particular item, and **the owner remembered** that it was consecrated **and the agent did not remember** but proceeded to fulfill his agency, it is **the agent** who has **misused** consecrated property and is liable to bring an offering, not the one who designated him, since the latter remembered and canceled the agency. There is no cause to question the truth of his statement that he had intended that the agent bring the item from the other place. Therefore, the fact that this statement is not accepted can serve as a proof that in general, unspoken matters that remain in the heart are not significant.

The *gemara* raises one more challenge: *ve-dilma shani hatam delimeiftar nafshia mikorban ka'ati* — perhaps we discount his claim not because *devarim she-ba-lev* are legally irrelevant, but because we suspect he is lying. He wants to escape liability for the *korban* and is fabricating a story about which side of the windowsill he meant. If so, we are discounting him because we do not believe him — not because his inner state would be irrelevant even if sincere.

### Easier Escape Routes

The *gemara* answers: *hava lei lemeimar mezid hava* — if he only wanted to escape the *korban*, he had easier things to say. He could have claimed to have acted intentionally — *mezid* — since a deliberate act of *me'ilah* does not carry the *korban* obligation. But *la avid inish demashvi nafshia rashi'a* — a person will not declare himself a *rasha*, will not portray himself as someone who deliberately misappropriated *hekdesh* funds, just to avoid a *korban*. Alternatively, *hava lei lemeimar nizkarti* — he could have said, "I remembered it was *hekdesh* while the *shaliach* was on the way, but by then I had no way to recall him." The *braita* establishes: *nizkar ba'al habayis velo nizkar shaliach — shaliach ma'al* — if the *ba'al habayis* remembered and the *shaliach* did not, the *shaliach* bears the liability. So the *ba'al habayis* had a clean, costless way to escape — and he did not use it. His claim about the windowsill therefore cannot be dismissed as motivated by self-interest. We believe him — and we still discount it. *Devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*, even when sincere.

### Exceptions Overview

Having established the principle, it is important to note that there are significant exceptions — cases where *devarim she-ba-lev* do carry halakhic weight.

### Self-Evident Exception

One major exception is called *devarim she-ba-lev kol adam* — intent that is self-evident from the circumstances to any reasonable person. Even if something is not explicitly articulated, if the context makes it entirely clear what a person must have intended, that intent may be given legal weight. This opens a broad area of discussion, but the basic idea is that when there is no need to access someone's private thoughts — when the circumstances themselves make the intent obvious — we are not truly in the domain of unarticulated inner states. Related to this are cases where a person makes an elliptical statement — as at the beginning of *Nedarim*, where someone says "I will be" as a *nazir* walks by, and we understand from context what was meant. There the full sentence is being completed from external evidence, not from speculation about private thought. Situations involving clear external compulsion also raise questions about how much weight to give the verbal statement versus the evident reluctance, though as we saw, the *gemara* earlier seemed to indicate that compulsion does not restore legal weight to inner states.

### One vs. Two-Sided

A further and very important distinction emerges from the Rambam, and the *Tosafot Rid* as well: the principle of *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* applies with particular force in two-sided transactions — a sale, a betrothal, an appointment of a *shaliach* — where there is another party whose interests and reasonable expectations must be protected. It would be deeply unfair to that other party if halakhic outcomes could be affected by the unarticulated private thoughts of one side. However, in more one-directional acts — giving a gift, delivering a *get*, where the act is essentially emanating from one side — there may be greater readiness to consider *devarim she-ba-lev*. Others among the *rishonim* frame the distinction not in terms of one-sided versus two-sided transactions, but in terms of how self-evident the intent is from the circumstances. In any case, the core principle — that purely private, unarticulated intentions that no one had access to cannot be given halakhic meaning — is firmly established by this *gemara*.

## Aliyah Condition Met?

### Went, Didn't Settle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּזַבְּנִינְהוּ לְנִיכְסֵיהּ אַדַּעְתָּא לְמִיסַּק לְאַרְעָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל. סְלֵיק וְלָא אִיתְּדַר לֵיהּ. אָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל דְּסָלֵיק – אַדַּעְתָּא לְמֵידַר הוּא, וְהָא לָא אִיתְּדַר לֵיהּ. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: אַדַּעְתָּא לְמִיסַּק, וְהָא סְלֵיק לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who sold his property with the intention of ascending to Eretz Yisrael** and explicitly stated this intention to the buyer. **He ascended** to Eretz Yisrael **but he was not able to settle** there. Upon his return to Babylonia, he sought to nullify the sale. **Rava said: Whoever ascends** to Eretz Yisrael does so **with the intention of settling** there, **and** as **he was not able to settle** there he can nullify the sale. **There are** those **who say** a different version, that Rava said the opposite: **He intended to ascend** to Eretz Yisrael, **and he ascended,** so he cannot nullify the sale.

The *gemara* now turns to a related case, this time assuming that the man did articulate his intent when selling his property. *Hahu gavra dezavin le-nikhsei ada'ata le-misak le-ara de-Yisrael* — he sold his property because he was planning to go up to Eretz Yisrael and said so. Unlike the previous case, the assumption here is that something was stated. He went — *salik* — attempted to make *aliyah*, but *ve-lo istadar lei* — he could not find a place to live, could not get settled, and returned. The condition was stated; was it met?

### Rava: Settling Intent

*Amar Rava kol de-salik ada'ata le-meidar hu* — Rava rules that when a person says he is selling in order to go up to Eretz Yisrael, he means to go up and settle there. *Ve-ha lo istadar* — and since he did not manage to settle, the intent was not fulfilled and the sale is reversed.

### Alternative: Going Only

*Ika de-amri* — some say the opposite ruling in Rava's name: *ada'ata le-misak ve-ha salik* — he said he was going to go, and he went. The condition of going was satisfied; the fact that he did not ultimately settle is beside the point. These are two competing interpretations of what his stated intent meant.

### Never Went Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּזַבְּנִינְהוּ לְנִיכְסֵיהּ אַדַּעְתָּא לְמִיסַּק לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, לְסוֹף לָא סְלֵיק. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִי בָּעֵי, סָלֵיק. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אִי בָּעֵי, לָא סָלֵיק? מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, דְּאִיתְיְלִיד אוּנְסָא בְּאוֹרְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: There was **a certain man who sold his property with the intention of ascending to Eretz Yisrael,** but **ultimately he did not ascend** there. **Rav Ashi said: If he** had **wanted** to do so, **he could have ascended.** Since the matter depended upon him, there are no grounds for nullifying the sale. **There are** those **who say** Rav Ashi said as follows: **If he** had **wanted** to do so, **couldn't he have ascended?** Since nothing prevented him from leaving, the sale is not nullified. The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** the two versions of Rav Ashi's statement? The Gemara answers: **There is a** difference **between them** in a case **when circumstances beyond his control** occurred **along the way,** preventing him from going. According to the first version of the statement of Rav Ashi, the sale is upheld; but according to the second version, where Rav Ashi responded in the form of a question, the implication is that if there actually had been something that prevented him from ascending, the sale would be nullified.

A further case: *hahu gavra d'zavin l'nichsei ada'ata l'misak l'Eretz Yisrael* — another man sold his property planning to go up to Eretz Yisrael. *Lesof lo salik* — in the end he changed his mind and never went. Does he have the right to reverse the sale?

### Option to Go

*Amar Rav Ashi: ibai salik* — Rav Ashi responds: who is stopping him? He can still go. The sale cannot be reversed simply because he has decided not to exercise the option. He retains the ability to fulfill the intent; he is simply choosing not to. *Ika de-amri: ibai lo salik* — some formulate it as: well, if he wants to, nothing prevents him from going. Both formulations say essentially the same thing: the option remains available.

### Impossible Travel

The *gemara* asks: *mai beinaihu* — what is the practical difference between the two formulations? *Ika beinaihu d'ishlei unsa be'orcha* — the difference emerges when travel is genuinely impossible, such as when road conditions make the journey truly impracticable. According to the formulation "who is stopping him," there is now an answer — something genuinely is stopping him — and the sale may be nullified. But according to the formulation "if he wants to, he can go," the implication is that so long as it is at all possible, the option exists and the sale stands. A legitimate external impediment, however, might nullify it.

### External vs. Central

This case raises a striking question that is addressed by the *rishonim*. Since when is a person compelled to fulfill such a condition? Normally, if I sell you a plot of land on the condition that I walk around the block, I may freely choose whether to walk or not — and my choice determines whether the sale stands. Why then does the *gemara* respond "who is stopping you? Go!" — implying he must go? The answer, developed by *Tosafot*, Ramban, and others, turns on a fundamental distinction between two types of conditions.

### Formal vs. Fundamental

When a condition is *external* to the basic intent of the transaction — such as "this sale will take effect if I walk around the block" — it is a technical device layered on top of the transaction. The person may fulfill it or not at will, but if he wants to use the condition to undo the transaction, he must have satisfied all the formal requirements of *mishpetei tna'im* — the formal rules of conditions modeled after the stipulation of *Bnei Gad u-Vnei Reuven*: the condition must be doubled, the positive stated before the negative, and so forth.

When, however, the reason for the transaction is itself central to the person's fundamental *da'at* — as when someone sells his home *because* he intends to make *aliyah*, and that intent is what motivates the entire transaction — we are not dealing with an external conditional device. We are speaking about the core of what the person wanted to do. In that case, there is no need to satisfy the formal *mishpetei tna'im*, because this is not a formal condition at all — it is an expression of what the transaction fundamentally was about. But by the same token, as long as the underlying intent remains achievable — as long as he could still make *aliyah* — the transaction reflects a genuine, intact *da'at*, and simply choosing not to act on it does not unwind what was done. The formal question of whether to fulfill the condition is replaced by the substantive question of whether the fundamental premise of the transaction has genuinely failed.

These discussions yield two major and interconnected principles. First, *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*: purely unarticulated intentions — even if sincerely held and even if we somehow know them — have no halakhic significance in transactions, particularly two-sided ones. Second, even when things are articulated, the legal analysis of what was said depends on whether it constitutes a formal external condition subject to technical *tna'im* requirements, or whether it expresses the fundamental *da'at* of the transaction. These are among the most consequential principles in the entire *Shas*.

## Shaliach Location

### Different Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: ״צֵא וְקַדֵּשׁ לִי אִשָּׁה פְּלוֹנִית בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ וְהָלַךְ וְקִדְּשָׁהּ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. ״הֲרֵי הִיא בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ וְקִדְּשָׁהּ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who says to his agent: Go and betroth for me so-and-so in such and such a place, and** the agent **went and betrothed her in a different place, she is not betrothed,** since he instructed that the betrothal take place in a particular location. But if he said: Go and betroth the woman for me, **she is in such and such a place; and** the agent **betrothed her in a different place, she is betrothed,** since he did not mean that the agent should betroth her specifically there, but was merely telling him where to find her.

The *gemara* turns to a new *mishnah*: *ha'omer lishluchо tze vekadesh li isha plonis bemakom ploni vehalach vekideshah bemakom acher* — a man says to his *shaliach*, "Go betroth this woman for me in such-and-such place," and the *shaliach* goes and finds her in a different place and betroths her there. The *mishnah* rules: *eina mekudeshet* — she is not betrothed. The *shlichus* was limited to that particular place.

### Mareh Makom

This ruling reflects the principle of *makpid* — that when a person specifies a location as part of a *shlichus*, we treat him as genuinely insisting on that location, not merely as indicating where the woman can be found. This is in contrast to the principle of *mareh makom* — merely indicating the woman's whereabouts as a point of reference, which would not be a binding limitation on the *shlichus*. The *mishnah* establishes: if the location was articulated as part of the act of *shlichus*, it is a substantive requirement, not a mere *mareh makom*. A dissenting *tanna* does appear in the parallel discussion regarding *get*, though not explicitly here in the *kiddushin* context.

### Get Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּתְנַן נָמֵי גַּבֵּי גִיטִּין: הָאוֹמֵר ״תְּנוּ גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר – פָּסוּל, ״הֲרֵי הִיא בְּמָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״ וּנְתָנוֹ לָהּ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: **And we also learned** in a mishna **with regard to bills of divorce** (*Gittin* 65a): With regard to **one who says** to his agents: **Give this bill of divorce to my wife in such and such a place, and they gave** it **to her in another place,** the divorce is **invalid.** If he said to them: **She is in such and such a place, and they gave** it **to her in another place,** it is **valid.**

The *mishnah* also teaches the parallel rule regarding *get*: *ha'omer tnu get l'ishti bemakom ploni unesanu lah bemakom acher — pasul* — if a man says to give his wife a *get* in a specific place and it is given in a different place, it is invalid. But if he says "she is in such-and-such place" and the *get* is given elsewhere, *kasher* — it is valid. There the reference to a location was purely *mareh makom*, indicating where the woman could be found, not a substantive condition of the *shlichus*.

### Marriage Reputation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא. דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גַּבֵּי קִידּוּשִׁין – מִשּׁוּם דִּלְקָרוֹבַהּ קָאָתֵי, בְּהַאי אַתְרָא רָחֲמוּ לִי וְלָא מְמַלְּ[לִ]י מִילֵּי עִלָּוַי, בְּהַאי אַתְרָא סָנוּ לִי מְמַלְּ[לִ]י מִילֵּי עִלָּוַי. אֲבָל גַּבֵּי גִּיטִּין, דִּלְרַחוֹקַהּ קָאָתֵי, אֵימַר לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* with regard to both betrothal and divorce, **because had** the *tanna* **taught us** only **with regard to betrothal,** you might say: **In a case when he is coming to draw her near** to him through betrothal, he thinks: **They love me in this place and will not say** negative **remarks about me,** but **they hate me in that place and will say** negative **remarks about me.** Therefore, he told the agent to perform the betrothal in a certain place and is particular that it take place only there. **But with regard to bills of divorce, when he is coming to distance her** from him, you might **say he does not care** where the divorce itself is performed. The *tanna* therefore informs us that this is not the case.

Why does specifying the location matter in the context of *kiddushin*? The *gemara* explains: *dekureva ka'asi* — the man is trying to bring her close, to marry her. When he specifies a place, it is because *behai asra rachmu li velo mamlu mili aluy* — in that neighborhood or community, people know him favorably and will speak well of him. He does not want the *shaliach* to approach her in a place where people might speak poorly of him and cause her to refuse. He is genuinely insisting on the location, not merely indicating where she might be found.

### Divorce Embarrassment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן גַּבֵּי גֵירוּשִׁין, בְּהַאי אַתְרָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּנִיבַּזֵּי, בְּהַאי אַתְרָא לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי קִידּוּשִׁין – אֵימָא לָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** conversely, **if** the *tanna* **had taught us** only **with regard to divorce,** I would have said that he is particular only in the case of divorce, because **in this place** it is **acceptable for him to degrade himself** through divorce, whereas **in that place** it is **not acceptable for him** to do so; **but with regard to betrothal,** which involves no degradation, you might **say he does not care** where he betroths her. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state the *halakha* in both cases.

Why must the parallel rule also be taught by *get*? The *gemara* explains that one might have thought that when it comes to *get*, where *delerichuka ka'asi* — the man is trying to push her away — perhaps *lo ikphas leh* — he does not particularly care where it happens, since she has no say in accepting a *get* and no one can obstruct it. So perhaps even an explicit mention of location is only *mareh makom* in the context of *get*.

Conversely, one might have thought that a man is more *makpid* about the location of a *get* than of *kiddushin*. Rashi explains: *delivzi shemegareshes ishto g'nai hu lo* — the act of divorcing one's wife carries a certain disgrace for the man, an embarrassing reflection on the failure of the marriage. He may have strong feelings about where this humiliating event occurs — *behai asra nicha leh denivzi* — this is a place where he can tolerate the embarrassment, whereas elsewhere he would be much more sensitive. So one might have thought he cares more acutely about the location of the *get* than of the *kiddushin*. Since the reasoning cuts in different directions, the *mishnah* must teach the rule in both contexts. The underlying principle remains: when the location is articulated as part of the *shlichus*, it is binding; when it is merely a *mareh makom*, it is not.

## Vows Condition

### Pre-Marriage Vows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים, וְנִמְצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. כְּנָסָהּ סְתָם וְנִמְצְאוּ עָלֶיהָ נְדָרִים – תֵּצֵא שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who betroths a woman on the condition that there are no vows** incumbent **upon her** to fulfill, **and it was found that there were vows** incumbent **upon her** to fulfill, **she is not betrothed,** since his condition was not fulfilled. If he **married her without specification, and it was found that there were vows** incumbent **upon her** to fulfill, the marriage takes effect. Nevertheless, he has the right to divorce her, and **she is divorced without** receiving payment of her **marriage contract,** as it is assumed that he would not have married her had he known that she was limited by her vows.

The *mishnah* presents another category of conditions: *hamekadesh es ha'isha al menas she'ein aleha nedarim venimtzu aleha nedarim* — if a man betroths a woman on the condition that she has no outstanding *nedarim*, and *nedarim* are found, *eina mekudeshet* — the *kiddushin* is void.

### Void Pre-Nisuin

If the *nedarim* are discovered before *nisuin* — before the full marriage is consummated — then the condition was explicitly unfulfilled and the *kiddushin* simply does not take effect, consistent with everything we have learned.

### No Ketubah Post-Nisuin

If, however, the man made this condition, they proceeded to *nisuin*, began living together, and only then discovered the *nedarim*, the situation is different. *Kensah stam venimtzu aleha nedarim — teitzei belo kesuvah* — if he married her without having explicitly made the condition and *nedarim* are found, or if the condition was made but discovered only post-*nisuin*, she must leave — but without a *kesuvah*. She receives no *kesuvah* because the marriage violated what was stipulated; however, she still requires a *get*.

### Nisuin Nullifies

Why does she need a *get* even though the condition was not met? This is based on the principle of *ein adam oseh beilaso beilas zenus* — we assume that a man does not wish for his act of marital intimacy to be treated as promiscuous. Once they proceeded to *nisuin* and consummated the marriage, there is a reasonable assumption — or at least a real possibility — that by proceeding he effectively waived the condition. Given that possibility, we treat her as genuinely married and requiring a *get*, though she loses her *kesuvah* since the condition was violated.

### Agunah Solution

This *mishnah* raises the broader question of *tna'i bekiddushin* as a proposed solution to the *agunah* problem. One suggested approach is to build a condition into the *kiddushin* itself — stipulating that if the couple separates and no *get* is given under certain circumstances, the *kiddushin* will be retroactively void. The question that this *mishnah* directly implicates is: once the couple begins living together, does the *nisuin* and subsequent cohabitation automatically constitute a *bittul* of the *tna'i*? Or can the condition be explicitly formulated in a way that survives the *nisuin*? This is one of the central practical complications in evaluating that proposed solution.

### Blemishes Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לָהּ מוּמִין וְנִמְצְאוּ בָּהּ מוּמִין – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. כְּנָסָהּ סְתָם וְנִמְצְאוּ בָּהּ מוּמִין – תֵּצֵא שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְתוּבָּה. שֶׁכׇּל הַמּוּמִין הַפּוֹסְלִין בַּכֹּהֲנִים פּוֹסְלִין בַּנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if he betrothed her **on the condition that there are no blemishes upon her, and she was discovered to have blemishes, she is not betrothed.** In a case where he **married her without specification and she was discovered to have blemishes,** he has the right to divorce her, and **she is divorced without** receiving payment of her **marriage contract.** As to what is defined as a blemish, the rule is **that all the blemishes that disqualify priests** from performing the Temple service, as detailed in tractate *Bekhorot*, also **disqualify women** from receiving their marriage contract in case of divorce.

The *mishnah* then states a parallel case: *al menas she'ein lah mumim venimtzu bah mumim* — on the condition that she has no physical blemishes, and blemishes are found. If discovered before *nisuin*, *eina mekudeshet* — the *kiddushin* is void. *Kinasa stam venimtzu bah mumim — teitzei belo kesuvah* — if discovered after *nisuin*, she leaves without a *kesuvah* but still requires a *get*, for the same reasons discussed above. The *mishnah* adds: *shekol hamumim haposlim bakohanim poslim banashim* — all the blemishes that disqualify *kohanim* from service similarly disqualify women under this type of condition — a striking and far-reaching statement that the *gemara* does not elaborate upon here.

## Savlonos Gifts

### Insufficient Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַדֵּשׁ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים בְּשָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אוֹ אִשָּׁה אַחַת בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁלַח סִבְלוֹנוֹת לְאַחַר מִכָּאן – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁמֵּחֲמַת קִידּוּשִׁין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים שָׁלַח. וְכֵן קָטָן שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who betroths two women** together **with** an item **worth one *peruta*,** so that the value of each woman's share was not worth one *peruta*, **or** who betroths **one woman with** an item **worth less than one *peruta*, despite** the fact **that he later sent** the traditional **gifts [*sivlonot*]** of a groom to the bride, **she is not betrothed, because he sent** the gifts **on account of the first betrothal,** i.e., the item whose value to the woman was less than one *peruta*, and not to effect betrothal. **And similarly,** if there was **a minor who betrothed** a woman, and he sent her gifts after he became an adult, the assumption is that he sent them on account of his betrothal when he was still a minor, and since betrothal performed by a minor is of no account, she is not betrothed.

The final *mishnah* of the daf addresses: *hamekadesh shtei nashim beshaveh perutah* — a man who betroths two women with a single *perutah* between them, each receiving only half a *perutah*, which is insufficient for valid *kiddushin* — or *isha achas bepachas mishaveh prutah*, one woman with less than a *perutah*. In these cases the *kiddushin* obviously does not take effect.

### Savlonos as Kiddushin

The *mishnah* then introduces the central question: *af al pi sheshalach savlonos achar kach einam mekudashos* — even if he subsequently sent *savlonos*, the traditional gifts sent by the groom to the bride's family after *kiddushin*, she is still not betrothed. Two possible concerns motivate the question. One — emphasized by Rashi — is whether the *savlonos* themselves might constitute new *kesef kiddushin*, functioning as a fresh act of betrothal. The other, which fits other parts of the *gemara* more naturally, is whether the sending of *savlonos* should lead us to assume that some valid act of *kiddushin* occurred that we do not know about, since *savlonos* typically follow *kiddushin*.

### Based on Initial

The *mishnah* resolves both concerns: *she'machmat kiddushin harishonim shelach* — the *savlonos* were sent on account of the initial *kiddushin*, invalid as it was. The man thought his *kiddushin* had taken effect, and that is why he sent the *savlonos*. They are therefore not a new act of *kiddushin*, and they do not indicate that some other valid *kiddushin* occurred — they are simply the natural continuation of what he (mistakenly) believed had already happened.

The same principle applies to *vechen katan shekiddesh* — a minor who performed *kiddushin* and then, after reaching majority, sent *savlonos*. She remains unbetrothed for the same reason: the *savlonos* are explained by the prior invalid act.

### Why All Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אַיְּידֵי דְּקָא נָפֵיק מָמוֹנָא מִינֵּיהּ – טָעֵי, אֲבָל פָּחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אֵימָא יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בְּפָחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה וְכִי קָא מְשַׁדַּר סִבְלוֹנוֹת – אַדַּעְתָּא דְּקִידּוּשִׁין קָא מְשַׁדַּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **And** it is **necessary** for all these cases to be stated in the mishna, despite their apparent similarity. **As, had** the *tanna* **taught us** only the case of one who betroths two women with an item **worth one *peruta*,** we would have said: **Since** enough **of his money** for betrothal **goes out,** i.e., is spent, **he errs** and thinks that he can betroth two women with one *peruta*, and the gifts he later sends are not for the sake of betrothal. **But** if he betrothed a woman with an item **worth less than one *peruta*,** you might **say: He knows that betrothal does not take effect with** an item **worth less than one *peruta*, and when he** later **sends gifts he sends** them **with the intention of betrothal,** and she should consequently become betrothed with them. The mishna therefore teaches us that she is not betrothed even in that case.

The *gemara* asks: *u'tzricha* — why must the *mishnah* teach all three cases — two women with one *perutah*, one woman with less than a *perutah*, and the case of the *katan*?

If only the case of two women with one *perutah* were taught, one might think: since a full *perutah* was paid, the man assumed it was valid, so obviously the *savlonos* are not a new *kiddushin*. But in the case of less than a *perutah* — *aval pachas mishaveh prutah* — perhaps he knew very well that an amount below a *perutah* is invalid, and when he sent *savlonos* he intended them as a new act of *kiddushin*. The *mishnah* teaches us that even there, we do not assume this.

Conversely, if only those two cases were taught, one might think that in cases involving money, men generally do not know or pay attention to the precise threshold — but when it comes to a *katan*, *hakol yodim she'ein kiddushei katan klum* — everyone knows that the *kiddushin* of a minor is entirely invalid. So when, after reaching adulthood, he sends *savlonos*, perhaps we should assume he knew his earlier act was void and now intends the *savlonos* as a new *kiddushin*. The *mishnah* teaches that even in that case we do not assume this. Without evidence of a new act of *kiddushin*, we attribute the *savlonos* to the earlier invalid act, and no new betrothal is established.

### Concerned About Savlonos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְסִבְלוֹנוֹת. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבָּה: חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְסִבְלוֹנוֹת. אָמַר רַבָּה: וּמוֹתְבִינַן אַשְּׁמַעְתִּין: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁלַח סִבְלוֹנוֹת לְאַחַר מִכָּאן – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת! אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הָתָם כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא: שֶׁמֵּחֲמַת קִידּוּשִׁין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים שָׁלַח.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that *amora'im* discussed the following matter. **Rav Huna says: One** must be **concerned about gifts.** If a woman agreed to a betrothal and the prospective husband sent her gifts in the presence of witnesses, one must be concerned about the possibility that he sent them for the sake of betrothal. Therefore, the woman may not become betrothed to another man without first receiving a bill of divorce from this one. **And Rabba similarly says** that **one** must be **concerned about gifts. Rabba says: And we raised an objection against** this ***halakha* of ours,** as the mishna teaches: **Even if he sent gifts later, she is not betrothed. Abaye said to him: There,** in the mishna, the **reason** is **as it teaches: Because he sent** the gifts **on account of the first betrothal.** Therefore, there is no concern that he might have sent them for the sake of betrothal. But in this case, where he had not betrothed her beforehand, he might have intended to betroth her by sending her gifts.

The *gemara* now presents an *amoraic* debate that stands in some tension with the *mishnah*. *Rav Huna amar chosheshin lesavlanos* — Rav Huna rules that when *savlonos* are sent, we are concerned about the possibility of a prior *kiddushin*. *Vechen amar Rabbah chosheshin lesavlanos* — Rabbah agrees. There is a major debate among the *rishonim* about what exactly this means. Rashi understands it to mean that we are concerned the *savlonos* themselves were intended as *kesef kiddushin*. Many *rishonim* resist this reading, pointing out that valid *kiddushin* requires explicit articulation of *harei at mekudeshet li* and the presence of witnesses. They prefer to read the concern as follows: the sending of *savlonos* — which are traditionally sent after *kiddushin* — raises the possibility that some valid *kiddushin* occurred beforehand that we simply do not know about.

### Resolves Contradiction

Rabbah then anticipates the apparent contradiction with our *mishnah*: if we are concerned about *savlonos*, how does the *mishnah* say *einam mekudashos*? Abaye answers: *hatam kideiktani ta'ama* — there, the *mishnah* itself explains why there is no concern: *she'machmat kiddushin rishonim shelach* — the *savlonos* are accounted for by the prior invalid *kiddushin*. The man sent them because he thought the first *kiddushin* had worked. That eliminates the evidentiary weight of the *savlonos*. But Rav Huna and Rabbah are addressing a different scenario: where *savlonos* are sent with no prior known *kiddushin* at all. In that case, we are concerned that a valid *kiddushin* took place beforehand.

### Savlonos Indicate Kiddus…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אָמַר רַבָּה: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא: שֶׁמֵּחֲמַת קִידּוּשִׁין הָרִאשׁוֹנִים שָׁלַח. הָכָא הוּא דְּטָעֵי. הָא בְּעָלְמָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** a different version of this discussion. **Rabba says: From where do I say** this *halakha*, that one must be concerned about gifts? I have learned it from **the reason** she is not betrothed, **as** the mishna **teaches: Because he sent** the gifts **on account of the first betrothal.** This indicates that **it is here** that there is no concern for that he betrothed her, **since he erred** and thinks she is already betrothed to him by the first act of betrothal, and that there is no need to betroth her again. **But generally,** where this type of mistake is not possible, the sending of gifts **does** effect **betrothal.**

*Ika de'amrei* — there is an alternative version of the exchange, in which Rabbah does not present the *mishnah* as a problem at all. Rather, he reads the *mishnah* itself as the source of his ruling. The *mishnah* says the *savlonos* are not a concern *because* the prior *kiddushin* explains them — *hacha hu detai*, in the *mishnah*'s case he is mistaken in thinking the prior act was valid. *Ha be-alma havei kiddushin* — but normally, when *savlonos* are sent without any preceding invalid act to account for them, we would assume that they reflect a prior valid *kiddushin*. Abaye, in this version, pushes back and says the *mishnah*'s point was that we need no concern whatsoever — not even where *savlonos* appear without any context — since the idea that *savlonos* indicate prior *kiddushin* is not self-evidently true, given that in some communities *savlonos* are sent before *kiddushin*.

### Practice Dependent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְקַדְּשִׁי וַהֲדַר מְסַבְּלִי – חָיְשִׁינַן, מְסַבְּלִי וַהֲדַר מְקַדְּשִׁי – לָא חָיְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** conclusion **was** reached **about it;** must one be concerned that the gifts might have been given for the sake of betrothal or not? The Gemara answers: **Rav Pappa says: In a place where** the custom is that men first **betroth** women **and afterward send gifts we are concerned** that he might already have betrothed her or is sending the gifts for the sake of betrothal, since that is the only situation in which gifts are typically sent. But in a place where men first **send gifts and afterward betroth** women **we are not concerned** that the gifts are for betrothal.

*Amar Rav Pappa* — the conclusion depends on local practice. *Be-asra demikadshi vehadar mesavli* — in a place where *kiddushin* comes first and *savlonos* follow, if we see *savlonos* being sent, *chassinan* — we are concerned that a *kiddushin* has taken place. *Mesavli vehadar mikadshi* — if the local practice is to send *savlonos* before *kiddushin*, *lo chassinan* — there is nothing to be concerned about.

### Kiddushin First Usually

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקׇּדְשֵׁי וַהֲדַר מְסַבְּלִי – פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּרוּבָּא מְקַדְּשִׁי וַהֲדַר מְסַבְּלִי, וּמִיעוּטָא מְסַבְּלִי וַהֲדַר מְקַדְּשִׁי. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: נֵיחוּשׁ לְמִיעוּטָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: In a place where **they betroth** women **and afterward send gifts,** it **is obvious** that if a man sends a woman gifts he must already have betrothed her. The Gemara clarifies: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* due to a place **where most** men **betroth** women **and afterward send gifts, and a minority** of men **send gifts and afterward betroth** women. **Lest you say we should be concerned about the minority** and say she is not betrothed, Rav Pappa therefore **teaches us** that the behavior of the minority is not taken into account.

The *gemara* notes that the ruling for the case where *kiddushin* typically precedes *savlonos* might seem self-evident — of course we are concerned. The ruling is therefore necessary for a more nuanced situation.

### Savlonos First Usually

If the practice is to send *savlonos* first, there is obviously no concern, since seeing *savlonos* tells us nothing about whether *kiddushin* has occurred.

### Majority Practice

The novelty of the ruling applies where the *majority* practice in a community is for *kiddushin* to precede *savlonos*, but a minority practice the reverse. One might have thought: this person sent *savlonos* and we know no *kiddushin* occurred — perhaps he is from the minority who do things in reverse order. The *gemara* rules that because of the weight of *eshes ish* — the gravity of a woman being considered a married woman — we do not attribute the *savlonos* to the minority practice. We follow the majority and are concerned that *kiddushin* already took place.

### Ketubah Writing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא מֵרָבָא: הוּחְזַק שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה בַּשּׁוּק, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְכִי מִפְּנֵי (שֶׁמַּחְזִיק) [שֶׁהוּחְזַק] שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה בַּשּׁוּק נַחְזִיק בָּהּ כְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ? מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּאַתְרָא דִּמְקַדְּשִׁי וַהֲדַר כָּתְבִי כְּתוּבָּה – חָיְשִׁינַן, כָּתְבִי וַהֲדַר מְקַדְּשִׁי – לָא חָיְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a related topic: **Rav Aḥa bar Rav Huna inquired of Rava:** If a woman's **marriage contract was established,** i.e., seen, by people **in the marketplace, what is** the *halakha*; is she assumed to be married? Rava **said to him: And should we establish her as a married woman because a marriage contract was established in the marketplace?** Since a marriage contract can be written by a man even before betrothal and without the woman's consent, its existence does not prove that she is married. The Gemara asks: **What** conclusion **was** reached **about it?** The Gemara answers: **Rav Ashi says: In a place where** men first **betroth** women **and afterward write a marriage contract, we are concerned** that he had betrothed her; but in a place where **they write** the marriage contract first **and afterward betroth** women, **we are not concerned.**

A parallel question is then asked: *hukhzak shtar kesuvah bashuk mahu* — if it becomes known that a *kesuvah* was written for a woman, does that indicate that *kiddushin* has already taken place? Rav Acha bar Rav Huna poses this question to Rava. The questioner wonders whether the writing of a *kesuvah* should be treated like the sending of *savlonos* — as evidence of a prior *kiddushin*. Rava responds with some skepticism: why should we simply assume, because a *kesuvah* was written, that *kiddushin* has occurred? Perhaps the *kesuvah* was written in preparation, and perhaps other explanations exist.

### Ketubah Practice

In a place where the standard practice is for *kiddushin* to occur before the *kesuvah* is written, and we hear that a *kesuvah* was written, *chassinan* — we are concerned that *kiddushin* has taken place. *Kasvi vehadar mikadshi* — in a place where the *kesuvah* is typically written before *kiddushin*, *lo chassinan*.

### Sofer Availability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְקַדְּשִׁי וַהֲדַר כָּתְבִי – פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ סָפְרָא. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא סָפְרָא הוּא דְּאִתְרְמִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: In a place where men first **betroth** women **and afterward write** the marriage contract it **is obvious** that there should be a concern that he has betrothed her. The Gemara clarifies: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach this *halakha* due to a place **where a scribe is not commonly available. Lest you say** that he wrote the marriage contract early because there was **a scribe who happened** to be there, and the prospective husband took the opportunity to avail himself of the scribe's services while intending to perform the betrothal later, Rav Ashi therefore **teaches us** that there is no concern that he might have done so since the custom is to first betroth the woman.

The *gemara* notes that the ruling for the case where *kiddushin* typically precedes the writing of the *kesuvah* might again seem obvious. The ruling is necessary for a case where *delo shakhiakh safra* — a *sofer* is not readily available. One can imagine a perfectly reasonable scenario: a man intends to marry, has not yet performed *kiddushin*, but a *sofer* happens to be available now and may not be available for months. So he has the *kesuvah* written in advance. Surely, one might think, we should assume this is what happened rather than concluding that *kiddushin* has already occurred.

### Ashes Ish Concern

The *gemara* rules that this reasoning does not override the standard practice. Since the norm in that community is to write the *kesuvah* only after *kiddushin*, even the plausible scenario of an early *kesuvah* written for the sake of *sofer* availability does not remove the concern. Just as with *savlonos* — where we follow the majority practice and do not assume this person belongs to the minority that sends *savlonos* first — here too we follow the standard practice, and the concern that *kiddushin* has already taken place, with all the gravity of *eshes ish*, leads us to treat the situation accordingly until we can investigate further.
