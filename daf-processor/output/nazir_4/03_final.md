# Nazir 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shimon vs. Rabbanan

### Nezirut Indivisible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִן הַחַרְצַנִּים״ וּ״מִן הַזַּגִּים״ וּ״מִן הַתִּגְלַחַת״ וּ״מִן הַטּוּמְאָה״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר, וְכׇל דִּקְדּוּקֵי נְזִירוּת עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one said: **I am hereby a nazirite** and therefore will refrain **from** grape **seeds, or:** I am hereby a nazirite and therefore will refrain **from** grape **skins, or: From shaving, or: From impurity, he is a nazirite. And all details of naziriteship** are incumbent **upon him.** Not only does the prohibition he mentioned take effect, he is bound by all of the strictures of naziriteship.

The *mishna* establishes that *nezirut* is an all-or-nothing commitment: one cannot accept upon oneself only a partial form of *nezirut* — only from grapes, only from hair-cutting, or only from *tumah*. It is a complete package. The *gemara* then engages Rabbi Shimon's position, which likewise treats *nezirut* as indivisible, but with an additional and sharper implication: if one attempted to accept *nezirut* from only one aspect of its obligations, one has done nothing at all. There is a conceivable third position — that partial *nezirut* is in fact valid — but this is a position the *gemara* rejects. The one exception, which will emerge in the coming *mishna*, is *nazir Shimshon*, since Shimshon himself became *tamei* regularly. Beyond that, there is no phenomenon of partial *nezirut*.

### Shimon: All or Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּדּוֹר מִכּוּלָּם. וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ לָא נְזַר אֶלָּא בְּחַד מִנְּהוֹן — הָוֵי נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** One **is not obligated** as a nazirite **until he vows** that **all** items and actions forbidden to a nazirite are forbidden **to** him. **And the Rabbis say: Even if he vowed** to abstain **from only one of them, he is a nazirite.**

The *gemara*, as it often does, seeks to identify the *pasuk* from which each side derives its position, and then to ask what the opposing side does with that same verse. In this context, the central *pasuk* under analysis is *miyayin veshechar yazir* — that a *nazir* will abstain from wine and intoxicating liquid. As discussed previously, the Sages understand that in the context of *nezirut*, *shechar* does not carry its ordinary meaning of non-wine intoxicants such as beer or mead, but refers only to wine-based mixtures — a reading that is admittedly not the straightforward *pshat* of the verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר יֵעָשֶׂה מִגֶּפֶן הַיַּיִן מֵחַרְצַנִּים וְעַד זָג״. וְרַבָּנַן, מַאי טַעְמָא — אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִיַּיִן וְשֵׁכָר יַזִּיר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Shimon? The verse states** with regard to a nazirite: "All the days of his naziriteship he shall not eat **from anything that is made of the grapevine, from pits to grape skin"** (Numbers 6:4), which indicates that his vow of naziriteship must include all the prohibitions of a nazirite. The Gemara continues to clarify: **And** according to **the Rabbis, what is the reason** that he becomes a nazirite even if he specified only one of the prohibitions of a nazirite? **The verse states: "He shall abstain from wine and strong drink"** (Numbers 6:3). This implies that even if one vows to abstain only from wine and strong drink, all of the *halakhot* of a nazirite take effect.

The *gemara* had explained that the *rabbanan* use this verse to teach that even an attempt to accept *nezirut* from only one element draws in the entire package. If so, what does Rabbi Shimon do with that verse? He holds that the verse comes to tell us that even a type of wine one might have thought should be excluded remains forbidden to the *nazir*.

### Miyayin Veshechar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״מִיַּיִן וְשֵׁכָר יַזִּיר״! הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ — לֶאֱסוֹר יֵין מִצְוָה כְּיֵין הָרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And also** according to **Rabbi Shimon, isn't it written "he shall abstain from wine and strong drink"?** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon **requires that** verse **to prohibit** a nazirite from drinking **wine** that is consumed for **a mitzva** just **as** he is prohibited from drinking **wine** whose consumption is **optional.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא — קִדּוּשְׁתָּא וְאַבְדָּלְתָּא? הֲרֵי מוּשְׁבָּע וְעוֹמֵד עָלָיו מֵהַר סִינַי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** wine that is consumed for a mitzva? Is it the wine of ***kiddush* and *havdala*?** **He is already sworn and obligated about it from Mount Sinai,** i.e., he is obligated by Torah law to keep the *halakhot* of naziriteship, and therefore it is obvious that he may not drink wine from *kiddush* or *havdala*, as drinking the wine is required by rabbinic law (Rambam).

The *gemara* initially suggests that the excluded wine might be the wine of *kiddush* and *havdalah*, but this is rejected. The *gemara*'s language — *mushba ve'omed me'har sinai* — is read by most *rishonim*, following Rabbeinu Tam, as ironic or rhetorical: obviously a *nazir* cannot drink the wine of *kiddush* and *havdalah*. No verse is needed to establish that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רָבָא ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֶשְׁתֶּה״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״ — אָתְיָא נְזִירוּת חָיְילָא עַל שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **like that which Rava said:** If one said: I hereby take **an oath that I will drink** wine, **and he then said: I am hereby a nazirite,** the **naziriteship comes** and **applies to the** subject of his **oath.** Although drinking wine is a mitzva for him due to his oath, his naziriteship supersedes the previous oath and renders it prohibited for him to drink wine.

The *gemara* therefore identifies a more meaningful case, introduced by Rava: suppose someone takes a *shevuah* that he will drink wine, and immediately afterward declares himself a *nazir*. One might have thought that the prior oath to drink wine cannot be overridden by the subsequent *nezirut*, and that he must therefore drink the wine. The verse *miyayin veshechar yazir* comes to teach that the *nezirut* does indeed take hold, and he is forbidden from that wine as well.

### Yayin Mitzvah Issue

The underlying principle here is that one cannot take a *shevuah* that directly countermands an existing obligation — *mushba ve'omed*. I cannot swear not to sit in a *sukkah* on *Sukkot*, for instance, since I am already obligated. Nor can I swear to drink wine and then swear not to drink wine; the second oath would directly contradict the first obligation. However, a *neder* operates differently. A *neder* works indirectly: if I declare all *sukkahs* forbidden to me, I am not directly countermanding my personal obligation. I am making the physical object of the *sukkah* forbidden, and then when the *mitzvah* arrives, I simply cannot fulfill it — the object is forbidden.

Similarly, when one declares oneself a *nazir*, one is not saying "I will not drink wine." One is changing one's status, one's identity. The natural consequence of having assumed the status of *nazir* is that wine becomes forbidden, but this is not a direct countermand of the prior *shevuah* to drink. One relates to the object — or in the case of *nezirut*, one becomes the object — and everything flows from that new status. This is why *nezirut* can override even an existing oath, because it is a change of personal status rather than a direct acceptance of individual obligations.

### Nezirut as Status

This distinction is crucial: when a person says *harei ani nazir*, he is not directly obligating himself not to drink wine, not to cut his hair, not to become *tamei*. He is imparting upon himself the status of *nazir*, and from that status all the individual *issurim* flow by Torah law. He himself becomes, in a sense, the object — the bearer of the status — and the prohibitions attach to him through that status, not through direct vow.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי, הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לֶאֱסוֹר יֵין מִצְוָה כְּיֵין רְשׁוּת! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״מִיַּיִן״. מַאי ״וְשֵׁכָר״? שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the Rabbis also, isn't** the verse **needed to prohibit** to a nazirite **wine** that is consumed as **a mitzva,** just **like wine** whose consumption is **optional?** The Gemara answers: **If** that is **so, let the verse say** only "he shall abstain **from wine"** (Numbers 6:3). **What** is the purpose of the additional phrase **"and strong drink"? Learn from it** that the verse teaches **two** *halakhot*, that one is a full-fledged nazirite even if he accepted only one of the prohibitions of naziriteship, and that a nazirite is prohibited from drinking wine even when its consumption is a mitzva.

Returning to the *gemara*'s give-and-take: the *rabbanan* counter that they too need the verse *miyayin veshechar yazir* to teach the case of the wine that was the subject of a prior *shevuah*. If so, they cannot use that verse for the all-or-nothing teaching. But the *gemara* responds: if the only purpose of the verse were to teach that this particular wine remains forbidden, it would have sufficed to say "from wine." The fact that it adds *veshechar* — the extra element — must come to teach something additional: namely, that even if one attempted *nezirut* only from *shechar*, one becomes a *nazir* with respect to everything. Rabbi Shimon's purpose for the word *shechar* is then asked: he holds that *shechar* in this verse comes to establish a *hekesh*, a comparison, between the law of the *nazir* and the law of the *Beit HaMikdash*.

## Shechar Definition

### Tziruf Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב ״שֵׁכָר״ — לְאַלּוֹפֵי ״שֵׁכָר״ ״שֵׁכָר״ לְמִקְדָּשׁ. דִּכְתִיב: ״יַיִן וְשֵׁכָר אַל תֵּשְׁתְּ אַתָּה וּבָנֶיךָ אִתָּךְ״, מָה גַּבֵּי נָזִיר: יַיִן הוּא דְּלִיתְּסַר, אֲבָל שְׁאָר מַשְׁקִין — לָא, אַף גַּבֵּי מִקְדָּשׁ נָמֵי: יַיִן הוּא דְּלִיתְּסַר, אֲבָל שְׁאָר מַשְׁקִין הַמִּשְׁתַּכְּרִין — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And Rabbi Shimon** could respond to this argument as follows: **This is the reason that** the verse **writes "strong drink":** It is **to teach** a verbal analogy between **"strong drink"** written here and **"strong drink"** written **with regard to** entering and performing service in **the Temple, as it is written** that Aaron the priest was commanded: **"Do not drink wine or strong drink, you nor your sons with you,** when you go into the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 10:9). This teaches: **Just as with a nazirite, it is wine** alone **that is forbidden but other beverages** are **not** forbidden, **so too, with regard to** the **Temple, it is wine that is forbidden** to priests, **but other intoxicating beverages** are **not** forbidden to them.

The verse in the context of the *Kohanim* — *yayin veshechar al teisht ata u-vanecha itach* — prohibits a *kohein* from entering the *Beit HaMikdash* in a state of intoxication. That verse likewise uses the phrase *yayin veshechar*. Since by *nazir* we establish that *shechar* cannot mean non-wine intoxicants but must refer to wine-based mixtures, the *hekesh* between *nazir* and *Mikdash* teaches that the same is true for a *kohein*: only wine, not other intoxicating liquids, would trigger the prohibition of entering the *Mikdash*. In both contexts, the prohibition extends specifically to wine-based drinks.

### Kohen Application

The technical distinction matters: for a *kohein* and wine, there is a formal measure — a *revi'is*, approximately three and a half ounces, which is less than the size of most *kiddush* cups. For *shechar* in the ordinary sense, the prohibition requires actual intoxication. The *gemara* reasons that since *shechar* cannot carry its plain meaning in the context of *nazir*, and the verse in *Mikdash* uses the identical phrase, it too must be restricted to wine-based drinks.

### Rabbi Yehuda: Broad Shechar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אָכַל דְּבֵילָה קְעִילִית, וְשָׁתָה דְּבַשׁ וְחָלָב, וְנִכְנַס לַמִּקְדָּשׁ — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **to the exclusion of** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If one **ate a dried fig from Ke'ila, and** similarly if one **drank honey or** if one drank **milk,** which can dull the senses, **and entered the Temple, he is liable** for violating the prohibition against strong drink.

This leads to a rejection of Rabbi Yehuda's position. A *baraita* records that Rabbi Yehuda holds: if a person ate pressed figs from Ke'ilis, or drank honey and milk, and then entered the *Beit HaMikdash*, he is liable — because all of these substances, even some that are solid foods rather than drinks, can cause a state of intoxication and are therefore encompassed within *shechar*. For Rabbi Yehuda, *shechar* extends to anything that produces this state of being intoxicated or somewhat elevated, regardless of whether it is a beverage at all.

### Shechar Wine-Based

The *rabbanan* reject this broad reading. Only wine-based substances are encompassed by *shechar*; other intoxicating substances — *sha'ar devarim hamishkarin* — are not included. There is a significant gap between these positions. Rabbi Yehuda takes the word *shechar*, which refers to a specific type of intoxicating beverage, and extends it to anything that produces this physiological state. A reasonable position might have rejected Rabbi Yehuda's extension to solids while still reading *shechar* literally as beer or mead. Yet the *gemara* here implies that even for entering the *Beit HaMikdash*, only wine-based substances are included. It should be noted that this is not the way we rule in practice: the *halacha* does in fact hold that *shechar* is included, and we tend to rule like Rabbi Yehuda that even other intoxicating substances are prohibited.

### Hora'ah Digression

This discussion naturally gives rise to a related practical question: if a person consumed a substance that caused intoxication — whether a food like fermented figs or a medication taken for serious pain that produced an altered state — would that disqualify him from rendering a *halachic* ruling or sitting on a *beit din*? The prohibition against *hora'ah* while intoxicated is not limited to wine; if we rule like Rabbi Yehuda that other intoxicating substances are included, then medication that produces a high might equally disqualify a *dayan*. One practical distinction found in the *Shulchan Aruch* is that if a person is merely relating what is already written explicitly in the books — not rendering a novel ruling — that is not considered *paskening* and would be permitted even in an intoxicated state. As was wryly observed: if one can remember the *siman* in *Shulchan Aruch*, perhaps one is not genuinely incapacitated.

## Isur Chal Al Isur

### No Double Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a different reason for the inclusion of the term "strong drink," according to Rabbi Shimon. **If you wish, say** instead that it is necessary because **Rabbi Shimon does not** generally **accept** the principle that **a prohibition takes effect upon** a preexisting **prohibition.**

The *gemara* continues. The *rabbanan* need the verse *miyayin veshechar yazir* for the case of the *shevuah* to drink wine; they use it to teach that even that wine remains forbidden after one accepts *nezirut*. But there is an alternative explanation for what Rabbi Shimon does with this verse. The *gemara* suggests: Rabbi Shimon does not hold the principle of *isur chal al isur* — that one forbidden status cannot take hold atop another. Because he rejects this principle generally, he needs a verse to establish that *nezirut* does indeed create a prohibition even where a prior *isur* already exists.

### Neveila/YK Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הָאוֹכֵל נְבֵילָה בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: One who eats an animal carcass on Yom Kippur is exempt** from the punishment of *karet* for eating on Yom Kippur. It is prohibited to eat an animal carcass, and therefore the additional prohibition against eating on Yom Kippur does not take effect with regard to it. The inclusion of the term "strong drink" alludes to the fact that with regard to naziriteship, a second prohibition does take effect. Consequently, if one took an oath not to drink wine and afterward vowed to be a nazirite, both prohibitions apply.

The *gemara* brings a *baraita* to illustrate Rabbi Shimon's position: Rabbi Shimon says that one who eats a *neveila* on *Yom Kippur* is exempt from violating *Yom Kippur*, because there is no *isur chal al isur*. This is a striking *gemara*. One can understand the principle when it applies to the status of an object: if a piece of meat is already a *neveila* and one then cooks it in milk, one might argue that the object cannot acquire the additional status of *basar b'chalav* — it already bears a forbidden status, and the second prohibition does not attach. That logic is coherent.

But the *Yom Kippur* case is far more puzzling. The prohibition of *Yom Kippur* does not rest on the status of the object; it rests on me — on the person's obligation of *inuy*, self-affliction. What does the forbidden status of the *neveila* have to do with whether I have violated my personal obligation of *Yom Kippur*? I have still engaged in an act of eating on *Yom Kippur*. Why should I be exempt? This is the primary case from which Rabbi Shimon derives his principle, and yet it is the most difficult to understand.

### Oath vs. Nezirut

The *gemara* applies this to *nezirut*: suppose one took a *shevuah* not to drink wine — the mirror image of the earlier case — and then declared himself a *nazir*, and then drank wine. According to Rabbi Shimon, the verse *miyayin veshechar yazir* establishes that even in this case he is forbidden on the wine through *nezirut* as well — not only through his prior *shevuah*. This is a case where *isur chal al isur* does apply even according to Rabbi Shimon, because the verse teaches it explicitly. The principle applies whether the prior *shevuah* was to drink the wine or not to drink it; in either case, the *nezirut* remains operative.

This again connects to the insight that *nezirut* functions as a change of status rather than a direct assumption of obligations. When one declares *harei ani nazir*, one is not directly confronting the prior oath at all; one is transforming one's identity, and the prohibitions follow from that.

## Shimon: No Shiur

### Combining Products

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר יֵעָשֶׂה מִגֶּפֶן הַיַּיִן״! אָמְרִי לָךְ רַבָּנַן: הָתָם לִימֵּד עַל אִיסּוּרֵי נָזִיר שֶׁמִּצְטָרְפִים זֶה עִם זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And also** according to **the Rabbis, isn't it written: "Anything that is made of the grapevine"** (Numbers 6:4), which seems to indicate, as stated by Rabbi Shimon, that one becomes a nazirite only if he vows to accept all the prohibitions of a nazirite? The Gemara answers: **The Rabbis** could have **said to you: There** the verse **taught** that **the prohibitions of a nazirite combine with each other.** In other words, if a nazirite eats less than an olive-bulk of both grape skins and grape seeds, but together they amount to an olive-bulk, he receives lashes for transgressing a Torah prohibition. The word *mikkol* — from all — indicates that all the different categories of forbidden grape products join together to form the minimum measure.

The *gemara* turns to the verse the *rabbanan* use for their all-or-nothing teaching. Rabbi Shimon had used *mikkol asher ye'aseh migefen ha-yayin* to mean that one must accept *nezirut* from everything, and that accepting only part produces nothing. What do the *rabbanan* do with this verse? They read it as teaching the law of *tziruf* — that the various grape-derived products forbidden to a *nazir* combine with one another. If one ate a small amount of grape skin and a small amount of seeds and some raisins, and together these combine to constitute a *kezayit*, one has violated the prohibition. The word *mikkol* — from all — indicates that all the different categories of forbidden grape products join together to form the minimum measure.

### Kol Shehu L'Malkos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ צֵירוּף, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּ לְמַכּוֹת, לֹא אָמְרוּ כְּזַיִת אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן קׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And Rabbi Shimon** does not interpret the verse in this manner because **he does not hold** that there is a need for the **combination** of quantities of different foods in order to render one liable to receive lashes, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** Even **the smallest quantity** of forbidden food is sufficient to render one liable **to** receive **lashes.** The Sages **stated** the measurement of **an olive-bulk only with regard to** the obligation to bring **an offering.**

What then does Rabbi Shimon do without this teaching of *tziruf*? The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon holds *kol shehu l'malkot* — even the smallest amount is sufficient for lashes. This is a general principle Rabbi Shimon advances throughout *Shas*, argued most fully in *Masechet Makkot*: wherever the Sages require a minimum measure, Rabbi Shimon holds that one violates the prohibition even with the smallest amount and is liable even to lashes.

### Shiur for Korban Only

Rabbi Shimon does allow for a minimum measure — but only in one specific context: *lo amru kezayit ela l'inyan korban* — the *kezayit* measure was stated only with respect to the *korban*. If one acted unintentionally and the question is whether one must bring an atonement offering, a minimum threshold applies. But for purposes of punishment — including lashes — even the smallest amount constitutes a full violation. This is a fascinating idea: willful transgression, even of a minute quantity, is a complete violation. Unintentional transgression triggers a *korban* only once it reaches a certain degree.

By *nezirut*, Rabbi Shimon therefore has no need for *tziruf*. For lashes, he does not need a *kezayit* of any single type, let alone a combination — even the tiniest amount suffices. For a *korban*, one would need a minimum measure, but by *nezirut* one does not bring a *korban* for violating the prohibition on grapes and wine. The *korban* is brought either upon the completion of *nezirut* or when one becomes *tamei*. There is therefore no context in which *tziruf* would be necessary according to Rabbi Shimon.

A *kezayit* measure does remain relevant according to most authorities, with Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that *chatzi shiur* — less than the minimum amount — constitutes an *issur de'oraita* (unlike Resh Lakish's position), though not at the full degree of violation. Rabbi Shimon's position goes further: he holds there is no minimum at all, even for purposes of punishment.

## Nazir Shimshon

### Identifying Shimshon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵינִי כְּשִׁמְשׁוֹן״, ״כְּבֶן מָנוֹחַ״, ״כְּבַעַל דְּלִילָה״, ״כְּמִי שֶׁעָקַר דַּלְתוֹת עַזָּה״, ״כְּמִי שֶׁנִּקְּרוּ פְּלִשְׁתִּים אֶת עֵינָיו״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one said: **I am hereby like Samson, like the son of Manoah, like the husband of Delilah, like the one who tore off the doors of Gaza, like the one whose eyes were gouged out by the Philistines, he is a nazirite** like **Samson,** whose *halakhot* are explained in the next mishna (see Judges, chapters 13–16).

The *mishna* introduces the institution of *nazir Shimshon*. If someone says *harei ani k'Shimshon* — behold I am like Shimshon — or *k'ven Manoach*, like the son of Manoach, or *k'ba'al Delilah*, like the husband of Delilah, or *k'mi she'akar daltot Azza*, like the one who tore down the doors of Gaza, or *k'mi shenakru Pelishtim et einav*, like the one whose eyes the Philistines gouged out — in any of these cases one becomes a *nazir Shimshon*. The larger *chiddush* here is not merely which phrases work, but that there exists this distinct institution of *nazir Shimshon*, in which one does not violate — or does not violate to the same degree — through becoming *tamei*, since Shimshon himself became *tamei l'met* routinely.

### Clarification Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנָא כׇּל הָלֵין? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי כְּשִׁמְשׁוֹן״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: שִׁמְשׁוֹן אַחֲרִינָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן ״כְּבֶן מָנוֹחַ״. וְאִי תְּנָא ״כְּבֶן מָנוֹחַ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אִיכָּא דְּמִיתְקְרֵי הָכִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן ״כְּבַעַל דְּלִילָה״ וּ״כְמִי שֶׁנִּקְּרוּ פְּלִשְׁתִּים אֶת עֵינָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the *tanna* **to teach all these** cases? It should be enough to state only the *halakha* where one says: Like Samson. The Gemara answers: These specifications **are necessary because if one said** only: **I am hereby like Samson, I would say** he was referring to **another Samson,** and this is not a nazirite vow. The *tanna* therefore **teaches us** that he adds: **Like the son of Manoah,** which shows he is referring to the biblical Samson. **And if** the *tanna* had **taught** that he said he would be: **Like the son of Manoah, I would say there is** some person **who is called that** name, Samson, son of Manoah, and this is not a reference to the biblical Samson and is not an acceptance of naziriteship. The *tanna* therefore **teaches us** that he adds: **Like the husband of Delilah,** or: Like the one who tore off the doors of Gaza, **or: Like the one whose eyes were gouged out by the Philistines.** It is therefore clear that he is referring to the biblical figure and that his statement is a vow of naziriteship.

The *gemara* asks: why does the *mishna* need to list all of these formulations? The answer is that each one is necessary. If one said only *harei ani k'Shimshon*, one might be referring to another Shimshon entirely — there are many people by that name. The *mishna* therefore adds *k'ven Manoach*. But if one said only *k'ven Manoach*, there might be other people called ben Manoach; the *mishna* therefore includes *k'ba'al Delilah*. And so on, each phrase clarifying and building upon the previous ones.

This raises an interesting question about how to read the *mishna*. On the surface, the *mishna* appears to offer these phrases as alternatives — any one of them suffices. But the *gemara*'s reasoning seems to suggest that they are cumulative: one must say *k'Shimshon*, then *k'ven Manoach*, then *k'ba'al Delilah*, each phrase required to remove ambiguity introduced by the prior one.

### Tosafot Reading

*Tosafot* addresses this tension. They hold that the first set of phrases — *k'Shimshon k'ven Manoach k'ba'al Delilah* — do indeed require one another, meaning all must be said together before *nazir Shimshon* status is established. However, *Tosafot* maintains that the final phrases — *k'mi she'akar daltot Azza* and *k'mi shenakru Pelishtim et einav* — are sufficiently explicit on their own. Even so, this reading does not align naturally with the straightforward *pshat* of the *mishna*, which seems to present each phrase as independently sufficient, nor does the *gemara* state this distinction explicitly.

This reading is in fact consistent with a theme we have encountered in the previous two days: the *mishna* appears to permit relatively vague formulations to be effective, while the *gemara* requires far greater specificity — as we saw with Shmuel's ruling that one must have a *nazir* standing in front of him and be holding one's hair. The *gemara* here similarly tightens the requirements of the *mishna*, which is fully consistent with what we have seen throughout the opening of this tractate.

## Olam vs. Shimshon

### Nazir Olam Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָה בֵּין נְזִיר עוֹלָם לְנָזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן? נְזִיר עוֹלָם, הִכְבִּיד שְׂעָרוֹ — מֵיקֵל בְּתַעַר, וּמֵבִיא שָׁלֹשׁ בְּהֵמוֹת. וְאִם נִטְמָא — מֵבִיא קׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **What is** the difference **between a permanent nazirite and a nazirite** like **Samson,** both of whom remain nazirites forever? In the case of **a permanent nazirite,** if **his hair grows** too **heavy** for him, **he lightens** it by cutting some hair **with a razor, and he** then **brings three animals** as a sin-offering, a burnt-offering, and a peace-offering, like one who completes his term of naziriteship. **And if he becomes ritually impure, he brings** the **offering** for **impurity** brought by a regular nazirite who became impure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן, הִכְבִּיד שְׂעָרוֹ — אֵינוֹ מֵיקֵל, וְאִם נִטְמָא — אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, in the case of **a nazirite** like **Samson,** if **his hair grows heavy he may not lighten** it, since he is entirely prohibited from cutting his hair. **And if he becomes impure, he does not bring an offering** for **impurity.**

The next *mishna* asks: *mah bein nazir olam l'nazir Shimshon* — what is the difference between a *nazir olam* and a *nazir Shimshon*? Both are permanent forms of *nezirut*, but they are mirror images of one another. A *nazir olam* is one who accepts *nezirut* upon himself forever, with no intention to terminate it. The reader will be aware that Rav Kook had a student known as "the Nazir" who accepted *nezirut* and remained a *nazir* for his entire life, since there is no *Beit HaMikdash* in which to bring the *korban* that would terminate *nezirut*. It is worth noting, however, that *nazir olam* is a specific institution: one who declares himself a *nazir* but never brings the concluding *korban* is not thereby a *nazir olam* — he is simply a *nazir* who has chosen not to end his *nezirut*. A *nazir olam* is one who by the very nature of his declaration intends it to be permanent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ נְזִיר עוֹלָם מַאן דְּכַר שְׁמֵיהּ? חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵינִי נְזִיר עוֹלָם״ — הֲרֵי זֶה נָזִיר. מָה בֵּין נְזִיר עוֹלָם לִנְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן? נְזִיר עוֹלָם, הִכְבִּיד שְׂעָרוֹ — מֵיקֵל בְּתַעַר, וּמֵבִיא שָׁלֹשׁ בְּהֵמוֹת, וְאִם נִטְמָא — מֵבִיא קׇרְבַּן טוּמְאָה. נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן, הִכְבִּיד שְׂעָרוֹ — אֵינוֹ מֵיקֵל בְּתַעַר,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Who mentioned anything about a permanent nazirite?** Since the mishna has not yet mentioned this concept, how can it analyze the differences between it and a nazirite like Samson? The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and is teaching the following:** In the case of **one who says: I am hereby a permanent nazirite, he is** a permanent **nazirite. What is** the difference **between a permanent nazirite and a nazirite** like **Samson?** In the case of **a permanent nazirite,** if **his hair grows** too **heavy** for him, **he lightens** it **with a razor and he** then **brings three animals** for offerings. **And if he becomes ritually impure, he brings** the **offering** for **impurity.** In the case of **a nazirite** like **Samson,** if **his hair grows heavy he may not lighten** it **with a razor,**

The *gemara* notes that the *mishna* has introduced the comparison without having first established what *nazir olam* is. It therefore fills in: *michsarei michsara vehachi katani* — the *mishna* is incomplete and should be read as having stated: one who says *harei ani nazir olam*, behold I am a *nazir* forever, is a *nazir olam*. Now, what is the difference between a *nazir olam* and a *nazir Shimshon*?

The *mishna* states: *nazir olam hichbid se'aro meikel beta'ar umevi shalosh behemot* — a *nazir olam*, when his hair becomes too heavy, is permitted to trim it with a razor, and when he does so he must bring three animals as offerings. This is remarkable: by all appearances, cutting one's hair violates a biblical prohibition for a *nazir*, yet the Sages derive from the case of Avshalom that this allowance exists for a *nazir olam*. We are learning from actual cases in *Nach* — Shimshon and Avshalom — that variant forms of *nezirut* exist which carry leniencies not found in the *Torah*'s presentation of *nezirut*. A *nazir Shimshon* may become *tamei lemet*; a *nazir olam* may trim his hair when it becomes excessively burdensome. Both seemingly violate biblical prohibitions, yet both are learned out from these later figures.

### Avshalom Source

*Tosafot* in the last *tosafot* of the *daf* explains: the requirement to bring three animals when cutting one's hair is a *halacha* transmitted to Moshe at Sinai, with support derived from the verse concerning Avshalom, who declared *ashaleim nidri* — I will fulfill my vow. The vow is understood as the same three-animal sacrifice — *chatat*, *olah*, and *shelamim* — that a *nazir tahor* brings upon completing his *nezirut*. In a certain sense, bringing these offerings constitutes a virtual completion of the *nezirut*, opening a window that permits the hair to be cut, even though the *nezirut* itself continues forever.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְזִיר עוֹלָם הֵיכָא כְּתִיב? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אַבְשָׁלוֹם נְזִיר עוֹלָם הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי מִקֵּץ אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה וַיֹּאמֶר אַבְשָׁלוֹם אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ אֵלְכָה נָּא וַאֲשַׁלֵּם אֶת נִדְרִי אֲשֶׁר נָדַרְתִּי לַה׳ בְּחֶבְרוֹן״, וּמְגַלֵּחַ אֶחָד לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי מִקֵּץ יָמִים לַיָּמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara clarifies a *halakha* taught in the mishna: **And where is** the concept of **a permanent nazirite written? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Absalom was a permanent nazirite, as it is stated: "And it came to pass at the end of forty years, that Absalom said to the king: I pray to you, let me go and pay my vow, which I have vowed to the Lord, in Hebron"** (II Samuel 15:7). **And he cut** his hair **once** every **twelve months, as it is stated:** "And when he polled his head, **now it was at every year's [*yamim*] end** that he polled it; because the hair was heavy on him" (II Samuel 14:26).

### Three Korbanot

When the *nazir olam* brings these three offerings and cuts his hair, he has not ended his *nezirut*. He must continue observing all the laws of *nezirut* going forward. The *korbanot* serve as a kind of partial punctuation — a moment of release from the burden of accumulated hair — while the fundamental status of *nazir* remains intact. The laws of *tumah* for a *nazir olam*, by contrast, are standard: *ve'im nitma meivi korban tumah* — if he becomes *tamei*, he must bring the *korban tumah* just as any other *nazir* would. There is no leniency by *tumah*.

### Shimshon Leniencies

*Nazir Shimshon* is precisely the reverse. *Hichbid se'aro eino meikel* — even if his hair grows impossibly heavy, he is not permitted to trim it. There is no allowance regarding hair. This, of course, reflects what we know of Shimshon himself: the relationship between scissors and his hair was not a favorable one. But *ve'im nitma eino meivi korban tumah* — if he becomes *tamei*, he is not obligated to bring a *korban tumah*. There is an allowance by *tumah*. The two institutions are thus mirror images, each reflecting the actual reality of the figure from whom they take their name.

It should be noted that *Tosafot* adds another difference between the two institutions that the *mishna* does not mention: a *nazir olam* can have his *nezirut* revoked by a *chacham* through *she'eila*, whereas a *nazir Shimshon* cannot. Since the *mishna* wished to enumerate differences in the practical observance of *nezirut* rather than in the manner of its dissolution, this distinction was not included.

## Nezirut on Shimshon

### Korban Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קׇרְבָּן הוּא דְּלָא מַיְיתֵי, אֲבָל נְזִירוּת חָיְילָא עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: From the words: But if he becomes impure he does not bring an offering for impurity, one can infer that **it is the offering that he does not bring. However,** all of the prohibitions of **naziriteship apply to him,** and it is prohibited for him to become impure from a corpse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי מַתְנִיתִין? לָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְלָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן — מוּתָּר לִיטַמֵּא לְמֵתִים, שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּשִׁמְשׁוֹן שֶׁנִּטְמָא. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הָאוֹמֵר ״נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם, שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ בְּשִׁמְשׁוֹן שֶׁיָּצָאת נְזִירוּת מִפִּיו.
>
> **Translation:** This leads to the following question: **Whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna?** It is **not** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda and not** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: A nazirite** like **Samson is permitted to become impure from a corpse** *ab initio*, **as we find with Samson that he became impure. Rabbi Shimon says: One who says** he will be **a nazirite** like **Samson has not said anything, since we do not find with Samson that** an utterance of a vow of **naziriteship left his mouth.** Samson never took a vow to be a nazirite. He received his status from the angel's instructions to his mother (see Judges 13:5). Consequently, Rabbi Shimon holds that one who vows to be a nazirite like Samson is not considered to have taken a nazirite vow.

The *gemara* now raises a sharp question about the *mishna*'s formulation. The *mishna* says that a *nazir Shimshon* does not bring a *korban tumah* — implying that becoming *tamei* is still prohibited but does not trigger a *korban*. But this creates an apparent paradox: if *nezirut* applies fully to a *nazir Shimshon* in all other respects, why would he not be obligated to bring a *korban tumah* upon becoming *tamei*?

*Mani matnitin?* the *gemara* asks — who is our *mishna* following? The answer is: neither Rabbi Yehuda nor Rabbi Shimon, as their positions will be shown to be more extreme than the *mishna* implies.

### Rabbi Yehuda Permits

A *baraita* records that Rabbi Yehuda holds: *nazir Shimshon mutar letamei lametim* — a *nazir Shimshon* is permitted *lechatchila* to become *tamei* to corpses, since that is precisely what we find that Shimshon himself did. His becoming *tamei* was not a violation at all.

### Shimon Rejects

Rabbi Shimon, on the other hand, holds: *ha'omer harei ani nazir Shimshon lo amar klum* — one who says "behold I am a *nazir Shimshon*" has said nothing whatsoever. The reason: *shelo matzinu b'Shimshon sheyatza nezirut mipiyu* — we do not find that Shimshon ever verbally accepted *nezirut* upon himself. The angel declared it; the status was conferred from birth. Shimshon's father did not accept it for him, and Shimshon himself never articulated an acceptance. There was no *halachic* act of vowing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הָאָמַר אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִילָּה, וּמַתְנִיתִין קָתָנֵי ״אִם נִטְמָא״. אִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — הָאָמַר לָא חָיְילָא עֲלֵיהּ נְזִירוּת כְּלָל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the question: **Whose** opinion is expressed in the mishna? **If** it is the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, didn't he say** that it is permitted for a nazirite of this kind to become impure from a corpse **even *ab initio*, but the mishna teaches: If he becomes impure,** which indicates that he is prohibited from doing so *ab initio*? However, **if** you say that the mishna is according to the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, didn't he say** that **naziriteship does not apply to him at all?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וְאַיְּידֵי דְּקָתָנֵי גַּבֵּי נְזִיר עוֹלָם ״אִם נִטְמָא״, תְּנָא נָמֵי גַּבֵּי נְזִיר שִׁמְשׁוֹן ״אִם נִטְמָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** and a nazirite like Samson may become impure from a corpse even *ab initio*. **And since it teaches with regard to a permanent nazirite: If he becomes impure,** as it is prohibited for a permanent nazirite to become impure from a corpse *ab initio*, the *tanna* **also taught** the same expression **with regard to a nazirite** like **Samson** and used the expression: **If he becomes impure.**

The *gemara* then resolves who the *mishna* follows: it must go like Rabbi Yehuda. The reason the *mishna* used the conditional formulation *im nitma* — "if he became *tamei*" — is only because the parallel structure required it: since by *nazir olam* the *mishna* stated *im nitma meivi korban tumah*, it mirrored that formulation for *nazir Shimshon*. But the true content of Rabbi Yehuda's position, as the *gemara* clarifies, is that a *nazir Shimshon* is *lechatchila* permitted to become *tamei*.

## Vow vs. Automatic

### Becoming Nazir Shimshon

The *gemara* now explores how exactly one becomes a *nazir Shimshon* according to Rabbi Shimon's understanding. All the phrases listed in the *mishna* — *k'Shimshon*, *k'ven Manoach*, *k'ba'al Delilah*, and so on — are formulations in which one says "I wish to be like Shimshon." This is not, on reflection, the acceptance of an independent institution called *nazir Shimshon*. What one is actually saying is: I want to take Shimshon's *nezirut* status and transfer it to myself. This mechanism of transferring a status from one object or person to another is called *hatfasah*.

### Hatfasah Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דִּתְנַן: ״הֲרֵי עָלַי כִּבְכוֹר״, רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹסֵר, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that** Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon **disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between these *tanna'im*, as we learned** in a *baraita*: If one says: This object **is hereby** forbidden **to me like a firstborn, Rabbi Ya'akov prohibits** the individual from deriving benefit from the object, as he holds that a vow of this sort is valid. **And Rabbi Yosei permits** it, because the sanctity of a firstborn is not the result of a vow or sanctification. Rather, it is sacred of its own accord, and therefore its forbidden status cannot be extended by means of a vow to other items.

*Hatfasah* — the act of "grabbing," of connecting one thing to another that already bears a status in order to transfer that status — is a recognized mechanism within the world of *nedarim*. If a sanctified animal is standing before me, and I point to a loaf of bread and say *zeh ka-zeh* — this is like that — I have transferred the *kedushah* of the first object to the second. This works because both the original object and the new one are operating within the same world of *nedarim*, the world in which status is imparted through speech acts.

But what if the first object's status was never imparted through words? What if it became *kadosh* automatically, without a human vow? Can one then use *hatfasah* to transfer that status to another object? This is the crux of the debate.

### Davar Hanidur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב, דְּאָמַר: לָא בָּעִינַן דָּבָר הַנִּידָּר. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: בָּעִינַן דָּבָר הַנִּידָּר?
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** the case that **Rabbi Yehuda holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ya'akov, who said** that in order for a vow to take effect, **we do not require** one to extend the forbidden status of **an item** rendered forbidden by means of **a vow?** Consequently, just as one can render an object forbidden by extending to it the sanctity of a firstborn animal, one can become a nazirite by accepting upon himself the status of Samson, whose prohibitions were not established by a vow. **And Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, who said** that in order for a vow to take effect, **we do require** one to extend the forbidden status of **an item** rendered forbidden by means of **a vow.** Consequently, one cannot become a nazirite by accepting upon himself the status of Samson.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בָּעִינַן דָּבָר הַנִּידָּר, וְשָׁאנֵי גַּבֵּי בְּכוֹר, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״לַה׳״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַבְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it can be explained **that everyone** agrees that **we require** one to extend the forbidden status of **an item** rendered forbidden by means of **a vow. And** according to Rabbi Ya'akov, the *halakha* **is different with regard to a firstborn, as it is written about** this in the verse pertaining to vows: "When a man vows a vow **to the Lord"** (Numbers 30:3). This comes **to include the firstborn** and teach that since the firstborn is consecrated, its status is comparable to animals designated as offerings by means of a vow, and one can extend its forbidden status to another item.

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon dispute a principle called *davar hanidur* — whether *hatfasah* is effective only when the original status came into being through a *neder*, a verbal act of consecration. Rabbi Shimon holds that *hatfasah* requires a *davar hanidur*: the original status must have been created through words. A status that came about automatically — like the status of *nazir* conferred upon Shimshon from the moment of his birth through the angel's declaration, without any human speech act — cannot be transferred through *hatfasah*. Since every formulation by which one becomes a *nazir Shimshon* is an act of *hatfasah* — I want what Shimshon had — and since Shimshon's status was not a *davar hanidur*, the declaration is meaningless.

## Bekhor Debate

### Bekhor Transferability

The *gemara* tests this principle through the parallel case of *bekhor*. A *baraita* records a dispute: if one says *harei alai k'bechor* — this loaf of bread shall be like this firstborn animal — does the *hatfasah* work? A *bekhor* is indeed *kadosh* and forbidden in benefit, but it became *kadosh* automatically, through birth, without any human *neder*. Rabbi Yaakov holds that the *hatfasah* works — the loaf is forbidden. Rabbi Yossi holds that it does not work.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר לָךְ: הָהוּא ״לַה׳״, מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבּוֹת חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei** could have **said to you** in response that **he needs that** expression: **"To the Lord," to include a sin-offering and a guilt-offering.** One may not obligate himself to bring these offerings by means of a vow. They are brought only when one becomes liable due to a transgression. Nevertheless, one can take a vow by extending to another item the forbidden status of a sin-offering or guilt-offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם וּלְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַבְּכוֹר? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם — שֶׁכֵּן מַתְפִּיסָן בְּנֶדֶר, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת הַבְּכוֹר — שֶׁאֵין מַתְפִּיסוֹ בְּנֶדֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rabbi Yosei's explanation: **And what did you see** that indicated to you **to include a sin-offering and a guilt-offering and to exclude a firstborn?** The Gemara answers: **I include a sin-offering and a guilt-offering, as one grants** consecrated status to the animals designated for these offerings **by** means of **a vow,** i.e., the act of designating specific animals for these offerings is comparable to taking a vow. **And I exclude a firstborn, as one does not grant it** consecrated status **by** means of **a vow.**

The *gemara* initially suggests that Rabbi Yehuda follows Rabbi Yaakov — holding that *hatfasah* is effective even when the original status was not imparted through words — and that Rabbi Shimon follows Rabbi Yosei. But the *gemara* then retracts: in fact, everyone agrees that *hatfasah* fundamentally requires a *davar hanidur*. The entire dispute between Rabbi Yaakov and Rabbi Yosei concerns only whether *bekhor* can be considered a *davar hanidur* despite the automatic nature of its sanctity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר לָךְ: בְּכוֹר נָמֵי מַתְפִּיסוֹ בְּנֶדֶר הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא: שֶׁל בֵּית רַבֵּינוּ אָמְרוּ: מִנַּיִן לְנוֹלַד לוֹ בְּכוֹר בְּתוֹךְ עֶדְרוֹ שֶׁמִּצְוָה עָלָיו לְהַקְדִּישׁוֹ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַזָּכָר תַּקְדִּישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ya'akov** could have **said to you** in response: **Also** in the case of **a firstborn, one grants it** consecrated status **by** means of **a vow, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The Sages **of the house of our Rabbi,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **said: From where** is it derived **that** when **a firstborn** male animal **is born in one's herd,** there is **a mitzva for him to consecrate it,** although it is consecrated from the time it is born? **As it is stated:** "All firstborns **males** that are born to your herd and to your flock **you shall sanctify"** (Deuteronomy 15:19).

How so? The verse says *ki tidor neder laHashem* — when you vow a vow to God. The extra phrase *laHashem* is taken to extend the law to include certain cases one might not have expected. Rabbi Yaakov reads *laHashem* as including the *bekhor*: even sanctity that began automatically as belonging to God can be part of the *hatfasah* system. Rabbi Yosei disagrees: *laHashem* is needed to include *chatat* and *asham*, which one might have thought are excluded from *hatfasah* because they are not voluntary offerings but obligatory ones. Their sanctity was imparted through speech — one does sanctify the animal — but they are not classic *nedarim* in the motivational sense; one was compelled to bring them. The *laHashem* establishes that even obligatory offerings are part of the *hatfasah* world. Having used the verse for that purpose, one cannot further stretch it to include *bekhor*, whose sanctity was never imparted through any speech act at all.

### Yosei: No Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר לָךְ: נְהִי דְּמִצְוָה לְהַקְדִּישׁוֹ, אִי לָא מַקְדֵּישׁ לֵיהּ, מִי לָא קָדוֹשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei** could have **said to you** in response: **Granted that** there is **a mitzva to consecrate it.** But **if he does not consecrate it, is it not consecrated** of its own accord? Since a firstborn is forbidden principally because of its inherent sanctity and not because of a vow, one cannot express a vow by extending a firstborn's forbidden status to another item.

Rabbi Yosei's position is thus: the *bekhor* is categorically different from *chatat* and *asham*. A *chatat* and *asham*, even though they are obligatory, have their sanctity imparted through the owner's speech — one goes and sanctifies the animal. The status came through words. Therefore there is reason to include them in the *hatfasah* world: *matfisan bineder*, their status was grabbed through a *neder*-type act. But a *bekhor*: *ein matfiso bineder* — its status does not come through words at all. It is automatically sacred from birth. There is therefore no basis to include it in the *hatfasah* world even through the extension of *laHashem*.

### Yaakov: Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר לָךְ: בְּכוֹר נָמֵי מַתְפִּיסוֹ בְּנֶדֶר הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא: שֶׁל בֵּית רַבֵּינוּ אָמְרוּ: מִנַּיִן לְנוֹלַד לוֹ בְּכוֹר בְּתוֹךְ עֶדְרוֹ שֶׁמִּצְוָה עָלָיו לְהַקְדִּישׁוֹ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַזָּכָר תַּקְדִּישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ya'akov** could have **said to you** in response: **Also** in the case of **a firstborn, one grants it** consecrated status **by** means of **a vow, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The Sages **of the house of our Rabbi,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **said: From where** is it derived **that** when **a firstborn** male animal **is born in one's herd,** there is **a mitzva for him to consecrate it,** although it is consecrated from the time it is born? **As it is stated:** "All firstborns **males** that are born to your herd and to your flock **you shall sanctify"** (Deuteronomy 15:19).

Rabbi Yaakov's response is that a *bekhor* does in fact have a dimension of status imparted through words. A *baraita* from the school of Rebbe teaches: from where do we know that one who has a *bekhor* born in his flock has a *mitzvah* to actively sanctify it — even though it is already automatically *kadosh*? From the verse *hazachar takdish* — the male you shall sanctify. Even though the *bekhor* is already sacred, there is a positive commandment to perform an act of sanctification upon it.

Rabbi Yaakov argues that this obligatory act of sanctification means that the *bekhor*'s status does have a dimension that comes through words, through a speech act of *hakdashah*. Therefore it can participate in the *hatfasah* world. Rabbi Yosei's counter is that this *mitzvah* of sanctification is ultimately superfluous — *i-lo makdish lei mi lo kadosh*? — even if one does not perform this act, the *bekhor* is already sacred. If one's words do not actually affect the *bekhor*'s status — since it would have been sacred regardless — then one has not imparted status through a *neder*, and it cannot serve as the basis for *hatfasah*.

The Rav offers a beautiful application of this dispute. When we recite *kiddush* on *Shabbat* — *zachor et yom hashabbat l'kadesho* — one might ask: does our *kiddush* actually add anything to *Shabbat*'s sanctity, which exists independently of us? Following Rabbi Yosei's logic, one might say that *kiddush* is merely an act of acknowledgment — we are recognizing a holiness that exists without us, and our words change nothing. But following Rabbi Yaakov's logic, one might say that our act of *kiddush* does add a human dimension of *kedushah* to a *Shabbat* that already possesses its own *kedushah*. The parallel is striking.

## Bekhor Debate…

### Shimshon Mystery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי נָזִיר נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״לַה׳״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Both Rabbi Ya'akov and Rabbi Yosei agree that the phrase "to the Lord" indicates that one can take a vow by associating the object of his vow with an item whose prohibition does not stem from a vow. **With regard to a nazirite as well, isn't it written:** "Shall clearly utter a vow, the vow of a nazirite, to consecrate himself **to the Lord"** (Numbers 6:2)? Why doesn't Rabbi Shimon derive from this verse that one can become a nazirite by accepting the naziriteship of Samson, despite the fact that Samson did not accept his naziriteship by means of a vow?

Having established that everyone agrees *hatfasah* requires a *davar hanidur*, the *gemara* leaves unresolved an important question: if this is indeed the rule, how does Rabbi Yehuda permit a person to become a *nazir Shimshon*? If Shimshon never verbally accepted *nezirut*, his status was not a *davar hanidur*, and one cannot transfer it to oneself through *hatfasah*. The *gemara* does not provide a direct answer.

### Rosh Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא, אָמַר שִׁמְעוֹן הַצַּדִּיק: מִיָּמַי לֹא אָכַלְתִּי אֲשַׁם נָזִיר טָמֵא, חוּץ מֵאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁבָּא אֵלַי מִן הַדָּרוֹם, יְפֵה עֵינַיִם וְטוֹב רוֹאִי, וּקְווּצּוֹתָיו סְדוּרוֹת לוֹ תַּלְתַּלִּים. אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: בְּנִי, מָה רָאִיתָ לְשַׁחֵת שֵׂעָר נָאֶה זֶה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** phrase **is required by him for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Shimon HaTzaddik said: In** all **my days** as a priest, **I never ate the guilt-offering of a ritually impure nazirite, apart from** the offering of **one man who came to me from the South,** who had **beautiful eyes and a fine countenance, and his locks were arranged in curls. I said to him: My son, what did you see** to become a nazirite, which would force you **to destroy this beautiful hair,** as a nazirite must cut off all his hair at the conclusion of his term?

The *Rosh* offers three possible resolutions. First: perhaps the verse by *nazir* uses the phrase *l'hazir laHashem*, and this *laHashem* extends to include *nazir Shimshon* — the same way some hold that *laHashem* by *neder* includes *bekhor*, so too it might include the *nezirut* of Shimshon. Second: Rabbi Yehuda may hold that Shimshon in fact did verbally accept *nezirut* upon himself when he came of age and became *bar mitzvah* — this is what the angel's instruction pointed toward, and naturally Shimshon fulfilled this obligation through an act of acceptance. Third: Shimshon's father may have made him a *nazir* when he was a child, as a father has the authority to do — *ish madir et beno b'nazir* — making the *nezirut* rest on a human verbal act after all.

According to these resolutions, everyone agrees that *nazir Shimshon* works by the mechanism of *hatfasah* — by transferring Shimshon's status — not by accepting an independent institution. The question is only how to establish that Shimshon's original status was itself a *davar hanidur*. The debate about Shimshon's *bar mitzvah* acceptance is, as one participant put it, an imaginative but halachically serious reconstruction.

## Shimon HaTzadik

### Refusing Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לִי: רוֹעֶה הָיִיתִי לְאָבִי בְּעִירִי, וְהָלַכְתִּי לִשְׁאוֹב מַיִם מִן הַמַּעְיָין, וְנִסְתַּכַּלְתִּי בַּבָּבוּאָה שֶׁלִּי, וּפָחַז יִצְרִי עָלַי, וּבִיקֵּשׁ לְטוֹרְדֵנִי מִן הָעוֹלָם. אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: רֵיקָה! מִפְּנֵי מָה אַתָּה מִתְגָּאֶה בְּעוֹלָם שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלְּךָ, שֶׁסּוֹפְךָ לִהְיוֹת רִמָּה וְתוֹלֵעָה? הָעֲבוֹדָה שֶׁאֲגַלֵּחֲךָ לַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to me: I was a shepherd for my father in my town, and I went to draw water from the spring, and I looked at my reflection** in the water. **And my** evil **inclination quickly** rose **against me and sought to drive me from the world. I said to** my evil inclination: **Empty one! For what** reason **are you proud in a world that is not yours, as your end is to be maggots and worms** when you die. I swear by **the** Temple **service that I will** become a nazirite and **shave you for** the sake of **Heaven.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמַדְתִּי וּנְשַׁקְתִּיו עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: כְּמוֹתְךָ יִרְבּוּ נְזִירִים בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל — עָלֶיךָ הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״אִישׁ כִּי יַפְלִא לִנְדֹּר נֶדֶר נָזִיר לְהַזִּיר לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Shimon HaTzaddik relates: When he heard his response, **I arose and kissed him on his head,** and **said to him: May there be more nazirites like you in Israel,** whose intentions are noble, and who would not regret their vow of naziriteship even if they became impure. **With regard to you the verse states: "When** either **a man** or a woman **shall clearly utter a vow, the vow of a nazirite, to consecrate himself to the Lord"** (Numbers 6:2). The verse speaks of a vow that is not undertaken out of anger or spite, but purely for the sake of God. The phrase "to the Lord" in this context means: For the sake of Heaven. It cannot be used to teach that if one declares his intention to become a nazirite like Samson, his statement constitutes a nazirite vow.

The *gemara* uses the phrase *l'hazir laHashem* in the verse for a different teaching entirely. A *baraita* reports: Shimon HaTzadik — from the era of the *Beit HaMikdash* — said: *miyamai lo achalti asham nazir tamei* — in all my days I never ate the *asham* brought by a *nazir* who had become *tamei*. He would eat other *ashamos*, which also arise from sin — unintentional sin — and therefore could theoretically raise similar concerns. But a *tamei nazir* was different in his eyes. Becoming *tamei* during *nezirut* means one must start the entire process again from the beginning. The concern the *gemara* had expressed was that such a person might retrospectively regret having ever accepted *nezirut*, rendering the entire *nezirut* retroactively invalid — something like a *neder* made in error — making the animal effectively *chullin ba'azarah*. Therefore, Shimon HaTzadik refused to eat the *asham* of such a person.

### Shepherd Story

He makes one exception. A man came to him from the south, *yafeh einayim v'tov roi* — beautiful eyes, a fine appearance — and his hair was extraordinary, flowing in layered locks. Shimon HaTzadik asked him: my son, what moved you to become a *nazir*? For at the end of his *nezirut*, this man would have to shave off this magnificent hair. This is an interesting irony: we have encountered earlier the suggestion that a *nazir*'s growing hair might itself be a source of attraction and pride, yet here the conclusion of *nezirut* demands that it all be cut off.

### Repentance Motivation

The man replied: I was a shepherd for my father in my village. I went to draw water from the well, and I saw my reflection. My *yetzer hara* was stirred up within me and sought to draw me down from this world into a life of self-indulgence and hedonism. I said to it: *reika* — empty one — why do you take such pride in a world that is not yours, a passing temporal world whose end is worms and maggots? *Ha'avodah* — I swear — I will shave this hair for heaven's sake. And so he became a *nazir* in order to redirect his life toward holiness and away from the pull of vanity.

### Right Motivation

Shimon HaTzadik stood and kissed him on his head, and said: *kamosecha yirbu nezirim b'Yisrael* — may there be many *nezirim* in Israel like you. And about you, the verse says: *ish ki yafla lindor neder nazir lehazir laHashem* — a man who makes his vow as a *nazir* to be separated for God's sake. The *laHashem* of the verse, the *gemara* thus teaches, is not a *halachic* point about the mechanism of *hatfasah*. It is a moral and religious point: *nezirut* must be truly and genuinely for God. A *nezirut* arising from the right motivations — redirecting one's life from the pull of the self toward dedication to the divine — is the *nezirut* that the verse envisions.

This reflects the *gemara*'s ongoing ambivalence about *nezirut*. Throughout *Masechet Nedarim*, the *gemara* was deeply skeptical of *nedarim* — one who vows is called a *chotei*, a sinner. *Nazir* is more nuanced. On one hand, a *nazir* is someone striving for holiness; on the other hand, the *gemara* worries about wrong motivations, about separation from society, about the *nazir tamei* who probably regrets everything. The ending of this *daf* captures that ambivalence beautifully: yes, *yirbu nezirim b'Yisrael* — there should be many *nezirim* — but only, and precisely only, when the motivation is truly *laHashem*.
