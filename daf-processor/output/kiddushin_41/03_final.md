# Kiddushin 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Merit & Mitzvot

### Intro & Prior Mishnah

Daf 41 opens at the very bottom of 40b, in the last of the narrow lines, continuing a discussion about the weight of *mitzvot* and their consequences for the individual. The previous *mishnah* had addressed anyone who performs a single *mitzvah*, enumerating the great good that follows, and conversely, the grave consequences of failing to perform one. The *Gemara* there interpreted this somewhat narrowly — the case of someone exactly balanced at fifty-fifty, for whom a single *mitzvah* tips the scales. That may seem like a narrow reading, since the *mishnah* appears to assign enormous weight to any single *mitzvah* regardless of context. But the *Gemara*'s framing is itself profound: if one is trained to regard oneself as always standing at perfect equilibrium — exactly half merit, half demerit — then every *mitzvah* one is about to perform carries enormous consequence, as does every *mitzvah* one neglects.

### Learning & Character

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֹּל שֶׁיֶּשְׁנוֹ בַּמִּקְרָא וּבַמִּשְׁנָה וּבְדֶרֶךְ אֶרֶץ – לֹא בִּמְהֵרָה הוּא חוֹטֵא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַחוּט הַמְשֻׁלָּשׁ לֹא בִמְהֵרָה יִנָּתֵק״. וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ לֹא בַּמִּקְרָא וְלֹא בַּמִּשְׁנָה וְלֹא בְּדֶרֶךְ אֶרֶץ – אֵינוֹ מִן הַיִּישּׁוּב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Anyone who is engaged in** the study of **Bible, and in** the study of **Mishna, and in** the **desired mode of behavior,** i.e., he performs labor and generally acts in an appropriate manner, **will not be quick to sin, as it is stated: "And a threefold cord is not quickly broken"** (Ecclesiastes 4:12). One who is involved in all three of these activities will not sin easily. **And anyone who does not engage in** the study of **Bible, nor** the study of **Mishna, nor** the **desired mode of behavior, is not part of society,** i.e., he is not considered a civilized person at all.

The *mishnah* that follows shifts from *mitzvot* in the narrow sense to a broader triad: *mikra*, *mishnah*, and *derech eretz*. One who is engaged with all three, the *mishnah* teaches, will not quickly sin — *lo bimhera hu chotei*. The three components map onto distinct dimensions of religious life. *Mikra* — Tanakh — orients a person toward God; it is a framework of religious consciousness. *Mishnah* corresponds to *halakhah*, the legal-observational dimension. *Derech eretz* admits of two interpretations. One reading, associated with the *Rambam* and developed extensively in his writings, is that *derech eretz* means having a livelihood, being embedded in the real world of labor and production — a corrective to the insularity that purely theoretical study can produce. The *Rambam* speaks at length about the dangers of being supported by the community without engaging in productive work. A second reading, which the *Rambam* himself gives in his *Perush HaMishnayot*, understands *derech eretz* as proper conduct between people — good character traits and refined interpersonal behavior. On this reading, the triad encompasses intellectual-religious formation (*mikra*), legal observance (*mishnah*), and character (*derech eretz*).

The *mishnah* is not primarily about reward in this world or the next; it is about what protects a person and keeps him on the right path. Without these three elements, the *mishnah* concludes, one is *eino min hayishuv* — not part of civilized society. With that framing in place, the *Gemara*'s next movement is natural: having discussed *mitzvot*, *mikra*, *mishnah*, and *derech eretz*, it turns to the question of which among them is most fundamental.

## Talmud vs. Maaseh

### Question in Lud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְבָר הָיָה רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וּזְקֵנִים מְסוּבִּין בַּעֲלִיַּת בֵּית נַתְּזָה בְּלוֹד. נִשְׁאֲלָה שְׁאֵילָה זוֹ בִּפְנֵיהֶם: תַּלְמוּד גָּדוֹל אוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה גָּדוֹל?
>
> **Translation:** In connection to the mishna's statement about the importance of Torah study, the Gemara relates the following incident: **And** there **already was** an incident in which **Rabbi Tarfon and the Elders were reclining in the loft of the house of Nit'za in Lod,** when **this question was asked of them:** Is **study greater or is action greater?**

The *Gemara* at the close of 40b records the following: Rabbi Tarfon and the elders were reclining in the upper chamber of the house of Nitzah in Lud — an almost Haggadah-like setting, evoking the famous scene in Bnei Brak. The question posed before them was: *talmud gadol o ma'aseh gadol* — which is greater, learning or doing?

### Tarfon vs. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַעֲנָה רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וְאָמַר: מַעֲשֶׂה גָּדוֹל. נַעֲנָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְאָמַר: תַּלְמוּד גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon answered and said: Action is greater. Rabbi Akiva answered and said: Study is greater.**

Rabbi Tarfon answered that *ma'aseh* — action, performance of *mitzvot* — is greater. Rabbi Akiva answered that *talmud* — learning — is greater.

### Consensus Reached

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַעֲנוּ כּוּלָּם וְאָמְרוּ: תַּלְמוּד גָּדוֹל, שֶׁהַתַּלְמוּד מֵבִיא לִידֵי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Everyone answered and said: Study is greater,** but not as an independent value; rather, it is greater **as study leads to action.**

The assembly then reached a consensus: *talmud gadol sheh-hatalmud meivi lidei ma'aseh* — learning is greater, because learning brings one to action. The formulation is striking. The reasoning offered in support of *talmud*'s greatness is itself an argument that *ma'aseh* is the ultimate goal. Learning is declared superior not because it has independent, intrinsic primacy, but because it is the instrument that generates action. At the axiomatic level, then, it almost sounds as though *ma'aseh* is what matters most — *talmud* is a supremely important *hechsher mitzvah*, a preparatory act that makes the real thing possible.

### Tosafot's Question

This tension is precisely what *Tosafot* identifies. The *Tosafot* opens with *teimah* — a difficulty — drawn from a *Gemara* at the end of the first chapter of Bava Kamma. There, Rabbi Yochanan washed his hands after using the bathroom, put on *tefillin*, and taught the following: at a *hesped*, a eulogy, we say *kiyyem* — this person fulfilled a life of *mitzvos* — but we do not say *limeid*, that he taught Torah. The *Gemara* there asks: but since we say *talmud gadol she-hatalmud meivi lidei ma'aseh*, shouldn't it be an even greater compliment to say that someone taught? Why would *limeid* not be said?

Rashi explains the *Gemara*'s question there as follows: *mi nitlah b'mi? kattan nitlah b'gadol* — the lesser depends on the greater. Since the *Gemara* says *talmud meivi lidei ma'aseh*, that actually betrays the fact that *ma'aseh* is the fundamentally superior category. If so, and we are willing to praise someone as a *ba'al ma'asim*, a person of *mitzvos*, should we not be all the more willing to praise him as someone who learned Torah — since *kiyyem* is already the greater compliment? The implication, then, is that *ma'aseh* is genuinely *gadol*, and the *Gemara* in Bava Kamma takes our *Gemara* as evidence of exactly that. *Tosafot* presses this: from that *Gemara*, it appears that declaring *talmud meivi lidei ma'aseh* actually concedes the supremacy of *ma'aseh* — so which is it?

---

## Hierarchy Analysis

### Personal vs. Teaching

*Tosafot* resolves the tension by distinguishing between different contexts. The key line is: *ha l'migmar ha l'agmurei* — this applies to one's own learning, that applies to teaching others. *Klomar*: if the comparison is between your own private learning and your own performance of *mitzvot*, then *ma'aseh* is indeed preferable. *L'migmar l'didei vadai ma'aseh* — when it comes to your own learning for yourself, doing is certainly more important. But when the comparison is between *ma'aseh* and teaching others — *l'agmurei achrinei vadai adif tfei mi-ma'aseh* — teaching others is at a higher level still, and that is why we do not routinely say *limeid* at a *hesped*, since not everyone reaches that level. The result is a hierarchy: one's own private learning is the lowest tier; performing *mitzvot* is the next; and teaching others is the highest religious achievement of all. If you must choose between going to perform a *mitzvah* and going to open a *Gemara* for your own study, you perform the *mitzvah*. But if you must choose between a *mitzvah* and delivering a *shiur*, you deliver the *shiur*.

### Pedagogy Approach

A second interpretive approach within *Tosafot* reframes the *Gemara*'s question entirely. Perhaps the debate was not about axiomatic religious priority at all, but about pedagogy — what is the right course of religious education for someone just beginning? Should one first focus on learning, or on doing more *mitzvos*? The answer, under this reading, is that one should learn first — *ein am ha-aretz chasid*, an unlearned person cannot be truly pious — spend time learning the *Mishnah Berurah*, understand what is required, and then translate that into action. But for someone who has already learned, *ha-ma'aseh tov yoter mi-limmud* — doing is better than further private study. This approach maintains the hierarchy: ultimately it is about doing; learning is the vehicle that brings it about.

### Talmud Gadol

On this reading, to say *talmud gadol* means only that learning is the practically superior starting point, not that it is axiomatically supreme. *Ma'aseh* remains the end; *talmud* is the preferred means, because *talmud* brings to *ma'aseh* while *ma'aseh* does not reciprocally bring to *talmud*. This is a practical preference, not a statement of fundamental ontological superiority.

## Talmud Torah Theology

### Talmud Torah Keneged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גָּדוֹל תַּלְמוּד שֶׁקָּדַם לְחַלָּה אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה, לִתְרוּמוֹת וּלְמַעַשְׂרוֹת חֲמִשִּׁים וְאַרְבַּע, לִשְׁמִיטִּים שִׁשִּׁים וְאַחַת, לְיוֹבְלוֹת מֵאָה וְשָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says:** Torah **study is greater, as it preceded** the mitzva of separating ***ḥalla*** by **forty years.** The Torah was given to the Jewish people soon after they left Egypt, whereas the mitzva of separating *ḥalla* came into effect only after they entered Eretz Yisrael. And it preceded the mitzva of ***terumot* and tithes** by **fifty-four** years, as the Jews become obligated in these mitzvot only fourteen years after they entered Eretz Yisrael, once they had conquered and divided the land. Furthermore, the Torah preceded the observance of **Sabbatical** Years by **sixty-one** years, as they began to count the seven-year cycle only once they had divided the land. Finally, it preceded the **Jubilee** Years by **103** years, as the fifty-year count to the first Jubilee Year began only after they had divided Eretz Yisrael.

This question does not rest here. For those who invest their entire lives in *yeshiva* and *kollel*, in uninterrupted learning and teaching, the formulation *talmud meivi lidei ma'aseh* raises a serious challenge. It is worth noting that *talmud Torah keneged kulam* — as it appeared in yesterday's *Gemara* — did not mean that the most important *mitzvah* to perform is *talmud Torah*. The context of that list was *ochel peiroteihen ba-olam hazeh* — things whose benefits one enjoys in this world. *Talmud Torah keneged kulam* in that context may simply mean that among all the things whose reward one reaps in this world, learning Torah produces the most such reward — precisely *because talmud meivi lidei ma'aseh*, because that learning translates into doing, shapes character, and transforms one's relationship to other people. It is not necessarily a declaration that learning is intrinsically the most important *mitzvah*; it may mean that learning is the most generative.

### Kodashim Question

Rabbeinu Yonah raises a pointed challenge: if it is all about *ma'aseh*, if the whole purpose of learning is to translate into action, then why do we learn *Masechet Kodashim* — the entire *Seder Kodashim*? Why do we learn all these *masechtos* dealing with laws that are simply not actionable today? One answer offered is that even abstract *halacha* translates into *yirat shamayim* and shapes character traits. But Rabbeinu Yonah's answer is notably not that *talmud Torah* is valuable even without *ma'aseh* — his answer remains firmly within the framework that learning's purpose is ultimately to shape a religious life.

This position finds expression in the *mishnah* in *Pirkei Avot*: *lo ha-midrash hu ha-ikar ela ha-ma'aseh*. Interestingly, Rabbeinu Yonah himself — who elsewhere argues that learning must translate into doing — interprets that line from *Pirkei Avot* quite differently. He reads it as: you do not primarily influence and teach people through what you say. You influence them through what you do. The injunction is not about the hierarchy of learning versus action in the abstract, but about the mechanism of religious influence: modeling through behavior is more powerful than instruction through speech.

### Two Readings

There is a second, fundamentally different way of reading our *Gemara* — one that does not subordinate learning to action. On this reading, both *talmud* and *ma'aseh* are *mitzvos*, each intrinsically important. The debate is simply: which *mitzvah* is greater — shaking a *lulav* or learning *Gemara*? And the answer is that learning *Gemara* is greater because it is, as it were, a *twofer*: you fulfill the *mitzvah* of *talmud Torah* and you also generate other *mitzvos*. Shaking a *lulav* does not typically lead to additional *mitzvos*; learning does. On this reading, *talmud* does not begin at a lower level than *ma'aseh* and earn superiority only through its instrumental power. Rather, it starts at an equal level and then surpasses *ma'aseh* because it also carries the capacity to bring about additional doing. These are two genuinely distinct theological positions regarding the relationship between *talmud Torah* and *mitzvos* — and this *Gemara* sits at the heart of that debate.

## Yosi's Chronology

### Timeline of Mitzvot

The *Gemara* continues with a *baraita*: Rabbi Yosi says *gadol talmud she-kodam la-challah arbaim shanah* — learning is great in that it preceded the *mitzvah* of *challah* by forty years. The Torah commanded *challah*, but the actual performance of that *mitzvah* did not begin until the Jewish people entered the land. Between the giving of the Torah and the first *challah* taken was forty years. For *terumot u-ma'asrot*, 54 years, since those *mitzvos* began only after fourteen years of conquest and settlement following entry into the land. For *shemitah*, 61 years — fourteen years of entering and settling the land, then beginning to count the seven-year *shemitah* cycle. For *yovel*, 103 years — since *yovel* takes effect at the beginning of its fiftieth year, *kasavar yovel mi-techilaso hu mashmit*, meaning the effect of *yovel* kicks in at the start of year fifty, which falls at the end of 49 years of counting after the 54-year baseline, yielding 103.

### Learning Precedence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁהַלִּימּוּד קוֹדֵם לְמַעֲשֶׂה כָּךְ דִּינוֹ קוֹדֵם לְמַעֲשֶׂה, כִּדְרַב הַמְנוּנָא. דְּאָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: אֵין תְּחִילַּת דִּינוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם אֶלָּא עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פּוֹטֵר מַיִם רֵאשִׁית מָדוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** **And just as study** comes **before action,** i.e., the mitzva of Torah study takes precedence over other mitzvot, **so too,** the **judgment** concerning Torah study **precedes** the judgment for **an action** of the performance of a mitzva. This is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rav Hamnuna, as Rav Hamnuna says: The beginning of a person's judgment is only concerning matters of Torah, as it is stated: "The beginning of judgment is as one lets out water"** (Proverbs 17:14). This is understood to refer to the sin of neglecting Torah, as the Torah is compared to water, which brings life to the world.

The point is that just as learning preceded the doing — the Torah was taught and studied long before these *mitzvos* could be performed — so too, judgment and reward for learning come first. This connects to a principle of Rav Hamnuna: *ein techilat dino shel adam ela al divrei Torah* — the first thing a person is judged for is his learning of Torah, as derived from the verse *peter mayim reshit madon*. And correspondingly, just as one is judged first for learning, one is rewarded first for learning: *va-yiten lahem artzos goyim va-amal le'umim yirashu* — God gave them the lands of the nations and the labor of the peoples — *ba-avur yishmeru chukav ve-torasav yintzoru* — because they observed His commandments and guarded His Torah.

The verse links guarding Torah with observing the commandments, and the order matters. The predominant sense throughout this section is not that learning and doing are two entirely unrelated categories ranked independently, with learning simply declared more important. The sense, rather, is that they are tightly related — one leads to the other — and the one that serves as the gateway and generative force is the one for which judgment and reward come first. As noted, that does not preclude the alternative reading, in which learning has equal intrinsic value *plus* additional instrumental value, making it overall superior. But the texture of this *Gemara* leans toward the view that the connection between learning and doing is constitutive, not incidental.

## Social Exclusion

### Unfit Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ לֹא בַּמִּקְרָא וְלֹא בַּמִּשְׁנָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וּפָסוּל לְעֵדוּת. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאוֹכֵל בַּשּׁוּק הֲרֵי זֶה דּוֹמֶה לְכֶלֶב. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים פָּסוּל לְעֵדוּת. אָמַר רַבִּי אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: הֲלָכָה כְּיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **anyone who does not engage in** the study of **Bible, nor** the study of **Mishna,** nor the desired mode of behavior, is not part of society. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And he is disqualified from bearing witness,** as this individual cannot be trusted. **The Sages taught: One who eats in the marketplace is comparable to a dog,** as he disrespects himself through his lack of embarrassment over eating in public. **And some say he is** even **disqualified from bearing witness. Rabbi Idi bar Avin said:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion cited in the name of: **Some say.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the social and legal consequences of failing to engage with *mikra*, *mishnah*, and *derech eretz*. Rabbi Yochanan rules: *kol she-eino lo be-mikra ve-lo be-mishnah ve-lo be-derech eretz — pasul le-eidus* — one who is not engaged in learning in any form, not connected to *halakhah*, and lacking decent character traits or a productive livelihood, is disqualified from testimony. The disqualification is *mi-d'rabanan*, but it reflects the view that such a person stands so far outside the structures of normative society that his reliability cannot be assumed.

### Marketplace Eater

A *baraita* adds: one who eats in the marketplace is like a dog — *ha-ochel ba-shuk harey ze domeh le-kelev* — since dogs eat publicly without social inhibition. Some say this disqualifies one from testimony. Rabbi Idi bar Avin rules that the *halacha* follows that view. The *Maharsha* is skeptical that this literally means eating in public, and suggests it refers to grabbing food from a stall — though as the *Maharsha* himself notes, that would simply make the person a *gazlan*, which raises its own questions.

### Character & Fruit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ בַּר קַפָּרָא: רַגְזָן לֹא עָלְתָה בְּיָדוֹ אֶלָּא רַגְזָנוּתָא. וּלְאָדָם טוֹב מַטְעִימִים אוֹתוֹ מִפְּרִי מַעֲשָׂיו. וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ לֹא בַּמִּקְרָא וְלֹא בַּמִּשְׁנָה וְלֹא בְּדֶרֶךְ אֶרֶץ – דּוֹר הֲנָאָה מִמֶּנּוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְמוֹשַׁב לֵצִים לֹא יָשָׁב״ – מוֹשָׁבוֹ מוֹשַׁב לֵצִים.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **bar Kappara taught: An angry person** **has managed** to acquire **only anger [*ragzanuta*],** i.e., nothing beneficial comes through anger; in the end he is left with nothing but the anger itself. **And a good person is given the fruit of his actions to taste. And** with regard to **any** person **who does not engage in** the study of **Bible, nor** the study of **Mishna, nor** the **desired mode of behavior,** one should **vow to not** derive **benefit from him,** and one should have no contact with him, **as it is stated: "Nor sat in the seat of the scornful"** (Psalms 1:1). The **seat** of this person is certainly the **seat of the scornful,** as he is engaged in nothing but idle matters.

Bar Kappara taught: *ragzan* — an angry person — *lo alta b'yado ela ragzanuto* — gains nothing from his anger but the anger itself. It produces no positive fruit. This connects back to the earlier discussion about *oseh pri* — what does a *mitzvah* or *aveirah* produce in the world? The *ragzan*'s anger produces nothing of value; whatever *pri* it yields is, so to speak, an ulcer. By contrast, a good person — *adam tov* — *mati'imin oto mi-pri ma'asav*, is rewarded with the fruit of his actions. The discussion here is no longer primarily about *mitzvos* and their formal reward but about character itself — what a person's way of being generates in the world.

### Complete Avoidance

Finally, *v'chol she-eino lo be-mikra ve-lo be-mishnah ve-lo be-derech eretz* — one who has no learning, no observance, and neither good character nor a productive livelihood — *dur hana'ah mimenu*: one should make oneself *mudar hana'ah* from such a person, as if by *neder*, keeping maximum distance. The prooftext is *u-v'moshav leitzim lo yashev* — wherever such a person will be found, it will be a gathering of mockers, a place of wasted time and trivial pursuit. This brings the first section of the daf to a close with a rich portrait: beginning from the enormous weight of any single *mitzvah*, moving through the relationship between learning and doing, and arriving at what these values produce — or fail to produce — in a person's character and in their place within society.

## Kiddushin Intro

### Mishnah Recitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ הָאִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית. הָאִישׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ בּוֹ וּבִשְׁלוּחוֹ. הָאִשָּׁה מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בָּהּ וּבִשְׁלוּחָהּ. הָאִישׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ כְּשֶׁהִיא נַעֲרָה, בּוֹ וּבִשְׁלוּחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** May we return to you chapter "a woman is acquired." MISHNA: **A man can betroth** a woman **by himself or by** means of **his agent.** Similarly, **a woman can become betrothed by herself or by** means of **her agent. A man can betroth his daughter** to a man **when she is a young woman,** either **by himself or by** means of **his agent.**

With *Hadran alach ha-ishah nikneis*, the massive first chapter — the longest in all of *Shas* — concludes, and the new *perek* begins. The opening *mishnah* announces: *ha-ish mekadesh bo u-v'shlucho* — a man betroths a woman directly or through his agent. *Ha-ishah mitkadeshet bah u-v'shluchah* — a woman may be betrothed directly, by receiving the money herself, or through her agent. The language shifts notably from the previous chapter's *kinyan* terminology: the *Gemara* later observes that *kinyan* was *lishnah d'kra* — the biblical vocabulary — whereas *kiddushin* is the rabbinic conceptualization of the institution.

### Father's Right

The *mishnah* adds: *ha-ish mekadesh et bito k'she-hi na'arah bo u-v'shlucho* — a father has the right to marry off his daughter when she is a *na'arah*, whether he does so directly or through an agent. This is a significant *chiddush*, since one might have thought that a father who is acting not as principal but as representative of his daughter's interest could not further delegate that authority to an agent. The *mishnah* teaches that he can.

The *na'arah* stage — a legally defined period of roughly six months following the onset of physical maturity at age twelve — might seem like an oddly narrow category to specify. But it is extensively developed in *halacha* because the Torah repeatedly invokes the status of *b'neureha beis aviha*, treating this transitional period as legally significant across many contexts. The *pshat* of the *pesukim* may not have conceived of it as a six-month window; *Chazal* derived that formal limitation. But regardless of the precise derivation, it is the critical period of marriageability and transition, dense with legal consequence.

## Personal Action

### Why Mention Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַשְׁתָּא בִּשְׁלוּחוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ, בּוֹ מִיבַּעְיָא?! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִצְוָה בּוֹ יוֹתֵר מִבִּשְׁלוּחוֹ. כִּי הָא דְּרַב סָפְרָא מְחָרֵיךְ רֵישָׁא, רָבָא מָלַח שִׁיבּוּטָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara starts by questioning the need for the seemingly extraneous *halakha* stated in the mishna: **Now** that the mishna stated that **one can betroth** a woman **by** means of **his agent, is** it **necessary** to state that a man can betroth a woman **by himself? Rav Yosef says:** The mishna writes both *halakhot* to teach that although the betrothal is valid either way, it is **more** fitting that the **mitzva** be performed **by** the man **himself than by** means of **his agent.** This is **like that** story **of Rav Safra,** who **would** himself **singe the head** of an animal on Shabbat eve to prepare it to be eaten on Shabbat, and **Rava,** who **would salt a turbot fish** himself, to fulfill the mitzva to prepare for Shabbat, although this could have been done by others.

The *Gemara* asks the obvious question: *hashta bi-shlucho mekadesh, bo miba'ya* — if a man can betroth through an agent, does it even need to be stated that he can do it himself? Of course he can do it personally. So why does the *mishnah* mention both? Rav Yosef answers: *mitzvah bo yoter mibishlucho* — to teach that it is preferable to perform the act personally. The mention of both modes is not redundant; it is instructive. The *mishnah* includes the *bo* — the personal performance — precisely to convey that while the *shlucho* is entirely valid, the *bo* is the preferred mode.

### Body Engagement

This principle extends well beyond *kiddushin*. Rav Safra would char the head of the fish or animal himself in preparation for *Shabbos*; Rava would salt the fish himself — despite these being great *Amoraim* whose time was extraordinarily valuable. They engaged their bodies personally in *kvod Shabbos* because *mitzvah bo yoter mi-bishlucho* is a general principle. Rashi explains: *dechi asi gufa be-mitzvot, mekabel schar tvei* — when one engages one's own body in a *mitzvah*, one receives greater reward. But this is not simply an accounting of additional brownie points for greater effort. The deeper point, connected to the earlier discussion of *ochel peiroteihem*, is that personal engagement produces a different kind of connection. When you personally do something, it impacts you differently — it enters your *kishkes*, as it were. The *mitzvah* becomes more real, more embodied, more yours. That is what it means to engage one's very person in the act.

### Man's Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּהָא אִיסּוּרָא נָמֵי אִית בֵּהּ, כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לָאָדָם שֶׁיְּקַדֵּשׁ אֶת הָאִשָּׁה עַד שֶׁיִּרְאֶנָּה, שֶׁמָּא יִרְאֶה בָּהּ דָּבָר מְגוּנֶּה וְתִתְגַּנֶּה עָלָיו, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say: With regard to this** particular mitzva of betrothal, **it also involves a prohibition, in accordance with that** which **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: It is forbidden for a man to betroth a woman until he sees her, lest he see something repulsive in her** after the betrothal, **and she will become repugnant to him,** which will cause him to hate her. **And** to prevent this violation of what **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And you shall love your neighbor as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18), the Sages ruled that a man must betroth a woman in person, to ensure that he approves of her.

Some say — *ika de-amri* — that for the man, failing to perform personally is not merely suboptimal but rises to the level of an actual *issur*. The source for this is a ruling of Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav, which the *Gemara* will now introduce: *assur le-adam she-yekadesh isha ad she-yirena* — a man is forbidden to betroth a woman until he has seen her first. This generates an *issur* dimension to the use of an agent when it would result in betrothing a woman one has never seen.

## Seeing the Woman

### Rav's Statement

Rav Yehudah says in the name of Rav: it is forbidden for a man to betroth a woman until he has seen her. This was entirely realistic as a concern in a society where betrothal was often arranged through intermediaries, sometimes without any prior meeting between the parties. Sending an agent to perform the act of *kiddushin* could easily mean that the man has never set eyes on his future wife.

### Lest Repulsion

The reason given is: *shema yireh bah davar maguneh ve-titganeh alav* — lest after the marriage he find something repulsive about her and she become repulsive to him. This would violate the obligation of *ve-ahavta le-re'acha kamocha* — love your neighbor as yourself — which extends to one's wife as a fellow human being.

### Marriage Context

One might initially find this rationale somewhat flat — is the whole basis for spousal affection merely the generic commandment to love one's neighbor? But the *Gemara* must be read against its social context. This is not a society in which marriage is primarily an emotional or romantic institution. The scenario being addressed is one where the first time a man might see his wife is at the moment of betrothal, when he hands her the ring. In such a world, where romantic love plays little role in the formation of marriages and people often remain married under deeply imperfect conditions — because divorce, while technically available, involves a *ketubah* payment and carries social weight — it remains genuinely important to ensure that the couple can at minimum regard each other without visceral repulsion. The *ahavah* invoked here is not the *ahavah* of romance; it is the baseline of human decency one owes to any fellow person. And yet there is something profound in insisting on even that baseline. Even in a society where marriage is fundamentally a practical institution, where couples may live together for decades without deep emotional connection, the rabbis are concerned that something as elemental as mutual contempt should not be the starting point.

## Men vs. Women

### Women Allowed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב יוֹסֵף – אַסֵּיפָא אִיתְּמַר, הָאִשָּׁה מִתְקַדֶּשֶׁת בָּהּ וּבִשְׁלוּחָהּ. הַשְׁתָּא בִּשְׁלוּחָהּ מִיקַּדְּשָׁא בָּהּ מִיבַּעְיָא?! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מִצְוָה בָּהּ יוֹתֵר מִבִּשְׁלוּחָהּ. כִּי הָא דְּרַב סָפְרָא מְחָרֵיךְ רֵישָׁא, רָבָא מָלַח שִׁיבּוּטָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if there is a prohibition against a man betrothing a women by means of an agent, then **when** the statement of **Rav Yosef was stated,** that it is merely preferable that the betrothal be performed without an agent, **it was stated with regard to the latter clause** of the mishna: **A woman can become betrothed by herself or by** means of **her agent. Now** that the mishna stated that **she can become betrothed by** means of **her agent, is** it **necessary** to state that she can become betrothed **by herself?** It was in response to this that **Rav Yosef says:** It is **more** fitting that the **mitzva** be performed **by** the woman **herself than by** means of **her agent.** This is **like that** story **of Rav Safra,** who **would** himself **singe the head** of an animal on Shabbat eve to prepare it to be eaten on Shabbat, and **Rava,** who **would** himself **salt a turbot fish.**

The *Gemara* clarifies a textual point about the *mishnah*: when it states *mitzvah bo yoter mi-bishlucho*, this was said specifically about the *seifa* — the latter clause — regarding the woman. *Ha-ishah mitkadeshet bah u-v'shluchah* — for her, it is a *mitzvah* to participate personally, but if she chooses to use an agent, *issur leit bah* — there is no prohibition. For the man, *ika de-amri* that there is an *issur* dimension; for the woman, there is no such *issur*.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בְּהָא אִיסּוּרָא לֵית בַּהּ, כִּדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: טָב לְמֵיתַב טַן דּוּ מִלְּמֵיתַב אַרְמְלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But in this** case of a woman who appoints an agent, **there is no prohibition, as that** which **Reish Lakish** said. **As Reish Lakish said:** Women have a saying: **It is better to sit** as **two bodies,** i.e., be married, **than to sit** lonely like **a widow.** Once a woman has decided to marry, she will accept any husband whose betrothal her agent accepts on her behalf, and there is no concern that she will find her betrothed repulsive and violate the mitzva of loving one's neighbor like oneself.

Why is there no concern on the woman's side about *ve-ahavta le-re'acha kamocha* — that she might find her husband repulsive? Because of the principle of Reish Lakish: *tav lemeitav tan du mi-lemeitav armelu* — better to sit in tandem than to live as a widow, better to be married, even imperfectly, than to live alone. The presumption is that women are willing to settle to a greater degree than men. Even if a woman does not find her husband particularly appealing, she will not experience the same degree of visceral antagonism that would result on the man's side. Therefore the concern that generates the *issur* for the man — that he will find her repulsive and this will corrode the marriage — does not apply with equal force to the woman.

The *Gemara* acknowledges that this principle carries implications beyond the sociological. The presumption that women are more tolerant of imperfect situations has, in other contexts, fed into a certain passivity around issues of *agunah* — a motivation to not be proactive about resolving situations where a woman is unable to receive a *get*, on the assumption that she can tolerate the circumstance. That is a significant and troubling extrapolation, and the *Gemara*'s observation here about differential marital dynamics should not be read as an endorsement of those downstream applications.

## Marriage as Mitzvah

### Language Question

The use of the term *mitzvah* — *mitzvah bo yoter mi-bishlucho* — raises a fundamental question that the *Gemara* has now prompted. The previous chapter analyzed *kiddushin* through the lens of *kinyanim*, commercial-legal transactions. But now the text speaks of *mitzvah*. Why should getting married be a *mitzvah*? If the earlier analysis framed it as an economic institution — the acquisition of a wife — then the language of *mitzvah* seems incongruous, just as one would not invoke *mitzvah bo yoter mi-bishlucho* when purchasing a boat.

### Rishonim Dispute

This prompts a genuine debate among the *Rishonim*. Many *Rishonim* hold that marriage is not a *mitzvah* per se. The majority of those who do consider it a *mitzvah* classify it as a *hechsher mitzvah* — a preparatory act that enables the fulfillment of *pru urvu*, the *mitzvah* to be fruitful and multiply. This is a natural fit: just as *kvod Shabbos* is not itself a Torah-level *mitzvah* but is a *hechsher* for *Shabbos*, so too *kiddushin* may be a *hechsher* for *pru urvu*. The counterargument, raised by the *Rosh*, is that one fulfills *pru urvu* even through children born outside of marriage — even through a *mamzer* — so that *kiddushin* cannot technically be a *hechsher* for the *mitzvah* if the *mitzvah* can be fulfilled without it.

### Rambam's View

The *Rambam*'s position is notably complex. When the *Rambam* discusses the *mitzvah* of *pru urvu*, he frames it as a two-part *mitzvah*: first, to marry a woman, and second, to have children with one's wife. He thus embeds marriage within the *mitzvah* of *pru urvu* itself — at least as the primary mode of fulfillment. Whether the *Rambam* would say one fails to fulfill *pru urvu* entirely through children born out of wedlock is not fully resolved in his writings. The *Rambam* also emphasizes an *issur* dimension: relations outside of marriage are forbidden, and thus one must marry before having children. On that reading, the *mitzvah* to marry is really a necessary condition for avoiding an *issur*, which makes the language of *mitzvah bo yoter mi-bishlucho* somewhat difficult to apply.

### Two Stages

A third dimension of the *Rambam*'s position draws attention to a structural *chiddush* of the Torah: marriage is a two-stage process. Before the Torah, one would proceed directly to living together. The Torah introduces the requirement that *kiddushin* precede *nisuin* — commitment must precede cohabitation. The *Rambam* makes a great deal of this distinction. One way to understand its significance is as follows: if marriage were defined entirely by the lived reality of the relationship — by the daily experience of life together — then any period of difficulty, conflict, or estrangement would undermine the marriage at its foundations. But if there is a prior commitment, a formal contractual bond that precedes and is independent of the lived reality, then the marriage has an anchor that can survive difficult periods. The couple committed before they began living together, and that commitment remains even when the current experience of the marriage is challenging. The *mitzvah* on the *Rambam*'s third reading would then be: when you wish to marry, do so in the manner that the Torah established — go through *kiddushin* first, then *nisuin*, honoring the structure the Torah created.

Practically speaking, it is the first reading — *kiddushin* as *hechsher* for *pru urvu* — that best supports the language of *mitzvah bo yoter mi-bishlucho*, with its parallel to *kvod Shabbos*. The other readings are harder to fit into that mold. It is also worth noting that the formulation *mitzvah bah yoter mi-bishlucha* is applied to the woman as well, even though *pru urvu* is formally the *mitzvah* of the man and not the woman according to standard *halachic* ruling — suggesting the term *mitzvah* may be used here in a looser, less technically precise sense.

The *Gemara* in *Yevamos* discusses whether a man who has already fulfilled *pru urvu* should nonetheless remain married after his wife's death. One answer given there is *lo tov heiyot ha-adam levado* — it is not good for a person to be alone, a value rooted in *Bereishit*. That fundamental human dimension of marriage — the value of shared life — is not captured by the formal *halachic* language of *mitzvah* and *hechsher mitzvah*, but it is real and acknowledged, even if it does not generate formal *halachic* terminology in the present context.

## Minor Daughter

### Minor Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִישׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ כְּשֶׁהִיא נַעֲרָה. כְּשֶׁהִיא נַעֲרָה – אִין, כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה – לָא. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיְּקַדֵּשׁ אֶת בִּתּוֹ כְּשֶׁהִיא קְטַנָּה, עַד שֶׁתִּגְדַּל וְתֹאמַר: ״בִּפְלוֹנִי אֲנִי רוֹצָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **A man can betroth his daughter** to a man **when she is a young woman.** The Gemara infers: **When she is a young woman, yes,** he can betroth her; **when she is a minor, no,** he cannot betroth her. This statement **supports** the opinion of **Rav, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says, and some say** it was said by **Rabbi Elazar: It is prohibited for a person to betroth his daughter** to a man **when she is a minor, until** such time **that she grows up and says: I want** to marry **so-and-so.** If a father betroths his daughter when she is a minor and incapable of forming an opinion of the husband, she may later find herself married to someone she does not like.

The *mishnah* states that a father betroths his daughter when she is a *na'arah*. The *Gemara* reads this as implying: when she is a *na'arah*, yes — but when she is a *ketanah*, a minor, he should not do so. This supports the ruling of Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav (with some attributing it to Rabbi Elazar): *assur le-adam she-yekadesh et bito ke-she-hi ketanah* — it is forbidden for a father to betroth his minor daughter.

### Consent Required

The reason is: *ad she-tigdal ve-tomar bi-ploni ani rotzah* — she must grow up and say, "I want to marry this specific person." Marriage requires her mature, personal assent to this particular man, and that is simply impossible when she is a minor.

### Adult vs. Minor

*Tosafot* anticipates an obvious question: if we earlier said that a woman can betroth through an agent without having seen the man, relying on the principle that women are willing to settle — *tav lemeitav tan du mi-lemeitav armelu* — why is it *assur* for a father to betroth his minor daughter? *Tosafot* draws a critical distinction. When an adult woman says, "Go find me a husband," she is making a conscious, autonomous decision to accept whoever her agent selects. She has effectively consented in advance to the arrangement and to whatever outcome it produces, and we trust that she will make her peace with it. But a minor never made any such declaration. She never agreed to be betrothed to an unknown party through her father's choice. In the adult woman's case, her willingness to settle was expressed through her own decision to delegate; in the minor's case, no such expression of consent ever occurred. Therefore the concern — that she will find her husband repulsive and the marriage will be deeply troubled — applies on her end as well, and it is *assur*.

### Tosafot Modern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי שׁוֹנִים לְקַדֵּשׁ בְּנוֹתֵיהֶם אַפִּילוּ קְטַנּוֹת, הַיְנוּ מִשּׁוּם שֶׁבְּכׇל יוֹם וָיוֹם הַגָּלוּת מִתְגַּבֶּרֶת עָלֵינוּ, וּשׁמָּא לְאַחַר זְמַן לֹא תִהְיֶה שְׁנוּיָּה בְּיָדוֹ וְתֵשֵׁב בִּתּוֹ אַגּוּנָה לָעַד.

The essay, as provided, includes a paraphrase of what Tosafot says, but does not contain the exact Hebrew/Aramaic text of the Tosafot statement. However, based on the Sefaria text provided, I can only work with what is there. Since the exact Tosafot text does not appear in the Sefaria blocks provided, I will not insert a blockquote here, but the essay text continues as is.

*Tosafot* then adds a poignant comment: *ve-achshav she-anu nogahin le-kadesh benoteinu afilu ketanot* — in our time, we do betroth our daughters even when they are minors. *Haynu mishum she-be-chol yom ve-yom ha-galut mitgaber aleinu* — because each day the exile presses harder upon us. With the Crusades and the mounting persecutions, if a father has the means *now* to provide his daughter with a dowry — *nadan* — and secure her future, *shema le-achar zeman lo yiheyeh sipek be-yado ve-teshev bito agunah le-olam* — lest a time come when he lacks the means and his daughter remains an *agunah* forever, without a husband to protect and provide for her. *Tosafot* thus acknowledges the practice as a deviation from the ideal, justified only by the desperate circumstances of exile.

This passage also raises a broader point. The Torah is replete with cases of fathers marrying off daughters — *natati et biti la-ish hazeh* and similar formulations appear throughout. Yet *Chazal* look at all of this and say it is *assur le-chatchila*. This reflects an important methodological principle: not everything that appears in the Torah, even repeatedly and without any critical framing, should be read as a *mitzvah* or a *le-chatchila* ideal. *Chazal* were capable of recognizing that the Torah sometimes accommodates social realities without endorsing them as the preferred norm.

## Agency Sources

### Divorce Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלִיחוּת מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשִׁלַּח״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that an agent has the power to effect betrothal. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that there is halakhic **agency?** The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah states with regard to one who divorces his wife: "That he writes her a bill of divorce, and gives it in her hand, and sends her [*veshilleḥah*] out of his house" (Deuteronomy 24:1). The verse employs the verb: **And he sends [*veshillaḥ*].** The fact that the verse employs the term *veshillaḥ*, as opposed to another verb denoting divorce, *vegereshah*, **teaches that he can appoint an agent [*shaliaḥ*],** as both words share the root *shin*, *lamed*, *ḥet*. The husband does not have to personally give his wife the bill of divorce.

Having discussed *shlichut* in the context of the *mishnah*, the *Gemara* now asks: *shlichut minalan* — what is the source that one can appoint an agent at all? The *baraita* derives it from the verse about divorce: *ve-shilcha mi-beito* — he shall send her from his house. From the word *ve-shalach* — the active sending — we learn *melamed she-oseh shaliach*, that a man can appoint an agent to give the *get*. This establishes *shlichut* in the context of *girushin*.

### Grammar Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְשִׁלְּחָהּ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִיא עוֹשָׂה ״שָׁלִיחַ״. ״וְשִׁלַּח״ ״וְשִׁלְּחָהּ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהַשָּׁלִיחַ עוֹשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, the fact that the term **"and he sends her [*veshilleḥah*]"** can also be read as: And she sends [*veshalleḥa*], **teaches that she** too **can appoint an agent** to accept her bill of divorce. Furthermore, in this same passage the verb is repeated in the phrases **"and he sends," "and he sends her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1–3), which serves to **teach that an agent can appoint another agent.**

From the same verse, the *Gemara* reads *ve-shalcha* — taking the final *heh* without its *mappik*, converting the verb to feminine — to teach *melamed she-ossa shaliach*, that the woman can appoint an agent to receive the *get* on her behalf. And from yet another reading of *ve-shalach ve-shalcha*, the *Gemara* derives *melamed she-shaliach oseh shaliach* — that an agent can in turn appoint a sub-agent. (The full complexity of whether *milei mimsran le-shaliach* limits this in the context of *gittin* is not the *Gemara*'s focus here.)

That establishes *shlichut* from *girushin*. But how do we know *shlichut* works for *kiddushin*?

### Terumah Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן בְּגֵירוּשִׁין, בְּקִידּוּשִׁין מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּיָלֵיף מִגֵּירוּשִׁין – מָה לְגֵירוּשִׁין, שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ! אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָצְאָה״ ״וְהָיְתָה״ – מַקִּישׁ הֲוָיָה לִיצִיאָה, מָה יְצִיאָה מְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ – אַף הֲוָיָה נָמֵי מְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for agency **with regard to divorce; from where do we** derive that there is agency **with regard to betrothal? And if you would say that** it **is derived from divorce,** i.e., just as a wife can be divorced from her husband by means of an agent, she can become betrothed to him in the same way, the two cases are not similar: **What** is unique **about divorce is that it** can be effected **against her will,** while betrothal cannot. Therefore, a means of effecting divorce cannot necessarily be used to effect betrothal. The Gemara answers: **The verse states: "And she departs** out of his house, and goes **and becomes** another man's wife" (Deuteronomy 24:2). Because the verse **juxtaposes becoming** married **to leaving** a marriage, **just as** a husband can **appoint an agent** for the purpose of **leaving** a marriage, **so too he** can **appoint an agent** for the purpose of **becoming** married.

One might suggest simply learning *kiddushin* from *girushin*, but the *Gemara* rejects this: *mah girushin she-ken yeshnan be-al korchah* — *girushin* can be effectuated against the woman's will. A one-sided transaction in which one party holds all authority is structurally simpler: when you are fully in control, it is more obvious that you can delegate. *Kiddushin*, by contrast, requires a meeting of minds between two parties; neither side holds unilateral power. The capacity to delegate in such a context is less obvious and cannot simply be inferred from *girushin*. The *Gemara* therefore derives *shlichut* for *kiddushin* from a different verse: *ve-yatz'ah ve-hayta* — she shall go out and become [the wife of another man]. The *Gemara* reads *havaya* (becoming a wife) as parallel to *yetziah* (leaving through divorce): *mah yetziah mashvei shaliach, af havaya nami mashvei shaliach* — just as you can appoint an agent for divorce, so too for betrothal.

### Comparison with Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דִּתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לִשְׁלוּחוֹ: ״צֵא תְּרוֹם״ – תּוֹרֵם כְּדַעַת בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. וְאִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ דַּעַת בַּעַל הַבַּיִת – תּוֹרֵם בְּבֵינוֹנִית, אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** there is **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 4:4): In the case of **one who says to his agent: Go out** and **separate *teruma*** from my field's produce for me, the agent **must separate** *teruma* **in accordance with the mindset of the owner.** He must separate the amount that he assumes the owner would want to give, as there is no fixed measure for the amount that one must set aside as *teruma*. A generous person would give as much as one-fortieth of the produce as *teruma*, while a stingy person would give one-sixtieth. **And if he does not know the mindset of the owner, he separates an intermediate** measure, which is **one-fiftieth** of the produce.

For *terumah*, the *Gemara* derives *shlichut* from the verse *gam atem* — "also you" — in the context of separating *trumat ma'aser*. The word *gam* — "also" — is read to include one's representative: *le-rabot et ha-shaliach*, even your agent counts as "you" in this context.

Why not simply learn *shlichut* for *kiddushin* from *girushin*, and for *terumah* from *girushin* as well? Because each of these domains has distinctive characteristics that make a simple derivation impossible. *Girushin* involves a fundamentally one-sided, passive transaction; *kiddushin* is bilateral and active. *Terumah* involves *kedushah* — a sanctity — that *girushin*, a secular matter, lacks. These differences mean that each domain requires its own source for *shlichut*, which the *Gemara* will now elaborate systematically.

## Korban Pesach

### Pesach Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: חֲבוּרָה שֶׁאָבַד פִּסְחָהּ, וְאָמְרוּ לְאֶחָד: ״צֵא וּבַקֵּשׁ וּשְׁחוֹט עָלֵינוּ״, וְהָלַךְ וּמָצָא וְשָׁחַט, וְהֵן לָקְחוּ וְשָׁחֲטוּ, אִם שֶׁלּוֹ נִשְׁחַט רִאשׁוֹן – הוּא אוֹכֵל מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְהֵם אוֹכְלִים וְשׁוֹתִים עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further clarifies the source for agency: Generally, the Paschal offering was brought by a group of people together. **But** there is **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 98b): In the case of **a group whose Paschal offering was lost** before it was sacrificed, **and they said to one** member of the group: **Go and search** for our Paschal offering, **and** when you find it, **slaughter** it **on our** behalf; **and** this person **went and found** the lost animal **and slaughtered** it on behalf of the entire group, **but** meanwhile, despairing of his return, **they took** a different animal **and slaughtered** it as a Paschal offering, the *halakha* is as follows: **If his** Paschal offering **was slaughtered first, he eats from his** offering, **and they eat and drink with him.** This demonstrates that one can act as an agent to slaughter the Paschal offering.

The *Gemara* introduces a third domain of *shlichut* through a *mishnah*: a group — *chabura* — that has pooled to bring a *korban Pesach* together finds that their lamb has run away. They send one member: *tze u-vakesh u-shchot aleinu* — go find it and slaughter it for us. He goes, finds the lamb, and *shechts* it at the *Beit Hamikdash*. Meanwhile, unsure whether he will succeed, they go and purchase another lamb and *shecht* it as well. The *mishnah* works out the rules for which *korban* is valid depending on the order of events — but for the *Gemara*'s purposes, the *case* establishes that the members of the *chabura* can appoint one of their number as *shaliach* to *shecht* the *korban Pesach* on behalf of all.

### Kodashim Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן? וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּיָלֵיף מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ, שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן חוֹל אֵצֶל קָדָשִׁים!
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive this *halakha*, that one can appoint an agent to slaughter an offering? **And if you would say that** it **is derived from these** other instances of agency, i.e., divorce and *teruma*, one could refute this derivation by saying: **What** is unique **about these is that they are** considered **non-sacred** matters **relative to offerings,** and perhaps offerings have their own set of *halakhot*.

The *chiddush* of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's derivation is this: if the *pasuk* had simply said one person *shechts* for the household, one might read that as the basic *structure* of the *mitzvah* — the *korban Pesach* just works that way, one person *shechts* on behalf of the group, not as agency but as the fundamental mode. But the *pasuk* says *kol ha-kehal* — the entire congregation — each individual — must *shecht*. That is an individual obligation. And yet individual *shechita* is clearly not what happens; one person *shechts*. The only explanation is that this one person is discharging each individual's personal obligation through *shlichut*. That is the proof.

## Set Theory Logic

### Kodashim Traits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: מִנַּיִן שֶׁשְּׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁחֲטוּ אֹתוֹ כֹּל קְהַל עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל בֵּין הָעַרְבָּיִם״, וְכִי כָּל הַקָּהָל כּוּלָּן שׁוֹחֲטִין? וַהֲלֹא אֵינוֹ שׁוֹחֵט אֶלָּא אֶחָד! אֶלָּא מִכָּאן שֶׁשְּׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** that **which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa** says, **as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa says: From where** is it derived **that** the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself? As it is stated** with regard to the Paschal offering: **"And the whole assembly of the congregation of Israel shall slaughter it in the afternoon"** (Exodus 12:6). **Is it so** that **the whole assembly slaughters** the offering? **But only one** person from each group **slaughters** it. **Rather,** it can be derived **from here that** the legal status of **a person's agent is like** that of **himself.**

Having established *shlichut* in three domains — *girushin*, *terumah*, and *korban Pesach* (*kodashim*) — the *Gemara* asks why each cannot simply be derived from the others, making the separate sources unnecessary. The answer lies in the distinctive characteristics of each domain, and the *Gemara* works through the logic systematically.

*Kodashim* (as represented by the *korban Pesach*) has three notable features: it involves *kedushah* (holiness), it is governed by *machshava* (thought — one can sanctify a *korban* and affect its legal status through intention alone), and *ma'aseihem al yedei shaliach* — actions in the *Beit Hamikdash* are routinely performed through representatives, the *kohanim* acting on behalf of the owners. This third feature means *kodashim* is an environment where the use of agents is the norm, not the exception, which makes it less surprising that *shlichut* would apply there.

### Terumah Traits

*Terumah* shares two of these features: *kedushah* (it is a sanctified portion given to the *kohanim*) and *machshava* (one can designate a portion as *terumah* through thought or speech alone, without a formal physical act). However, *terumah* does not have the feature of *ma'aseihem al yedei shaliach* — the routine delegation of all actions to representatives — in the same way *kodashim* does. The owner's own act of separation is the primary mode.

### Girushin Traits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּקָדָשִׁים, וְנֵיתֵי הָנָךְ וְנִיגְמְרוּ מִינֵּיהּ! מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִפְרַךְ: מָה לְקָדָשִׁים, שֶׁכֵּן רוֹב מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן עַל יְדֵי שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** In light of that exposition, the Gemara asks: **Let the Merciful One write** in the Torah the *halakha* of an agent **with regard to offerings, and these,** i.e., agency in the cases of betrothal, divorce, and *teruma*, **can come and be derived from it.** The Gemara answers: Agency in these matters cannot be derived in this manner **because** the derivation **can be refuted: What** is unique **about offerings** is that the **majority of their actions** are performed **by** means of **an agent.** Since most of the sacrificial service is performed by priests, who serve as the agents of those bringing the offerings, the general *halakha* of agency cannot be derived from there.

*Girushin*, by contrast, is essentially *chol* — secular. It involves no *kedushah* whatsoever. The *get* is not a sanctified object; the divorce is not a religious sanctification in the sense of *terumah* or *kodashim*. *Girushin* also has no element of *machshava* affecting the legal status of the transaction — it is a formal legal act, not one governed by intention in the way that sanctification is.

### Universal Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא מֵחֲדָא לָא אָתְיָא, תֵּיתֵי חֲדָא מִתַּרְתֵּי? – הָא תֵּיתֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: For the reasons stated, the *halakha* of agency **cannot be derived** for any **one** of these **from** any other **one** of them. Still, **derive one** of these **from** the other **two.** The Gemara clarifies: **Which** of them will be **derived** from the others?

The *Gemara* thus applies set theory. One cannot learn *shlichut* universally from any two domains that share a distinctive characteristic, because one might argue the shared characteristic is what enables *shlichut* in those domains. *Kodashim* and *terumah* both share *kedushah* and *machshava* — so learning from them together does not prove *shlichut* applies where neither is present. *Terumah* and *girushin* together share a characteristic on the other end: they are both at a level of holiness *less than* that of *kodashim* — and one might argue that *shlichut* requires a lower threshold of holiness than full *kodashim*. That shared feature prevents learning the rule universally from this pair.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּקָדָשִׁים וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ, שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן חוֹל אֵצֶל קֳדָשִׁים. לָא נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּגֵירוּשִׁין וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ, שֶׁכֵּן יֶשְׁנָן בְּמַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** If you say: **Let the Merciful One not write** the *halakha* of agency **with regard to offerings and derive** agency with regard to offerings **from these,** i.e., divorce and *teruma*, that derivation can be refuted: **What** is unique **about these is that they are** considered **non-sacred** matters **relative to offerings,** as even *teruma* is not sacred compared to offerings, and sacred procedures such as offerings may have their own *halakhot*. If you say: **Let the Merciful One not write** the *halakha* of agency **with regard to divorce, and derive** agency with regard to divorce **from these,** i.e., *teruma* and offerings, this too can be refuted: **What** is unique **about these is they can be** designated **by means of thought,** and therefore may be easier to effect than divorce.

But *girushin* and *kodashim* share *no* common characteristic. *Girushin* is *chol*; *kodashim* is the highest level of *kedushah*. *Girushin* has no *machshava*; *kodashim* does. *Girushin* has no routine delegation through agents; *kodashim* does routinely involve agents. Since these two domains share absolutely nothing, and yet *shlichut* applies to both, the principle must be universal — it applies regardless of the presence or absence of any of these features. The *Gemara* concludes accordingly: *shlichut* is established universally from the combination of *girushin* and *kodashim*. The *Gemara* then notes that tomorrow's discussion will address why, given this, the independent verse for *terumah* was nonetheless necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּתְרוּמָה, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ! הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is the third derivation that is possible: **Let the Merciful One not write** the *halakha* of agency **with regard to *teruma*, and derive** agency **from these,** i.e., offerings and divorce. Although *teruma* is sacred, offerings are more sacred, and yet the *halakha* of agency applies to them. Although the separating of *teruma* can be accomplished by means of thought, as opposed to divorce, offerings can also be designated by means of thought. The Gemara answers: It is **indeed** so; this is the derivation for the applicability of agency to *teruma*.
