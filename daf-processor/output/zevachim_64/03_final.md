# Zevachim 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Southwest Corner

### Locating SW Corner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים כּוּ׳. חַטַּאת הָעוֹף – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that there were **three matters** for which the portion of the southwest corner below the red line served as the proper location: The sacrificing of a bird sin offering, and the bringing of the meal offerings near the altar, and pouring the remaining blood. The Gemara cites sources to prove that each of these three matters was performed at the southwest corner: The source with regard to **the bird sin offering** is **that which we said** earlier (63b), that it is derived from a comparison to meal offerings.

The *daf* opens on the second line of 64a, continuing the *mishnah*'s discussion of bird sacrifices and their connection to the southwest corner of the *mizbeach*. The *mishnah* had established that three things were done at the bottom of that corner and three at the top. The three performed below are the *chatas ha-of* (the bird sin offering), the *mincha*, and the spilling of the remainder of the blood.

The *gemara* begins by asking: how do we know that the *chatas ha-of* is performed at the southwest corner? The answer was derived on the previous *daf* through a textual link between the *minchas chotei* and the *chatas ha-of*. The *pasuk* states *vehigisha el hamizbeach* — you shall bring it near to the altar — and specifies that this contact occurs *lifnei Hashem*. "The face of the *mizbeach*" refers to the south, from which one ascends the ramp, and *lifnei Hashem* refers to the west, which faces the *ulam* and the *heichal*. The intersection of south and west yields the southwest corner. It is from this derivation regarding the *mincha* — specifically the act of *hagashah*, the touching of the *mincha* to the *mizbeach* — that we also locate the *chatas ha-of*.

As for *shiurei hadam* — the remainder of the blood — the *gemara* derives its location from the *pasuk*: *ve'et kol dam hapar yishpoch el yesod mizbach hamizbeach*, "all the remnant of the blood you shall pour at the base of the altar." The *gemara* links the blood of a *chatas* performed atop the *mizbeach* to the procedure on *Yom Kippur* inside the *heichal*, where upon exiting one pours the remainder at the western base, which faces the *ulam* and the *heichal*. By analogy, when descending from the *mizbeach* after a *chatas*, one pours the remainder at the base immediately facing the descent — that is, the southern side — which places it precisely at the southwest corner.

### Why SW: No Base

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגָּשׁוֹת – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִגִּישָׁהּ אֶל פְּנֵי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֶת כׇּל דַּם הַפָּר יִשְׁפֹּךְ אֶל יְסוֹד הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״.
>
> **Translation:** The source for **bringing** meal offerings **near** the altar at the southwest corner is derived from that **which is written: "And he shall bring it to the front of the altar."** The source for pouring **the remaining blood** at the southwest corner of the altar is **as it is written: "And all the remaining blood of the bull he shall pour out at the base of the altar"** (Leviticus 4:7), which the Gemara derives to be referring to the southwest corner (see 53a).

Having established what is done at the southwest corner, the *gemara* asks why this corner specifically rather than the entire southern side. The answer lies in the structure of the *yesod*, the base of the altar. The *yesod* did not extend along the southern or eastern sides of the *mizbeach*. It began at the southwest corner, continued along the entire western side, then along the entire northern side, and concluded at the northeast corner. Since the *yesod* was required for the pouring of the blood remainder, only that corner — the point where the southern approach meets the beginning of the *yesod* — could serve this function.

### Three SW Practices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִלְּמַעְלָה: נִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם, וְהַיַּיִן, וְעוֹלַת הָעוֹף כְּשֶׁהִיא רַבָּה בַּמִּזְרָח.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that there were three matters for which the portion of the southwest corner **above** the red line served as the proper location: **The water and wine libations, and** sacrificing **a bird burnt offering when** they were **numerous in the east,** i.e., the southeast corner.

Turning now to the three practices performed at the *top* of the southwest corner, the *mishnah* identifies *nisuch hamayim veha-yayin* — the pouring of the water and the wine — as first among them. This was necessarily performed at the southwest corner because the *mizbeach* had been extended to the south and to the west so that this corner would sit directly above the *shittin*, the underground caverns, allowing the poured liquids to be absorbed into the ground beneath the altar.

The third practice at the top of the southwest corner is the *olat ha-of* when performed there — which requires explanation, since the *olat ha-of* is ordinarily performed at the southeast corner. The *mishnah* teaches that when there were too many bird *olot* being performed at the southeast corner, the service would be moved to the southwest corner. The *gemara* now turns to explain why the southeast corner is the standard location.

## SE Corner Default

### Proximity to Ashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקְּרוֹבָה מִבֵּית הַדֶּשֶׁן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** the sacrificial rites of a bird burnt offering were ideally supposed to be performed at the southeast corner of the altar? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Because it is the closest** of all the corners **to the place of the ashes,** i.e., the place where the ashes from the altar are placed every morning. The crop and feathers of a bird burnt offering are placed there as well.

Rabbi Yochanan explains that the *olat ha-of* is ordinarily performed at the southeast corner because it is closest to the *beis hadeshen*, the place where the ashes are deposited. Two *pesukim* together establish the location of the *beis hadeshen*. The first, regarding the *terumas hadeshen*, states *vesamo etzel hamizbeach* — he shall place it beside the altar. The second, regarding the *olat ha-of*, states that the crop and innards shall be cast *etzel hamizbeach kedma* — beside the altar, to the east — *makom hadashen*, the place of the ashes. Combining these, the *beis hadeshen* is established as being to the east of the ramp. Since the *kohein* performing the *olat ha-of* at the southeast corner would be casting the crop and feathers toward this spot, proximity is maximized there. Rabbi Yochanan then adds: *bo re'e kama gadol kochan shel kohanim* — come and see how great was the physical strength of the *kohanim* — for there is nothing lighter among bird parts than the crop and surrounding feathers, yet there were times a *kohein* would have to throw them more than thirty *amos*.

### Throwing Distance Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה גָּדוֹל כֹּחָן שֶׁל כֹּהֲנִים – שֶׁאֵין לָךְ קַל בָּעוֹפוֹת יוֹתֵר מִמּוּרְאָה וְנוֹצָה, פְּעָמִים שֶׁהַכֹּהֵן זוֹרְקָן יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a comment pertaining to the mishna's statement that when the southeast corner of the altar is too crowded, the sacrificial rites of a bird burnt offering can be performed at the southwest corner: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Come and see how great** was **the strength of the priests, as you have no** parts **of birds lighter than the crop and feathers,** and there were **times when the priest would toss them more than thirty cubits** from the southwest corner of the altar to the place of the ashes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: נָטַל מַחְתָּה שֶׁל כֶּסֶף, וְעָלָה לְרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, וּפִינָּה אֶת הַגֶּחָלִים אֵילָךְ וְאֵילָךְ, וְחָתָה מִן הַמְאוּכָּלוֹת הַפְּנִימִיּוֹת, וְיוֹרֵד. הִגִּיעַ לָרִצְפָּה – מַחֲזִיר פָּנָיו כְּלַפֵּי צָפוֹן, וְהוֹלֵךְ לְמִזְרָחוֹ שֶׁל כֶּבֶשׁ כְּעֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת. צָבַר אֶת הַגֶּחָלִים עַל גַּבֵּי הָרִצְפָּה רָחוֹק מִן הַכֶּבֶשׁ שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים – מָקוֹם שֶׁנּוֹתְנִין מוּרְאָה וְנוֹצָה, וְדִישּׁוּן מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי וְהַמְּנוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Tamid* 28b) describing the procedure of separating the ashes: The priest **would take a coal pan of silver and ascend to the top of the altar and clear the** upper layer of **coals to** this **side and to** that **side, and,** using the coal pan, **scoop up** coals **from** among **the inner, consumed** coals **and descend** the ramp. When he **reached the floor** of the Temple courtyard, he would **turn his face** to the left, **toward the north, and walk about ten cubits along the eastern** side of the ramp. There, he would **heap the coals upon the floor** at **a distance** of **three handbreadths from the ramp.** This was **the location where one places** the **crop and feathers, and the ashes removed from the inner altar and the Candelabrum.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי טְפֵי מִתְּלָתִין וְחַד הָוְיָין! מְקוֹם גַּבְרֵי לָא חָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: The distance from the southwest corner to the location described in that mishna **is more than thirty-one cubits.** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yochanan **did not consider the space** taken up by **people,** i.e., the priest performing the service, in his calculation.

The *gemara* grounds this statement in a *mishnah* from *Tamid* describing the *terumas hadeshen* procedure. The *kohein* would take a silver shovel, ascend to the top of the *mizbeach*, scoop up the deeply burned ash, descend the ramp, and upon reaching the ground turn to face north — back toward the face of the altar. He would then walk to the east side of the ramp, proceeding *ke'eser amos* — approximately ten *amos* — inward toward the *mizbeach*, and pile the coals on the floor there, three *tefachim* away from the ramp. The same location served for depositing the crop and feathers of the *olat ha-of*, the ash of the inner *mizbeach* used for the *ketores*, and the ash of the *menorah*.

This establishes that the *beis hadeshen* lay on the east side of the ramp, twenty-two *amos* removed from the base of the *mizbeach*. When the *kohein* performed the *olat ha-of* at the southeast corner, the geometry of the throw can be modeled as a triangle: the ash pit is twenty-two *amos* from the wall of the *mizbeach*, and the southeast corner itself is approximately six *amos* up from the base (accounting for one *amah* for the *yesod* and one *amah* for the *sovev*). The diagonal distance from the southeast corner to the ash pit is therefore slightly more than twenty-two *amos* — well under thirty.

The more demanding case arises when the *kohein* is at the southwest corner. Now the triangle has two legs: twenty-two *amos* from the ash pit to the wall of the *mizbeach*, and twenty-two *amos* from that point along the base of the altar to the southwest corner (sixteen *amos* across the width of the ramp and another six *amos* to reach the corner). The diagonal is then 22 times the square root of 2. The *gemara* uses the approximation that the square root of 2 is 1.4 — a solid estimate — yielding 22 × 1.4 = 30.8, essentially thirty-one *amos*.

The *gemara* itself notes that the figure comes out to somewhat more than thirty-one *amos*. It then resolves the apparent discrepancy with the stated figure of thirty by noting: *mekom gavra lo kashiv* — the *kohein* does not count the space he himself occupies, that being approximately one *amah*, which brings the figure close enough to thirty *amos*. The force of Rabbi Yochanan's observation is thus clear: to throw something as light and insubstantial as a crop and feathers over thirty *amos* required extraordinary physical strength.

## Traffic Patterns

### Counterclockwise/SW Acce…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הָעוֹלִים לְמִזְבֵּחַ, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: נְסָכִים – שֶׁמָּא יִתְעַשְּׁנוּ, וְעוֹלַת הָעוֹף – שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת בֶּעָשָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **everyone who ascends** the ramp **to the altar** ascends via the right side of the ramp, circles the altar until reaching the southwest corner, and descends via the left side of the ramp, with the exception of one ascending in order to perform the wine or water libations, or to sacrifice a bird burnt offering on the southwest corner of the altar. In those cases the priest would ascend directly to the southwest corner. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for these exceptions to the standard practice? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to the **libations,** the priest would not walk all the way around the altar **lest** the wine or water **absorb** the **smoke** of the altar fire. **And** with regard to **a bird burnt offering** he would not walk all the way around **lest** the bird **die from** inhaling **the smoke.**

The *gemara* establishes that all who ascend the *mizbeach* normally proceed counterclockwise — all turns are to the right — and this applies to every standard service. However, for the three things performed at the southwest corner at the top of the *mizbeach*, rather than making the full counterclockwise rotation, the *kohein* would go directly to the southwest, turning left instead of right. The *gemara* asks: *mai taama* — what is the reason one is permitted to deviate from the normal directional pattern?

Rabbi Yochanan explains that for the *nesachim*, the wine and water, going the long way around risks their becoming smoky from all the burning on the *mizbeach*, rendering them *pasul*. For the *olat ha-of* similarly: *shema tamus ba-ashan* — the bird might die from the smoke as it is carried all the way around.

### Kohein Gadol Rotation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: בָּא לוֹ לְהַקִּיף אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ, מֵאַיִן הוּא מַתְחִיל? מִקֶּרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית, מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית; וְנוֹתְנִין לוֹ יַיִן לְנַסֵּךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the following mishna (*Tamid* 33b): If the High Priest was performing the sacrificial rites of the daily offering, after he would place the limbs on the fire on top of the altar and **he would come to circle the altar, from where does he start** walking? He starts **from** the **southeast corner,** continues to the **northeast** corner, then to the **northwest** corner, and finally arrives at the **southwest** corner. **And he is given wine to pour** on the altar. This indicates that the High Priest circles the altar while holding the wine for the libation, and there is no concern that it will absorb too much smoke.

A challenge is raised from a *baraisa* discussing the *Kohein Gadol*, who was entitled to perform the *tamid* offering himself at any time, taking all its component services upon himself. After placing the dismembered pieces on the fire, he would rotate around the *mizbeach* before performing the pouring of the wine. The *baraisa* details his full counterclockwise rotation: from the southeast corner, to the northeast, to the northwest, and finally to the southwest — where *notnim lo yayin lenasech*, they would give him the wine to pour. This appears to contradict the principle that one carrying the *nesachim* may take the short route to avoid smoke.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַקָּפָה בָּרֶגֶל. אָמַר רָבָא: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״נוֹתְנִין לוֹ יַיִן לְנַסֵּךְ״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״אוֹמֵר לוֹ נַסֵּךְ״; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The **circling** around the altar by the High Priest is done **on foot,** i.e., empty-handed, as he was given the wine to pour only once he arrived at the southwest corner. **Rava said:** The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches** that **he is given wine to pour, and** the mishna **does not teach** that **one tells him** to **pour** the wine, which would have indicated that the High Priest was already holding the wine as he circled the altar. The Gemara comments: **Conclude from** the language of the mishna that the High Priest was given the wine only once he arrived at the southwest corner of the altar.

The resolution offered by Rabbi Yochanan is elegant: *hakafa baregel* — the *Kohein Gadol* merely walked; he was not carrying the wine. Having ascended to perform the complete *tamid* service, including the burning of the meat, the wine was not in his hands during the circuit. He would complete the full rotation and receive the wine only upon reaching the southwest corner. Since he was not transporting the wine, there was no concern about it becoming smoky en route.

*Rava* adds a further textual confirmation — *deika nami de-katni* — that this reading is also supported by the precise language of the *baraisa*: it says *notnim lo yayin lenasech*, "they gave him wine to pour," not *omrim lo nasech*, "they told him to pour." The clear implication is that the wine was not yet in his possession until that moment. The *gemara* affirms: *shema mina* — this is indeed a valid inference.

### Ascent/Descent Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הָעוֹלִים לְמִזְבֵּחַ – עוֹלִים דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין וְיוֹרְדִים דֶּרֶךְ שְׂמֹאל, עוֹלִים דֶּרֶךְ מִזְרָח וְיוֹרְדִים דֶּרֶךְ מַעֲרָב; חוּץ מִן הָעוֹלֶה לִשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ – שֶׁעוֹלִים דֶּרֶךְ מַעֲרָב וְיוֹרְדִין דֶּרֶךְ מַעֲרָב, עוֹלִים דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין וְיוֹרְדִין דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **All** those **who ascend** the ramp to **the altar ascend via** the **right** side of the ramp **and descend via** the **left** side; they **ascend via** the **east** side of the ramp **and descend via** the **west** side, **except for one who ascends for** one of **these three matters:** The wine libation, the water libation, and the sacrificial rite of a bird burnt offering. In these cases, one **ascends via** the **west** side of the ramp **and descends via** the **west** side; one **ascends via** the **right** side **and descends via** the **right** side.

A *baraisa* is cited elaborating the traffic rules: *kol olim la-mizbeach olim derech yamin ve-yordim derech smol* — all who ascend the *mizbeach* go up on the right side and descend on the left. Equivalently, they go up on the east side of the ramp and descend on the west. The exception applies to those performing the three southwest-corner services: for these, one goes up on the west and descends on the west as well.

This detail is somewhat surprising. One might have assumed that even for these services, traffic would remain as normal as possible: go up on the east side, reach the top, then turn left. At the top, little traffic conflict would arise, since under normal counterclockwise flow no one traverses the space between the east and west sides of the ramp. But the *baraisa* states explicitly that for these three services one ascends on the left — the west — which would actually place one against the normal flow of descending traffic. The *baraisa* stands as stated.

### Directional Terminology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין״?! דֶּרֶךְ שְׂמֹאל הוּא! אָמַר רָבִינָא: תְּנִי ״שְׂמֹאל״. רָבָא אָמַר: מַאי ״יָמִין״ – יָמִין דְּמִזְבֵּחַ, וּמַאי ״שְׂמֹאל״ – שְׂמֹאל דְּגַבְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does one who ascends via the west side of the ramp do so **via** the **right** side? Ascending via the west side of the ramp **is** the same as ascending **via** the **left. Ravina says:** Emend the *baraita* so that it **teaches** that he ascends via the **left** side. **Rava says: What** is the meaning of the **right** side mentioned in the latter clause of the *baraita*? It is the **right** side from the standpoint **of** the **altar,** facing south, in which case the altar's west side is synonymous with its right side. **And what** is the meaning of the **left** side mentioned in the first clause of the *baraita*? It is the **left** side from the standpoint **of a person** facing the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי אוֹ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּמִזְבֵּחַ, אוֹ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּגַבְרָא! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But let** the *baraita* be consistent and **teach** both **this** case **and that** case, i.e., the first clause and latter clause, from the standpoint **of the altar, or** let it teach both **this** case **and that** case from the standpoint **of a person** facing the altar. The Gemara concedes: This is **difficult.**

The concluding line of the *baraisa* refers to descending on the right — *derech yamin* — even though it is the west side, which has previously been called the left. The *gemara* raises the difficulty: *derech yamin? Derech smol hu!* — it is the left, not the right.

Two responses are offered. Ravina simply emends the text: *tani smol* — it should read "left." Rava, however, defends the text as written: *mai yamin yamin de-mizbeach* — "right" here refers to the right side of the *mizbeach* itself, as though the altar were a person whose face is the side from which one ascends. From the *mizbeach*'s perspective, the west is its right — even though from the perspective of a person facing the altar it is the left. This explains the inconsistency: the first part of the *baraisa* described the west as "left" from the observer's perspective (*smol de-gavra*), while the conclusion described it as "right" from the altar's perspective (*yamin de-mizbeach*). The *gemara* objects that the *baraisa* should have maintained a consistent perspective throughout — *kashya* — and so Ravina's emendation remains the more natural resolution.

## Bird Sin Offering

### Melikah Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חַטַּאת הָעוֹף כֵּיצַד הָיָה נַעֲשֵׂית? (עָלָה לַכֶּבֶשׁ וּפָנָה לַסּוֹבֵב בָּא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית), הָיָה מוֹלֵק אֶת רֹאשָׁהּ מִמּוּל עׇרְפָּה וְאֵינוֹ מַבְדִּיל, וּמַזֶּה מִדָּמָהּ עַל קִיר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם הָיָה מִתְמַצֶּה עַל הַיְסוֹד; וְאֵין לְמִזְבֵּחַ אֶלָּא דָּמָהּ, וְכוּלָּהּ לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The sacrifice of **the bird sin offering, how was it performed?** The priest **would pinch** off **the** bird's **head** by cutting **opposite its nape** with his thumbnail **and** would **not separate** the bird's head from its body. **And he sprinkles from its blood on the wall of the altar** below the red line. **The remaining blood would be squeezed out** from the body of the bird **on the base** of the altar. **The altar has only its blood and the entire** bird goes **to the priests** for consumption.

The *mishnah* now turns to a detailed account of the *chatas ha-of*. *Chatas ha-of keitzad hayeta na'asit?* — How was the bird *chatas* performed? The *kohein* would perform *melikah* — slaughtering by means of his thumbnail from the nape of the neck — but would *not* fully sever. "Not sever" carries a double meaning: first, the head would not be physically detached from the body, and second, only one of the two *simanim* — the trachea or the esophagus — would be cut, not both.

Following the *melikah*, the blood would squirt directly onto the wall of the *mizbeach* at the southwest corner, as established above. The remainder of the blood — *sheyarei hadam* — would *mitmatzeh el hayesod*, either dribbling naturally down to the *yesod* or being actively squeezed out against the wall until the remainder ran down to the base. Importantly, nothing of the bird would be burned on the altar; the blood alone was the *mizbeach*'s portion. The entire body of the bird was eaten by the *kohanim*.

### Blood Below Midline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִזָּה מִדַּם הַחַטָּאת״ – מִגּוּף הַחַטָּאת. הָא כֵּיצַד? אוֹחֵז בָּרֹאשׁ וּבַגּוּף, וּמַזֶּה עַל קִיר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ; וְלֹא עַל קִיר הַכֶּבֶשׁ, וְלֹא עַל קִיר הֵיכָל, וְלֹא עַל קִיר אוּלָם. וְאֵיזֶה? זֶה קִיר הַתַּחְתּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the bird sin offering: **"And he shall sprinkle of the blood of the sin offering** upon the side of the altar" (Leviticus 5:9). This teaches that the blood is sprinkled directly **from the body of the sin offering,** not with the priest's finger or from a vessel. **How so?** The priest **holds the head and the body and sprinkles** its blood **on the wall of the altar. And** he does **not** sprinkle it **on the wall of the ramp, and not on the wall of** the **Sanctuary, and not on the wall of** the **Entrance Hall. And which** is this wall of the altar that the blood is sprinkled on? **This is the lower wall,** below the red line.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא קִיר הָעֶלְיוֹן? וְדִין הוּא: וּמָה בְּהֵמָה, שֶׁעוֹלָתָהּ לְמַטָּה – חַטָּאתָהּ לְמַעְלָה; עוֹף, שֶׁעוֹלָתוֹ לְמַעְלָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁחַטָּאתוֹ לְמַעְלָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* suggests: **Or** perhaps the verse is referring **only** to **the upper wall,** above the red line. **And** this should be **a logical inference: Just as** with regard to **an animal** offering, **where** the blood of **a burnt offering** is applied **below** the red line (see 10b), nevertheless, the blood of **the sin offering** is applied **above** the red line (see Leviticus 4:30), with regard to **a bird** offering, **where** the blood of **the burnt offering** is sprinkled **above** the red line (see 65a), **is it not logical that** the blood of **its sin offering** is sprinkled **above** the red line?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַנִּשְׁאָר בַּדָּם יִמָּצֵה אֶל יְסוֹד הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ – קִיר שֶׁהַשְּׁיָרִין שֶׁלּוֹ מִתְמַצִּים לַיְסוֹד; וְאֵיזֶה? זֶה קִיר הַתַּחְתּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states: "And the rest of the blood shall be squeezed out [*yimmatze*] at the base of the altar"** (Leviticus 5:9), which teaches that the priest sprinkles the blood on the **wall where its remaining** blood **drains to the base** of the altar. **And which** wall is this? **This is the lower wall.** The surrounding ledge of the altar is located one cubit above the red line, and any blood sprinkled above this ledge will not drip down to the base of the altar. The verse teaches that the blood is sprinkled on the wall of the altar from which it would drip down to the altar's base unimpeded.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַעְבֵּיד מֵעִילַּאי, וַהֲדַר נַעְבֵּיד מִתַּתַּאי! אָמַר רָבָא: מִי כְּתִיב ״יְמַצֶּה״?! ״יִמָּצֵה״ כְּתִיב, דְּמִמֵּילָא מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let** the priest **perform** the sprinkling of the blood **above,** as the *baraita* logically deduced, **and then perform** the squeezing **below** in fulfillment of the aforementioned verse. **Rava said: Is it written: He shall drain [*yematze*]? It is written: It shall be drained [*yimmatze*], which indicates** that the blood trickles down to the base of the altar **by itself.** Accordingly, the blood must be sprinkled on a part of the altar wall from which it can trickle down to the base of the altar.

A careful question arises: is the blood of the bird *chatas* applied above or below the *chut hasikra*, the red line running around the middle of the *mizbeach*? The *baraisa* poses this as an internal question, noting that a logical argument could be made for above. By a mammal, the *olah* is applied below the line and the *chatas* above. Since the bird *olah* (to be discussed in the next *mishnah*) is applied at the top of the *mizbeach*, one might argue *kal va-chomer*: if even the bird *olah* is applied at the top, surely the bird *chatas* — which by mammals goes above — should also go above.

The *baraisa* rejects this inference from a *pasuk*: *vehanish'ar badam yimatzeh el yesod hamizbeach* — and the remainder of the blood shall be squeezed out to the base of the altar. The derivation is: *kir sheshirav mitmatzin liyesod* — the blood must be applied to a section of the wall from which the remainder will flow naturally to the *yesod*. Above the midline, there is an *amah*-wide protrusion — the *sovev*, the walk-around platform — that would intercept any blood running down the wall before it could reach the *yesod*. Only blood applied below the midline can flow directly and unimpeded to the base. This is therefore the proof that the blood of the bird *chatas* is applied to the lower portion of the wall.

One might then ask: why not apply the blood above the line for the primary *avoda* and then squeeze the remainder below? *Amar Rava*: the *pasuk* does not say *yimatzeh* in the *pa'al* form (active squeezing), but rather as a form that can be read as naturally flowing — *de-memaila mashma* — it dribbles down of its own accord. If the application itself will cause the blood to naturally flow down to the base, this means the blood must have been applied in a position from which it flows directly to the *yesod* — i.e., below the midline. There is no room, on this reading, for a separate act above the line followed by a separate squeezing below.

### Melikah Technique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב: כֵּיצַד מוֹלְקִין חַטַּאת הָעוֹף? אוֹחֵז גַּפָּיו בִּשְׁתֵּי אֶצְבָּעוֹת וּשְׁתֵּי רַגְלַיִם בִּשְׁתֵּי אֶצְבָּעוֹת, וּמוֹתֵחַ צַוָּארָהּ עַל רוֹחַב גּוּדָלוֹ, וּמוֹלְקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Zutra bar Toviyya says** that **Rav says: How** does the priest **pinch** the nape of **a bird sin offering?** He holds the bird by its back in the palm of his hand and **holds its wings with two fingers,** i.e., the middle and index fingers, **and** its **two legs with** his next **two fingers,** i.e., the ring finger and pinky, leaving the head resting between his index finger and thumb, **and he stretches its neck over the width of his thumb and pinches** its nape with his thumbnail.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תְּנָא: צִיפְּרָא מִלְּבַר; אוֹחֵז גַּפָּיו בִּשְׁתֵּי אֶצְבָּעוֹת וּשְׁנֵי רַגְלַיִם בִּשְׁתֵּי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, וּמוֹתֵחַ צַוָּארוֹ עַל רוֹחַב שְׁתֵּי אֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו, וּמוֹלֵק. וְזוֹ עֲבוֹדָה קָשָׁה שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a varying opinion: **It was taught in a *baraita*: The bird** is not in the priest's palm, but rather **on the outside** of his hand. The priest **holds its wings with two fingers and** its **two legs with two fingers.** Using his thumb, the priest bends its head into his palm **and stretches its neck over the width of his two fingers, and pinches** its nape. **And this** is the most **difficult** sacrificial **rite in the Temple** to perform.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ הִיא – וְתוּ לָא?! וְהָאִיכָּא קְמִיצָה וַחֲפִינָה! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: זוֹ עֲבוֹדָה קָשָׁה מֵעֲבוֹדוֹת קָשׁוֹת שֶׁבַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This is** the most difficult rite to perform, **and no other? But isn't there** also the **removal of the handful** of the meal offering **and the handful** of the incense on Yom Kippur? The Gemara answers: **Rather, say** that the pinching procedure was **a difficult** sacrificial **rite,** one **of the** most **difficult** sacrificial **rites in the Temple** to perform.

*Amar Rav Zutra bar Tuvya amar Rav* — how exactly does one perform *melikah*? The *kohein* holds the bird in his right hand. He grips the wings with two fingers (Rashi identifies these as the index and middle fingers), the feet with two other fingers (the pinky and ring finger), and stretches the neck out along the breadth of his thumb — across the space between the thumb and index finger — with the right thumb. He then uses the thumbnail itself to slice through the neck.

A *baraisa* offers an alternative hold: *tzipra mi-levara* — the body of the bird is positioned at the back of the hand rather than in the palm, with the wings and feet held by the same fingers and the neck stretched out behind the fingers rather than in the crook of the thumb. In either description, the *melikah* is performed with the thumbnail against the stretched neck.

The *baraisa* concludes: *ve-zo hi avodah kashah she-be-mikdash* — this was the most difficult service in the *beis hamikdash*. The challenge is self-evident: the angle is inherently awkward, slaughtering with a fingernail while controlling the entire bird in one hand. The *gemara* challenges this superlative: is this truly the most difficult? Other *avodos* are also known to be exceptionally demanding — the *kemitzah*, taking a precise handful of *mincha* smoothed between thumb and pinky, and the *chafinah*, the double-fisted scooping of burning coals for the *Yom Kippur ketores* service. The *gemara* accordingly amends the claim: *zu avodah kashah me-avodos kashos she-ba-mikdash* — it is not uniquely the hardest, but *among* the most difficult services in the *beis hamikdash*.

## Bird Burnt Offering

### Complete Severance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עוֹלַת הָעוֹף כֵּיצַד נַעֲשֵׂית? עָלָה לַכֶּבֶשׁ וּפָנָה לַסּוֹבֵב, בָּא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית. הָיָה מוֹלֵק אֶת רֹאשָׁהּ מִמּוּל עׇרְפָּה, וּמַבְדִּיל, וּמִיצָּה אֶת דָּמָהּ עַל קִיר הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. נָטַל אֶת הָרֹאשׁ וְהִקִּיף בֵּית מְלִיקָתוֹ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, סְפָגוֹ בְּמֶלַח, וּזְרָקוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הָאִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The sacrifice of **the bird burnt offering, how was it performed?** The priest **ascended the ramp and turned to the surrounding ledge and came to** the **southeast corner** of the altar. He **would** then **pinch** off **the** bird's **head** by cutting **at its nape** with his thumbnail **and separate** the bird's head from its body, **and** would **squeeze out its blood on the wall of the altar. He took the head and neared the place of its pinching,** i.e., its nape, **to the altar** in order to squeeze the blood from the head. **He would** then **absorb** the remaining blood **with salt and throw** the head **onto the fire** on the altar.

The *mishnah* turns to the *olat ha-of*, which was performed at the top of the *mizbeach*. The *kohein* would ascend the ramp and proceed via the sub-ramp to the *sovev*, the walk-around platform, arriving at the southeast corner — or, in cases of overflow, at the southwest corner. Rashi notes that when performing the service at the southwest corner due to the large number of *olot*, the *kohein* would go all the way to the top of the *mizbeach* and turn left immediately, since there was no sub-ramp providing direct access to that corner from the walk-around.

The *melikah* of the *olat ha-of* contrasts sharply with that of the *chatas ha-of* in two respects. First, *u-mavdil* — the *kohein* does completely sever: he cuts through both the trachea and the esophagus, and fully detaches the head from the body. Second, the blood is applied in a single act of squeezing — *u-mitzah es damah al kir hamizbeach* — directly pressing the body against the wall, with no initial squirting as in the *chatas*.

Once the body's blood has been squeezed out, the *kohein* takes the now-severed head, turns the place of *melikah* against the *mizbeach* — *hikif beis melikaso la-mizbeach* — and squeezes out the remainder of the blood from the head as well. The head is then salted and cast onto the fire. The *mishnah* is precise in specifying these as two distinct acts of *mitzui*: the first from the body, the second from the head.

### Crop/Feathers Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לַגּוּף, וְהֵסִיר אֶת הַמּוּרְאָה וְאֶת הַנּוֹצָה וְאֶת בְּנֵי הַמֵּעַיִם הַיּוֹצְאִין עִמָּם, וְהִשְׁלִיכָן לְבֵית הַדֶּשֶׁן. שִׁיסַּע, וְלֹא הִבְדִּיל. וְאִם הִבְדִּיל – כָּשֵׁר. סוֹפְגוֹ בְּמֶלַח, וְזֹרְקוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הָאִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **He** then **arrived at the body and removed the crop and the feather** attached to it **and the innards that emerge with them and he tossed them to the place of the ashes. He** then **ripped** the bird lengthwise **and did not separate** the two halves of the bird; **but if he separated** them, the offering is **valid.** He would then **absorb** the remaining blood **with salt and throw** the body of the bird **onto the fire** on the altar.

After processing the blood of both body and head, the *kohein* removes the crop along with the surrounding feathers (*mor'ah venotzah*) and the innards that emerge along with them (*bnei me'ayim hayotzim imam*). These are cast to the *beis hadeshen* — thrown those twenty-two-or-more *amos* to the east of the ramp, as the geometric analysis above demonstrated.

### Salting & Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הֵסִיר הַמּוּרְאָה וְלֹא הַנּוֹצָה וְאֶת בְּנֵי מֵעַיִים הַיּוֹצְאִין עִמָּם, וְלֹא סְפָגוֹ בְּמֶלַח – כֹּל שֶׁשִּׁינָּה בָּהּ מֵאַחֵר שֶׁמִּיצָּה אֶת דָּמָהּ, כְּשֵׁירָה. הִבְדִּיל בַּחַטָּאת וְלֹא הִבְדִּיל בָּעוֹלָה – פָּסוּל. מִיצָּה אֶת דַּם הָרֹאשׁ וְלֹא מִיצָּה דַּם הַגּוּף – פְּסוּלָה. דַּם הַגּוּף וְלֹא מִיצָּה דַּם הָרֹאשׁ – כְּשֵׁירָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **he neither removed the crop, nor the feather and the innards that emerge with them, nor absorbed** the blood **with salt,** with regard to **any** detail **that he changed in** the sacrificial rites **after he squeezed out its blood,** the offering is **valid.** If **he separated** the head from the body **in** sacrificing **the sin offering, or** if **he did not separate** the head from the body **in** sacrificing **the burnt offering,** the offering is **disqualified.** In a case where **he squeezed out the blood of the head and did not squeeze out the blood of the body, it is disqualified.** If he squeezed out the **blood of the body and did not squeeze out the blood of the head,** the offering is **valid.**

The salting of the body and its placement on the fire follow. However, the *mishnah* establishes a critical principle: *kol sheshina ba le-acharei mitzas hadam keshera* — any deviation that occurs after the blood-squeezing service has been properly completed does not invalidate the *korban*. Whether the *kohein* failed to remove the crop and innards, omitted the salting, or performed the burning incorrectly, the *korban* remains valid — just as with animal sacrifices, once the blood service is properly completed the *korban* is not undermined by subsequent procedural errors.

Conversely, errors in the blood service itself do invalidate the offering. *Hivdil b'chatas* — if the *kohein* fully severed for the *chatas* (either removing the head or cutting both *simanim*) — *pasul*. *Lo hivdil b'olah* — if he failed to fully sever for the *olah* — *pasul*. Similarly, if he squeezed the blood of the head but not of the body, it is *pasul*, since the primary blood service concerns the body. If he squeezed the blood of the body but not of the head, the *korban* remains *kosher*.

## Invalidations

### Improper Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטַּאת הָעוֹף שֶׁמְּלָקָהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ; מִיצָּה דָּמָהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ; לִשְׁמָהּ וְשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ; אוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ וְלִשְׁמָהּ – פָּסוּל. עוֹלַת הָעוֹף כְּשֵׁירָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא עָלְתָה לִבְעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a bird sin offering that** the priest **pinched not for its sake,** or if **he squeezed out its blood not for its sake,** or if he did so **for its sake and** then **not for its sake, or not for its sake and** then **for its sake,** it is **disqualified,** like all other sin offerings that are disqualified when performed not for their sake (see 2a). **A bird burnt offering** sacrificed not for its sake is **valid;** it is **just that it did not satisfy the obligation of the owner.**

The *mishnah* now addresses *shelo lishma* and *pigul* in the context of bird offerings. For the *chatas ha-of*, if the *melikah* was performed *shelo lishma*, or if the *mitzuy hadam* was performed *shelo lishma*, or if one of the two key *avodos* was *lishma* and the other *shelo lishma* — *pasul*. This mirrors the rule for the *chatas* of a *behemah*. Note that the bird *chatas* has only two *avodos* rather than the four of a mammal offering: the *melikah* (corresponding to *shechita*) and the squeezing of the blood (corresponding to the application of blood to the corners).

For the *olat ha-of*, performance *shelo lishma* renders the offering *kosher* — since *shelo lishma* by an *olah* is valid — provided that the owners do not thereby discharge their obligation.

### Pigul Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד חַטַּאת הָעוֹף וְאֶחָד עוֹלַת הָעוֹף, שֶׁמְּלָקָן וְשֶׁמִּיצָּה אֶת דָּמָן – לֶאֱכוֹל דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל, וּלְהַקְטִיר דָּבָר שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לְהַקְטִיר חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ; פָּסוּל וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת; וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיַּקְרִיב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **both a bird sin offering and a bird burnt offering where** the priest **pinched its nape or squeezed out its blood** with the intent **to partake of an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **partakes** of it, **or to burn an item whose** typical **manner** is such that one **burns** it on the altar, **outside its** designated **area,** the offering is **disqualified.** But **there is no** liability to receive ***karet* for** one who partakes of the offering. If his intent was to eat it or burn it **beyond its** designated **time,** the offering is ***piggul* and** one is **liable** to receive ***karet* for** partaking of the offering, **provided that the permitting factor,** the blood, **was sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva.**

The *mishnah* now addresses *pigul* for both bird offerings equally. If the *kohein* performed the *melikah* or the *mitzuy hadam* with intent to eat that which is normally eaten — the meat of the *chatas ha-of* — or to burn that which is normally burned — the meat of the *olat ha-of* — outside its proper location (*chutz limkomo*), the *korban* is *pasul* but does not carry *kares*. If the intent was to do so at the wrong time (*chutz lizimano*), the *korban* becomes *pigul* and eating it incurs *kares*.

However, this *pigul* status applies only when the *matir* — the permitting act — was performed *ke-mitzvaso*, in the proper manner. The *mishnah* specifies: if the *melikah* was performed without intent (*bi-shtikah*) and the *mitzuy hadam* with a *chutz lizimano* intent, or if the *melikah* was *chutz lizimano* and the *mitzuy* was *bi-shtikah*, or if both were performed with a *chutz lizimano* intent — in all these cases the *matir* is considered to have been performed *ke-mitzvaso* and *pigul* takes effect.

### Pigul Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד קָרַב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ? מָלַק בִּשְׁתִיקָה, וּמִיצָּה הַדָּם חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ; אוֹ שֶׁמָּלַק חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וּמִיצָּה הַדָּם בִּשְׁתִיקָה; אוֹ שֶׁמָּלַק וּמִיצָּה הַדָּם חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – זֶהוּ שֶׁקָּרַב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **How** is **the permitting factor sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva?** If one **pinched** the nape **in silence,** i.e., with no disqualifying intent, **and squeezed out** its **blood** with the intent to partake of the parts typically eaten or to burn the portions that are to be burned on the altar, **beyond its** designated **time; or** in the case of one **who pinched** with the intent to partake of the offering or burn it on the altar **beyond its** designated **time, and squeezed out** its **blood in silence; or** in the case of one **who pinched and squeezed out the blood** with the intent to partake of the offering or burn it on the altar **beyond its** designated **time, that is** the case of a bird offering **where** the **permitting factor is sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד לֹא קָרַב הַמַּתִּיר כְּמִצְוָתוֹ? מָלַק חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, מִיצָּה הַדָּם חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ; אוֹ שֶׁמָּלַק חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וּמִיצָּה הַדָּם חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ; אוֹ שֶׁמָּלַק וּמִיצָּה הַדָּם חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ;
>
> **Translation:** **How** is **the permitting factor not sacrificed in accordance with its mitzva?** If the priest **pinched** the nape of the bird with the intent to partake of it or burn it **outside its** designated **area** and **squeezed out** its **blood** with the intent to partake of it or burn it **beyond its** designated **time, or** he **pinched** with the intent to partake of it or burn it **beyond its** designated **time and squeezed out** its **blood** with the intent to partake of it or burn it **outside its** designated **area, or** if the priest **pinched and squeezed out the blood** with the intent to partake of it or burn it **outside its** designated **area,**

The *mishnah* now delineates the cases in which *pigul* does not take effect, because the *matir* was *not* performed *ke-mitzvaso*. If the *melikah* was performed *chutz limkomo* and the *mitzuy hadam chutz lizimano*, or if the *melikah* was *chutz lizimano* and the *mitzuy chutz limkomo*, or if both were performed *chutz limkomo* — in all these instances the offering is invalid on account of *chutz limkomo*, but it does not attain the status of *pigul*. Where an additional invalidation (*chutz limkomo*) is present alongside the *pigul* intent (*chutz lizimano*), the *matir* is not considered to have been performed *ke-mitzvaso*, and the *pigul* mechanism does not activate.

The *daf* concludes here, with the remainder of this *mishnah* to be continued at the top of daf 65.
