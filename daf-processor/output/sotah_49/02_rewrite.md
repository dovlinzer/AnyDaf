# Sotah 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Emuna & Yeridas Hadoros

### Eliezer: Bread & Faith

The final daf of Masechet Sotah picks up at the very bottom of 48b, and the overarching theme — that since the destruction of the *beit hamikdash* everything has gone to pot — frames the entire discussion. The Gemara opens with a statement attributed to Rabbi Yitzchak: *upasku anshei emuna* — the people of faith have ceased. Who are these people of faith? They are those who truly believe in God with complete trust. A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Eliezer HaGadol says that anyone who has bread in his basket and worries about what he will eat tomorrow is among those of little faith — *mikatnei emuna*. God will provide for tomorrow; anxiety about tomorrow's sustenance reflects an incomplete trust in divine providence.

Rabbi Eliezer further interprets the verse *ki mi baz leyom katnut* — who has despised a day of smallness? — as referring to the righteous whose tables in the *olam haba* will not be fully laden. Why? *Katnut shehayah bahem* — because of the smallness that was in them, meaning that even they did not maintain complete faith in God. This is a striking formulation: even the righteous, if they harbored some anxiety about their livelihood, are measured accordingly, a case of *mida kneged mida*, in which their table in the future world is diminished because they did not fully trust that God would fill it in this world.

### Rava: Young Wicked

Rava offers a sharp reinterpretation of the very same verse. He says: *elu katnei bnei reshei Yisrael* — these are the young children of wicked Jews. These are children who died young, presumably on account of punishment visited upon their wicked fathers. The phrase *ki mi baz leyom katnut* is read by Rava as a reference to those who diminish the judgment deserved by the fathers in the world to come — *shemevazin dina deavihem leatid lavo*. These children argue before God: *Ribono shel olam*, if You were going to exact full punishment from our fathers in the future world, why did You blunt their teeth already — why did You cause us to die young? Our early death should count as partial atonement for our fathers' sins.

This is quite an interesting twist on the theme, and it does not obviously connect to the surrounding discussion of *emuna*. Normally one might be satisfied to see the wicked punished, yet here there is a note of compassion: even the wicked suffer when they lose young children, and at least that suffering ought to count for something as partial atonement *leatid lavo*.

### David's Prayer

Rabbi Ilai bar Yevarchia offers a statement: were it not for the prayer of David, all of Israel would have been reduced to selling *rivav* — some lowly commodity that only the poor are reduced to peddling. The verse cited is *shisa Hashem mora lahem* — God, place upon them a teacher, or give them mastery. Rashi explains this by tracing the context: the preceding verse reads *ki lo lanetzach yishachach evyon* — God will not eternally forget the poor — *tikvat aniyim tovad la'ad* — the hope of the lowly shall not be lost forever. Rashi reads *mora* as *marut* — mastery or status — and understands the verse as David's prayer on behalf of the poor, asking God to give them some standing in this world, so that the nations who look upon them with a degree of honor should know that they are merely human and not divine.

The precise connection of this statement to the theme of *emuna* is not entirely transparent. Perhaps the link is that David's prayer relates to anxiety about financial well-being, or to the general suffering of poverty discussed in the surrounding passages. Rabbi Ilai bar Yevarchia is mentioned again immediately for additional teachings, and the repeated citation of the same *amora* may itself be the editorial thread connecting these passages.

### Chabakuk Prayer

Rabbi Ilai bar Yevarchia further states: what was the function of the prayer of Chabakuk? *Habat talmidei chachamim shemiskasim betalis achas* — that Torah scholars so impoverished that they would share a single garment between them, and yet continue to learn Torah together, should be sustained. The image captures both their devotion and their destitution.

The verse cited is from Chabakuk: *Hashem shamati shimacha yarasi* — I have heard Your report and I was awed — *bekerev shanim chayeihu* — in the midst of years give it life. The Gemara reads *bekerev shanim* not as "in the midst of years" but as *bekiruv shnayim* — in the proximity of two people — *chayeihu* — give them life, give them *parnasa*. Who are those who hear God's message and witness God's deeds? Those who study Torah. So the prayer of Chabakuk on behalf of those two Torah scholars sharing a single garment is that God sustain them with a livelihood. This, too, connects to the broader theme of faith and sustenance: even in the most desperate circumstances, one who learns Torah out of poverty can trust that his prayer — or the prayer of the great — will be heard on his behalf.

### No Torah Discourse

Rabbi Ilai bar Yevarchia continues: two Torah scholars who travel together on the road and do not speak words of Torah between them are deserving of being consumed by fire — *re'uyim lehisharef ba'esh*. The proof is from the episode of Elisha and Eliyahu: *vayehi heima holchim haloch vedaber* — they were walking and speaking — *vehinei rechev esh* — and behold a chariot of fire. The Gemara infers: the reason the fire did not harm them is because they were speaking Torah. *Ta'ama de'ika dibur ha leika dibur re'uyim lehisharef* — had there been no Torah discourse, they would have been deserving of being burned.

### Two Sages Tallit

The image of two Torah scholars sharing a single *talis* is recalled precisely here in contrast to the next teaching: whereas the shared *talis* reflects an intimacy and mutual dedication in the pursuit of Torah, the following passage depicts the opposite dynamic.

### Scholars Without Respect

Rabbi Ilai bar Yevarchia adds: two Torah scholars who live in the same city and are not gracious and pleasant to one another in matters of *halacha* — *ein nochim zeh lazeh bahalacha* — such that there is no reciprocal respect, no willingness to say "you might also ask the other rabbi," no collegial discourse at all — will come to a bad end: *echad meis ve'echad goleh*, one will die and the other will go into exile.

The verse cited is *lanus shama rotze'ach asher yirtzach es re'eihu bevli da'as* — the city of refuge is for one who kills his friend *bevli da'as*, without knowledge. And *da'as* means Torah, as the verse says *nidmu ami mibli hada'as* — my people are struck down for lack of knowledge. So if there is no Torah shared between two scholars in the same city, it is as if one has killed the other through that absence, and the punishment of exile follows.

### Study in Hardship

Rabbi Yehudah bar breih de-Rabbi Chiya teaches: any Torah scholar who learns Torah out of *dachak* — out of pressing straits, in privation — *tefilato nishma'at*, his prayer will be heard. This builds on the foregoing: David and Chabakuk had to pray on behalf of the poor scholars; here the scholar himself, by virtue of learning in hardship, earns that his own prayers are answered directly.

The proof is from the verse *ki am be-Tziyon yeshev bi-Yerushalayim bacho lo tivke chanon yachnecha le-kol za'akecha ke-shom'ato anacha* — to the voice of your crying out, when God hears, He will answer. The next verse — *ve-natan lachem Hashem lechem tzar u-mayim lachatz* — God will give you bread of privation and water of oppression — is read as the situational context: these are people who have very little bread and very little water, yet learn Torah nonetheless. It is precisely because of that privation that God answers their cry immediately.

### Divine Presence

Rabbi Abbahu adds a further dimension. The question hovering over this entire passage is that *emuna* is all well and good, but the reality is that many Torah scholars do suffer, and the promises of answered prayer do not always seem to manifest materially. Rabbi Abbahu addresses this: *masbi'in oto mi-ziv Shechina* — such a person is sustained from the radiance of the Divine Presence itself. The verse cited is *ve-hayu einecha ro'ot et morecha* — your eyes will see your teacher. The experience of God's presence is itself a form of sustenance, even when it does not translate into food on the table. The comparison that naturally arises — Moshe Rabbeinu, who was literally in God's presence and did not eat — suggests that there are indeed souls capable of being sustained on something far beyond physical nourishment.

### Curtain Not Sealed

Rabbi Yochanan adds: *af ein ha-pargod nin'al be-fanav* — the heavenly curtain is not sealed before such a person, meaning his prayers have direct access. The verse cited is *ve-lo yikanef od morecha* — your teacher will no longer be hidden beneath a garment, covered from you. God will no longer be concealed. The spiritual access, even when material provision remains elusive, represents a real and sustaining form of divine response.

### Daily Curse

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua: from the day the *beit hamikdash* was destroyed, there is no day that does not carry within it some element of curse.

### Increasing Curse

Rava intensifies this: *be-chol yom va-yom meruba klalato mi-shel chaveiro* — not only is every day cursed since the destruction, but every single day the curse is greater than the day before. Every passing day is worse than the previous one. The verse cited is *ba-boker tomar mi yiten erev u-va-erev tomar mi yiten boker* — in the morning you will say, who will give evening, and in the evening you will say, who will give morning.

### Morning/Evening

The Gemara asks: when it is evening and you long for morning, which morning are you longing for? Perhaps the morning yet to come? But the Gemara dismisses this: maybe tomorrow morning will be even worse — *mi yada mai havi*. Rather, *ella dechalif* — you are longing for the morning that has already passed. When it is night, you wish it were yesterday morning, because yesterday morning things were not yet as bad as they are tonight. Every passing moment, a person wishes it were the moment before. Things are continuously getting worse.

### Kedusha & Kaddish

*Ve'ella alma amai mikayeim* — if the world is getting progressively worse, what sustains it? The Gemara answers: *a'Kedusha DeSidra ve'Yehei Shmei Rabba de'Agadata* — it is the *kedusha* of the *seder* recited during *U'va LeTziyon*, and the *Yehei Shmei Rabba* recited after a public teaching of *agadata*.

This is a striking Gemara, one of the very few places where these prayers are explicitly identified as sustaining the world. The *Kedusha DeSidra* is the *Kedushas Hashem* recited in *U'va LeTziyon* after *Shacharis*, where *kadosh kadosh* is recited along with its Aramaic translation. The *Yehei Shmei Rabba* refers to *Kaddish* recited specifically after public *agadata* teachings.

### Rashi: Universal Torah

Rashi's commentary here is essential. He explains: *seder kedusha shelo tiknuha ela sheyehu kol Yisrael oskin baTorah bechol yom davar mu'at* — this order of *kedusha* was established precisely so that all Jews would engage in Torah study each day, even if only a little. The key feature of *Kedusha DeSidra* is that the *pesukim* are recited together with their Aramaic translation, *targumo*. Aramaic was the vernacular, which meant that everyone — scholars and *amei ha'aretz* alike — actually understood what was being said. They were genuinely learning Torah, not merely reciting words. It created no hierarchy; it was accessible to all. And it combined two elements simultaneously: *Kedushas Hashem* and *Talmud Torah*. Rashi calls this *chaviva* — precious, beloved.

Similarly, the *Yehei Shmei Rabba* recited after *agadata* teachings has the same character: *agadata* was the form of Torah accessible to everyone, the public sermon of the *darshan* that would draw the whole community together on *Shabbos*. The people would gather, everyone would hear the Torah teaching, and everyone would respond with *Yehei Shmei Rabba*. Again: *Kedushas Hashem* combined with *Talmud Torah*, universal and egalitarian. It is this minimum — not some extraordinary spiritual achievement, but a daily communal practice accessible to all — that keeps the world going even as deeds continue to deteriorate.

The Gemara then cites the verse: *eretz afaisa kmo ofel tzalmaves velo sedarim* — a land shrouded in darkness and the shadow of death, without order. *Hayesh sedarim tofei'ah me'ofel* — if these orders of *kedusha* and *kaddish* are in place, then light breaks through the darkness.

### Taharah Loss

The Gemara continues: *v'lo yardah l'vrachah v'nitma'et ta'am peirot* — the rains have not descended for blessing since the destruction, and the taste of fruit has diminished.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches: *taharah batlah ta'am v'rei'ach* — the loss of *taharah* caused the loss of taste and smell in fruit. Rashi explains beautifully: because people were *tehorim* and conducted themselves in *taharah*, God purified their fruit of bad smell and bad taste — the metaphysical purity of the people generated a corresponding purity and sweetness in the produce of the land. When *taharah* was lost, that spiritual refinement of the fruit was lost as well.

The Gemara adds: *ma'aser bitlu shuman dagan* — the failure to give *ma'aser* caused the fatness of the grain to cease. The *ma'aser* is described in the *Torah* as *kol chelev dagan* — the fatness of the grain — because one gives from the best to God. If one withholds this, the fatness disappears. And indeed, *mida kneged mida*: the very fatness one tried to keep for oneself by not giving it is the fatness that is lost.

### Huna's Date

Rav Huna once found a particularly sweet and rich date — *tamarta d'chinunita*, a species known for its exceptional sweetness and juiciness. He took it, wrapped it in his handkerchief, and was going to give it to his son. His son Rabbah arrived and said: *murchina reicha d'chinunita* — I smell the fragrance of this amazing date. Rav Huna responded: *bni, taharah yesh becha* — my son, you must possess a special purity, because as we have seen, *taharah* enables one to perceive taste and smell. He gave it to him. Then Rabbah's own son Abba arrived, and Rabbah took the date and gave it to his son.

Rav Huna said to Rabbah: *bni, simachtani libi* — you made my heart rejoice that you could perceive the fragrance of the date, which speaks to your purity of soul. *Aval hikhita et shinai* — but you blunted my teeth, meaning you hurt me: rather than keeping what I gave you, you immediately passed it on to your own son. That is what people say: *rachamei d'abba a-vnei* — a father's love flows toward his children. *Aval rachamei d'vnei a-vnei d'havu lei* — but the love of children flows not back to the parent but downward to their own children. Parents give more and feel more for their children than children feel for parents — not that this is always so, and certainly there are exceptions, but it reflects a natural asymmetry in the direction of parental love.

### Grandson Dishonor

This theme finds a darker echo immediately after. Rav Aha bar Yaakov raised his daughter's son, Rav Yaakov bar bartei, as his own. When the boy grew up, Rav Aha said to him: *ashkin maya* — could you please pour me some water? The grandson replied: *lav avuna at* — you are not my father, and I owe you no honor. The Gemara comments: *vehainu d'amri inshi* — that is what people say: *rabi rabi verabi lei* — raise children, raise them up — *ubesof amar bar bartekh ana* — and in the end he says, I am your daughter's son, not your son, and therefore I owe you nothing. A dark coda to the theme of how things have declined.

## War Decrees

### Vespasian Decrees

The last *mishna* of Masechet Sotah turns to a related but somewhat different note: not merely the world going to pot of its own deterioration, but the communal response to the *churban* — the *gezeirot* enacted to limit expressions of joy as a *zecher l'churban*. It is worth noting, as Rav Soloveitchik emphasized, that there is a distinction between *zecher l'mikdash* — commemorating what the *beit hamikdash* was like in its glory — and *zecher l'churban* — commemorating the fact of its destruction. These decrees fall into the latter category.

*Bepulmus shel Vespasian* — in the wars of Vespasian — they decreed against *atarot hatanim*, the crowns worn by grooms, and against *hairus* (a kind of drum instrument, which we will return to).

### Titus Decrees

*Bepulmus shel Titus* — in the wars of Titus, who succeeded Vespasian — they decreed against *atarot kallot*, the crowns worn by brides, and *shelo yilamed adam et beno yevanit*, that a person should not teach his son Greek wisdom. The reasoning behind this latter decree will be explained in the *gemara* below.

### Carriage Decree

*Bepulmus ha'acharon* — in the last war, which may refer either to the destruction itself or, according to some opinions, to the great revolt concluded around the time of Bar Kochba — they decreed *shelo tetze kallah ba'apiryon betokh ha'ir*, that a bride should not go out in a carriage or litter through the city during her wedding procession. The *mishna* notes that subsequently *veraboteinu hitiru* — the Rabbis permitted it again. Indeed, in Masechet Ketubot, the public procession of the bride in such a litter was a marker of a first marriage.

## Deaths of Sages

### Meir Parables

The *mishna* continues with a litany of losses, enumerating what the world lost upon the death of each great sage. *Mishemmet Rabbi Meir batlu moshlei meshalim* — when Rabbi Meir died, those who could craft parables were gone, for he was known for his mastery of the *mashal*.

### Ben Azzai

*Mishemmet Ben Azzai batlu hashakdanim* — when Ben Azzai died, those characterized by unceasing, tireless diligence in Torah study were gone.

### Ben Zoma

*Mishemmet Ben Zoma batlu hadarshanim* — when Ben Zoma died, those who could derive teachings from *pesukim* were gone. Rashi explains that this refers not to *agadic* homiletics but to the kind of textual derivation associated with Ben Zoma himself, as in the famous teaching *ad she-darsha Ben Zoma*, where he derived the requirement to remember the Exodus at night from a careful reading of the verse.

### Akiva Power

*Mishemmet Rabbi Akiva batel kvod haTorah* — when Rabbi Akiva died, the honor of Torah departed — *v'nistatmu ma'ayanot hachachmah*, the wellsprings of wisdom were stopped up. This is because Rabbi Akiva was the supreme *mechadesh*, the one who drew new Torah from every extra letter and from every *kots shel yud*, and with his death that power of creative *chiddush* was lost.

### Chanina Miracles

*Mishemmet Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa batlu anshei ma'aseh* — when Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa died, those who performed wondrous deeds were gone. The stories surrounding Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa are replete with accounts of miraculous occurrences in his presence, and with his death that dimension of the world's experience was closed.

### Pious End

*Mishemmet Abba Yosi ben Katnuta batlu chasidim* — when Abba Yosi ben Katnuta died, the truly pious were gone. And why was he called *Katnuta* — the small one? *She-hayah mikatnei chasidim* — because he was even one of the most minor of the pious. The very fact that the smallest of the pious was still around had sustained the world; once even he was gone, the category of *chasid* was extinguished.

### Yochanan Wisdom

*Mishemmet Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai batal ziv hachochmah* — when Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai died, the luminous radiance of wisdom departed. Rashi acknowledges with characteristic honesty: *lo yadana mai hi* — I do not know precisely what the connection is.

### Gamliel Health

*Mishemmet Rabban Gamliel HaZaken batel kvod haTorah* — when Rabban Gamliel HaZaken died, the honor of Torah was lost, and *umesah taharah u'ferishut* — purity and asceticism ended. Rashi points to the Gemara in Megillah which records that after his death, people became far less healthy, and when people are not well, their Torah study necessarily diminishes.

### Yishmael Glory

*Mishemmet Rabbi Yishmael ben Pabi batlah ziv hakehunah* — when Rabbi Yishmael ben Pabi died, the glory of the priesthood departed. He was a wealthy man who supported the *kohanim*, and with him the *kavod* of the *kehunah* was extinguished.

### Rebbe Humility

*Mishemmet Rebbe batlah anavah v'yirat chet* — when Rebbe died — that is, Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi, the final editor of the *Mishna* — both humility and true fear of sin departed from the world. This is a somewhat sobering note on which to end the *mishna*. Most final *mishnayot* of a *masechet* conclude on an uplifting note, and some *rishonim* indeed have a different *girsa* that includes additional lines here.

## Path to Mashiach

### Pinchas Ladder

Some versions of the *mishna* — though not in the standard printed *gemara* text — include the following statement from Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair, which in many *mishnayot* prints appears as the conclusion of the *masechet*:

*Zerizut mevia lidei nekiut, nekiut mevia lidei taharah, taharah mevia lidei perishut, perishut mevia lidei kedushah, kedushah mevia lidei anavah, anavah mevia lidei yirat chet, yirat chet mevia lidei chasidut, chasidut mevia lidei ruach hakodesh, ruach hakodesh mevia lidei techiyat hametim, u'techiyat hametim ba al yedei Eliyahu zachur latov.*

Diligence leads to cleanliness, cleanliness to *taharah*, *taharah* to *perishut*, *perishut* to *kedushah*, *kedushah* to *anavah*, *anavah* to fear of sin, fear of sin to *chasidut*, *chasidut* to *ruach hakodesh*, *ruach hakodesh* to the resurrection of the dead, and the resurrection will come through Eliyahu, may his memory be for blessing.

This is a profoundly different note on which to end the *masechet*. Everything may indeed be going to pot — but here is a ladder. Small, incremental steps of spiritual discipline, each one drawing the next, can reverse the entire process and lead ultimately to the coming of *Mashiach* and the resurrection of the dead. That this statement serves as the structural framework for the Ramchal's *Mesillat Yesharim* is well known. It is genuinely sad that it is not in our standard *girsa*, because it is such a powerful and necessary counterweight to everything that preceded it.

## Pre-Mashiach Chaos

### Chutzpah & Heresy

The *gemara* continues — whether as *baraita* or otherwise — with the famous passage about *ikveta d'Meshicha*, the footsteps of *Mashiach*, the period immediately preceding the redemption. *Be'ikvata d'Meshicha chutzpa yasga* — in the footsteps of *Mashiach*, insolence will proliferate. *Veyoker yaamir* — honor will be perverted. *Hagefen titein pirya vehayayin beyoker* — even though the vine will give its fruit, wine will be expensive, because people will be running after hedonistic pleasures. *U'malchut tahafech leminut* — the kingdom will turn to *minut*, heresy.

This last phrase requires explanation. Why should we care whether a non-Jewish government becomes heretical? Rashi — in the passage that appears in the outer margin of some editions, in text that was edited out of the standard Rashi — explains: the kingdom that rules over most of the world will turn toward *minut*, following the error of a particular figure and his disciples, who were called *minim*. Rashi is referring to the Roman Empire becoming the Christian Holy Roman Empire. The phrase *tahafech leminut* thus means the Christianization of Rome.

## Path to Mashiach…

### Ikvata Meshicha

The placement of these passages — everything getting worse right before *Mashiach* — is itself understood as a positive spin on the preceding litany of decline. The worse things get, the more they constitute the *ikveta d'Meshicha*, the footsteps, the foreshadowing of the redemption. The darkness is itself the sign that dawn approaches.

## Pre-Mashiach Chaos…

### No Rebuke

*Ve'ein tochecha* — there will be no one to remonstrate, because everyone is equally sinning and no one can rebuke another. *Beit va'ad yihiyeh liznut* — the house of assembly, formerly the place of Torah, will become a place of prostitution. *Vehagalil yecherav vehagavlan yaishom* — the Galil will be destroyed and this place called *Gavlan* will be desolate — *ve'anshei hagvul yesovevu me'ir le'ir velo yechonenu* — and the people of the border will wander from city to city and find no rest.

### Wisdom Lost

*Ve'chochmat sofrim tisrach* — the wisdom of the scribes will become corrupted and putrid. *Veyirei chet yimasu* — those who fear sin will be despised. *Ve'ha'emet tehei ne'ederet* — truth will be absent, will be a rarity.

### Youth Shame Elders

*Ne'arim pnei zekenim yalbinu* — youth will shame and humiliate elders. *Zekenim yaamdu mipnei ktanim* — elders will be expected to stand before the young. *Ben menavel av bat kama be'ima kala bachamota* — a son will disgrace his father, a daughter will rise against her mother, a daughter-in-law against her mother-in-law. *Oivei ish anshei beito* — a person's enemies will be the members of his own household — an image that conjures the world of informers and denunciations.

### Generation Face

*Pnei hador k'pnei hakelev* — the face of the generation will be like the face of dogs. Whether this means cruelty, indifference, or simply shamelessness is left somewhat open, though the following phrase clarifies the register.

### Son Shame

*Habein eino mitbayes me'aviv* — a son will not be embarrassed before his father. The breakdown of shame, of basic filial inhibition, is the culminating sign.

### Father Heaven

*Ve'al mah yesh lanu lehisha'en? Al avinu shebashamayim* — upon whom do we have to rely? Only upon our Father in heaven. This phrase serves as the conclusion of the *mishna* in some versions, and it is a genuine note of faith: despite the complete collapse of social and moral order, the only stable anchor that remains is God Himself.

## War Decrees…

### Groom Crowns

The *gemara* now returns to elaborate on the details of the *mishna*'s decrees. Rav says: the prohibition on grooms' crowns applies only to those made of salt and sulfur — some type of mineral formation used for such adornments. But crowns made of myrtle or rose — *shel hadas veshel vered* — are permitted.

### Crown Dispute

Shmuel disagrees: even a crown of myrtle and rose is forbidden. However, one made of reeds or some type of seaweed — *shel kanim veshel chilat* — is permitted. Levi goes further still: even reeds and seaweed are forbidden, and this is what he also taught in his *baraita*.

### Irus Drum

What exactly is the *irus* mentioned in the *mishna*? Rav Elazar says: *tavla dechad puma* — a tablet with one mouth, which Rashi understands as some type of clapper or gong. The discussion then turns to drums. Rabbah bar Rav Huna made a *tanbura* — a type of drum — for his son. His father came and broke it. He said: if you make that kind of drum, it will be confused with a *tavla dechad puma*, which is forbidden. If you want to make a drum for your son, make one on the mouth of a clay pot — *apuma dechatztzava* — or on the opening of some other household container, so that it will not be confused with the instruments that are actually prohibited.

### Bride Crowns

What were the *atarot kallot* — the brides' crowns — that were prohibited during Titus's wars? Rabbah bar bar Chana says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ir shel zahav* — a golden city, some type of tiara made to look like the city of Jerusalem worked in gold. A *baraita* confirms this, and adds that what one may do instead is make *kippah shel milat* — some type of head covering of silk.

### Wedding Canopy

The *gemara* also records that a *tanna* taught that the prohibition extended to *chuppat chatanim* — a particular type of wedding canopy. What was this *chuppah*? *Zehorit mezuhevet* — a crimson fabric adorned with gold, a *tallit* dyed crimson with golden ornaments woven through it, which would be fashioned into a tent-like canopy. Rashi describes this in some detail. What remains permitted is a *papirit* — Rashi describes this as a frame of wooden arches upon which one can hang various ornaments and decorations as one wishes, which is the permissible alternative canopy.

### Permitted Alternatives

The *gemara* thus establishes that while the most ornate and elaborate forms of wedding adornment were prohibited in the wake of the various wars, the Rabbis permitted alternative forms that preserved the dignity and joy of the occasion without the extravagance associated with the pre-destruction era.

### Pig & Greek

A famous *baraita* is now cited to explain the prohibition on teaching Greek wisdom. When the Hasmonean kings were at war with one another — Hyrcanus on the outside of Jerusalem with Roman troops, and Aristobulus on the inside — the besieged would daily lower *dinarim* in a basket, and those outside would send up animals for the *temidim*, the daily sacrifices, because everyone still cared about the *avodah* in the *beit hamikdash*.

There was an elderly man on the inside of the walls who knew *chochmah Yevanis* — Greek wisdom, apparently a method of covert signaling. He communicated to those outside: as long as those inside continue performing the *avodah*, they will not be surrendered into your hands. The next day, when the besiegers lowered the *dinarim*, instead of sending up a lamb, they sent up a pig. When the pig reached halfway up the wall, it dug its claws into the wall, and the entire land of Israel shook for a distance of 400 *parsah*. At that moment they declared: *arur adam she-yigadel chazirim* — cursed is the person who raises pigs — *ve-arur adam she-yelamed livno chochmah Yevanis* — and cursed is the person who teaches his son Greek wisdom.

The Gemara adds that in that same year, *mavi'in ha-omer mi-gagots tzerifin* — they had to bring the *omer* from grain stored on rooftops or from distant places like *Bik'at Ein Socher*, since the besieging troops had burned all the fields around Jerusalem, and normal supplies were unavailable.

### Greek Language

Is the prohibition really absolute? The Gemara raises the question, for Rav teaches: Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi said that in Eretz Yisrael, one should not speak *Sursi* — Aramaic, a vernacular considered coarse — but rather either *leshon hakodesh* or Greek. If one is not going to speak the holy tongue, at least speak a refined and culturally elevated language. And Rav Yosef similarly teaches that in Bavel, one should not speak Aramaic but rather either *leshon hakodesh* or Persian — *leshon Parsi* — meaning Farsi, which was considered the elevated vernacular of that region.

The resolution: *leshon Yevanis lachud, chochmah Yevanis lachud* — the Greek *language* is one matter, Greek *wisdom* is quite another. One may speak Greek; what is forbidden is the study of *chochmah Yevanis* in its technical sense — the esoteric or philosophical wisdom that was used deceptively in the incident above.

### Gamliel Family

Rav Yehudah in the name of Shmuel cites a statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in explanation of the verse *eini olelah le-nafshi mi-kol benos iri* — my eye brings grief to my soul more than all the daughters of my city. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: my father's household had a thousand children. Five hundred learned Torah and five hundred learned *chochmah Yevanis*. And of all of them, only I remain, and the son of my father's brother who is in Asia. Clearly, then, the Nasi's household itself had half its children studying Greek wisdom.

### Close to Govt

The Gemara explains: *shani shel beis Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel de-krovim le-malchus havu* — the household of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was different, because they were close to the Roman government and had to maintain those relations. A *baraita* confirms this principle: Avtolmos ben Reuven was permitted to wear his hair in the *komi* style — a particular Roman hairstyle that would normally constitute *darchei ha-Emori*, pagan practices — because he was close to the government and had to appear a certain way. Similarly, the household of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was permitted to learn *chochmah Yevanis* because of their proximity to the Roman government. The category of *karov le-malchus* — proximity to the government — thus functions as a recognized exception to otherwise forbidden practices.

## Halachic Details

### Crown Materials

The prohibition on grooms' crowns enacted during Vespasian's wars, the *gemara* clarifies, applies specifically to crowns made of salt and sulfur. This is the baseline position — not all materials are equally forbidden.

### Crown Dispute

The debate among Rav, Shmuel, and Levi, as detailed above, represents a spectrum of increasing stringency: Rav permits myrtle and rose; Shmuel forbids those but permits reeds and seaweed; Levi forbids even reeds and seaweed. Levi's position is supported by his own *baraita*.

### Drum Making

Rabbah bar Rav Huna's attempt to make a *tanbur*-style drum for his son, and his father's intervention, illustrates the concern about confusion with genuinely forbidden instruments. The practical ruling is to construct a simple drum from a household pot or similar vessel — *apuma dechatztzava* or *apuma dekafiza* — precisely so that no one mistakes it for a *tavla dechad puma*, which falls within the scope of the decree.

## Final Hope

### Yosef Humility

The Gemara concludes, following the *mishna*'s statement that when Rebbe died *batlah anavah* — humility departed — with a wonderful moment of humor. Rav Yosef said to the *tanna* reciting the *baraita*: do not recite that *anavah* is gone — *d'ika ana*, I am still here! This is the Gemara's way of not ending on pure despair. Humility does not mean denying one's own greatness; as the Gemara implies by bringing Rav Yosef's protest here, true *anavah* is about how one carries oneself, not false modesty. Moshe Rabbeinu was *anav mikol adam* and yet he knew his own stature. Rav Yosef's claim is itself a statement about what *anavah* means.

### Nachman Fear

Similarly, Rav Nachman said to the *tanna*: do not say that *yirat chet* has disappeared — *d'ika ana*, I am still around. Again, a note of hope: however grim the picture painted by the *mishna* and *baraita*, there is still someone left. Things are not completely lost. The Gemara ends with a gentle insistence that we are not yet beyond redemption.

### Hadran

*Hadran alach Masechet Sotah.*

הדרן עלך מסכת סוטה ודעתן עלך מסכת סוטה דעתך עלן לא נתנשי מינך מסכת סוטה ולא תתנשי מינן לא בעלמא הדין ולא בעלמא דאתי.

We return to you, Masechet Sotah, and our minds are upon you, Masechet Sotah. Your mind is upon us; we shall not forget you, Masechet Sotah, nor shall you forget us — neither in this world nor in the world to come.
