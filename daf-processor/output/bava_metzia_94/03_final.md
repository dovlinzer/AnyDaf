# Bava Metzia 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## T'nai Fundamentals

### T'nai Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַתְנֶה שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם לִהְיוֹת פָּטוּר מִשְּׁבוּעָה, וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל לִהְיוֹת פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר לִהְיוֹת פְּטוּרִין מִשְּׁבוּעָה וּמִלְּשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **An unpaid bailee may stipulate** with the owner **that** he will **be exempt from** taking **an oath** if the item is lost, **and** similarly, **a borrower** may stipulate **that** he will **be exempt from** having **to pay,** and **a paid bailee or a renter** can stipulate **that** he will **be exempt from** taking **an oath and from** having **to pay,** as one can relinquish his monetary rights.

The *mishnah* on 94a opens with the subject of *t'nai* — conditional statements — and the question of when a given condition is considered halachically valid or invalid. The basic framework is as follows: if a *t'nai* is valid, the action to which one has committed oneself conditionally will take effect only if the condition is fulfilled — the Mishnah uses the language *mekuyam*, fulfilled. If the *t'nai* is somehow invalid, then it is *batel*, nullified, and the action takes effect unconditionally regardless of whether the condition was ever met.

### Shomrim Conditions

The *mishnah* begins with the four *shomrim*, the four categories of guardians, which have been introduced in previous *mishnayot*. The language is: *matneh shomer chinam lihyot patur mishvuah vehashoel lihyot patur milishalem noseh sachar vehasocher lihyot pturim mishvuah umilishalem*. A *shomer chinam* — a free guardian — is obligated only for negligence (*pshia*), and is exempt from payment for any other occurrence — theft, loss, or an act of God — provided he takes an oath. So if this *shomer* conditions his acceptance of the role on being exempt from taking such an oath, the *mishnah* says that condition is valid. At the opposite end of the spectrum, the *sho'el* — the borrower — bears the highest level of responsibility, being liable even for acts of God, and he too may condition his borrowing on being released from these obligations. Between these two extremes, the *noseh sachar* and the *socher* — the paid guardian and the renter — may similarly condition their undertaking on exemption from both the oath and from payment in the cases of *gneivah* and *aveidah*, theft and loss.

### Against Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַמַּתְנֶה עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה – תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל,
>
> **Translation:** With regard to matters that do not involve monetary claims, **anyone who stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah, his stipulation is void.**

The *mishnah* then states its second rule: *kol hamatneh al mah shekatuv baTorah t'na'o batel* — anyone who makes a condition against something the Torah has commanded, that condition is void, and the person has committed themselves to the act unconditionally. The *gemara* will raise a sharp question about this in light of the first rule, since the obligations of the *shomrim* are themselves rooted in the Torah. The example of such an impermissible condition will be illustrated through the case of *kiddushin*.

### Ma'aseh B'Tchilatov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל תְּנַאי שֶׁיֵּשׁ מַעֲשֶׂה בִּתְחִילָּתוֹ – תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And any condition that is preceded by an action,** i.e., the agreement is formulated with the promise of an action followed by a statement that this action will be carried out only under certain terms, **the condition is void** and the promise remains intact.

The third rule of the *mishnah* is: *vechol t'nai sheyesh ma'aseh bitchilato t'na'o batel* — any condition in which the *ma'aseh*, the action, is stated before the conditional clause, is also invalid. Rashi explains: *shehikdim ma'aseh she'alav la'asos l'tnai shehu sho'el mimenu* — the person has stated first the action he is committing to do, and only then articulated the condition he is requesting. For example, saying "This thing is yours, if you do such-and-such" is invalid because the *then* precedes the *if*.

The reason for this rule is that a valid *t'nai* must resemble the model of *bnei Gad u'bnei Reuven*. At the end of *Parshat Matot*, the tribes of Gad and Reuven came before Moshe and asked to remain in the Trans-Jordan. Moshe ultimately agreed, and the structure of his conditional grant was: *im ya'avru u'nesatem* — "if you cross before your brothers and help them conquer the land, then you will be given this land." The conditional statement — the *if* — comes first; the consequent action — the *then* — follows. That is the required form. Rashi cites Yaakov's words to Lavan similarly: *im ta'aseh li hadavar hazeh ashuvah ereh tzonecha* — if you do this thing for me, then I will go and shepherd your flocks. The condition precedes the commitment.

What does it mean for the condition to be *batel*? Rashi explains: *v'havei ma'aseh mikuyam* — the action takes effect regardless. Even though the person has nominally conditioned his act, if the condition was stated improperly, it is as though no condition was made, and the *then* clause is binding whether or not the *if* is ever fulfilled.

### Efshar L'Kaymo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְקַיְּימוֹ בְּסוֹפוֹ, וְהִתְנָה עִמּוֹ מִתְּחִילָּתוֹ – תְּנָאוֹ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to any condition **that one can ultimately fulfill, but he stipulated with him initially,** i.e., in practice the action is performed first, followed by the fulfillment of the condition, nevertheless, because it was formulated in the proper manner, with the condition first, **his condition is valid.** If the condition cannot be fulfilled at all, once the action has been carried out the condition is void.

The *mishnah*'s fourth rule: *vechol she'efshar lo l'kaymo b'sofo v'hitnah imo mit'chilato t'na'o kayam* — any condition that is possible to fulfill at the end, and which was stipulated at the outset, is a valid condition. Rashi explains: provided the *t'nai* was stated before the *ma'aseh* — that is, the proper order was observed — then if the condition is possible to fulfill, it is a good *t'nai*. However, if the condition is impossible to fulfill — *ee efshar l'kaymo* — then the *t'nai* is *batel* and the action stands regardless. The *gemara* will supply examples and will clarify the law.

## Shomer & Torah

### Initial Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַמַּאי? מַתְנֶה עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה הוּא, וְכׇל הַמַּתְנֶה עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks a question with regard to the mishna's statement that bailees can issue conditions and change the liabilities imposed on them by Torah law: **Why** are they able to do so? This **is** a case of one who **stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah,** as the Torah determines who takes an oath and who must pay, **and** with regard to **anyone who stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah, his condition is void.**

The *gemara* opens with an immediate difficulty. The *mishnah*'s first rule permits a *shomer* to condition his guardianship on being released from obligations the Torah itself imposes on him. But the *mishnah*'s second rule declares that any condition made against what is written in the Torah is void. These two rules appear contradictory. The *gemara* frames the question sharply: *amai?* Why is that a valid *t'nai*? *Matneh al mah shekatuv baTorah hu* — it is precisely a condition against what the Torah has written. And the *mishnah* itself says *kol hamatneh al mah shekatuv baTorah t'na'o batel*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בְּדָבָר שֶׁבְּמָמוֹן – תְּנָאוֹ קַיָּים. דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֵין לִיךְ עָלַי שְׁאֵר כְּסוּת וְעוֹנָה״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וּתְנָאוֹ בָּטֵל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּדָבָר שֶׁבְּמָמוֹן – תְּנָאוֹ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** that of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that if the condition that runs counter to that which is written in the Torah is referring **to monetary matters, his condition is valid. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me on the condition that you have no** claim **against me** to give you **food, clothing, and conjugal rights, she is betrothed but his condition is void;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Yehuda said: With regard to monetary matters,** i.e., her food and clothing, **his condition is valid.**

The *gemara* initially suggests: *ha mani? Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this *mishnah* follows the view of Rabbi Yehuda. *De'amar bedavar shebemamon t'na'o kayam* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that while one cannot generally condition against a Torah obligation, in monetary matters such a condition is valid. The *shomer*'s exemption from payment is precisely a monetary matter — he is attempting to be released from a financial obligation — and therefore according to Rabbi Yehuda the condition stands.

### Marriage Case

The *gemara* adduces a *baraita* in support: *amar le'ishah harei at mekudeshet li al menat she'ein li alayich she'er kesut ve'onah harei zu mekudeshet vetna'o batel divrei Rabbi Meir*. A man says to a woman: you are betrothed to me on the condition that you have no claim against me for *she'er*, *kesut*, and *onah* — food, clothing, and conjugal rights. Rabbi Meir rules: she is *mekudeshet*, and the condition is *batel*, because this is a condition against what the Torah has commanded. Then: *Rabbi Yehuda omer bedavar shebemamon t'na'o kayam* — Rabbi Yehuda says that regarding monetary matters, namely *she'er* and *kesut* (food and clothing), the condition is valid.

However, the *gemara* pushes back: can we really attribute the *mishnah*'s first rule to Rabbi Yehuda when the very next clause — *kol hamatneh al mah shekatuv baTorah t'na'o batel* — is an unqualified statement that seems to reflect the position of Rabbi Meir? The *gemara* attempts to resolve this by saying that the latter clause refers only to non-monetary ritual matters, *davar she'eino shel mamon*, and the two clauses can coexist under Rabbi Yehuda. But this resolution is immediately challenged: the clause following that — *kol t'nai sheyesh bo ma'aseh bitchilato t'na'o batel*, the rule about the *ma'aseh* preceding the *t'nai* — is demonstrably the position of Rabbi Meir, as attested by the *baraita* in which Abba Chalafta says in the name of Rabbi Meir: *t'nai kodem l'ma'aseh harei zeh t'nai; ma'aseh kodem l'tnai eino t'nai*. If the latter clause is Rabbi Meir's, the *mishnah* cannot be split between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir in an unstated, inconsistent manner.

### Rabbi Meir View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית מוֹקְמַתְּ לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אֵימָא סֵיפָא: כׇּל הַמַּתְנֶה עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה – תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל, אֲתָאן לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וְסֵיפָא – בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁל מָמוֹן. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: כׇּל תְּנַאי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה בִּתְחִילָּתוֹ – תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל, מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאִית לֵיהּ הָא סְבָרָא – רַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּתַנְיָא: אַבָּא חֲלַפְתָּא אִישׁ כְּפַר חֲנַנְיָא אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: תְּנַאי קוֹדֵם לַמַּעֲשֶׂה – הֲרֵי זֶה תְּנַאי, מַעֲשֶׂה קוֹדֵם לַתְּנַאי – אֵינוֹ תְּנַאי. אֶלָּא כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא שַׁעְבֵּד נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But can you establish** the mishna **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **Anyone who stipulates counter to that which is written in the Torah, his condition is void.** In this clause **we arrive at** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** The Gemara answers: **This** is **not difficult,** as **actually** you can explain that the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and the latter clause** is referring **to non-monetary matters.** The Gemara continues to question this explanation: But **say the latter clause** from near the end of the mishna: **And any condition that is preceded by an action, the condition is void. Who have you heard who accepts this reasoning?** It is **Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Abba Ḥalafta, from** the **village of Ḥananya, said in the name of Rabbi Meir:** If **a condition** was stated **before the action, this is** a valid **condition,** but if **the action** came **before the condition, it is not** a valid **condition.** **Rather,** the Gemara retracts the previous explanation and states that the **entire** mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.** Why, then, is the bailee exempt from payment or an oath? Because **here it is different, as at the outset he did not obligate himself** in the *halakhot* of a bailee as stated in the Torah. Before he entered into the agreement, he clearly stated that he is unwilling to accept upon himself the liabilities of a paid or an unpaid bailee by Torah law.

The *gemara* therefore arrives at a new resolution: *ela kula Rabbi Meir hi* — the entire *mishnah* is the view of Rabbi Meir. And as for the first rule, permitting the *shomer* to condition his guardianship — *shani hacha de'me'ikara lo sha'abed nafshei* — the case of the *shomer* is exceptional because at the very outset he did not obligate himself.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi's explanation here is illuminating. Even though we might initially think the *shomer chinam* who conditions his exemption from the oath is making a condition against the Torah, that is not so. *Me'ikara lo shavei nafshei* — from the very beginning he did not bind himself. When Reuven asks Shimon to be his *shomer chinam* and Shimon says *ee efshi she'eheyeh shomer shelcha ella b'kach* — I am unwilling to be your guardian except on these terms — he has not yet become a *shomer* at all. *V'shomer lo nachit lishmira ad d'mashach l'vehema* — a person does not become a *shomer* until the act of *meshicha*, the legally valid act of acquisition, takes place, whereby he actually takes hold of the animal. *V'hai ki mashach kvar pirash al menat shelo alav shvua* — and when he did take hold of the animal, he had already preceded that with his condition. Thus he has committed himself only to a partial form of guardianship: *v'lo shavei nafshei l'ayeilei b'torat shomrim ella b'miksas* — he has only obligated himself in a limited sense. He is not accepting the full biblical category of *shomer chinam*; he is saying, in effect, I will watch your animal for free, but without any of the attendant biblical obligations.

The contrast with *kiddushin* is instructive. *Aval hamikadesh et ha'ishah, miki amar lah harei at mekudeshet li, ikadsha lah* — the moment a man pronounces the words of *kiddushin*, she is betrothed. When he then adds that he bears no financial obligations to her, that is *matneh al mah shekatuv baTorah* — a condition against the Torah — because *ein ishut l'chatza'in*, there is no such thing as being partially married. The act of *kiddushin* takes complete effect the moment it occurs. *V'havei matneh al mah shekasuv baTorah*. With the *shomer*, by contrast, he never initially took on the full biblical role; he stepped into a more limited, self-defined form of custodianship before the *kinyan* ever made him a Torah-defined guardian.

## Shomer Sachar

### Shomer->Sho'el

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וּמַתְנֶה שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר לִהְיוֹת כְּשׁוֹאֵל. בְּמַאי? בִּדְבָרִים?! אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּשֶׁקָּנוּ מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** A Sage **taught** in a *baraita*: Just as a bailee can issue a condition that he should be exempt, the converse is also possible: **A paid bailee can stipulate to be like a borrower,** i.e., he can accept upon himself all the responsibilities of a borrower. The Gemara asks: **By what** means is this commitment binding? Is it merely **by speech** alone? Mere speech is not sufficient to demonstrate a commitment of this kind. **Shmuel said:** No; it is referring to a case **where** the owner **performed** an act of **acquisition with** the bailee affirming this arrangement. The obligation goes into effect only if there was an act of acquisition.

A *baraita* now raises a striking case: *umatneh shomer sachar lihyot k'sho'el* — a *shomer sachar* conditions his guardianship such that he accepts the full responsibilities of a *sho'el*, the borrower, who is liable even for acts of God. This might seem puzzling — why would anyone voluntarily accept greater liability? Yet this is precisely the case the *baraita* contemplates.

### Kinyan Required?

The *gemara* records a dispute over whether such an elevation of responsibility requires a formal *kinyan*. *Amar Shmuel beshikanu miyado* — Shmuel says yes, a *kinyan* must be performed. If the owner agrees — and why would he not? — and they perform a *kinyan*, then the *shomer sachar* takes on the full obligations of a *sho'el*.

### Reputation Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּשֶׁלֹּא קָנוּ מִיָּדוֹ, בְּהָהִיא הֲנָאָה דְּקָא נָפֵיק לֵיהּ קָלָא דְּאִינִישׁ מְהֵימְנָא הוּא – גָּמַיר וּמְשַׁעְבֵּד נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: You** may **even say** that it is referring to a situation **where** the owner **did not perform** an act of **acquisition with** the bailee, and even so he is liable as a borrower. The reason is that **by** means of **that benefit** he receives from the fact **that a rumor goes out about him that he is** a **trustworthy person, he** commits wholeheartedly to **obligate himself,** even by means of a verbal promise alone. Therefore, there is no need for a formal act of acquisition.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *afilu teima beshelo kanu miyado* — even without a *kinyan*. His reasoning is: *behahi hana'ah d'kanfik leh kala d'inish meheiman hu gamir umeshabed nafshei* — through the benefit (*hana'ah*) that accrues to him from the reputation he gains as a trustworthy person, he fully binds himself to this higher level of responsibility. The relationship between *hana'ah* and responsibility is a direct one: the *sho'el* bears maximum liability because he receives maximum benefit — the free use of an object. But Rabbi Yochanan identifies a different kind of *hana'ah* available to a *shomer sachar* who accepts *sho'el*-level responsibility: his reputation for reliability. The word will spread that he is a person of his word and of exceptional trustworthiness, and that reputational benefit is sufficient, without a formal *kinyan*, to create the binding obligation.

## Impossible Conditions

### Possible vs Impossible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְקַיְּימוֹ בְּסוֹפוֹ וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב טַבְלָא אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן תֵּימָא. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְקַיְּימוֹ בְּסוֹפוֹ וְהִתְנָה עָלָיו מִתְּחִילָּתוֹ – תְּנָאוֹ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: With regard to **any** condition **that ultimately can be fulfilled,** if he stipulated it initially, his condition is valid. **Rav Tavla says** that **Rav says: This is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima, but the Rabbis say: Even though one cannot ultimately fulfill** the condition, **and he stipulated with regard to it initially, his condition is valid.**

The *mishnah*'s rule — that a *t'nai* which is possible to fulfill at the end is valid, while one that is impossible is *batel* — is now taken up by the *gemara* in greater detail. *Amar Rav Tavla amar Rav zo divrei Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema* — this *mishnah* reflects the position of Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema.

### Merely Torment

The *gemara* explains that an impossible condition is nothing but *mifliga vekasher* — mere torment — an attempt to make life miserable for the other party. There is no genuine conditional intent; the person making the condition knows it cannot be met.

### Divorce Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּעֲלִי לָרָקִיעַ, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתֵּרְדִי לַתְּהוֹם, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּבְלְעִי קָנֶה שֶׁל מֵאָה אַמָּה, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּעַבְרִי אֶת הַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל בְּרַגְלַיִךְ״, נִתְקַיֵּים הַתְּנַאי – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט, לֹא נִתְקַיֵּים הַתְּנַאי – אֵינוֹ גֵּט. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן תֵּימָא אוֹמֵר: כָּזֶה – גֵּט. כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן תֵּימָא: כֹּל שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְקַיְּימוֹ בְּסוֹפוֹ, וְהִתְנָה עָלָיו מִתְּחִילָּתוֹ – אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כְּמַפְלִיגָהּ וְכָשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a man said to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you ascend to the skies,** or **on the condition that you descend to the depths;** or **on the condition that you swallow a reed of one hundred cubits** in size; or **on the condition that you cross the Great Sea by foot,** only if **the condition was fulfilled** and she did as he demanded **is this** a valid **bill of divorce,** but if **the condition was not fulfilled** it is **not** a valid **bill of divorce.** According to this *tanna*, the condition is binding despite the fact that it cannot be fulfilled in practice. **Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima says:** A document **such as this is** a valid **bill of divorce.** The condition is negated, and therefore the bill of divorce is valid even though the condition was not fulfilled. As **Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima stated a principle:** With regard to **any** condition **that one cannot ultimately fulfill,** i.e., a condition that cannot be fulfilled at all, **and** yet **he stipulated** to this effect, even if he did so **initially, he is** considered **as only exaggerating, and** the document is **valid.** The supposed condition is not taken seriously and is not binding.

The *baraita* supplies vivid examples in the context of divorce. *Harei zeh gittech al menat sheta'ali larakia* — this is your bill of divorce, on condition that you ascend to the heavens. *Al menat sheterdi latehom* — on condition that you descend to the depths of the sea. *Al menat shetivli kaneh shel meah amah* — on condition that you swallow a rod one hundred *amot* in length. *Al menat sheta'avri et hayam hagadol beraglayich* — on condition that you cross the Mediterranean Sea on foot. The *baraita* says: if the condition is fulfilled, it is a valid *get*; if not, it is not a *get*. Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema says: in all such cases it is a valid *get*, and frames the matter as a general principle: *kol she'i efshar l'kaymo besofo v'hitnah alav mitechilato eino ela kemifliga* — any such impossible condition is merely the person tormenting the other party, and the *get* is valid regardless.

### Halacha Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן תֵּימָא. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: כׇּל שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְקַיְּימוֹ בְּסוֹפוֹ וְהִתְנָה עָלָיו מִתְּחִילָּתוֹ – תְּנָאוֹ קַיָּים, הָא אִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְקַיְּימוֹ – תְּנָאוֹ בָּטֵל. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: The mishna is also precisely** formulated in accordance with this opinion, **as it teaches:** With regard to **any** condition **that ultimately he can fulfill, and he stipulated with regard to it initially, his condition is valid.** This indicates that if **he cannot fulfill it, his condition is void.** The Gemara affirms: One can **learn from this** formulation that the unattributed mishna does in fact represent the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima.

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Rav: halacha keRabbi Yehuda ben Tema* — Rav Nachman rules that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema. An impossible condition does not void the accompanying act. This ruling is then supported by a careful reading of the *mishnah* itself: the *mishnah* says that a condition which is possible to fulfill is valid — the implication, *ha ee efshar*, being that if it is impossible, the condition is *batel* but the act stands. This inference from the *mishnah* corroborates the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema.

## Ba'alav Imo

### Cow Owner Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹאֵל אֶת הַפָּרָה וְשָׁאַל בְּעָלֶיהָ עִמָּהּ, אוֹ שָׂכַר בְּעָלֶיהָ עִמָּהּ, שָׁאַל הַבְּעָלִים אוֹ שְׂכָרָן וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁאַל אֶת הַפָּרָה וָמֵתָה – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם בְּעָלָיו עִמּוֹ לֹא יְשַׁלֵּם״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who borrowed a cow and borrowed** the services of **its owner with it, or** he borrowed a cow and **hired its owner with it,** or he **borrowed** the services of **the owner or hired him and afterward borrowed the cow;** in all such cases, if the cow **died,** the borrower is **exempt** from liability. Although a borrower is generally liable to pay if a cow he borrowed dies, here he is exempt, **as it is stated: "If its owner is with him, he does not pay"** (Exodus 22:14).

The *mishnah* at this point addresses the principle of *ba'alav imo* — the owner being present with the borrower. The *mishnah* distinguishes between two scenarios. First: *hashoel parah vesha'alah ba'aleha imah* — one borrows a cow and simultaneously borrows or hires the labor of its owner. Second: *sha'al haba'alim o secharan ve'achar kach sha'al et haparah* — one first borrows or hires the owner and only then borrows the cow. In both of these cases, if the cow subsequently dies, the borrower is exempt: *patur*, because the owner was present when the borrowing took effect. By contrast, if one borrowed the cow first and only afterwards engaged the owner, and then the cow dies — *chayav*, he must pay.

### Torah Source

The *mishnah*'s ruling is grounded in the Torah's language. The verse states: *v'chi yish'al ish me'im re'ehu v'nishbar o met ba'alav ein imo shalem yeshalem* — if a person borrows from his fellow and the animal is injured or dies, if the owner was not with him he shall pay in full. *Im ba'alav imo lo yeshalem* — if the owner was with him, he need not pay. The *mishnah* is defining what *ba'alav imo* means: the owner's presence or employment must precede or be concurrent with the borrowing of the animal itself. If the borrowing of the animal preceded the engagement of the owner, it is as though the owner was not present — *ba'alav ein imo* — at the critical moment when the borrowing took effect, and the borrower remains liable.

### Simultaneity Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל שָׁאַל אֶת הַפָּרָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁאַל אֶת הַבְּעָלִים, אוֹ שְׂכָרָן, וָמֵתָה – חַיָּיב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּעָלָיו אֵין עִמּוֹ שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם״. גְּמָ׳ מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁאַל אֶת הַפָּרָה, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא דְּקָתָנֵי ״עִמָּהּ״ – עִמָּהּ מַמָּשׁ. עִמָּהּ מַמָּשׁ, מִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ פָּרָה בִּמְשִׁיכָה, וּבְעָלִים בַּאֲמִירָה?
>
> **Translation:** **But** if one first **borrowed the cow and** only **afterward borrowed** the services of **the owner or hired him, and** the cow **died,** he is **liable** to pay the owner for the cow. This is the *halakha* even if the owner was working for the borrower at the time, **as it is stated: "If its owner is not with him, he shall pay"** (Exodus 22:13). GEMARA: **From** the fact **that the latter clause teaches: And afterward borrowed the cow, it may be inferred that** when **the first clause teaches:** Borrowed its owner **with it,** the intention is: **Literally with it,** i.e., at the same moment. The Gemara asks: **Can you find** such a case where the owner obligates himself to serve the borrower **literally with it?** Given that one borrows the **cow through pulling** the cow **and** contracts the services of the **owner through** their **verbal** agreement, it emerges that if they agree to both matters at the same time, one borrows the services of the owner before he borrows the cow, which is the case mentioned in the latter clause of the mishna. What, then, is the case mentioned in the first clause?

The *gemara* raises a sharp question. The *mishnah*'s first case is understood to describe simultaneous engagement — both the cow and its owner are taken on at once. But *ima mamash mi meshkachat lah para be-meshicha u-baalim be-amira?* — how is that actually possible? Acquiring the *parah* requires a *meshicha*, a formal act of *kinyan* whereby one physically takes hold of the animal. Engaging the owner's labor, however, is accomplished through *amira*, a verbal commitment. These are qualitatively different acts. How can a *meshicha* and a verbal commitment occur simultaneously?

### Simultaneity Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כְּגוֹן דְּקַיְימָא פָּרָה בַּחֲצֵרוֹ דְּשׁוֹאֵל, דְּלָא מְחַסְּרָא מְשִׁיכָה. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״אַתְּ גּוּפָךְ לֹא תִּשָּׁאֵל עַד שְׁעַת מְשִׁיכַת פָּרָתְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that the mishna is referring to a case **such as where** the **cow is standing in the borrower's courtyard,** which can effect acquisition of the rights to use the cow for him, **so** that **it does not lack,** i.e., there is no need for, **pulling.** Accordingly, both the borrowing of the owner's services and the borrowing of his cow will take effect simultaneously upon their agreement. Alternatively, **if you wish, say** that the case is **where** the borrower **says to him: You, yourself, will not be lent** to me **until** the **moment of** the **pulling of your cow.**

The *gemara* offers two solutions. The first: *ibayit eima kagon de-kaima para be-chatzeiro de-sho'el de-la michtara meshicha* — one may say that the animal was already standing in the *chatzer*, the property, of the borrower. In that case no separate *meshicha* is necessary, since a person's *chatzer* can effect *kinyan* on their behalf. The entire verbal transaction then occurs as one act, and the borrower simultaneously acquires the animal and engages the owner. The second solution: *de-amar lei at gufach lo tishal ad she-at meshichat paratecha* — one can construct the verbal engagement with a conditional clause, saying to the owner: your personal commitment to me does not take effect until the moment I perform *meshicha* on your animal. Even though the verbal statement was made first in time, it takes legal effect only at the precise instant the *meshicha* occurs, creating genuine simultaneity.

## Sources Shomrim

### Three Parshiyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם, אַרְבָּעָה שׁוֹמְרִים הֵם: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר. שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם נִשְׁבָּע עַל הַכֹּל, וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַכֹּל, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר – נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל הַשְּׁבוּרָה וְעַל הַשְּׁבוּיָה וְעַל הַמֵּתָה, וּמְשַׁלְּמִים אֶת הָאֲבֵידָה וְאֶת הַגְּנֵיבָה. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: פָּרָשָׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה נֶאֶמְרָה בְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, שְׁנִיָּה – בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, שְׁלִישִׁית – בְּשׁוֹאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyses the *halakhot* of the four types of bailees that were delineated in the previous chapter: **We learned** in a mishna **there** (93a): **There are four** types of **bailees: An unpaid bailee, and the borrower, a paid bailee, and the renter.** The mishna there continues: If the item was stolen, lost, or broken, or if the animal died in any manner, the *halakhot* with regard to them are as follows: **An unpaid bailee takes an oath over every** outcome; whether the item was lost, stolen, or broken, or if the animal died, the unpaid bailee must take an oath that it happened as he described, and he is then exempt from payment. **And the borrower** does not take an oath, but **pays** for **every** outcome, whether it was stolen or lost, even in a circumstance beyond his control. The *halakhot* of **a paid bailee and a renter** are the same: They **take an oath over an injured** animal, **over a captured** animal, **and over a dead** animal, attesting that the mishaps were caused by circumstances beyond their control, and they are exempt; **but they must pay** for **loss or theft.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verses in the Torah about bailees can be divided into three passages. The **first passage** (Exodus 22:6–8) **is stated about an unpaid bailee;** the **second** (Exodus 22:9–12) is **about a paid bailee;** and the **third** (Exodus 22:13–14) is **about a borrower.**

The *gemara* now turns to a more fundamental inquiry: where in the Torah do we find the source for the obligations of the four *shomrim*? A *baraita* is cited: *parsha rishona ne'emra b'shomer chinam, shniya b'shomer sachar, shlishit b'sho'el* — the first Torah passage deals with the *shomer chinam*, the second with the *shomer sachar*, the third with the *sho'el*. The third passage — *pesukim* 13 through 14 of the relevant section — is explicit: *v'chi yish'al ish me'im re'ehu* — if a man borrows from his fellow. That passage is unmistakably about the *sho'el*. The first passage, *pesukim* 6 through 8, describes one person entrusting an object to another for safekeeping, without specifying payment. The second passage, *pesukim* 9 through 12, discusses an animal entrusted for watching, introduces the oath for acts of God, and the obligation to pay if the animal is stolen.

### Attribution Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁלִישִׁית בְּשׁוֹאֵל – מְפוֹרָשׁ: ״וְכִי יִשְׁאַל אִישׁ מֵעִם רֵעֵהוּ וְנִשְׁבַּר אוֹ מֵת בְּעָלָיו אֵין עִמּוֹ שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם״. אֶלָּא רִאשׁוֹנָה בְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, שְׁנִיָּה בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – אֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** it is clear that the **third** passage is **about a borrower,** as the verse **explicitly** states: **"And when a man borrows from another, and it is injured or dies, if its owner is not with him, he shall pay"** (Exodus 22:13). **But** with regard to the claim of the *baraita* that the **first** passage is **about an unpaid bailee** and the **second is about a paid bailee, I could** also say the **reverse,** as the verses do not state which type of bailee they are referring to.

The *gemara* raises the difficulty: *bishlama shlishit b'sho'el meforash* — the third passage is explicitly about a borrower. But as for the first two passages — *ela rishona b'shomer chinam shniya b'shomer sachar ipuch ana?* — perhaps the assignment should be reversed? Perhaps the first *parsha* describes the *shomer sachar* and the second the *shomer chinam*? Neither passage names its subject explicitly. What forces us to read them in the order the *baraita* assigns?

### Kefel Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא שְׁנִיָּה בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיב בִּגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה. אַדְּרַבָּה: רִאשׁוֹנָה בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, שֶׁכֵּן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara justifies the claim of the *baraita*: Clearly a paid bailee carries a more severe level of liability than an unpaid bailee. Accordingly, **it stands to reason** that the **second** passage is **about a paid bailee, as** it states a stringency, that the bailee is **liable** even **in** cases of **theft or loss** (see Exodus 22:11). The Gemara challenges this claim: **On the contrary,** it stands to reason that the **first** passage is **about a paid bailee, as** it states a stringency, that the bailee **pays** the **double payment in** a case where he takes a false oath **stating the claim** that **a thief** stole the item he was safeguarding (see Exodus 22:6).

The *gemara* begins to work through the analysis. *Mistavra shniya b'shomer sachar sheken chayav b'gniva v'aveidah* — it makes sense that the second passage belongs to the *shomer sachar* because he is liable for theft and loss, which is the higher level of responsibility mentioned in that passage. But the *gemara* immediately counters: *adderaba, rishona b'shomer sachar* — on the contrary, perhaps the first passage should be assigned to the *shomer sachar*. Why? *Sheken meshalem tashlumei kefel b'toen ta'anat ganav* — because it is specifically in the context of the first passage that we derive the rule of *kefel*, the double payment penalty. The case is this: a *shomer chinam* who swears that the animal was stolen — and thereby exempts himself from payment — and is subsequently found to have stolen it himself, is treated as a *ganav* and must pay double. This rule of *kefel* for *to'en ta'anat ganav* — making a claim of theft to evade liability — applies in the first passage. The *shomer sachar*, who is already obligated to pay if the animal is stolen, cannot swear to exempt himself in that case, and therefore the mechanism that triggers *kefel* does not apply to him. On this reading, the first passage — which contains the *kefel* provision — would be assigned to the *shomer sachar*, who engages in a different kind of legal exposure than the *shomer chinam*.

### Keren vs Kefel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, קַרְנָא בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה עֲדִיפָא מִכְּפֵילָא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Even so,** being liable to pay **the principal** whenever one claims the deposit was stolen, **without** the ability to exempt oneself by taking **an oath, is a greater** stringency **than** being liable for **the double payment,** as that liability is only **when** one takes a false **oath** to that effect. Otherwise, he is exempt.

The *gemara* then presents the counter-argument. *Keren belo shvuah adifa mikfela bishvuah* — paying the *keren*, the principal, without any oath, is actually a greater liability than paying *kefel* under circumstances of false swearing. Proof: *teida deha shavek kol hana'ah shelo v'eino meshalem ela keren* — observe that the *sho'el*, who receives the greatest possible benefit from the object, pays only the *keren*, the principal value, when liable. The highest level of *shmira* liability in the system caps at paying the *keren*. That penalty is therefore weightier than the exceptional *kefel* case, which arises only from dishonest dealing outside the normal parameters of guardianship. Accordingly, the first passage, which contains the *kefel* provision, should be assigned to the *shomer chinam* after all.

## Sho'el Theft

### Paid Shomer Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל הַשְּׁבוּרָה וְעַל הַשְּׁבוּיָה וְעַל הַמֵּתָה, וּמְשַׁלְּמִים אֶת הָאֲבֵידָה וְאֶת הַגְּנֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *halakhot* of **a paid bailee and a renter** are the same: They **take an oath over an injured** animal, **over a captured** animal, **and over a dead** animal, attesting that the mishaps were caused by circumstances beyond their control, and they are exempt; **but they must pay** for **loss or theft.**

Returning to the *mishnah* from *Shevuot*: *nosei sachar vehasocher nishba'in al hashvura v'al hashvuyah v'al hameta umeshalmin et ha'aveida v'et hagniva* — the *shomer sachar* and the *socher* swear regarding acts of God — injury, kidnapping, or death of the animal — and pay for loss (*aveidah*) and theft (*gneivah*).

### Theft & Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא גְּנֵיבָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם גָּנוֹב יִגָּנֵב מֵעִמּוֹ יְשַׁלֵּם לִבְעָלָיו״, אֶלָּא אֲבֵידָה מְנָא לַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִם גָּנֵב יִגָּנֵב״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא גְּנֵיבָה, אֲבֵידָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִם גָּנוֹב יִגָּנֵב״, מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** a paid bailee is liable in the case of **theft, as it is written: "If it is stolen from him, he must pay its owner"** (Exodus 22:11). **But** in a case of **loss, from where do we** derive that a paid bailee is liable? The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: From the verse: **"If it is stolen [*ganov yigganev*]," I have** derived **only** that a paid bailee must pay for theft. How do I know he must also pay for *aveidah*, loss? *Talmud lomar im ganov yigganev*, repeating the verb for emphasis. This teaches that he is liable **in any case,** i.e., for loss as well as theft.

*Minah hani milei?* — where is this derived? The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *im ganov yiganeiv ein li ela gneivah aveidah minayin* — from the *pasuk* "if it is stolen" (*im ganov yiganeiv*), I know only that the *shomer sachar* must pay for theft. How do I know he must also pay for *aveidah*, loss? *Talmud lomar im ganov yiganeiv mikol makom* — the doubled language "if it is stolen, it is stolen" (*ganov yiganeiv*) teaches us: in any case of loss of this kind, including *aveidah*.

### Torah Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָה לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: לָא אָמְרִינַן דִּבְּרָה תוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אָמְרִינַן דִּבְּרָה תוֹרָה כלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says: We do not say** that **the Torah spoke in the language of people,** and so any repeated verb must serve to teach additional *halakhot*. **But according to the one who says: We do say** that **the Torah spoke in the language of people,** and so the use of a repeated verb is only for stylistic reasons, **what can be said?** From where is it derived that the bailee is liable for losing the item?

The *gemara* notes that this derivation from the emphatic double language depends on the view that the Torah does not speak the way ordinary people speak — *lo amrinan dibra torah k'lashon b'nei adam*. On that view, the doubled form is intentionally significant and available for *derasha*. But according to those who hold *dibra torah k'lashon b'nei adam* — that the Torah does speak in the manner of ordinary speech — the emphatic form is merely rhetorical intensification and cannot be used as the basis for deriving *aveidah*.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: קַל וָחוֹמֶר; וּמָה גְּנֵיבָה שֶׁקְּרוֹבָה לְאוֹנֶס – מְשַׁלֵּם, אֲבֵידָה שֶׁקְּרוֹבָה לִפְשִׁיעָה – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן! וְאִידַּךְ, מִילְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וְחוֹמֶר טָרַח וְכָתַב לַהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: It is derived through **an *a fortiori*** inference: **If** in a case of **theft, which is near to** being a case of **circumstances beyond his control,** the *halakha* is that the bailee **pays** the owner the item's value, then in a case of **loss, which is near to** being a case of **negligence,** is it **not all the more so** logical that he should pay? The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** opinion, why is there a need for a verse to teach this *halakha* if it can be derived from an *a fortiori* inference? The Gemara answers: At times, there is **a matter that can be derived through an a *fortiori*** inference, and the **verse** nevertheless **takes the trouble and writes it** explicitly.

For those who take the latter position, *amrei d'ma'arava* — the Sages of the west, in Eretz Yisrael, say: the derivation is through a *kal vachomer*. *Uma gneivah shekrova l'ones m'shaleim aveidah shekrova lipshiya lo kol sheken?* — if the *shomer sachar* is required to pay for theft, which is relatively close to an act of God — something largely outside his control — then surely he must pay for *aveidah*, loss, which is closer to negligence and therefore more clearly within the scope of his responsibility. The *kal vachomer* is compelling. The *gemara* then asks: if the *kal vachomer* suffices, why does the Torah bother with the doubled language? The answer: *milta d'ati b'kal v'chomer tarach v'chtav lah kra* — sometimes even when a matter is derivable through *kal vachomer* the Torah writes it out explicitly anyway.

## Sho'el Kidnapping

### Shvuyah Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַכֹּל. בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁבוּרָה וּמֵתָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִי יִשְׁאַל אִישׁ מֵעִם רֵעֵהוּ וְנִשְׁבַּר אוֹ מֵת״. אֶלָּא שְׁבוּיָה בְּשׁוֹאֵל מְנָא לַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna cited above states: **And the borrower** does not take an oath, but **pays** for **every** outcome, whether it was stolen or lost, even in a circumstance beyond his control. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the bailee is liable if the animal is **injured or dies, as it is written: "And if a man borrows from another and it is injured or dies…**he shall pay" (Exodus 22:13). **But from where do we** derive the liability **of a borrower** when the animal is **captured?**

The *mishnah* states that the *sho'el* pays for everything — *vehashoel meshalem et hakol*. The *gemara* examines the sources for this. The verse explicitly covers *shvura* and *metah* — injury and death: *v'chi yish'al ish me'im re'ehu v'nishbar o meis* — and the animal is broken or dies. But what about *shvuyah* — kidnapping? The verse makes no mention of it. How do we know the *sho'el* is liable if the animal is taken captive?

### Comparison Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא נֵילַף מִשְּׁבוּרָה וּמֵתָה, מָה לִשְׁבוּרָה וּמֵתָה – שֶׁכֵּן אוּנְסָא דְּסָלֵיק אַדַּעְתָּא הוּא. תֹּאמַר בִּשְׁבוּיָה – שֶׁכֵּן אוּנְסָא דְּלָא סָלֵיק אַדַּעְתָּא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Let us derive** it **from** the fact the borrower is liable if the animal was **injured or dies** because they are all similarly cases of circumstances beyond his control, then one could counter as follows: **What** is notable **about** a case where the animal is **injured or died?** It is notable **in that it is** the type of **unavoidable accident whose** possibility of occurring **entered one's mind,** so it is reasonable that the borrower accepted responsibility for such mishaps. **Can you say** the same **about** being **captured, which is an unavoidable accident whose** possibility of occurring **does not enter one's mind?**

One might propose to derive the *sho'el*'s liability for *shvuyah* by analogy to the *shomer sachar*, who also lists injury, death, and kidnapping in his passage. The *gemara* rejects this: *ika l'mifrach ma lishomer s'char sheken liftor tomar b'sho'el sheken l'chiyuv* — the two cases are not analogous, because in the *shomer sachar*'s passage, *shvura*, *shvuyah*, and *metah* are listed as cases in which he is *exempt*, whereas for the *sho'el* we are trying to establish cases in which he is *obligated*. One cannot draw an analogy between cases of opposite legal effect.

### Rabbi Natan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: נֶאֶמְרָה שְׁבוּרָה וּמֵתָה בְּשׁוֹאֵל, וְנֶאֶמְרָה שְׁבוּרָה וּמֵתָה בְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר. מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁבוּיָה עִמּוֹ, אַף כָּאן שְׁבוּיָה עִמּוֹ. אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר שֶׁכֵּן לִפְטוּר, תֹּאמַר בַּשּׁוֹאֵל שֶׁכֵּן לְחִיּוּב! אֶלָּא כְּרַבִּי נָתָן, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: ״אוֹ״ לְרַבּוֹת שְׁבוּיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the liability of a borrower in a case where the animal is captured is derived as follows: The cases where the animal is **injured or dies are stated in** the passage **about a borrower, and** similarly the cases where it is **injured or dies are stated in** the passage **about a paid bailee. Just as below,** i.e., in the passage in the Torah concerning a paid bailee, the case in which it is **captured is** included **with it,** as the verse there states: "If a man deliver to his neighbor a donkey, or an ox, or a sheep, or any beast, to safeguard, and it die, or be hurt, or is captured" (Exodus 22:9); **so too here,** with regard to a borrower, the case where it is **captured is** included **with it.** The Gemara explains that this derivation **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is notable **about a paid bailee?** He is notable **in that** the cases in which the animal is injured or dies are mentioned in order **to exempt** him from liability. **Can you say** this is also true **with regard to a borrower, where** the cases are mentioned in order **to** teach that he **is liable?** **Rather,** the liability of a borrower in a case where the animal is captured is derived **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Natan, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan says:** The word **"or"** in the phrase "injured or dies" (Exodus 22:13) serves **to include** the case in which the animal was **captured.**

The *gemara* therefore turns to Rabbi Natan: *ela k'Rabbi Natan. DeTanya Rabbi Natan omer: o l'rabos shvuyah* — the word *o*, "or," in the verse *v'nishbar o meis*, "broken or died," is superfluous in its plain sense and is therefore there to include *shvuyah*.

### Or Necessity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״אוֹ״ מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְחַלֵּק. דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: עֵד דְּמִיתַּבְרָא וּמֵתָה לָא מְחַיֵּיב, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **this** word **"or" is required** in order **to divide** the cases, **as** otherwise **it could enter your mind to say** that the bailee **is not liable unless** the animal is first **injured and** then also **dies.** Therefore, the term "or" is necessary to **teach us** that these are two separate cases.

The *gemara* challenges this: *hai o mibay'lei l'chalek* — that word *o* is necessary for another reason. Without it, one might think that the *sho'el* is liable only if both injury *and* death occur together. *D'salka da'atcha amina, ad d'mitbra umeisa lo mechayaiv* — one might have thought that unless the animal is both injured and then dies, there is no liability. The word *o* is therefore needed to tell us that injury alone, or death alone, each independently triggers the obligation. If *o* is already needed for that purpose, it is no longer available to include *shvuyah*.

### Logical Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יְקַלֵּל אֶת אָבִיו וְאֶת אִמּוֹ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ. אָבִיו בְּלֹא אִמּוֹ, אִמּוֹ בְּלֹא אָבִיו, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ קִלֵּל״. אָבִיו קִלֵּל, אִמּוֹ קִלֵּל. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מַשְׁמַע שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּאֶחָד, וּמַשְׁמַע אֶחָד בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – עַד שֶׁיִּפְרוֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב ״יַחְדָּו״. אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה, לְחַלֵּק הָכָא לָא צָרִיךְ. מַאי טַעְמָא – סְבָרָא הוּא: מָה לִי קַטְלַהּ כּוּלָּהּ, מָה לִי קַטְלַהּ פַּלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan,** that it is obvious that the borrower is liable even if the animal died without being injured first. Consequently, "or" is unnecessary, and so it can be used for Rabbi Natan's derivation. **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya, what is there to say?** According to his opinion, it is necessary for the Torah to state "or" in this case, to teach that the borrower is liable even if the animal dies without being injured first, and so it cannot be used for a derivation. The Gemara explains: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: From the verse: **"A man who curses his father and his mother** shall die" (Leviticus 20:9), **I have** derived **only** that one is liable if he curses both **his father and his mother. From where** do I derive that if one curses **his father but not his mother,** or **his mother but not his father,** that he is liable? The continuation of **the verse states: "His father and his mother he has cursed,** his blood is upon him." In the first part of the verse, the word "curses" is in proximity to "his father," and in the last part of the verse, it is in proximity to "his mother." This teaches that the verse is referring to both a case where **he cursed** only **his father** and a case where **he cursed** only **his mother;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.** **Rabbi Yonatan says:** There is no need for this derivation, because the phrase "his father and his mother" **indicates** that one is liable if he curses **both of them together, and it** also **indicates** that he is liable if he curses either **one** of them **on their own,** **unless the verse specifies** that one is liable only where he curses both **together,** which it does not do in this case. The Gemara answers: **You** can **even say** that Rabbi Natan's derivation from the word "or" is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya,** as **here,** in the verse concerning a borrower's liability, there is **no need** for the word "or" **to divide** the cases. **What is the reason?** The fact that injury and death are alone sufficient to engender liability **is** based on **logical reasoning,** as follows: **What** difference does it make **to me** if **it is entirely killed,** and **what** difference does it make **to me** if **it is partially killed,** i.e., injured? Either way, the animal is not in the state in which it was borrowed, so the borrower is liable to compensate the owner.

The *gemara* then asks: this works according to Rabbi Yonatan — but what does Rabbi Yoshiya say? The *baraita* cited discusses the verse *ish asher yekaleil et aviv v'et imo* — a person who curses his father and his mother. Rabbi Yoshiya holds that the conjunctive language could be read to require both parents to be cursed simultaneously; it is only the closing clause *aviv v'imo kileil* that teaches us that cursing either one individually is sufficient. The implication of Rabbi Yoshiya's hermeneutic is that the word *o* in a verse is necessary to establish disjunction — that the cases are separate, not conjunctive — and therefore the *o* in *v'nishbar o meis* is needed for exactly that purpose and cannot be used to include *shvuyah*. Rabbi Yonatan, by contrast, holds that disjunction is always the default reading unless the Torah specifies *yachdav*, "together," explicitly; therefore for him the word *o* is available for *derasha*.

The *gemara* resolves even Rabbi Yoshiya's difficulty: *afilu teima Rabbi Yoshiya, l'chalek hacha lo tzarich* — even according to Rabbi Yoshiya, in this particular case the word *o* is not needed to establish disjunction, because that conclusion is a matter of pure logic. *Mah liktla kula, mah liktla palga* — what difference does it make whether the animal is killed entirely or only partially? Once the Torah establishes liability for injury, it is self-evident that death is also covered. Therefore the word *o* is not needed to separate the cases — and it is consequently available to teach the inclusion of *shvuyah*, even according to Rabbi Yoshiya.
