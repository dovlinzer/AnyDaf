# Bava Metzia 94 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## T'nai Fundamentals

### T'nai Definition

The *mishnah* on 94a opens with the subject of *t'nai* — conditional statements — and the question of when a given condition is considered halachically valid or invalid. The basic framework is as follows: if a *t'nai* is valid, the action to which one has committed oneself conditionally will take effect only if the condition is fulfilled — the Mishnah uses the language *mekuyam*, fulfilled. If the *t'nai* is somehow invalid, then it is *batel*, nullified, and the action takes effect unconditionally regardless of whether the condition was ever met.

### Shomrim Conditions

The *mishnah* begins with the four *shomrim*, the four categories of guardians, which have been introduced in previous *mishnayot*. The language is: *matneh shomer chinam lihyot patur mishvuah vehashoel lihyot patur milishalem noseh sachar vehasocher lihyot pturim mishvuah umilishalem*. A *shomer chinam* — a free guardian — is obligated only for negligence (*pshia*), and is exempt from payment for any other occurrence — theft, loss, or an act of God — provided he takes an oath. So if this *shomer* conditions his acceptance of the role on being exempt from taking such an oath, the *mishnah* says that condition is valid. At the opposite end of the spectrum, the *sho'el* — the borrower — bears the highest level of responsibility, being liable even for acts of God, and he too may condition his borrowing on being released from these obligations. Between these two extremes, the *noseh sachar* and the *socher* — the paid guardian and the renter — may similarly condition their undertaking on exemption from both the oath and from payment in the cases of *gneivah* and *aveidah*, theft and loss.

### Against Torah

The *mishnah* then states its second rule: *kol hamatneh al mah shekatuv baTorah t'na'o batel* — anyone who makes a condition against something the Torah has commanded, that condition is void, and the person has committed themselves to the act unconditionally. The *gemara* will raise a sharp question about this in light of the first rule, since the obligations of the *shomrim* are themselves rooted in the Torah. The example of such an impermissible condition will be illustrated through the case of *kiddushin*.

### Ma'aseh B'Tchilatov

The third rule of the *mishnah* is: *vechol t'nai sheyesh ma'aseh bitchilato t'na'o batel* — any condition in which the *ma'aseh*, the action, is stated before the conditional clause, is also invalid. Rashi explains: *shehikdim ma'aseh she'alav la'asos l'tnai shehu sho'el mimenu* — the person has stated first the action he is committing to do, and only then articulated the condition he is requesting. For example, saying "This thing is yours, if you do such-and-such" is invalid because the *then* precedes the *if*.

The reason for this rule is that a valid *t'nai* must resemble the model of *bnei Gad u'bnei Reuven*. At the end of *Parshat Matot*, the tribes of Gad and Reuven came before Moshe and asked to remain in the Trans-Jordan. Moshe ultimately agreed, and the structure of his conditional grant was: *im ya'avru u'nesatem* — "if you cross before your brothers and help them conquer the land, then you will be given this land." The conditional statement — the *if* — comes first; the consequent action — the *then* — follows. That is the required form. Rashi cites Yaakov's words to Lavan similarly: *im ta'aseh li hadavar hazeh ashuvah ereh tzonecha* — if you do this thing for me, then I will go and shepherd your flocks. The condition precedes the commitment.

What does it mean for the condition to be *batel*? Rashi explains: *v'havei ma'aseh mikuyam* — the action takes effect regardless. Even though the person has nominally conditioned his act, if the condition was stated improperly, it is as though no condition was made, and the *then* clause is binding whether or not the *if* is ever fulfilled.

### Efshar L'Kaymo

The *mishnah*'s fourth rule: *vechol she'efshar lo l'kaymo b'sofo v'hitnah imo mit'chilato t'na'o kayam* — any condition that is possible to fulfill at the end, and which was stipulated at the outset, is a valid condition. Rashi explains: provided the *t'nai* was stated before the *ma'aseh* — that is, the proper order was observed — then if the condition is possible to fulfill, it is a good *t'nai*. However, if the condition is impossible to fulfill — *ee efshar l'kaymo* — then the *t'nai* is *batel* and the action stands regardless. The *gemara* will supply examples and will clarify the law.

## Shomer & Torah

### Initial Problem

The *gemara* opens with an immediate difficulty. The *mishnah*'s first rule permits a *shomer* to condition his guardianship on being released from obligations the Torah itself imposes on him. But the *mishnah*'s second rule declares that any condition made against what is written in the Torah is void. These two rules appear contradictory. The *gemara* frames the question sharply: *amai?* Why is that a valid *t'nai*? *Matneh al mah shekatuv baTorah hu* — it is precisely a condition against what the Torah has written. And the *mishnah* itself says *kol hamatneh al mah shekatuv baTorah t'na'o batel*.

### Rabbi Yehuda

The *gemara* initially suggests: *ha mani? Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this *mishnah* follows the view of Rabbi Yehuda. *De'amar bedavar shebemamon t'na'o kayam* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that while one cannot generally condition against a Torah obligation, in monetary matters such a condition is valid. The *shomer*'s exemption from payment is precisely a monetary matter — he is attempting to be released from a financial obligation — and therefore according to Rabbi Yehuda the condition stands.

### Marriage Case

The *gemara* adduces a *baraita* in support: *amar le'ishah harei at mekudeshet li al menat she'ein li alayich she'er kesut ve'onah harei zu mekudeshet vetna'o batel divrei Rabbi Meir*. A man says to a woman: you are betrothed to me on the condition that you have no claim against me for *she'er*, *kesut*, and *onah* — food, clothing, and conjugal rights. Rabbi Meir rules: she is *mekudeshet*, and the condition is *batel*, because this is a condition against what the Torah has commanded. Then: *Rabbi Yehuda omer bedavar shebemamon t'na'o kayam* — Rabbi Yehuda says that regarding monetary matters, namely *she'er* and *kesut* (food and clothing), the condition is valid.

However, the *gemara* pushes back: can we really attribute the *mishnah*'s first rule to Rabbi Yehuda when the very next clause — *kol hamatneh al mah shekatuv baTorah t'na'o batel* — is an unqualified statement that seems to reflect the position of Rabbi Meir? The *gemara* attempts to resolve this by saying that the latter clause refers only to non-monetary ritual matters, *davar she'eino shel mamon*, and the two clauses can coexist under Rabbi Yehuda. But this resolution is immediately challenged: the clause following that — *kol t'nai sheyesh bo ma'aseh bitchilato t'na'o batel*, the rule about the *ma'aseh* preceding the *t'nai* — is demonstrably the position of Rabbi Meir, as attested by the *baraita* in which Abba Chalafta says in the name of Rabbi Meir: *t'nai kodem l'ma'aseh harei zeh t'nai; ma'aseh kodem l'tnai eino t'nai*. If the latter clause is Rabbi Meir's, the *mishnah* cannot be split between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir in an unstated, inconsistent manner.

### Rabbi Meir View

The *gemara* therefore arrives at a new resolution: *ela kula Rabbi Meir hi* — the entire *mishnah* is the view of Rabbi Meir. And as for the first rule, permitting the *shomer* to condition his guardianship — *shani hacha de'me'ikara lo sha'abed nafshei* — the case of the *shomer* is exceptional because at the very outset he did not obligate himself.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi's explanation here is illuminating. Even though we might initially think the *shomer chinam* who conditions his exemption from the oath is making a condition against the Torah, that is not so. *Me'ikara lo shavei nafshei* — from the very beginning he did not bind himself. When Reuven asks Shimon to be his *shomer chinam* and Shimon says *ee efshi she'eheyeh shomer shelcha ella b'kach* — I am unwilling to be your guardian except on these terms — he has not yet become a *shomer* at all. *V'shomer lo nachit lishmira ad d'mashach l'vehema* — a person does not become a *shomer* until the act of *meshicha*, the legally valid act of acquisition, takes place, whereby he actually takes hold of the animal. *V'hai ki mashach kvar pirash al menat shelo alav shvua* — and when he did take hold of the animal, he had already preceded that with his condition. Thus he has committed himself only to a partial form of guardianship: *v'lo shavei nafshei l'ayeilei b'torat shomrim ella b'miksas* — he has only obligated himself in a limited sense. He is not accepting the full biblical category of *shomer chinam*; he is saying, in effect, I will watch your animal for free, but without any of the attendant biblical obligations.

The contrast with *kiddushin* is instructive. *Aval hamikadesh et ha'ishah, miki amar lah harei at mekudeshet li, ikadsha lah* — the moment a man pronounces the words of *kiddushin*, she is betrothed. When he then adds that he bears no financial obligations to her, that is *matneh al mah shekatuv baTorah* — a condition against the Torah — because *ein ishut l'chatza'in*, there is no such thing as being partially married. The act of *kiddushin* takes complete effect the moment it occurs. *V'havei matneh al mah shekasuv baTorah*. With the *shomer*, by contrast, he never initially took on the full biblical role; he stepped into a more limited, self-defined form of custodianship before the *kinyan* ever made him a Torah-defined guardian.

## Shomer Sachar

### Shomer->Sho'el

A *baraita* now raises a striking case: *umatneh shomer sachar lihyot k'sho'el* — a *shomer sachar* conditions his guardianship such that he accepts the full responsibilities of a *sho'el*, the borrower, who is liable even for acts of God. This might seem puzzling — why would anyone voluntarily accept greater liability? Yet this is precisely the case the *baraita* contemplates.

### Kinyan Required?

The *gemara* records a dispute over whether such an elevation of responsibility requires a formal *kinyan*. *Amar Shmuel beshikanu miyado* — Shmuel says yes, a *kinyan* must be performed. If the owner agrees — and why would he not? — and they perform a *kinyan*, then the *shomer sachar* takes on the full obligations of a *sho'el*.

### Reputation Benefit

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *afilu teima beshelo kanu miyado* — even without a *kinyan*. His reasoning is: *behahi hana'ah d'kanfik leh kala d'inish meheiman hu gamir umeshabed nafshei* — through the benefit (*hana'ah*) that accrues to him from the reputation he gains as a trustworthy person, he fully binds himself to this higher level of responsibility. The relationship between *hana'ah* and responsibility is a direct one: the *sho'el* bears maximum liability because he receives maximum benefit — the free use of an object. But Rabbi Yochanan identifies a different kind of *hana'ah* available to a *shomer sachar* who accepts *sho'el*-level responsibility: his reputation for reliability. The word will spread that he is a person of his word and of exceptional trustworthiness, and that reputational benefit is sufficient, without a formal *kinyan*, to create the binding obligation.

## Impossible Conditions

### Possible vs Impossible

The *mishnah*'s rule — that a *t'nai* which is possible to fulfill at the end is valid, while one that is impossible is *batel* — is now taken up by the *gemara* in greater detail. *Amar Rav Tavla amar Rav zo divrei Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema* — this *mishnah* reflects the position of Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema.

### Merely Torment

*Vachachamim omrim af al pi she'ee efshar l'kaymo besofo vehitnah alav mitchilato t'na'o kayam* — the *Chachamim*, however, hold that even if the condition is impossible to fulfill, the *t'nai* is nonetheless valid. Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema's counter-position is that an impossible condition is nothing but *mifliga vekasher* — mere torment — an attempt to make life miserable for the other party. There is no genuine conditional intent; the person making the condition knows it cannot be met.

### Divorce Examples

The *baraita* supplies vivid examples in the context of divorce. *Harei zeh gittech al menat sheta'ali larakia* — this is your bill of divorce, on condition that you ascend to the heavens. *Al menat sheterdi latehom* — on condition that you descend to the depths of the sea. *Al menat shetivli kaneh shel meah amah* — on condition that you swallow a rod one hundred *amot* in length. *Al menat sheta'avri et hayam hagadol beraglayich* — on condition that you cross the Mediterranean Sea on foot. The *baraita* says: if the condition is fulfilled, it is a valid *get*; if not, it is not a *get*. Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema says: in all such cases it is a valid *get*, and frames the matter as a general principle: *kol she'i efshar l'kaymo besofo v'hitnah alav mitechilato eino ela kemifliga* — any such impossible condition is merely the person tormenting the other party, and the *get* is valid regardless.

### Halacha Decision

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Rav: halacha keRabbi Yehuda ben Tema* — Rav Nachman rules that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema. An impossible condition does not void the accompanying act. This ruling is then supported by a careful reading of the *mishnah* itself: the *mishnah* says that a condition which is possible to fulfill is valid — the implication, *ha ee efshar*, being that if it is impossible, the condition is *batel* but the act stands. This inference from the *mishnah* corroborates the ruling of Rabbi Yehuda ben Tema.

## Ba'alav Imo

### Cow Owner Order

The *mishnah* at this point addresses the principle of *ba'alav imo* — the owner being present with the borrower. The *mishnah* distinguishes between two scenarios. First: *hashoel parah vesha'alah ba'aleha imah* — one borrows a cow and simultaneously borrows or hires the labor of its owner. Second: *sha'al haba'alim o secharan ve'achar kach sha'al et haparah* — one first borrows or hires the owner and only then borrows the cow. In both of these cases, if the cow subsequently dies, the borrower is exempt: *patur*, because the owner was present when the borrowing took effect. By contrast, if one borrowed the cow first and only afterwards engaged the owner, and then the cow dies — *chayav*, he must pay.

### Torah Source

The *mishnah*'s ruling is grounded in the Torah's language. The verse states: *v'chi yish'al ish me'im re'ehu v'nishbar o met ba'alav ein imo shalem yeshalem* — if a person borrows from his fellow and the animal is injured or dies, if the owner was not with him he shall pay in full. *Im ba'alav imo lo yeshalem* — if the owner was with him, he need not pay. The *mishnah* is defining what *ba'alav imo* means: the owner's presence or employment must precede or be concurrent with the borrowing of the animal itself. If the borrowing of the animal preceded the engagement of the owner, it is as though the owner was not present — *ba'alav ein imo* — at the critical moment when the borrowing took effect, and the borrower remains liable.

### Simultaneity Problem

The *gemara* raises a sharp question. The *mishnah*'s first case is understood to describe simultaneous engagement — both the cow and its owner are taken on at once. But *ima mamash mi meshkachat lah para be-meshicha u-baalim be-amira?* — how is that actually possible? Acquiring the *parah* requires a *meshicha*, a formal act of *kinyan* whereby one physically takes hold of the animal. Engaging the owner's labor, however, is accomplished through *amira*, a verbal commitment. These are qualitatively different acts. How can a *meshicha* and a verbal commitment occur simultaneously?

### Simultaneity Solutions

The *gemara* offers two solutions. The first: *ibayit eima kagon de-kaima para be-chatzeiro de-sho'el de-la michtara meshicha* — one may say that the animal was already standing in the *chatzer*, the property, of the borrower. In that case no separate *meshicha* is necessary, since a person's *chatzer* can effect *kinyan* on their behalf. The entire verbal transaction then occurs as one act, and the borrower simultaneously acquires the animal and engages the owner. The second solution: *de-amar lei at gufach lo tishal ad she-at meshichat paratecha* — one can construct the verbal engagement with a conditional clause, saying to the owner: your personal commitment to me does not take effect until the moment I perform *meshicha* on your animal. Even though the verbal statement was made first in time, it takes legal effect only at the precise instant the *meshicha* occurs, creating genuine simultaneity.

## Sources Shomrim

### Three Parshiyot

The *gemara* now turns to a more fundamental inquiry: where in the Torah do we find the source for the obligations of the four *shomrim*? A *baraita* is cited: *parsha rishona ne'emra b'shomer chinam, shniya b'shomer sachar, shlishit b'sho'el* — the first Torah passage deals with the *shomer chinam*, the second with the *shomer sachar*, the third with the *sho'el*. The third passage — *pesukim* 13 through 14 of the relevant section — is explicit: *v'chi yish'al ish me'im re'ehu* — if a man borrows from his fellow. That passage is unmistakably about the *sho'el*. The first passage, *pesukim* 6 through 8, describes one person entrusting an object to another for safekeeping, without specifying payment. The second passage, *pesukim* 9 through 12, discusses an animal entrusted for watching, introduces the oath for acts of God, and the obligation to pay if the animal is stolen.

### Attribution Problem

The *gemara* raises the difficulty: *bishlama shlishit b'sho'el meforash* — the third passage is explicitly about a borrower. But as for the first two passages — *ela rishona b'shomer chinam shniya b'shomer sachar ipuch ana?* — perhaps the assignment should be reversed? Perhaps the first *parsha* describes the *shomer sachar* and the second the *shomer chinam*? Neither passage names its subject explicitly. What forces us to read them in the order the *baraita* assigns?

### Kefel Argument

The *gemara* begins to work through the analysis. *Mistavra shniya b'shomer sachar sheken chayav b'gniva v'aveidah* — it makes sense that the second passage belongs to the *shomer sachar* because he is liable for theft and loss, which is the higher level of responsibility mentioned in that passage. But the *gemara* immediately counters: *adderaba, rishona b'shomer sachar* — on the contrary, perhaps the first passage should be assigned to the *shomer sachar*. Why? *Sheken meshalem tashlumei kefel b'toen ta'anat ganav* — because it is specifically in the context of the first passage that we derive the rule of *kefel*, the double payment penalty. The case is this: a *shomer chinam* who swears that the animal was stolen — and thereby exempts himself from payment — and is subsequently found to have stolen it himself, is treated as a *ganav* and must pay double. This rule of *kefel* for *to'en ta'anat ganav* — making a claim of theft to evade liability — applies in the first passage. The *shomer sachar*, who is already obligated to pay if the animal is stolen, cannot swear to exempt himself in that case, and therefore the mechanism that triggers *kefel* does not apply to him. On this reading, the first passage — which contains the *kefel* provision — would be assigned to the *shomer sachar*, who engages in a different kind of legal exposure than the *shomer chinam*.

### Keren vs Kefel

The *gemara* then presents the counter-argument. *Keren belo shvuah adifa mikfela bishvuah* — paying the *keren*, the principal, without any oath, is actually a greater liability than paying *kefel* under circumstances of false swearing. Proof: *teida deha shavek kol hana'ah shelo v'eino meshalem ela keren* — observe that the *sho'el*, who receives the greatest possible benefit from the object, pays only the *keren*, the principal value, when liable. The highest level of *shmira* liability in the system caps at paying the *keren*. That penalty is therefore weightier than the exceptional *kefel* case, which arises only from dishonest dealing outside the normal parameters of guardianship. Accordingly, the first passage, which contains the *kefel* provision, should be assigned to the *shomer chinam* after all.

## Sho'el Theft

### Paid Shomer Rules

Returning to the *mishnah* from *Shevuot*: *nosei sachar vehasocher nishba'in al hashvura v'al hashvuyah v'al hameta umeshalmin et ha'aveida v'et hagniva* — the *shomer sachar* and the *socher* swear regarding acts of God — injury, kidnapping, or death of the animal — and pay for loss (*aveidah*) and theft (*gneivah*).

### Theft & Loss

*Minah hani milei?* — where is this derived? The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *im ganov yiganeiv ein li ela gneivah aveidah minayin* — from the *pasuk* "if it is stolen" (*im ganov yiganeiv*), I know only that the *shomer sachar* must pay for theft. How do I know he must also pay for *aveidah*, loss? *Talmud lomar im ganov yiganeiv mikol makom* — the doubled language "if it is stolen, it is stolen" (*ganov yiganeiv*) teaches us: in any case of loss of this kind, including *aveidah*.

### Torah Language

The *gemara* notes that this derivation from the emphatic double language depends on the view that the Torah does not speak the way ordinary people speak — *lo amrinan dibra torah k'lashon b'nei adam*. On that view, the doubled form is intentionally significant and available for *derasha*. But according to those who hold *dibra torah k'lashon b'nei adam* — that the Torah does speak in the manner of ordinary speech — the emphatic form is merely rhetorical intensification and cannot be used as the basis for deriving *aveidah*.

### Kal Vachomer

For those who take the latter position, *amrei d'ma'arava* — the Sages of the west, in Eretz Yisrael, say: the derivation is through a *kal vachomer*. *Uma gneivah shekrova l'ones m'shaleim aveidah shekrova lipshiya lo kol sheken?* — if the *shomer sachar* is required to pay for theft, which is relatively close to an act of God — something largely outside his control — then surely he must pay for *aveidah*, loss, which is closer to negligence and therefore more clearly within the scope of his responsibility. The *kal vachomer* is compelling. The *gemara* then asks: if the *kal vachomer* suffices, why does the Torah bother with the doubled language? The answer: *milta d'ati b'kal v'chomer tarach v'chtav lah kra* — sometimes even when a matter is derivable through *kal vachomer* the Torah writes it out explicitly anyway.

## Sho'el Kidnapping

### Shvuyah Derivation

The *mishnah* states that the *sho'el* pays for everything — *vehashoel meshalem et hakol*. The *gemara* examines the sources for this. The verse explicitly covers *shvura* and *metah* — injury and death: *v'chi yish'al ish me'im re'ehu v'nishbar o meis* — and the animal is broken or dies. But what about *shvuyah* — kidnapping? The verse makes no mention of it. How do we know the *sho'el* is liable if the animal is taken captive?

### Comparison Issue

One might propose to derive the *sho'el*'s liability for *shvuyah* by analogy to the *shomer sachar*, who also lists injury, death, and kidnapping in his passage. The *gemara* rejects this: *ika l'mifrach ma lishomer s'char sheken liftor tomar b'sho'el sheken l'chiyuv* — the two cases are not analogous, because in the *shomer sachar*'s passage, *shvura*, *shvuyah*, and *metah* are listed as cases in which he is *exempt*, whereas for the *sho'el* we are trying to establish cases in which he is *obligated*. One cannot draw an analogy between cases of opposite legal effect.

### Rabbi Natan

The *gemara* therefore turns to Rabbi Natan: *ela k'Rabbi Natan. DeTanya Rabbi Natan omer: o l'rabos shvuyah* — the word *o*, "or," in the verse *v'nishbar o meis*, "broken or died," is superfluous in its plain sense and is therefore there to include *shvuyah*.

### Or Necessity

The *gemara* challenges this: *hai o mibay'lei l'chalek* — that word *o* is necessary for another reason. Without it, one might think that the *sho'el* is liable only if both injury *and* death occur together. *D'salka da'atach amina, ad d'mitbra umeisa lo mechayaiv* — one might have thought that unless the animal is both injured and then dies, there is no liability. The word *o* is therefore needed to tell us that injury alone, or death alone, each independently triggers the obligation. If *o* is already needed for that purpose, it is no longer available to include *shvuyah*.

### Logical Inference

The *gemara* then asks: this works according to Rabbi Yonatan — but what does Rabbi Yoshiya say? The *baraita* cited discusses the verse *ish asher yekaleil et aviv v'et imo* — a person who curses his father and his mother. Rabbi Yoshiya holds that the conjunctive language could be read to require both parents to be cursed simultaneously; it is only the closing clause *aviv v'imo kileil* that teaches us that cursing either one individually is sufficient. The implication of Rabbi Yoshiya's hermeneutic is that the word *o* in a verse is necessary to establish disjunction — that the cases are separate, not conjunctive — and therefore the *o* in *v'nishbar o meis* is needed for exactly that purpose and cannot be used to include *shvuyah*. Rabbi Yonatan, by contrast, holds that disjunction is always the default reading unless the Torah specifies *yachdav*, "together," explicitly; therefore for him the word *o* is available for *derasha*.

The *gemara* resolves even Rabbi Yoshiya's difficulty: *afilu teima Rabbi Yoshiya, l'chalek hacha lo tzarich* — even according to Rabbi Yoshiya, in this particular case the word *o* is not needed to establish disjunction, because that conclusion is a matter of pure logic. *Mah liktla kula, mah liktla palga* — what difference does it make whether the animal is killed entirely or only partially? Once the Torah establishes liability for injury, it is self-evident that death is also covered. Therefore the word *o* is not needed to separate the cases — and it is consequently available to teach the inclusion of *shvuyah*, even according to Rabbi Yoshiya.
