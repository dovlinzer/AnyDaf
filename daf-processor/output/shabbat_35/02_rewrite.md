# Shabbat 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bein Hashmashot Intro

### Opening Remarks

Today's *daf* is 35, and we are in the middle of a *sugya* in *Shas* on *bein hashmashot* — though there is another *Gemara* in Pesachim that leads some *Rishonim* to radically reassess this *Gemara*. The full range of positions will emerge as the discussion unfolds.

### Definition of BH

The *Gemara* has established that the period of *bein hashmashot* runs from the time the sun sets until the sky darkens. There is, however, a subsidiary question: perhaps even a few minutes after sunset it is still day, so long as the sky remains red. This distinction generates the difference between the two primary positions.

### Two-thirds/Three-quarter…

The disagreement between two-thirds of a *mil* and three-quarters of a *mil* turns on whether *bein hashmashot* begins immediately at sunset or only once the redness of the sky has dissipated. Both versions of Rabbi Yehuda's position, however, agree that *tzeis* occurs three-quarters of a *mil* after sunset — the only question is when *bein hashmashot* itself begins. Three-quarters of 18 minutes is approximately 14 minutes; three-quarters of 24 minutes is 18 minutes. So even on the latest reckoning, *tzeis* falls between 14 and 18 minutes after sunset — a figure that will become significant once the *Gemara* in Pesachim enters the picture.

### Basket Digression

Before returning to *bein hashmashot*, the *Gemara* digresses briefly. The passage notes: *v'hilufa b'khalsa* — the reversal of the two-and-three pattern just seen in the debate between Rabbah and Rav Yosef also appears in an entirely different *halachic* context involving a large type of basket called a *khalsa*.

## Vessel Muktzah Rules

### Rabbah vs Rav Yosef

The *Gemara* records the following dispute. Rabbah rules: a *khalsa* holding two *kor* may be carried on *Shabbat* — it retains the status of a vessel — but one holding three *kor* may not be carried, as it is no longer considered a vessel and is therefore *muktzah*. Rav Yosef disagrees: even a *khalsa* of three *kor* may be carried, and only one of four *kor* is forbidden. A *kor* is a very large dry volume measure; we are speaking of containers of truly enormous size — perhaps comparable in volume to a large appliance.

### Two/Three Reversal

The mnemonic connection to the *bein hashmashot* debate is the following: Rabbah, who cited three-quarters of a *mil* in the name of Shmuel, says *two* here; Rav Yosef, who cited two-thirds of a *mil*, says *three* here. The numbers two and three reverse themselves across the two contexts. There is no logical correspondence between the *halachot* — it is simply a useful memory device.

It should be clarified: both Rabbah and Rav Yosef were transmitting teachings attributed to Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, but they themselves disagree about the content of those teachings, and that disagreement now manifests in an entirely different domain.

### Abaye's Question

Abaye reports that he posed a practical question to Rabbah when an actual case arose: he had a very large basket and wished to move it. Rabbah had taught that *two kor* is permitted and *three kor* is forbidden, but in the real case Rabbah did not even permit a basket of *two kor*. This suggests Rabbah was applying a stricter standard in practice than his stated ruling implied.

### Mishna Connection

The *Gemara* asks: whose position does Rabbah's strict ruling follow? It answers: *k'man k'hai tanna* — like the *Tanna* of the following *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* lists several large containers: a *kiveret* made of straw, a *kiveret* made of reeds, a cistern, and the freshwater tank of an Alexandrian ship. An Alexandrian ship, as a student clarified, is a vessel that crosses open seas rather than hugging the coast or sailing rivers — and therefore requires a much larger freshwater supply for the journey. These containers all have a floor, which might suggest they qualify as vessels. Nevertheless, the *Mishnah* rules that if they hold 40 *se'ah* of liquid — the minimum volume of a *mikvah*, roughly the amount of water needed to fully immerse a person — which corresponds to two *kor* of dry measure, they are *tahor*, meaning they cannot become *tamei* because they are not classified as vessels at all.

Accordingly, once a container reaches the capacity of two *kor* dry measure, it loses vessel status — consistent with Rabbah's ruling that even two *kor* should not be carried on *Shabbat*. It is worth noting that while *tumah* law and *muktzah* law are formally distinct categories, the *Gemara* here applies a common definitional threshold across both domains. This is not unusual: halakhic definitions are sometimes extended across categories even where independent reasoning might have drawn the lines differently.

### Dry/Liquid Measures

Abaye draws an inference from the *Mishnah*'s dual formulation — 40 *se'ah* of liquid and two *kor* of dry measure — and observes: *shma mina hai gudesha tilsa havei*, we learn from this that the heaping of a dry measure adds one third. The reason two measures are cited is that a dry measure, unlike a liquid measure, permits heaping — grain piled above the rim of the container. Rashi notes that this effect is particularly pronounced when the vessel is wide but not very tall. The two *kor* of dry measure therefore exceeds the 40 *se'ah* of liquid measure by exactly one third, owing to the additional volume of the heap.

## Redness/Western Sky

### Abaye Watches Rava

The *Gemara* returns to *bein hashmashot*. Until this point the discussion has concerned the redness of the sky and when it darkens. Now: *Abayei chazyei leRava dekahadvi lema'arav* — Abaye observed Rava peering toward the west after sunset, examining the color of the sky. This is puzzling. The earlier discussion stated that one watches the *penei mizrach* — the face of the east — to determine when the redness dissipates. Why, then, is Rava looking west?

### Penei Mizrach Explained

Abaye asked Rava about this, invoking the phrase *kol zman shepenei mizrach maadimin* — as long as the face of the east is red. Rava replied: *mi savart penei mizrach mamash?* Do you really think it means literally the eastern face of the sky? No — *panim hamaadimim et hamizrach*: it means the side of the sky that *causes* the east to become red, which is the west. This is a striking reading. The practical consequence is significant: the western sky remains red far longer than the eastern sky, since the sun sets in the west. If it is the western redness that must dissipate before *bein hashmashot* advances, the transition to night comes considerably later.

### Alternative Account

The *Gemara* offers an alternative version of this exchange: *ika d'amri*, some say it was Rava who observed Abaye — not Abaye who observed Rava — and it was Abaye who had been looking eastward. In that version, Rava is the one who delivers the explanation that *penei mizrach* refers not to the east itself but to the face opposite the east, namely the west.

### Window Metaphor

This second version accompanies the mnemonic *vesimanach kivsa* — think of a window, where the light source is behind the window and illuminates the wall opposite. So too, the west, where the sun descends, illuminates the eastern sky from behind, as it were.

Yet this reading raises at least two difficulties. First, the earlier *baraita* described the stages in a specific order: the redness departs from *penei mizrach* and then the sky grows dark. If *penei mizrach* refers to the western horizon — which is the last part of the sky to darken — then the order in the *baraita* is inverted relative to what one would actually observe. Second, why does the redness matter at all? *Tzeis*, after all, is determined by the sky growing dark, not by the departure of redness. The redness is only relevant according to the version that holds the sky remains "day" while it is still red — a position that appears to be rejected in practice. These tensions in the *Gemara* are left unresolved here, though the *Maharam Alshakar* wrote an extensive *teshuva* on this topic that engages not only with the *Gemarot* but also with the Yerushalmi and with the actual phenomenology of the sky at twilight.

## Reconciling Gemarot

### Textual Problems

The difficulties with the *Gemara* as it stands are the following. On the one hand, if *penei mizrach* refers to the western horizon, then the order of darkening described in the *baraita* — redness first, then full darkness — is reversed, since the western sky is the last to darken. On the other hand, if the stage of redness is only relevant according to the opinion that it is still day while the sky is red, and that opinion is rejected in practice, why does the *Gemara* emphasize it at all? The standing position is that *bein hashmashot* begins at sunset, with *tzeis* coming three-quarters of a *mil* later — 14 to 18 minutes — and the redness stage is at most an intermediate marker.

### Pesachim Gemara

These tensions become sharper once the *Gemara* in Pesachim is introduced. That *Gemara* (90b) addresses, among other things, the time required to walk a *mil*, and states that from *alot hashachar* — the appearance of the morning star — until sunrise is four *mil*; and similarly, from sunset until *tzeis hakochavim* is four *mil*. Four *mil* at 18 minutes per *mil* yields 72 minutes. This is the foundation of *shitat Rabbeinu Tam*: the stars appear, and therefore night truly begins, only 72 minutes after sunset. The obvious question is how to reconcile this with the *Gemara* here in Shabbat, which gives three-quarters of a *mil* — at most 18 minutes — as the duration until *tzeis*.

### Geonim/Gra Approach

The *Geonim*, and following them the Gra — separated by roughly a thousand years but united in methodology — take the approach of giving priority to the *Gemara* that constitutes the primary *sugya* on a given topic. Since we are here in Shabbat, in the midst of a dedicated and extended discussion of *bein hashmashot*, this *Gemara* is the authoritative source. *Tzeis* is three-quarters of a *mil* after sunset. Full stop. The *Gemara* in Pesachim is not establishing a *halachic* definition of nightfall at all.

### Gra's Rakia Model

What, then, is the *Gemara* in Pesachim discussing? The Gra explains that it is addressing an astronomical question: how thick is the *rakia*, the firmament. The *Gemara*'s cosmological model is that a dome — the *rakia* — arches over the earth. The sun rises in the east, traverses the sky, and at sunset enters a kind of tunnel or window in the *rakia*, passing through its thickness. During this passage, light continues to emanate from the tunnel opening, producing the twilight glow. Once the sun fully exits the far end of the tunnel and begins its reverse journey westward beneath the *rakia* (which is opaque), all residual light ceases. The *Gemara* in Pesachim is asking: how long does it take the sun to traverse the thickness of this tunnel? The answer — four *mil* — is derived symmetrically from both morning and evening phenomena.

### Star Explanation

The Gra then explains how stars fit into this model. Stars are distributed throughout the thickness of the *rakia*. As the sun moves deeper through the tunnel after sunset, progressively more stars are revealed — those embedded near the inner edge of the *rakia* appear first, while those near the outer edge appear last. The reason more stars become visible as evening deepens is that they emerge in sequence as the sun's position within the *rakia*'s thickness changes. When the *Gemara* in Pesachim speaks of "stars coming out" after four *mil*, it refers to all the stars — the full complement, including those at the outermost edge of the *rakia*. This is entirely different from the three medium-sized stars that define *tzeis* for *halachic* purposes. Accordingly, the Gra concludes: the *Gemara* in Pesachim and the *Gemara* in Shabbat are not in tension at all. Pesachim is speaking of a cosmological phenomenon; Shabbat is speaking of the *halachic* onset of night.

### Rabbeinu Tam's View

Rabbeinu Tam takes the opposite approach. He treats the *Gemara* in Pesachim as the primary and authoritative source, and *tzeis* does not occur until 72 minutes after sunset. How then does he read this *Gemara* in Shabbat? The three-quarters of a *mil* — approximately 14 minutes — does not mark the end of *bein hashmashot*; rather, it marks its *beginning*, counting backward from the 72-minute endpoint. So *bein hashmashot* occupies the last 14 minutes of that 72-minute window. The first 58 minutes after sunset are, according to Rabbeinu Tam, unambiguously day. One could, in principle, perform *melacha* throughout that period without transgressing *Shabbat*.

### Tam's Geography

It has been widely noted that Rabbeinu Tam lived in France, where at certain latitudes and seasons it remains visibly light for well over an hour after sunset. It would have been phenomenologically impossible for him to accept that 14 or 18 minutes after sunset constitutes nightfall — the sky is still clearly bright. This geographic reality almost certainly influenced his reading of the sources, even if Rabbeinu Tam does not say so explicitly. His position attracted broad support among the *Rishonim* of France and Germany, many of whom lived in similarly high-latitude environments where late darkness was a fact of daily experience. It is telling that the *Ramban*, who lived in Spain, and the *Mechaber*, who lived in *Eretz Yisrael*, also ruled like Rabbeinu Tam — illustrating how a *shita*, once established, can take on a life independent of the geographic considerations that generated it. The *Mechaber* even applies Rabbeinu Tam *l'kula*, treating the period up to 58 minutes after sunset as unambiguous day — a position virtually no one relies upon today.

### Modern Practice

In practice, most communities follow neither the Gra's 14 minutes nor Rabbeinu Tam's 72 minutes. The prevalent practice is approximately 42 to 45 minutes after sunset. This position traces essentially to Rabbeinu Yonah in Berachot, who proposed setting aside the contradictory *Gemarot* and instead going outside to observe when three stars become visible across the sky. When stars appear distributed throughout the heavens, one has certainly met the threshold of three medium-sized stars that the *Gemara* will shortly describe as the marker of night. Based on astronomical reckoning, this corresponds to roughly 42 to 45 minutes after sunset, depending on location and season.

### Latitude Dependence

All of these positions are, in principle, latitude-dependent. Darkness comes at different rates depending on how far one is from the equator and what season it is. The Gra states explicitly that his number is not fixed but varies with location. In Jerusalem, where it grows dark relatively quickly, the practice associated with the Gra's position is approximately 25 minutes after *shkiya* for *motzei Shabbat* — not 14 or 18 minutes. At higher latitudes, even the Gra's figure would extend further. Rabbeinu Tam's 72 minutes is similarly subject to this variation. Rav Moshe Feinstein argues that Rabbeinu Tam's 72 minutes, adjusted for the latitude of America, yields approximately 45 minutes — which would mean that the common American practice of 42 to 45 minutes is effectively a latitude-adjusted Rabbeinu Tam.

### Rav Moshe's View

The difficulty with Rav Moshe's position is the following. If Rabbeinu Tam's 72 minutes were empirically calibrated to France, one could argue that the equivalent in America, given the difference in latitude, is 45 minutes. But Rabbeinu Tam is not making an empirical claim about France — he is presenting a textual argument that the *Gemara* itself, rooted in the realities of *Eretz Yisrael* and Babylonia, requires 72 minutes. There is no way to derive that 72 minutes in those latitudes corresponds to 45 minutes in America. Nevertheless, Rav Moshe maintains this position. His view is that those who follow the 42 to 45 minute practice are in effect following a modified Rabbeinu Tam adjusted for location. The more straightforward understanding of the 42 to 45 minute practice, however, is that it reflects direct astronomical observation of when three stars appear — an approach that does not depend on resolving the contradiction between the *Gemarot* at all.

Practically speaking, there is a strong argument against relying on Rabbeinu Tam *l'kula* under ordinary circumstances. If one routinely performs *melacha* after 45 minutes — effectively rejecting Rabbeinu Tam's *shita* week after week — it is difficult to invoke that same *shita* selectively when a situation of need arises. The coherence of one's *halachic* practice requires some consistency in which positions one takes seriously.

## Halachic Rulings

### Rabbi Nechemiah

The *Gemara* now introduces Rabbi Nechemiah's position: *bein hashmashot* lasts for the time it takes a person to walk half a *mil* from sunset — approximately nine minutes.

### Mount Carmel Test

Rabbi Chanina offers a practical illustration: if you want to experience the duration of Rabbi Nechemiah's *shiur*, leave the sun at the top of Mount Carmel, descend to the Mediterranean, immerse in the sea, and return to the top — that interval is the *shiur* of Rabbi Nechemiah. Mount Carmel is located near Haifa, adjacent to the Mediterranean coast, so the geography is plausible. Rashi understands this as a way of expressing the nine-minute half-*mil* — the descent, immersion, and return constituting roughly that amount of time, though the logistics of how exactly going down a mountain, performing a *tevila*, and returning maps onto nine minutes is not entirely straightforward, particularly since *ya'ale* might mean emerging from the *tevila* rather than literally climbing back up the mountain.

### Tosafot Reading

Tosafot offers a fundamentally different reading of the passage. Rather than describing the duration of *bein hashmashot* from sunset, Tosafot understands the scenario as follows: when the sun is still at the top of Mount Carmel — meaning, before sunset — the time it takes to descend, perform a *tevila*, and return gives you an indication of when the sun will have set. In other words, it is telling a *kohen* who wishes to immerse and then eat *terumah* after dark: if you begin your descent when the sun is at the top of the mountain, you will have completed your *tevila* and ascended before sunset, ensuring that your immersion occurs during definite daytime. On this reading, the example is not about the duration of *bein hashmashot* at all but about a practical landmark for knowing when sunset is near — relevant specifically to a *kohen* who needs to time his *tevila* correctly. What remains somewhat unclear is why the *Gemara* specifies the return ascent, since once the *tevila* is complete the *kohen*'s status changes regardless of where he is physically standing.

The extended discussion of *bein hashmashot* now arrives at a crucial ruling. Before examining it, it is worth noting that the most stringent position in this *Gemara* is Rabbi Yehuda's: from sunset until three-quarters of a *mil* is *bein hashmashot*, before which is definite day and after which is definite night. The other positions — Rabbi Nechemiah's half-*mil* and Rabbi Yosi's blink-of-an-eye — are comparatively less stringent in duration. Were it not for the *Gemara* in Pesachim and the subsequent position of Rabbeinu Tam, the natural reading of this *Gemara* would yield *tzeis* at 14 to 18 minutes after sunset as the most stringent available ruling.

### Two-Sided Strictness

Rabbah bar bar Channah said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *halakha k'Rabbi Yehuda l'inyan Shabbat* — we rule like Rabbi Yehuda for matters of *Shabbat*. *V'halakha k'Rabbi Yosi l'inyan terumah* — and we rule like Rabbi Yosi for matters of *terumah*.

### Strictness Explained

The *Gemara* unpacks this ruling. Ruling like Rabbi Yehuda for *Shabbat* is straightforward: it is *l'chumra*, the strict position, meaning *Shabbat* begins at sunset and does not end until *bein hashmashot* is complete. But what does it mean to rule like Rabbi Yosi for *terumah*? The *Gemara* first considers whether it means *kohanim* may immerse during Rabbi Yehuda's *bein hashmashot* period because according to Rabbi Yosi that period is definite day — but this is rejected, since immersion is a *d'Oraita* requirement and one cannot be lenient there.

The correct interpretation: ruling like Rabbi Yosi for *terumah* means that *kohanim* may not eat *terumah* until Rabbi Yosi's *bein hashmashot* — the blink of an eye — has elapsed following Rabbi Yehuda's three-quarters of a *mil*. This is a two-sided strictness. At the beginning of *Shabbat*, we are strict like Rabbi Yehuda and treat sunset as the onset of *bein hashmashot*. At the end of *Shabbat*, we are strict like Rabbi Yosi and do not permit the eating of *terumah* (or, by extension, the performance of *melacha*) until even Rabbi Yosi's instantaneous transition has passed — ensuring that one is in definite night before acting. We are strict at the onset and strict at the conclusion.

This presupposes a clear understanding of when Rabbi Yosi's "blink of an eye" occurs. One might have thought it happens somewhere in the middle of Rabbi Yehuda's *bein hashmashot* window; the *Gemara* establishes that it occurs at the very end — once Rabbi Yehuda's full three-quarters of a *mil* has elapsed, then for that final split second is Rabbi Yosi's *bein hashmashot*, and only after that is it unambiguous night.

### Tosafot Question

Tosafot raises two questions. First: why does the *Gemara* need to switch from *Shabbat* to *terumah*? It could simply have said we are strict at the beginning of *Shabbat* and strict at the end of *Shabbat*. Tosafot answers that the point is to emphasize that this strictness applies not only to the weighty prohibitions of *Shabbat* but even to *terumah*, which — particularly in the present era when *terumah* may be eaten only by *kohanim* in a state of purity — is somewhat less acute as a practical matter. Even so, we maintain the two-sided stringency.

Tosafot's more probing question is: why does any of this need to be stated for a blink of an eye? What practical difference does a split second make? Tosafot suggests that perhaps Rabbi Yosi's instantaneous transition does not occur precisely at the end of Rabbi Yehuda's period but a few minutes later. This would give real content to the ruling — one must wait not just the three-quarters of a *mil* of Rabbi Yehuda but an additional brief interval before eating *terumah* or performing *melacha*.

In any case, the *Gemara*'s ruling as understood here remains tethered to the framework of this *sugya*: *bein hashmashot* is bounded by sunset on one end and roughly 20 minutes on the other, with additional positions derived either from the *Gemara* in Pesachim or from adjustment for geographic latitude.

## Stars as Markers

### Star Counting

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: one star visible in the sky — it is still day; two stars — it is *bein hashmashot*; three stars — it is night. This is a striking and practical alternative to the earlier measures. Rather than counting minutes from sunset or watching the sky's color shift, one simply looks upward.

### Baraita Confirmation

A *baraita* confirms the identical rule: one star is day, two stars is *bein hashmashot*, three stars is night. The *baraita* also includes the teaching of Rabbi Yosi bar Avin: one who performs *melacha* during *bein hashmashot* on Friday evening and again during *bein hashmashot* on Saturday night — having thus spanned the ambiguous period on both ends of *Shabbat* — is *chayav chatat*, obligated in a sin-offering. One might think this is impossible to prove, since perhaps both acts of *melacha* fell in whichever half of the ambiguous period was "really" day. Rashi and Tosafot therefore clarify: the ruling applies when one performed *melacha* continuously throughout the entire *bein hashmashot* period on each occasion. In that case, at least some portion of the *melacha* necessarily fell during the actual *Shabbat* — whether night arrived at the beginning or the end of the ambiguous window.

### Chatat Rule

This ruling exposes a deeper conceptual question, which connects to an earlier *Gemara* concerning a *zav*. When we speak of *bein hashmashot* as an uncertain period, are we saying that night truly begins at one specific moment within that window and we simply cannot identify it? Or are we saying something more fundamental: that *bein hashmashot* is by its nature a mixed category — simultaneously quasi-day and quasi-night — not because we lack information but because that is what dusk inherently *is*? The first reading leads naturally to the requirement that *melacha* must span the entire period to guarantee culpability. The second reading would entail a different analysis entirely. Rashi and Tosafot incline toward the first reading, but the second is a genuine alternative.

### Medium Stars

Rabbi Yosi clarifies an essential detail: the three stars are not the large, bright bodies sometimes visible even in daylight, nor are they the faint stars that emerge only well into the night. They are *beinonim* — medium-sized, average stars.

### Rava's Objection

Rava adds a further clarification consistent with the *chatat* discussion above: one is not *chayav* based merely on performing *melacha* at some moment during *bein hashmashot*. The *melacha* must be performed throughout the entire period.

### Astronomy Basis

The common practice of 42 to 45 minutes after sunset is grounded in astronomical observation calibrated to this *Gemara*. When stars are visible across the full extent of the sky, one has certainly reached the threshold of three medium-sized stars visible to the naked eye. Astronomers have calculated that this condition is met approximately 42 to 45 minutes after sunset, depending on latitude and season.

### Conceptual Issue

The *chatat* rule in this context ties directly back to the conceptual question noted above. If *bein hashmashot* is a period of genuine uncertainty — somewhere in these minutes night begins, but we cannot pinpoint it — then one is only unambiguously *chayav* when *melacha* was performed throughout, ensuring it necessarily overlapped with the onset of night. If, however, *bein hashmashot* is an inherently hybrid category, the analysis differs. Both readings have implications for how one understands the structure of this whole *sugya*.

## Practical Signs

### Sun/Palm Trees

Rava said to his attendant: since you are not expert in the precise rabbinic calculations of time (*d'lo kaim-lehu b'shiurei d'rabbanan*), here is a practical guide. When the sun is level with the tops of the palm trees — *ad de-shimsha rei-sh diklei* — that is when you should light a candle, because sunset is imminent.

### Animal Signs

On a cloudy day when the sun cannot be seen, different indicators apply. In the city, observe the roosters: they begin settling onto their perches. Rashi explains they take up their roosting positions while it is still day. In the field, observe the ravens: they return to their nests as nightfall approaches.

### Grass Sign

An additional indicator: a type of grass called *adanei* begins to incline toward the west as nightfall draws near.

### Sign Puzzle

This passage raises an interesting difficulty. If *bein hashmashot* is defined by sunset, why are alternative practical signs needed at all? Anyone can look outside and see whether the sun has set. One possible resolution is that these signs are given for situations where the actual sunset is obscured — perhaps by mountains, or by topography that prevents a clear view of the horizon. When one is surrounded by palm trees but cannot see all the way to the horizon, seeing the sun at the treetops is a more reliable indicator than guessing at the moment of actual sunset. It may also be relevant that what counts as "sunset" for *halachic* purposes — observable sunset vs. astronomical sunset at sea level — depends on the observer's elevation and vantage point, adding additional complexity to the simple instruction to "just look at the sunset."

There is a further implication here. The fact that practical signs are given for when to stop *melacha* and light candles — a period *before* *bein hashmashot* properly begins — suggests that the practice of lighting candles was already firmly associated with the transition into *Shabbat*, laying the groundwork for the later institution of lighting candles 18 minutes (or, in Jerusalem, 40 minutes) before *shkiya*.

## Six Tekiot

### Six Blasts Intro

A *baraita* teaches: six *tekiot* — shofar blasts — were sounded on *erev Shabbat*. This practice is associated with Jerusalem and the *Beit Hamikdash*, though there was a designated place on the Temple precincts from which the blasts were made, as discussed in the context of tractate Middot.

### First Blast

The first blast served to signal those working in the fields: cease your labor, gather your tools and implements, and begin moving toward the city. Given the distance they needed to travel and the time required to return implements and transition from fieldwork, this blast was sounded well in advance of *Shabbat*.

### Second Blast

The second blast signaled those within the city: merchants should close their shops, manufacturers should cease production, and commerce should conclude. City-dwellers needed less travel time, so this blast came somewhat later.

### Third Blast

The third blast, according to Rabbi Natan, was the signal to light candles. By this point, the community had had sufficient time to wind down its activities, and the act of candle-lighting was the final *melacha* to be performed — beginning to associate it, institutionally, with the arrival of *Shabbat*.

### Tefillin Alternative

Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi offers a different understanding of the third blast: it signaled the removal of *tefillin*. Since *Shabbat* itself is an *ot* — a sign — it is inappropriate to wear *tefillin* on *Shabbat*; and since *tefillin* are not worn at night in any case, the third blast marked the point at which *tefillin* were taken off. According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's reading, when candles were lit is not specified — Rashi suggests it occurred at the fourth blast, though the text does not spell this out.

### Final Three Blasts

After a brief interval — the time it takes to fry a small fish (*k'dei tzliyat dag katan*), or alternatively, enough time to stick bread to the oven wall and let a crust form — a final sequence of *tekiah teruah tekiah* was sounded. This three-part blast constituted the formal announcement that *Shabbat* had arrived and all *melacha* must cease.

### Babylonian Practice

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel raises a question about the Babylonian practice: *ma na'aseh leBavliyim?* The Babylonians sounded *tekiah teruah* and then stopped in the middle of the *teruah* — *veshovtim mitoch meria*. This differs from the *tekiah teruah tekiah* prescribed above, both in the sequence and in the fact that *Shabbat* begins mid-*teruah* rather than after the sequence concludes. The *Gemara* resolves the count: they actually sounded *tekiah*, then again *tekiah teruah*, halting midway through the *teruah*. The *Gemara* responds simply: *minhag avoteihem beyadeihem* — they maintained their ancestral practice. One might note that it is also worth recalling an earlier usage of the phrase *ma na'aseh leBavliyim* — in the context of candle oils — where a similar appeal to the practical realities of communities outside *Eretz Yisrael* was invoked.

## Detailed Timeline

### First Tekiah Detail

The *tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* now provides a more detailed narrative account of the six-blast sequence. When the first *tekiah* began — note the language *hitchil litkoa*, "began to sound," suggesting each blast was sustained over some minutes — those working in the fields ceased plowing, hoeing, and all agricultural labor. Notably, those working closer to the city were not permitted to enter until those working further out had also gathered and converged, so that all entered together as a group.

### Suspicion Issue

Rashi explains the reason for this communal re-entry: if some workers returned to the city noticeably earlier than others, the later arrivals might be suspected of having continued working after the blast — violating *Shabbat*. By waiting for the more distant workers and entering together, all are equally beyond suspicion.

### Second Tekiah Detail

When the second *tekiah* began, the shutters (*trisim*) — boards that shopkeepers would remove and place on stands as display tables for their wares — were taken down, the stores were locked, and all manufacturing activity in the city ceased. Yet the kitchens were still active: hot water remained on the stove and pots continued to simmer.

### Third Tekiah Detail

When the third *tekiah* began, those with food on the fire removed it. Those who intended to preserve heat for their *Shabbat* meals performed *hatmana* — wrapping and insulating their pots. Candles were lit. And if bread was still in the oven, one could quickly stick a fresh loaf to the wall and allow a crust to form — the last permissible act before the final announcement.

### Shabbat Arrival

Then came the *tekiah teruah tekiah* — the concluding three-part blast — and *Shabbat* arrived. All *melacha* ceased.

## Late Lighting/Shofar

### Late Lighting Leniency

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Chanina rules: if someone has not yet lit candles by the time the six *tekiot* are completed, he may still light for a brief additional interval.

### Shofar-Blower Travel

The rationale: the Sages allotted time for the *chazan haknesset* — the one who sounded the *shofar* — to take his *shofar* home after the final blast. During that brief window, *Shabbat* has not yet fully "taken hold" in the most practical sense, and candle-lighting may still be performed.

### Objection

The *Gemara* objects: if the permissible window depends on the distance between the *shofar*-blower's position and his home, the boundary of *Shabbat* becomes entirely subjective and flexible — which undermines the whole purpose of the communal announcement system.

### Roof Storage

The *Gemara* resolves this: the *shofar*-blower has a discreet place (*makom tzanua*) on the roof of the building from which he blows, where he stores the *shofar*. He does not carry it home at all — his "travel" is simply ascending or descending a flight of stairs, a fixed and minimal amount of time. The window is therefore defined and consistent.

### Muktzah Problem

This resolution also explains why the *shofar* must be stored immediately nearby rather than carried through the city: *she'ein metaltelin lo et hashofar v'lo et hachatzotzrot* — neither the *shofar* nor the *chatzotzrot* (Temple trumpets) may be carried on *Shabbat*. This is not merely a question of carrying in a domain without an *eruv*, but a matter of *muktzah*: these are instruments designated for a specific sacred function and are therefore set aside and not handled on *Shabbat*. The discussion of *muktzah* will continue on the following *daf*.
