# Yoma 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Interruption of Service

### Meir vs. Eliezer/Shimon

The daf opens by returning to a dispute already set in motion: what happens when the *avodah* is interrupted in one of the sections of the *Beit HaMikdash* — whether in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the *Heikhal*, or at the *mizbe'ach* — and the sprinkling of blood is disrupted, requiring the *shechita* of another animal to obtain fresh blood? The debate turns on whether one must begin again from the start of that section or may resume from the point of interruption. Rabbi Meir holds that one starts again from the beginning of that section, ensuring that all of the blood-work within it comes from a single animal. Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon hold that one picks up where one left off, so that a single continuous process is not fragmented into two.

### Blood Spill in Asham

Rabbi Yochanan — following the *girsa* of Rabbeinu Chananel as quoted in Tosafot, *shenishpach damo kodem gemar matnotav* — now applies this dispute to the *asham metzora*. When a *metzora* concludes his period of impurity, he brings an *asham* whose blood is sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach* and then applied to his ear, thumb, and toe. If in the middle of that process the blood spills, Rabbi Yochanan says this falls directly into the same dispute. According to Rabbi Meir — who holds that when blood spills mid-section one brings another animal and starts again from the beginning — here too one would bring another *asham*, perform a new *shechita*, and repeat the entire process of sprinkling on the *mizbe'ach* and applying to the ear, thumb, and toe. According to Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon — who hold that one picks up where one left off — the case becomes striking: *hacha ein lo takana*, here there is no remedy. Normally their position forces one to continue from the point of interruption rather than start again; but here the Torah specifies *keves echad*, one ram for the *asham*, which means one cannot use two rams as part of a single process. Unable to start from the beginning and unable to resume mid-process, a person in this situation would be entirely stuck.

### Rav Chisda's Challenge

Rav Chisda presses the analysis further. The verse states *v'hikriv oto le-asham asher hunaf* — the very animal over which the *tenufa* was performed is the one to be brought as the *asham*. The word *oto* is a limiting term: that specific one. Rav Chisda argues that even according to Rabbi Meir, who would otherwise permit bringing another animal and starting again, the word *oto* should prevent this, since the replacement animal is not the one over which the initial *tenufa* was performed. If so, even Rabbi Meir's position leads to an impasse, and the *gemara* registers this as a genuine difficulty: *ve-namshikh kashya*.

### Baraita Evidence

A *baraita* is then cited in support of Rabbi Yochanan's framework. The *baraita* discusses an *asham metzora* that was slaughtered *shelo lishmo*, or — the case relevant to our discussion — where the blood was placed on the *mizbe'ach* but not applied to the ear and thumb of the *metzora*. In such a case, the *baraita* rules *harei zeh oleh legabei mizbe'ach v'te'un nesachim*: the animal is valid as a *korban* and requires the accompanying wine and flour and oil. However, *u-tzarikh asham acher lehakhshiro*: if one now wishes to complete the *metzora*'s purification, a second *asham* is needed. This ruling appears to support Rabbi Meir's position — that the first animal, having had its blood placed on the *mizbe'ach*, achieved partial validity, while a second animal can be brought to complete the purification process. This shows that the framework of bringing another *korban* and resuming the process is at least coherent, and that for Rabbi Meir there is no conceptual barrier to doing so.

### Stuck Position

Rav Chisda, however, reads the *baraita* differently. When the *baraita* says *tzarich asham acher*, it means not that one can bring another *asham* but that one needs to — yet cannot. This is the force of the formulation *tzarich v'ein lo takana*: it is required, and yet there is no remedy. In Rav Chisda's reading, the word *oto* in the verse creates an absolute barrier: you cannot use more than one animal in total for this process, which means that according to everyone — including Rabbi Meir — one is stuck. The *baraita* does not show that a second animal can successfully complete the *metzora*'s purification; it only shows that technically one is needed, while simultaneously making clear that there is no viable path to providing it.

## Tzarich v'Ein Takana

### Tanna's Language

This invites a methodological challenge: would a *tanna* formulate a teaching by saying *tzarich* — you need to do X — when what he really means is that it is required but impossible to fulfill? To say "you need another *asham*" when in fact there is no mechanism by which that *asham* can accomplish anything seems at best misleading and at worst contradictory.

### Nazir Precedent

The *gemara* responds with a parallel case. A *nazir memora* — a *nazir* who is naturally bald — faces an analogous problem. The Torah requires that at the conclusion of his *nezirus* he shave his hair; what happens when he has no hair to shave? Beit Shammai say *tzarich ha'avarat ta'ar* — he must pass a razor over his head. Beit Hillel say *ein tzarich ha'avarat ta'ar* — he does not need to. So when Beit Shammai say *tzarich*, what does that mean in practice?

### Rav Avina's View

Rav Avina explains: when Beit Shammai say *tzarich*, they mean *tzarich v'ein lo takana* — it is required and there is no remedy. The passage of the razor over a bald head accomplishes nothing; it is not a meaningful act. And yet the formulation is simply *tzarich*, without explicit elaboration that the requirement cannot be fulfilled. This vindicates Rav Chisda's reading of the *baraita*: a *tanna* can indeed say *tzarich* to mean "required, yet impossible."

### Rav Padat's View

Rav Padat disagrees with this interpretation of Beit Shammai, arguing instead that *tzarich* means one does what is actually possible. In his reading, Beit Shammai genuinely require the physical act of passing the razor over the head — not as a meaningless gesture, but as the closest available fulfillment of the requirement. This interpretation stands in direct tension with Rav Avina's: Rav Padat reads *tzarich* as "do whatever you can," while Rav Avina reads it as "required but impossible."

### Metzora Missing Limb

Rav Padat draws support from the case of a *metzora* who lacks a thumb or a toe. The *mishna* states *ein lo tahara olamit* — he can never achieve purification. Rabbi Elazar disagrees: *noten al mekomo veyotzei* — one places the blood where the thumb or toe would have been, and the requirement is thereby fulfilled. Rabbi Shimon goes further and says that *bedi'avad* one may even place it on the left thumb if the right is missing.

The significance for Rav Padat is clear: Rabbi Elazar, who holds that one simply places the blood on the stub or the site where the limb would have been, represents the same interpretive move as Beit Shammai. Just as Beit Shammai require the razor to actually pass over the head — not as a legal fiction but as a genuine act, however attenuated — Rabbi Elazar requires placing the blood where the thumb was as the fulfillment of the *mitzvah*, not as a declaration of impossibility. Both positions reflect the principle that *tzarich* means "do whatever is doable," not "it is required but hopeless." This debate has significant implications beyond the immediate cases. The question of whether the Torah demands the specific act or whether it demands the closest achievable approximation touches on how one approaches ritual requirements in the face of physical constraints or disability.

## Two Kohanim Process

### Vessel vs. Hand

The *gemara* shifts to a *baraita* exploring the mechanics of the *asham metzora* ritual. The verse states *v'lakach midam ha'asham*, the *kohen* takes from the blood of the *asham*. One might have thought this blood — like ordinary sacrificial blood — is caught in a vessel and applied from there. The verse's formulation, however, links the *lekicha* (taking) to the *netina* (placing): just as the placing on the ear, thumb, and toe is done *b'atzmo shel kohen*, with the *kohen*'s own body, specifically his finger, so too the taking is *b'atzmo shel kohen*, caught directly in his hand. One might then wonder whether blood destined for the *mizbe'ach* is similarly caught by hand; the Torah answers this by stating *ki kachatat ha'asham hu l'Hashem* — this *asham* is like a *chatat*, and just as a *chatat* requires the blood to be caught in a vessel, so too the blood going on the *mizbe'ach* requires a vessel.

### Two Kohanim Role

The result is a striking structural division within a single *korban*. The *asham metzora* thus has two distinct identities: as a standard *korban* whose blood goes to the *mizbe'ach* via a vessel, and as an instrument of purification whose blood is applied to the *metzora* by hand. The *baraita* draws the logical conclusion: *asham metzora shnei kohanim mikablim et damo*, two *kohanim* are required — *echad b'yad v'echad bikli*, one receiving the blood in his hand and one in a vessel. *Ze shekibel bikli ba lo etzel mizbe'ach* — the one who received in the vessel proceeds to the *mizbe'ach* and performs the standard *korban* rite. *V'ze shekibel b'yad ba lo etzel Metzora* — the one who received in his hand goes to the *metzora* and applies the blood to his ear, thumb, and toe. This is not merely a logistical division. The two processes are functionally segregated: the blood that goes on the *mizbe'ach* and the blood that goes on the *metzora* are not drawn from the same vessel, nor could one process serve the other. They are parallel but independent — two *kohanim*, two modes of reception, two destinations.

## Leftover Animals

### Eliezer: All Equal

Returning to the main thread, the *gemara* takes up the question of what happens to all the animals that were slaughtered in the course of repeated interruptions. When the process of blood-sprinkling is interrupted and a new animal must be slaughtered, multiple carcasses accumulate. The normal rule for the *par* and *se'ir* of Yom Kippur — animals whose blood was brought inside the *Heikhal* — is that they are taken *chutz l'machaneh* and burned outside. Does this apply to all the accumulated animals or only to one of them?

A *Tosefta* is cited: Rabbi Eliezer holds *v'kulam metamin begadim v'nisrafin b'veit hadeshen* — all of them convey impurity to the garments of those who handle them and all of them are burned in the designated place outside the *Mikdash*. Since the blood of each was used in a ritual — even if ideally there should have been only one — each animal has the same *halacha* as if it were the sole animal brought.

### Shimon: Last Only

The *chachamim* disagree: *ein mitam'in begadim ve'ein nisrafin beveit hadeshen ela ha'acharon* — only the last animal has full status, both for conveyance of impurity and for burning outside. The reasoning: *hu ve'gamar bo kapara*, it was with that animal that the *kapara* was completed.

### Completion Principle

This ruling deserves reflection, because one might have expected the opposite conclusion. The blood brought into the *Kodesh Kodashim* seems more significant than blood applied at the *mizbe'ach*; why would the last animal — which may only have contributed to the final stage — be considered the primary one? The answer lies in how the Torah frames completion. The verse states *ve-chilah mikaper et hakodesh ve'et ohel mo'ed ve'et hamizbe'ach* — the atonement for the *kodesh*, the *ohel mo'ed*, and the *mizbe'ach* is described as finished only at the moment of conclusion. Just as one cannot declare a room clean until the last corner has been done, the *kapara* is not *kapara* until it is fully complete. The principle *kilah kiper* — when finished, atonement is achieved — means that real status accrues only to the animal whose blood sealed the process. The earlier animals must still be disposed of properly, but they do not carry the full *halachic* identity of the primary *korban*.

## Which La'Azazel Goat

### Rava's Question

Rava poses a question to Rav Nachman: *kama se'irim meshale'ach* — if the blood of the *se'ir* whose blood was used inside spilled and the entire process had to be repeated multiple times, each time requiring a new pair of goats with a new *hagralah*, how many goats are sent to *azazel*? Each time two goats are brought, a *hagralah* is performed, and one is designated *la'azazel*. Does each of those designated goats get sent?

### One vs. Many

Rav Nachman responds: *vechi eder hu meshale'ach* — would one send an entire flock to *azazel*? Clearly only one can be sent. But Rava pushes back: *vechi eder hu soref* — yet the position just stated holds that all the accumulated animals are burned outside, so why is it not equally absurd to burn more than one?

The *gemara* draws a clear distinction. By the burning, the verse has no limiting word: any animal whose blood was brought inside is burned, and nothing in the text restricts this to one. But by the sending to *azazel*, the verse states *leshale'ach oto la'azazel hamidbarah* — the word *oto*, that one, limits it to a single animal. Moreover, the entire ritual of sending is focused on one act: there is a *viduy*, a confession, a physical journey, a singular dramatic moment of expiation. Multiplying that act undermines its coherence in a way that multiplying a disposal-by-burning does not. So while all animals whose blood was brought inside may be burned, only one can be sent *la'azazel*.

### Last Gets Status

Now the further question: which one? Two opinions are reported in the name of Rava, transmitted through different students.

Rav Shimi, citing Rava, says *acharon meshale'ach* — the last one is sent. This follows directly from the principle of *kilah kiper*: since the animal whose blood completed the process is considered the primary *korban*, its paired *la'azazel* goat bears the primary status. Consistency demands that if the last blood-animal has full status, so too does its mate. The goat sent to *azazel* should be the one paired with the animal whose blood ended the *avodah*.

### First Gets Status

Rav Pappa, also citing Rava, says *rishon meshale'ach* — the first one is sent. His reasoning invokes the principle of Rabbi Yosi: *mitzvah barishon*, in analogous cases priority is given to the first.

### Rabbi Yosi Priority

The *gemara* asks: which Rabbi Yosi holds this? It considers the Rabbi Yosi associated with the *kupot* in the context of *Shekalim*. Three baskets were used to remove money from the *lishkah* for purchasing communal *korbanot*, labeled one, two, three so that the order would be known. A *baraita* records Rabbi Yosi asking why the baskets are labeled at all. His answer: *shemitzvah barishon* — because it is a *mitzvah* to use the first basket's money first, before the second's. By the same logic, the first goat designated *la'azazel* should have priority.

### Rejection of First

The *gemara* rejects this analogy. In the case of the baskets, when the first basket was scooped, the second basket had not yet come into existence. For that moment, however brief, the *mitzvah* focused exclusively on the first basket. There was a period of time during which only the first was fit. Here, however, none of the *la'azazel* goats is ready to be sent at the moment it is designated. A goat becomes fit for *azazel* only after all the blood-work is complete. Until the very end of the entire *avodah*, no designated *la'azazel* goat is yet eligible. Consequently, when the moment of readiness finally arrives, all the designated *la'azazel* goats become simultaneously fit. There is no period during which only the first was ready; they all achieve readiness at the same instant.

The *gemara* then turns to a different case of Rabbi Yosi: *hamaphrish pishcho ve'avad* — one designated a *korban Pesach* and it became lost; one designated a second animal in its place; then the first was found. The *chachamim* say one may bring whichever one he wishes. Rabbi Yosi says *mitzvah barishon* — one should bring the first. Crucially, Rashi points out that this could all have occurred before *chatzos*: both animals were designated earlier in the day, and *chatzos* — the moment they become fit to be brought — arrived while both were present. Rabbi Yosi still gives priority to the one that was sanctified first, even though they became simultaneously fit.

This seems to support Rav Pappa: even when multiple candidates are equally ready at the same moment, Rabbi Yosi awards priority to the one that was first designated. By that logic, the first goat designated *la'azazel* should be the one sent, even though all of them became eligible simultaneously when the *avodah* was completed.

The *gemara*'s conclusion leaves the question unresolved in a straightforward way, though it notes that for the remaining goats one presumably waits until they develop a *mum* and redeems them, since *ein chatas tzibur meisah* — a communal *chatat* is not left to die, unlike an individual's *chatat*.

## Two Goats Matching

### Ideal Requirements

Having completed that extended discussion, the *gemara* turns to the *mishna* that opens the new *perek* and addresses the two goats of Yom Kippur directly. *Shnei se'irei Yom HaKippurim mitzvatan sheyihyu shneihem shavin b'mareh u'v'komah u'v'damim u'vilkichatan k'echad* — the ideal requirement is that the two goats be similar in appearance (*mareh*), similar in height (*komah*), similar in cost (*damim*), and purchased at the same time (*likchiatan k'echad*). Rashi clarifies that *mareh* does not mean they must be identical twins, but that they should be similar — both white or both black, for example. The underlying purpose of these requirements is to reinforce the power of the *hagralah*: when everything about the two animals is equal — their appearance, their stature, their provenance, even the moment of their acquisition — the designation of one *l'Hashem* and one *la'azazel* is entirely a function of the *goral* alone, with nothing external to distinguish one path from the other. That equality is the precondition for the *hagralah* to carry its full significance, whether one understands it in terms of divine providence (*hashgacha*) or the drama of undetermined fate.

### Death Before Lottery

Notwithstanding the ideal requirements, *ve'af al pi she'ein shavin kasher bedi'avad* — even if they are not identical, the *avodah* is valid after the fact. Similarly, if one was purchased today and one tomorrow, it is *kasher bedi'avad*.

If one of the goats dies before the *hagralah* has been performed, the remedy is straightforward: *yikach zug lasheini*, bring a second goat and conduct the lottery with the surviving animal.

### Death After Lottery

If one of the goats dies after the *hagralah* has been conducted — one having been designated *l'Hashem* and one *la'azazel* — the matter is more complex. One cannot simply pair the surviving goat with a new one: the surviving goat's status emerged from a specific *goral*, and a new animal cannot be inserted into that completed lottery retroactively. Therefore *yavi zug acher v'yagril aleihem batchila*: a new pair is brought and a fresh *hagralah* is conducted.

The *mishna* then instructs what to say at the new lottery: if the *l'Hashem* goat died, one declares over whichever of the new pair the lot for *Hashem* falls, *ze she'alah alav hagoral l'Hashem yitkayeim tachtav* — this one that the lot designated for *Hashem* shall stand in its place. The second of the new pair — the one designated *la'azazel* in the new lottery — now creates a surplus: there is one surviving original goat (the *la'azazel* from the first lottery) and one newly designated *la'azazel*. The *mishna* instructs that the second one — which according to its plain reading appears to be the newly acquired one — grazes until it develops a *mum* (*yeireh ad sheyista'eiv*), at which point it is sold and the proceeds go toward a *nedavah* for a communal *korban* such as an *olah*. This is because *ein chatas tzibur meisah* — a communal *chatat* does not die; unlike an individual's *chatat*, which must be left to perish when it cannot be used, a communal one is redeemed.

The *gemara* notes a conceptual awkwardness in this arrangement: if the surviving original *la'azazel* goat and the newly designated *la'azazel* goat were paired with animals that went through different lotteries, there is something peculiar about using them together. The *mishna*'s language, however, strongly implies that it is the newly acquired animal that is set aside, not the surviving original. The *gemara* questions this because it creates the odd situation of a *l'Hashem* goat from one lottery being matched with a *la'azazel* goat from a different lottery — animals that were never designated as a pair through the same *hagralah*. Nevertheless, since the *mishna* says *ze she'alah alav hagoral yitkayeim tachtav* in relation to one animal and then says the second one grazes, the plain reading is that the surviving original remains and the newly acquired one is set aside.

Rabbi Yehuda adds the position that a *chatas tzibur* that cannot be used should in fact die, like an individual's *chatat*, rather than being redeemed as a *nedavah*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Cases

Rabbi Yehuda further extends the analysis to cases where it is not the death of a goat that creates the problem but rather a disruption in the blood-work. If the blood of the *l'Hashem* goat spilled, the *la'azazel* goat must die, and the entire process must begin again with a new lottery. If the *la'azazel* goat died after the *l'Hashem* goat's blood had already been applied, the blood is voided and the process likewise starts again. Once the animal has moved into the stage of becoming *dam* — once it has been slaughtered and its blood collected — the substitution mechanism of the *mishna* no longer applies. At that point, one does not work around the loss by designating a replacement; instead, the entire process begins fresh.

## Paired Animals Rule

### Goats Shnei Teaching

The *gemara* now turns to a systematic derivation of the requirement for matching animals across multiple contexts. A *baraita* begins with the goats of Yom Kippur. The verse says *yikach shnei se'irei izim* — he shall take two he-goats. The word *se'irei* in the plural already implies a minimum of two; why then does the verse add the word *shnei*? The extra word teaches *sheyihyu shneihem shavin* — that they must be similar. How do I know that *bedi'avad*, if they are not similar, the *avodah* is still valid? Because the verse also uses the word *se'ir* individually in multiple places — referring to one and then the other separately — which indicates that each can stand as an independent entity, and *bedi'avad* even unmatched goats are acceptable.

### Se'ir Individual Form

Given that the individual form of *se'ir* teaches *bedi'avad* validity, why would anyone have thought that non-matching would be absolutely invalidating? Because the word *shnei* appears so many times in the relevant passages — *shnei la'azazel*, *shnei asirim*, and so on — one might have concluded that this repetition of the paired form makes matching an absolute requirement, invalid even *bedi'avad*. The individual appearances of *se'ir* therefore come to override that inference and establish that *bedi'avad* it remains *kasher*.

### Multiple Matching Ways

Having established that *bedi'avad* non-matching is acceptable because of the individual *se'ir*, the *gemara* asks: since the individual form frees *bedi'avad* cases from absolute requirement, why does the Torah say *shnei* so many times? The answer: each instance teaches a different dimension of the *lechatchila* matching requirement — one for *mareh* (appearance), one for *komah* (height), and one for *damim* (cost). The multiple uses of the paired form each establish a different way in which the two goats should ideally be alike.

### Metzora Sheep Pattern

The same pattern of derivation applies to the sheep brought in the *korban* of a *metzora*. *Yikach shnei kevasim* — the word *shnei* teaches that they must be similar *lechatchila*. The individual use of *keves* throughout the passages, each referring to one sheep separately, teaches that *bedi'avad* even non-matching sheep are valid. The potential source of *ikuva* — the reason one might have thought non-matching would be absolutely invalidating — is the word *tihyeh* in the verse *zot tihyeh torat hametzora*, which implies an exact and fixed procedure. Without the individual *keves* form, *tihyeh* would suggest that every detail must be performed precisely as stated, with no room for *bedi'avad* leniency. The individual form overrides this, and the *tihyeh* is then reapplied to all the other requirements of the *metzora* for which no comparable dispensation exists.

### Birds Pattern

The same structure governs the birds of the *metzora* ritual. *Tziporim* in the plural implies a minimum of two; the word *shtei* (two) teaches they must be similar *lechatchila*. The individual use of *tzipor* — once for the bird that is slaughtered and once for the bird that is sent free — teaches that *bedi'avad* even non-matching birds are valid. Again, the *tihyeh* of *zot tihyeh torat hametzora* would have imposed an absolute requirement, but the individual form of *tzipor* provides the *bedi'avad* dispensation, and *tihyeh* is redirected to the other requirements of the *metzora*.

### Tamidim No Match

The *gemara* then challenges this pattern by asking whether it should apply equally to the *tamidim*, the daily offerings. The verse says *shnayim layom* — two a day — and one might apply the same reasoning: the word *shnayim* teaches that they should be similar *lechatchila*, and the individual use of *keves* throughout teaches *bedi'avad* validity. If so, the *tamidim* should also require matching in appearance. Yet there is no evidence anywhere in rabbinic literature that the two daily *tamidim* must match, and indeed the tradition never imposes such a requirement.

### Sun Positioning

The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that *shnayim layom* is not available to teach matching, because it is needed for an entirely different purpose — the *baraita* of *keneged hayom*, "opposite the sun." The morning *tamid* was slaughtered on the north-west corner of the *azarah* at the second ring (*taba'at shniya*), and the afternoon *tamid* on the north-east corner at the second ring. This positioning ensured that each *korban* was placed to receive the maximum available sunlight given the architecture of the *Mikdash*: in the morning the sun rises in the east, and the north-west position, furthest from the eastern wall, captures the most light from above; in the afternoon the sun has moved west, and the north-east position similarly optimizes. Why the second ring? Tosafot explains that using the first ring risked the animal soiling the area near the *mizbe'ach*; Rashi explains that the *kohen* used the second row of rings for support while positioning the animal at the first.

Since *shnayim layom* is entirely dedicated to teaching this spatial principle of *keneged hayom*, it cannot simultaneously teach the matching requirement, and therefore the *tamidim* never enter the framework of required similarity at all.

### Shabbat Matching

The *gemara* concludes by noting one exception. The two lambs brought as the *musaf* of Shabbat — the additional offering of the day — do require matching, because the word *shnayim* in that context functions as an *addition* to the standard daily offering rather than as a description of the two *tamidim* themselves. In that capacity it can bear the weight of teaching the matching requirement, just as *shnei* does for the goats and the sheep of the *metzora*. Whether non-matching *bedi'avad* is acceptable for the Shabbat lambs is a question that Rashi and Tosafot address: presumably yes, since no source ever declares them invalid *bedi'avad*, though the *gemara* does not spell out the mechanism for this explicitly.

At the conclusion of this analysis it becomes clear that the matching requirement is not unique to Yom Kippur. It applies to the goats of Yom Kippur, the sheep of the *metzora*, the birds of the *metzora*, and the Shabbat *musaf* lambs. What remains uniquely stringent by the goats of Yom Kippur is not the bare existence of the matching requirement but its scope: only there does the Torah demand similarity in *mareh*, *komah*, *damim*, and *likchiatan k'echad* simultaneously. All those dimensions of equivalence, derived from the multiple instances of *shnei* in the Yom Kippur passages, remain a singular intensification that sets the *shnei se'irei Yom HaKippurim* apart from all other paired offerings.
