# Bava Kamma 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbah vs. Rav Yosef

### Ye'ush Acquisition

The daf opens in the midst of a *machlokes* between Rabbah and Rav Yosef on whether *ye'ush* — the despair or relinquishment of hope by the original owner — effects a *kinyan* for a *ganav*. Rav Yosef holds that *ye'ush* has no effect whatsoever: the *ganav* does not acquire the stolen object through the owner's despair alone. Rabbah, by contrast, holds that *ye'ush* does create a *kinyan* for the *ganav*. The discussion that preceded this point also touched on *shinui ma'aseh* and *shinui hashem* — physical transformation and change of name — as additional mechanisms that might, independently or in combination with *ye'ush*, effect an acquisition.

### Chametz over Pesach

Rav Yosef, who holds *ye'ush* has no effect, raises a challenge against Rabbah, who holds that it does. The case is *gazal chametz v'avar alav ha-pesach* — someone stole another's *chametz* just before Pesach, and the holiday came and went while the *chametz* remained in the *ganav*'s possession. The relevant background is the rabbinic *knas* governing *chametz she'avar alav ha-pesach*: if one possessed *chametz* throughout Pesach without selling it, declaring it ownerless, or otherwise disposing of it, the *chametz* becomes completely worthless after the holiday — forbidden for consumption, sale, or any use. The penalty applies regardless of fault.

In this case, as Pesach approaches, the original owner despairs of recovering his stolen *chametz*. He does not want it back; even if it were returned at that moment, it would be of no use. There is *vadai ye'ush* — certain, unambiguous despair. Rav Yosef's challenge runs as follows: if *ye'ush* is *koneh*, then the *ganav* acquired the *chametz* through the owner's despair, and the *ganav* must therefore pay its value — he cannot simply return it with *harei shelcha lefanecha*, "here is yours before you." Yet in practice, the *ganav* can say *harei shelcha lefanecha* and discharge his obligation by returning the object. This suggests that *ye'ush* is not *koneh*, for if it were, the object would have become the *ganav*'s property and he would owe *damim* rather than being permitted to return the thing itself.

### Neither Wants It

Rabbah responds that this case is atypical and cannot serve as a challenge to his general position. In a normal case of theft, he explains, there are effectively two parties to the event: the *ganav* actively wants to possess the stolen item — that is why he stole it — and at some point the owner despairs of recovering it. The *ye'ush* of the owner combines with the desire of the *ganav* to complete the acquisition. The *chametz* case, however, is entirely different. Not only does the owner not want the *chametz* back — it would be useless to him — but the *ganav* himself does not want it either, since possessing *chametz she'avar alav ha-pesach* would render him in violation of a Torah prohibition. Neither party wants the object, and so the normal dynamic of *ye'ush* cannot operate. The case is therefore sui generis and provides no general proof against Rabbah's position.

### Stolen Korban

Abaye raises a separate challenge, this one from the laws of *korbanot*. The verse states *korbano v'lo ha-gazul* — one may only bring his own sacrifice, not a stolen one. The question is: what case does this verse address? If it speaks of a case *lifnei ye'ush* — before the owner has despaired — then it is entirely obvious that a stolen animal cannot be brought as a *korban*. Would God accept such a thing? No verse would be necessary to teach this. The verse must therefore be speaking of a case *achar ye'ush* — after the owner has already despaired. And yet even then, the verse teaches that the *korban* is invalid, implying that *ye'ush* does not effect a *kinyan* and the stolen animal remains the owner's — which is a direct challenge to Rabbah.

### God's Domain

Rava responds on Rabbah's behalf. He points to a parallel formulation: *mishkavo v'lo ha-gazul* — the bed of a *zav* transmits impurity only if it is his own bed, not a stolen one. What case does *this* verse address? If the *ganav* took someone's raw wool and wove it into a blanket — a classic *shinui ma'aseh* — there is no dispute that it is now his; everyone agrees that a real physical transformation acquires the object for the thief. The verse cannot be addressing such a case. Rather, it must be addressing a case where someone stole an actual bed — no transformation at all — and then lay on it in a state of *tumah*. The verse teaches that this does not convey *tumah* to the bed, because the stolen object is not truly *his*. So too with *korbano v'lo ha-gazul*: the verse is not speaking about someone who stole an ordinary *chullin* animal and attempted to bring it as a *korban* after *ye'ush*, but rather about someone who stole a *korban* that was already consecrated and attempted to offer it. A consecrated animal is already, in a sense, in God's domain — *b'gaza deRachmana* — and the concept of *ye'ush* simply does not apply there. God does not relinquish ownership in the manner that a human owner does. These cases therefore tell us nothing about whether *ye'ush* is *koneh* in ordinary circumstances.

## Tumat Orot

### Thief's Machshavah

Having defended Rabbah's position against challenges, the Gemara now turns to challenge Rav Yosef, who holds that *ye'ush* is not *koneh*. Abaye raises a challenge from the laws of *tumah* as they apply to hides — *tumat orot*.

A raw piece of leather is not *mekabel tumah* in its unprocessed state. Whether it becomes susceptible to *tumah* depends on what the owner intends to use it for and how finished it is. The relevant *mishnah* teaches: *oros shel ba'al ha-bayit machshavah metam'asan* — the hides of a private householder: his *machshavah*, his intention, renders them susceptible to *tumah*. If he decides the hide is finished and ready for use, that thought is sufficient. *Veshel avdan ein machshavah metam'asan* — but the hides of a professional tanner: his *machshavah* does not suffice, because the final determination belongs to the customer who will use the hide, not the craftsman who processed it.

Then comes the critical ruling: *shel ganav machshavah metam'asan* — if a *ganav* stole a hide, his *machshavah* does render it susceptible to *tumah*. *Shel gazlan ein machshavah metam'asan* — but a *gazlan*'s *machshavah* does not. The reasoning offered is that a *gazlan* robs openly and is known; the original owner can identify him, pursue him, and may well recover the stolen property. The owner therefore has not despaired, and since *ye'ush* has not occurred, the property has not been acquired by the *gazlan*, and his *machshavah* carries no legal weight. The *ganav*, by contrast, steals secretly; the owner does not know who took the object, despairs of recovering it, and *ye'ush* has occurred.

Rabbi Shimon reverses the ruling: *shel gazlan machshavah metam'asan* — the *gazlan*'s *machshavah* does render it susceptible to *tumah*, because the owner sees how brazen and powerful the robber is and despairs immediately. *Veshel ganav ein machshavah metam'asan* — the *ganav*'s *machshavah* does not, because the owner has not yet despaired; he hopes the investigation will uncover the thief and the object will be recovered.

The challenge to Rav Yosef is clear: according to both the first *tanna* and Rabbi Shimon, *ye'ush* occurs for at least one of the two — *ganav* or *gazlan* — and in that case the thief's *machshavah* is effective. This presupposes that the thief has acquired the object through *ye'ush*, and his intention consequently carries legal force. But Rav Yosef maintains *ye'ush* is not *koneh* at all. *Shema mina ye'ush kaneh* — one ought to conclude from this *mishnah* that *ye'ush* is indeed *koneh*.

### No Trimming Needed

Rav Yosef attempts to deflect the challenge by arguing that the case in the *mishnah* is not one of pure *ye'ush* alone, but involves a *shinui ma'aseh* as well. Specifically, he suggests the case concerns *kitze'an* — the *ganav* trimmed the hide a little. The trimming constitutes a minor act of transformation; combined with *ye'ush*, this is what renders the acquisition effective and the *machshavah* operative.

Rabba bar Rav Chanan objects: the *mishnah* about the hides follows immediately upon a longer discussion involving a specific object, an *itzba* — a type of tray or mat. The earlier *mishnah* states the principle: *kol makom she-ein chisron melachah machshavah metam'asan* — wherever no further work remains to be done on the hide, *machshavah* alone renders it susceptible to *tumah*. *Yesh chisron melachah ein machshavah metam'asan* — if work remains to be done, *machshavah* is insufficient. The exception is an *itzba*, which does not require any further *melachah* even where a *chisron melachah* would otherwise be relevant.

The force of the objection is this: Rav Yosef proposed that the case involves trimming — a minor *shinui ma'aseh* — combined with *ye'ush*. But the preceding *mishnah* teaches that for an *itzba* specifically, no *melachah* at all is needed. The case of the *ganav* is drawn from this context, meaning no trimming is required. *Itzba lo tzericha kitzu'a* — the *itzba* needs no trimming. Therefore, *ye'ush* alone appears to be operative, and Rav Yosef's deflection fails.

### Shinui Hashem

Rava then reports a remarkable detail: this very difficulty had been pressing Rav Yosef for twenty-two years — *esrin ve-tartein shanin* — and he had no answer. Only when Rav Yosef became *Rosh Yeshiva* was he finally able to resolve it, suggesting, as the Gemara implies, that his elevated position brought with it a corresponding elevation in *siyata de-shamaya*.

The answer Rav Yosef ultimately gave is: *shinui hashem ke-shinui ma'aseh dami* — a change of name is legally equivalent to a physical change. The case of the *itzba* may require no trimming, but it does involve a *shinui hashem*. Before the *ganav* stole the hide, it was simply a piece of leather — *mashka*, as the Gemara puts it. Now, in the hands of the *ganav*, it is being used and designated as a tray or mat, and is called something different: *arveza*. This change of designation, even without any physical transformation, constitutes a recognized *shinui*. Combined with *ye'ush*, it is sufficient to render the *ganav* the owner and give his *machshavah* legal effect.

The distinction being drawn is precise. If the *ganav* stole a sheep and it is still the identical sheep two days later — same adult ram, same appearance, nothing done to it — there is neither *shinui ma'aseh* nor *shinui hashem*, and Rav Yosef would say *ye'ush* alone accomplishes nothing. But in the case of the hide, even without physical alteration, the object now bears a different name and serves a different function in the household context. That suffices to qualify as a *shinui*, and it is *ye'ush* together with this *shinui hashem* — not *ye'ush* in isolation — that accomplishes the acquisition. The challenge from the *itzba* therefore does not undermine Rav Yosef's core position, because that case was never a case of pure *ye'ush* to begin with.

## Reversible Shinui

### Stolen Beam Case

Having established that *shinui hashem* functions like *shinui ma'aseh*, the Gemara probes the limits of this principle. If a *shinui hashem* is sufficient to effect acquisition together with *ye'ush*, one would expect it to work consistently. But there is a famous case that creates a difficulty — *mareish ha-gazul she-bano be-vira*: a stolen beam that one has built into the roof of one's house.

The Gemara notes that in this case, before Pesach even enters the picture, there is an apparent *shinui hashem*: what was previously a *keshura* — a beam in its raw state at the lumberyard — is now a *tafala*, a rafter integrated into the roof of a building. That is a change of name and function. And yet the well-known *takanah* — *takanat ha-shavim* — presupposes that absent this special enactment to facilitate repentance, the *ganav* would be required to demolish his house and return the actual beam. The *takanat ha-shavim* is needed precisely because the law would otherwise demand the return of the object itself; we relax that requirement only so as not to discourage *baalei teshuvah* from making restitution at all. This implies that *shinui hashem* alone did not transfer ownership to the *ganav* — the beam still belongs to the original owner — which stands in tension with Rav Yosef's answer that *shinui hashem* suffices.

Rav Yosef responds: the beam has not actually undergone a *shinui hashem*. A beam is still called a beam even when it is installed in a roof. The Gemara supports this from the book of Yechezkel, whose description of construction in the third *Beit HaMikdash* uses the term for beams — *marei shem* — even in the context of a completed structure. So the two by four that was at the lumberyard is still called a beam after it is placed in the roof. The name has not changed. Therefore, the beam case does not challenge the position that *shinui hashem* is effective, because in the beam case there is simply no *shinui hashem* to speak of. The case of the *itzba* and the tray, by contrast, does constitute a genuine change of name.

### Reversible Shinui

Rav Zeira offers an alternative resolution. Rather than arguing that the beam case involves no *shinui hashem* at all, Rav Zeira distinguishes between reversible and irreversible changes of name. The principle he articulates is: *shinui ha-chozer le-veryato be-shinui hashem lo havei shinui* — a *shinui hashem* that can revert to its original state is not a valid *shinui*. In the case of the beam, if the house were to collapse, the beam would simply be the same beam as before. Nothing was done to alter it permanently — it was not dyed, shaped, carved, or molded. It was simply placed in a supporting role. Since the *shinui* is reversible, it is not a legally cognizable *shinui*, and this is why the *takanat ha-shavim* is necessary: without it, the *ganav* would have to return the beam because he never truly acquired it through a reversible change of name. The case of the *itzba* or the tray that is genuinely repurposed and given a new designation, particularly where some work has been done to it, involves an irreversible *shinui hashem* and thus does qualify.

### Pipe Case

The Gemara then tests Rav Zeira's formulation: if a reversible *shinui hashem* does not qualify, is it conversely true that an irreversible *shinui hashem* always does? The Gemara raises the case of a *tzinor* — a wooden pipe. If one takes a block of wood, hollows it out to make a pipe, and then attaches it to the ground so that rainwater flows through it into a pit serving as a *mikveh*, the pipe is *posel* — it invalidates the *mikveh* — because the water passes through a vessel that carries *torat keli*, the legal status of a vessel, and is thus considered *mayim she'uvim*, drawn water. However, if one first plants the block of wood into the ground and only afterward hollows it out, the resulting pipe does not invalidate the *mikveh*.

The question posed is: if an irreversible *shinui hashem* always effects acquisition — since a hollowed wooden pipe cannot simply un-hollow itself — then in the second case, where the block was first planted in the ground and then hollowed out, there has been an irreversible *shinui hashem* from block to pipe. Should this not also be *posel* the *mikveh*? Why does the order of operations matter?

### Kelli Status

The response distinguishes the two cases not on the basis of *shinui hashem* but on the basis of *torat keli*. The prohibition of *mayim she'uvim* in a *mikveh* is itself rabbinic in origin, and even within that rabbinic framework, the determinative question is whether the object had the status of a *keli* at the relevant moment. In the first case — where the block was hollowed out first and then attached to the ground — before it was affixed to the ground it was already a *keli*, a completed vessel. Water flowing through a *keli* is *she'uvin*. In the second case — where the block was first planted into the ground and only then hollowed out — at the moment of affixing it to the ground, it was still just a block of wood, not a *keli*. It never had *torat keli* at the time it was attached, and therefore the water flowing through it is not considered *she'uvin*.

The upshot is that the *tzinor* case is resolved not by challenging or refining the principle of *shinui hashem*, but by reference to the independent legal category of *keli* status. The position of Rav Yosef remains intact: a case of pure *ye'ush* alone does not transfer ownership, but *ye'ush* combined with a genuine *shinui* — whether *shinui ma'aseh* or an irreversible *shinui hashem* — is effective. The challenges raised from these various cases have all been addressed, and the Gemara affirms that no one has yet mounted a successful challenge against the core of Rav Yosef's position.

## Consecration Cases

### Thief Consecrates

The Gemara raises another potential challenge to Rav Yosef. The *mishnah* states: *ha-ganav ve-ha-gazlan ve-ha-annasin — hikdishan hekdesh, u-terumatan terumah, u-ma'aseratan ma'aser* — a thief, a robber, or one who takes by force: if they consecrate stolen property, it is *hekdesh*; if they separate *terumah* from it, it is valid *terumah*; if they separate *ma'aser*, it is valid *ma'aser*. Prima facie, this is a challenge to Rav Yosef: if *ye'ush* is not *koneh*, how can the *ganav* consecrate or separate *terumah* or *ma'aser* from property that is not his?

### Status Change

Rav Yosef answers: these cases all involve a *shinui hashem*. When one steals *tevel* — untithed produce — and separates *terumah* from it, the *tevel* before separation is called *tevel*, and afterward it is called *terumah*. It is a different legal category entirely, governed by its own rules of sanctity and restriction. When one steals an ordinary *chullin* animal and consecrates it as a *korban*, the animal was *chullin* before and is *hekdesh* afterward — a genuine and irreversible *shinui hashem*. These are therefore not cases of pure *ye'ush*; they are cases of *ye'ush* combined with a *shinui hashem*. Rav Yosef's position remains that in a case of pure *ye'ush* without any *shinui* whatsoever, no acquisition takes place.

## Rav Yonatan Teachings

### Shinui Koneh

The Gemara now presents two teachings transmitted in the name of Rav Chisda in the name of Rav Yonatan, which are striking because they are diametrically opposed — and both are derived from the very same verse.

The first teaching: *minayin le-shinui shehu koneh* — from where do we know that *shinui* effects acquisition for a thief? The source is the verse *ve-heshiv et ha-gezelah asher gazal* — he shall return the stolen object which he stole. The question is: what does the phrase *asher gazal* add? It would seem self-evident that one returns the stolen object that one stole rather than something else. The answer is that the verse is establishing a condition: only if the object remains in the state in which it was stolen — *ke-ein she-gazal* — must it be returned in kind. If not — *ve-im lav* — monetary compensation suffices. The verse is thus teaching that *shinui* creates a new legal reality; the object need not be returned because the *shinui* has transferred ownership, and the *nigzal* receives *damim* instead.

### Shinui Lo Koneh

The second teaching: *minayin le-shinui she-eino koneh* — from where do we know that *shinui* does not effect acquisition? Also from the same verse: *ve-heshiv et ha-gezelah* — he returns *the* stolen object, in any condition, regardless of transformation. The *ganav* must always return the object itself because *shinui* carries no acquisitive power. The phrase *asher gazal* in this reading is not limiting the return to cases of unchanged objects; rather, it is the source for the law that the heirs of a thief who return stolen property on behalf of their deceased father are not required to add the *chomesh* — the one-fifth surcharge — that the father himself would have owed upon voluntary confession. The verse thus exclusively informs the *chomesh* law, leaving *ve-heshiv et ha-gezelah* to teach an unrestricted obligation to return the stolen object. As the Gemara notes, both sets of laws can be derived simultaneously from the two clauses of the verse, depending on which reading one adopts.

### Ye'ush Irrelevant

Ulla now advances an independent proof that *ye'ush* is not *koneh*. He derives it from the verse: *vahasatem gazul ve-et ha-pisach ve-et ha-choleh* — you bring before God that which is stolen, lame, and sick. God rejects all of these as unfit *korbanot*. Ulla argues that *gazul* must be understood *dumya de-pisach* — by analogy to *pisach*, the lame animal. A lame animal has no remedy; as long as it is lame, it cannot be brought as a *korban*, regardless of any circumstance. So too a stolen animal: it cannot be brought as a *korban* regardless of circumstances — *velo shna lifnei ye'ush velo shna le-achar ye'ush*. Whether *ye'ush* has occurred or not makes no difference. The stolen animal can never be offered, which implies that *ye'ush* does not transfer ownership.

### Rava Counter

Rava articulates a competing position from a different verse: *korbano v'lo ha-gazul* — his offering, but not a stolen one. The question is again what case the verse is addressing. If it speaks of a case before *ye'ush*, it is entirely obvious — no verse is needed. The verse must therefore be addressing a case after *ye'ush*. And from the fact that even after *ye'ush* the stolen animal cannot be offered, one derives: *shma mina ye'ush lo kani* — *ye'ush* does not effect a *kinyan*.

One might ask: did not Rava earlier explain *korbano v'lo ha-gazul* differently — as referring specifically to a case where the *ganav* stole a consecrated animal (*korban dechavreih*) rather than a *chullin* animal that he later tried to consecrate? The Gemara acknowledges this tension and resolves it either by saying that Rava retracted his earlier position — *hadar beih* — or that one of these two contrary opinions attributed to Rava was in fact stated by Rav Pappa. In any event, what the Gemara is establishing through these latter two teachings is a reinforcement of the view that *ye'ush* is not *koneh*.

## Arba'ah V'Chamishah

### Only Shor/Seh

The *mishnah* states that the law of multiple payments — *tashlumei arba'ah va-chamishah*, paying four or five times the value — applies exclusively to an *ox* and a *sheep*, and to no other animal. One who steals a donkey, a goat, or any other animal and slaughters or sells it does not incur these multiplied payments. The question is why the law is restricted in this way.

One might have thought to derive a broader principle by analogy to *Shabbat*. The verse in *Shabbat* mentions *shorcha va-chamorcha* — your ox and your donkey — but the *Shabbat* prohibition of labor extends to all animals. Similarly, the verse here mentions *shor o seh* — ox or sheep — and perhaps the law of multiple payments should extend to all animals by the same reasoning. Why is *arba'ah va-chamishah* restricted to these two?

### Mentioned Twice

Rabbah offers the textual basis for the restriction. The Torah writes *shor* and *seh* twice in the passage governing these payments. The verse begins: *ki yignov ish shor o seh* — if a man steals an ox or a sheep — and then concludes: *chamisha bakar yeshalem tachat ha-shor ve-arba tzon tachat ha-seh* — he shall pay five cattle in place of the ox and four sheep in place of the sheep. Both the beginning and the end of the *pasuk* specify *shor* and *seh*. *Shor va-seh in, midi acharina lo* — ox and sheep yes, anything else no. The doubling is legally significant; it functions as a limitation.

### Pasuk Analysis

The Gemara does not simply accept this argument without scrutiny. If the verse could have been written differently and still conveyed the same laws, then the repetition of *shor* and *seh* is not truly significant — it is merely the most natural way to write the verse, and one cannot derive a limiting principle from it. The Gemara therefore examines whether an alternative formulation would have been both linguistically and legally sufficient.

The Gemara first asks: could the Torah have omitted the second mention of *shor* and *seh* and written simply *chamisha bakar yeshalem tachtav ve-arba tzon tachtav* — five cattle in its place and four sheep in its place — using the pronoun *tachtav* rather than naming the animal again? The answer is no, because *tachtav* referring back to an earlier mention of both *shor* and *seh* would be ambiguous: one might think that for either a stolen *shor* or a stolen *seh* one must pay both five cattle and four sheep — a total of nine — rather than understanding that five applies to the *shor* case and four to the *seh* case separately. Although the Gemara considers whether using *tachtav* twice might solve this ambiguity, it concludes that such a double usage of *tachtav* would itself be needed for a separate *derashah* — teaching that the payments must be made with quality animals, not inferior ones. Thus the end of the verse cannot be reformulated, and the repetition of *shor* and *seh* there is required.

The Gemara then asks: could the Torah have omitted the first mention of *shor o seh* and begun the verse as *ki yignov ish* without specifying the animal, letting the end of the verse clarify what was stolen? The answer is again no: without the opening *shor o seh* and its disjunctive *o* — "or" — one might have thought the law applies only when one steals both an ox and a sheep together, never when one steals either animal alone. Even if one would not think one must slaughter both or sell both, there remains a plausible reading that one must steal both before the multiple-payment law is triggered. The opening specification of *shor o seh* is therefore also necessary.

### Exclusion Proven

Since both the opening *shor o seh* and the concluding *tachat ha-shor... tachat ha-seh* are each independently required to prevent misreadings, neither is simply redundant. The Gemara ultimately concludes that the Torah could alternatively have written the verse using the opening *shor o seh* together with *tachtav* at the end, dispensing with the specific mention of *shor* and *seh* in the concluding clause, and the law would have been adequately conveyed. The fact that the Torah instead chose to repeat *shor* and *seh* explicitly in the concluding clause — beyond what was strictly necessary — constitutes the basis for the limiting *derashah*: *shor va-seh in, midi acharina lo* — only an ox and a sheep incur the multiple payments; no other animal does. Whether one finds the *derashah* fully compelling or not, the underlying *halacha* is clear: *arba'ah va-chamishah* is categorically restricted to *shor* and *seh* alone.
