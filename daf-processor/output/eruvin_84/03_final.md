# Eruvin 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָזֶה בְּפֶתַח וְלָזֶה בְּפֶתַח — הַיְינוּ חַלּוֹן שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת. לָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה וְלָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה — הַיְינוּ כּוֹתֶל שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת. לָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל וְלָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל — הַיְינוּ חָרִיץ שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** It is similarly obvious that if a place can be used by the residents **of this** courtyard only **by throwing** an object onto it **and** by the residents **of that** courtyard only **by throwing,** but it cannot be conveniently used by either set of residents, then **this is** equivalent to the case of **a wall between two courtyards.** If there is a wall between two courtyards, it may not be used by either courtyard. Likewise, if a place can be used by the residents **of this** courtyard only **by lowering** an object down to it **and** by the residents **of that** courtyard **by** a similar act of **lowering, this is** comparable to the *halakha* of **a ditch between two courtyards,** which may not be used by the residents of either courtyard.

The entirety of Eruvin 84a is devoted to working out the debate between Rav and Shmuel, which had been introduced in the preceding daf. The underlying question concerns competing access to a shared space by two different domains. The Gemara had established three basic configurations. First, when both parties have equally easy access — *lazeh bapetach u'lazeh bapetach* — the object or space in question is considered jointly relevant to both domains, and without an *eruv*, neither party may use it. Second, when both parties have equally difficult access — whether *lazeh bizrika u'lazeh bizrika* (both must throw upward) or *lazeh beshilshul u'lazeh beshilshul* (both must lower downward), also expressed as *lazeh bekasha u'lazeh bekasha* — the same principle applies: equal difficulty means equal status, and the space remains forbidden to both. Third, when access is easy for one party and difficult for the other, the space is attributed to the party with easy access.

### Shmuel's Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל וְלָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה, מַאי? אָמַר רַב: שְׁנֵיהֶן אֲסוּרִין. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: נוֹתְנִין אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁבְּשִׁלְשׁוּל. שֶׁלָּזֶה תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּנַחַת, וְלָזֶה תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּקָשֶׁה, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ לָזֶה בְּנַחַת וְלָזֶה בְּקָשֶׁה — נוֹתְנִים אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּנַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The ruling in each of the aforementioned cases is clear. **What** is the *halakha* concerning a place that can be used by the residents **of this** courtyard only **by lowering** an object down to it **and by** the residents **of that** courtyard only **by throwing** an object on top of it? In other words, if an area is lower than one courtyard but higher than the other, so that neither set of residents has convenient access to it, which of them is entitled to use it? **Rav said:** It is **prohibited** for **both** sets of residents to use it. As the use of the area is equally inconvenient to the residents of both courtyards, they retain equal rights to it and render it prohibited for the other group to use. **And Shmuel said:** The use of the area **is granted to those who** can reach it **by lowering, as** it is relatively easy **for them** to lower objects to it, and therefore **its use is** more **convenient;** whereas **for the others,** who must throw onto it, **its use is** more **demanding. And** there is a principle concerning Shabbat: **Anything whose use is convenient for one** party **and** more **demanding for another** party, **one provides it to that** one **whose use** of it **is convenient.**

The debate between Rav and Shmuel concerns a fourth configuration: what happens when both parties have difficult access, but the difficulty is of different kinds? Specifically, when one party's access requires *zrika* (throwing upward) and the other's requires *shilshul* (lowering downward). Shmuel holds that *shilshul* — lowering something down, or bending down into a depression — represents easier access than *zrika*. Accordingly, when access to a shared space is difficult in both directions, but one direction is downward and the other is upward, Shmuel attributes the space to the party for whom the approach is downward. It should be noted that while the height under discussion is ten *tefachim* — a perfectly manageable vertical distance when approached from above — Shmuel's reasoning turns on the comparative ease of the downward direction rather than on any absolute measure.

### Rav's Equal Status

Rav disagrees. In his view, when both parties face difficult access — regardless of whether one goes up and the other goes down — they are considered equally situated with respect to the space. Neither side has a dispositive advantage, and the space is therefore forbidden to both, just as in the case of equally easy access. The remainder of the daf consists of the Gemara's sustained effort to resolve the tension between this dispute and various cases in the *Mishnah*, testing whether those cases support Rav or Shmuel.

## Mound Case Analysis

### Initial Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר וְאַנְשֵׁי מִרְפֶּסֶת שֶׁשָּׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ, כָּל שֶׁגָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — לַמִּרְפֶּסֶת, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — לֶחָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** In order to decide between these two opinions, the Gemara attempts to adduce a proof from the mishna: If **the residents of** houses that open directly into **a courtyard and the residents of** apartments that open onto **a balcony** from which stairs lead down to that courtyard **forgot and did not establish an *eiruv*** between them, **anything** in the courtyard **that is ten handbreadths high** belongs **to the balcony,** while anything that is **less than this** height belongs **to the courtyard.**

The Gemara opens with a challenge from the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* describes a mound (*tel*) situated in a courtyard (*chatzer*), with a *mirpeset* — a balcony or elevated porch — positioned above. The *Mishnah* rules that if the mound is ten *tefachim* or higher, it belongs to the people of the *mirpeset*; if it is less than ten, it belongs to the *chatzer*. The Gemara asks: does this not constitute a case of competing difficult access — the *chatzer* residents must climb up to reach the top of the mound, while the *mirpeset* residents must descend — and yet the *Mishnah* assigns the mound to those above? Does this not vindicate Shmuel, who gives preference to the party whose access is downward (*shilshul*)?

### Rav's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, מַאי מִרְפֶּסֶת — בְּנֵי עֲלִיָּיה. וּמַאי קָרוּ לַהּ מִרְפֶּסֶת? דְּקָסָלְקִי בְּמִרְפֶּסֶת, אַלְמָא: כָּל לָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל וְלָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה — נוֹתְנִין אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁבְּשִׁלְשׁוּל! כִּדְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לְאוֹתָן הַדָּרִים בַּמִּרְפֶּסֶת. הָכִי נָמֵי: לְאוֹתָן הַדָּרִין בַּמִּרְפֶּסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first explains: **It might have entered your mind** to say: **What is** the meaning of the **balcony** mentioned in the mishna? It is referring to **the residents of an upper story** above the balcony; **and** if so, **why do we call** the upper story **a balcony?** Because the residents of the upper story **ascend** and descend to and from their apartments **by** way of **the balcony. From here** the Gemara infers: With regard to **any** place **that** can be used **by one** set of residents only **by lowering** an object down to it **and by another** set of residents only **by throwing** an object on top of it, **we grant** Shabbat use of **it to those who** can use it **by lowering,** as the residents of the upper story who use the area ten handbreadths high do so by means of lowering. Apparently, the mishna supports Shmuel and presents a difficulty to Rav. The Gemara rejects this argument: **As Rav Huna said** with regard to a different issue discussed in a subsequent mishna, that the *tanna* is referring **to those who live in** apartments that open directly onto **the balcony** rather than those who live in an upper story; **here too,** the *tanna* is speaking **of those who live in** apartments that open directly onto **the balcony.** In this case, the use of an area ten handbreadths high is convenient for the residents of the balcony, as it is on their level; whereas its use is relatively inconvenient for the residents of the courtyard. Consequently, the right to use this area is granted to the residents of the balcony.

Rav's response reframes the scenario entirely. The *Mishnah*, Rav explains, is not describing the situation of people living one full story above in an *aliyah* (upper story), for whom the descent to the mound would be considerable. Rather, we are speaking of people who live on the *mirpeset* itself. For them, the mound is within ten *tefachim* of their position — perhaps only nine *tefachim* away. That proximity constitutes easy access. The mound is therefore attributed to the *mirpeset* residents not because of a preference for downward access, but because for them the access is formally easy, falling within the ten-*tefach* threshold. This reading eliminates any proof for Shmuel from the *Mishnah*.

### Seifa Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — לֶחָצֵר. אַמַּאי? לָזֶה בְּפֶתַח וְלָזֶה בְּפֶתַח הוּא! מַאי ״לֶחָצֵר״? אַף לֶחָצֵר. וּשְׁנֵיהֶן אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **If so, say the next clause** of the mishna: Anything that is **lower than this,** i.e., lower than ten handbreadths, its use belongs **to the courtyard.** But **why** should this be the *halakha*? This is similar to a case of residents of two courtyards who have equally convenient access to a certain area. The residents **of this** courtyard access the area **through** one **entrance, and** the residents **of that** courtyard access the area **through** another **entrance.** In our case, the use of the area is equally convenient for the inhabitants of both the balcony and the courtyard; why should the latter be granted exclusive right of use? The Gemara answers: **What is** the meaning of the phrase **to the courtyard?** It means **also to the courtyard.** In other words, even the residents of the courtyard can make use of this mound or post, **and** therefore residents of **both** the courtyard and the balcony **are prohibited.**

The Gemara immediately presses on: if Rav's reading is correct, consider the *seifa* — the concluding clause of the *Mishnah*, which states that if the mound is less than ten *tefachim*, it belongs to the *chatzer*. The Gemara asks: *amai* — why? If the *mirpeset* residents are close to the mound and enjoy easy access, and if the mound is now lower than ten, then the *chatzer* residents also enjoy easy access. This creates a case of equally easy access for both parties, in which case both should be forbidden the use of the mound — not just granted to the *chatzer*. The Gemara resolves this by reading the *Mishnah*'s phrase *lachatzer* not as "exclusively to the *chatzer*" but as *af lachatzer* — "even to the *chatzer*." When the mound is lower than ten, it falls within easy access of the *mirpeset* residents (for whom it was always accessible) and now also of the *chatzer* residents. Both parties have easy access, and therefore neither may use it without an *eruv*: *ushnayhem asurim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים בִּסְמוּכָה. אֲבָל בְּמוּפְלֶגֶת, אֲפִילּוּ גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — לֶחָצֵר. מַאי ״לֶחָצֵר״? אִילֵּימָא: לֶחָצֵר וּשְׁרֵי, אַמַּאי? רְשׁוּתָא דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ הוּא! אֶלָּא מַאי ״לֶחָצֵר״ — אַף לֶחָצֵר, וּשְׁנֵיהֶן אֲסוּרִין. הָכִי נָמֵי: מַאי ״לֶחָצֵר״ — אַף לֶחָצֵר, וּשְׁנֵיהֶן אֲסוּרִין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** to explain the mishna in this manner, **as it was taught in the latter clause** of the mishna: **In what case is this statement said? When** the mound or embankment is **near** the balcony; **but in** a case where it is **distant** from it, **even** if it is **ten handbreadths high,** its use belongs **to the courtyard. What,** then, **is** the meaning of the phrase **to the courtyard** in this context? **If you say** it means **to the** residents of **courtyard, and** therefore the use of the mound or embankment **is permitted** to them, **why** should this be so? **It is the domain of** the residents of **both** the courtyard and the balcony, as the mound or embankment is positioned near enough to the balcony for its residents to use it as well. **Rather, what is** the meaning of the phrase **to the courtyard?** It means **also to the courtyard. And,** consequently, as the residents of both the courtyard and the balcony can use it, **both are prohibited** to carry there on Shabbat. **Here too,** in the earlier part of the mishna, **what is** the meaning of the clause **to the courtyard?** It likewise means **also to the courtyard, and** therefore **both** sets of residents are **prohibited** to carry. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that this is the correct interpretation of this phrase.

The Gemara adds that this reading is linguistically supported by the *seifa* of the *Mishnah*'s later clause concerning the *mupleget* case, where the same logic of *af lachatzer* applies. This parallel confirms Rav's interpretive approach.

### Mupleget Distance

A further distinction arises from the *Mishnah*'s teaching that when the mound is *mupleget* — set at a distance of more than four *tefachim* from the *mirpeset* — even if the mound is ten *tefachim* high, it belongs to the *chatzer*. The Gemara raises the obvious difficulty: if the mound is very high, it is difficult for the *chatzer* residents to climb it, and if it is far from the *mirpeset*, it is also difficult for the *mirpeset* residents to reach it. Why should the mere fact of distance beyond four *tefachim* automatically consign the mound to the *chatzer*, even when it stands, say, a hundred *amot* high? In such a case, the access seems equally difficult for both parties, and Rav would say they are equal — and therefore both should be forbidden.

The Gemara addresses this by once again applying the reading *af lachatzer*: when the *Mishnah* says the mound belongs to the *chatzer* in the *mupleget* case, it means it belongs *even* to the *chatzer* — i.e., since the *mirpeset* residents can no longer be said to have easy access (they must traverse a gap exceeding four *tefachim* and then climb), and the *chatzer* residents are always within reach of the base, the mound is equally difficult for both, and therefore *asur* to both. The *Mishnah*'s language is consistently read through this lens: attribution to the *chatzer* signals a state of mutual forbiddenness, not exclusive ownership.

## Well Case

### Well Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: חוּלְיַית הַבּוֹר וְהַסֶּלַע שֶׁהֵן גְּבוֹהִין עֲשָׂרָה — לַמִּרְפֶּסֶת, פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן — לֶחָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to adduce further proof from the mishna to resolve the dispute between Rav and Shmuel. **We learned** in the mishna: **The embankments** that surround **a cistern or a rock that are ten handbreadths high** may be used **by the balcony;** if they are **lower than that** height, the right to use them belongs **to the courtyard.**

The Gemara now turns to the *Mishnah*'s next clause: *chulyat habor v'hasela* — the mound of earth surrounding a well (*bor*) and a bare rock (*sela*). The *Mishnah* applies the same basic rule: if these are ten *tefachim* or higher, they belong to the *mirpeset*; if less, to the *chatzer*. The case of the simple *sela* (rock) poses no new difficulty — it follows the same logic as the mound case already analyzed. But the well introduces a genuine complication.

### Gemara Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לְאוֹתָן הַדָּרִים בַּמִּרְפֶּסֶת. תִּינַח סֶלַע. בּוֹר מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara assumes that the phrase to the balcony is referring to the residents of an upper story, who access their apartments through the balcony. The mishna indicates that if one set of residents can make use of a place by lowering and another set of residents can use it by throwing, the use of the place is granted to those who lower their objects, in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel and contrary to the opinion of Rav. The Gemara answers: **Rav Huna said** that the phrase to the balcony is to be understood here literally as referring **to those who live in** apartments that open directly onto **the balcony.** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** in the case of **a rock,** the residents of the balcony can use it conveniently, as its surface is more or less level with the balcony itself. But with regard to **a cistern, what can be said?** The water in the cistern is lower than the balcony and can be reached only by lowering a bucket down to it. How, then, can it be argued that the cistern is conveniently used by the residents of the balcony but not by the residents of the courtyard?

Pictured concretely: there is a well sunk into the ground, surrounded by a raised earthen wall (the *chulyah*). The *chatzer* residents on the ground floor wish not only to stand on top of the mound around the well, but more practically to draw water from the well's depth. For the *mirpeset* residents, reaching the water requires only a downward motion — lowering a bucket. For the *chatzer* residents, reaching the water requires going *up* over the mound and then *down* into the well: both *zrika* and *shilshul* combined. Shmuel has no difficulty here: he always prefers the party whose access is purely downward, so the well belongs to the *mirpeset* residents. But for Rav, the question is acute. Rav's entire justification for assigning the mound to the *mirpeset* in the earlier case was that the top of the mound was within ten *tefachim* of the *mirpeset* residents — that is, it was easy access for them. But the relevant access here is to the water at the bottom of the well, which lies more than ten *tefachim* below the *mirpeset* and more than ten *tefachim* below the *chatzer*. By Rav's logic, access is equally difficult for both, and the well should be *asur* to both — a difficult conclusion when one party may have constructed and relies upon the well.

### Rav Huna's Answer

Rav Huna clarifies a preliminary point that parallels the mound discussion. When the *Mishnah* says the mound or well belongs to the *mirpeset*, it does not mean the people of the *aliyah* — residents of a full upper story, who would be far above. Rather, *mirpeset* refers specifically to those who live on the balcony level itself, for whom the distance to the top of the surrounding mound remains within ten *tefachim*.

### Filled Well

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה: הָכָא בְּבוֹר מְלֵאָה מַיִם עָסְקִינַן. וְהָא חָסְרָא! כֵּיוָן דְּכִי מַלְיָא שַׁרְיָא — כִּי חָסְרָא נָמֵי שַׁרְיָא. אַדְּרַבָּה, כֵּיוָן דְּכִי חָסְרָא אֲסִירָא — כִּי מַלְיָא נָמֵי אֲסִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, said: We are dealing here with a cistern full of water,** as the water can be drawn from the cistern's upper portion, near the balcony. The Gemara raises an objection: **But doesn't** the cistern gradually **lose** its water as the liquid near the surface is drawn out? Although the water might at first reach the balcony, the water level gradually recedes. Eventually, the only way to reach the water will be by lowering a bucket into the cistern. The Gemara answers: **Since** it **is permitted** to draw water from the cistern **when** it is **full, it is likewise permitted** even **when it is lacking.** The Gemara counters this argument: **On the contrary,** you should say that **since** the cistern **is prohibited when it is lacking, it should likewise be prohibited** even **when it is full.**

Rav Yitzchak son of Rav Yehuda proposes the first resolution: the case in the *Mishnah* concerns a well filled with water — *bor melei'ah mayim*. If the well is completely full to the brim, then reaching the water does not require descending more than ten *tefachim*; the water's surface is effectively at the top of the mound, within easy reach. This restores the scenario to one of straightforward proximity. The Gemara objects: *v'ha chasra* — once people begin drawing the water, the level will drop, and what was once within ten will soon be more than ten below the *mirpeset*. The Gemara replies: *keivan d'ki malya shari, ki chasra nami shari* — since when it is full it is permitted, even when it is not quite full it remains permitted. But the Gemara immediately inverts the reasoning: *aderaba*, one should argue the opposite — *keivan d'ki chasra asira, ki malya nami asira* — since when it is depleted it is problematic, even when it is full it should be treated as problematic from the outset. This objection is not easily dismissed and stands against the water-filled resolution.

### Fruit-Filled Well

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּבוֹר מְלֵיאָה פֵּירוֹת עָסְקִינַן. וְהָא חָסְרִי. בְּטִיבְלָא. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי דּוּמְיָא דְּסֶלַע. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said: Here we are dealing with a cistern full of produce,** as the upper produce is near the balcony. The Gemara raises an objection: **But doesn't** the amount of produce also **diminish,** as the produce is removed, increasing the distance between the pile and the balcony? The Gemara answers: This teaching is referring **to untithed produce,** which one may not tithe on Shabbat. Since this produce may not be used, the height of the pile will remain constant for the duration of Shabbat. The Gemara comments: The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches** the *halakha* of an embankment of a cistern **together with** that of **a rock.** Just as in the case of the rock only the upper surface is used, so too, in the case of the embankment of the cistern, the mishna is referring to the use of the surface of the cistern and not its contents. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from this** that this is the correct explanation.

Abaye offers an alternative: *hacha b'vor melei'ah peiros askinan* — the case concerns a well filled with fruit. Solid fruit filling the well to the top converts what is functionally a hole in the ground into what is effectively a mound: there is no depth to descend into, and access is simply to the flat top surface, identical in character to the *sela* case. The Gemara again raises the same *v'ha chasri* objection — the fruit will eventually be removed, and access will again require descending. Abaye's answer: *b'tivla* — the fruit is *tevel*, untithed produce that is forbidden for consumption on Shabbat. Since the fruit cannot be eaten or removed on Shabbat, it remains in place indefinitely, and the well functions exactly as a mound for these purposes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵא בּוֹר וּלְמָה לִי לְמִיתְנֵא סֶלַע? צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן סֶלַע — דְּלֵיכָּא לְמִיגְזַר, אֲבָל בּוֹר — לִיגְזוֹר זִמְנִין דְּמַלְיָא פֵּירוֹת מְתוּקָּנִין, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if this is indeed correct, and the cistern and rock are similar in all respects, **why do I need** the *tanna* **to state** the case of **a cistern, and why do I need** him **to state** the case of **a rock** as well? The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to teach both cases. **As had** the mishna **taught us** only about **a rock,** one might have said that only a rock may be used by the residents of the balcony, **as there is no** need **to decree** in case its height is diminished. **But** with regard to **a cistern,** perhaps **we should decree** and prohibit its use, as **at times it might be filled with tithed produce,** which may be removed and eaten, thereby diminishing its height. It was therefore **necessary** to teach us that this is not a concern, and a cistern, as well as a rock, may be used by the residents of the balcony.

The Gemara notes *deika nami* — this reading is also supported by the *Mishnah*'s phrasing, which presents the well case in parallel with the *sela*, suggesting a structural similarity. On the surface this parallel actually seems more naturally to favor Shmuel's reading — that both the rock and the well simply go to the *mirpeset* because access is downward. But for Rav, the necessity of presenting both cases is explained as follows: had the *Mishnah* mentioned only the *sela*, one might have thought that a well could never go to the *mirpeset* because one might decree against permitting it — fearing that the well might be filled with proper, permitted fruit (*peiros metukanim*) that residents would remove, thereby eventually lowering the accessible surface below ten *tefachim*. The separate mention of the well teaches that when it is in fact filled with *tevel*, there is no such concern and it is treated exactly like the rock.

## Airspace Division

### Airspace Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר וְאַנְשֵׁי עֲלִיָּיה שֶׁשָּׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ — אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁין בָּעֲשָׂרָה הַתַּחְתּוֹנִים, וְאַנְשֵׁי עֲלִיָּיה מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁין בָּעֲשָׂרָה הָעֶלְיוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Returning to the dispute between Rav and Shmuel, the Gemara suggests a different proof: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: **If the residents of** houses opening directly into **a courtyard and the residents of** apartments in **an upper story forgot and did not establish** an ***eiruv*** together, **the residents of the courtyard may use the lower ten** handbreadths of the wall near them, **and the residents of the upper story may use the upper ten** handbreadths adjacent to them.

The Gemara now introduces a new category of proof. The case concerns residents of a *chatzer* and residents of an *aliyah* — a full upper story — who forgot to make an *eruv* together. Despite the absence of an *eruv*, the Gemara maintains that the *chatzer* residents may use the lower ten *tefachim* of the shared airspace, and the *aliyah* residents may use the upper ten *tefachim*. The question the Gemara raises is a practical one: the relevant space is entirely air — how can one "use" airspace in a way that renders these formal divisions operative?

### Ziz Protrusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? זִיז יוֹצֵא מִן הַכּוֹתֶל לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — לֶחָצֵר, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — לָעֲלִיָּיה. הָא דְּבֵינֵי בֵּינֵי — אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If **a ledge protrudes from the wall below ten** handbreadths from the ground, its use is **for** the residents **of the courtyard;** but if it protrudes **above ten** handbreadths, its use is **for** the residents **of the upper story.** The Gemara infers: **Consequently,** a ledge situated **between** this **and between** the other, i.e., in-between the courtyard and the upper story, **is prohibited.**

The Gemara explains by reference to a *ziz* — a protrusion or ledge extending from the shared wall between the two domains. If a *ziz* protrudes from the wall within the lower ten *tefachim* (measured from the ground), it falls within easy access of the *chatzer* residents and belongs to them. If it protrudes above ten *tefachim*, it lies within easy access of the *aliyah* residents and belongs to them. Any protrusion in the middle space — between the lower ten and the upper ten — is *asur* to both. This middle-space ruling is presented as direct proof for Rav: when access is equally difficult for both parties (neither within their respective ten-*tefach* easy-access zone), the Gemara does not give preference to the party who must descend. It simply forbids both, exactly as Rav would predict.

### Rav Nachman's Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הָכָא בְּכוֹתֶל תִּשְׁעָה עָשָׂר עָסְקִינַן, וְזִיז יוֹצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ. לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — לָזֶה בְּפֶתַח, וְלָזֶה בְּשִׁלְשׁוּל. לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה — לָזֶה בְּפֶתַח, וְלָזֶה בִּזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** No proof can be adduced from this teaching, as **here we are dealing with a wall of nineteen** handbreadths **that has a protruding ledge.** If the ledge protrudes **below ten** handbreadths from the ground, **for this** set of residents, those of the courtyard, it can be used **as an entrance, and for that** set of residents, those of the upper story, it can be used only **by lowering.** If the ledge protrudes **above ten** handbreadths, **for this** set of residents, those in the balcony, it can be accessed **as an entrance, and for that** set of residents, those of the courtyard, it can be used only **by throwing.** In this case, there is no middle area between the ten-handbreadths available to each set of residents. Consequently, this case cannot serve as a proof with regard to the dispute between Rav and Shmuel.

The Gemara, sensitive to the obvious implication of the proof — that there appears to be a "middle" zone inaccessible to either party — introduces Rav Nachman's clarification. He states: *hacha b'kotel tish'asar askinan* — the case concerns a wall that is exactly nineteen *tefachim* high. With such a wall, there is no middle space between the lower ten and the upper nine. Any point on the wall is either within the lower ten (easy access for the *chatzer*, difficult for the *aliyah*) or within the upper nine (easy access for the *aliyah*, difficult for the *chatzer*). The *ziz* at any given point therefore always sits squarely in one domain's easy-access zone and the other domain's difficult-access zone, cleanly assigning it to one party rather than creating a contested middle ground. The division is exhaustive: below ten belongs to the *chatzer*; above ten — which in a nineteen-*tefach* wall means within nine *tefachim* of the top — belongs to the *aliyah*. There is no gap.

## Two Gazuztraot

### Gazuztraot Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁתֵּי גְזוּזְטְרָאוֹת זוֹ לְמַעְלָה מִזּוֹ. עָשׂוּ לָעֶלְיוֹנָה, וְלֹא עָשׂוּ לַתַּחְתּוֹנָה — שְׁתֵּיהֶן אֲסוּרוֹת עַד שֶׁיְּעָרְבוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to cite yet another proof to resolve the dispute between Rav and Shmuel. **Come** and **hear** a mishna: If a balcony extends over a body of water, and the residents of the balcony cut out a hole in the floor and constructed a partition ten handbreadths high around the hole, water may be drawn through the hole on Shabbat. If there are **two balconies** of this kind, **one above the other,** and **they erected** a partition **for the upper** balcony **but they did not erect** one **for the lower one, they are both prohibited** from drawing water, **unless they establish an *eiruv*** between them.

The Gemara continues with another *ta shema* — come and hear — and introduces the case of two *gazuztraot* (ledges or protrusions) extending from different apartments over a body of water. The scenario is that two households, living at different heights, both have ledges projecting outward over the sea or a river. Each household might wish to use the water below — either to draw water or to dispose of waste — and the question is whether the vertical and horizontal configuration of the two ledges creates a situation of competing access, analogous to the *chatzer*-and-*mirpeset* cases discussed throughout the daf.

### Ledges & Partition

The *Mishnah* rules that if a *mechitza* (partition) was made for the upper ledge (*elyonah*) but not for the lower ledge (*tachtonah*), both are *asur* until they make an *eruv* together. This at first appears puzzling: the upper ledge has its partition and is technically set up for proper use; why should the lower ledge's failure to have a partition disable both? The answer lies in the principle of competing access. Even though the upper household has done everything structurally necessary to use the water, the lower ledge is considered to be within the access range of both parties — and since they have not made an *eruv* with one another, the shared access of the lower household disqualifies the upper household's use as well.

### Rava bar Avin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: בְּבָאִין בְּנֵי תַחְתּוֹנָה דֶּרֶךְ עֶלְיוֹנָה לְמַלּאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** Here we are dealing with a case **where the residents** of the **lower** balcony **go** up **to the upper** balcony by means of a ladder **to draw** their water from there.

Rava bar Avin addresses a subtlety in the case. He explains: *bevo'im bnei tachtonah derech elyonah lemalaot* — the case is specifically one in which the residents of the lower ledge actually walk up through the upper ledge's space in order to draw water. This is not merely a theoretical claim of access; the lower residents are in practice passing through the upper residents' area to reach the water. This practical overlap of access, Rava bar Avin explains, is what renders both ledges mutually forbidden.

### Abaye's Formal Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּקַיָּימִין בְּתוֹךְ עֲשָׂרָה דַּהֲדָדֵי, וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא עָשׂוּ לַתַּחְתּוֹנָה וְלֹא עָשׂוּ לָעֶלְיוֹנָה — דַּאֲסִירִי, דְּכֵיוָן דִּבְגוֹ עֲשָׂרָה דַּהֲדָדֵי קַיָּימִין — אָסְרָן אַהֲדָדֵי, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ עָשׂוּ לָעֶלְיוֹנָה וְלֹא עָשׂוּ לַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כֵּיוָן דְּלָזֶה בְּנַחַת וְלָזֶה בְּקָשֶׁה — לִיתְּבֵיהּ לָזֶה שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּנַחַת, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: כֵּיוָן דִּבְגוֹ עֲשָׂרָה קַיָּימִין — אָסְרָן אַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** Here we are dealing with a case, **where** the two balconies **are situated within ten** handbreadths **of each other, and** the *tanna* **was speaking** in the style of: **There is no need.** In other words, the mishna should be understood in the following manner: **There is no need** to say that if **they erect** a partition **for the lower** balcony **but they did not erect** one **for the upper one, they are** both **prohibited** to draw water. The reason is **that since they are positioned within ten** handbreadths **of each other, they** render it **prohibited for one another** anyway. **Rather,** the *halakha* is the same **even** if **they established** a partition for the **upper** balcony **and they did not establish** a partition **for the lower** one, despite the fact that **it might have entered your mind to say** the following: **Since for this,** the residents of the upper balcony, its use **is convenient, while for that** lower balcony, its use **is demanding,** as the lower balcony can draw water only by hoisting its bucket upward, the use of the hole **should** therefore **be granted to the one whose use is convenient.** This reasoning would render the hole permitted to the upper balcony and prohibited to the lower balcony. To counter this hypothetical argument, the mishna **teaches us** that **since** the upper and lower balconies **are located within ten** handbreadths of each other, they render it **prohibited for one another.**

Abaye offers a different reading that cuts to the heart of the formal-versus-practical debate. He says: *kigon d'kaiman betoch asara dehadadi* — the case concerns two ledges that are within ten *tefachim* of each other. The formal distance between the two ledges is less than ten *tefachim*, and that single fact establishes that they are in each other's domain of easy access. There is no need to invoke actual foot-traffic between the ledges.

Abaye then presents the teaching as a *lo mibaya* — a fortiori — construction. It goes without saying (*lo mibaya*) that if the lower ledge had its partition and not the upper, both would be forbidden, because the access is identical in direction for both parties: both must lower (*shilshul*), and they are within ten of each other. That is the obvious case of equal access. The novel ruling — *afilu asu le'elyona velo asu letachtona* — is that even when the partition was made for the upper ledge and not the lower, both remain forbidden. One might have thought to say that in practice, the lower residents must travel upward slightly to reach the water — they must cross the gap of the ledge heights and then lower — while the upper residents lower directly. This practical advantage ought to favor the upper residents, assigning the water to them exclusively. Abaye teaches that this practical consideration is entirely overridden by the formal rule: *keivan d'begov asara kaiman, asrin ahadadi* — since they are formally within ten *tefachim* of each other, both are treated identically, and neither may use the water without an *eruv*. Whether in practice one party has a somewhat easier approach than the other is irrelevant; formal proximity within ten *tefachim* places both in the same legal category.

This ruling is, as the Gemara implies, a genuinely significant *chidush*. It crystallizes a principle that has been operating implicitly throughout the entire daf: the Gemara does not evaluate access on a case-by-case practical basis. It defines "easy" and "difficult" by reference to a single formal threshold — ten *tefachim* — and applies that threshold uniformly. If two parties are both within ten *tefachim* of a space, they are considered to have equal access, full stop, regardless of whether one's path is in fact more physically demanding than the other's.

## Roof Case

### Roof Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: גַּג הַסָּמוּךְ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — צָרִיךְ סוּלָּם קָבוּעַ לְהַתִּירוֹ. סוּלָּם קָבוּעַ אִין, סוּלָּם עֲרַאי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **similar to** a teaching **that Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said:** In the case of **a roof that is adjacent to a public domain, there must be a fixed ladder** from the courtyard to the roof **to permit** the use of the roof to the residents of the courtyard. The Gemara infers: If there is **a fixed ladder, yes,** the residents of the courtyard may use the roof; if there is merely **a temporary ladder, no,** they are prohibited to use it.

The Gemara now cites a ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel that further illustrates this principle. The case is *gag hasamuch l'reshut harabim* — a roof situated near a public domain (*reshut harabim*), also accessible from an adjacent private courtyard (*chatzer*). The question is whether the roof may be considered part of the *chatzer*'s *reshut hayachid*, allowing the *chatzer* residents to use it on Shabbat, or whether its proximity to the *reshut harabim* creates a competing access that disqualifies it.

### Fixed vs. Temporary

Rav Nachman rules: *tzarich sulam kavua l'hatira* — to render the roof permissible for the *chatzer* residents, a fixed ladder (*sulam kavua*) is required. A fixed ladder, yes; a temporary ladder (*sulam arai*), no. The distinction is sharp and, on first reading, counterintuitive. If anything, a temporary ladder physically present in the *chatzer* actually makes access to the roof easier for the *chatzer* residents — they can climb up whenever they like. Why should the presence of a movable ladder fail to establish the roof as part of the *chatzer*'s domain?

### Formal vs. Practical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּכֵיוָן דִּבְתוֹךְ עֲשָׂרָה דַּהֲדָדֵי קָיְימִי — אָסְרָן אַהֲדָדֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this distinction? Is it **not** that since the balcony and the public domain **are situated within ten** handbreadths **of each other,** the residents of both render it **prohibited for one another,** in accordance with the opinion of Abaye?

The Gemara's answer is precisely the principle Abaye articulated in the *gazuztraot* case: the Gemara does not ask practical questions when the parties fall within the same formal category. The roof is within ten *tefachim* of the *reshut harabim* and is also accessible (via ladder) from the *chatzer*. In formal terms — the only terms the Gemara applies — both parties stand within ten *tefachim* of the roof, placing both in the category of "easy access." At that point, neither party has a dispositive advantage over the other, and without a *sulam kavua* that structurally and permanently anchors the roof to the *chatzer*, the roof is treated as jointly accessible and therefore forbidden to the *chatzer* residents. The temporary ladder makes things practically easier for the *chatzer* residents, but that practical consideration is categorically set aside in favor of the formal rule.

### Rav Papa's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: וְדִילְמָא כְּשֶׁרַבִּים מְכַתְּפִין עָלָיו, בְּכוּמְתָּא וְסוּדָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa** strongly **objected to this** argument, claiming that this proof can be refuted: **But perhaps** this applies only to a roof upon which **many people place their hats [*kumta*] and shawls** when they are in need of rest.

Rav Papa raises an objection to the Gemara's proof structure. The Gemara had cited the *sulam kavua* ruling as a demonstration that the ten-*tefach* formal rule overrides practical assessment. Rav Papa asks: how do we know the ruling is motivated by the formal rule at all? Perhaps the roof is simply easy access for the *reshut harabim* people as well — they lean over, rest packages on the roof, place their hats there (*miktafei alei b'kumsa v'sudra*). If the *reshut harabim* residents genuinely and practically use the roof's surface, then the case isn't about abstract formal rules at all; it is just a straightforward case of practical equal access, which would be forbidden on those grounds alone. The *sulam kavua* ruling would then not prove anything about the primacy of formal over practical reasoning. This objection is left unresolved by the Gemara at this point, and the proof remains less than fully established.

## Formal vs. Practical

### Principle

The objection of Rav Papa does not, however, undermine the broader principle the Gemara has been building toward throughout the daf. The principle itself — that formal legal rules defined by the ten-*tefach* threshold take absolute precedence over case-by-case practical assessment — has been stated and restated in multiple contexts. Whether the question involves a mound within four *tefachim* of a *mirpeset*, two *gazuztraot* within ten *tefachim* of each other, or a roof reachable from both a *chatzer* and a *reshut harabim*, the Gemara consistently applies the same logic: the formal category governs. If two parties are within the same formal threshold, they are treated as equal — even when one's practical access is plainly more convenient than the other's. The Gemara has no interest in adjudicating access empirically. It wants an exact, objective rule.

### Irony

There is an undeniable irony in this approach. The drive toward formal precision — rooted in the Gemara's preference for objective, verifiable legal categories over subjective practical judgments — generates precisely the complexity and apparent arbitrariness that makes these laws so difficult for ordinary people to navigate. As Abaye's ruling makes vivid: even if in practice it is easier for one party to reach the space than for the other, as long as both fall within ten *tefachim*, the law treats them identically and forbids both. A legal system motivated by the desire to provide clear, consistent, and predictable rulings ends up producing intricately differentiated outcomes that depend on measurements down to fractions of a *tefach*. Yet the Gemara's commitment to this methodology is unwavering. Formal rules, however demanding, provide a stable framework; practical assessments, however intuitive, introduce subjectivity that a legal system cannot easily administer. The entirety of Eruvin 84a is, in a sense, a sustained demonstration of that jurisprudential choice.
