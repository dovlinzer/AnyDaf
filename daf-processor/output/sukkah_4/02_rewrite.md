# Sukkah 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Raising the Floor

### Pillows & Blankets

The daf opens two lines from the bottom of 3b, picking up a series of cases relating to the problems of maximum and minimum height in a *sukkah*. Having already explored the various explanations for the twenty-*amah* maximum and the debate over minimum dimensions — whether a *sukkah* must measure *arba amot al arba amot* as Rebbi requires, or merely enough to contain one's body, head, and a small tray-table (*shulchan*) of roughly seven-and-a-half by seven-and-a-half *tefachim* — the *gemara* now turns to practical remedies.

The first case addresses a *sukkah* that is taller than twenty *amot*. Rather than dismantle and rebuild, one might consider raising the floor instead of lowering the ceiling: place pillows and blankets on the floor so that the interior height — measured from the floor to the base of the *schach* — is reduced below twenty *amot*. This approach assumes that height is reckoned as the interior dimension from floor to the underside of the *schach*, and not as an exterior measurement.

The *gemara* rules that pillows and blankets do not constitute a raising of the floor. Even if one formally declares, *"I am nullifying these pillows and blankets as part of my floor; they will remain here for the entire week,"* this declaration is ineffective. The reason is *batla daato etzel kol adam* — one's subjective intention is negated by the standards of ordinary people — and ordinary people do not leave pillows and blankets on the floor for a week. Rashi (on 3b, bottom) states explicitly: *lo hava miut she-ein sofo le-haniach sham kol shiva p'nei hefsed mamono* — it is not considered a reduction because no one genuinely expects to leave such items there for all seven days, given the risk of financial loss to the objects.

### Batla Daato

The principle of *batla daato etzel kol adam* deserves careful attention. Halacha does not always defer to one's private intention; very often it weighs that intention against prevailing norms. One might even press the point further: even if a particular culture regularly leaves floor-cushions in place permanently, such that the factual premise of Rashi's reasoning does not apply, the deeper point remains that pillows on a floor are always perceived as *pillows on a floor* — a distinct layer atop the floor — and never as the floor itself. Their presence does not redefine the structural floor no matter how long they remain.

The principle resonates across many areas of *halacha*. A clear parallel is the law of *chatzitza* in immersion: if a person is *lo makpid* — unconcerned — about an intervening substance on the body, it does not constitute a *chatzitza*. The question immediately arises: what governs — the individual's subjective attitude or the norm of most people? When one's private intention would generate a *kula* that contradicts what ordinary people would do, *halacha* frequently negates that subjective intention. *Batla daato etzel kol adam* is the formal articulation of that principle, though it is not invoked universally and must be applied case by case.

### Straw & Dirt

The *gemara* proceeds to materials that are more naturally suited to becoming part of a floor. If one has straw (*teven*) and actively performs *bitul* — a formal act of nullification, declaring the straw to be incorporated as part of the floor — *havei miyut*, that does work: the floor is considered raised. All the more so with dirt (*afar*): if one is *mevatel* the dirt, *ve-chol sheken*, it certainly becomes part of the floor, since dirt is identical in kind to the floor beneath it.

It is worth noting the structural character of this passage. Rather than the typical format of a *tanna* or *amora* issuing a ruling, the anonymous voice of the *gemara* presents these cases in a more apodictic style. Rashi (second *rashi* from the bottom on 3b) explains: *piskei shemu'ot hen* — these are bottom-line rulings, consolidated traditions. Rashi attributes their formulation to the *benei yeshivah* in the *beit midrash* of Rav Ashi, the editor of the *gemara*, who presented these conclusions in a more direct, non-discursive form.

### Material & Intention

The *gemara* now maps out a continuum of intermediate cases, varying both the quality of the material and the degree of intention. The two variables generate a matrix: at one end, *bitlo* — active, conscious designation of the material as part of the floor; at the other, *atid lifnoto* — a positive plan to remove it. Between them lie two middle positions: *ein da'ato lifnoto*, meaning one has consciously decided not to remove the material, though without actively designating it as floor, and *stam*, meaning one has simply not thought about it at all.

Matched against material type, the combinations work as follows. Pillows and blankets (*karim ve-ksetot*) represent the worst material for floor incorporation: even active *bitul* does not help. Dirt (*afar*) represents the best material, essentially identical to the floor itself. Straw (*teven*) is intermediate — it can plausibly become part of a floor but is not naturally so.

The interesting middle cases are: *afar stam* — dirt with no particular intention either way — and *teven ve-eino atid lifnoto* — straw that one has consciously decided not to remove. The question is whether either of these combinations suffices. Each pairing combines a moderately favorable factor with a moderately favorable intention.

The logic is intuitive: dirt placed on a dirt floor, even without any conscious declaration, might naturally become part of that floor simply by virtue of physical identity. Straw, though not identical to the floor, is a normal floor-covering material; if one has consciously chosen to leave it in place, that degree of intention may be sufficient to bridge the gap.

### Reb Yosei vs. Rabbis

To adjudicate these middle cases, the *gemara* cites a *mishnah* from tractate *Ohalot* — though Tosafot notes that what follows is not literally the text of that *mishnah* but a paraphrase rendered more directly applicable to the present discussion. The context in *Ohalot* is: if a corpse lies in a house and the house is filled with straw (*teven*) or rocks (*tzerot*), and one performs *bitul*, the material is considered incorporated and the *ohel* structure is thereby eliminated, with all the attendant *halachic* consequences for *tumah*.

The *gemara* infers from the formulation *u-vitlo, mevutal* — that active *bitul* is required — that without it, the filling would not be effective: *bitlo in, lo bitlo lo*.

A *baraita* then records the dissent of Rabbi Yosei: *teven ve-eino atid lifnoto harei hu ke-afar stam u-vatel*. According to Rabbi Yosei, straw that one has consciously decided not to remove is equivalent to dirt that one has simply left without any particular intention — and both of these middle cases are *batel*, incorporated as part of the floor. Two favorable middle combinations suffice; one does not need the full force of active *bitul* paired with the most floor-like material.

The *baraita* continues: *afar ve-atid lifnoto harei hu ke-stam teven, ve-lo batel*. If the material is dirt — favorable — but the intention is the least favorable short of pure *stam*, namely one is planning to remove it (*atid lifnoto*), then it is equivalent to having straw with no intention at all (*stam teven*) — and that combination does not work. The lesson is that one may shift either variable one step toward the unfavorable and still succeed (Rabbi Yosei's rule), but shifting either variable two steps, or moving both variables simultaneously in the unfavorable direction, will cause the combination to fail.

## Too High Schach

### Hanging Extensions

The next case involves a different remedy for a *sukkah* too tall by twenty *amot*. The *gemara* states: *hayeta gevoha me-esrim amah ve-hotzi neradim betoch kaf* — the *sukkah* was higher than twenty *amot*, but extensions, hanging twigs or strands (*neradim*), hang down from the *schach* so that their lower ends reach within twenty *amot* of the floor. The question is whether these hanging extensions can be considered the effective bottom of the *schach*, such that the measured interior height — from floor to the underside of the operative *schach* — falls within the permitted range.

### Shade vs. Sunlight

The *halacha* is: *im tzilto merubba me-chamatah, kesherah* — if the hanging extensions themselves provide more shade than sunlight, then they are considered valid *schach* in their own right. Accordingly, it is their lower boundary, not the upper bulk of the *schach*, that defines the operative ceiling, and the *sukkah* is valid. But *ve-im lav, pesulah* — if the extensions do not independently produce more shade than sunlight, they cannot themselves constitute *schach*, and the measurement reverts to the upper layer, which remains above twenty *amot*.

The underlying principle is that the shade-sufficiency test is definitional for *schach*: what makes something operative *schach* is precisely that it provides more shade than it permits sunlight. Extensions that fail this test are not *schach* at all and cannot serve as the referent for measuring interior height.

## Too Low Sukkah

### Abaye's Shade Test

The *gemara* now turns to the inverse problem: a *sukkah* only ten *tefachim* high — the absolute minimum — where strands hang down from the *schach* to within ten *tefachim* of the floor. If those hanging strands are counted as *schach*, the effective ceiling drops below the minimum required height.

*Savar Abaye le-meimar* — Abaye proposed applying the same shade test symmetrically: *im chamtzan merubba me-tzilatan, kesherah* — if the dangling strands produce more sun than shade, they do not count as *schach*, and therefore the height is measured to the upper layer, which remains at exactly ten *tefachim*. The *sukkah* would be valid.

### Rava's Uninhabitability

Rava rejects Abaye's reasoning. *Hadira s'rucha hi* — it is an uninhabitable dwelling. Even if technically the height measured to the upper *schach* is ten *tefachim*, the practical reality is that one cannot inhabit such a space: you are already constrained to lie down in a space only ten *tefachim* high, and now strands of *schach* are hanging into your face. *Ein adam dar be-dira s'rucha* — no person can dwell in a ruined or uninhabitable structure.

Tosafot presses the point from a different angle: even a plain ten-*tefach* space in which one cannot stand upright is already a questionable habitation. The addition of hanging strands makes it completely uninhabitable, and invalidation follows from that ground independently of any measurement concern.

## Platform Solution

### Middle Wall Platform

The *gemara* returns to the problem of excessive height — *hayeta gevoha me-esrim amah* — and introduces the remedy of an *itztaba*, a platform. In the first case, the platform is built opposite the middle wall: *uvanah bah itztaba kineged dofen ha-emtza'i*. The *gemara* operates with the standard assumption that a *sukkah* has three walls. The platform runs the full length of the back wall, and above the platform the height from the platform's surface to the *schach* is less than twenty *amot*. That area above the platform has three walls surrounding it — the back wall and the portions of the two side walls adjacent to it — and its minimum area qualifies as a kosher *sukkah*: *ve-yesh ba hachshar sukkah, kesherah*.

Rashi adds a *chiddush*: not only the space directly above the platform is valid, but the remainder of the *sukkah* outside the platform is also valid. This is a notable claim. The general principle is that when the *schach* of a kosher *sukkah* extends beyond its walls, one may sit under that extended *schach* and be considered part of the kosher *sukkah* — that principle typically applies when the problem is one of walls, not of *schach*. Rashi seems to be reading the problem of excessive height as a wall problem rather than a *schach* problem. If the problem were a *schach* problem, the *schach* outside the platform would itself be invalid, and the rest of the *sukkah* would not be saved. The matter is not straightforward and depends on how one characterizes the underlying defect of a *sukkah* too tall.

### Dofen Akumah Multi-Wall

The more complex case follows: *ubana itztava b'emtza'itah* — the platform is built in the middle of the *sukkah*, not against the back wall. Now the platform is not adjacent to any of the three exterior walls. The area above the platform is at the right height, but it lacks walls on the sides where the walls of the *sukkah* are distant. The *gemara* rules: *im yesh mi-sfat ha-itztava le-dofen arba amot le-chol ruach ve-ruach, pesulah* — if the distance from the edge of the platform to each of the walls exceeds four *amot*, the *sukkah* is invalid. *Pachot me-arba amot, kesherah* — but if in all three wall-directions the gap is less than four *amot*, it is valid.

The operative principle is *dofen akumah*, the bent wall. The mechanism works as follows. Normally, if a section of a roof collapses and is replaced with *schach*, the valid *schach* sits in the middle, flanked on each side by the regular solid roof. If the distance from the actual wall to the edge of the *schach* is less than four *amot*, the *halacha* allows us to conceptually bend the wall: the solid roof section is viewed not as roof but as a continuation of the wall, curving horizontally inward until it meets the *schach*. The *schach* in the middle is thus considered to have proper walls on all sides. The same logic applies to the platform: the distance between the platform's edge and the *sukkah* wall, if less than four *amot*, allows the wall to be considered bent inward at the height of the platform, so that the area above the platform has the wall effectively at its perimeter.

The *gemara* objects — this principle has already been taught in the case of the fallen roof! — and answers: one might have thought the *dofen akumah* principle applies only when the wall is already of valid height and merely too distant from the *schach*. There, bending brings the wall closer but does not change its qualification as a wall. Here, the wall is twenty-one *amot* tall — it is too high to serve as a valid *sukkah* wall where it stands. By bending it and conceptually relocating it to the height of the platform, we are not merely bringing it closer but simultaneously redefining its effective height. One might have thought that *dofen akumah* cannot accomplish so much — that you cannot both reposition and resize a wall through a single fictional operation. The *gemara* teaches that *kamashma lan* — as long as the gap is within four *amot*, both operations are accomplished together.

### Wall Bending Limits

Before proceeding, it is worth situating *dofen akumah* alongside the other major principle of conceptual wall construction, *gud asik mechitzta* — the wall that extends upward. *Gud asik* operates on the vertical plane: a wall that is physically only, say, nine *tefachim* high but qualifies as a halachic wall by other measures can, once it reaches ten *tefachim*, be imagined to continue rising straight up until it meets the *schach* above. *Dofen akumah* operates on the horizontal plane: a wall that is distant from the *schach* horizontally can be imagined to bend inward, with the intervening roof material serving as the bent portion of the wall. In both cases the underlying problem is that the physical wall does not reach the *schach* — one problem is a vertical gap, the other is a horizontal gap. Combining the two principles — using *dofen akumah* horizontally and *gud asik* vertically simultaneously — would be a double fictional operation and is not permitted.

## Digging Pits, Lavud

### Pit Digging

The *gemara* turns to the minimum-height problem: a *sukkah* less than ten *tefachim* tall. The remedy proposed is to dig a pit in the floor — *ve-chakak bah kedei le-hashlima le-asarah* — so that the depth of the pit, combined with the height of the walls above ground, produces an interior height of ten *tefachim* measured from the floor of the pit to the *schach*.

### Lavud vs. Akumah

The analogy to the platform case suggests that the pit must be within four *amot* of the surrounding walls for some bending principle to operate. Remarkably, the *gemara* rules differently: *im yesh mi-sfat ha-chakak le-kotel gimel tefachim, pesulah; pachot mi-shlosha tefachim, kesherah* — if the edge of the pit is three *tefachim* or more from the wall, the *sukkah* is invalid; less than three *tefachim*, it is valid. The relevant threshold is three *tefachim*, not four *amot*.

The *gemara* explains the disparity directly. By the platform case, *hatam de-it lei dofen* — there you already have a wall; the wall exists but is too far away. You invoke *dofen akumah* to bend the existing wall into proximity. Here, *hacha leshavei dofen* — the goal is not to bring an existing wall closer but to constitute the very status of a wall in the first place. A wall of nine *tefachim* is not a halachic wall at all; you cannot bend something that has not yet achieved the minimum qualifications of a *mechitzah*. There is nothing to bend. Similarly, *gud asik* — extending a wall upward — requires that the wall already stand at ten *tefachim*; only from that foundation can the imagination extend it upward. You must start with a wall.

The operative principle instead is *lavud*: within three *tefachim*, things are considered physically contiguous without any fictional operation. *Lavud* does not require a preexisting wall; it does not bend or extend anything. It simply treats any gap of less than three *tefachim* as if it were no gap at all. If the pit floor is within three *tefachim* of the surrounding walls, the pit is considered to extend fully to those walls, producing a valid ten-*tefach* interior height adjacent to them.

### Lavud Principle

Rashi's explanation of the word *lavud* is illuminating. He glosses it as *snif* — an appendage or extension — and compares it to inserting a leaf into a table to lengthen it: *davar she-ma'arichin oto al yedei snifin*, something extended by means of appendages. Concretely: *ke-dasnif chakak al chakak*, it is as if one extended a pit onto a pit. Within three *tefachim*, the object is considered to virtually extend and thus be contiguous with what lies beside it. The principle is not a fiction of the imagination like *dofen akumah* or *gud asik*; it is a more fundamental rule about the threshold below which gaps are legally nonexistent.

### Lavud vs. Shabbat

Tosafot raises a sharp comparative point. In the laws of *Shabbat*, a virtually identical configuration can create a *reshut ha-yachid*: dig a pit in the ground, the walls around the pit (measuring their depth from their base to the ground level above) combine with the pit's depth to reach ten *tefachim*. Yet for *Shabbat*, Tosafot says, the pit does not need to be within three *tefachim* of the surrounding walls. Why should *sukkah* require *lavud* when *Shabbat* does not?

The answer points to a fundamental distinction in what walls accomplish in each domain. For *sukkah*, the walls must constitute a *structure* — a habitable enclosure in which one dwells. In a house, the walls are physically contiguous with the living space; they define and enclose it. Therefore, when the pit constitutes the effective floor of the *sukkah*, the walls must be contiguous with that floor — hence the requirement of *lavud*, of being within three *tefachim*. For *reshut ha-yachid* on *Shabbat*, by contrast, the walls function not as components of a structure but as barriers that protect and demarcate a zone. A *mechitzah* blocks movement; the space it encloses becomes a protected domain. The walls can be distant from the designated space because the requirement is not contiguity of structure but sufficiency of demarcation. The *reshut ha-yachid* is a more abstract legal category — not a dwelling place but a protected zone — and therefore its constitutive walls need not be physically integrated with the space they define.

## Gud Asik Platform

### Abaye's Gud Asik

A new case: a *sukkah* higher than twenty *amot* in which a platform has been built in the middle, the platform standing ten *tefachim* tall, and the space above the platform qualifying as the minimum size for a *sukkah*. The platform is too far from the walls for *dofen akumah* to apply. Abaye proposes invoking *gud asik mechitzta*: since the outer walls of the platform are ten *tefachim* tall, those walls can be extended vertically upward until they reach the *schach*, constituting valid *mechitzot* for the *sukkah* above.

### Rava's Visible Walls

Rava rejects Abaye's proposal: *beinan mechitzot hanikarot ve-leika* — we require walls that are obviously, evidently walls, and these do not qualify. The outer face of a platform is not a wall in the halachically evident sense. Two possible grounds for this ruling emerge. The first is that the extended walls are entirely invisible — they exist only in legal imagination above the platform's physical height. The second, more substantive ground, is that the outer face of a solid platform has no interior; it is merely the surface of an object, not a wall enclosing a space. Something that is merely the exterior face of a structure, without an interior, does not bear the character of a *mechitzah*.

This invites the question: when *is* *gud asik* legitimately invoked? The answer is developed in the following case.

## Four Posts Debate

### Posts on Roof

A *baraita*: *na'atz arba'ah kundasin ve-sichech al gaban* — four posts are set up on a rooftop and *schach* is placed across them. There are no walls whatsoever. Rabbi Yaakov declares it valid; the *chachamim* declare it invalid.

### Rav Huna's View

Rav Huna limits the scope of this debate: *machloket al sfat ha-gag* — they argue only about posts at the edge of the roof. There, Rabbi Yaakov holds *amrinan gud asik mechitzta* — we extend the walls of the building upward; the *chachamim* hold we do not. But if the posts were placed in the middle of the roof, all agree it would be invalid — there are no walls of the building to extend, and no basis for any wall claim at all.

### Middle Roof Debate

The *gemara* then cites a second *baraita*: *na'atz arba'ah kundasin ba-aretz ve-sichech al gabeihen* — four posts are planted in the ground, with *schach* above them. Rabbi Yaakov again validates; the *chachamim* again invalidate. The *gemara* notes that posts in the ground are equivalent to posts in the middle of a roof — there are no building walls anywhere nearby — yet Rabbi Yaakov rules it valid. This directly contradicts Rav Huna, who claimed that in the middle of the roof all would agree it is invalid. *Tiyuvta de-Rav Huna tiyuvta* — Rav Huna's position is refuted.

### Dyomdin Logic

Rav Nachman's alternative reading had placed the debate in the middle of the roof, and by implication suggested that at the edge of the roof all would agree it is valid. The *gemara* raises the possibility that the four-posts-on-the-ground case contradicts Rav Huna on two counts: first, that Rabbi Yaakov validates even without any extending wall; second, that it may imply the *chachamim* agree the roof edge is valid.

Rav Huna responds: the fact that the debate is stated in the case of the ground — which is equivalent to the middle of a roof — does not resolve what the *chachamim* hold about the roof edge. The reason the debate is framed in the ground case is *le-hodiecha kocho de-Rav Yaakov* — to convey the full extent of Rabbi Yaakov's position, namely that even without any walls of any kind, he considers four posts sufficient. The *chachamim*'s position regarding the roof edge remains undetermined.

The explanation for Rabbi Yaakov's position then follows. *Tanu rabbanan*: if the four posts are thick enough — specifically, thick enough that one could carve out of each a square of one *tefach* by one *tefach* — *nidonim mishum dyomed*, they are considered corners (*dyomadin*). Rabbi Yaakov holds *dyomdei sukkah tefach*: the corners of a *sukkah* need be only one *tefach* wide. If each post is sufficiently thick to yield such a corner, one can imagine them carved into corner-pieces, and from those imagined corners, one can imagine full walls connecting them. The frame of implied walls constitutes a valid enclosure.

### Real Walls Required

The *chachamim* reject this reasoning entirely. Their requirement is *ad she-yehu shtayim ke-hilchatan ve-shlishit afilu tefach* — two full halachic walls plus a third of at least one *tefach*. Implied corners and imagined walls do not satisfy this threshold. Even granting that thick posts could theoretically generate *dyomadin*, the *chachamim* require substantive physical walls, not doubly-fictional constructs built from imagined carvings.

## Magic Walls Summary

### Recap Magic Walls

To draw the threads together: the *gemara* has worked through two major principles of conceptual wall construction. *Dofen akumah* — the bent wall — operates on the horizontal plane: when the actual wall is too distant from the *schach* by less than four *amot*, the intervening solid roof or other structure is reconceived as a bent portion of the wall, bringing the wall into legal contiguity with the *schach*. *Gud asik mechitzta* — the extending wall — operates on the vertical plane: a wall that physically reaches ten *tefachim* is imagined to continue rising straight upward until it meets the *schach* above. In both cases, the precondition is that a genuine halachic wall already exists; both principles extend or reorient an existing wall rather than conjure one from nothing.

### Substantive Walls

The requirement of *mechitzot hanikarot* — evident, discernible walls — emerges as the governing constraint on *gud asik*. Rava's objection to the platform case illustrates this: the outer face of a platform, being merely the exterior surface of an object without any interior, does not possess the evident character of a wall. The case of four posts on a rooftop tests the boundary: Rav Nachman's reading allows that walls of a building, though invisible to someone standing on the roof, may still qualify as *nikarot* because they are genuinely walls with an interior, evident to anyone inside the building. Whether even the *chachamim* would accept *gud asik* in that case, when the wall is *nikar* to those below but not to the person above, remains an open question — *teiku*.

The broader lesson, captured in the final discussion touching on the *Rambam*'s treatment in the *halachot* of *sukkah* and *Shabbat*, is that *lavud* too operates within this framework of substantive reality: even with *lavud* filling the gaps between thin posts, if the posts themselves are insufficiently substantial to bear the name of a wall, no amount of legal contiguity will generate a valid *mechitzah*. You must begin with something real.
