# Niddah 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rok Tafal

### Definition of Tasteless…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיזֶהוּ רוֹק תָּפֵל. תָּנָא: כָּל שֶׁלֹּא טָעַם כְּלוּם מִבָּעֶרֶב. סְבַר רַב פָּפָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא לְמֵימַר, כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא טְעַם מִידֵּי בְּאוּרְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: מִי קָתָנֵי "בָּעֶרֶב"? "מִבָּעֶרֶב" קָתָנֵי, לְאַפּוֹקֵי הֵיכָא דְּקָדֵים וְאָכֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **What is tasteless saliva?** A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: What is the definition of tasteless saliva? **Any** saliva **where** the person **had not tasted anything since the evening.** **Rav Pappa,** who was sitting **before Rava, thought to say:** This **is in accordance with** the opinion of the **one who said** that **he did not eat anything** all **night** long. **Rava said to him: Is it taught:** One who had not tasted anything **in the evening,** which would indicate that it is referring only to one who did not eat since nightfall? No, the *baraita* **teaches:** Where the person had not tasted anything **since the evening,** which means even if he ate after nightfall, but did not eat for the rest of the night. This serves **to exclude** a case **where he** arose **early** in the morning **and ate,** as in such a case it is no longer tasteless saliva.

The daf opens at the top of 63a, where the Gemara continues its treatment of the various cleansers used to remove or examine blood stains. Among these cleansers is *rok tafal* — tasteless spit. The Mishnah describes it simply as spit from one who has not tasted anything since the evening (*kol she-lo ta'am klum me-ha-erev*), but this formulation requires further precision.

### Rava's Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵיזֶהוּ רוֹק תָּפֵל? כֹּל שֶׁעָבַר עָלָיו חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה וּבְשֵׁינָה. לְמֵימְרָא דִּבְשֵׁינָה תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא? וְהָתְנַן: יָשַׁן כׇּל הַיּוֹם — אֵין זֶה רוֹק תָּפֵל, נֵיעוֹר כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה — הֲרֵי זֶה רוֹק תָּפֵל! הָתָם בְּמִתְנַמְנֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What is tasteless saliva? Any** saliva **where** the person did not eat any food and **he passed the middle of the night and** he was **in** a state of **sleep.** The Gemara asks: Is that **to say that the matter depends on** whether or not he had **sleep? But didn't we learn** in a *baraita*: Even if **he slept the entire day, that is not tasteless saliva;** but if **he was awake the entire night, that is tasteless saliva?** This indicates that sleep is not a critical factor in producing tasteless saliva. The Gemara resolves this apparent contradiction by explaining that **there,** in the latter clause of the *baraita*, it is referring to a case **where** he was awake all night and did not sleep properly, but **was dozing** off and on.

Rav Pappa, learning this passage in front of Rava, initially understood the criterion to mean that one had not eaten anything the entire previous night — like a patient told by a doctor not to eat anything before a test, meaning no dinner. Rava rejected this reading. The text does not say *ba-erev*, "at night," but *me-ha-erev*, "since it became night." Rava explains: *le-afokei heicha d'kadmu ve-achil* — to exclude the case where one rose early in the morning and ate before the day properly began. The point is not that dinner is prohibited; one may have had dinner the night before. What disqualifies the spit is eating after one has gone to sleep and then woken up — eating in the early morning hours before the appropriate time. This is closer to the medical instruction not to eat or drink from the time you go to sleep until the test is administered.

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

Rabbah bar bar Chana then cites Rabbi Yochanan's more precise formulation: *rok tafal* is spit that has passed through midnight while one is asleep (*kol she-avar alav chatzot layla bi-sheina*). One need not sleep the entire night; the requirement is that one slept and that midnight has since passed. Dinner the prior evening remains permissible.

The Gemara then challenges this: does the criterion truly depend on sleep? The Beraita states that one who sleeps the entire day does not produce *rok tafal*, and one who stays awake the entire night (*ne'or kol ha-layla*) does produce *rok tafal*. The second case seems to indicate that sleep is not required. The Gemara responds: *hasam b'misnamnem* — "awake the whole night" in this Beraita does not mean fully awake; it means dozing.

### Misnamnem Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי מִתְנַמְנֵם? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: נִים וְלָא נִים, תִּיר וְלָא תִּיר, דְּקָרוּ לֵיהּ — וְעָנֵי, וְלָא יָדַע לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי סְבָרָא, וְכִי מַדְכְּרוּ לֵיהּ — מִדְּכַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **dozing? Rav Ashi said:** It is referring to a situation in which one is **asleep and** yet **not** fully **asleep,** and **awake and** yet **not** fully **awake. If someone calls him he answers, and he is** in a mental state in which **he does not know** how **to provide** an answer that requires logical **reasoning, but when people remind him** about something when he is in that state that has happened previously **he remembers** it.

The Gemara then defines this state of *misnamnem* — dozing — a phrase that appears in this precise formulation in several places throughout *Shas*. Rav Ashi explains: *num velo num, teir velo teir* — asleep but not really asleep, awake but not fully awake. If you call to such a person, he will respond, but he cannot reason or articulate any coherent thought. If you remind him of something, he can confirm it — but he cannot independently produce a logical argument. This is a state of half-awareness: one can hear and minimally respond, but cannot engage intellectually.

This description likely reflects something physiologically real about how sleep affects the quality of saliva. When one is fully awake at night, the mouth remains active. When one sleeps, even during the day, the stagnation of the mouth produces the particular staleness characteristic of *rok tafal*. The Gemara thus combines both elements — nighttime and sleep (or at minimum dozing) — as necessary conditions.

### Eating & Speaking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: הִשְׁכִּים וְשָׁנָה פִּרְקוֹ — אֵין זֶה רוֹק תָּפֵל. וְעַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר שֵׁילָא, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כֹּל שֶׁיָּצָא רוֹב דִּבּוּרוֹ שֶׁל שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: If one **rose** early in the morning **and learned** aloud **his chapter** of the Torah, **that** saliva in his mouth is **not tasteless saliva,** as speech weakens the strength of the saliva. **And how much** learning and talk removes the strength of the saliva? **Rav Yehuda bar Sheila says** that **Rav Ashi says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: Any** case **where he uttered most of his** normal amount of **speech** that he usually says **in three hours.**

A Tanna adds a further ruling: if one rose early and reviewed one's chapter (*hishkim ve-shana pirko*), that too disqualifies the spit and it is no longer *rok tafal*. The implication is that talking — or at least a certain amount of purposeful speech — changes the character of the saliva, just as eating does.

### Three-Hour Speech

The Gemara then quantifies the speech threshold. Rav Yehuda bar Shila, citing Rav Assi citing Rabbi Eliezer, rules: *kol she-hotzi et rov diburo shel shalosh sha'ot* — if one has spoken the majority of the amount one would typically speak over the course of three hours. Once that threshold is crossed, the spit is no longer *rok tafal*. The underlying intuition is that the oral activity of speaking has sufficiently altered the saliva's character. The cumulative picture is thus: one slept, it was nighttime and past midnight, and since waking one has neither eaten nor spoken excessively. All these criteria together are required.

## Cleansers & Methods

### Mei Grisin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵי גְרִיסִין — לְעִיסַּת גְּרִיסִין שֶׁל פּוֹל וְכוּ׳. לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: רוֹק תָּפֵל צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא עִם כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד? דִּלְמָא הַבְלָא דְפוּמָּא מְעַלֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Liquid from split beans** is created through **the chewing of split beans** that divided naturally, not by human hand, which are then applied to the stain. The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this ruling **supports** the opinion **of Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish said: Tasteless saliva must be** mixed **with each and every one** of the other six substances in order to remove the blood stain. The Gemara answers that this is no proof, as **perhaps** it is not due to the saliva, but rather **the heat of his mouth** is what **helps** the split beans remove the stain.

The Gemara turns to *mei grisin* — water or paste made from ground beans (not barley, as the Aramaic might suggest at first hearing). The question is raised whether this supports the view of Reish Lakish, who held that *rok tafal* must accompany each of the other cleansers as well — meaning that tasteless spit is not merely its own standalone cleanser but must be mixed into all the others. The connection to *mei grisin* is that the beans are chewed in the mouth, thus introducing saliva into the paste. The Gemara, however, deflects: *dilma havla d'fuma ma'ali* — perhaps it is not the spit itself that matters but the humidity of the mouth, the particular quality introduced by having the substance processed in the oral cavity. The question of Reish Lakish's position thus remains open.

### Rabbi Yehuda & Salt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מֵי גְרִיסִין רוֹתֵחַ, וְעוֹבֵר שֶׁיִּתֵּן לְתוֹכוֹ מֶלַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: The liquid from split beans** is effective in removing blood stains only when it is **boiling, and before [*over*] one puts salt into** the pot.

The Gemara notes that the Mishnah's formulation does not accord with Rabbi Yehuda. The Beraita cites Rabbi Yehuda's view that the *mei grisin* must be boiling (*rotei'ach*) and must be used *over* (before) salt is added — meaning the bean paste at the stage of cooking before any salt has been introduced. This formulation differs from the Mishnah on two counts: it specifies boiling and specifies the timing relative to salting.

### Etymology of Over

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי "עוֹבֵר" לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַקְדּוֹמֵי הוּא? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דְּאָמַר קְרָא "וַיָּרׇץ אֲחִימַעַץ דֶּרֶךְ הַכִּכָּר וַיַּעֲבוֹר אֶת הַכּוּשִׁי". אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: מֵהָכָא "וְהוּא עָבַר לִפְנֵיהֶם", וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: "וַיַּעֲבוֹר מַלְכָּם לִפְנֵיהֶם וַה׳ בְּרֹאשָׁם".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred that** the word ***over* is a formulation of priority? Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said that the verse states: "And Ahimaaz ran by the way of the plain, and overran [*vayya'avor*] the Cushite"** (II Samuel 18:23), i.e., Ahimaaz overtook the Cushite. **Abaye said:** It is derived **from here: "And he passed [*avar*] before them"** (Genesis 33:3). **And if you wish, say** instead that the proof is **from here: "And their king passed [*vayya'avor*] before them and the Lord at their head"** (Micah 2:13).

The Gemara pauses to ask: what is the semantic basis for reading the word *over* to mean "before"? One might have expected the text to use *kodem*. This question arises in multiple contexts throughout *Shas*, most famously in the rule that blessings must be recited *over la-asiyatan* — before the performance of a commandment. The Gemara establishes the usage through scriptural proof. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak cites the verse: *va-yarotz Achima'atz derekh ha-kikar va-ya'avor et ha-Kushi* — Achima'atz ran through the valley and passed ahead of the Cushite (2 Samuel 18:23), where *va-ya'avor* implies passing in front of. Abaye cites *ve-hu avar lifneihem* (Genesis 33:3) and *va-ya'avor malkam lifneihem* (Micah 2:13). In all these cases, the word *over* combined with the sense of *lifneihem* — in front of — establishes that the term can denote priority of position or time. This lexical precedent grounds the use of *over* to mean "before" in the halachic formulation.

### Aged Urine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵי רַגְלַיִם שֶׁהֶחְמִיצוּ. תָּנָא: כַּמָּה חִימּוּצָן? — שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: The **urine** that is an effective detergent is specifically urine **that fermented.** A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: For **how** long must it be **fermented?** For **three days.**

Among the other cleansers listed is urine that has become rancid (*mei raglayim she-himtziyu*). The Tanna specifies: *kama chimutzan? Shelosha yamim* — how long must it be left? Three days.

### Quantities Need Detail

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל שִׁיעוּרֵי חֲכָמִים בִּכְתָמִים — צָרִיךְ שִׁיעוּר לְשִׁיעוּרָן. דְּיֶלֶד אוֹ דְזָקֵן, דְּאִישׁ אוֹ דְאִשָּׁה, מְכוּסִּים אוֹ מְגוּלִּים, בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה אוֹ בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of the urine used to remove a blood stain, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All the measures of the Sages with regard to** blood **stains require a measure for their measure.** There are many types of urine, each of which has different properties, and it is unclear which is to be used. Is it urine **of a young** person **or of an old** person? Is it urine **of a man or of a woman?** Is it urine that has been kept **covered or uncovered?** Is it urine **from the summer or from the rainy season?**

Rabbi Yochanan then makes a sweeping observation: *kol shi'urei chachamim bi-ktzamim, tzarich shi'ur le-shi'uran* — all the quantities specified by the Sages with respect to these cleansers are themselves in need of further specification. Three-day urine sounds like a precise answer, but consider: is it the urine of a child or an elder? Of a man or a woman? Was it stored covered or uncovered? In summer or in winter? Each variable plausibly affects the potency and therefore the relevant time period. As Rabbi Yochanan makes plain, once you try to codify what was likely a piece of practical knowledge embedded in a particular social and material context, the formulation is nearly impossible to render precisely without the surrounding context. The point may be that such knowledge was self-evident to those who lived in that world, and the attempt to reduce it to a written rule inevitably leaves crucial detail unstated.

## Rubbing & Order

### Three Rubbings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְצָרִיךְ לְכַסְכֵּס שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים. בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אַמְטוֹיֵי וְאֵתוֹיֵי חַד, אוֹ דִלְמָא אַמְטוֹיֵי וְאֵתוֹיֵי תַּרְתֵּי? מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And one must rub** each and every one of the substances **three times. Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma** with regard to this rubbing: Is the **going and coming** of the hand over the surface of the rubbed item considered **one** rubbing, **or perhaps** the **going and coming** are considered **two** actions and two distinct rubbings? **What** is the correct count? The Gemara states: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara states that one must rub with each cleanser three times (*shalosh pe'amim*). Rabbi Yirmiyah raises the question whether this means three strokes in one direction, or three back-and-forth motions — that is, does each full back-and-forth count as one, or does each individual movement count as one? The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

### Two Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶעֱבִירָן שֶׁלֹּא כְּסִדְרָן. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִקְדִּים שְׁנִיִּים לָרִאשׁוֹנִים, תָּנֵי חֲדָא: שְׁנִיִּים עָלוּ לוֹ, רִאשׁוֹנִים לֹא עָלוּ לוֹ. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: רִאשׁוֹנִים עָלוּ לוֹ, שְׁנִיִּים לֹא עָלוּ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches: If **one applied them** in a manner that is **not in their** prescribed **order,** or if one applied all seven substances simultaneously, he has done nothing. **The Sages taught** two *baraitot* with regard to this matter. If one applied the substances from the **second** half of the list, i.e., natron, *borit*, Cimolian earth, and potash, **before the** substances from the **first** half of the list, i.e., tasteless saliva, liquid from split beans, and urine, it **is taught** in **one** *baraita*: The **second** set **count for him,** but the **first do not count for him. And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The **first count for him,** but the **second do not count for him.**

If the cleansers were applied out of order, the Gemara rules this is problematic. The Tanna teaches: *he'eviran shelo k'sidran* — if done out of sequence. The Gemara, as understood by Rashi (with Tosafot not disagreeing), assumes that the seven cleansers divide implicitly into two groups. The first group consists of the more naturally occurring, homegrown items — spit, bean paste, and urine. The second group consists of the more commercially available or processed substances: *neter*, *borit*, *kammonya*, and *ashlag*. Two conflicting Baraitot then arise: one states that if the second group was done before the first, the second group counts and the first does not; the other states the opposite — the first counts and the second does not.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִידִי וְאִידִי שְׁנִיִּים עָלוּ לוֹ, וְלֹא רִאשׁוֹנִים, וּמַאי "רִאשׁוֹנִים"? רִאשׁוֹנִים לִכְסִדְרָן, וּשְׁנִיִּים לְהַעֲבָרָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that there is no dispute between these two *baraitot*: Both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* agree that the substances that he applied **second count for him, and not** the substances that he applied **first. And what** does the second *baraita* mean when it uses the term: **First?** It means the **first according to the order** of the mishna, which were the **second in their application.**

Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction: *idi va-idi shniim alu lo ve-lo rishonim* — in both Baraitot, the second ones (in the order actually performed) count, not the first. The logic is as follows: suppose the correct order is 1-2-3-4-5-6-7, but the woman did 4-5-6-7 first and then 1-2-3. Abaye says that 1-2-3, performed second in this scenario, counts, and she may then proceed to redo 4-5-6-7. The group that was done second in practice effectively functions as the valid starting point, and the subsequent reapplication of the first group completes the process.

### First vs Second

The Gemara then explains how the two Baraitot can both be correct even though they seem to say opposite things. When one Beraita says "the second ones count," it means the group that was performed second — i.e., 1-2-3 in the reversed scenario. When the other Beraita says "the first ones count," it means the group that is normally first in the prescribed order — which in the rearranged scenario happens to have been applied second. The two Baraitot are not contradictory; they simply describe the same fact from different angles. In either case, the result is that the group which belongs first in the prescribed order counts, regardless of when it was actually applied in the session, and the other group must be reapplied afterward.

## Vestos Introduction

### Mishnah Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' כׇּל אִשָּׁה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ וֶסֶת — דַּיָּהּ שְׁעָתָהּ. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן הַוְּסָתוֹת: מְפַהֶקֶת, וּמְעַטֶּשֶׁת, וְחוֹשֶׁשֶׁת בְּפִי כְרֵיסָהּ, וּבְשִׁפּוּלֵי מֵעֶיהָ, וְשׁוֹפַעַת, וּכְמִין צְמַרְמוֹרוֹת אוֹחֲזִין אוֹתָהּ, וְכֵן כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן. וְכֹל שֶׁקָּבְעָה לָהּ שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים — הֲרֵי זֶה וֶסֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: For **any woman who has** a fixed menstrual **cycle** that is not time dependent, but is dependent on a physical sensation, **her time is sufficient,** i.e., she does not transmit ritual impurity retroactively, for twenty-four hours or until the last time she examined herself (see 2a). **And these are the** fixed menstrual **cycles** based on sensation: When a woman menstruates after **she yawns [*mefaheket*], or** after **she sneezes, or** after **she senses** pain **near her stomach or in her lower abdomen, or** after **she secretes** a discharge, **or** after **a type of** feverish **shuddering [*tzemarmorot*] overtakes her. And likewise** the same applies with regard to any sensation **of the like. And** in the case of **any** woman **who establishes** a pattern **for herself** by experiencing such a sensation **three times** before the onset of menstruation, **that is** a fixed menstrual **cycle.**

With the discussion of stains and cleansers substantially concluded, the Gemara moves to one of the most practically significant topics in *hilchos niddah*: the laws of *vestot* — a woman's anticipated period. The Mishnah opens with the general rule that a woman with an established *veset* need only reckon from the time she actually sees blood (*dayah sha'atah*), relieving her of the concern that bleeding may have begun up to twenty-four hours earlier. The Mishnah then identifies the *vestot* themselves: *mipaheket* — yawning fits; *me'ateshes* — sneezing fits; *chosheshet be-fi kerisah* — cramping sensation at the upper abdomen; *u-ve-shifulei me'eha* — or lower abdominal sensation in the groin area; *ve-shofaat* — or a certain type of flow; and *ke-min tzimarmoros* — shivering or cramping-type sensations; *ve-chein kayotzei ba-zeh* — and anything similar. Any of these, when they occur three consecutive times, constitutes an established *veset*.

### Three Occurrences

It is notable that the Mishnah frames the discussion around three occurrences (*sheloshah pe'amim*). This will become significant, as the Gemara will complicate the picture by introducing a hierarchy that may allow fewer than three.

### HaGuf vs HaYamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' תְּנֵינָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא: כׇּל אִשָּׁה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ וֶסֶת דַּיָּהּ שְׁעָתָהּ! הָתָם — בִּוְסָתוֹת דְּיוֹמֵי, הָכָא — בִּוְסָתוֹת דְּגוּפָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that for any woman who has a fixed menstrual cycle, her time is sufficient. The Gemara objects: **We** already **learned** that her time is sufficient on **another occasion** (2a): For **any woman who has** a fixed menstrual **cycle,** and she examined herself at that time and discovered blood, **her time is sufficient,** and it is only from that moment that she transmits ritual impurity. The Gemara answers: **There,** it is referring **to** a fixed menstrual **cycle of** a certain number of **days; here,** it is referring **to** a fixed menstrual **cycle based on a physical** sensation.

What is striking about this Mishnah is that it presents the bodily signs — *veset ha-guf* — as the primary, paradigmatic form of a *veset*, without even mentioning the calendar-based cycle (*veset ha-yamim*). This is noteworthy. For many women, particularly those who do not have a highly regular monthly cycle, *veset ha-guf* may in fact be a more reliable predictor than a fixed day-count. A woman who consistently experiences, for example, specific cramping or spotting before her period has a more direct and personal indicator of imminent bleeding than a generic twenty-eight-day calculation.

The Gemara confirms this framing. When it observes that the principle of *dayah sha'atah* was already taught earlier in the tractate — there in reference to *vestot* of days — it clarifies: *hatam be-vestot de-yomei, hacha be-vestot de-gufa* — there the Mishnah was speaking of day-based cycles; here it comes specifically to introduce the category of bodily *vestot*. The Mishnah's purpose is thus not to repeat a known rule but to establish that bodily experiences can and do constitute a *veset* in their own right.

This is a *halakha* that has been, to a considerable degree, forgotten in practice. Much of the focus in *kalla* classes and responsa literature is devoted to the complex calendar calculations of *veset ha-yamim* and *veset she-eino kavu'a*. Yet those calculations, while technically necessary when *veset ha-guf* is absent, are far more complicated and often less predictive than attending to one's own bodily signals. When a woman establishes a *veset ha-guf*, she is relieved of the need to worry about the other calculations. The Mishnah's framing of *veset ha-guf* as the primary category deserves to be taken seriously in practice.

The relationship between *veset ha-guf* and *hargashah* — the bodily sensation that Shmuel elsewhere identifies as a necessary component of a woman's becoming a *niddah* — is worth noting. One might ask whether the cramping described in our Mishnah is the same as Shmuel's *hargashah*. Most *rishonim* and *poskim* say no, because these bodily signs function as advance predictors occurring before bleeding, whereas Shmuel's *hargashah* refers to a sensation at the actual moment of discharge. The Rambam, however, seems to indicate that these may be related or overlapping phenomena.

### Shofaat Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹפַעַת? הָא שָׁפְעָה וְאָזְלָה! אָמַר עוּלָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב עִלַּאי: בְּשׁוֹפַעַת דָּם טָמֵא מִתּוֹךְ דָּם טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna includes the case where **she secretes** a discharge as one of the physical sensations. The Gemara understands this as referring to a continuous discharge of blood, and therefore asks: **But** during menstruation **she is continuously discharging** blood; how can this be a signal of the onset of menstruation? **Ulla, son of Rav Ilai, said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where she discharges ritually impure blood in the midst of** discharging **ritually pure blood.** For example, if she normally first discharges blood that is not red, and it therefore does not render her impure, and then she experiences a discharge of red blood.

The Mishnah lists *shofaat* — a type of flow — as one of the bodily *vestot*. This requires clarification: if she is already flowing blood, she is a *niddah*; in what sense is the flow a *veset*, a predictor? Ulla son of Rav Ilai explains: *be-shofaat dam tamei mitoch dam tahor* — she first produces *tahor* blood and then *tamei* blood follows. In practice, this means she first spots or discharges in a color that does not render her a *niddah* — perhaps brown or yellowish staining — and this predictably precedes her actual period by some hours.

This is itself a meaningful and underutilized basis for establishing a *veset*. Many women do spot before their periods begin. If that spotting is small enough or the color is not problematic, she is not yet a *niddah*. If she notices that such spotting consistently precedes her actual period by a predictable interval, she can establish this as a *veset ha-guf*. The Gemara makes this explicit, yet the *poskim* discuss it surprisingly little.

One might also ask whether a *veset ha-guf* can be combined with a time element — for instance, spotting that occurs only after at least twenty-five days have passed. In principle, *vestot ha-guf* can be established independently of the calendar; that is precisely what distinguishes them from *veset ha-yamim*. The *Shulchan Aruch* confirms this. Yet in practice, a hybrid formulation is certainly possible and may well describe the actual experience of many women.

## Expanded Physical Signs

### Rabbah's Signs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְמִין צְמַרְמוֹרוֹת וְכוּ׳. "וְכֵן כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן" — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא: לְאֵתוֹיֵי אִשָּׁה שֶׁרֹאשָׁהּ כָּבֵד עָלֶיהָ, וְאֵבָרֶיהָ כְּבֵדִים עָלֶיהָ, וְרוֹתֶתֶת, וְגוֹסָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Or a type of** feverish **shuddering** overtakes her, **and likewise** the same applies with regard to any sensation **of the like.** The Gemara asks: **What is added** by this last phrase? **Rabba bar Ulla said:** It serves **to include a woman whose head is heavy upon her or her limbs are heavy upon her, or she trembles or belches** constantly.

The Mishnah's phrase *ve-chein kayotzei ba-zeh* — and anything similar — invites expansion. The Gemara asks: what does this include? Rabbah bar Ulla answers: *l'atuyei hargashat roshah kaved aleha* — to include a sensation of heaviness in the head; *ve-eivareiha kvedim aleha* — heaviness of limbs; *rotetat* — trembling; and *gosah* — belching. Any of these specific, recurring bodily experiences can constitute a *veset* if they reliably predict bleeding.

### Shmuel's Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר חִיָּיא, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ לְיָמִים — שְׁנַיִם, לִוְסָתוֹת — אַחַת, לְמַה שֶּׁלֹּא מָנוּ חֲכָמִים — שְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Ḥiyya says** that **Shmuel says:** The Sages **said, with regard to** establishing a set period of **days,** that **two** days are sufficient, i.e., if a woman experiences bleeding twice on the same date of the month or after the same interval, she has a fixed menstrual cycle. By contrast, **with regard to** a fixed menstrual **cycle** based on a physical sensation, it is sufficient if she experiences bleeding even **once.** But **for those** sensations **which the Sages did not include** in the mishna, she has a fixed cycle only if she experiences bleeding **three** times accompanied by one of those symptoms.

Rav Huna bar Chiyya then cites a teaching in the name of Shmuel that introduces a hierarchy among the categories. Shmuel says: *harei amru le-yamim shenayim, le-vestot echad* — it has been taught that a *veset* of days requires two occurrences to be established, whereas a *veset* of the bodily type (the items listed in the Mishnah) requires only one. This is striking. A single occurrence of a specific bodily sign suffices to establish it as a predictive indicator, while the day-based cycle requires at least two data points.

One can understand the logic: two occurrences of the same interval between periods (say, twenty-eight days each time) already demonstrates a consistent gap, since the gap itself requires two endpoints. But more importantly, the hierarchy reflects the assumption that bodily signs are inherently more predictive than calendar patterns. The body speaking in a specific, unusual, and recognizable way is a stronger signal than the passage of a fixed number of days.

Shmuel labels the day-based cycle *yamim* and the bodily signs *vestot*, echoing the Mishnah's implicit prioritization of the latter.

The Gemara also notes that things not enumerated in the Mishnah — outside the listed categories entirely — would require three occurrences to establish a *veset*. Rav Yosef identifies these unlisted items: *roshah kaved aleha, eivareiha kvedim aleha, rotetat, gosah* — the very items Rabbah bar Ulla had cited as expansions of the Mishnah's *ve-chein kayotzei ba-zeh*. So according to Rav Yosef, these fall outside the Mishnah proper and thus require three occurrences.

### Veset Ma'aseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַה שֶּׁלֹּא מָנוּ חֲכָמִים. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לְאֵתוֹיֵי רֹאשָׁהּ כָּבֵד עָלֶיהָ, וְאֵבָרֶיהָ כְּבֵדִין עָלֶיהָ, וְרוֹתֶתֶת, וְגוֹסָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? מַתְנִיתִין הִיא! דְּהָא פָּרְשַׁהּ רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא. אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְאֵתוֹיֵי אָכְלָה שׁוּם וְרָאֲתָה, וְאָכְלָה בְּצָלִים וְרָאֲתָה, כָּסְסָה פִּלְפְּלִים וְרָאֲתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: When Shmuel said: **For those which the Sages did not include, what** did he intend **to add? Rav Yosef says:** This serves **to add** the case mentioned above, i.e., **her head is heavy upon her, or her limbs are heavy upon her, or she trembles or belches** constantly. **Abaye said** to Rav Yosef: **What is** that addition **teaching us?** In effect, **it is** already taught in **the mishna, as Rabba bar Ulla explained** above that the additional phrase: The same applies with regard to any sensation of the like, serves to include those sensations. **Rather, Abaye said:** Shmuel's phrase serves **to add** a case where **she ate garlic and saw** menstrual blood, **or ate onions and saw** menstrual blood, or **chewed pepper and saw** menstrual blood, i.e., these triggers can give rise to a fixed cycle, but only after three occurrences.

Abaye then challenges Rav Yosef's contribution — why is this a novel teaching if Rabbah bar Ulla already interpreted the Mishnah's open-ended phrase to include precisely this list? The Gemara senses that Abaye's objection has force.

Abaye therefore offers an alternative expansion: *achla shum ve-ra'atah, achla betzalim ve-ra'atah, kissa de-pilpelin ve-ra'atah* — she ate garlic and then saw blood; she ate onions and then saw; she chewed peppercorns and then saw. This too can constitute a predictive *veset*, but it is categorically different from the bodily signs listed in the Mishnah. It belongs to a third category: *veset ma'aseh* — a *veset* triggered by an action rather than arising from purely internal bodily experience.

This third category must be distinguished from another type of *veset ha-ma'aseh* discussed elsewhere in the Gemara — namely, actions like jumping or physical exertion. Tosafot (*d'achla shum*) explains the distinction. When physical exertion causes bleeding, the causal mechanism is direct: an external force dislodges blood mechanically. That is considered *mach'mat ones* — compulsion from an external factor — and such a *veset* cannot be established independently of a day-based cycle. By contrast, when a woman eats garlic or peppers and subsequently sees blood, the causal chain is indirect and internal: the food interacts with her body's hormonal and physiological systems and eventually triggers discharge. This is a natural, if unusual, bodily process. Tosafot: *she-ein ha-re'iyah ba'ah yedei torach ela mi-meila* — the bleeding does not come through the force itself but of its own accord through what goes on inside the body. This type of *veset ma'aseh* can therefore be established without combining it with a specific day-count, and it requires three occurrences to be established.

This analysis has immediate contemporary relevance. With the widespread use of hormonal contraception, a woman who consistently bleeds within a predictable interval after stopping the active pill has, in effect, a *veset* that functions very much like *veset ma'aseh* in Abaye's sense: an ingested substance interacts with her hormonal system and triggers bleeding. The *poskim* have engaged seriously with this question, many affirming that such a pattern can constitute an established *veset*. The situation differs in that the pill suppresses rather than triggers — and then withdrawal triggers — but the underlying mechanism of an ingested substance interacting with the body's physiology is structurally analogous.

### Teaching Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא שְׁמִיעַ לִי הָא שְׁמַעְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: I did not hear this tradition** concerning the opinion of Shmuel, that with regard to days a cycle is fixed if she experiences bleeding twice, whereas for physical sensations a cycle is fixed after one occurrence. Rav Yosef had fallen ill and forgotten his studies and was therefore unable to remember that such a ruling had been issued.

Rav Yosef, who had forgotten many of his teachings due to illness, responds to Abaye's presentation of the garlic-and-pepper teaching: *lo shemi'a li ha-shema'ta* — I have never heard this. Abaye then tells him: *at manita nialan* — you yourself taught it to us. This introduces the next phase of the discussion, in which Abaye recalls additional material Rav Yosef had taught and uses it to clarify the apparent contradiction between the one-two-three hierarchy and the Mishnah's uniform requirement of three.

## Three vs One-Two

### Day Changes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַתְּ אַמְרִיתַהּ נִיהֲלַן, וְאַהָא אַמְרִיתַהּ נִיהֲלַן — הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה יוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר, וְשִׁינְּתָה לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים — זֶה וְזֶה אֲסוּרִין. שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים — הוּתַּר יוֹם חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר, וְקָבְעָה לָהּ יוֹם עֶשְׂרִים, שֶׁאֵין אִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת עַד שֶׁתִּקְבָּעֶנָּה שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** His student, **Abaye, said to him: You** yourself **told us** this *halakha*, **and** it was **with regard to this** following matter that **you told** this *halakha* **to us,** taught in the mishna below: If a woman **was accustomed to see** the flow of blood on the **fifteenth day and she deviated** from the norm to see the flow of blood **on** the **twentieth day,** then on both **this** day, the fifteenth, **and that** day, the twentieth, it is **prohibited** for her to engage in intercourse. If she deviated from the norm to see the flow of blood **on** the **twentieth day three times, it becomes permitted** for her to engage in intercourse on the **fifteenth day, and she** has **established the twentieth day for herself** as the day of her fixed menstrual cycle, **as a woman establishes** a fixed menstrual **cycle only after she establishes it three times.**

Abaye reminds Rav Yosef of the following teaching from a coming Mishnah: if a woman was accustomed to seeing on day fifteen and switched to day twenty, she must worry about both days until the new pattern is firmly established. And specifically: *shalosh pe'amim le-yom esrim* — three consecutive occurrences on day twenty are required before day fifteen is released and day twenty is established as the exclusive *veset*. This Mishnah, like the one we just read, requires three times.

### Shmuel's Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַרְתְּ לַן עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בַּר רַבִּי, שֶׁאָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: רָאֲתָה — אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה לֹא לִשְׁנוֹת וְלֹא לְשַׁלֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **And you,** Rav Yosef, **said to us with regard to this** mishna that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: This is the statement of Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said** it **in the name of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. But the Rabbis say:** If **she saw** a discharge of blood even once, she has a fixed cycle and **does not need to repeat** and experience bleeding a second time **or to repeat a third** time.

Abaye then recalls that Rav Yosef had taught in the name of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *zu divrei Rabban Gamliel bar Rabbi she-amar mi-shum Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the requirement of three times consistently stated in our Mishnayot reflects the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. But the *chachamim* disagree: *veset einah tzericha lo lishnot velo leshalesh* — a *veset* need not be repeated twice or three times. The blanket requirement of three belongs to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel alone.

### One-Two Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִינַן לָךְ: לִשְׁנוֹת אָמְרַתְּ לַן, לְשַׁלֵּשׁ מִיבַּעְיָא? וַאֲמַרְתְּ לַן: לִשְׁנוֹת — בִּוְסָתוֹת, לְשַׁלֵּשׁ — בְּיָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And we said to you:** Since **you said to us** that she does not need **to repeat** a second time, **is it necessary** for you to mention that she does not need **to repeat a third** time? **And you said to us** that when you said that she does not need **to repeat** a second time, this is referring **to** a fixed menstrual **cycle** based on a physical sensation, whereas when you said she does not need **to repeat a third** time, that is referring **to** a fixed cycle of **days.**

The explanation Rav Yosef had offered: *lishnot be-vestot, leshalesh be-yamim* — when it comes to *vestot* (bodily signs), you do not even need to do it twice; once suffices. When it comes to the spacing of days, you do not need three; two suffice. What requires three is presumably the third category — things not listed in the Mishnah, the *veset ma'aseh* of garlic and peppers.

So the full picture according to the *chachamim* is: bodily *vestot* — once; day-based patterns — twice; unlisted or action-based *vestot* — three times. The Mishnah's uniform requirement of three reflects the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who applies a formalistic rule of three to everything. This mirrors the classic dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi (his son, who edited the Mishnah) regarding *chazaka* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds a *chazaka* is established only after three occurrences; Rabbi holds two suffice. Indeed, the Gemara in Yevamot notes that the halakha varies by context. Here, the *seder* of the Mishnah consistently follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, even though — remarkably — Rabbi himself does not appear to represent his own position as the *stam*. Our accepted practice follows three times.

The hierarchy, however, reflects an important underlying principle: *veset ha-guf* is the most reliable predictor, bodily signs carry the greatest epistemic weight, and therefore require the fewest repetitions to be taken seriously.

## Bodily Timing

### Beginning Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה בִּתְחִלַּת הַוְּסָתוֹת — כׇּל הַטְּהָרוֹת שֶׁעָשְׂתָה בְּתוֹךְ הַוְּסָתוֹת טְמֵאוֹת. בְּסוֹף הַוְּסָתוֹת — כׇּל הַטְּהָרוֹת שֶׁעָשְׂתָה בְּתוֹךְ הַוְּסָתוֹת טְהוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a woman **was accustomed to see** the flow of blood **at the beginning of the** sensation that accompanies her **fixed** cycle, even if on one occasion she happened to experience bleeding only at the end of the sensation, **all the ritually pure items that she handled within the** duration of that sensation that accompanies her **fixed** cycle **are ritually impure.** If the woman was accustomed to experience bleeding **at the end of the** sensation that accompanies her **fixed** cycle, **all the ritually pure items that she handled within the** duration of that sensation that accompanies her **fixed** cycle **are pure.**

The Mishnah now addresses the question of timing within the *veset ha-guf* experience. If a woman is accustomed to seeing blood at the beginning of her *veset* sign — say, at the onset of a sneezing fit — and she indeed bleeds while that fit occurs, then *kol ha-taharos she-asatah be-toch ha-veset teme'ot* — anything she may have handled or rendered judgment on during that entire sneezing fit is presumed impure. Since her pattern is to bleed at the outset of the sign, we assume bleeding may have begun from the very moment the sign began.

### End Period

Conversely, if her pattern is to see only at the end of the *veset* sign — and she does see then — the opposite applies: *kol ha-taharos she-asatah be-toch ha-veset tehorot* — everything she did during the sign, prior to the endpoint, is presumed pure. Because her pattern is to bleed only at the end of the fit, not at the beginning, she need not retroactively assume contamination from the onset of the sign.

### Rabbi Yosi Hours

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף יָמִים וְשָׁעוֹת וְסָתוֹת. הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה עִם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה — אֵינָהּ אֲסוּרָה אֶלָּא עִם הָנֵץ הַחַמָּה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַיּוֹם שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Even** specific **days and** specific **hours** determine a fixed menstrual **cycle:** If a woman **was accustomed to see** the flow of blood on a certain day of the month **at sunrise,** it is **prohibited** for **her** to engage in intercourse with her husband **only at sunrise;** but during the night before and the following day, it is permitted for her to engage in intercourse. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Once sunrise passed and she did not menstruate, **the entire day is hers** and she may engage in intercourse, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei; but contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, intercourse the night before is prohibited.

Rabbi Yosi adds: *af yamim ve-sha'ot vestot* — even days and hours can function as *vestot*. This statement, which might initially appear to claim that day-based cycles are themselves a novel *chiddush*, is understood by the Gemara differently. The established pattern of a woman seeing every twenty-eight or twenty-five days is taken for granted. What Rabbi Yosi adds is that even within a day-based pattern, one can be precise about the *hour* of expected bleeding, not merely the day — just as with *veset ha-guf*, one can distinguish the beginning from the end of the bodily experience.

## Day Cycles

### Yosi Precision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' תָּנָא: כֵּיצַד אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי יָמִים וְשָׁעוֹת וְסָתוֹת? הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה לִהְיוֹת רוֹאָה מִיּוֹם עֶשְׂרִים לְיוֹם עֶשְׂרִים, וּמִשֵּׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת לְשֵׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת. הִגִּיעַ יוֹם עֶשְׂרִים וְלֹא רָאֲתָה — אֲסוּרָה לְשַׁמֵּשׁ כׇּל שֵׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת רִאשׁוֹנוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר עַד שֵׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת, וְחוֹשֶׁשֶׁת בְּשֵׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: A *tanna* **taught** in a *baraita*: **In what case did Rabbi Yosei say** that specific **days and hours** determine a fixed menstrual **cycle?** For example, if a woman **was accustomed to see** the flow of blood **on the twentieth day** and again **on the twentieth day, and in the sixth hour** of the day and again **in the sixth hour** of the day the next time; if the **twentieth day arrives** a third time **and she does not see** a flow of blood, **it is prohibited for her to engage** in intercourse for **the entirety of the first six hours** of the day; **this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei deems** it **permitted** for her to engage in intercourse **until the sixth hour** of the day, **and** says that she must be **concerned** only **during the sixth hour.**

The Beraita illustrates Rabbi Yosi's position. If a woman was accustomed to seeing every twenty days specifically at the sixth hour (approximately 11:00 AM), and day twenty has arrived, Rabbi Yosi holds that she is forbidden only during the sixth hour — the time unit in which she normally sees. Before that, she is permitted. This is analogous to saying she sees at the end of the sneezing fit: the precise hour is the off-limits window, not the broader unit.

### Yehuda Broader

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבְרוּ שֵׁשׁ שָׁעוֹת, וְלֹא רָאֲתָה — אֲסוּרָה לְשַׁמֵּשׁ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר מִן הַמִּנְחָה וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If **the sixth hour passed and she did not see** a flow of blood, **it is prohibited for her to engage** in intercourse for **the entirety of the day;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And Rabbi Yosei deems** it **permitted** for her to engage in intercourse **from *minḥa*** time **onward,** i.e., from the beginning of the seventh hour.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. When the pattern is day-based (as opposed to purely bodily), the unit of concern is the broader *onah* — either the daytime unit (sunrise to sunset) or the nighttime unit (sunset to sunrise). One cannot narrow it down to a single hour within the day. *Kol ha-yom shelah* — the entire daytime is hers to be concerned about. We rule like Rabbi Yehuda.

### 20-Day Example

The Gemara elaborates on the example. She sees every twenty days at the sixth hour. Day twenty arrives. *Assur le-shamesh kol shesh sha'ot rishonot* — according to Rabbi Yehuda, from sunrise until 11:00 AM is forbidden. Then the sixth hour passes without any bleeding. According to Rabbi Yehuda, she remains forbidden for the rest of the entire day. His reasoning is that the relevant unit is the entire daytime *onah*, and she sees within that unit. The entire unit before and after is off-limits. Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, permits her from *mincha gedola* onward (roughly 12:30 PM), having extended the window only slightly past noon.

The discussion of units and boundaries also leads to an important point about how the boundary of the unit is defined. Units here are defined by sunrise and sunset — not by *alot ha-shachar* and *tzeit ha-kochavim*, which are the reference points typically used in *hilchos niddah* for other purposes. Even if a woman sees immediately before sunrise — which, by the standard *niddah* reckoning, already falls within the halachic nighttime — the formalistic definition here places her in the nighttime unit, and the entire night is off but the entire subsequent day is permitted. The rule is entirely unit-based, not minute-based.

If a woman's bleeding spans the border of two units — say, from just before sunrise to just after — the general consensus among *rishonim* is that one follows the beginning of the bleeding to determine the unit.

### Conflicting Baraisot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה לְמוּדָה. וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה שֶׁלָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to a woman who **was accustomed to see** the flow of blood on a certain day of the month at sunrise, that Rabbi Yehuda says: Once sunrise passed and she did not menstruate, the entire day is hers and she may engage in intercourse, but intercourse is prohibited the night before. The Gemara objects: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: The entire night is hers?** This indicates that she may engage in intercourse during the night.

Two Baraitot appear to conflict regarding Rabbi Yehuda's ruling. One states: Rabbi Yehuda says she is forbidden *before* her *veset* and permitted *after*. The other states: she is forbidden *after* her *veset* and permitted *before*.

### Beginning/End Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא — הָא דִּרְגִילָה לִרְאוֹת בִּתְחִלַּת יְמָמָא, וְהָא דִּרְגִילָה לִרְאוֹת בְּסוֹף לֵילְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that it is **not difficult. Here,** when it is permitted for her to engage in intercourse the entire night, it is referring to a case **where she is accustomed to see** a flow of blood **at the beginning of the day,** i.e., immediately after sunrise. **And there,** in the mishna, which deems it prohibited for her to engage in intercourse all night, it is referring to a case **where she is accustomed to see** a flow of blood **at the end of the night,** i.e., just before sunrise.

The resolution parallels what we saw with *veset ha-guf*: it depends on when within the unit her pattern of bleeding falls. If she normally sees at the very end of the night — just before sunrise — then the night is the forbidden unit and the subsequent day is entirely permitted. She is forbidden *before* that moment (in the sense that the night unit is off), and permitted *after* (the day is fine). Conversely, if she normally sees right at the beginning of the day — at or just after sunrise — then the daytime is the forbidden unit and the prior night is fine. She is forbidden *after* that cusp moment (the whole day), and permitted *before* (the whole night).

The conclusion is entirely formalistic: whichever unit contains the moment she sees is entirely off-limits; the adjacent unit is entirely permitted.

## Prisha & Halakha

### Rava Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וּמִי אָמַר רָבָא הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: "וְהִזַּרְתֶּם אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִטּוּמְאָתָם" — מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אַזְהָרָה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיִּפְרְשׁוּ מִנְּשׁוֹתֵיהֶן סָמוּךְ לְוִסְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara asks: **And did Rava** actually **say this? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a menstruating woman: **"You shall separate the children of Israel from their impurity"** (Leviticus 15:31). **From here, Rabbi Yirmeya said** that there is **a warning to the children of Israel that they should separate from their wives close to** the time of **their** menstrual **cycles.**

Rava rules definitively: *halakha ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — the law follows Rabbi Yehuda. When a woman has a *veset ha-yamim*, the entire *onah* in which she is expected to bleed is forbidden for marital relations, defined by the broad daytime or nighttime unit.

### Biblical Source

The Gemara then raises a challenge. A Beraita cites the verse: *ve-hizartem et benei Yisrael mi-tum'asam* — and you shall warn the children of Israel from their impurities (Leviticus 15:31). Rabbi Yirmiyah (some say Rabbi Yoshiyah) derives from this verse: *azhara li-vnei Yisrael she-yifreshu mi-nesheihem samuch le-vestan* — a warning to men to separate from their wives close to the time of expected bleeding. This is the Torah's basis for the requirement of *prisha* — preemptive separation before the anticipated *veset*.

### Prisha Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רָבָא: עוֹנָה. מַאי לַָאו עוֹנָה אַחֲרִיתִי? לָא, אוֹתָהּ עוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And how** close? **Rava says: One** twelve-hour **period,** i.e., either from sunrise to sunset, or from sunset to sunrise. The Gemara explains the difficulty: **What, is it not** referring to **an additional** twelve-hour **period,** i.e., the prohibition applies to the time of her expected period itself, either day or night, as well as the previous twelve hours? This is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who deems it prohibited for her to engage in intercourse only for the twelve hours of the expected time of her period. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is referring to **that** same twelve-hour **period.**

How long must this separation last? Rava says: *onah* — one unit. The *prisha* required is for the duration of the *onah* in which bleeding is expected.

### Scripture vs Halakha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן הָא — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי לִטְהָרוֹת, אֲבָל לְבַעְלָהּ — לָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need **two** statements of Rava, i.e., both that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda and that a woman must separate for only one twelve-hour period? The Gemara answers that both are **necessary, because if** Rava had **taught us** only **this,** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda in the mishna, **I would say** that **this statement** applies only **to** the handling of **pure** items, **but with regard to** engaging in intercourse with **her husband,** it does **not** apply, and with regard to intercourse, the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that even with regard to intercourse, the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

The Gemara asks: does *samuch le-vestan* in the verse not imply an additional unit of separation — the unit *before* the expected *onah*? If she bleeds at noon, should she not be forbidden from the prior nighttime as well, as a prophylactic buffer? The Gemara responds: *lo osa onah* — no, the verse refers to that very *onah* itself. The *samuch* — the "proximity" — is satisfied by the breadth of the *onah* unit, which already encompasses the time around the expected bleeding. This is why Rava's ruling and the biblical verse are both necessary: without the verse, one might have thought the *onah* restriction applies only to *taharot* and not to marital relations; without the ruling like Rabbi Yehuda, one might have thought the verse mandates an additional prior unit. Together they establish that exactly one *onah* — the unit in which bleeding is expected — is the forbidden period, covering both marital relations and *taharot*.

### Or Zarua Stringency

The Or Zarua maintains a *chumra* that some observe even today: separating not only during the expected *onah* but also during the prior unit. This is precisely the rejected *hava amina* of the Gemara — the possibility that the verse mandates an additional prior *onah*. While the Gemara clearly rejects this reading as the normative ruling, the Or Zarua treats it as an additional stringency. How one who follows the Or Zarua defends this position given the Gemara's explicit rejection requires its own explanation.

### Ritva Position

The Ritva offers a position that navigates between these poles. The core prohibition — separation during the precise period immediately around when she is expected to bleed — carries the force of Torah law (*d'oraita*). Something like Rabbi Yosi's formulation — the precise hour of expected bleeding — is the *d'oraita* baseline. When we expand the prohibition to the entire *onah*, as per Rabbi Yehuda's ruling adopted by Rava, that expansion is rabbinic in character (*de-rabbanan*). The Ritva thus identifies a *d'oraita* core surrounded by a rabbinic expansion, a position that elegantly explains how other authorities sometimes permit leniences that would be impossible to sustain if the entire *onah* prohibition were of Torah force.
