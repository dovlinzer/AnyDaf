# Menachot 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Menorah Structure

### Lamps Not Attached

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוֹרָה בָּאָה מִן הַכִּכָּר, וְאֵין מַלְקָחֶיהָ וּמַחְתּוֹתֶיהָ וְנֵרוֹתֶיהָ בָּאָה מִן הַכִּכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The frame of the Candelabrum was produced from the talent** of gold, **but its tongs and its pans and its lamps were not produced from the talent.**

We begin at the very bottom of 88b, concluding a discussion about the *Menorah*, since one of the measures under consideration was the חצי *Log* of oil for each lamp. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Karchah states: *Menorah* באה מן הכיכר ואין מלקחיה ומחתותיה ונרותיה באין מן הכיכר — the *Menorah* itself came from the *Kikar*, the talent of gold, but the tongs, the small shovels used for scraping and cleaning, and the lamps themselves did not come from the *Kikar*. The practical implication is significant: it is considerably easier to clean the lamps if they are not physically attached to the *Menorah*.

The reasoning flows from the way the *torah* assigns the *Kikar*. The *torah* says two things about the *Menorah*: that it requires a *Kikar* of gold, and that it is *Mikshah* — beaten, formed as one unit. The assumption is that these two characteristics belong together. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Karchah therefore holds that only the *Menorah* proper was formed from the *Kikar* and was part of that unified structure, while the implements and the lamps were not.

### Unified Menorah, Separat…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים אֵת "כׇּל הַכֵּלִים הָאֵלֶּה" – שֶׁהָיוּ כֵּלִים שֶׁל זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, how do I realize** the meaning of the verse: "Of a talent of pure gold it shall be fashioned **with all these vessels"?** It teaches only **that** all the **vessels** associated with the Candelabrum **were** made **of gold.**

What, then, does the phrase *ella rather* מה אני *Mekayem* את כל הכלים האלה — "what do I make of all these vessels" — mean, given that the *torah* speaks of both the *Kikar* and all these vessels? The answer is שהיו כלי זהב — it means they are of gold, not that they are from the *Kikar* and not that they are attached. The *Gemara* objects: but that they are gold is already explicit, as the verse states ועשית את נרותיה שבעה... מלקחיה ומחתותיה זהב *Tahor why didn't*. If the goldness of the implements is already stated explicitly, what purpose remains for the verse?

### Pure Gold Lips

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָהָב בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בּוֹ, "וְעָשִׂיתָ אֶת נֵרֹתֶיהָ שִׁבְעָה וְהֶעֱלָה אֶת נֵרֹתֶיהָ וְהֵאִיר אֶל עֵבֶר פָּנֶיהָ. וּמַלְקָחֶיהָ וּמַחְתֹּתֶיהָ זָהָב טָהוֹר". לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְפִי נֵרוֹת, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּפִי נֵרוֹת אַשְׁחוֹרֵי מַשְׁחַר, הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל,
>
> **Translation:** But the requirement that the vessels be made of **gold is explicitly written in** the verse: **"And you shall fashion its lamps seven, and they shall kindle its lamps, and it will enlighten toward its face. And its tongs and its pans shall be of pure gold"** (Exodus 25:37–38); why then is it necessary for the *baraita* to derive this from the phrase "with all these vessels"? This derivation of the *baraita* **is necessary only to** teach that the same applies **to** the **mouth of** the **lamps,** where the wicks rest. Otherwise, it might **enter your mind to say** that **since** the **mouth of** the **lamps blackens** and is damaged by the burning wick, therefore the principle that **the Torah spared the money of the Jewish people** should be applied,

The *Gemara* answers: לא נצרכה *ella rather* לפי *Neros* — the verse is needed only for the lips of the lamps themselves. One might have thought — סלקא דעתך אמינא — that since the lips of the lamps become black from direct contact with fire, and since the *torah* is concerned with the money of Israel, התורה חסה על ממונם של ישראל, they need not be made of pure gold. Rashi explains this to mean a gold alloy rather than pure gold, since pure gold would be damaged precisely where the fire burns. Therefore, קא משמע לן: the verse teaches that despite this concern, the lips too must be of pure gold.

This also concludes our *Menorah* discussion, though what remains especially interesting is the question raised in the previous session: whether the lamps were physically connected, and how the *Menorah* was relit if a lamp went out during the night. The simple reading of the *Gemara* suggested one position, while the Rambam's strikingly different reading — that both the cleaning and the relighting were performed twice daily, at the onset of night and again first thing in the morning — stands against the other *Rishonim* and many parallel *Gemoros*.

## Todah Oil & Ratios

### Todah Half Log

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲצִי לוֹג שֶׁמֶן לְתוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A half-*log* of oil** was used to measure oil **for the thanks offering.**

At the top of 89a, the *Mishna mentioned* states: חצי *Log* *shemen* לתודה — a half *Log* of oil for the *todah*. The normal amount of oil accompanying a *mincha* is a *Log* per *issaron*. The *todah*, however, includes עשרה *Esronot 6-4-3* for the ten loaves of *chametz I believe gets* and עשרה *Esronot 6-4-3* for the thirty loaves of *matzah loaves*. The חצי *Log* under discussion here applies only to the *matzah loaves* loaves, meaning a half *Log* for thirty loaves — עשרה *Esronot 6-4-3*. This yields a ratio of roughly one to twenty, far less oil than the standard one-to-one ratio of a *Log* per *issaron*.

### Double Oil Mention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "בַּשֶּׁמֶן" "בַּשֶּׁמֶן" שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים? אִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא "בַּשֶּׁמֶן" אֶחָד, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הוּא כְּכׇל הַמְּנָחוֹת לְלוֹג. עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁכָּתַב "בַּשֶּׁמֶן" "בַּשֶּׁמֶן", הָוֵי רִיבּוּי אַחַר רִיבּוּי, וְאֵין רִיבּוּי אַחַר רִיבּוּי אֶלָּא לְמַעֵט – מִיעֲטוֹ הַכָּתוּב לַחֲצִי לוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Akiva says: Why** must **the verse state: "With oil," "with oil,"** writing it **twice;** why was the first time not sufficient? Because **were** the term **"with oil" stated only once, I would have said** that, with regard to the amount of oil required, the meal offerings that accompany the thanks offerings **are like all** other **meal offerings to** the extent that they require one ***log*** of oil. **Now that** the verse **wrote "with oil," "with oil," it constitutes** one **amplification following** another **amplification, and** the principle is that one **amplification following** another **amplification** serves **only to restrict** the extent of the *halakha*. Accordingly, in this case **the verse restricted** the amount of oil used in the three types of unleavened meal offerings **to a half-*log*** for all of them together.

The *braita teaches* records Rabbi Akiva's derivation. The verse concerning the *todah* reads: *chalot* *matzot* בלולות *bashamen mixed* ורקיקי *matzot* משוחים *bashamen mixed* וסולת מרבכת *chalot* בלולות *bashamen mixed* — the word *bashamen mixed* appears three times in connection with the three types of *matzah loaves* loaves. Rabbi Akiva asks: why does the verse say *bashamen mixed* twice? Had it said *bashamen mixed* only once, I would have assumed it is like all other *menachot* and receives a *Log* — even though that is already a *Log* for עשרה *Esronot 6-4-3*, which is itself unusual. But since it says *bashamen mixed* twice, this constitutes *ribui you're never going* אחר *ribui you're never going*, and ואין *ribui you're never going* אחר *ribui you're never going* *ella rather* למעט — a doubled expansive formulation comes only to diminish. Hence, מיעטו הכתוב לחצי *Log* — the verse reduces the amount to a half *Log*.

The *Gemara*, however, raises a technical difficulty: is this truly *ribui you're never going* אחר *ribui you're never going*? The first *bashamen mixed* is needed simply to indicate that oil is required at all — had it only said it was a *mincha*, one would already know that oil is needed. So the first *bashamen mixed* is already a *ribui you're never going* in its own right, and the second *bashamen mixed* constitutes *ribui you're never going* אחר *ribui you're never going*, from which we derive the reduction.

### Three Oil Mentions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְהֵא חֲצִי לוֹג זֶה מִתְחַלֵּק לִשְׁלֹשֶׁת מִינִין, לַחַלּוֹת וְלָרְקִיקִין וְלָרְבִיכָה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר "בַּשֶּׁמֶן" בָּרְבִיכָה, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר, רִיבָּה שֶׁמֶן לָרְבִיכָה. הָא כֵּיצַד? מֵבִיא חֲצִי לוֹג שֶׁמֶן וְחוֹצֵיהוּ לַחַלּוֹת וְלָרְקִיקִין, וְחֶצְיוֹ לָרְבִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** Once it has been established that the meal offerings require only a half-*log* of oil, one **might** have thought that **this half-*log* should be** equally **divided between** the **three types** of unleavened meal offerings, i.e., one-sixth of a *log* **for** the **loaves,** one-sixth **for** the **wafers, and** one-sixth **for** the **poached** loaves. **When** the verse **states: "With oil," with regard to** the **poached** loaves, that is seemingly superfluous, **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** the requirement to use part of the half-*log* of oil, being that this requirement has already been established. It must therefore serve to **amplify** the amount of **oil** used **for a poached** loaf as opposed to the other two types. **How so? One brings a half-*log* of oil and divides it** equally into two. **Half of it** is further divided and used both **for** the ten regular **loaves and for** the ten **wafers, and** the other **half of it** is used entirely **for** the ten **poached** loaves.

From here, the *Gemara* considers the third mention of *bashamen mixed*. One might think the half *Log* is divided equally among the three types of *matzah loaves*: the *chalot*, the *rekikin*, and the *revicha proportionally it's still very small related*. This would mean taking *Chatzi Log* and dividing it into three. But כשהוא אומר *bashamen mixed* ברביכה — the fact that the verse says *bashamen mixed* a third time by the *revicha proportionally it's still very small related* specifically, where שאין תלמוד לומר — there was no need to state it, tells us שריבה *shemen* לרביכה — the *revicha proportionally it's still very small related* receives more oil than the *chalot* and the *rekikin*.

The practical calculation: מביא חצי *Log* *shemen* וחוציהו — take the half *Log* and divide it in half. חציו לחלות ורקיקין — one half, a *revi'it*, goes to the *chalot* and *rekikin* together. וחציו לרביכה — and the other *revi'it* goes to the *revicha proportionally it's still very small related* alone. A *revi'it* is roughly three and a half ounces; one might reflect on how small this amount is relative to the flour involved. The thirty *matzah loaves* loaves are made from עשרה *Esronot 6-4-3* — approximately ten five-pound bags of flour — and the entire oil allotment for all of them is a half *Log*, amounting to about six ounces total.

The ten loaves of *chametz I believe gets* that are also part of the *todah* have their own separate half *Log*. And of the thirty *matzah loaves* loaves, which are ten each of *chalot*, *rekikin*, and *revicha proportionally it's still very small related*, each group of ten is made from שלשה ושליש *Esronot 6-4-3*. The twenty loaves of *chalot* and *rekikin* share one *revi'it*, while the ten *revicha proportionally it's still very small related* loaves receive the other *revi'it* — hence twice as much oil proportionally for the *revicha proportionally it's still very small related*, though proportionally it remains tiny in comparison to the standard *mincha*. As for which loaves precisely receive more based on the sequence of the verse, the *Gemara* does not resolve this definitively.

### Halacha L'Moshe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: עֲקִיבָא, אִם אַתָּה מְרַבֶּה כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ "בַּשֶּׁמֶן" "בַּשֶּׁמֶן" – אֵינִי שׁוֹמֵעַ לָךְ, אֶלָּא חֲצִי לוֹג שֶׁמֶן לְתוֹדָה, וּרְבִיעִית שֶׁמֶן לְנָזִיר, וְאַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם שֶׁבֵּין נִדָּה לְנִדָּה – הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said to** Rabbi Akiva: **Akiva,** even **if you** were to **amplify** *halakhot* **the entire day** from the terms **"with oil," "with oil," I** would **not listen to you** and accept your claims. **Rather,** the *halakha* that **a half-*log* of oil** is required **for** the **thanks offering, and** similarly, the *halakha* that **a quarter-*log* of oil** is required **for** the loaves of **a nazirite, and** the *halakha* that a woman who experiences an emission of blood during the **eleven days that are between** one seven-day period of **menstruation** and the next period of **menstruation** is a *zava*, each of these is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai;** they are not derived from verses.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah responds to Rabbi Akiva with characteristic directness: *Akiva*, אם אתה מרבה כל היום כולו *bashamen mixed* *bashamen mixed* איני שומע לך — you can multiply *bashemens* all day long, I will not accept it. This is not how we know these things. Rashi understands Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah to be bothered specifically by the technical point the *Gemara* raised — that it is not truly *ribui you're never going* אחר *ribui you're never going*, since the first *bashamen mixed* functions for its own sake. But the objection seems more fundamental: there is no reliable path from textual nuances of this kind to precise quantitative conclusions, and Rabbi Akiva's method of deriving exact *shiurim you're* from the repetition of words is simply not accepted by all *tanna'im agreed*.

This exchange is part of a recognizable pattern in the *Gemara*. It is not always the same *tanna who's issuing* who challenges Rabbi Akiva — sometimes it is Rabbi Yishmael, sometimes others — but Rabbi Akiva is consistently the one addressed. His approach, according to the *Gemara*, extended even to דרשת הקוצו של יוד, finding legal significance in the smallest textual details. Other *tanna'im agreed* did not reject his conclusions outright in all cases, but insisted that you cannot reach those conclusions through hermeneutics alone. Perhaps the ruling is known from tradition; perhaps it is learned elsewhere. But you will not get everything from textual derivation alone.

### Mesorah & Shiurim

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah then states what these *shiurim you're* truly are: חצי *Log* *shemen* לתודה — the half *Log* for the *todah*; ורביעית *shemen* לנזיר — the quarter *Log* for the *nazir*'s *mincha*, which is also far less than the standard *Log* per *issaron*; ואחד עשר יום שבין *niddah* לנדה — and the eleven days between *niddah* cycles. All of these are *halacha* למשה *MiSinai*. They come from tradition, not from hermeneutical derivation.

The inclusion of the eleven days warrants brief explanation, particularly since it will be treated at length in מסכת *niddah*. According to the *torah*, when a woman bleeds during her menstrual cycle, she is a *niddah* for seven days, after which she immerses in the *mikvah*. If she bleeds outside her menstrual period — בלא עת נדתה — and does so for three consecutive days, she becomes a *zavah*, requiring seven clean days before immersion. The question is: what constitutes "outside her menstrual period"? The answer, transmitted as *halacha* למשה *MiSinai*, is that after the seven days of *niddah*, there is an eleven-day window during which any bleeding is considered non-menstrual. Any bleeding after those eleven days may again be menstrual. Together, this implies an assumed minimum menstrual cycle of eighteen days.

It is worth noting that there is indeed a *Sifra* that attempts to derive the eleven days from the *pesukim*, but that discussion belongs to מסכת *niddah*.

The broader methodological point here is a rich one. There is a genuine debate, both among *Rishonim* and scholars generally, about whether the *chachamim more oil* began with knowledge of the *halacha* and worked backward to find textual support — *asmakhta okay* — or whether they were actually deriving the law from the text. The truth is probably somewhere in between and varies by case. When a real *machlokes* turns on how to read a *pasuk says*, one gets the sense of genuine derivation. In other cases, the sense is stronger that the conclusion was known and the verse is being recruited in its support.

The Rambam addressed this indirectly in his discussion of *gezera* שוה. He was troubled by the fact that the *Gemara* requires a *messorah becomes forgotten* for a *gezera* שוה to be used, yet the *Gemara* still entertains questions like "why derive it this way rather than that way?" — which would make no sense if the specific derivation were itself the *messorah becomes forgotten*. The Rambam's resolution is that the *messorah becomes forgotten* is not for the particular *gezera* שוה, but for the principle that the law may be derived through *gezera* שוה — while the specific construction of the derivation remains open to analysis. This represents a middle position between pure *asmakhta okay* and genuine textual derivation: the conclusion is known, the general method of derivation is authorized, and the work of the *tanna'im agreed* is to identify the most coherent path between the text and the known result.

## Log Per Mincha

### Fixed Log Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּלוֹג הָיָה מוֹדֵד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that with the vessel of one ***log* one would measure** the oil for all the standard meal offerings.

The *Mishna mentioned* states that one always measures with a *Log*, even for a *mincha* of sixty *Esronot 6-4-3* — one does not use a larger measure but simply measures with a *Log* sixty times. This parallels the rule for solid measures, where the only vessel is an *issaron*: even for a large *mincha*, one measures out sixty times.

### Metzora Paradigm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּמְצוֹרָע עָנִי כְּתִיב "עִשָּׂרוֹן ... בָּלוּל ... וְלֹג", לִימֵּד עַל עִשָּׂרוֹן שֶׁטָּעוּן לוֹג, דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **With regard to a poor leper,** who cannot afford the standard offerings that are required as part of the purification process and is instead required to bring one lamb as a guilt offering and a tenth of an ephah of flour as a meal offering, **it is written:** "And **a tenth**-**part** of an ephah of fine flour **mixed** with oil for a meal offering, **and a *log*** of oil" (Leviticus 14:21). The verse juxtaposes the need for a *log* of oil with the fact the offering is made of a tenth of an ephah of flour. Accordingly, it **teaches about** each **tenth** of an ephah of flour **that it requires** one ***log* of oil;** this is **the statement of the Rabbis.**

The *braita teaches* establishes: במצורע עני כתיב *issaron* סולת בלול *bashamen mixed* *v'log* *shemen* — it says of the poor *metzora*, one *issaron* of flour mixed with oil, and a *Log* of oil. לימד על *issaron* שטעון *Log* — this teaches that each *issaron* requires a *Log*. That is the position of the *chachamim more oil*.

The verse in question reads: ואם דל הוא ואין ידו משגת ולקח *Keves* אחד *asham generically does* לתנופה לכפר עליו ועשרון סולת אחד בלול *bashamen mixed* למנחה ולג *shemen* — if he is poor, he brings one lamb for an *asham generically does*, one *issaron* of flour mixed with oil for a *mincha*, and a *Log* of oil. It must be noted that this *Log* of oil is not what is mixed into the *mincha*; it is used for the ritual of the *metzora* itself, applied to the ear and so on. Yet because the verse juxtaposes an *issaron* with a *Log*, we use it as the paradigm establishing a *Log* per *issaron* for all *menachot*. This is not the plain meaning of the verse — it is a *Drasha* — but that is how the ratio is derived.

### Log Ratio Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ מִנְחָה שֶׁל שִׁשִּׁים עֶשְׂרוֹנִים אֵין לָהּ אֶלָּא לוּגָּהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לְמִנְחָה וְלֹג שֶׁמֶן".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Eliezer say:** Each meal offering, irrespective of its size, and **even a meal offering of sixty tenths** of an ephah of flour, requires **only its** single ***log*** of oil, **as it is stated** with regard to offering of a leper: "And a tenth of an ephah of fine flour mixed with oil **for a meal offering, and a *log* of oil."** The juxtaposition of "a meal offering" with "a *log* of oil" teaches a principle for all meal offerings: Each offering requires only one *log* of oil.

Rabbi Nechemia and Rabbi Eliezer disagree: אפילו *mincha* של ששים *Esronot 6-4-3* אין לה *ella rather* לוגה — even a *mincha* of sixty *Esronot 6-4-3* has only one *Log*. This follows from reading the verse as למנחה *v'log* *shemen* — not "one *issaron* and a *Log*" but "the *mincha* and a *Log*," meaning no matter the size of the *mincha*, it receives one *Log*.

The *Gemara* then asks: what do Rabbi Nechemia and Rabbi Eliezer do with the juxtaposition of *issaron* and *Log*? 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, הַאי "עִשָּׂרוֹן בָּלוּל וְלֹג" מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? הָהוּא לְגוּפֵיהּ, דְּקָא אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: לַיְיתֵי חַד עִשָּׂרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, what do they do with this** verse: "And **a tenth**-**part** of an ephah of fine flour **mixed** with oil for a meal offering **and a *log*** of oil," which links the need for a *log* of oil with the fact the offering is made of a tenth of an ephah of flour? The Gemara explains: **That** verse is required **to** teach the matter **itself, as the Merciful One states: Let** a poor leper **bring** an offering of just **a tenth of an ephah** of flour.

They respond that ההוא *l'gufia* — that mention of *issaron* is needed to state the law of the *metzora* itself: the *torah* is telling us that the *metzora* brings a *mincha* of one *issaron*. It is not teaching the *Log*-per-*issaron* ratio.

The *chachamim more oil* counter: 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, לְגוּפֵיהּ לָא צְרִיךְ, מִדְּגַלִּי רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי מְצוֹרָע שְׁלֹשָׁה קׇרְבָּנוֹת וּשְׁלֹשָׁה עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת, הָכָא דְּחַד קׇרְבָּן – חַד עִשָּׂרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the other,** i.e., the Rabbis, how do they respond to this claim? They maintain that **it is not necessary to** teach the matter **itself,** as **from** the fact **that the Merciful One revealed with regard to a leper** who is not poor that he must bring **three** animal **offerings and** a meal offering of **three-tenths** of an ephah as part of his purification process, it may be inferred that **here,** with regard to a poor leper, **who** brings only **one offering,** that he similarly brings a meal offering of only **a tenth of an ephah.** Accordingly, the fact that the verse mentions that his offering is only a tenth of an ephah is superfluous and can teach a principle for all meal offerings.

לגמרי לא אשכחן — we never find the *torah* eliminating a requirement entirely; it only substitutes. Since the *torah* replaced two sheep with two birds rather than eliminating them, it would never simply drop the *mincha*.

Rabbi Nechemia and Rabbi Eliezer respond: 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ – אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְחָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלֵיהּ לְאֵתוֹיֵי בְּדַלּוּת, אֵימָא לָא לִיבְעֵי מִנְחָה כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: **And** as for **the other,** Rabbi Neḥemya and Rabbi Eliezer, how would they respond to this claim? They maintain that it **was necessary** for the verse to state the size of his meal offering, as otherwise it might **enter your mind to say** that **since the Merciful One spared** the money of the poor leper by allowing him **to bring** an offering affordable **in poverty,** one might **say** that the Merciful One **does not require from him** to bring any meal offering **at all.**

This entire dispute thus turns on whether the word *issaron* is redundant. If it is redundant, it serves to establish the universal ratio of *Log* per *issaron*. If it is not redundant — if it was needed to specify that the *metzora* brings one *issaron* at all — then it establishes only the local law of the *metzora* and teaches nothing about the universal ratio.

### Derivation Methods

*[Continued from above]*

This discussion opens onto a broader methodological question that is worth dwelling upon. As the text of the *torah* does not provide a *Shiur* for how much oil goes into a *mincha* — it simply says the flour is mixed with oil — the entire enterprise of establishing a *Shiur* requires a derivation, and that derivation comes from this one case of the *metzora*, where the *Log* is technically not the amount mixed into the *mincha* at all, but is the separate *Log* used for the ritual. The juxtaposition alone is the basis for establishing the ratio. Then there is a further dispute as to what that ratio even is.

One might reasonably conclude that in cases such as this, the *chachamim more oil* knew the *halacha* and were working to identify its textual grounding. This is the middle position the Rambam implies: not pure *asmakhta okay*, in which the derivation is entirely post-hoc and decorative, and not pure textual derivation, in which the law is unknown until the verse is analyzed. Rather, there is a tradition that the law is derivable from the text, and the work of derivation — though not free-form — proceeds with a known destination in mind.

The *Gemara*'s final statement here is worth noting: when the *Mishna mentioned* says it teaches about the minimum size of a *mincha* — that it cannot be less than an *issaron* — this follows from למנחה *v'log*: something that requires a *Log* must by definition be at least an *issaron*. Rabbi Nechemia and Rabbi Eliezer extract two teachings from this phrase simultaneously: the minimum size of a *mincha*, and the rule that a *mincha* of any size receives only one *Log*.

## Nesachim Ratios

### Hin Conversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִין – תְּרֵיסַר לוּגֵּי הָוַיִין, דִּכְתִיב: "שֶׁמֶן זַיִת הִין", וּכְתִיב: "שֶׁמֶן מִשְׁחַת קֹדֶשׁ יִהְיֶה זֶה לִי לְדֹרֹתֵיכֶם", זֶה בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא תְּרֵיסַר הָוַיִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: Now, one ***hin* is twelve *log*, as it is written: "And of olive oil a *hin*"** (Exodus 30:24), **and it is written** afterward in the same verse: **"Sacred anointing oil, this [*zeh*] shall be for Me, throughout your generations."** The **numerical value [*gimatriyya*] of *zeh* is twelve.** Once it is established that one *hin* is twelve *log*, it is possible to calculate how may *log* are in one-half, one-third, and one-quarter of a *hin*.

The *Mishna mentioned* taught: ששה לפר ארבעה לאיל שלשה לכבש — six לוגין for an ox, four for a ram, three for a lamb. These are derived from the rule that a *hin it's* is twelve לוגין: half a *hin it's* for a *par* is six, a third for an *ayil* is four, a quarter for a *Keves* is three. The question is: how do we know a *hin it's* is twelve לוגין?

The verse states *shemen* זית *hin it's* — the oil used for the *shemen* המשחה was a *hin it's*. A second verse states *shemen* משחת קודש יהיה *zeh* לי לדורותיכם — this shall be the holy oil of anointing. The word *zeh* בגימטריא תריסר הוי — the numerical value of *zeh* is twelve (zayin = 7, heh = 5), hence a *hin it's* is twelve לוגין. One might ask why a scriptural verse is needed for what might seem to be a self-evident conversion between known measures, but the *torah* mentions both *Log* and *hin it's* without ever stating the conversion factor explicitly. Rather than rely on transmitted knowledge alone, the *Gemara* anchors the conversion in a textual derivation — though here too, one presumes this is effectively an *asmakhta okay*.

### Menorah Night Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה וּמֶחֱצָה לַמְּנוֹרָה, חֲצִי לוֹג לְכׇל נֵר. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: "מֵעֶרֶב עַד בֹּקֶר" – תֵּן לָהּ מִדָּתָהּ שֶׁתְּהֵא דּוֹלֶקֶת וְהוֹלֶכֶת מֵעֶרֶב עַד בֹּקֶר. דָּבָר אַחֵר: "מֵעֶרֶב עַד בֹּקֶר" – אֵין לְךָ עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁכְּשֵׁירָה מֵעֶרֶב עַד בֹּקֶר אֶלָּא זוֹ בִּלְבַד. וְשִׁיעֲרוּ חֲכָמִים חֲצִי לוֹג מֵאוּרְתָּא וְעַד צַפְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Three and a half** *log* of oil were required **for the Candelabrum,** as there were seven lamps and **a half-*log*** was required **for each lamp.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the Candelabrum: "Aaron and his sons shall arrange it **from evening to morning,** before the Lord" (Exodus 27:21). This indicates that **you shall put into** each lamp **its** required **quantity** of oil **so that it will continue burning from evening until morning. Alternatively,** the phrase **"from evening to morning"** indicates that the mitzva is fulfilled throughout the night, and **you have no** other **rite that is valid from evening until morning except for this one alone. And the Sages calculated** that **a half-*log*** of oil for each lamp is necessary to ensure that they continue burning **from evening until morning.**

The *Mishna mentioned* also taught: שלשה ומחצה למנורה חצי *Log* לכל נר — three and a half לוגין for the *Menorah*, a half *Log* per lamp. The *braita teaches* derives this from the verse *meariev* עד בוקר — the oil must be sufficient to burn from evening through the morning. The teaching is twofold: first, that the oil must be measured to last the full night; and second, that the lighting of the *Menorah* is אין לך *Avoda* שכשירה *meariev* עד בוקר *ella rather* זו בלבד — the only *Avoda* that is valid specifically from evening through the night. ושיערו *chachamim more oil* חצי *Log* מאורתא ועד צפרא — the *chachamim more oil* estimated that a half *Log* per lamp, half a *Log* from evening until morning, is the required amount.

### Estimation Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה שִׁיעֲרוּ, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: מִמַּטָּה לְמַעְלָה שִׁיעֲרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** How did the Sages reach the conclusion that a half-*log* of oil is needed? **There are** those **who say** that the Sages **calculated** it **by** initially using a large quantity of oil, more than necessary to burn throughout the night, and then **decreasing** the quantity by a small amount each night until they saw that at the end of the night there was no oil remaining. **And there are** those **who say** that **they calculated** it **by** initially using a small quantity of oil and then **increasing** the quantity each night until they saw that the quantity was sufficient to allow the lamps to burn throughout the night.

The *Gemara* records two views on how this estimation was actually conducted. איכא דאמרי מלמעלה למטה שיערו — some say they began with a larger amount and reduced it incrementally, and איכא דאמרי מלמטה למעלה שיערו — others say they began with a smaller amount and increased it.

The rationale behind each approach reflects competing values. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר מִמַּטָּה לְמַעְלָה שִׁיעֲרוּ – הַתּוֹרָה חָסָה עַל מָמוֹנָן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִמַּעְלָה לְמַטָּה שִׁיעֲרוּ – אֵין עֲנִיּוּת בִּמְקוֹם עֲשִׁירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: The **one who said** that **they calculated** it **by increasing** the quantity each night holds that they did so in accordance with the principle that **the Torah spared the money of the Jewish people,** so the Sages wished to minimize the financial cost of their experimentation. **And** the **one who said** that **they calculated** it **by decreasing** the quantity each night holds that in the Temple one's actions should not be motivated by a concern for the financial cost, as **in a place of wealth there is no poverty.**

It is worth noting, as the *Yerushalmi* records, that in the summer months when the nights are short, thicker wicks were used so the oil would not continue burning into the next day, while in the winter thinner wicks were used to ensure the oil lasted through the longer nights. The *chachamim more oil*'s estimation of the half *Log* was thus calibrated for an average night, with the wick size adjusted seasonally.

### Tosafot Challenge

*[Continued from above]*

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question on the phrase ושיערו *chachamim more oil*: למה היה צריך לקדש השמן בכלי שרת? Why was it necessary to sanctify the *Menorah* oil in a כלי שרת at all? The *Gemara* had already assumed that the measuring vessels used for the *Menorah* oil served as כלי שרת, and that this was in fact the only reason a half-*Log* measure was needed as a כלי שרת.

Tosafot's question cuts to something fundamental. Normally a כלי שרת is required because the object being sanctified is a *korban* — flour is offered, wine is poured, oil is mixed into a *mincha*. But the oil of the *Menorah* is not being offered. One is not quantitatively sacrificing a certain amount; the goal is to achieve light. If it is a short night, less oil suffices. This is unlike a *mincha* or a wine libation, where precise quantities are intrinsic to the act of offering. The proof is precisely the *Shiur* discussion: had this been an offering of a fixed quantity, the question of the night's length would be irrelevant.

This observation sharpens a broader distinction: the הדלקת המנורה is an *Avoda* performed in the *Mekadesh*, but it may not be a *korban* in the technical sense. This distinction has implications elsewhere — for example, in the discussion of why the principle of טומאה הותרה בציבור was not invoked to override the need for pure oil at the time of the miracle of Chanukah. If the *Menorah* is not a *korban*, the rules governing public offerings in a state of impurity may simply not apply. Tosafot's brief remark thus opens onto a significant question about the nature of the *Menorah* service.

## Mixing Minchot

### Par-Ayil Mix OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעָרְבִין נִסְכֵי פָרִים בְּנִסְכֵי אֵילִים, נִסְכֵי כְבָשִׂים בְּנִסְכֵי כְבָשִׂים, שֶׁל יָחִיד בְּשֶׁל צִיבּוּר, שֶׁל יוֹם בְּשֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Many animal offerings are brought together with a meal offering and a wine libation. These additions are collectively referred to as libations. **One may mix together the libations of bulls with the libations of rams;** the meal offerings may be mixed as they both share the same ratio of flour to oil, i.e., two *log* of oil for each tenth of an ephah of flour. Likewise, one may mix together **the libations of lambs with the libations of** other **lambs,** as the meal offerings both share the same ratio of three *log* of oil for each tenth of an ephah of flour. And one may mix together the libations **of** the offering of **an individual with** those **of a communal** offering. And one may mix together the libations **of** an offering brought on one **day with** those **of the day before,** if the meal offerings have the same ratio of oil to flour.

The *Mishna mentioned* taught: מערבין *niskei* *par* עם *niskei* אילים — one may mix the *menachot* of oxen with those of rams. This is because the ratio of flour to oil is consistent between them: three *Esronot 6-4-3* for a *par* with six לוגין of oil and wine, two *Esronot 6-4-3* for an *ayil* with four לוגין — both maintain a two-to-one ratio. If those *menachot* get mixed together, the proportion of oil to flour has not changed, and one may offer them together on the *mizbe'ach*.

### Keves Mix Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אֵין מְעָרְבִין נִסְכֵי כְבָשִׂים בְּנִסְכֵי פָרִים וְאֵילִים, וְאִם בְּלָלָן אֵלּוּ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן וְאֵלּוּ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן וְנִתְעָרְבוּ – כְּשֵׁרִים, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּלַל – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **But one may not mix together the libations of lambs with the libations of bulls or** the libations of **rams,** as the meal offerings have different ratios of oil to flour. **And** nevertheless, **if one intermingled** the flour and oil of **these** lamb offerings **by themselves and** the flour and oil of **these** bull or ram offerings **by themselves, and** only then **were they mixed together,** then they remain **fit** to be sacrificed. **If** they were mixed together **before** the oil and flour of each offering **were** independently **intermingled** to form the meal offering, then they are **disqualified.**

By contrast, the *Keves* receives three *Esronot 6-4-3* of flour with three לוגין of oil and wine — a one-to-one ratio, rather than two-to-one. Mixing the *mincha* of a *Keves* with that of a *par* or *ayil* disrupts the proportions: one becomes too oil-rich and the other too dry. Hence אבל אין מערבין *niskei* כבשים בנסכי פרים ואילים — one may not mix the *mincha* of lambs with that of oxen or rams.

The *Mishna mentioned* also specifies that this applies regardless of whether the *korban* is an individual's or communal, and even when mixing offerings from different days — since the *mincha* accompanying a *korban* does not need to be brought on the same day as the *korban* itself: מנחתם ונסכיהם אפילו מכאן ועד עשרה ימים, it may be brought up to ten days later. Provided the *menachot* being mixed are of the same proportional type, they may be combined.

### Blila First

*[Continued from above]*

The *Mishna mentioned* adds a crucial qualification: ואם בללם אלו בפני עצמן ואלו בפני עצמן *V'nitarvu* *Ksherim* — if one first mixed each type of dough separately — the flour with its appropriate oil — and they subsequently became intermingled, the result is *kasher*. But אם עד שלא בלל *pasul because it'll* — if the mixing occurred before the individual blendings, it is *pasul because it'll*.

The governing principle is that the *Mitzvah* of *Blila* — the proper mixing of flour with oil in the correct proportions — must be fulfilled. Once each portion of dough has been mixed correctly, it is valid; the subsequent intermingling does not invalidate it even if proportions become confused, because the obligation has already been discharged.

### Burn Separately

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״וְהִקְטִירוֹ״, שֶׁלֹּא יְעָרֵב חֲלָבִים בַּחֲלָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that different types of libations may be mixed together provided that the meal offerings have the same flour to oil ratio. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this from a *baraita* discussing the verse: "And he shall sacrifice, from the peace offerings, a fire to the Lord: The fat covering the innards, and all the fat that is on the innards, and the two kidneys with the fat that is on them, which is over the loins; and the diaphragm with the liver, with the kidneys, he shall take away. And Aaron's sons shall burn it on the altar, apart from the burnt offering, which is on the wood that is on the fire; it is a fire of a pleasing aroma to the Lord" (Leviticus 3:3–5). The direct object of the term: **"And they shall burn,"** i.e., the pronoun "it," is singular, despite referring to the many types of fats listed in the verse. This indicates **that one may not mix fats** of one offering **with the fats** of a different offering, but should burn the parts from each offering separately. Similarly, it follows that the libations accompanying different animal offerings should not be mixed together.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge. A *braita teaches* derives from the verse *vehiktiru* — written in the singular — that שלא יערב חלבים בחלבים: one must not mix the fats of one sacrifice with those of another. More broadly, this teaches that anything burned on the *mizbe'ach* must be burned separately. How, then, does our *Mishna mentioned* permit mixing *menachot* — which are ultimately burned — even when they are of the same proportional type?

### Wine Mixing Criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: "אִם נִתְעָרְבוּ" קָא אָמַר. אִי הָכִי, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין נִסְכֵי כְבָשִׂים בְּנִסְכֵי פָרִים וְאֵילִים, וַאֲפִילּוּ נִתְעָרְבוּ נָמֵי לָא? וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: בְּלָלָן אֵלּוּ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן וְאֵלּוּ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָן וְנִתְעָרְבוּ כְּשֵׁרִין, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא לְכַתְּחִלָּה קָא אָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The mishna **stated** only that **if** libations of different offerings **were mixed** together one may still sacrifice them, but not that this is permitted *ab initio*. Accordingly, there is no contradiction between the mishna and *baraita*. The Gemara infers: **If that is so,** then when the mishna states in its first clause: **And one may not mix together the libations of lambs with the libations of bulls or** the libations of **rams,** the intention is that **even** after the fact, if **they were mixed together,** they are **also not** valid. The Gemara challenges: **But from** the fact **that the latter clause teaches:** If **one intermingled** the flour and oil of **these** lamb offerings **by themselves and** the flour and oil of **these** bull or ram offerings **by themselves and** only then **were they mixed together** then they remain **fit** to be sacrificed, which is explicitly referring to the *halakha* after the fact, **it may be inferred that the first clause stated** the *halakha **ab initio***.

Rabbi Yochanan suggests reading the permissive language of the *Mishna mentioned* as אי נתערבו — referring to what happens if they become mixed, not to active mixing *lechatchila*. However, the *Gemara* objects: if so, the prohibitive language later in the *Mishna mentioned* — אין מערבין *niskei* כבשים בנסכי פרים — would mean that if they do become mixed, they are invalid *bedi'avad situations*. But the end of the *Mishna mentioned* shows otherwise: *V'nitarvu* *Ksherim* — they are *kasher*, precisely in the *bedi'avad situations* case of having been accidentally mixed. מכלל דרישא *lechatchila* קאמר — the opening clause therefore must be stating a *lechatchila* permission to mix. The contradiction with the principle of burning separately thus stands.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכִי קָאָמַר, מְעָרְבִין יֵינָן, אִם נִתְעָרֵב סׇלְתָּן וְשַׁמְנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that **this** is what the mishna **is saying:** When different animal offerings are sacrificed, **their wine** libations **may be mixed together** *ab initio*, but only **if their fine flour and their oil** from their respective meal offerings **were** already **mixed together,** albeit improperly.

Abaye resolves the difficulty with a reinterpretation of the *Mishna mentioned*. The prohibition of *vehiktiru* — burning separately — applies to *haktara burning*, the actual burning. Wine is poured out but not burned; therefore, wine from different sacrifices may be mixed without violating this principle. Oil and flour, however, are burned as part of the *mincha* and must be kept separate.

In practice, one would want to maintain this distinction consistently: do not mix wines lest you come to mix flour and oil as well. But in two circumstances, this concern falls away. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵינָן לְכִתְחִלָּה לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּסֹלֶת וָשֶׁמֶן, אֲבָל יַיִן מְעָרְבִין. אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכִי קָאָמַר – הֵיכָא דְּהוּקְטַר סׇלְתָּן וְשַׁמְנָן, מְעָרְבִין יַיִן לְכַתְּחִלָּה. הֵיכָא דְּלָא הוּקְטַר – אִם נִתְעָרֵב סׇלְתָּן וְשַׁמְנָן, מְעָרְבִין נָמֵי יֵינָן, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין מְעָרְבִין, דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאִיעָרוֹבֵי סֹלֶת וְשֶׁמֶן לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is it correct that one may **not** mix together the **wine** libations of different offerings ***ab initio*** unless their flour and oil were already mixed? **But isn't it taught** in the continuation of the *baraita* just cited: **In what** case **is this statement,** that one may not mix together parts from different offerings, **said?** It is said only **with regard to flour and oil. But one may mix together** the **wine** libations of different offerings. The implication is that this is the *halakha* even if their meal offerings had not been mixed together. **Rather, Abaye said** that **this** is what the mishna **is saying:** With regard to offerings of similar types of animals, **where their fine flour and their oil** from their respective meal offerings **have been burned** on the altar, **one may mix together** the **wine** libations ***ab initio*.** And even **where** their fine flour and oil **have not been burned, if their fine flour and oil have** at least **been mixed together, one may mix together their wine** libations *ab initio*. **But if** the fine flour and oil have **not** been mixed together, then **one may not mix** the libations **together.** The reason is that if one does mix the libations, **perhaps one will come to mix together** their **flour and oil *ab initio*,** which is prohibited.

First: היכא דהוקטר סולת ושמן — once the flour and oil have already been burned, there is no longer any risk of coming to mix them, and the wine may therefore be mixed *lechatchila*. Second: if the flour and oil happened to get mixed — and were of matching proportions and hence still valid — then mixing their wines is permitted as well.

Abaye thus reads the *Mishna mentioned* as referring throughout to wine, not to the flour-and-oil *mincha* itself. The active mixing permitted by the *Mishna mentioned* is the mixing of wines, either after the *menachot* have been burned or under the condition that the *menachot* themselves have already gotten mixed and are *kasher*. Mixing the flour and oil *menachot* actively and *lechatchila* remains prohibited on account of the rule of *haktara burning*.

The intermingling of the second half of the *Mishna mentioned* — where pre-mixed doughs that subsequently got mixed together are *kasher* — remains as a separate teaching about the timing requirement of *Blila*: the key moment is when the dough is properly constituted, not when it is burned.

## Omer Lamb Offering

### Double Flour Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֶּבֶשׂ הַבָּא עִם הָעוֹמֶר, תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "וּמִנְחָתוֹ שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנִים", לִימֵּד עַל כֶּבֶשׂ הַבָּא עִם הָעוֹמֶר שֶׁמִּנְחָתוֹ כְּפוּלָה. יָכוֹל כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמִּנְחָתוֹ כְּפוּלָה, כָּךְ יֵינוֹ כָּפוּל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "וְנִסְכּוֹ יַיִן רְבִיעִית הַהִין". יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא יֵינוֹ כָּפוּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִבְלָל עִם מִנְחָתוֹ, אֲבָל יְהֵא שַׁמְנוֹ כָּפוּל שֶׁנִּבְלָל עִם מִנְחָתוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "וְנִסְכּוֹ" – כׇּל נְסָכָיו לֹא יְהוּ אֶלָּא רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the lamb** offering **that comes with the *omer*** meal offering, which is accompanied by another meal offering and a wine libation, **even though** the quantity of flour used in **its meal offering is doubled,** i.e., one uses twice the amount that is generally used for meal offerings that accompany the sacrifice of a lamb, **its** oil and wine **libations were not doubled;** rather, three *log* of oil and three *log* of wine were used, per the standard quantities used for a lamb. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the *omer* meal offering: **"And its meal offering shall be two tenths"** (Leviticus 23:13). This verse **teaches about** the **lamb that comes with the *omer* that** the size of **its meal offering is doubled.** One **might** have thought that **just as its meal offering is doubled, so** too **its wine** libation should be **doubled,** i.e., instead of using a quarter-*hin*, as is generally done for lambs, one should use a half-*hin*. To counter this, **the verse states,** in its continuation: **"And its libation shall be of wine, a quarter-*hin*"** (Leviticus 23:13). One **might** have thought that it is only **its wine** libation that **should not be doubled, as it is not intermingled with** the flour of **its meal offering, but its oil should be doubled, as it is intermingled with** the flour of **its meal offering.** To counter this, **the verse states: "And its libation** shall be of wine, a quarter-*hin*," which teaches that **all of its libations shall be only a quarter**-***hin***, but no more.

The final section of the *Mishna mentioned* concerns the lamb brought with the *omer*. The *torah* specifies שני *esronim* for this lamb's *mincha* — double the normal amount of flour. The question is whether the oil and wine are similarly doubled. The *braita teaches* states: יכול כשם שמנחתו כפולה כך יינו כפול — perhaps since the flour is doubled, the wine is also doubled? The verse answers: *unisko means* יין *revi'it* ההין — the wine is explicitly a quarter *hin it's*, which is three לוגין, the standard amount for a lamb. The wine is not doubled.

One might then propose: יכול לא יהיה יינו כפול שאין נבלל עם מנחתו — wine is not doubled because it is not blended with the flour; but שמנו כפול שנבלל עם מנחתו — the oil should be doubled since it is mixed with the flour, and the general principle of the *chachamim more oil* is that more flour requires more oil. The verse answers again: *unisko means* — the full *Log*, all of its liquid measures, לא יהיה *ella rather* *revi'it* — none of them are doubled; all remain at the standard *revi'it* ההין.

### Niskah-Nisko Equation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כְּתִיב "וְנִסְכָּהּ", וְקָרֵינַן "וְנִסְכּוֹ". כֵּיצַד? "נִסְכָּהּ" דְּמִנְחָה כְּ"נִסְכּוֹ" דְּיַיִן – מָה יַיִן רְבִיעִית, אַף שֶׁמֶן נָמֵי רְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the final proof in the *baraita*: **What is the** biblical **derivation** here? **Rabbi Elazar says:** There is an ambiguity as to whether the possessive pronoun in the term "and its libation" is referring to the lamb offering or the meal offering, both of which are mentioned previously in the verse. This is due to a disparity between the way the Hebrew word for the term is written and the way it is vocalized. **It is written** as ***veniskah*,** with the possessive pronoun in the feminine form. This would be referring to the meal offering [*minḥa*], which is a feminine noun. Accordingly, this means: The libation of the meal offering, and it is referring to the oil that is intermingled in the meal offering. **And we read it** as ***venisko*,** with the possessive pronoun in the masculine form. This would be referring to the lamb offering itself [*keves*], which is a masculine noun. Accordingly, this means: The libation of the lamb offering, which is a reference to the wine libation that accompanies the lamb offering. **How** can this be explained? It teaches that **the libation of the meal offering,** i.e., its oil, **is like the libation of** the lamb **of wine: Just as** the quantity of **wine** used is **a quarter**-*hin*, **so too,** the quantity of **oil** used is **a quarter**-*hin* and no more.

The *Gemara* asks where this is derived. Rabbi Elazar states: כתיב *u'nishkah* וקרינן *unisko means* — the word is written with a ה but read with a ו. How are both readings meaningful? *nishkah* דמנחה כנסכו דיין — the *nishkah* of the *mincha*, meaning its liquid component, is equated with the *nisko* of the wine. Just as the wine libation is a *revi'it* ההין, so too the oil of the *mincha*: מה יין *revi'it* אף *shemen* נמי *revi'it* — the oil, like the wine, remains at the standard quantity and is not doubled along with the flour.

## Sheelo Lishmo Status

### Asham Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, טָעוּן נְסָכִים, שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן – פְּסַלְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a related discussion: A guilt offering brought by a leper as part of his purification process is distinct from other guilt offerings in that there is an additional requirement that it must be brought together with a meal offering and a wine libation. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of **a guilt offering of a leper that one slaughtered not for its own sake,** although the leper can therefore no longer fulfill his obligation with it, the offering continues to be regarded as a guilt offering of a leper and still **requires libations,** i.e., a meal offering and a wine libation; **as if you do not say this, you have disqualified it.** To bring it without libations one would have to regard it as a standard guilt offering, but the *halakha* is that an offering may not be sacrificed for a different purpose than the one for which it was originally consecrated.

Rabbi Yochanan states: *asham generically does* *metzora* ששחטו שלא לשמו טעון *nesachim* — an *asham generically does* *metzora* that was slaughtered שלא לשמו still requires its accompanying *mincha*. The reasoning: שאם אי אתה אומר כן *pasalto you'll make* — if you do not say this, you will have invalidated it. Since an *asham generically does* *metzora* that is brought without its *mincha* is invalid, and this sacrifice is still valid as a *korban* even though it does not discharge the owner's obligation, it must retain its original identity as an *asham generically does* *metzora* and therefore must be accompanied by its *mincha*.

The underlying question is significant. Normally an *asham generically does* does not come with *nesachim*; the *mincha* here is unique to the *metzora* ritual, as we read earlier in the daf. When such a *korban* is brought שלא לשמו, it is valid but does not fulfill the owner's obligation. Does it then retain its original identity as an *asham generically does* *metzora*, with all the attendant requirements? Or does it shed that identity and become a generic *asham generically does* — which would require no *mincha*?

### Identity Retention Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר גַּדָּא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, כֶּבֶשׂ הַבָּא עִם הָעוֹמֶר שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, תְּהֵא מִנְחָתוֹ כְּפוּלָה, שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן – פְּסַלְתּוֹ. וְתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, יְהֵא טָעוּן שְׁנֵי גְזִירִין בְּכֹהֵן אֶחָד, שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן – פְּסַלְתּוֹ. וְתָמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, יְהֵא טָעוּן שְׁנֵי גְזִירִין בִּשְׁנֵי כֹהֲנִים, שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן – פְּסַלְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Menashya bar Gadda objects to this: If that is so,** that whenever an offering is consecrated for a specific purpose that requires various additional conditions to be fulfilled beyond those normally required for that type of offering, then even if it is then slaughtered not for its own sake those conditions must still be fulfilled in order for the offering to be valid; then with regard to the **lamb** offering **that comes with the *omer*** meal offering the *halakha* should likewise be that if **one slaughtered it not for its own sake,** the flour in **its meal offering should** still **be doubled** in quantity, as normally required for the *omer* meal offering, because it continues to be regarded as an *omer* meal offering, **as if you do not say this, you have disqualified it.** And similarly, with regard to the **daily morning offering,** if **one slaughtered it not for its own sake, it should** still **require** the arrangement of **two logs** of wood on the fire on the altar **by one priest,** because it continues to be regarded as a daily morning offering; **as if you do not say this, you have disqualified it. And** similarly, with regard to the **daily afternoon offering,** if **one slaughtered it not for its own sake, it should** still **require** the arrangement of **two logs** of wood on the fire on the altar **by two priests,** because it continues to be regarded as a daily afternoon offering; **as if you do not say this, you have disqualified it.**

Rav Menashya bar Gadata challenges Rabbi Yochanan with a series of parallel cases. If the principle is that a *korban* brought שלא לשמו retains its full original identity and therefore all its requirements must be maintained — otherwise it would be invalidated — then consider: *Keves* הבא עם העומר ששחטו שלא לשמו תהא מנחתו כפולה — the lamb of the *omer* brought שלא לשמו should require the doubled *mincha* of שני *Esronot 6-4-3*, for otherwise you would invalidate it. Similarly: *tamid* של שחר ששחטו שלא לשמו יהא טעון שני גזירים ושני *kohanim* — the morning *tamid* brought שלא לשמו should require the two logs of wood and two *kohanim* associated with the *tamid* offering, since the *Gemara* assumes these logs of wood are an essential part of the *tamid*. Likewise for the afternoon *tamid*. Each case would equally have to retain its full original character, with all attendant requirements.

The force of the challenge is that it extends the principle to conclusions that seem difficult to sustain. If the identity is fully retained in every case, the consequences ramify broadly.

### Ein Hachi Nami

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: חֲדָא מִינַּיְיהוּ נְקַט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara deflects the objection: **Yes, it is indeed so** that Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling should be extended to other cases, as Rav Menashya bar Gadda claimed. **Rather, Abaye said:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **mentioned** just **one of them** as an example, even though they are all correct.

The initial response recorded in the *Gemara* is simply: אין הכי נמי — indeed, that is precisely the case. What appears to be a reductio ad absurdum is answered with a matter-of-fact affirmation: yes, even when a *korban* is brought שלא לשמו, it retains its full original identity, and all its requirements — the double *mincha* for the *omer* lamb, the logs of wood for the *tamid* — must be met. If they are not, the *korban* is invalidated.

Tomorrow, Abaye will challenge this position and argue that a *korban* brought שלא לשמו does in fact shed its original identity, becoming generic in character. The tension between these two views — full retention of identity versus a shift to generic status — is the substantive question with which we close.
