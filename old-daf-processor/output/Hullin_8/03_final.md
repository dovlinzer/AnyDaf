# Chullin 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## White-Hot Knife

### Sharpness Precedes Heat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לִיבֵּן סַכִּין וְשָׁחַט בָּהּ – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, חִידּוּדָהּ קוֹדֵם לְלִיבּוּנָהּ. וְהָאִיכָּא צְדָדִין? בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה מִירְוָוח רָוַוח.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Shmuel says:** If **one heated a knife until** it became **white hot [*libben*] and slaughtered** an animal **with it, his slaughter is valid,** as cutting the relevant *simanim* with the knife's **sharp** blade **preceded** the effect of **its white heat.** Had the effect of the heat preceded the cutting, the animal would have been rendered a *tereifa*, an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months, before the slaughter was completed, by searing the windpipe and the gullet. The Gemara asks: **But aren't there the sides** of the knife, which burn the throat and render the animal a *tereifa*? The Gemara answers: **The area of the slaughter** in the throat **parts** immediately after the incision, and the tissue on either side of the incision is not seared by the white-hot blade.

We open on 8a with the statement of Rav Zeira in the name of Shmuel. Having concluded the earlier digressions about tzaddikim and treif food, the Gemara returns to its central theme of shechita — and begins to focus not merely on who may perform it, but on the act itself. The ruling is as follows: *liben sakin veshachat bah* — if one heated a knife white-hot and then used it to shecht, the shechita is nevertheless valid, *shechitato kesheira*. The reason given is *chiduda kodem lelibuna* — the sharpness of the knife precedes the heat. In other words, even though the knife is white-hot, the cutting edge makes contact with and severs the throat before the heat has any material effect. It is therefore a valid shechita.

### Heat Burning the Simanim

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty. One might well grant that as the knife first enters the throat, the sharpness does its work. But as the knife continues through the cut — moving laterally through the trachea and esophagus — the heat of the blade is burning the inner walls of the opening it has created. Is this not burning the simanim before the cut is complete, potentially rendering the animal a treifa? At the very least, the heat seems to be acting upon the animal in a problematic way before the shechita is finished.

### Wound Opens (Mivrach)

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara responds: *beit hashechita mivrach ravach* — the place of the cut opens up as the shechita proceeds. When one draws the knife through the throat, the wound naturally spreads open, so that the sides of the blade — which carry the bulk of the heat — are no longer in direct contact with the flesh. Only the cutting edge itself, the very tip that is making contact, is touching the skin at any given moment, and that edge is doing the work of sharpness, not of heat.

### Blood Sealing

*[Continued from above]*

This explanation prompts a further observation worth noting: the heat might in principle be sealing the wound as the knife passes through, preventing blood from flowing. But this does not invalidate the shechita. The halachic requirement of shechita is specifically the severing of the trachea and esophagus — the simanim — and not the draining of blood. While cutting the carotid artery facilitates the subsequent removal of blood, it is not part of the formal requirement of *hechsher shechita*. The blood-sealing concern would therefore not passel the shechita itself; it might complicate the later stages of preparing the meat, but the act of shechita stands as valid.

## Hot Poker & Tzara'at

### Shechin vs. Michva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לִיבֵּן שַׁפּוּד וְהִכָּה בּוֹ, מִשּׁוּם שְׁחִין נִדּוֹן אוֹ מִשּׁוּם מִכְוָה נִדּוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If **one heated a skewer [*shappud*] until** it became **white hot and struck** a person **with it,** and after the wound healed a leprous mark developed, is that mark **adjudged as** a leprous **boil or is it adjudged as** a leprous **burn?**

The Gemara now introduces a related question by way of analogy: *liben shipud vehika bo* — if someone heats a poker white-hot and strikes another person with it, is the resulting lesion classified as a *shechin* (a wound) or as a *michva* (a burn)? These two categories are drawn from the Torah's discussion of tzara'at, where lesions arising from either a wound or a burn are treated in specific ways. The question is which classification applies when an injury involves both impact and heat simultaneously.

### One Week Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ – לְכִדְתַנְיָא: שְׁחִין וּמִכְוָה מְטַמְּאִין בְּשָׁבוּעַ אֶחָד, בִּשְׁנֵי סִימָנִין – בְּשֵׂעָר לָבָן וּבְפִסְיוֹן, וְלָמָּה חִלְּקָן הַכָּתוּב? לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין מִצְטָרְפִין זֶה עִם זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the** practical **difference** whether it is adjudged a boil or a burn? The difference is **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: Both a leprous **boil and** a leprous **burn become impure during one week** of quarantine **with two symptoms: With white hair** that grows in the leprous mark **and with spreading** of the leprous mark. **And why did the verse divide them** into two separate passages even though their halakhic status is the same? The verse divided them **to say that they do not join together** to constitute the requisite measure of impure leprous marks. Rather, there is impurity only if the boil or the burn constitutes that measure individually.

The practical difference is established through a braita: *shechin umichva *Metama'im beshavua echad* *bishnei simanim* *Bese'ar lavan* uvifisyon* — both a shechin and a michva are assessed over only one week, not two. In most cases of tzara'at, if the appearance of the spot is indeterminate at the end of the first week, it is closed up for a second week of observation. But for lesions arising from a wound or a burn, there is only one week, and two signs are looked for: the growth of white hair and the spreading of the lesion.

Now, if shechin and michva carry the same law, one might ask why the Torah devotes separate passages to each. The answer is *she'ein mitztarfin ze im ze* — they do not combine with one another. The minimum size for a leprous spot is *k'gris*, the size of a lentil bean. If a person has two adjacent spots, each half a gris, one a shechin and one a michva, they cannot be combined to satisfy the minimum size. Each must independently meet the threshold. This is why the distinction between the two categories matters at all.

### Shechin Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ שְׁחִין וְאֵיזֶהוּ מִכְוָה? לָקָה בְּעֵץ, בְּאֶבֶן, בְּגֶפֶת, בְּחַמֵּי טְבֶרְיָא, וּבְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא מֵחֲמַת הָאוּר, לְאֵתוֹיֵי אֲבָר מֵעִיקָּרוֹ – זֶהוּ שְׁחִין. וְאֵיזֶהוּ מִכְוָה? נִכְוָה בְּגַחֶלֶת, בְּרֶמֶץ, בְּסִיד רוֹתֵחַ, בְּגִפְסִיס רוֹתֵחַ, וּבְכׇל דָּבָר הַבָּא מֵחֲמַת הָאוּר, לְאֵתוֹיֵי חַמֵּי הָאוּר – זוֹ הִיא מִכְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Which** wound **is a boil and which is a burn?** If **one was struck with wood, with a stone, with pomace, with the hot springs of Tiberias, or with any item that is not heated by fire,** a phrase that serves **to include lead** that was mined **from its source** in the ground, which is occasionally hot enough to burn a person, **this** impression left on the skin **is a boil. And which** wound **is a burn?** If **one was burned with a coal, with hot ashes, with boiling limestone, with boiling gypsum [*begippesit*], or with any item that is heated by fire,** a phrase that serves **to include** water **heated by fire, this** impression left on the skin **is a burn.**

The Gemara proceeds to define each category. A shechin is produced when one is struck or burned by something that is not fire. The mishna gives examples: being struck by wood or stone (*laka b'etz b'even*), being exposed to *gefet* — the lees of olives, which generate heat through organic decomposition — or to the hot springs of Tiberias (*chamei Tverya*). The principle is captured in the phrase *u'vchol davar shelo ba machamat ha'or* — anything whose heat is not derived from fire. This category also includes, according to one reading, metal in its naturally heated state at the point of mining — Rashi appears to indicate that certain metals, such as lead, are hot when first extracted from the ground, and some have suggested this refers to molten ore or naturally heated mineral deposits.

### Michva Definition

*[Continued from above]*

A michva, by contrast, requires heat that originates from fire. The mishna gives examples: being burned by a coal (*gachelet*), by hot ashes (*remetz*), by boiling plaster heated by fire (*sid roteach*), or by *gafsit* — another type of plaster that has been heated. The governing principle is *u'vchol davar haba machamat ha'or* — anything whose heat derives from fire, including water that has been heated by fire (*chamei ha'or*). It is specifically the origin of the heat — fire versus non-fire — that determines the classification.

### Last Status Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: שְׁחִין וּמִכְוָה, אִם שְׁחִין קוֹדֵם לַמִּכְוָה – בִּטֵּל מִכְוָה אֶת הַשְּׁחִין, וְאִם מִכְוָה קוֹדֶמֶת לַשְּׁחִין – בִּטֵּל שְׁחִין אֶת הַמִּכְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: If there is **a boil and a burn** on the same place on the skin and a leprous mark developed, the later wound determines the nature of the leprosy. Therefore, **if the boil preceded the burn, the burn nullifies the boil** and the mark is a leprous burn. **And if the burn preceded the boil, the boil nullifies the burn** and the mark is a leprous boil.

When a single lesion involves both categories — for example, when there is first an impact and then a burn, or vice versa — the halacha follows the last status. A braita states: *im shechin kodem l'michva, batla michva et hashechin* — if the wound came first and the burn came afterward, the michva status nullifies and supersedes the shechin. Conversely, *im michva kodemet l'shechin, batla shechin et hamichva* — if the burn came first and the wound followed, the shechin status prevails. The most recent stage of the lesion's origin determines how it is classified.

### Half-Gris Lesions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן דַּהֲוָה בֵּיהּ חֲצִי גְּרִיס שְׁחִין מֵעִיקָּרָא, וְלִיבֵּן שַׁפּוּד וְהִכָּה בּוֹ, וּנְפַק בֵּיהּ חֲצִי גְּרִיס אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And here,** where the dilemma was raised whether the mark that develops from being struck with a hot skewer is a boil or a burn, **what are the circumstances?** It is a case **where initially there was a boil half** the size of **a split bean on** the person's skin, **and one heated a skewer until** it became **white hot and struck** him **with it, and another** mark **half** the size of **a split bean emerged** on the skin there.

This priority rule creates the specific scenario the Gemara has been building toward. Suppose a person already had a spot on his skin — a shechin that was exactly half a gris in size, insufficient on its own to constitute a leprous lesion. Now someone heats a poker and strikes him near that spot, and a new half-gris spot appears adjacent to the first. Combined, the two would satisfy the minimum size requirement — but do they combine?

### Back to Sharpness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חַבְטָא? קָדֵים וְאָתֵי הַבְלָא וּמְבַטֵּל לֵיהּ לְחַבְטָא, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ שְׁחִין וּמִכְוָה, וְלָא מִצְטָרְפִין; אוֹ דִלְמָא הַבְלָא קָדֵים, וְאָתֵי חַבְטָא וּמְבַטֵּל לֵיהּ לְהַבְלָא, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ שְׁחִין וּשְׁחִין, וּמִצְטָרֵף?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the dilemma: **What** is the *halakha*? **Does** the effect of **the blow come first and** then the effect of **the heat comes and nullifies** the effect of **the blow, and it is** a half-measure **boil and** a half-measure **burn and they do not join together** to constitute a full measure? **Or perhaps** the effect of **the heat comes first and** then the effect of **the blow comes and nullifies** the effect of **the heat, and it is** a half-measure **boil and** a half-measure **boil and they join together.**

The answer depends on how the poker strike is classified. If the impact comes first and is then overlaid by the heat, the last status is michva. The new spot is therefore a michva. Now we have a shechin and a michva side by side — and as established, they do not combine. But if the heat comes first and the impact follows, the last status is shechin. Both spots are now shechin, and they do combine. The question — *chavata kadim* (does the wound precede) or *havala kadim* (does the heat precede) — is therefore halachically determinative.

The Gemara notes that this question is distinct from the white-hot knife case. There, one could argue with confidence that the sharpness of the blade acts first, since the cutting edge is specifically designed to sever. But in the case of a blunt or even pointed poker, there is no obvious a priori reason to prefer one over the other. If anything, one might reason that the skin reacts to heat more immediately than to blunt impact, and that the black-and-blue of blunt trauma develops only afterward — which would suggest heat comes first. This is the tension the Gemara is working through.

### Pointed Poker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לִיבֵּן סַכִּין וְשָׁחַט בָּהּ – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁירָה, חִידּוּדָהּ קוֹדֵם לְלִיבּוּנָהּ. אַלְמָא חַבְטָא קָדֵים! חִדּוּד שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from that **which Rabbi Zeira says** that **Shmuel says:** If **one heated a knife until** it became **white hot and slaughtered** an animal **with it, his slaughter is valid,** as cutting the relevant *simanim* with the knife's **sharp** blade **preceded** the effect of **its white heat. Apparently,** the effect of **the blow comes first.** The Gemara rejects that proof: Cutting with **a sharp** blade **is different** from striking with a blunt object, and only in the case of a blade does the cut precede the effect of the heat.

The Gemara attempts to resolve the question by citing the ruling with which this entire sugya opened. Since Rav Zeira said in the name of Shmuel that *chiduda kodem lelibuna* — the sharpness precedes the heat — perhaps this proves that in general, physical impact precedes heat, and therefore the wound comes first even in the poker case. The Gemara rejects this inference: *chidud shani* — sharpness is different. A knife is specifically constructed to cut, and the geometry of the blade ensures that the cutting action makes contact before the heat can spread. That tells us nothing about a blunt impact.

### Blunt vs. Heat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לִיבֵּן שַׁפּוּד וְהִכָּה בּוֹ – נִדּוֹן מִשּׁוּם מִכְוַת אֵשׁ, אַלְמָא חַבְטָא קָדֵים! הָתָם נָמֵי, דְּבַרְזֵייהּ מִיבְרָז, דְּהַיְינוּ חִדּוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from a *baraita*: If **one heated a skewer until** it became **white hot and struck** a person **with it** and after the wound healed a leprous mark developed, that mark **is adjudged as** a leprous **burn** caused **by fire. Apparently,** the effect of **the blow precedes** the effect of the burn. The Gemara rejects that proof: **There too,** the reference is to a case **where he stabbed** the skin with the skewer, **which is** the same as cutting with **a sharp** blade.

The Gemara then cites an explicit braita: *liben shipud vehika bo, nidon mishum michvas eish* — if one heated a poker and struck with it, it is classified as a burn. This would seem to prove that the heat precedes the wound. But the Gemara is not satisfied and qualifies the braita: *d'barzi marza, d'hainu chidud* — the case in the braita involves a poker with a sharpened, pointed tip. In that case, just as with the knife, the point punctures the skin before the heat has its effect, and the last stage is therefore the heat. This is analogous to the knife case, and equally unhelpful for resolving the blunt-force question.

### Kilkul vs. Tikun

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara ultimately leaves the question open. When a blunt white-hot object strikes the skin, does the physical force or the heat register first as the causative agent of the wound? The question is left unresolved. The practical stakes — whether the resulting half-gris lesion is a shechin or a michva, and therefore whether it combines with an adjacent half-gris lesion of the other type to produce actionable tzara'at — remain without a definitive answer.

It is worth noting, as the Gemara itself seems to register, why this digression into tzara'at is the vehicle of choice rather than some other case. The tzara'at framework offers a precise legal question that maps onto the same binary the shechita case presents: which of two simultaneous forces is the operative one? The narrow issue the Gemara cares about — not the macro consequences of an act, but the specific causal priority between impact and heat at the point of contact — is best isolated in the tzara'at context, where classification of a single lesion carries halachic consequence.

## Idolatry Knife

### Agricultural Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: סַכִּין שֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה מוּתָּר לִשְׁחוֹט בָּהּ, וְאָסוּר לַחְתּוֹךְ בָּהּ בָּשָׂר. מוּתָּר לִשְׁחוֹט בָּהּ – מְקַלְקֵל הוּא, וְאָסוּר לַחְתּוֹךְ בָּהּ בָּשָׂר – מְתַקֵּן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman says that Rabba bar Avuh says:** With regard to **a knife** used **for idol worship,** it is **permitted to slaughter** an animal **with it, but** it is **prohibited to cut meat with it.** It is **permitted to slaughter** an animal **with it** because slaughtering it **is a destructive** action vis-à-vis the animal, which is worth more when it is alive. **But** it is **prohibited to cut meat with it,** because once the animal is slaughtered, cutting it **is a constructive** action that renders the meat manageable.

The Gemara now shifts to a series of rulings about knives that have been used in problematic contexts, each raising distinct kashrus concerns. Rav Nachman said in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: a knife used for avoda zara — idolatry — may be used to shecht with, but may not be used to cut meat. The operative distinction is *kilkul* versus *tikun*: shechita is considered a *kilkul*, a destruction or degradation, while cutting already-slaughtered meat is a *tikun*, an improvement or benefit. Since one may not derive benefit from objects used in avoda zara, using the knife as a cutting implement is forbidden. But using it to shecht is permitted, because shechita is not considered an act of deriving benefit.

### Ill Animal Exception

*[Continued from above]*

This ruling is striking to the modern sensibility, since the entire meat industry exists to profit from the slaughter of animals. The answer lies in the agricultural context presupposed by the Gemara. In an agrarian economy, animals are kept primarily for their ongoing productivity — for milk, eggs, wool, and labor. Slaughter is not the productive use of the animal; it terminates that productivity. One would only slaughter an animal for a special occasion or when it had outlived its agricultural utility. Shechita, in this context, represents the end of the animal's value, not the realization of it. Cutting meat into usable portions, by contrast, transforms a carcass into something directly edible and is therefore a clear *tikun*.

This framework is admittedly difficult to internalize given the reality of modern industrial meat production. Yet the Gemara is operating from a coherent, internally consistent economic logic in which the living animal — not the slaughtered carcass — is the primary asset.

### Kashering by Heat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּעָמִים שֶׁהַשּׁוֹחֵט אָסוּר – בִּמְסוּכֶּנֶת, וּמְחַתֵּךְ מוּתָּר – בְּאַטְמֵי דְּקָיְימָן לְקוּרְבָּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There are **times when** it is **prohibited** for **one who slaughters** an animal to use a knife used for idol worship, e.g., **in** the case of an animal that is in **danger,** meaning that it is about to die. If he does not slaughter the animal it would become an unslaughtered carcass and depreciate in value. **And** there are times when it is **permitted** for **one who cuts** meat to use a knife of idol worship, e.g., **in** the case of an animal whose **thighs are intended to** be sent as **a gift** to a person of stature. Cutting it into pieces would render it unfit for this purpose, thereby diminishing its value.

Rava introduces nuance: *pe'amim sheshochet assur* — there are times when shechita itself becomes forbidden with the knife, because it constitutes a genuine benefit. This is the case of a *mesukenet*, a dangerously ill animal that is on the verge of dying on its own. In this situation, shechita is actually an economic salvage operation: if the animal dies without being shechted, even its meat cannot be used. By shechting it in time, one rescues the value of the animal. Here, shechita is unambiguously a tikun and not a kilkul — and therefore forbidden with the avoda zara knife.

Rava also notes the converse: **Umchatach Muttar** — there are times when cutting meat is actually permitted, because it constitutes a degradation rather than an improvement. This would be the case of fine whole cuts of meat that are suitable to be presented as gifts — for example, a whole thigh or rib roast — where cutting them into smaller pieces actively diminishes their value. The classification of an act as kilkul or tikun is therefore context-dependent and not intrinsic to the act itself.

### Peeling vs. Rinsing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם שַׁמְנוּנִית דְּאִיסּוּרָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And derive** that **it** is prohibited to use a knife used for idol worship, not because benefit from it is prohibited, but **due to** the residue of **fat of forbidden** carcasses on the knife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּחֲדָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that possibility: Rav Naḥman is referring **to** the case of **a new** knife on which there is no residue.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָשָׁה, בֵּין לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בֵּין לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, מְשַׁמְּשֵׁי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הֵן, וּמְשַׁמְּשֵׁי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֵינָן אֲסוּרִין עַד שֶׁיֵּעָבֵדוּ! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, דִּפְסַק בֵּיהּ גְּוָוזָא לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, בִּישָׁנָה שֶׁלִּיבְּנָהּ בָּאוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: If it is **a new** knife, **both** according **to Rabbi Yishmael and** according **to Rabbi Akiva,** who disagreed about whether an idol is forbidden from the moment that one crafts it or from the moment that one worships it, a knife is merely in the category of **accessories of idol worship, and accessories of idol worship are forbidden only after they are** used for idol **worship.** The Gemara explains: **If you wish, say** that the reference is to a case **where he cut a branch [*gevaza*] for idol worship with** the knife, which leaves no residue. **And if you wish, say** instead that Rav Naḥman is referring **to** the case of **an old** knife **that he burned until** it became **white hot in the fire,** and therefore, there is no residue on the knife.

The Gemara then raises a separate concern that operates independently of the avoda zara prohibition: *shamnunit de'issura* — the residue of forbidden substances on the knife. Even setting aside the question of deriving benefit, a knife that has been used to slaughter animals for idolatry would presumably be coated with fat and residue from those animals, which are neveilah. Using such a knife to shecht kosher animals would transfer that residue to the place of the cut.

The Gemara resolves this by positing two possible answers. The first is that the knife was used for *gazra* — chopping wood for idolatrous purposes — and therefore has no meat residue on it at all. This is acknowledged as an *okimta*, a qualifying deflection rather than a principled resolution. The second answer is that the knife, although previously used with forbidden meat, has been kashered: **Beyeshana Shelivna Ba'or** — it was an old knife that was heated white-hot in fire, thereby burning off all absorbed or surface residue. This kashering method — *libun* — removes the concern about transferred taste and renders the knife fit for use. With this, the Gemara transitions from the avoda zara framework into the broader questions of taste absorption and transfer in kashrus.

## Non-Jewish Knife

### Heat & Absorption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּסַכִּין שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, רַב אָמַר: קוֹלֵף, וְרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר: מֵדִיחַ. לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה צוֹנֵן, וּמָר סָבַר: בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה רוֹתֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** With regard to **one who slaughters** an animal **with the knife of gentiles, Rav says: He peels** off a layer of the flesh from the place on the animal where the knife touched the flesh and the forbidden residue on the knife was absorbed. **And Rabba bar bar Ḥana says: He rinses** the place where the knife touched the flesh. The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **they disagree about this, that** one **Sage,** Rabba bar bar Ḥana, **holds: The area of the slaughter** on the throat **is cold** and does not absorb the forbidden residue, and therefore rinsing is sufficient. **And** one **Sage,** Rav, **holds: The area of the slaughter** on the throat **is hot** and therefore it absorbs the forbidden residue.

The Gemara now introduces a new but closely related case: *ha-shochet be-sakin shel ovdei kochavim* — one who shechts with a knife belonging to non-Jews. The phrasing is deliberately distinct from the earlier case of *sakin shel avodat kochavim* (a knife of idolatry). There, the primary issue was deriving forbidden benefit; here, the idolatry concern is absent, and the sole question is the transfer of non-kosher residue. A knife that has been used by non-Jews to cut their meat will have non-kosher fat and residue on it. When used to shecht a kosher animal, does that residue transfer to the *beit hashechita* — the specific area of the neck where the cut is made — in a halachically significant way?

Rav rules: *koleif* — one must peel away the surface layer of the meat at the site of the cut before eating. Rabba bar Bar Chana rules: *madiach* — it suffices to rinse the area with water. Both positions accept that some transfer occurs; the debate is whether the residue penetrates beneath the surface or remains on it.

### Knife Pressure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה רוֹתֵחַ הוּא; מַאן דְּאָמַר קוֹלֵף – שַׁפִּיר, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר מֵדִיחַ – אַיְּידֵי דִּטְרִידִי סִימָנִין לְאַפּוֹקֵי דָּם לָא בָּלְעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that suggestion: **No,** it is possible **that everyone** holds that **the area of the slaughter** on the throat **is hot.** For **the one who says** that **he peels** off a layer, it works out **well, and the one who says** that **he rinses** the place where the knife touched the flesh holds that **since the two organs that must be severed in ritual slaughter [*simanim*],** i.e., the windpipe and the gullet, **are occupied** with **discharging blood, they do not absorb** the residue.

The Gemara explores what underlying disagreement drives this dispute. One possibility is that Rav holds *beit hashechita roteiach* — the site of the slaughter is halachically hot, i.e., hot enough to cause absorption of taste into the flesh — while Rabba bar Bar Chana holds *beit hashechita tzonen*, that it is cold, and therefore no absorption occurs. Alternatively, the Gemara suggests that both may agree the site is hot; the one who rules that rinsing suffices can still explain his position by arguing *aidi de-tridi simanim le-apukei dam lo bali* — since the simanim are occupied, so to speak, with expelling blood at the moment of shechita, there is no capacity for simultaneous absorption of external matter.

### Yad Soledet Bo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה צוֹנֵן. מַאן דְּאָמַר מֵדִיחַ – שַׁפִּיר, מַאן דְּאָמַר קוֹלֵף – אַגַּב דּוּחְקָא דְסַכִּינָא בָּלַע.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say that everyone** holds that **the area of the slaughter** on the throat **is cold.** For **the one who says** that **he rinses** the place where the knife touched the flesh, it works out **well, and the one who says** that **he peels** off a layer holds that although that area is cold, **due to the pressure of the knife** on the throat, the flesh **absorbs** the residue.

A further possibility inverts the previous one: perhaps both Rav and Rabba bar Bar Chana actually agree that the beit hashechita is cold. On that reading, Rabba bar Bar Chana's ruling that rinsing suffices follows naturally. But Rav's requirement of peeling demands explanation: why would peeling be necessary if there is no heat? The Gemara answers: *agav *duchka de-sakina* bala* — because of the pressure of the knife itself, absorption occurs even without heat. The physical force of the blade pressing through the flesh drives the surface residue inward, bypassing the normal requirement of heat for taste transfer.

### Rav Aha & Ravina

*[Continued from above]*

This sugya gives rise to two significant halachic discussions. The first concerns the definition of *yad soledet bo* — the threshold of heat required for halachic absorption of taste, relevant both to shabbat and to kashrus. Several Rishonim assume that the heat needed for taste absorption in kashrus is identical to the standard of yad soledet bo. Since the Gemara here presents a debate about whether the beit hashechita is hot or cold, some authorities reason that this site must be near the borderline of that threshold — otherwise there would be no debate — and attempt to translate that into a quantifiable temperature. The figure arrived at is quite low, perhaps around 102–106 degrees Fahrenheit.

However, a number of other Rishonim reject this line of reasoning entirely, arguing that yad soledet bo is a shabbat category and that the heat required for taste absorption in kashrus may in fact be lower. On that view, this Gemara is irrelevant to establishing the yad soledet bo threshold, and the debate about the beit hashechita operates on a different scale.

The second halachic issue concerns *duchka de-sakina* — whether the pressure of a knife can by itself transfer taste into food, independent of heat. The Gemara raises this as a possibility, but Tosafot argues, based on other Gemarot, that pressure alone is insufficient. Rather, what produces absorption in the relevant cases is a combination of heat that is close to — but perhaps just below — the formal threshold, together with the added factor of knife pressure. Whether knife pressure entirely without heat can drive absorption into food remains unclear from this Gemara alone.

These two principles have direct practical application to the question of purchasing fish from a non-kosher counter. If one can confirm the species by visible signs — scales, skin pattern — the remaining concern is whether the knife used to cut it transfers non-kosher residue into the fish. According to the stricter view, one should koleif — scrape away the surface at the cut. According to the more lenient view, rinsing suffices. If the fish store uses separate knives or demonstrably cleans its blade, there may be further grounds for leniency. The separate and more significant concern is the intentional mislabeling of fish species, which is a distinct problem unrelated to the knife question.

## Treifa Knife

### Surface-Level Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַכִּין טְרֵיפָה, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָמַר: בְּחַמִּין, וְחַד אָמַר: בְּצוֹנֵן, וְהִלְכְתָא: אֲפִילּוּ בְּצוֹנֵן, וְאִי אִיכָּא בְּלִיתָא דִּפְרָסָא לְמִיכְפְּרֵיהּ, לָא צְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § **With regard to a knife** with which an animal that is **a *tereifa*** was slaughtered, **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree. One says:** One purges it **in hot** water to remove the absorptions from the *tereifa*, **and one says:** One rinses it **in cold** water, and that is sufficient. **And the *halakha*** is: One may rinse it **even in cold** water. **And if there is a tattered** piece **of a curtain** with which **to wipe** the knife, **one need not** rinse it.

The Gemara now turns to an analogous but distinct scenario: a knife that has been used to shecht a treifa animal. This is not a hypothetical — it happens in the normal course of shechita, since an animal may turn out to have had a treifot only after the shechita is completed. The fat and meat residue on the knife are now from a treif animal. Rav Aha and Ravina debate how such a knife must be kashered. One holds that it requires heat — *bachamin* — implying that the forbidden matter has been absorbed into the metal of the knife itself. The other holds that cold water suffices — *b'tzonin* — implying that the residue is only on the surface.

### Blood Adhesion

*[Continued from above]*

The halacha is ruled in accordance with the lenient position: *afilu b'tzonin* — cold water is sufficient. Moreover, if one has a material such as steel wool with which to scrub the surface, that alone suffices even without water, since the concern is only surface residue and not absorbed matter. The underlying principle is that the knife does not become heated to the point where it absorbs the forbidden taste from the animal into its metal. The forbidden matter remains on the surface and can be removed mechanically or by rinsing.

### Heat During Penetration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּחַמִּין, מַאי טַעְמָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָלְעָה אִיסּוּרָא? דְּהֶיתֵּירָא נָמֵי בָּלְעָה אֵבֶר מִן הַחַי! אֵימַת בָּלְעָה? לְכִי חָיְימָא. אֵימַת קָא חָיְימָא? לְכִי גָמְרָה שְׁחִיטָה, הָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא הֶיתֵּירָא הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to the one who says** that one purges it **in hot** water, **what is the reason** that he must do so; is it **due to** the premise **that** the knife **absorbed forbidden** residue? That reasoning should not be limited to a case where he slaughtered a *tereifa*. A knife with **which** he slaughtered an animal that is **permitted** should **also** require purging, because **it absorbed** residue from **the limb from a living** animal before the slaughter was completed. The Gemara answers: **When** is there concern that the knife **absorbed** the residue? It is **when** the throat **grows warm. When does it grow warm?** It is **at** the point **when the slaughter is complete.** At **that moment, it is** already **permitted.**

Rashi raises a pointed question: if we are concerned about the knife absorbing treif fat and meat, why are we not equally concerned about the blood that flows over the knife with every shechita? Blood is, after all, a forbidden substance, and it is always present at the site of the cut. Rashi's answer is that blood is substantially less adhesive than fat and meat; it does not cling to or penetrate the metal in the same way. The concern for residue on the knife is specifically about fat and meat, which tend to coat and adhere to the surface. Blood, by contrast, slides off and presents no comparable risk of transfer.

### Post-Shechita Absorption

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara then addresses a subtle but important question. If one holds that absorption into metal does occur through heat — the position that was rejected as halacha but is worth understanding — one might ask: even when the animal is kosher, doesn't the knife absorb the taste of the flesh as it cuts through? The Gemara responds: *eimas d'ka chamima, l'chi gamra shechita* — the moment at which the knife is surrounded by the heat of the animal is only when the blade is fully embedded deep within the neck, and that is precisely when the shechita is already complete. The absorption, if any, would occur after the animal has already been rendered kosher.

### Non-Shechted Meat: Absorption Only After Shechita Completes

*[Continued from above]*

This logic resolves a potential circularity. One might have worried that the very beginning of the shechita — before it is complete — involves the knife contacting flesh that is still *eiver min hachai*, meat from a living (or not-yet-shechted) animal, which is itself forbidden. The Gemara addresses this directly: the heat that would drive any absorption is only present when the knife has penetrated deeply into the neck. But by that point, the shechita is already complete and the animal is permissible. There is therefore no moment at which the knife is simultaneously hot enough to absorb and in contact with forbidden meat — the two conditions never coincide in the shechita of a kosher animal.

The overall picture that emerges from these final sugyot is one of carefully calibrated physical and halachic reasoning: the metal of the knife is less absorbent than flesh; blood is less adhesive than fat; the heat at the beit hashechita is at or near the borderline of halachic significance; and the sequencing of events within the shechita itself ensures that the knife's moment of deepest heat exposure is also the moment of the animal's permissibility. Two halachic principles of broad application — the definition of yad soledet bo and the question of *duchka de-sakina* — emerge from this discussion as live and contested matters among the Rishonim and Poskim.
