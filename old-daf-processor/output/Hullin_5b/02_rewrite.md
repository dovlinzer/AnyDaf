# Chullin 5b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mumarim & Sacrifices

### Mumar Explanations

We resume at the very last line of Amud 1, where the Gemara presents a Baraita establishing that a Mumar can serve as a Shochet, and offers two explanations for this ruling. The first explanation is that we are dealing with a Mumar Ochel Nevelot Le-Te'avon — someone who simply does not care about Kashrut and eats forbidden meat for his own pleasure. The second explanation involves a full-fledged Mumar, even one who worships idols.

### Idolaters & Shabbat

The Gemara ultimately rejects the second explanation based on a Baraita that places the public violator of Shabbat in the same category as the idolater. This reflects the well-established principle that an idolater and a public Shabbat violator are categorically excluded from a range of Mitzvot, their very personal status having been fundamentally compromised. We are therefore left with the first explanation: a person who violates a single area — even Kashrut — retains sufficient standing to perform Shechita under the right circumstances. The Ochel Nevelot Le-Te'avon has not been categorically excluded, while the idolater and public Shabbat violator have been, and that is indeed where the Halacha stands.

### Source of Exclusion

Picking up at the very last line, the Gemara raises a pointed question: *Ve-ha me-hakha naffka me-hatam naffka?* — do we really derive the exclusion of a Mumar from bringing sacrifices from this particular verse about *Mi-kem*? The Gemara notes that the exclusion appears to come from elsewhere entirely. The verse in the context of the sin offering reads *Me-am ha-aretz* — "from the people of the land" — where the word *mem* signals a limitation: *Prat le-mumar*, excluding a habitual violator or rebel from bringing a sin offering.

### Two Exclusions

A second source of exclusion is derived from the verse *Asher lo te-asena bi-shgaga ve-ashem* — "which should not be done, and he did so in error and is guilty." From this the Gemara derives: *Ha-shav mi-da'ato mevi korban al shigagato* — one who, had he been aware of what he was doing, would have refrained and corrected himself, is obligated to bring a Korban for his unintentional sin. But *eino shav mi-da'ato* — one who would not have changed his behavior even had he known the facts — does not bring a Korban for that error. The logic is stark: if a person routinely eats treif and this time happened to eat something non-kosher by accident, he cannot bring a Chatas, because even had he known it was forbidden, he would have eaten it anyway. So we have two categories of exclusion: the Mumar whose habitual sinning disqualifies him, and the person who would have sinned deliberately regardless.

### Rav Hamnuna Case

The Gemara then asks *mai beinaihu* — what practical difference exists between these two categories of exclusion? After all, both seem to say that a habitual sinner does not bring a Korban. Rav Hamnuna answers: *Mumar le-ekhol chelev ve-hevi korban al hadam ika beinaihu* — the difference emerges in the case of someone who is a habitual violator in one area but accidentally sins in a related but distinct area. Take a person who regularly eats Cheilev — forbidden fat — but never eats Dam, forbidden blood. This time he accidentally consumed blood.

As Tosafot point out, Cheilev and Dam are a closely linked cluster of prohibitions: the Torah addresses both in a similar manner, both are associated with the sacrificial rites, and both carry the penalty of Karet. They inhabit the same halachic universe, even though they are distinct. Now, does such a person bring a Korban for his accidental consumption of blood? According to the reasoning of *eino shav mi-da'ato* — "he would have done it anyway" — this person would actually be *obligated* to bring a Korban, since he would *not* have eaten blood intentionally; his habitual violation is of fat, not blood. But according to the reasoning of categorical Mumar status, since his ongoing sinful pattern falls within this cluster of related behaviors, his status as a Mumar in that domain excludes him from the sin offering even for errors in the adjacent area.

### Status vs Unworthiness

This distinction carries real weight. Tosafot emphasize that the categorical Mumar exclusion — the kind we have been discussing in the context of idolaters and Shabbat violators — does not apply here. This person is not globally invalidated on account of eating Cheilev. What he is, rather, is excluded from bringing sin offerings within this specific cluster of behaviors. The two exclusions thus operate differently: one is a broad disqualification of personal status, and the other is a targeted exclusion from the Korban process in a particular domain of conduct.

One might ask how such a status would even be established — would it require testimony before a Beit Din? Would it have to be "public" in some formal sense? There is indeed discussion in Halacha about exactly these questions, particularly concerning the Shabbat violator and what "in public" requires. But it is clear that the category does not require any formal proclamation or public marking of the person.

### Chatas vs Olah

The Gemara then asks: since we now have two overlapping exclusions of Mumarim from Korbanos, why do we need both? The answer is *chad be-chatas ve-chad be-olah u-tzricha* — one exclusion applies to the Chatas and one to the Olah, and both are necessary.

Had the Torah only taught the exclusion regarding a Chatas, one might have said: the Chatas is denied because *mishum de-chapparah hi* — it is an instrument of atonement, and an unrepentant sinner does not deserve atonement. But an Olah, which is *de-doron hu* — a gift to God — might perhaps still be accepted even from a habitual violator; there seems less affront in offering a gift. Had the Torah only taught the exclusion regarding an Olah, one might have argued: the Mumar is excluded from an Olah because *de-lav chovah hu* — he has no binding obligation to bring it, so there is no reason to force the issue. But a Chatas is *de-chovah hu* — it is an obligatory offering — and one might think we should still insist that he bring it. Moreover, as Rashi notes, we do not want the sinner to benefit by being let off the hook from an obligation simply because of his sinful status. Hence *tzricha* — both exclusions are needed.

Tosafot further notes that these are not truly parallel exclusions: one involves categorical disqualification of personal status (as with the idolater or Shabbat violator), while the other is a more specific exclusion rooted in the inappropriateness of bringing a Korban when one is a willful violator in the relevant area. Regardless, both exclusions together establish the full scope of who is barred from the Korban process.

To return to the application for Shechita: the type of person categorically excluded in all matters is the idolater and the Shabbat violator. But a person who sins in a particular area — even Kashrut itself — may under the right circumstances still be trusted to perform Shechita properly.

## Behemah Terminology

### Behemah Meanings

The Gemara pauses at this juncture to address a point raised by the earlier Baraita. That Baraita had derived from the word *Behemah* in the verse about Korbanos that even people who are sinners, described as being "like animals," may still bring sacrifices. But does the word *Behemah* necessarily carry a negative, derogatory connotation? The Gemara challenges this assumption: *ve-hakesiv Adam U'Behemah Toshiya Hashem* — the verse in Tehillim states "man and beast God will save," and surely that is a positive context, not a pejorative one.

The Gemara resolves this by citing Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *Eilu Bnei Adam she-hen Arumim be-Da'as u-mesimin atzmam ke-Behemah* — this verse refers to people who are clever in their intellect but nonetheless conduct themselves with the humility and lowliness of an animal, suppressing arrogance. Here *Behemah* is unambiguously positive. So why does the earlier Baraita interpret it negatively as referring to sinners?

### Context Matters

The Gemara draws a clean hermeneutical line: *hasam kesiv Adam u-Behemah, hacha Behemah lechudei kesiv* — there, the verse links Adam and Behemah together, so we read the animal as a type of human being: humble, non-arrogant, like an animal. Here, where the word Behemah stands alone, we interpret it negatively, as referring to sinners.

The Gemara presses the point further: is it always the case that where the verse says "man and beast" together the meaning is positive? A counter-example appears in Yirmiyahu: *ve-zarati es Beis Yisrael zera adam ve-zera Behemah* — "I will plant the house of Israel, the planting of man and the planting of beast." The Gemara in Sotah interprets this verse as referring to people who are learned in Torah alongside those who are not — meaning *Behemah* there refers to ignoramuses, which is not a flattering characterization. Yet this is a verse that pairs *adam* and *Behemah*!

The Gemara answers: *hachalki kra, zera adam lechudei ve-zera Behemah lechudei* — examine the verse more carefully and you will see that it does not actually fuse the two; it enumerates them separately — "the planting of man" and "the planting of beast" — unlike the earlier verse which said *Adam u-Behemah* in a single unified phrase. Where they are truly linked, *Behemah* takes on a positive human connotation; where they are listed separately, the word reverts to a negative register.

## Kutim Shechita

### Rabban Gamliel Ban

Returning to the central theme of the sugya — who is and is not acceptable as a Shochet — the Gemara now revisits the case of the Kuti, the Samaritan. Earlier discussion had explored the degree to which Kutim could be trusted: whether they observe the laws, whether they observe them with sufficient scrupulousness, and whether one needs to guard against them deliberately sabotaging the Shechita even while personally observing it. The working assumption had been that under certain conditions a Kuti's Shechita was acceptable.

Now a significant new tradition is introduced: *Amar Rabbi Chanan amar Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi mishum Bar Kappara* — Rabban Gamliel and his court convened, reassessed the situation of the Kutim, and issued a ruling forbidding their Shechita.

### Zeira's Question

Rabbi Zeira responds to Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi, through whom this tradition was reported, with a question: *shema lo shama Rebbe ella be-shein Yisrael omed al gabav* — perhaps this ruling applies only when there is no direct Jewish observation of the Shechita? We had already established that trusting a Kuti requires either direct observation (*Yisrael omed al gabav*) or compelling him to eat from his own meat. Rabbi Zeira was suggesting that perhaps Rabban Gamliel's prohibition was not an across-the-board ban but merely a reiteration — or a sharper formulation — of the requirement for supervision.

### Yaakov Response

Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi's reply is stinging: *dami hai me-rabbanan ke-dlo gmir inash shma'ata* — "this rabbi seems like someone who has never learned anything in his life." His point: if the ruling merely meant that an unobserved Kuti's Shechita is forbidden, there was never any need to say it. That requirement has always been in place. For Rabban Gamliel and his entire court to formally convene and issue a ruling, they must be saying something new — namely, that the Shechita of a Kuti is invalid even under direct observation.

### Kutim as Non-Jews

This conclusion carries a weighty implication. We know of only one category of individuals whose Shechita is invalid even under complete, direct Jewish supervision: the non-Jew. Even a Cheresh, Shoteh, or Katan — a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor — can perform valid Shechita when directly supervised. If Rabban Gamliel's ruling extends to cases of direct observation, the only coherent explanation is that Kutim are being classified as non-Jews, whose Shechita is categorically invalid regardless of supervision. That is precisely the direction in which the Gemara will develop the discussion.

### Later Amoraim Eat

Against this ruling stands the testimony of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, who reports in the name of Rav Assi: *Ani ra'isi es Rabbi Yochanan she-achal mi-shechitas Kuti* — "I saw Rabbi Yochanan eat from the Shechita of a Kuti." This was clearly after the ruling of Rabban Gamliel and his Beit Din, and yet Rabbi Yochanan was prepared to eat from it. Similarly, Rav Assi himself ate from the Shechita of a Kuti.

### Zeira Bewildered

Rabbi Zeira was astounded. He posed the question in two directions: *Lo Shemia Lehu* — perhaps Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi simply never received the tradition of Rabban Gamliel's ruling; had they heard it, they would certainly have accepted it and refrained. Or alternatively — *shemia lehu ve-lo kiblu* — perhaps they did hear it and consciously rejected it, ruling that the Shechita of a Kuti remained permissible.

The Gemara then reports that Rabbi Zeira himself resolved the dilemma: *mistavra de-shemia lehu ve-lo kiblu* — it is more reasonable to conclude that they heard and did not accept. Why? Because if they had never heard the ruling, and would have accepted it had they heard it, then in practice they were eating meat that they themselves would have considered forbidden. And that scenario is theologically untenable.

### Tzaddikim Principle

The Gemara invokes a striking principle: *Hashta behematan shel tzaddikim ein ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu mavi takala al yadam, tzaddikim atzmam lo kol sheken* — if God does not even allow the animals of tzaddikim to stumble into wrongdoing, how much more certainly does He prevent tzaddikim themselves from stumbling. The paradigmatic case is the donkey of Pinchas ben Yair, which refused to eat anything it was not supposed to eat — a story the Gemara will return to later. If God protects even the animals of tzaddikim from consuming something forbidden, He would assuredly prevent tzaddikim themselves from unknowingly eating non-kosher food. Since Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi in fact ate from the Kuti's Shechita, they must have done so deliberately, having ruled that it was *mutar*. They heard the ruling and disagreed with it.

### Zeira Accepts Ban

This resolution also tells us something critical about Rabbi Zeira's own position. Recall that Rabbi Zeira had initially raised the possibility that Rabban Gamliel's ruling applied only without direct observation. If he still held to that reading, he would have had a simple answer when confronted with Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi eating from the Kuti's Shechita: they were simply eating under direct observation, which was always permissible even by Rabban Gamliel's ruling. The fact that Rabbi Zeira had no such simple resolution — that he was genuinely troubled and needed to conclude they heard and rejected the ruling — proves that he himself had by this point accepted that Rabban Gamliel's ban extended even to cases of direct observation. *Ela lav shma mina kibla minei* — from this we infer that he did accept the response, and with it the broader scope of the prohibition.

The picture that emerges, then, is that there are two valid positions in the tradition: the ruling of Rabban Gamliel and his Beit Din, accepted by Rabbi Zeira, that the Shechita of a Kuti is invalid in all circumstances — implying their classification as non-Jews — and a dissenting position maintained by Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi, who held that Kuti Shechita remained acceptable.

### Forbidden Food Effect

The invocation of the principle that God protects tzaddikim from eating forbidden food warrants a closer look, since this same idea will resurface later in the daf. Tosafot raises an obvious objection: the Gemara in other places records cases where tzaddikim did in fact sin — even grievously — so how can we claim that God prevents tzaddikim from erring? Tosafot answers sharply: this principle applies specifically, and exclusively, to the consumption of forbidden food. The argument of *behematan shel tzaddikim tzaddikim atzmam lo kol sheken* is only ever invoked in the context of eating something that is *assur*.

The underlying reasoning appears to be that forbidden food is not simply a sin like other sins. When something forbidden enters the body, it is not merely a transgression of a command — it is understood to carry some form of corrupting effect, whether physical or spiritual, upon the person who consumes it. This is a qualitatively different kind of wrong: not only a sin but an objective contamination of the body. That framing carries significant halachic implications. For instance, the question arises whether one may permit an infant below the age of Chinuch to eat non-kosher food — after all, one does not stop such an infant from inadvertently violating Shabbat. Is food different? Is treif food seen as having a harmful, corrupting effect that overrides the usual leniency toward pre-Chinuch children, independent of any formal prohibition? That is a genuine area of halachic discussion, and it is precisely this notion — that forbidden food acts upon the person in a way that transcends mere sin — that drives it.
