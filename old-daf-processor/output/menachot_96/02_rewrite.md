# Menachot 96 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chavitei Kohen Gadol

### Preparation Steps

The daf opens with a mishna concerning the chavitei Kohen Gadol — the special mincha that the Kohen Gadol was required to bring every day, half in the morning and half in the evening. To arrive at this mincha, a series of preparatory steps was necessary: the wheat had to be ground, sifted, kneaded (lisha), shaped (arichah), and baked (afiyah). As the mishna states, lishatan, va'arichatan, ve'afiyatan bifnim — the kneading, shaping, and baking had to be performed inside, that is, within the azara of the Beit HaMikdash. These final steps also vedochot et hashabbat, override Shabbat, since this is a minchat tamid that must be performed each day without exception, including on Shabbat.

### Work Location

However, not all steps carry the same status. Techinatan veharkadatan einan dochot et hashabbat — the grinding of the flour and the repeated sifting through multiple sieves do not override Shabbat. These preliminary steps could and should have been completed before Shabbat arrived.

### Rabbi Akiva's Rule

This distinction leads to a general principle articulated by Rabbi Akiva: kol melacha she'efshar la'asotah me'erev Shabbat einah dochah et HaShabbat, veshe'ee efshar la'asotah me'erev Shabbat dochah et HaShabbat. Any labor that could have been performed on erev Shabbat does not override Shabbat; only labor that could not have been done beforehand may be performed on Shabbat itself. An analogous principle applies to Yom Tov: one may cook on Yom Tov, but one may not harvest and grind grain on that day, since those preparatory steps could have been done in advance. In the case of the chavitim, it is from lisha ve'eilach — from the kneading onward — that the work is docheh et HaShabbat. The reason is practical: you could not knead the dough in advance and have it sit overnight without refrigeration, and in any event the flour itself could not be placed until the day it was needed, since the issaron had to be divided in half on that day. The grinding and sifting, by contrast, could easily be completed beforehand.

### Vessel Requirement

The mishna then states: kol haminchot yesh bahen ma'aseh kli bifnim ve'ein bahen ma'aseh kli bachutz — all minchot require the action of a kli sharet performed inside, and none of the required work may be done outside. There is some ambiguity as to the precise scope of this statement. It may be a general principle applying to every mincha without exception, or it may be a continuation of the discussion of the chavitim and similar offerings such as the Lechem Hapanim and Shtei HaLechem. Either way, the chiddush is at minimum twofold: first, that a kli sharet is required, and second, that these actions must take place inside the azara. This does not necessarily exclude all preliminary steps from being done elsewhere — it was established elsewhere that, for instance, measuring and sifting could be done outside — but from lisha onward, the work had to be done inside the azara, in a kli sharet.

The Gemara then asks how we know this, and Rabbi Zira derives it from a pasuk in Yechezkel: Vayomer elai zeh hamakom asher yevashlu sham hakohanim et ha'asham ve'et hachatat, asher yofu et hamincha levilti hotzi el hachatzer hachitzonah. The verse describes the place where the kohanim cook the chatat and asham, and states that the mincha too must be baked there, so as not to bring it out to the outer courtyard. Since the mincha is mentioned in the same context as the chatat and asham, we derive by comparison: just as those offerings require a kli and must be performed inside, so too the mincha requires a kli and must be performed inside.

## Shtei HaLechem

### Dimensions

With the setting established, the daf turns to a topic that demands both careful textual attention and some spatial reasoning — the dimensions of the Shtei HaLechem and the Lechem Hapanim. The Shtei HaLechem, brought on Shavuot, had the following dimensions: arkan shiva tefachim — length of seven tefachim — verachban arba — width of four — vekarnoseihen arba etzbaot, with corners four etzbaot high. These loaves were rectangular, not braided, with a definite thickness, and their raised corners gave them a distinctive appearance resembling horns, hence the term karnot.

The Lechem Hapanim — the Showbread placed on the shulchan each week — was larger: arko yud tefachim, verachbo chamisha tefachim, vekarnosav zayin etzbaot. That is, ten by five tefachim, with corners seven etzbaot high.

To help remember these two sets of dimensions, Rabbi Yehuda provides a mnemonic: shelo titeh, zad-yah-z. The first set (for the Shtei HaLechem) yields 7-4-4, and the second set (for the Lechem Hapanim) yields 10-5-7. Rabbi Yehuda was known for such mnemonics — he similarly constructed detzach adash be'achav in the Haggadah — though he does not tell us which set of numbers belongs to which offering; the mnemonic simply ensures you remember the numbers themselves.

### Ben Zoma

Ben Zoma comments on the verse venatata al hashulchan lechem panim lefanai tamid, interpreting it to mean lechem panim sheyihiyu lo panim — the bread must have a panim, a face. At first glance this statement is cryptic, and Ben Zoma offers no elaboration. His comment is best understood as an introduction to the opinions that follow in the mishna, which will describe how the bread was positioned and folded so as to present multiple surfaces or "faces." That explanation will emerge from the discussion below.

### Table Dimensions

The shulchan's dimensions are given as amatayim orko ve'amah rochbo — two amot in length and one amah in width. To work with these measurements alongside the bread's dimensions in tefachim, one must know the conversion rate. Here there is a machloket: an amah is either five or six tefachim. If an amah is five tefachim, then two amot (the table's length) equal ten tefachim, and one amah (the width) equals five tefachim. If an amah is six tefachim, then two amot equal twelve tefachim and one amah equals six tefachim. This conversion will be decisive in understanding the two opinions about how the bread was placed on the table.

## Bread Placement

### Rabbi Yehuda

The central spatial challenge is this: the Lechem Hapanim is ten tefachim in length, but the width of the shulchan (the dimension against which the bread's length was aligned) is only five tefachim according to Rabbi Yehuda's measure of the amah. The bread was not placed lengthwise along the length of the table; rather, noten arko keneged rochvo shel shulchan — the length of the bread was placed against the width of the shulchan. This means a ten-tefach loaf had to fit within a five-tefach space. The solution was to fold up the edges. Since you must do the same thing on both sides, the five tefachim of width are distributed as follows: two and a half tefachim are folded up on one side, two and a half on the other, with the base of the bread corresponding exactly to the width of the table. As the mishna states: vechofel tefachim umechetza mikan vefachim umechetza mikan nimtza arko melo rochvo shel shulchan, divrei Rabbi Yehuda. The length, once folded, exactly fills the width of the shulchan.

### Rabbi Meir

Rabbi Meir holds that an amah is six tefachim. Under his measure, the table is twelve tefachim long and six tefachim wide. The bread's dimensions remain unchanged: ten tefachim in length, five in width. But now the bread's length must fit within a width of six tefachim rather than five. This allows for a different folding arrangement: tefachayim mikan vefachayim mikan, two tefachim folded up on each side, and then vetechayim revach be'emtza — two tefachim of open space in the middle — kedei shetehei haruach menashevet bahen, so that air can circulate and the bread will not become moldy. As for the length: if the table is twelve tefachim long and each stack of bread is five tefachim wide, the two stacks placed side by side leave a gap of two tefachim between them down the length of the table.

This also explains Ben Zoma's remark about the bread having panim: once folded up on two sides, the loaf presents multiple faces — a bottom, two folded sides, and openings — giving it a distinctly three-dimensional, faced appearance.

### Abba Shaul

Abba Shaul introduces a related point: sham hayu notnim shenei bezichei levona shel lechem hapanim. According to his view, the two cups of levona — frankincense — that accompanied the Lechem Hapanim were placed in that space of two tefachim between the stacks. The levona served both a practical purpose, since it was offered on the mizbeach together with the Lechem Hapanim, and an aromatic one.

### Frankincense

An objection is raised: the verse says venatata al hama'aracha levona zacha — place the levona on the ma'aracha, the pile or stack, not between them. How then does Abba Shaul put it in the space between the stacks? Abba Shaul responds by citing the verse ule'vinyamin amar yedid Hashem yishkon lavetach alav, where the language of "on" or "upon" clearly does not mean literally on top of, but rather near. So too, al hama'aracha means near the stack — that is, in the adjacent space between the two stacks. The response is compelling: "upon" in such contexts is a common idiom for proximity rather than literal placement atop.

## Table Structure

### Golden Panels

The mishna describes ve'arba'ah snifin shel zahav hayu sham — four golden side panels flanking the stacks of bread, two for each side. These snifim served as supports for the structure. The word mefutzalim merosheihem is the subject of debate: one interpretation holds that the tops of these panels were split like the tines of a fork; another holds that they had small protruding pegs, like dowels on a coat rack. On this second interpretation, the half-hollowed reeds (kanim) that were inserted between the loaves would be placed with their hollow ends resting on these pegs, keeping them in place. A third interpretation holds that holes were bored into the panels themselves, and the rods were threaded through from one side to the other.

### Reeds and Rods

The mishna states: ve'esrim ushmonah kanim kechatzi kaneh chalul — twenty-eight rods, each like a half-hollowed reed. With two stacks of bread on the shulchan (fourteen loaves per stack, split into two ma'arachot of six each), there were fourteen rods per side and fourteen for the other, totaling twenty-eight: ve'arba'ah asar laseder zeh ve'arba'ah asar laseder zeh.

### Erev Shabbat Work

Lo sidur kanim velo netilatan docheh et HaShabbat — neither the arrangement of the reeds nor their removal overrides Shabbat. Instead, ela nichnas me'erev Shabbat veshomtan venotnan le'orko shel shulchan: on erev Shabbat, one would enter and slide the reeds out, placing them along the length of the shulchan. The exact nature of the melacha involved — whether it constitutes mefarek, makeh bapatish, or something else — is not resolved here. Part of the reasoning is practical: the reeds needed to be removed in order to extract the bread, since the bread had been baked with indentations corresponding to the rods, and removing the loaves without first withdrawing the rods was not possible. The detailed order of operations for erev Shabbat is a subject the Gemara takes up elsewhere.

### Vessel Orientation

A general principle is stated: vechol hakelim shehayu bamikdash orkan le'orko shel bayit — all the vessels of the Mikdash were aligned with their length running along the length of the building. This statement is mentioned here in connection with the reeds being placed le'orko shel shulchan, and the shulchan itself being aligned accordingly.

## Height Calculations

### Yehuda Height

Rabbi Yochanan now turns to calculating the height of the stacked bread. According to the opinion that each loaf was folded two and a half tefachim on each side (Rabbi Yehuda's position), the height of a single loaf as it stands on the table corresponds to two and a half tefachim. With six loaves stacked per ma'aracha, the total height is six times two and a half, which yields fifteen tefachim: nimtza hashulchan mekadesh chamisha asar tefachim lemalah.

### Meir Height

According to the opinion that each loaf was folded two tefachim on each side (Rabbi Meir), the height per loaf is two tefachim. Six loaves stacked yields twelve: nimtza hashulchan mekadesh yud-beis tefachim lemalah.

### Rod Embedding

One might object: ve'haika kanim — what about the rods inserted between the breads? Won't they add to the total height? The answer is sheku'ei meshaka lehu — the rods were embedded in the bread, meaning when the loaves were baked, indentations were formed on the underside to receive the rods.

This answer immediately raises a further question: mai ta'ama mishum demapish lehu — if the reason for the rods was to allow air to circulate so the bread would not spoil, and the rod is entirely buried in the bread, then what purpose do the rods serve? If the breads are sitting directly on each other, there is no airflow. The answer is demagbah lei purta — the rod was embedded only partially. The indentation was shallow, so the bread rested on the rod but retained a small airspace between each loaf. That minimal airspace, while technically adding to the height, is treated as ve'ika purta aval k'man de'leita dami — so slight that it is negligible and does not affect the round numbers.

### Corner Height

The Gemara now asks: ve'ika bezychim — what about the cups of levona? According to Rabbi Meir, who placed them between the stacks in the open space of two tefachim, they lie beside rather than atop the bread and do not add to the height. According to Rabbi Yehuda, who placed them on the bread itself, the Gemara explains: l'gabbei d'lechem havei yatbei, ul'hadei lechem havei kaimei — they were set into a hollow in the top loaf, so they did not add height beyond the loaf itself.

Ve'ika karnot — and what about the corners? Recall that the Lechem Hapanim had karnot seven etzbaot high. Converting: seven etzbaot equal one and three-quarters tefachim. Now consider what the total width of the bread amounts to when accounting for its thickness and karnot. The bread had a thickness of one tefach. Folded up on each side, the folded portion includes: one tefach of thickness, then one and three-quarters tefachim for the keren, then another one and three-quarters for the other keren, and then another tefach of thickness on the other side. The total: one plus one and three-quarters plus one and three-quarters plus one equals five and a half tefachim. But under Rabbi Yehuda's system, an amah (the table's width) is only five tefachim. There is half a tefach too many. How does it fit?

### Curved Corners

Karnot l'gabbei lechem kaif lehu u'lechem aleihu nayach — the corners were curved inward. Rather than standing straight up, the karnot bent inward toward each other, each side curving in by a quarter of a tefach, accounting for the surplus half tefach in total. The bread then rested on these curved corners without the total width exceeding the five tefachim of the table. This was accomplished through the use of a baking mold that shaped the bread accordingly.

## Table Frame

### Biblical Source

The Gemara now considers another structural feature: ve'ika misgarto — and what about the misgeret, the frame of the shulchan? The relevant pesukim state: ve'asita shulchan atzei shittim — the table was to be made of acacia wood — amatayim orko ve'amah rochbo ve'amah vachetzi komato; vetzipita oto zahav tahor, ve'asita lo zer zahav saviv; ve'asita lo misgeret tefach saviv; ve'asita zer zahav lemisgarto saviv. The table was to be overlaid with pure gold, fitted with a zer zahav (a golden crown) around its edge, and given a misgeret one tefach wide, itself crowned with a second zer zahav.

The question is: what is this misgeret, and where was it positioned relative to the tabletop?

### Frame Position

There are two opinions. The misgeret may have been the structural element connecting the four legs beneath the tabletop — the crosspieces that stabilize the legs and form the lower frame, upon which the daf (the flat tabletop panel) rested above. This is called misgarto l'mata — the frame was below. Alternatively, the misgeret may have been a raised border above the surface of the tabletop, forming a kind of fence around its perimeter. This is misgarto l'ma'ala — the frame was above.

### Angled Bread

If the misgeret was below, there is no problem: the bread was placed on top of the panel, and the frame adds no height to the bread stack. But ul'man d'amar misgarto l'ma'ala haichi hava — according to the opinion that the misgeret was above, you now have an extra tefach of height before the bread even begins, and that tefach cannot be dismissed as negligible rounding. The answer given is d'peruktei hava mefarkta v'lichma gabei d'shulchan hava yasiv — the misgeret was sloped or angled rather than a full vertical tefach, and the bottom loaf was similarly angled to rest in the groove created by this slope. The bottom bread was thus not raised a full tefach above the surface but rather slid into the angled misgeret, sitting at the level of the table itself. The total height was therefore not affected.

### Rabbi Yosei

A baraita introduces a third view: d'tanya Rabbi Yosei omer lo hayu sham snifin — Rabbi Yosei holds that there were no side panels (snifim) at all. Rather, misgarto shel shulchan ma'amedet et halechem — the misgeret of the shulchan itself held the bread in place. The misgeret stabilized the lowest loaf, and the remaining loaves were stacked above it with the assistance of the rods. The response in the Gemara is amar leih misgarto l'mata hayta — the misgeret was indeed present, but it was positioned below, and therefore the snifim were needed to support the stacks from the sides. According to the view that the misgeret was below, there is no surface feature above the tabletop to support the bread, making the snifim indispensable.

## Ritual Impurity

### Transportable Status

Rabbi Yochanan raises a question: according to the one who says the misgeret was below, the daf — the flat tabletop panel — can be reversed and placed either way, which would make it a movable object. According to the one who says the misgeret was above, the daf is essentially fixed in one orientation. But underlying this entire inquiry is a prior question: ke'ilu shulchan bar kibbul tumah hu — the assumption is that the shulchan is susceptible to tumah. Is that assumption warranted?

The difficulty is that kli etz ha'asui lenachat hu — a wooden vessel that is meant to remain stationary is not mekabel tumah. The principle derives from the pasuk mikol kli etz o beged o or o sak: medumiya desak, mah sak mittaltel malei vereikan af kol mittaltel malei vereikan — just as a sack is taken up whether full or empty, so too any vessel capable of tumah must be something one transports in both states. The shulchan, however, was not generally a portable object. It sat in the Beit HaMikdash and stayed there. Accordingly, one might argue it cannot be mekabel tumah at all.

### Reish Lakish

The answer is that the shulchan was in fact moved: shulchan nami mittaltel malei vereikan, kid'Reish Lakish. Reish Lakish derives this from the verse: mai d'cheitiv al hashulchan hatahor. The verse calls it the "pure" shulchan — tahor miklal de'ika tamei, implying that tumah is a real possibility. Furthermore, and more concretely, it teaches: ella melamed shemaggbihin oto le'olei regalim umarin bo lechem ve'omrin lahem reu chibatchem lifnei HaMakom. On the Regalim, the priests would lift the shulchan and carry it out to show the assembled pilgrims, saying: "See how beloved you are before God." The showbread — lechem hapanim, sometimes rendered as "showbread" precisely because of this display — was exhibited to the people. Since the shulchan was indeed transported on these occasions, it qualifies as a movable vessel and is therefore susceptible to tumah.

### Miracle of Bread

This practice prompts a related teaching: de'amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi nes gadol na'asa belechem hapanim — a great miracle was performed with the Lechem Hapanim, shenemar lasum lechem cham beyom hikalcho, that on the day it was taken off the table it was still warm, as fresh as the day it was placed. The Lechem Hapanim was placed on Shabbat and replaced the following Shabbat. The bread had been on the table for a full week — potentially exposed to conditions that would make it stale, cold, or moldy — yet when it was shown to the pilgrims, it appeared and felt freshly baked, steaming hot. The pilgrims saw this and were moved: God's love for Israel was manifest in the miraculous freshness of the bread brought before Him.

### Gold Plating

Having established that the shulchan could become tamei, the Gemara asks: vetipok lei mishum tzipui — why not resolve the entire question more simply, based on the gold plating? If the gold covering determines the character of the object, then the shulchan would be considered a metal vessel — and metal vessels are certainly mekabel tumah, regardless of whether they are transported or stationary.

The relevant principle is examined from a mishna: mi lo tnan hashulchan vehadofleki shenifchetu o shechipan beshayish veshayer bahem mekom hanachat kosot tamein — a table or bench that was broken or covered with marble (or another material) but still retains a portion large enough to place goblets upon it remains tamei. The principle derived is shayer in, lo shayer lo: if some of the original surface remains, it retains its original identity; if none remains, then no. Since the shulchan was completely gold-plated, nothing of the wood surface was left exposed. It would therefore take on the identity of the gold covering, which is metal, which is mekabel tumah.

A further nuance is raised: perhaps it depends on whether the tzipui omeid — whether the covering is firmly attached — or she'eino omeid — whether it is only loosely applied, abutted rather than bonded. Reish Lakish was asked this question by Rabbi Yochanan, and the answer was: lo shana tzipui omeid velo shana tzipui she'eino omeid — it makes no difference whether the plating is firmly attached or not. In either case, the plating determines the identity of the vessel. And whether the rims (livzivin) were plated or not was similarly irrelevant.

Given all this, why can't we simply say the shulchan is mekabel tumah because it is gold-plated metal? Perhaps because vchi teima achti chashivi velo batli — if the underlying material is itself of great value or importance, it may not be nullified by the plating. Reish Lakish held that this exception applied only to kelim from Alexandria that came from overseas; ordinary vessels, even valuable ones, are nullified by their plating. But Rabbi Yochanan held that even locally made valuable vessels are nullified. On his view, then, one should have a clear answer that the gold-plated shulchan is treated as metal.

### Torah's Term

The resolution is: shani shulchan d'rachmana karyei eitz — the shulchan is different, because the Torah itself calls it wood. The verse states, shenemar hamizbeach eitz shalosh amot gavoah ve'arko shtayim amot umiktze'otav lo ve'arko vekirotav eitz vayedaber elai zeh hashulchan asher lifnei Hashem. What begins as a description of the mizbeach made of wood concludes with "this is the shulchan that is before Hashem." The verse calls it eitz — wood — even though it was in fact gold-plated. The Torah's own characterization of the shulchan as wood overrides the general principle that plating determines identity. Therefore, even under Rabbi Yochanan's view that plating normally nullifies the underlying material, the shulchan retains its identity as a wooden vessel, and the question of whether its daf can be mekabel tumah remains live.

## Agada

### Altar Teaching

The daf closes with a striking observation of aggadic import. The verse just cited begins with the mizbeach and ends with the shulchan: patach bamizbeach vesiyem bashulchan. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar both teach: kol zman sheBeit HaMikdash kayam mizbeach mechaper al ha'adam, ve'achshav she'ein Beit HaMikdash kayam shulchano shel adam mechaper alav. When the Beit HaMikdash stood, the mizbeach brought atonement for a person. Now that it no longer stands, a person's own table brings atonement. This is the source for the well-known teaching that one's dining table is like a mizbeach — through Birkat HaMazon, divrei Torah spoken at the table, and the hospitality shown to guests, the table assumes the atoning and sanctifying role that once belonged to the altar. The transition from mizbeach to shulchan within a single verse is thus read as a deliberate divine hint, embedded in the Torah itself, anticipating the time when the Temple would no longer stand and the table of every Jewish home would carry the weight of the sacred.
