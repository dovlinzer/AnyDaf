# Menachot 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Todah & Measures

### Todah Overview

Daf Ayin Zayin continues a discussion of the specifics of how *menachot* are made, but at this point we move on to the *todah*, which has not received much attention until now. One might even question whether the breads of the *todah* should be called a *minchah* at all — they do not come on their own accord. The same is true of the *omer* and the *shtei halechem*, though the *omer* does come together with its *korban* in a more independent fashion. And beyond that, the *todah* is **Kodshei Kodashim** and its meat is eaten by the owners throughout all of *Yerushalayim*. Still, the breads of the *todah* are very much like a *minchah*, and our *Mishnah* turns its attention to them.

The *todah* is essentially a *shelamim* eaten within one day, and it comes accompanied by forty loaves of *challah*, of four different types. One loaf of each type is given to the *kohen*, and the remainder is eaten by the owners throughout *Yerushalayim*, just like the meat of the *shelamim*. As for when one brings a *todah* — the *Torah* does not specify a particular occasion. It is defined as a thanksgiving *korban* (*im al todah yakrivenu*), and *Chazal*, based on *pesukim* in *Tehillim*, identify the **Arba'ah Tzrichim Lehodot** — the four who are obligated to give thanks — which is the basis for the practice of reciting *Birkat HaGomel*, for instance after an overseas journey or recovery from serious illness. The *Torah*, however, does not limit the *todah* to those circumstances.

### Five Se'ahs = Six

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַתּוֹדָה הָיְתָה בָּאָה חָמֵשׁ סְאִין יְרוּשַׁלְמִיּוֹת, שֶׁהֵן שֵׁשׁ מִדְבָּרִיּוֹת. שְׁתֵּי אֵיפוֹת, הָאֵיפָה שָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין, עֶשְׂרִים עִשָּׂרוֹן – עֲשָׂרָה לֶחָמֵץ וַעֲשָׂרָה לַמַּצָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The flour for the loaves accompanying **the thanks offering would come** from a measure of **five Jerusalem *se'a*** of flour, **which are** equivalent to **six wilderness** *se'a*. The *se'a* referred to in the Bible when the Jewish people were in the wilderness is smaller than the *se'a* used later in Jerusalem. This is equivalent to **two ephahs,** each **ephah** being **three** wilderness ***se'a*.** These two ephahs are **twenty** measures of **a tenth** of an ephah. **Ten** of these tenths were used **to** make **leavened** loaves **and ten** of these tenths were used **to** make unleavened loaves, i.e., ***matza*.**

What happened is this: whatever the measurement of a *se'ah* was at the time of the *Torah* — the wilderness measure — when Israel entered the Land, the measures were changed at a certain point. What had formerly been called a *se'ah* and a fifth was now called a *se'ah*. The container was made larger; the same name, *se'ah*, was applied to a bigger quantity. So what used to be six measures in the desert became five of the new measures. An analogy would be the revaluation of the Israeli *shekel*, where the new *shekel* was set equal to ten of the old *shkalim*: the underlying value did not change, only the denomination.

It is important to note that the *se'ah* here refers not to wheat but to actual flour. This is a substantially greater quantity than one might initially assume. Six *se'in* of the original wilderness measure equal *shtei ephot*, because an *ephah* is three *se'ah* — so six original *se'in* constitute two *ephot*. Since an *issaron* is an *asirit ha-ephah*, a tenth of an *ephah*, two *ephot* yield twenty *issronot*.

This stands in contrast to what was learned previously, where one begins with *asarah issronot* of wheat in order to produce one *issaron* of flour. Here, we are speaking of twenty *issronot* of flour — the finished product.

### Se'ah Redefinition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה לְחָמֵץ – עִשָּׂרוֹן לְחַלָּה, וַעֲשָׂרָה לְמַצָּה – וּבְמַצָּה שָׁלֹשׁ מִינִין: חַלּוֹת, רְקִיקִין, וּרְבוּכָה. נִמְצְאוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה עֶשְׂרוֹנִים וּשְׁלִישׁ לְכׇל מִין וּמִין, וְשָׁלֹשׁ חַלּוֹת לְעִשָּׂרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna elaborates: There are **ten** tenths **for the** loaves of **leavened bread, a tenth** of an ephah **per loaf. And** there are **ten** tenths **for the** loaves of ***matza*. And among** the loaves of ***matza*** there are **three types: Loaves, wafers, and** those **poached in water,** ten loaves of each type. **Consequently,** there are **three-and-one-third tenths** of an ephah **for each and every type, three loaves per tenth** of an ephah.

The *Mishnah* at the bottom of *Ayin Vav Amud Bet* states: **HaTodah hayetah ba'ah chamishah se'in Yerushalmiyyot she-hen shishah* Midbariyyot* — the *todah* (meaning here its accompanying breads) would be brought with five *se'in* of Jerusalem measures, which were six of the wilderness measures.

The *Mishnah* continues: *asarah le-chametz va-asarah le-matzah* — ten *issronot* for the *chametz* breads and ten for the *matzah* breads. There were forty loaves in total: ten loaves of *chametz* and thirty loaves of *matzah* (three different types of *matzah* of ten loaves each). Yet the division of flour is equal between *chametz* and *matzah*: the ten loaves of *chametz* receive ten *issronot*, and the thirty loaves of *matzah* also receive ten *issronot*. This means that each loaf of *chametz* receives one full *issaron*, while each loaf of *matzah* receives only a third of an *issaron*.

To appreciate the scale: a standard *minchah* consists of one *issaron* of flour, divided into ten loaves. The *omer* is one loaf made from a full *issaron*. The *shtei halechem* is two loaves, each from a full *issaron*. The *todah* is comparable in kind to the *omer* and *shtei halechem* — each of its ten *chametz* loaves is made from a full *issaron*, roughly the equivalent of a five-pound bag of flour. Each *chametz* loaf is thus the size of an entire *minchah*, and since there are twenty *issronot* in total, the *todah* brings twenty times the flour of a single *minchah*.

The *Mishnah* then translates these quantities into new Jerusalem measures. The total of five new *se'in* equals thirty *kav* (since each *se'ah* is six *kav*). This total is divided equally: fifteen *kav* for *chametz* and fifteen *kav* for *matzah*. Each *chametz* loaf receives a *kav* and a half — which equals one old *issaron*. For the *matzah*, fifteen *kav* divided among thirty loaves across three types yields five *kav* per type of ten loaves, which is two *challot* per *kav*. All of this is equivalent to what was already expressed in the original measures; the new measures simply use familiar contemporary terms.

A student raised the question of whether the change in measurement meant that people in the time of the **Beit Hamikdash** were required to bring twenty percent more flour. The answer is no — the underlying amount was identical. The terminology changed, but not the quantity. What we formerly called *shesh se'ah* is now called *chamesh se'ah*, because the container referred to as a *se'ah* has become larger. The *asirit ha-ephah*, the *issaron*, remains a tenth of the *ephah* as defined in the wilderness. One is simply calibrating back to the *midbar* measures when determining the actual quantities involved.

### Se'ah to Ephah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְמִדָּה יְרוּשַׁלְמִית הָיוּ שְׁלֹשִׁים קַב, חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר לְחָמֵץ וַחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר לְמַצָּה. חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר לְחָמֵץ – קַב וּמֶחֱצָה לְחַלָּה, חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר לְמַצָּה. וּבְמַצָּה שָׁלֹשׁ מִינִין: חַלּוֹת, וּרְקִיקִין, וּרְבוּכָה. נִמְצְאוּ חֲמֵשֶׁת קַבִּין לְכׇל מִין וּמִין, וּשְׁתֵּי חַלּוֹת לְקַב.
>
> **Translation:** **And in the Jerusalem measure there were thirty *kav*, fifteen** *kav* **for** the loaves of **leavened bread and fifteen for** the loaves of ***matza*.** The mishna elaborates: There are **fifteen** *kav* **for** the loaves of **leavened bread, one and one-half *kav* per loaf.** And there are **fifteen** *kav* **for** the loaves of ***matza*. And among** the loaves of ***matza*** there are **three types: Loaves, wafers, and** those **poached in water. Consequently,** there are **five *kav* for each and every type, two loaves per *kav*.**

The top of *Ayin Zayin Amud Alef* spells out the distribution in greater detail. There are ten *issronot* for the *chametz*, one *issaron* per *challah*. For the *matzah*, there are ten *issronot* divided among three types — *challot*, *rekikin*, and *revuchah* — yielding three and a third *issronot* per type, and since each type has ten loaves, each *matzah* loaf receives a third of an *issaron*. In new Jerusalem measures, each type of *matzah* has five *kav*, or two loaves per *kav*.

## Ephah = 3 Se'ahs

### Chametz & Matzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַתּוֹדָה הָיְתָה בָּאָה חָמֵשׁ סְאִין יְרוּשַׁלְמִיּוֹת. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא "הָאֵיפָה וְהַבַּת תֹּכֶן אֶחָד יִהְיֶה (לָכֶם)". מָה בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין, אַף אֵיפָה שָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: The flour for the loaves accompanying **the thanks offering would come** from a measure of **five Jerusalem *se'a*,** which are equivalent to six wilderness *se'a*. The *se'a* referred to in the Bible when the Jewish people were in the wilderness is smaller than the *se'a* used later in Jerusalem. This is equivalent to two ephahs, each ephah being three wilderness *se'a*. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** i.e., that there are three *se'a* in an ephah, derived? **Rav Ḥisda said:** They are derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "The ephah and the *bat* shall be of one measure"** (Ezekiel 45:11). Therefore, **just as the *bat*,** a measure for liquids, is **three *se'a*, so too the ephah,** a measure for dry goods, **is three *se'a*.**

The *Gemara* returns to the opening line of the *Mishnah* — *chamesh se'in Yerushalmiyyot* — and asks: *minah hani mili?* Where does this come from? The question is not simply how we know the flour quantity for the *todah*, but more specifically: how do we know the equivalence stated in the *Mishnah* that an *ephah* equals three *se'ah*? This is critical because the *issaron*, the *Torah*'s standard unit of flour measurement for *menachot*, is defined as an *asirit ha-ephah* — a tenth of an *ephah*. To translate that into familiar terms, one must know how large an *ephah* is.

### Matzah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַת גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן? אִילֵּימָא מִדִּכְתִיב "לָשֵׂאת (אֶת) מַעְשַׂר הַחֹמֶר הַבָּת", אֵיפָה נָמֵי הָכְתִיב "וַעֲשִׂירִית הַחֹמֶר הָאֵיפָה"! אֶלָּא חוֹמֶר לָא יָדַעְנָא כַּמָּה, הָכָא נָמֵי לָא יָדַעְנָא כַּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the *bat* itself, from where do we** derive its measure? **If we say** that we derive it **from** that **which is written** in the same verse: **"That the *bat* may contain the tenth-part of a *ḥomer*,"** and since one *ḥomer* consists of thirty *se'a*, one *bat* is equivalent to three *se'a*, there is a difficulty: With regard to **an ephah as well, isn't it written** in the same verse: **"And the ephah the tenth-part of a *ḥomer*"?** Why, then, must the measure of an ephah be derived from that of a *bat*? **Rather, I do not know how much** the measure of **a *ḥomer*** is; consequently, when the verse states that the ephah is one-tenth of one *ḥomer*, this does not demonstrate the measure of the ephah. **Here too, I do not know how much** the measure of the *bat* is.

The *Gemara* considers whether the *bat* can be derived from the *pasuk* in *Yechezkel* that states it is a tenth of a *chomer*. If a *chomer* is thirty *se'ah*, then a tenth of it — the *bat* and the *ephah* — would be three *se'ah*. But the *Gemara* objects: *chomer lo yadana kama* — we do not actually know how large a *chomer* is. Knowing that both *bat* and *ephah* are a tenth of a *chomer* tells us their relationship to each other, but not their absolute size. So we remain stuck.

Similarly, the *pasuk* that says *va-asirit ha-chomer ha-ephah* tells us the *ephah* is a tenth of the *chomer*, and we already knew the *ephah* equals the *bat*. But if we do not know the size of the *chomer*, we cannot determine the size of the *ephah* from either *pasuk* alone.

### Kav Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: "וְחֹק הַשֶּׁמֶן הַבַּת הַשֶּׁמֶן וּמַעְשַׂר הַבַּת מִן הַכּוֹר עֲשֶׂרֶת הַבַּתִּים חוֹמֶר כִּי עֲשֶׂרֶת הַבַּתִּים חוֹמֶר".
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** derive the volume of the ephah **from here,** from a subsequent verse, where it is written with regard to separating *teruma*: **"And the set portion of oil, the *bat* of oil, shall be the tithe of the *bat* out of the *kor*, which is ten *bat*, even a *ḥomer*; for ten *bat* are a *ḥomer*"** (Ezekiel 45:14). The verse states that there are ten *bat* in one *kor* and ten *bat* in one *ḥomer*. Since it is known that one *kor* is equivalent to thirty *se'a*, the verse indicates that there are also thirty *se'a* in one *ḥomer*. It can therefore be derived from the verse that there are three *se'a* in one *bat*, and consequently, three *se'a* in one ephah.

The *Gemara* therefore turns to a different *pasuk*: *ve-chok ha-shemen ha-bat ha-shemen uma-asar ha-bat min ha-kor, ve-ha-kor aseret ha-batim* — a tenth of a *kor* is a *bat*, and ten *batim* make a *kor*. What does this add? The *pasuk* here uses the word *kor* rather than *chomer*, and it tells us explicitly that ten *batim* make a *kor*. This confirms two equivalences: *chomer* and *kor* are the same measure, and *bat* and *ephah* are the same measure. We now know the ratio — the *kor*/*chomer* is ten times a *bat*/*ephah* — but we still need to know the absolute size.

The *Gemara* proceeds on the assumption — cited in *Rashi* and *Tosafot* — that *Chazal* had an established tradition that a *kor* equals thirty *se'ah*. With that anchor, the translation is complete: a *kor* is thirty *se'ah*, so a *bat* and an *ephah*, each being a tenth of a *kor*, are three *se'ah*. The *issaron*, a tenth of an *ephah*, is therefore three-tenths of a *se'ah*.

One might note that this *pasuk* from *Yechezkel* involves oil — *shemen* — while we are ordinarily concerned with dry measures. The *Gemara* tends to be careful in distinguishing *midas lach* from *midas yavash*, yet it uses this *pasuk* as its primary Rosetta Stone for converting *Torah* measures into known quantities. The apparent inconsistency is noted but not fully resolved.

What the *Gemara* is doing, at its core, is finding a point of translation. The *Torah* says *asirit ha-ephah*, the *issaron*. We need to know what an *ephah* is in terms of measures we recognize. *Yechezkel* provides the bridge: an *ephah* is a tenth of a *chomer*/*kor*, and a *kor* is thirty *se'ah* — a measure *Chazal* knew. Now we can convert *Torah* measures into something concrete.

### Measurement Source

The *Gemara* having established that an *ephah* is three *se'ah* and an *issaron* is a tenth of an *ephah*, the practical upshot is that an *issaron* equals three-tenths of a *se'ah*. According to contemporary estimates — for instance the conversion chart in Yitzhak Frank's *Practical Talmudic Dictionary* — an *issaron* is approximately 2.49 liters, which is slightly more than a two-liter bottle, and indeed roughly equivalent to a five-pound bag of flour. This is the consistent working estimate used throughout the discussion.

## Shmuel's Sixth Rule

### Ephah-Bat Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל הַמִּדּוֹת יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת, וְלֹא עַל הַמַּטְבֵּעַ יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת, וְהַמִּשְׂתַּכֵּר לֹא יִשְׂתַּכֵּר יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the Sages increased the size of the measures so that five Jerusalem measures are equal to six wilderness measures. With regard to the practice of augmenting measures, **Shmuel says:** If the residents of a certain place want to change the standard of their measures and augment them by a certain fraction, **they may not increase the measures** by **more than one-sixth, and** they may **not** increase the value of **a coin** by **more than one-sixth** of its previous value. **And one who profits** from his sales **may not profit** by **more than one-sixth.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a famous ruling of Shmuel, one whose primary context is *Bava Batra* but which is directly relevant here. *Amar Shmuel: ein mosifin al ha-midot yoter mi-shish* — one may not add to measures by more than a sixth. This addresses precisely the phenomenon described in the *Mishnah*: the transition from *midbar* measures to Jerusalem measures. When a community changes what it calls by a given name — for instance, what was formerly called a *se'ah* is now called by the same name but refers to a larger quantity — that change cannot exceed a sixth.

Shmuel adds two parallel rulings: *velo al ha-matbe'a yoter mi-shish* — the same applies to currency; one cannot revalue a *shekel* so that it is more than a sixth larger than the old *shekel*. And *velo ha-mistaker yoter mi-shish* — a person making a profit cannot profit by more than a sixth.

This last point requires careful clarification. *Ona'ah* is a *Torah* prohibition against overcharging — specifically, charging above or below the established market rate for a commodity. If there is no established rate, or if one is pricing an original creation, there is no *issur* of *ona'ah*. The *ona'ah* framework uses the threshold of a sixth: less than a sixth variance is presumed to be within the range of acceptable fluctuation and is *mochel*; exactly a sixth requires the overcharge to be returned but the sale remains valid; more than a sixth results in *bitul mekach*, annulment of the sale. That is the *ona'ah* framework.

Shmuel's ruling about profit is distinct: it addresses not overcharging relative to a market rate, but the profit margin itself. Even when there is no going rate to violate, Shmuel holds that one may not profit more than a sixth. The straightforward case Shmuel envisions is that of a middleman who purchases goods in one place and sells them in another. Whether and how this extends to creative works or situations without a clear cost basis is a subject that awaits fuller treatment in *Bava Batra*.

### Chomer-Kor Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם אַפְקוֹעֵי תַּרְעָא – אִי הָכִי, שְׁתוּת נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes these statements. When Shmuel said: They may not increase the measures by more than one-sixth, **what is the reason** for this? **If we say** it is **because** doing so causes market **prices to rise,** the same concern should apply to raising the prices by **one-sixth,** and therefore that should **also not** be allowed.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for the limit of a sixth on measure changes? The first proposed explanation is *mishum afkuei tar'a* — out of concern for disruption of the *sha'ar*, the rate of exchange, meaning market prices. The concern is that during a transitional period, not everyone receives the new information simultaneously. If a seller is still quoting prices based on the old *se'ah* and the buyer is measuring with the new, larger *se'ah*, the buyer scoops out more than the seller intended to sell — effectively receiving a double *se'ah* for the price of one old *se'ah*. The resulting confusion could trigger what might look like sudden price doubling or market panic, even though no underlying change in value has occurred.

The *Gemara* rejects this explanation: *ee hachi shtus nami lo* — if the concern is that people will panic at the new prices, then even a sixth is too large a change and should also be prohibited.

### Measure Translation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם אוֹנָאָה, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לֶיהֱוֵי בִּיטּוּל מִקָּח, וְהָא אָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמִדָּה וְשֶׁבְּמִשְׁקָל וְשֶׁבְּמִנְיָן אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹס מִכְּדֵי אוֹנָאָה – חוֹזֵר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one might say that the prohibition is **due to** concern for **exploitation;** and they may increase the measures only by up to one-sixth so **that there will not be nullification of the transaction,** as the transaction is nullified only when the disparity is more than one-sixth of the value of the item. The Gemara raises an objection: **But doesn't Rava say:** With regard to **any item that** is otherwise subject to the *halakhot* of exploitation, and it is sold **by measure, or by weight, or by number, even** if the disparity was **less than the measure of exploitation** in the transaction, the transaction is **reversed?** A disparity of one-sixth between the value of an item and its price constitutes exploitation only in cases where there is room for error in assessing the value of an item. In a case where the details of the item are easily quantifiable, any deviation from the designated quantity results in a nullification of the transaction. The statement of Shmuel concerns sales involving measures.

The *Gemara* then proposes *mishum ona'ah* as the rationale: the concern is that the confusion between old and new measures will lead to situations of overcharging or undercharging. If the variance is kept to a sixth, it remains within the *ona'ah* framework where the purchase stands and the difference is simply returned. More than a sixth would produce *bitul mekach*.

But this too is rejected, on the basis of Rava's ruling: *kol davar she-be-midah u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan afilu pachas mi-kdei ona'ah chozer* — anything sold by precise measure, weight, or count is annulled even if the variance is less than the *ona'ah* threshold. When a precise measure is agreed upon, any deviation from that measure — however small — constitutes *bitul mekach*, because there is no tolerance for error where exactness is specified. If I agree to sell you exactly one *kav* and you receive a slightly different amount, the sale is void regardless of how close it comes. Therefore, if the concern were *ona'ah*, even a change of less than a sixth would be problematic, and fixing the limit at a sixth would not help.

### Dry-Wet Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם תַּגָּרָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִמְטְיֵיהּ דְּיָאנָה. דְּיָאנָה הוּא דְּלָא לִימְטְיֵיהּ, רְוָחָא לָא בָּעֵי? זְבֵן וְזַבֵּין – תַּגָּרָא אִיקְּרִי?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the prohibition is for the benefit of **the merchant, so that there will not be a loss** suffered **by** a merchant who might not realize that a new standard was issued, and might sell in accordance with the old standard. Since a merchant usually enjoys a profit of one-sixth of the value of an item, if the standard is not increased by more than this amount he will not suffer a loss, as at worst he will forfeit his profit margin. The Gemara notes: This explanation is also difficult, since even if the aim is to ensure **that there will not be a loss** for the merchant, **does he not need** to earn **a profit?** There is a well-known adage in this regard: If you **buy and sell** without earning any profit, **will** you **be called a merchant?** A merchant must profit from his sales; therefore, if this decree was instituted for the protection of merchants, the Sages should have ensured that they earn a profit.

The *Gemara* then proposes *mishum tigra* — out of concern for the merchant. The worry is that a merchant who buys goods in one town at the old price and travels to sell them in a town using the new, larger measure will unknowingly sell more goods than he intended for the same price, thereby suffering a loss. By limiting the measure change to a sixth, the worst-case scenario is that his profit margin is somewhat reduced, but he does not incur a net loss.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *revacha lo ba'ei?* But shouldn't he also need to make a profit? If even a small change in the measure can cut into his profit margin, why is a sixth the cutoff? And further — *zavin ve-zavin tigra ikrei?* Can someone who buys and sells without making any money even be called a merchant? Merely avoiding a loss is insufficient if there is no profit.

Ultimately, the *Gemara* concedes that none of these explanations adequately accounts for why a sixth specifically is the threshold, as in each case the logic would seem to demand either more or less restriction. This leads to a different approach entirely.

### Isaron-Liter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שְׁמוּאֵל קְרָא אַשְׁכַּח וּדְרַשׁ, "הַשֶּׁקֶל עֶשְׂרִים גֵּרָה עֶשְׂרִים שְׁקָלִים חֲמִשָּׁה וְעֶשְׂרִים שְׁקָלִים עֲשָׂרָה וַחֲמִישָּׁה שֶׁקֶל הַמָּנֶה יִהְיֶה לָכֶם", מָנֶה מָאתָן וְאַרְבְּעִין הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ḥisda said:** The prohibition is not based on logical reasoning. Instead, **Shmuel found a verse and interpreted it homiletically: "And the shekel shall be twenty *gera*; twenty shekels, five and twenty shekels, ten, and five shekels, shall be your *maneh*"** (Ezekiel 45:12). According to this verse, the sum of all of these numbers, sixty shekels, is equivalent to a *maneh*. This is problematic: How can **a *maneh*** consist of sixty shekels? Since each biblical shekel is equivalent to four dinars, if a *maneh* is equal to sixty shekels, a *maneh* **is two hundred and forty** dinars. But a *maneh* is actually equal to twenty-five shekels, which are one hundred dinars.

The *Gemara* concludes: *ela amar Rav Chisda, Shmuel kra ashkhach ve-darash* — Shmuel found a *pasuk* and expounded from it. The *pasuk* is again from *Yechezkel*: *ha-shekel esrim gerah*, a *shekel* is twenty *gerah*, and then it enumerates — twenty *shkalim*, twenty-five *shkalim*, fifteen *shkalim* — *ha-maneh yiheyeh lachem*. So a *maneh* equals sixty *shkalim*. Since a *shekel* is four *zuz*, this makes a *maneh* equal to 240 *zuz*.

But a *maneh* is ordinarily one hundred — the word *maneh* in *Gemara* usage means a hundred *zuz*. The *Gemara* therefore observes: *maneh matan ve-arba'in havei?* Does this mean a *maneh* is 240 *zuz*? The resolution is that the measures of the *Mikdash* are double those used outside it — a known principle. So a *maneh* of the *Mikdash* is double the ordinary *maneh*. Half of 240 is 120. But a *maneh* is 100, so how do you get from 100 to 120?

Here the *Gemara* applies the principle of measuring the sixth *milbar* — from the outside, from the final figure. From 100 to 120 is a twenty percent increase of the base. But 20 out of 120 — measuring from the end — is exactly a sixth. So the measure was increased by a fifth of the original amount, which equals a sixth of the final amount. That is the model: you may add up to a sixth measured from the new, larger figure, which corresponds to adding a fifth of the original.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מָנֶה שֶׁל קוֹדֶשׁ כָּפוּל הָיָה, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מוֹסִיפִין עַל הַמִּדּוֹת וְאֵין מוֹסִיפִין יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שְׁתוּתָא מִלְּבַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one can **learn from** the verse **three** matters: **Learn from it** that the **sacred *maneh* was doubled,** so that it equaled two hundred, not one hundred, dinars. **And** furthermore, as Ezekiel stated that the *maneh* will be sixty shekels, not fifty, **learn from it** that a community may **increase measures, but they may not increase** them by **more than one-sixth. And learn from it** that **the one-sixth** is calculated **from the outside,** i.e., it is one-sixth of the final sum, which is one-fifth of the previous sum.

From this single *pasuk* three conclusions are drawn: that the *maneh shel kodesh* was double the ordinary *maneh*; that measures are indeed changed from time to time; and that the change may not exceed a sixth (measured from the outside). The *Mishnah* itself then serves as confirmation: *amar Ravina matnitin nami dika* — our *Mishnah* is precise on this point. The *todah* used to require *shesh se'in* in the wilderness and now requires *chamesh se'in* of the Jerusalem measure. Five new *se'in* equal six old ones, meaning the new *se'ah* is one-and-a-fifth the old. Adding a fifth of the base is exactly adding a sixth of the new total. *Shema mina.*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: תּוֹדָה הָיְתָה בָּאָה חָמֵשׁ סְאִין יְרוּשַׁלְמִיּוֹת שֶׁהֵן שֵׁשׁ מִדְבָּרִיּוֹת, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: The mishna is also precisely** formulated so as to reflect the fact that the one-sixth increase is calculated from the outside, **as it teaches:** The flour for the loaves accompanying **the thanks offering would come** from a measure of **five Jerusalem *se'a*** of flour, **which are** equivalent to **six wilderness** *se'a*. One can infer that the *se'a* could be increased by only one-sixth from the outside. The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is the *halakha*.

It is worth noting that while the *pasuk* provides the formal *shiur*, the analysis of the various concerns raised — inflation, *ona'ah*, merchant protection — likely informs the underlying rationale. The *pasuk* sets the ceiling; the concerns explain why a ceiling was needed at all.

## Terumah Division

### Shmuel's Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִכּוּלָּם הָיָה נוֹטֵל אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה תְּרוּמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְהִקְרִיב מִמֶּנּוּ אֶחָד מִכׇּל קׇרְבָּן תְּרוּמָה לַה׳", "אֶחָד" – שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל פָּרוּס, "מִכׇּל קׇרְבָּן" – שֶׁיְּהוּ כׇּל הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת שָׁוִין, שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל מִקׇּרְבָּן עַל חֲבֵירוֹ. "לַכֹּהֵן הַזֹּרֵק אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים לוֹ יִהְיֶה", וְהַשְּׁאָר נֶאֱכָל לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **From all** of the four types of loaves accompanying the thanks offering, **one takes one** loaf **from** each set of **ten as *teruma*,** to be given to a priest, **as it is stated: "And he shall present from it one of each offering as a *teruma* unto the Lord;** to the priest that sprinkles the blood of the peace offerings against the altar it shall be given"** (Leviticus 7:14). The verse is analyzed: **"One"** indicates **that one should not take a sliced** loaf; **"of each offering"** indicates **that all the offerings should be equal,** i.e., **that one should not take** a loaf **from** one type of **offering for another** type; **"to the priest that sprinkles the blood of the peace offerings against the altar it shall be given," and the rest** of the loaves are **eaten by the owner.**

The next *Mishnah* introduces the *terumah* of the *todah* loaves: *mikulam haya notel echad me-asarah truma* — from each group of ten loaves, one is taken as *terumah*. The result is that one loaf from each of the four types — one *chametz* and three types of *matzah* — is given to the *kohen*, for a total of four loaves. The *pasuk* says *ve-hikriv mimenu echad mikol korban trumah la-Hashem*: one from each *korban* group is given as *terumah*. From the word *echad* — one — we learn that it must be a whole loaf, not a partial one. From *mikol korban* we learn that all groups must be treated equally: one may not take two loaves from the *chametz* group in lieu of one from a *matzah* group. Each type yields exactly one loaf to the *kohen*. The *kohen* who performed the blood service of the *shelamim* receives those four loaves, and the remaining thirty-six are eaten by the owners throughout *Yerushalayim*, as with the meat of the *shelamim*.

### Market Stability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "וְהִקְרִיב מִמֶּנּוּ" – מִן הַמְחוּבָּר, "אֶחָד" – שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל פָּרוּס, "מִכׇּל קׇרְבָּן" – שֶׁיְּהוּ כׇּל הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת (שָׁווֹת) [שָׁוִוין], שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן עַל חֲבֵירוֹ, "תְּרוּמָה לַה׳" – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מִכַּמָּה הִיא,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches some of the *halakhot* of *teruma* to be taken from the loaves of the thanks offering that are derived from the verse: "And he shall present from it one of each offering as a *teruma* unto the Lord." The Gemara cites a *baraita* that interprets the same verse: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The phrase **"And he shall present from it"** indicates that the loaves must all be as one, i.e., *teruma* may be taken **from** the loaves only when they are joined **together** in one place. **"One"** indicates **that one should not take a sliced** loaf. **"Of each offering"** indicates **that all the offerings should be equal,** i.e., **that one should not take** a loaf **from one** type of **offering for another** type. When the verse states: **"As a *teruma* unto the Lord," I do not know from how many** loaves the *teruma* **is** taken.

The *Beraita* then records a *tanna* asking: *eini yodeia mikamah hi* — from the word *trumah* alone, I do not know the proportion. Is *terumah* one out of ten, one out of fifty, one out of five? The *pasuk* says *ve-hikriv mimenu echad mikol korban trumah la-Hashem*, one from each group — but how large is the group?

### Ona'ah Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵינִי דָּן: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן "תְּרוּמָה", וְנֶאֱמַר בִּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר "תְּרוּמָה", מָה לְהַלָּן אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה, אַף כָּאן אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **I** therefore **derive** this from a verbal analogy: It **is stated here,** in the passage of the loaves describing the thanks offering: **"*Teruma*," and** it **is stated with regard to *teruma* of the tithe:** "Then you shall set apart from it **a *teruma*** for the Lord, even a tithe of the tithe" (Numbers 18:26). **Just as below,** with regard to *teruma* of the tithe, **one** out of every **ten** is separated as *teruma*, **so too here,** with regard to the loaves of the thanks offering, **one** out **of** every **ten** loaves is separated as *teruma*.

A *gezeirah shavah* is proposed. The word *trumah* appears here regarding the loaves, and the same word appears in the context of *trumat ma'aser* — the tithe of the tithe that the *Levi* separates and gives to the *kohen*. Just as *trumat ma'aser* is one-tenth of the *Levi*'s tithe, so here the one loaf is one-tenth of its group. This establishes that each group of ten loaves yields one loaf to the *kohen*, meaning each group contains ten loaves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן "תְּרוּמָה", וְנֶאֱמַר בְּבִכּוּרִים "תְּרוּמָה", מָה לְהַלָּן אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר, אַף כָּאן אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps, **go this way** and derive the measure from a different verbal analogy: It **is stated here: "*Teruma*," and** it **is stated with regard to first fruits: "*Teruma*"** (Deuteronomy 12:17). **Just as below,** with regard to first fruits, **it has no measure, so too here,** say that **it has no measure.**

The alternative argument considers that *bikurim* are also called *trumah* (*trumat yadecha*), and *bikurim* have no fixed minimum — one may bring as little or as much as one wishes of the first fruits. Perhaps, then, the *terumah* of the *todah* loaves also has no fixed measure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה? דָּנִין תְּרוּמָה שֶׁאֵין אַחֲרֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה, מִתְּרוּמָה שֶׁאֵין אַחֲרֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ בִּכּוּרִים, שֶׁיֵּשׁ אַחֲרֵיהֶן תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Let us consider to which** of the two cases, *teruma* of the tithe or the first fruits, the case of *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks offering is more **similar.** It is logical that **one derives** the *halakha* of **a *teruma* that is not followed by** another ***teruma*,** such as *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks offering, **from a *teruma* that is not followed by** another ***teruma*,** such as *teruma* of the tithe. **And** the case of the **first fruits will not** serve as **proof, since they are followed by** another ***teruma*,** as *teruma* and tithes are taken after the first fruits are separated.

The *Gemara* adjudicates between these two comparisons using the following principle: one should learn a *trumah* that has no subsequent *trumah* from another *trumah* that has no subsequent *trumah*. The loaves of the *todah*: once the one loaf is given to the *kohen*, there are no further *matnot kehunah* owed. Similarly with *trumat ma'aser*: once the *Levi* gives it to the *kohen*, no further gifts remain. But with *bikurim*, the owner still owes *terumah* and *ma'asrot* on the remainder of his produce after separating *bikurim*. So we learn from *trumat ma'aser*, not from *bikurim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ, דָּנִין תְּרוּמָה הַנֶּאֱכֶלֶת בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ, מִתְּרוּמָה הַנֶּאֱכֶלֶת בְּמָקוֹם קָדוֹשׁ, וְאַל תּוֹכִיחַ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר, שֶׁנֶּאֱכֶלֶת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps, **go this way** and reason that **one derives** the *halakha* of ***teruma*** of the loaves of the thanks offering, **which is eaten in a sacred place,** in Jerusalem, **from *teruma*** of the first fruits, **which is eaten in a sacred place,** in Jerusalem. **And** the case of ***teruma* of the tithe will not** serve as **proof, as it may be eaten anywhere.**

The counterargument proposes learning from *bikurim* based on a different criterion: both the *terumah* of the *todah* loaves and *bikurim* are eaten by *kohanim* in the **Beit Hamikdash** — the *makom kadosh* — whereas *trumat ma'aser* may be eaten anywhere. The *Gemara* resolves this through the shared language *mimenu trumah* appearing in both the *todah* verse and the *trumat ma'aser* verse — a *gezeirah shavah* — which anchors the comparison to *trumat ma'aser* and confirms the one-in-ten proportion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמָה לַה׳", וּכְתִיב בִּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר "מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמָה" – לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה.
>
> **Translation:** Since both of these comparisons are equally plausible, **the verse states** with regard to the loaves of the thanks offering: **"From it…a *teruma* unto the Lord," and,** similarly, **it is written with regard to *teruma* of the tithe: "From it a *teruma*," to** indicate that there is **a verbal analogy** between the two, from which it can be derived that one separates one out of every ten loaves of the thanks offering as *teruma*.

### Precise vs Imprecise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ לִתְרוּמָה שֶׁהִיא אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה, אֲבָל אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מִכַּמָּה הִיא חַלָּה. הֲרֵינִי דָּן: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן "לֶחֶם", וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן בִּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם "לֶחֶם", מָה לְהַלָּן עִשָּׂרוֹן לְחַלָּה, אַף כָּאן עִשָּׂרוֹן לְחַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **We have learned with regard to *teruma*** of the loaves of the thanks offering **that its** measure is **one** out **of** every **ten. But I do not know** from this verbal analogy **from how much** flour each leavened **loaf** is to be prepared. **I** therefore **derive** this from a verbal analogy: It **is stated here,** in the passage concerning the loaves of the thanks offering: **"Bread"** (Leviticus 7:13), **and** it **is stated there, with regard to the two loaves,** i.e., the public offering on *Shavuot* of two loaves from the new wheat: **"Bread"** (Leviticus 23:17). **Just as there,** with regard to the two loaves, one prepares the loaves with **a tenth** of an ephah **per loaf, so too here,** with regard to the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering, one prepares the loaves with **a tenth** of an ephah **per loaf.**

Having established that each group has ten loaves and that one-tenth is given to the *kohen*, the *Beraita* turns to the next unknown: *eini yodeia mikamah hi challah* — I do not know how much flour goes into each *challah* loaf.

The *Torah* uses the word *lechem* for the *chametz* loaves of the *todah*, and the same word is used for the *shtei halechem*. The *gezeirah shavah* of *lechem-lechem* suggests that just as each loaf of *shtei halechem* is an *issaron*, so too each *chametz* loaf of the *todah* is an *issaron*.

### Merchant Profit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ, נֶאֱמַר כָּאן "לֶחֶם", וְנֶאֱמַר בְּלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים "לֶחֶם", מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת, אַף כָּאן שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps, **go this way** and derive the *halakha* from a different verbal analogy: It **is stated here,** with regard to the loaves of the thanks offering: **"Bread," and it is stated** there, **with regard to the shewbread: "Bread"** (Leviticus 24:7). One can conclude that **just as there,** with regard to the shewbread, each of the twelve loaves is baked from **two-tenths** of an ephah of flour, **so too here,** each of the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering should be baked from **two-tenths** of an ephah of flour.

The alternative comparison is to *lechem hapanim*, where each loaf is two *issronot*. One might argue: learn *lechem* from *lechem hapanim* and require two *issronot* per loaf. The *Gemara* presents both sides and then adjudicates.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה? דָּנִין מִנְחָה הַבָּאָה חָמֵץ עִם הַזֶּבַח, מִמִּנְחָה הַבָּאָה חָמֵץ עִם הַזֶּבַח, וְאַל יוֹכִיחַ לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים, שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּא חָמֵץ עִם הַזֶּבַח.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Let us consider to which** of the two cases, the two loaves or the shewbread, the case of the leavened loaves of the thanks offering is more **similar.** It is logical that **one derives** the *halakha* of the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering, which is **a meal offering that comes** as **leavened bread** along **with an animal offering, from** another **meal offering that comes** as **leavened bread** along **with an animal offering,** such as the two loaves, which are also leavened, and which come with animal offerings (see Leviticus 23:17–19). **And the shewbread will not** serve as **proof, since it does not come** as **leavened bread,** nor does it come **with** an animal **offering.**

To learn from *shtei halechem*: the *todah* is (1) *chametz* and (2) accompanies a *korban*. The *shtei halechem* is also *chametz* and also accompanies a *korban*. These two characteristics are a strong basis for comparison. *Lechem hapanim*, by contrast, is neither *chametz* nor does it accompany a *korban* — two reasons not to use it as the model.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ, דָּנִין מִנְחָה הַבָּאָה מֵאָרֶץ וְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, מִן הֶחָדָשׁ וּמִן הַיָּשָׁן, מִמִּנְחָה הַבָּאָה מֵאָרֶץ וְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, מִן הֶחָדָשׁ וּמִן הַיָּשָׁן, וְאַל יוֹכִיחוּ שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, שֶׁאֵין בָּאוֹת אֶלָּא מִן הֶחָדָשׁ וּמִן הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps, **go this way** and reason that **one derives** the *halakha* of the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering, which is **a meal offering that** can **come from** the grain of **Eretz** Yisrael **or** of **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **from new** grain **or from old** grain; **from** the *halakha* of the shewbread, which is also **a meal offering that can come from** the grain of **Eretz** Yisrael **or from outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **from new** grain **or from old** grain. **And the two loaves will not** serve as **proof, since they** can **come only from new** grain **and from Eretz** Yisrael.

The counterargument for *lechem hapanim*: the flour used for the *todah* loaves and for *lechem hapanim* may come from anywhere — old or new grain, from inside or outside of *Eretz Yisrael*. The *shtei halechem*, on the other hand, must come specifically from the new grain of *Eretz Yisrael*. In that respect, the *todah* resembles *lechem hapanim* more than it resembles *shtei halechem*.

### Maneh Doubling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "מִמּוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם תָּבִיאוּ לֶחֶם תְּנוּפָה שְׁתַּיִם", שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "תָּבִיאוּ", וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "תָּבִיאוּ"? שֶׁכׇּל מַה שֶּׁאַתָּה מֵבִיא מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר הֲרֵי הוּא כָּזֶה, מַה לְּהַלָּן עִשָּׂרוֹן לְחַלָּה אַף כָּאן עִשָּׂרוֹן לְחַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Since both of these comparisons are equally plausible, **the verse states** concerning the two loaves: **"You shall bring out of your dwellings two loaves of waving"** (Leviticus 23:17). **As** there is **no** need for **the verse to state: "You shall bring," what,** then, is the meaning when **the verse states: "You shall bring"?** This indicates **that whatever,** i.e., any meal offering, **you bring from a different place,** it must **be like this,** i.e., the two loaves. Therefore, **just as there,** in the case of the two loaves, the measure is **a tenth** of an ephah **per loaf, so too here,** in the case of the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering, the measure is **a tenth** of an ephah **per loaf.**

The *Gemara* resolves the ambiguity through an additional *drasha*. The *pasuk* by *shtei halechem* states *mimoshvoteichem tavi'u lechem tnufah*. The word *tavi'u* is deemed superfluous — the verse could simply have said *mimoshvoteichem lechem tnufah*. The extra *tavi'u* teaches that any other bread you bring that is comparable to *shtei halechem* will be like it: *mah l'halan issaron l'challah, af kan issaron l'challah* — just as there each loaf is an *issaron*, so here too. Furthermore, the word *tihyena* in the context of *shtei halechem* — containing the extra *yud*, which stands for ten — teaches that the standard is ten *issronot*, one *issaron* per loaf, rather than two *issronot* for the total pair. With this, we know: ten *chametz* loaves receive ten *issronot*, one per loaf.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת, אַף כָּאן שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "תִּהְיֶינָה".
>
> **Translation:** But **if** so, perhaps one can derive that **just as there,** in the case of the two loaves, the entire measure for the two loaves is **two-tenths** of an ephah, **so too here,** in the case of the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering, the entire measure for all ten loaves of leavened bread is **two-tenths.** To exclude this possibility, **the** same **verse states: "They shall be,"** to indicate that each leavened loaf is prepared from a tenth of an ephah. Accordingly, the ten loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering are prepared with a total of ten tenths of an ephah.

For the *matzah* loaves, the *Torah*'s phrasing in the *todah* passage places the *matzot* alongside the *chametz* and implies equality: *al chalot lechem chametz* — bringing the *matzot* together with the *chametz* as two equal halves. This establishes *chametz neged chametz havei matzah* — the *matzah* must be equal in total flour to the *chametz*. Since the *chametz* uses ten *issronot*, so too the *matzah* uses ten *issronot*.

### Sixth Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ עֲשָׂרָה לְחָמֵץ, עֲשָׂרָה לְמַצָּה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "עַל חַלּוֹת לֶחֶם חָמֵץ".
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **We have learned** that there are **ten** tenths of an ephah of flour **for the** loaves of **leavened bread** accompanying the thanks offering. **From where** is it derived that there are **ten** tenths of an ephah **for** the thirty loaves of ***matza*? The verse states: "If he brings it for a thanks offering, then he shall bring with the thanks offering unleavened cakes mingled with oil, and unleavened wafers spread with oil, and cakes mingled with oil, of fine flour poached. **With cakes of leavened bread** he shall present his offering, with the sacrifice of his peace offerings for thanks" (Leviticus 7:12–13).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶגֶד חָמֵץ, הָבֵא מַצָּה. נִמְצְאוּ עֶשְׂרִים עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה, עֲשָׂרָה לְחָמֵץ, וַעֲשָׂרָה לְמַצָּה.
>
> **Translation:** By mentioning all the loaves of unleavened bread in one verse and the loaves of leavened bread in the subsequent verse, it is indicated that one must **bring** the ***matza*** in a measure **corresponding to** the measure of the loaves of **leavened bread. Consequently,** there are **twenty tenths** of flour for the **loaves of the thanks offering, ten for the** loaves of **leavened bread, and ten for the *matza*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל עֲשָׂרָה שֶׁבְּמַצָּה לֹא יְהוּ כּוּלָּן אֶלָּא מִמִּין אֶחָד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "אִם עַל תּוֹדָה יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ וְהִקְרִיב עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה חַלּוֹת מַצּוֹת בְּלוּלֹת בַּשֶּׁמֶן וּרְקִיקֵי מַצּוֹת מְשֻׁחִים בַּשָּׁמֶן סֹלֶת מֻרְבֶּכֶת וְגוֹ׳". נִמְצְאוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה עֶשְׂרוֹנִים וּשְׁלִישׁ לְכׇל מִין וּמִין, וְשָׁלֹשׁ חַלּוֹת לְעִשָּׂרוֹן, וְנִמְצְאוּ לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה אַרְבָּעִים. נוֹטֵל מֵהֶן אַרְבַּע וְנוֹתֵן לְכֹהֵן, וְהַשְּׁאָר נֶאֱכָלִים לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that one brings **ten** loaves of ***matza*,** and that **all of them shall be of only one type.** Therefore, **the verse states: "If he brings it for a thanks offering, then he shall bring with the thanks offering unleavened cakes mingled with oil, and unleavened wafers spread with oil, and cakes mingled with oil, of fine flour poached.** With cakes of leavened bread he shall present his offering." The verse indicates that one must bring three types of *matza*. **Consequently,** there are **three-and-one-third** of a tenth of an ephah of flour **for each and every type, and three loaves to a tenth** of an ephah. **And consequently,** there are a total of **forty loaves of the thanks offering.** The owner of the offering **takes four of them,** one loaf of each type, **and gives** them **to the priest, and the remaining** loaves **are eaten by the owner** and any ritually pure Jew to whom he wishes to give the loaves. This concludes the *baraita*.

Having established that the *matzah* receives ten *issronot* for thirty loaves — since there are three types and each type has ten loaves — one might think all thirty *matzah* loaves could be of one type. An explicit *pasuk* forecloses this: the *Torah* enumerates three distinct categories of *matzah* — *challot matzot blulot ba-shemen*, *rekikei matzot meshuchim ba-shemen*, and *solet murbechet* — and all three must be present.

The totals therefore work out as follows. Ten *issronot* are divided among three types of *matzah*, yielding three and a third *issronot* per type. Since each type has ten loaves, that is three loaves per *issaron*. Combined with the ten *chametz* loaves at one *issaron* each, the *todah* totals twenty *issronot* of flour and forty loaves. From each group of ten, one loaf is given to the *kohen* — four loaves in total — and the remaining thirty-six are eaten by the owners. So concludes the *Beraita*, which will be further unpacked in tomorrow's shiur.
