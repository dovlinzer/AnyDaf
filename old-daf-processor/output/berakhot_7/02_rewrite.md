# Berakhot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mincha Prayer

### Rav Huna on Mincha

The daf begins, at the bottom of 6b, with a series of statements transmitted by Rabbi Chelbo in the name of Rav Huna. The first: *l'olam yehei adam zahir b'tefillat hamincha* — one should always be careful about the Mincha prayer. The proof is that Eliyahu was answered by God specifically at the time of Mincha.

### Elijah & Mincha

The verse adduced is *v'yehi ba'alot hamincha vayigash Eliyahu hanavi* — it was at the time of Mincha that Eliyahu the prophet came forth and said *aneni Hashem aneni*, "answer me God, answer me." The doubled language of "answer me" is explained as two separate requests: first, that fire should descend from heaven to demonstrate the acceptance of his sacrifice, and second — *shelo yomru ma'aseh kshafim hem* — that people should not dismiss the miracle as mere witchcraft. Even after a miracle, people find ways to discount what they have witnessed. In any case, Eliyahu was answered at the Mincha hour, which provides the proof text for the special importance of that prayer.

### Maariv Prayer

Rabbi Yochanan then extends the argument: not only Mincha, but Maariv as well deserves special emphasis. The verse cited is *tikon tefillati ketoret lefanecha mas'at kapai minchat arev* — let my prayer be established like incense before You, the lifting of my hands like the gift of evening. Here *minchah* carries the sense of a gift or offering, not the specific prayer time, and *arev* — evening — grounds the verse in the context of the Maariv prayer.

### Shacharit Prayer

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds Shacharit to the list: *Hashem boker tishma koli boker e'erach lach va'atzapeh* — in the morning You will hear my voice, in the morning I will set my prayer before You and look toward You.

One can understand the Gemara's motivation here. Maariv has a somewhat second-class status — there is a debate whether it is *reshut* or *chova* — and people tend to be tired at night and may not bring to it the focus it deserves. Mincha, falling right in the middle of the day amid all its busyness, requires a particular act of will to stop and daven. One understands why the Gemara wants to underscore both. But by the time every tefillah has been given its supporting verse, the reader is left wondering what has actually been accomplished. If everything is equally important, it is not entirely clear that the original point about Mincha has been made.

## Simchat Chatan Kallah

### Wedding Feast Obligation

The Gemara continues with another statement of Rabbi Chelbo in the name of Rav Huna, now moving away from tefillah entirely: *kol hane'eneh misudat chatan v'eino mesamcho* — anyone who benefits from the wedding feast of the *chatan* and does not rejoice him transgresses *b'chamisha kolot*, the five voices. It is notable that the text focuses on the *chatan* alone. The verse cited is *kol sasson v'kol simcha kol chatan v'kol kalla kol omrim hodu Hashem Tzevaot* — the voices of joy, of gladness, of groom, of bride, and of those who praise God. These five voices represent what is meant to be created and sustained at a wedding feast. To attend and eat while merely serving one's own appetite — treating what ought to be a spiritual experience as an occasion for physical gratification — is to transgress all of them. The formulation is reminiscent of what Rav Yisrael Salanter expressed differently: *yenems gashmius is dayn ruchnius* — another person's physical needs are your spiritual needs. Here the inversion is instructive: one is taking something that should be a spiritual experience and reducing it to the merely physical.

### Five Voices Reward

The flip side: if one does rejoice the *chatan* and *kalla*, what is the reward? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says *zocheh l'Torah shenitna b'chamisha kolot* — one merits Torah, which was given in five voices. The verse from the Sinai narrative lists *kolot u'vrakim v'anan kaved al hahar v'kol shofar* and *v'yehi kol hashofar holech v'chazek m'od* — five distinct voices. The Gemara raises an objection: but the verse *v'chol ha'am ro'im et hakolot* implies six voices, not five. The answer is that those additional voices preceded *matan Torah*; at *matan Torah* itself there were only five. The deeper point is that the act of bringing joy to others at their wedding — what amounts to *gemilut chasadim* in its most communal expression — ultimately merits the highest form of speech, which is Torah.

### Todah Sacrifice

Rav Papa offers a different formulation of the reward: *ke'ilu hikriv todah* — it is as though one offered a sacrifice of thanksgiving. The verse is *mevi'im todah beit Hashem*, from the same passage in Yirmiyahu that contains the *kol sasson v'kol simcha*. The connection is significant: when one connects genuinely with another's joy rather than merely experiencing gratitude for one's own blessings, that communal sense is itself an expression of thanksgiving.

### Building Jerusalem

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak says: *ke'ilu bana achat michurvot Yerushalayim* — it is as though one built one of the destroyed places of Jerusalem. The verse is *ki ashiv et shevut ha'aretz kevarishonah amar Hashem* — I will restore the land as it was at first. The significance here is transparent, particularly in light of the *nevuah* of Yirmiyahu that underlies one of the *sheva brachot*: the vision that only in messianic times would *kol chatan v'kol sasson v'kol simcha* again be heard in the *rechovot Yerushalayim*. By sustaining and creating that joy, one helps bring about the reality that Yirmiyahu could only envision for the future. This makes the experience of attending a wedding in Jerusalem today particularly charged, given that the *pesukim* in the *sheva brachot* originally expressed a hope that seemed realizable only at the end of days.

## Yirat Shamayim

### Yirat Shamayim Power

The Gemara continues with yet another statement of Rabbi Chelbo in the name of Rav Huna: *kol adam sheyesh bo yirat shamayim devarav nishma'im* — anyone who possesses fear of heaven, his words are heard. The verse is *sof davar hakol nishma et ha-Elokim yera* — the end of the matter, all is heard: fear God. The *pshat* of the verse is simply the concluding injunction to fear God; but the Gemara reads *hakol nishma* — "all is heard" — as conditioned on *et ha-Elokim yera*, meaning: it is when a person genuinely fears God that his words carry the weight of being heard. This is not only a metaphysical promise; experience attests that the humble, genuinely pious person commands a quality of attention that mere charisma does not always produce.

### Tzaddik & Creation

The verse continues: *ki zeh kol ha'adam* — for this is the whole of man. Rabbi Elazar interprets this in the name of the Holy One: *kol ha'olam kulo lo nivra ela bishvil zeh* — the entire world was created only for the sake of this person, the one who fears God. This is the *kol ha'adam* — the whole purpose of humanity. One reading is simply that this is the ideal all should strive toward. Another reading, which the subsequent statements seem to bear out, is that in order for one truly God-fearing person to exist in the world, an entire civilization is required to support and sustain that person — the clothing, the food, the infrastructure of human society all go into enabling this one individual.

### Tzaddik = World

Rabbi Abba bar Kahana offers a distinct formulation: *shakol zeh keneged kol ha'olam kulo* — this God-fearing person is equivalent in worth to the entire world.

### Others Accompany

Rabbi Shimon ben Azzai (and some attribute the statement to Rabbi Shimon ben Zoma) says: *kol ha'olam kulo lo nivra ela letzavot lazeh* — the entire rest of the world was created solely to accompany this person. One cannot live outside society; the *yarei Elokim* needs friends, community, and the full apparatus of human civilization. Everyone else exists to make that possible. These are statements of remarkable hierarchical force — while they celebrate the achievement of *yirat Elokim* as the highest human attainment and the purpose of creation, they assign the rest of humanity an essentially supporting role. It is worth noting that Rambam, in *Moreh Nevuchim* or perhaps in his *Peirush HaMishnah*, uses precisely this framework but applies it to the philosopher — arguing that the philosophers who achieve genuine intellectual knowledge of God are the ones for whose sake civilization exists, and everyone else exists to make the philosophers possible.

## Greeting Shalom

### Initiate Shalom

Another statement of Rabbi Chelbo in the name of Rav Huna: *kol she-yodea be-chavero she-hu ragil litein lo shalom yaktim lo shalom* — if one knows that a friend is in the habit of greeting him, one should take the initiative and greet first. Do not wait for the other person to express enthusiasm. The verse: *bakeshu shalom v'radfeihu* — seek peace and pursue it. The point is that creating a sense of connection goes in both directions, and it is important that the greeting not be a one-way street.

### Theft of Shalom

If one greeted another and received no response, *nikra gazlan* — he is called a thief. The verse: *ve-atem bi'artem ha-kerem gezelat he-ani be-vateichem* — you have destroyed the vineyard; the theft from the poor is in your houses. What has been stolen from the poor person is not money but the *shalom aleichem* itself — the acknowledgment of his presence, the sense of being seen and welcomed. That is all he needed, and it was withheld. This is a pointed teaching, and its practical application in daily life — on *Shabbos*, on the street, in a community where one encounters strangers — is difficult to miss.

## God's Prayer

### God Davens

The Gemara now moves to 7a with a statement of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yosi: *minayin she-HaKadosh Baruch Hu mitpallel* — how do we know that God prays? The verse: *va-haviotim el har kodshi ve-simachtim be-veit tefillati* — I will bring them to my holy mountain and make them rejoice in my house of prayer. The Gemara notes that the verse says *tefillati* — my prayer — not *tefillatan*, their prayer. This implies not merely the house where prayer is received, but where prayer is offered. Hence: God prays. Yesterday's discussion established that God wears *tefillin* inscribed with the praises of Israel. Now we learn that God also *davens*. The question naturally arises: to whom does God pray?

### Prayer Content

Rav Zutra bar Tuvia answers in the name of Rav. God's prayer is: *yehi ratzon milfanai she-yichbeshu rachamay et ka'asi* — may it be My will that My compassion subjugate My anger. *V'yigolu rachamay al middotai* — and may My compassion wave over My other attributes; the verb *yigolu* is particularly evocative, suggesting the waves of compassion washing over the other attributes. *V'etnaheig im banai be-middat ha-rachamim v'ekaneis lahem lifnim mi-shurat ha-din* — and may I deal with My children with the attribute of compassion and go beyond what the strict letter of the law would require.

This is, on its surface, strange: God praying to Himself. Yet any parent can recognize the prayer — the hope that when confronted with the failures of one's children, one will find the capacity for compassion rather than anger. The *midat hadin* is not presented as wrong; there is a place for it. But the genuine aspiration is that the *midat harachamim* should prevail. The theology is deeply anthropomorphic, and that will disturb those troubled by such formulations. But the Maharsha reads this through a kabbalistic lens: above all the *sefirot* is *ein sof*, which admits no anthropomorphism, but the first of the *sefirot* that can be personified is *keter* — the crown. The name *Aktriel*, which appears momentarily, literally means "my crown is God," and it is with *keter* that the struggle between the divine attributes is associated. The prayer of *keter* is that it should prevail over the lower attributes — a reading that fits the structure of the passage.

### Ishmael's Vision

A *braita* is then cited: Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha relates that once he entered the *Kodesh Kodashim* to offer the *ketoret* — establishing that he was serving as *Kohen Gadol*. That a *tana* in a *braita* also served as *Kohen Gadol* is itself notable, requiring that this *braita* was composed before the *churban*. He relates: *vera'iti Aktriel Yah Hashem Tzevaot she-hu yoshev al kise ram venisa* — I saw God, enthroned on a high and elevated throne. God said to him: *Yishmael beni barcheni* — Ishmael, my son, bless me. So not only does God pray; He requests a blessing from a human being. The blessing Rabbi Yishmael offered was the very same prayer quoted above — that God's compassion should subjugate His anger and that He should deal with His children beyond the letter of the law. *Venana li b'rosho* — God nodded His head in acquiescence, which explains how we know that this is the content of God's prayer: it is precisely the blessing He desired.

### Blessing Significance

The Gemara draws the following lesson: *shelo tehe birkat hedyot kala be'einecha* — do not regard the blessing of a common person as insignificant. If God Himself desired a blessing from a human being, then every blessing carries significance and should not be treated lightly. The lesson is clear enough, though it has not always been universally observed.

## Appeasement Rules

### Wait for Calm

The Gemara continues: *amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Yosi minayin she-ein martzin lo le-adam bi-sh'as ka'aso* — how do we know that one does not attempt to appease a person in the moment of his anger? The verse: *panai yeleichu va-hanichoti lach* — my face will go before you and I will give you rest. The Gemara reads *panai* as *panim shel za'am* — the face of anger — yielding the meaning: let My anger pass away, and then I will deal with you calmly. God says to Moshe: wait until my *panim shel za'am* passes, and then we can speak. The practical wisdom is evident.

### God's Anger

The Gemara then pauses to ask: does God actually get angry? The question is somewhat surprising given that the prayer just discussed prays that God's compassion should overcome His anger — implying that anger is a real possibility. Nor is this unclear from reading the Torah itself. Nevertheless, the Gemara cites a *braita*: *v'El zo'eim b'chol yom* — God gets angry every day. How long does His anger last? A *rega*. And how long is a *rega*? One part of fifty-eight thousand eight hundred and eighty-eight of an hour — an almost incomprehensibly small fraction of time. *Ve-ein kol beriyah yecholah le-chavein osah sha'ah* — no creature can pinpoint that moment, with one exception.

### Bilaam & Anger

*Chutz mi-Bil'am ha-rasha* — except for Bil'am the wicked. Bil'am claimed of himself *yodea da'at Elyon* — I know the knowledge of the Most High. The Gemara's rejoinder is one of the more memorable deflating lines in the tractate: *hashta da'at behemto lo hava yadai da'at Elyon hava yadai?* — he could not even know what his own donkey was thinking, and he knows what God knows? The answer, however, is that Bil'am did not possess comprehensive divine knowledge; what he possessed was one precise skill: *she-hayah yodea le-chavein osah sha'ah she-ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu ko'eis bah* — he knew how to identify that precise infinitesimal moment of divine anger. The strategic implication was that if he could tap into that moment, he could effectively curse Israel.

Rabbi Elazar explains the verse from Michah — *lemaan da'at tzidkot Hashem* — as God saying to Israel: know how many acts of kindness I performed for you, *she-lo ka'asti bimei Bil'am ha-rasha* — for I did not permit myself to become angry during the entire period of Bil'am's activity. *She-ilmalei ka'asti lo nishtayer mi-sone'eihem shel Yisrael sarid u-falit* — had I allowed myself even that one moment of anger, not even the smallest remnant of the enemies of Israel — meaning Israel itself — would have survived, because Bil'am would have exploited it. *Melamed she-kol otam ha-yamim lo za'am* — throughout all those days, God restrained even that infinitesimal moment of anger.

The Gemara then returns: how much is a *rega*? *Rega k'meimrei* — the time it takes to say the word *rega*. This is a larger unit than the fraction given earlier, but still a fraction of a second.

Tosafot raise a practical question: even granting that Bil'am could identify that split second, what could he possibly say in half a second that would constitute a curse? Tosafot answer: he could have said the single word *kalem* — destroy them. Alternatively, Tosafot suggest that *me'acher she-haya matchil kilalato b'otah sha'ah* — if he had begun his curse in that moment, *haya mazik afilu le'achar mikan* — it would have had damaging effect even beyond that moment. This observation has a human resonance as well: tapping into someone's anger at its peak allows the anger to spill beyond its natural bounds. There is also a halakhic application noted in the name of the Rogatchover: he was asked whether one can begin davening when there is not enough time remaining before *sof zman tefillah* to complete the entire prayer. Based on this Tosafot, he ruled that beginning within the time is sufficient, since beginning at the right moment is the essential act.

The larger point about the *rega* of divine anger is also worth drawing out. God has a moment of anger, but only a moment; it is contained, not dominant. In human terms, one may need a brief moment to express or vent anger, but the ideal is to contain it to a small, bounded period and not allow it to become a defining characteristic. The commandment *v'halachta bi'drachav* — to emulate God — is sometimes invoked to justify harsh judgment and the calling out of evil. But this Gemara suggests that even granting that capacity, the *rega* of anger is not what defines the divine character or what *ve-halachta bi'drachav* primarily demands of us.

### Failed Curse

The Gemara relates a story connected to this theme. There was a certain *tzadoki* in the neighborhood of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi who caused him considerable affliction by constantly challenging the rabbinic tradition with prooftexts. One day Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi took a rooster, positioned it by the legs of his bed, and watched it, waiting for the exact moment when its comb would turn fully white — the sign of God's anger — so that he might exploit that moment to curse the *tzadoki*, just as Bil'am had sought to do. When that moment arrived, he fell asleep. He concluded: *lav orcha l'me'evad hachi* — this is not the proper way to act. *V'rachamav al kol ma'asav ktiv* — God's compassion rests on all His creatures. *V'chtiv gam anos l'tzaddik lo tov* — and Scripture says it is not good even for a *tzaddik* to bring about punishment upon others. Even when the motivation is *l'shem shamayim*, attempting to exploit divine anger to bring punishment upon others is not the way of those who act with genuine sanctity.

### When Angry

A *baraita* is then cited in the name of Rabbi Meir explaining precisely when that moment of divine anger occurs. It is not random: *b'sha'ah shehachamah zorachat v'chol malchei mizrach u'maarav manichim kitreihem b'rasheihem u'mishtachavim lachamah* — at the moment that the sun rises and all the kings of East and West remove their crowns and prostrate themselves before the sun, effectively declaring the sun to be the true deity, *miyad ko'es ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — at that moment God becomes angry. According to Rabbi Meir, the anger is not arbitrary but a specific response to the near-universal abandonment of God that this moment represents. This formulation also has the advantage of making the moment more identifiable than a fraction of a second within an hour.

## Mussar Internalization

### One Moment Matters

Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yosi then offers another statement: *tova mardut achat b'libo shel adam yoter mikama malkiyot* — better is one moment of self-directed internalization in a person's heart than many beatings from the outside. The word *mardut*, as Rashi explains, derives from the sense of beating down — but here it is the heart being beaten down inward, the bringing of a message home to oneself. The verse is from Hoshea's *mashal* of the faithless wife, who is described as being in a fallen state after having abandoned her husband: the turning point comes when she says to herself *elcha v'ashuva el ishi harishon ki tov li az me'ata* — I will return to my first husband, for it was better for me then. It is the moment of self-realization, not the external circumstances of suffering, that constitutes the genuine turning point.

### Understanding Wins

Resh Lakish goes further: *yoter mime'ah malkiyot* — better is it than even a hundred beatings. The verse: *tachas ga'arah b'mevin m'hakot ksil me'ah* — better is a rebuke to one who understands than beating a fool a hundred times. External punishment, however severe, cannot substitute for the moment when a person genuinely internalizes the message. A million things in life may signal that one is going in the wrong direction, but none of it registers until one finally brings it inward.

## Moses' Three Requests

### Shechinah on Israel

The Gemara continues with another statement of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yosi, one that ties back to earlier themes of *yissurin shel ahavah*. *Sheloshah devarim bikkesh Moshe milifnei ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu ve-natan lo* — Moshe requested three things from God, and all three were granted. The first: *bikkesh she-tishreh Shechinah al Yisrael* — that the divine presence should rest on Israel. The proof that this was granted: *halo be-lechtecha immanu* — is it not in Your going with us, which Moshe asked after the *cheit ha-eigel*, when God announced He would withdraw His direct presence. In the end, *gam et ha-davar asher dibarta e'eseh* — God said He would do what Moshe had asked.

### Israel's Uniqueness

The second request: *bikkesh she-lo tishreh Shechinah al ovdei kochavim* — that the *Shechinah* should not rest upon the nations. This is not a request to exclude the nations from any relationship with God, but that the unique quality of the *Shechinah's* indwelling should be specific to Israel. The proof text: *ve-niflinu ani ve-ammecha* — that we shall be distinct, I and Your people. This theme of Israel's unique covenantal relationship with God runs through the *sefer Devarim*, as the Gemara noted earlier in its discussion of what is inscribed in God's *tefillin*.

### God's Ways

The third request: *bikkesh le-hodio derachav shel ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — Moshe wished to know God's ways, how God acts in matters of justice in the world. The verse: *hodieni na et derachecha* — please make Your ways known to me. What followed was Moshe's question: *ribbono shel olam, mippenei mah yesh tzaddik v'tov lo v'yesh tzaddik v'ra lo yesh rasha v'tov lo v'yesh rasha v'ra lo* — why does it appear that some righteous people prosper, others suffer, some wicked prosper, and others suffer? The world does not seem to operate according to the principle that the Torah implies — that the righteous are rewarded and the wicked punished. This question is, of course, a perennial one in *Chazalic* literature, addressed earlier in the tractate through the framework of *yissurin shel ahavah*. Here, the Gemara casts Moshe himself as the one posing it. The *pshat* of *hodieni derachecha* in the *pesukim* connects to God's self-revelation as *El rachum v'chanun* — Moshe asking to know how to invoke these attributes when God's anger is aroused. The Gemara broadens this into the wider question of theodicy.

## Theodicy Problem

### Four Categories

God's response to Moshe's question: *tzaddik v'tov lo tzaddik ben tzaddik, tzaddik v'ra lo tzaddik ben rasha, rasha v'tov lo rasha ben tzaddik, rasha v'ra lo rasha ben rasha* — a righteous person who prospers is a righteous person the child of a righteous person; one who suffers is the child of a wicked person; a wicked person who prospers is the child of a righteous person; one who suffers is the child of a wicked person. The underlying principle is that the ledger of a person's parents' deeds continues to affect one's own fate, such that apparent inconsistency between a person's own deeds and their experience is explained by the parental account still being cleared.

### Parents' Deeds

This answer is not entirely satisfying as a theological solution, and it will be subjected to immediate scrutiny. But the basic framework is that the apparent injustice in one's own experience reflects not an arbitrary world but an inherited moral accounting.

### Contradicting Verses

The Gemara immediately challenges the answer: *vehachtiv poked avon avot al banim* — one verse says God visits the sins of the fathers upon the children. *Ve-chtiv u-vanim lo yumtu al avot* — another verse says children are not put to death for their parents' sins. How can both be true, and how does this affect the answer? The *remez* is that *chazal* were not the only ones troubled by this contradiction; Yirmiyahu raised it as well. The resolution: *lo kashya* — this is not a difficulty. *Ha ke-she-ochazim ma'aseh* — one suffers for a parent's deeds only when one continues in the parent's ways. A person who breaks from a parent's wicked path is not held accountable for that parent's sins. The first answer — that the parental ledger always determines outcomes — therefore overstates the case.

### Complete Incomplete

The Gemara therefore proposes an alternative formulation: *tzaddik gamur v'tov lo, tzaddik she-eino gamur v'ra lo, rasha she-eino gamur v'tov lo, rasha gamur v'ra lo* — a fully righteous person prospers, a partially righteous person suffers; a partially wicked person prospers, a fully wicked person suffers. This abandons the parental explanation entirely and grounds the outcome in the individual's own deeds, while acknowledging that nobody is purely righteous or purely wicked. The suffering or prosperity that seems to contradict one's basic moral character is the clearing of the ledger for the minority of deeds that go the other way. This framework implicitly assumes that the full balance is settled in *Olam Haba*, particularly in cases where the magnitude of a person's suffering or prosperity seems disproportionate to any residual account from their own deeds.

### Undeserved Kindness

The Gemara then notes: *u-pliga d'Rabbi Meir* — this entire discussion is in tension with the view of Rabbi Meir. *Shtayim nitnu lo v'achas lo nitna lo* — of Moshe's three requests, two were granted but the third was not. The proof: God says *v'chanoti et asher achon af al pi she-eino hagun, v'richamti et asher arachem af al pi she-eino hagun* — I will show grace to whom I choose, even if undeserving, and I will show compassion to whom I choose, even if undeserving. The answer is, in effect, that there is no answer: divine mercy is not calibrated by a comprehensible calculus. This addresses the *rasha v'tov lo* — God is sometimes simply kind beyond what is deserved. But it does not explain *tzaddik v'ra lo*, and Rabbi Meir does not claim it does.

The deeper significance of this entire passage lies not in any particular answer but in the question itself. *Chazal* are grappling forthrightly with the fact that the world does not operate as a straightforward reward-and-punishment system, despite what a surface reading of the Torah might suggest. The various answers — parental merit, clearing the ledger, *Olam Haba*, undeserved kindness, and ultimately the divine *gezeirah milfanai* that admits of no rational justification — represent *Chazal's* honest engagement with a question that does not yield a fully satisfying answer. Rabbi Meir's answer is essentially the same posture as what the Gemara elsewhere records God saying to the angels who witnessed Rabbi Akiva's martyrdom: *shtok, kach aleta b'machshavah lefanai* — be silent, this is what has risen in My thought. Sometimes the answer is that we will not know.

## God's Face

### Missed Opportunity

The Gemara now turns to the verse *vayomer lo tuchal lirot panai* — you cannot see My face. A *tana* in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: *kesheratziti lo ratzita, achshav she-atah rotze eini rotze* — when I wanted to reveal Myself to you at the *sneh*, you were not willing; now that you are willing, I am no longer willing. At the burning bush, Moshe hid his face because he feared to look at God. Now, when Moshe earnestly wants to see God's face, the moment has passed. The observation touches something universally recognizable: there are moments of readiness and moments that pass.

### Three Merits

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani citing Rabbi Yonatan offers a divergent reading: *bischar shalosh zachah l'shalosh* — for the merit of three things, Moshe merited three things. Because *vayaster Moshe panav* — Moshe hid his face — he merited *klaster panim*, that his face shone when he emerged from the *Ohel Moed*. Because *ki yarei mig-gashet* — he was afraid to draw near — he merited that *vayiru mi-geshet elav* — Israel was afraid to draw near to him. Because he refrained from staring at God, he merited *uletmunat Hashem yabit* — to behold the form of God. In each case, the precise word used for what Moshe withheld at the *sneh* reappears later in the Torah describing what he ultimately attained.

This reading is in direct tension with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's view. Where Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha frames Moshe's hiding as a missed opportunity — an unwillingness that God later declined to forgive — Rabbi Yonatan reads it as an act of appropriate humility that was itself meritorious, a recognition that he was not yet ready for that degree of intimacy. Rambam, in *Moreh Nevuchim*, employs this principle in the intellectual domain: certain philosophical and theological inquiries should not be undertaken until one has properly prepared oneself. Moshe's restraint at the *sneh* represents the understanding that genuine intimacy with the divine cannot be forced before its time.

### God's Back

*Va-hasiroti et kapi ve-ra'ita et achorai* — I will remove My hand, and you shall see My back. Rav Huna bar Bizna in the name of Rabbi Shimon Chasida: *melamed she-her'ah ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu le-Moshe kesher shel tefillin* — this teaches that God showed Moshe the knot of the *tefillin*. The interpretation ties back to the earlier discussion of God wearing *tefillin*. Seeing God's back means seeing the *tefillin*, not a physical form. The anthropomorphism is thereby moved away from, and the encounter is mediated through something concrete and covenantal that we can relate to. Rambam offers a philosophical parallel: we can never know the essence of God, but we can know God through history, through God's actions, through the wake God leaves in the world. We do not see the face — the essence — but we see the back: everything that God has done, and through that we gain some understanding of what God is.

### Promises Irrevocable

Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yosi: *kol dibur v'dibur she-yatza mipi ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu le-tovah afilu al tnai lo chazar bo* — any word God speaks for good, even if it is conditional, God does not retract. The proof is drawn from Moshe's own case.

### Moses' Descendants

God had said to Moshe: *heref mimeni v'ashmidem v'e'eseh otcha l'goy atzum* — let me be and I will destroy them and make you a great nation. Moshe prayed and the decree against Israel was annulled. Yet the promise to make Moshe a great nation was not thereby cancelled. *Afilu hachi ukmah be-zar'ei* — even so, it was fulfilled through his descendants. In *Divrei HaYamim*, the sons of Rechavyah — descendants of Moshe's son Eliezer — *rabu l'malah l'malah*, increased exceedingly. Rav Yosef taught: *l'malah mi-shishim ribo* — more than six hundred thousand. The verbal parallel is established through *gezeirah shavah*: the word *rabu* here corresponds to *vayirbu* in the account of Israel's proliferation in Egypt, indicating that Moshe's descendants became as numerous as the entire *Bnei Yisrael* who left Egypt. Thus *ve-e'eseh otcha l'goy gadol* was indeed fulfilled, not through the destruction of Israel but through the expansion of Moshe's own lineage. Any word God speaks for good, even one embedded in a conditional that was not fulfilled, finds its realization in another form.
