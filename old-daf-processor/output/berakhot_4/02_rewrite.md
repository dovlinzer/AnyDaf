# Berakhot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dovid's Midnight Practice

### Harp Wakes Dovid

We pick up at the bottom of daf 3b, at the line beginning וְנוֹשֶׁבֶת בּוֹ. The Gemara has been discussing how Dovid HaMelech would rise at midnight to praise God, and it now explains the mechanism: a northern wind would blow through his harp — וּמְנַגֵּן מֵאֵלָיו — and the harp would play by itself at midnight. מִיָּד הָיָה עוֹמֵד וְעוֹסֵק בְּתוֹרָה — he would immediately rise and engage in Torah study. This is interesting because an earlier verse had framed his rising as לְהוֹדוֹת לָךְ, to praise God, yet here it is framed as learning Torah. Perhaps the learning was prior to the praise, but in any case the Gemara presents it as his midnight practice. עַד שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר — he continued until the morning star rose, until the first light approximately an hour before sunrise.

### Sages Seek Parnassah

כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָלָה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, once the morning star appeared, נִכְנְסוּ חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶצְלוֹ — the sages of Israel would come to him. These were his policy representatives, the people with whom he would discuss the affairs of the kingdom. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֲדוֹנֵינוּ הַמֶּלֶךְ, עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל צְרִיכִין פַּרְנָסָה — our master the king, the nation of Israel needs to make a livelihood; things are not going well financially.

אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וְהִתְפַּרְנְסוּ זֶה מִזֶּה — go and sustain yourselves from one another, do business among yourselves. They responded with two images: אֵין הַקֹּמֶץ מַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת הָאַרְיֵה — the tiny handful does not satisfy the lion — and וְאֵין הַבּוֹר מִתְמַלֵּא מֵחֻלְיָתוֹ — nor is a pit filled from its own clods of earth.

Rashi explains the second image: if you already have a pit and you pull out a little clump of earth from within it and throw it back, you will not fill the pit. The metaphor is apt — you are already in the red, and whatever you take from one part of yourselves to fill the need, you are simply robbing from Peter to pay Paul. Tosafot offers related interpretations: Rabbeinu Tam suggests the image of an underground stream that cannot fill a well from below; the Ri makes the same essential point, that digging in one place to fill another merely creates a new hole. The common thrust is clear: our needs are far greater than what internal commerce can supply. Note too the respectful register of their reply — they do not tell the king he is wrong; they answer through metaphor, as befits addressing a monarch.

### Milchemet Reshut

אָמַר לָהֶם: לְכוּ וּפִשְׁטוּ יְדֵיכֶם בַּגְּדוּד — he said to them: go and extend your hands against a military band, go out to war. This is, frankly, a striking response. One might ask whether there is any halachic significance to the suggestion that Jews in a state of poverty simply attack others. For roughly two thousand years following the composition of the Gemara this was a moot question, but it raises serious issues. It is not cited as a normative halachic ruling.

What the Gemara does develop, and what the Rambam later formalizes, is the distinction between מִלְחֶמֶת מִצְוָה and מִלְחֶמֶת רְשׁוּת. Milchemet Mitzvah encompasses the conquest of the land of Israel, the war against the seven nations, the destruction of Amalek, and — as the Rambam makes explicit — wars of self-defense when Israel is attacked. Milchemet Reshut, by contrast, is war undertaken for territorial expansion. The campaign described here — going to war to improve the economy, to seize the wealth of others — falls squarely into the category of Milchemet Reshut. That category itself raises questions about what legitimizes such a war, and indeed the Gemara immediately proceeds to describe the process that must precede it.

### War Protocol

מִיָּד יוֹעֲצִין בַּאֲחִיתֹפֶל — immediately they take counsel with Achitofel. Then וְנִמְלָכִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין — they ask permission of the Sanhedrin. The distinction between יוֹעֲצִין and נִמְלָכִין is significant: the former is consultation for good policy; the latter is asking for permission or at least the imprimatur of that body. Then וְשׁוֹאֲלִין בָּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים — they consult the Urim veTumim, the divine instrument worn by the Kohen Gadol. One of the earliest appearances of the Urim veTumim in Tanach is precisely in the context of going to war — יְהוּדָה יַעֲלֶה — asking who goes up first, whether the campaign is right. And then, after all of this, the orders are handed to the general.

אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף מַאי קְרָא — Rav Yosef asks: what is the verse? וְאַחֲרֵי אֲחִיתֹפֶל בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע וְאֶבְיָתָר וְשַׂר צְבָא לַמֶּלֶךְ יוֹאָב. After Achitofel come Benayahu ben Yehoyada and Evyatar, and then the general of the king, Yoav. The Gemara identifies the players: אֲחִיתֹפֶל זֶה יוֹעֵץ — Achitofel is the advisor, as the verse attests that his counsel was like asking the word of God. Then at the top of daf 4a: בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע זֶה סַנְהֶדְרִין — Benayahu ben Yehoyada represents the Sanhedrin. וְאֶבְיָתָר אֵלּוּ אוּרִים וְתוּמִּים — and Evyatar, who was the Kohen, represents the Urim veTumim. The verse offered is: וּבְנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע עַל הַכְּרֵתִי וְעַל הַפְּלֵתִי. Why are they called Kreiti uPleiti? כְּרֵתִי שֶׁכּוֹרְתִים דִּבְרֵיהֶם — because their words are cut and definitive; פְּלֵתִי שֶׁמֻּפְלָאִים בִּדְבְרֵיהֶם — and their words are astounding. According to Chazal, the Urim veTumim functioned through the letters inscribed on Aharon's breastplate rearranging themselves to provide a divine answer.

### Rashi vs Tosafot

Rashi and Tosafot read the verse differently. Rashi understands that Benayahu ben Yehoyada preceded the Kreiti uPleiti — that is, he came before the Urim veTumim, and the Kreiti uPleiti refers to the Urim veTumim itself. His identification of Benayahu as the head of the Sanhedrin is not derived from the verse but is more or less assumed. Tosafot objects: we have no need for a verse to establish that Evyatar was the Urim veTumim — he was the only Kohen left alive after Shaul's massacre of Nov ir haKohanim; that is obvious. What we do need a verse for is identifying Benayahu ben Yehoyada as head of the Sanhedrin. Therefore Tosafot reads the verse differently: עַל הַכְּרֵתִי וְעַל הַפְּלֵתִי means Benayahu was over the Kreiti uPleiti — he headed them. And the Kreiti uPleiti on this reading refers to the Sanhedrin itself, with the plural language שֶׁכּוֹרְתִים דִּבְרֵיהֶם שֶׁמֻּפְלָאִים בִּדְבְרֵיהֶם describing the body collectively. The Gemara also notes that although it quotes the verse as בְּנָיָהוּ בֶּן יְהוֹיָדָע, in Divrei HaYamim the name actually appears as יְהוֹיָדָע בֶּן בְּנָיָהוּ, and the Gemara equates the two.

### Sanhedrin's Role

One might have expected that asking permission from the Sanhedrin meant seeking their formal approval before undertaking a war of Reshut — the Sanhedrin representing the people, much as they lay their hands on the eglah arufah as the people's representatives. But Rashi, in his comment on וְנִמְלָכִין בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין, says: נוֹטְלִין מֵהֶם רְשׁוּת כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּתְפַּלְלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם — they take permission from them so that the Sanhedrin will pray on their behalf. This is a striking Rashi. That you want the sages and the people praying for you is perfectly understandable, but that this is why you ask permission — that is unusual. You would normally assume that soliciting prayers happens after the decision is made, not as the process of authorization.

A further point sharpens this: the very next Rashi, on וְשׁוֹאֲלִין בָּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים, reads אִם יַצְלִיחוּ — asking whether they will succeed. So even the Urim veTumim is framed less as granting permission and more as seeking reassurance of success. The overall picture Rashi paints is of a king who has already decided, and who is gathering blessings, prayers, and divine assurance — not truly asking whether he may proceed. The formal permission structure may be less about granting authority and more about ensuring divine and communal support for what is already determined.

וְאַחַר כָּךְ שַׂר צְבָא הַמֶּלֶךְ — and only then are the orders given to Yoav, the general.

### Dovid Wakes Morning

אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַדָּא — Rav Yitzchak bar Adda asks: what is the verse showing that Dovid would rise so early — not merely at midnight to learn, but already involved in the affairs of kingship before sunrise? עוּרָה כְבוֹדִי עוּרָה הַנֶּבֶל וְכִנּוֹר אָעִירָה שָׁחַר — Bestir my honor, bestir the nevel and the kinnor; I will wake the morning. The culminating phrase is אָעִירָה הַשַּׁחַר — I will wake the morning. Rashi puts it beautifully: שְׁאָר מְלָכִים הַשַּׁחַר מְעוֹרְרָן — other kings need the morning to wake them, וַאֲנִי מְעוֹרֵר הַשַּׁחַר — I am up so early that I wake the morning.

## Kechatzos — Why Approx

### Harp as Alarm Clock

The Gemara now returns to the question that motivated this entire digression: if Dovid knew exactly when midnight was, how did Moshe not know, given that Moshe said כַּחֲצוֹת — approximately midnight? רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: מֹשֶׁה לְעוֹלָם הֲוָה יָדַע — actually, Moshe did know exactly when midnight was. וְדָוִד נַמִי הֲוָה יָדַע — and so did Dovid. These figures were so wise they could determine the precise moment of midnight without any instrument. וְכֵיוָן דְּדָוִד הֲוָה יָדַע כִּנּוֹר לָמָּה לִי — if Dovid knew, then what was the harp for? לְאַתְּרוּיֵי מִשִּׁינְתֵיהּ — to wake him from sleep. It was an alarm clock. He did not need the harp to tell him the time; he needed it to rouse him.

### Astrologers & Moshe

וְכֵיוָן דְּמֹשֶׁה הֲוָה יָדַע, לָמָּה לֵי לְמֵימַר כַּחֲצוֹת — if Moshe knew exactly when midnight was, why did he say approximately? מֹשֶׁה קָסָבַר שֶׁמָּא יִטְעוּ אִצְטַגְנִינֵי פַּרְעֹה — Moshe was concerned that Pharaoh's astrologers might make an error. This is why astrologers are specifically mentioned: in antiquity, time at night was reckoned by the stars, which is precisely what astrologers tracked. So perhaps they would misjudge the moment by a little, וְיֹאמְרוּ מֹשֶׁה בַּדַּאי הוּא — and they would say: Moshe is a liar. He said midnight and it did not come at exactly midnight.

### Eini Yodeia Maxim

דְּאָמַר מָר: לַמֵּד לְשׁוֹנְךָ לוֹמַר אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ — there is a teaching from Masechet Derech Eretz: train your tongue to say "I do not know," שֶׁמָּא תִּתְבַּדֶּה וְתֵאָחֵז — lest you fabricate and be caught in the error. The teaching addresses the human tendency to answer even when uncertain; you will be found out and your words will lose credibility.

What Moshe is doing goes even one step further. He actually knew the answer. But he was so concerned that people might perceive him as having been mistaken that he chose deliberate imprecision over correct precision. The logical inference is a kal vachomer: if someone who knows the answer should sometimes speak with reservation to avoid the appearance of error, how much more so should one who genuinely does not know resist the temptation to fake confidence.

### Rashi & Oral Torah

Rashi in his commentary on the Chumash chose this explanation — that Moshe said כַּחֲצוֹת so the astrologers would not call him a liar — and not the earlier answer of the Gemara that Moshe simply did not know. This is worth pausing over. Rashi so thoroughly integrates the world of Torah sheba'al peh into the biblical narrative that readers of his Chumash commentary absorb particular Talmudic readings as if they were the plain meaning of the text. His commentary positions the learner seamlessly within the rabbinic universe. At the same time, one must remember that even when Rashi adopts a particular strand, the Gemara itself preserves a much wider range of interpretation, and what Rashi selects is one choice among many.

### Rav Ashi: Kaf Dimyon

רַב אַשִׁי אָמַר: בְּפַלְגָא אוֹרְחָא דִּתְלֵיסַר נָגֵי אַרְבַּע עֶשְׂרֵה הֲוֵי קָאֵי — Rav Ashi offers yet another explanation. Here the כ is not a כ of approximation at all, but a כ of comparison. When Moshe spoke at midnight of the 13th, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ מֹשֶׁה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — this is what he said: אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, לְמָחָר כַּחֲצוֹת הַלַּיְלָה כִּי הָאִידָּנָא — tomorrow, like midnight right now, at exactly this moment, אֲנִי יוֹצֵא בְּתוֹךְ מִצְרַיִם. The כ there does not mean "approximately"; it means "like this very moment." Midnight of the 14th will be at precisely the same point of night as midnight of the 13th, when Moshe is speaking.

## Dovid as Posek Halacha

### Chasid Ani Debate

The Gemara turns now to a verse from Tehillim: שָׁמְרָה נַפְשִׁי כִּי חָסִיד אָנִי — protect my soul, for I am a chasid. לֵוִי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק disagree about what Dovid is saying here before God.

The first opinion: חַד אָמַר — one says that Dovid is telling God, לֹא חָסִיד אָנִי — am I not righteous? שֶׁכָּל מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב יְשֵׁנִים עַד שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת — all the kings of East and West sleep until three hours into the day, וַאֲנִי חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה אָקוּם לְהוֹדוֹת לָךְ — while I rise at midnight to praise You. He is pointing to his extraordinary dedication to divine service.

The second opinion interprets the claim differently. Dovid is not comparing when he wakes up, but what he does during his active hours. שֶׁכָּל מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב יוֹשְׁבִים אֲגוּדּוֹת אֲגוּדּוֹת בִּכְבוֹדָם — all other kings sit surrounded by their retinues in honor and glory, embodying their power and majesty. וַאֲנִי — what do I do?

### Dovid & Niddah Psak

יָדַי מְלֻכְלָכוֹת בְּדָם וּבְשָׁפִיר וּבְשִׁלְיָא — my hands are dirty with blood, embryo, and placenta, כְּדֵי לְטַהֵר אִשָּׁה לְבַעְלָהּ — in order to declare a woman pure for her husband. Rashi reminds us of much that was learned in Masechet Niddah: not all shades of blood render a woman a niddah, and a woman who miscarries may or may not be considered a yoledet depending on the state of the fetus and placenta, with corresponding implications for her tum'ah status. Dovid is saying that he dirties his hands with precisely this work — because his role is not merely that of king or poet, but of someone who cares for every individual and is immersed in the daily particulars of halacha.

### Dovid Talmid Chacham

This is a striking image, and one that tends to stay with anyone who has learned the Gemara. Here is Dovid HaMelech — not on his throne, not surrounded by his guard, but examining a placenta and inspecting blood. The contrast with the other kings is stark and very deliberately physical. But what is also fascinating is the picture the rabbis want to paint of Dovid: not merely the composer of Tehillim, not merely the king who follows God, but a talmid chacham, a posek halacha, someone to whom people bring she'alos and who issues rulings. Indeed there is something poignant about people bringing their most personal halachic questions to the king — this imagines a Dovid who participates in the intimate texture of every Jewish household.

One cannot help but note the resonance of "blood on his hands" with the verse in which Dovid is told he cannot build the Beis HaMikdash because his hands are full of blood. The Gemara here reframes that image: yes, his hands are full of blood — the blood of niddah examination, brought in service of taharat hamishpacha. And earlier in this very passage Dovid told the sages to go to war, which also leaves blood on his hands. The rabbis seem to be consciously taking the constellation of imagery around Dovid and blood and redirecting it toward an act of chesed and halachic service.

### Mefiboshet Consult

וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא, כָּל מַה שֶּׁאֲנִי עוֹשֶׂה, אֲנִי נִמְלָךְ בִּמְפִיבֹשֶׁת — not only that, but whatever I do in the halachic realm, I first consult Mefiboshet. The Gemara derives this from a wordplay on the name: מְפִיבֹשֶׁת רַבִּי — I say to him: "Mefiboshet my master" — יָפֶה דַנְתִּי? יָפֶה חִיַּבְתִּי? יָפֶה זִכִּיתִי? יָפֶה טִהַרְתִּי? יָפֶה טִמֵּאתִי? — Did I judge correctly? Was I right to obligate? To exempt? To declare pure? To declare impure? And ולֹא בֹשְׁתִּי — I am not embarrassed.

Notably, Dovid does not ask Mefiboshet before ruling but after. When someone comes to the king with a shaylah, the king must embody his role and give an answer; he cannot defer to someone else in the moment. But afterwards he checks his ruling, and if he was wrong, he can correct it and learn for the future.

Rashi explains the name itself: מְפִיבֹשֶׁת — בִּדְבְרֵי הֲלָכָה הַיּוֹצֵא מִפִּי הָיָה בּוֹשֵׁשׁ אוֹתִי — from the words that came out of his mouth there would be embarrassment for Dovid, שֶׁפְּעָמִים שֶׁהָיָה טוֹעֶה, וְאוֹמֵר לוֹ טָעִיתָ — because sometimes Dovid would be wrong and Mefiboshet would tell him so. The balance Rashi is striking: I was not so embarrassed as to refrain from asking, even though when he told me I had erred it was indeed embarrassing. The willingness to be exposed was the mark of Dovid's greatness.

### Neged Melachim Verse

אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מַאי קְרָא — what is the verse that supports this? וַאֲדַבְּרָה בְעֵדֹתֶיךָ נֶגֶד מְלָכִים וְלֹא אֵבוֹשׁ — I will speak of Your testimonies, Your laws, in the presence of kings and I will not be ashamed. This verse from the same chapter of Tehillim — the chapter containing חֲצוֹת לַיְלָה אָקוּם — speaks to two things simultaneously: that Dovid is not embarrassed to dirty his hands with the practical details of halacha rather than sit in royal splendor, and that he is not embarrassed to consult Mefiboshet and be corrected.

### Kilav vs. Mefiboshet

תָּנָא: לֹא מְפִיבֹשֶׁת שְׁמוֹ אֶלָּא אִישׁ בֹּשֶׁת שְׁמוֹ — his real name was not Mefiboshet but Ishboshet. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ מְפִיבֹשֶׁת — why was he called Mefiboshet? שֶׁהָיָה מְבַיֵּשׁ פְּנֵי דָוִד בַּהֲלָכָה — because he would embarrass Dovid in halacha. לְפִיכָךְ זָכָה דָוִד יָצָא מִמֶּנּוּ כִּלְאָב — and because Dovid was willing to subject himself to this, he merited that his son Kilav came forth from him.

וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא כִּלְאָב שְׁמוֹ אֶלָּא דָּנִיֵּאל שְׁמוֹ — Rabbi Yochanan says his real name was Daniel, as listed in Divrei HaYamim, and the Gemara reconciles the two names as referring to the same person. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ כִּלְאָב — why was he called Kilav? שֶׁהָיָה מַכְלִים פְּנֵי מְפִיבֹשֶׁת בַּהֲלָכָה — because he showed up Mefiboshet in halacha. The name encodes the story: כְּ-אָב — he shamed the "father," the halachic master, Mefiboshet. And the verse with which Shlomo praises this: בְּנִי אִם חָכַם לִבֶּךָ יִשְׂמַח לִבִּי גַּם אָנִי — my son, if your heart is wise, my heart too will rejoice — and further: חָכַם בְּנִי וְשַׂמַּח לִבִּי וְאָשִׁיבָה חוֹרְפִי דָבָר — my son has been wise, my heart has rejoiced, and I will respond to those who disgrace me.

That word חוֹרְפִי — those who disgrace me — is telling. The Gemara frames the dynamic between Dovid and Mefiboshet not merely as student and teacher but as something sharper: Mefiboshet appears to have delivered his corrections in a way that was genuinely humiliating, not simply constructive. That Dovid submitted to this and continued asking is all the more remarkable. And the sense of vindication when his son bested Mefiboshet suggests that the treatment was not entirely forgotten, even as Dovid's willingness to endure it is itself held up as the very reason he merited such a son.

### Dot Over Lulei

וְדָוִד מִי קָרֵי לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ חָסִיד — but does Dovid call himself a chasid? The verse in Tehillim seems to contradict this: לוּלֵא הֶאֱמַנְתִּי לִרְאוֹת בְּטוּב ה' בְּאֶרֶץ חַיִּים — had I not believed I would see the goodness of God in the land of the living. The Gemara understands אֶרֶץ חַיִּים as referring to Olam Haba. And there is a tradition in the name of Rabbi Yosi: לָמָּה נָקוּד עַל לוּלֵא — why is there a dot over the word לוּלֵא? The dot signals qualification: yes, he said "had I not believed" — but perhaps sometimes his belief did falter. As the Yiddish expression captures: וַיִּשָּׁקֵהוּ מִיט פִּינְטְלֶעךְ — with dots, meaning with reservation. So the verse with the dot reads: had I not believed — with dots.

### Shema Yigrom HaChet

Why did his belief falter? מוּבְטָח אֲנִי בְּךָ שֶׁאַתָּה מְשַׁלֵּם שָׂכָר טוֹב לַצַּדִּיקִים לֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא — I have no doubt, God, that You reward the righteous in the world to come. וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם יֶשׁ לִי חֵלֶק בֵּינֵיהֶם אִם לָאו — but I do not know whether I will merit a share among them. Why not? שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא — perhaps sin will cause it to be lost.

This resolves the apparent contradiction. When Dovid says כִּי חָסִיד אָנִי, he is not being arrogant; he knows what he has achieved, and that knowledge is not immodest but accurate. Yet knowing what one has accomplished so far does not guarantee the future. The principle לֹא תַאֲמִין בְּעַצְמְךָ עַד יוֹם מוֹתְךָ — do not trust yourself until the day of your death — captures precisely this: you cannot be certain that even great accumulated merit will not be undone by future failure. Dovid holds both things simultaneously: a clear-eyed recognition of his own righteousness, and a persistent awareness of his own frailty. That combination is not contradiction but spiritual maturity.

### Yaakov's Fear

רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי shows a parallel contradiction: the verse states וְהִנֵּה אָנֹכִי עִמְּךָ וּשְׁמַרְתִּיךָ בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר תֵּלֵךְ — God promised Yaakov He would be with him wherever he went. Yet when Yaakov returned, וַיִּירָא יַעֲקֹב מְאֹד — Yaakov was very afraid. How could he be afraid after an explicit divine promise?

The answer is the same: שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא. For Yaakov the formulation shifts slightly — שֶׁמָּא גָּרַם הַחֵטְא — perhaps sin has already caused the promise to be forfeited, whereas for Dovid the concern was about future sin. Yaakov feared that in the interval since the promise was given he might have sinned in ways that caused him to no longer deserve its fulfillment. The Gemara does not identify any specific sin; the concern is more of a generalized vigilance about the distance between divine promise and one's continuing worthiness of it. This is expressed in the verse אַשְׁרֵי אָדָם מְפַחֵד תָּמִיד — praised is the person who is always afraid — which the Gemara understands as referring to a constant awareness of the aveiros one may have committed or may yet commit.

### Second Entry & Sin

כְּדְתַנְיָא: עַד יַעֲבֹר עַמְּךָ ה' עַד יַעֲבֹר עַם זוּ קָנִיתָ — the verse at the Yam Suf reads: until Your people pass over, God, this nation You have acquired. עַד יַעֲבֹר עַמְּךָ ה' זוּ בִּיאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה — the first clause refers to the first entry into the land of Israel under Yehoshua. עַד יַעֲבֹר עַם זוּ קָנִיתָ זוּ בִּיאָה שְׁנִיָּה — the second clause refers to the second entry at the time of Ezra. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: רְאוּיִים הָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהֵיעָשׂוֹת לָהֶם נֵס בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה לָהֶם בִּימֵי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן — they deserved that the second return should be like the first: a full sovereign return in which they conquered the land and stood independent. But they returned under Persian permission, non-sovereign, a subject people even as they built the Beis HaMikdash. אֶלָּא שֶׁגָּרַם הַחֵטְא — but sin caused it to fall short.

This brings the abstract concern of שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא into concrete historical relief. Yes, things are anticipated, promised, or anticipated by trajectory — and sometimes sin intervenes and the fulfillment is real but diminished.

### Rambam Nevuah Issue

This raises a significant question about the Rambam's treatment of nevuah. The Rambam states that a prophecy of good cannot be retracted — it is only a prophecy of doom that can be undone by teshuvah, as in the case of Yonah and Nineveh (עוֹד אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם וְנִינְוֵה נֶהְפָּכֶת). A negative prophecy that does not come to pass does not impugn the prophet, because the people may have done teshuvah and God had mercy. But if a prophet promises something good and it does not come about, that demonstrates he is a false prophet — because God never withdraws a promise of good.

How then does שֶׁמָּא יִגְרוֹם הַחֵטְא work? The case of Yaakov received an explicit divine promise, and the case of the second entry involves what the Gemara treats as an anticipated divine blessing. Yet in both cases the full realization was diminished. There are various ways one could distinguish these cases — for instance, whether the promise was formally proclaimed as nevuah before the people — but the tension with the Rambam's principle is real and the Gemara's discussion here raises it squarely.

## Chatzos as Rabbinic Fence

### Chatzos Fits Neither

וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים עַד חֲצוֹת — and the Chachamim say until midnight. The Gemara asks: what position are they holding? אִי כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סְבִירָא לְהוּ — if they hold like Rabbi Eliezer, who limits the time for evening Shema to the first few hours of the night (since that is when people typically go to sleep), then they should say a few hours into the night. ואִי כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לְהוּ — and if they hold like Rabban Gamliel, who says all night — because as Rashi explains, Rabban Gamliel understands that the mitzvah of Shema is tied to night itself rather than to the act of going to sleep, so the whole night qualifies — then they should say all night. Midnight fits neither position.

## Chatzos as Rabbinic Fe…

### Fence Against Aveirah

וּלְעוֹלָם כְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סְבִירָא לְהוּ — in principle they hold like Rabban Gamliel that the time extends all night. וְהָא דְּקָאָמְרִי עַד חֲצוֹת, כְּדֵי לְהַרְחִיק אָדָם מִן הָעֲבֵירָה — the reason they said until midnight is to distance a person from transgression, as we learned in the baraita: חֲכָמִים עָשׂוּ סְיָג לְדִבְרֵיהֶם — the rabbis made a fence around their words.

### Worker & Sleep Trap

The baraita describes the danger: כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא אָדָם בָּא מִן הַשָּׂדֶה בָּעֶרֶב — a man should not come home from the field in the evening, וְיֹאמַר: אֵלֵךְ לְבֵיתִי — and say let me go home first, וְאֹכַל קִמְעָה, וְאֶשְׁתֶּה קִמְעָה, וְאִישַׁן קִמְעָה — I'll have a little to eat, a little to drink, a little nap, וְאַחַר כָּךְ אֶקְרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאֶתְפַּלֵּל — and then I'll daven. וְתַחְטְפֶנּוּ שֵׁינָה — and sleep will seize him, וְנִמְצָא יָשֵׁן כָּל הַלַּיְלָה — and he will sleep the whole night.

Instead: נִכְנַס לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת — before going home, go to shul. What is fascinating is the Gemara inserts: אִם רָגִיל לִקְרוֹת קוֹרֵא, אִם רָגִיל לִשְׁנוֹת שׁוֹנֶה — if you are accustomed to read, read; if accustomed to learn mishnah, learn. First do a little learning to make the transition from a hard day's work, to get yourself into the right mental state for davening. Then וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִתְפַּלֵּל — say Shema and daven. Then go home, eat your bread, and make a berachah. That is the proper sequence.

### Chayav Misah — Why?

וְכָל הָעוֹבֵר עַל דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים חַיָּב מִיתָה — anyone who transgresses this rabbinic ruling is liable to death. The Gemara asks: מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי חַיָּב מִיתָה — why here specifically does the language of חַיָּב מִיתָה appear, when elsewhere a violation of rabbinic enactment does not typically carry such language?

Two answers are given. The first: אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא אוֹנֶס שֵׁינָה — because here the compelling force of sleep makes the failure so likely that we need to impress upon you with maximum urgency that you must not put yourself in that position. The second: אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא לְאַפּוּקֵי מִמַּאן דְּאָמַר תְּפִלַּת עַרְבִית רְשׁוּת — to exclude the opinion that Maariv, the evening prayer, is only optional. There is a debate about whether the evening Shemoneh Esrei is truly obligatory. By using the language of חַיָּב מִיתָה, the baraita signals that both the Shema and the Shemoneh Esrei of Maariv are genuine obligations, not to be taken lightly.

One might note that even setting midnight as a deadline may not by itself create urgency in a world without electric lights, where nobody is routinely awake until midnight in any case. The real concern is not that people will casually push things off until after midnight; it is that they will intend to daven and then fall asleep. The fence of midnight and the language of חַיָּב מִיתָה are both aimed at that specific failure mode.

## Geulah Samuch Tefillah

### Order Supports Yochanan

אָמַר מָר: קוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִתְפַּלֵּל — the baraita stated: you say Shema and then you daven. מְסַיֵּעַ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן — this supports Rabbi Yochanan, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵיזֶהוּ בֶּן עוֹלָם הַבָּא — who is a person who merits Olam Haba? זֶה הַסּוֹמֵךְ גְּאֻלָּה לִתְפִלָּה שֶׁל עַרְבִית — one who juxtaposes the geulah to the tefillah in the evening. You say Shema, culminating in גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל, and immediately go into Shemoneh Esrei, just as in the morning.

### Tefillot B'Emtza

אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: תְּפִלּוֹת בָּאֶמְצַע תִּקְּנוּם — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi holds that the Shemoneh Esrei prayers were instituted in the middle, meaning the structure is: Shema in the morning, then Shemoneh Esrei; in the evening, Shemoneh Esrei first, and then Shema. The Shema of each service is flanked on the outside, making morning Shema the first thing after waking and evening Shema the last thing before sleeping.

### Geulah at Night?

בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי — what is the underlying dispute? The Gemara offers two possible frameworks. The first is a matter of סְבָרָא, independent reasoning about the nature of the Exodus. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: גְּאֻלָּה מֵאוֹרְתָּא נַמִּי הֲוִיָא — there was a genuine beginning of geulah at night. It was with מַכַּת בְּכוֹרוֹת, the plague of the firstborn at midnight, that the process of the Exodus began. אֶלָּא גְּאֻלָּה מְעַלְּיָיתָא לָא הֲוִיָא אֶלָּא צַפְרָא — the full, consummate geulah did not occur until the morning at the Yam Suf, but the beginning was genuinely at night. Therefore when we say גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל in the evening Shema, it is a real mention of geulah, and it follows that we juxtapose it to Shemoneh Esrei just as in the morning.

רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הֲוִיָא אֶלָּא צַפְרָא, לָאו גְּאֻלָּה מְעַלְּיָיתָא הִיא — since the true, complete geulah happened in the morning, what occurred at night was not a full-fledged geulah. Therefore although we do say the third paragraph of Shema and do say גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל at night, it does not carry the same weight, and we need not juxtapose it to Shemoneh Esrei. This debate is closely tied to the broader question addressed in a later mishnah in Berachot: is there a mitzvah to remember Yetziat Mitzrayim at night? And does the requirement to recite the third paragraph of Shema, which speaks of redemption, apply equally at night?

### Mekish Shechivah Kimah

The second framework derives the dispute from a verse: דִּשְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ — both Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi derive their positions from the same verse: בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ — when you lie down and when you rise. The verse connects lying down and rising up, and the question is the direction of the connection.

רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: מַקִּישׁ שְׁכִיבָה לְקִימָה — lying down is compared to rising. מַה קִּימָה קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאַחַר כָּךְ תְּפִלָּה — just as when rising you say Shema and then daven, אַף שְׁכִיבָה נַמִּי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְאַחַר כָּךְ תְּפִלָּה — so too when lying down you say Shema and then daven. The structural parallel runs in the same direction each time.

רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי סָבַר: מַקִּישׁ שְׁכִיבָה לְקִימָה in a different sense — מַה קִּימָה קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע סָמוּךְ לְמִיטָּתוֹ — just as rising, Shema is said right next to the bed, the first thing after getting up, אַף שְׁכִיבָה נַמִּי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע סָמוּךְ לְמִיטָּתוֹ — so too lying down, Shema should be said right at the bedside, the very last act before sleep. On this reading, the parallelism of בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ is actually more naturally fulfilled by placing Shema at the temporal edges of sleep: the last thing at night, the first thing in the morning. This makes Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's position in some ways a more elegant reading of the verse, even though the practical result is that Shemoneh Esrei and Shema are not juxtaposed in the evening.

The liturgical reality of Maariv reflects the tension between these two positions: the normative structure follows Rabbi Yochanan, with Shema and its berachot preceding Shemoneh Esrei. Yet the halacha acknowledges Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's position in a practical context: if one arrives late to Maariv and the tzibbur is about to daven Shemoneh Esrei, one davens Shemoneh Esrei with the tzibbur and then says Shema with its berachot afterwards — an accommodation that would not be permissible in the morning, where the order of Shema then Shemoneh Esrei is not compromised.

### Hashkiveinu Objection

מֵיתִיב מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא — Mar Bereih deRavina raises an objection. We say two berachot before the evening Shema and two after it. The second berachah after Shema is הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ, Hashkiveinu, which intervenes between גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל and Shemoneh Esrei. אִי אָמְרַת... בָּעֵי לִסְמוֹךְ — if you hold there is a concept of semikat geulah litfillah in the evening, the Hashkiveinu berachah breaks the juxtaposition. הָא לָא קַסְמֵיךְ גְּאֻלָּה לִתְפִלָּה — so you are not actually joining them.

The answer: כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנָן הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ, כְּגְּאֻלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא — since the rabbis instituted Hashkiveinu as a fixed requirement, it is regarded as an extension of the geulah itself, a "long geulah." This answer is, as one must note, formally striking. The content of גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל and Hashkiveinu are quite different in theme: the former is about the historical redemption from Egypt, the latter about protection through the night. Yet the Gemara holds that once the rabbis instituted Hashkiveinu as part of the evening liturgy, it becomes absorbed into the liturgical unit of geulah and does not constitute an interruption.

### Sefasai Tiftach Parallel

The Gemara draws an instructive parallel for the morning. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּתְּחִלָּה אוֹמֵר ה' שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח — Rabbi Yochanan himself ruled that before beginning Shemoneh Esrei one says ה' שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח, and at the conclusion one says יִהְיוּ לְרָצוֹן אִמְרֵי פִי. The opening phrase ה' שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח intervenes between גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל and the body of Shemoneh Esrei — yet we do not regard it as breaking the semikhah. אֶלָּא הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנָן לְמֵימַר ה' שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח, כְּתְפִלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא — since the rabbis ordained that this is how Shemoneh Esrei opens, it becomes part of Shemoneh Esrei itself. Indeed, the universally accepted practice is that Shemoneh Esrei begins with ה' שְׂפָתַי תִּפְתַּח. הָכָא נַמִּי כֵּיוָן דְּתַקִּינוּ רַבָּנָן לְמֵימַר הַשְׁכִּיבֵנוּ, כִּגְּאֻלָּה אֲרִיכְתָּא דָּמְיָא — and so too Hashkiveinu, having been instituted by the rabbis, becomes part of the geulah literature and not an interruption of the semikhah.

What is philosophically interesting here is the way the rabbis' institutional act transforms the status of a berachah. The content does not change. But the fact that the rabbis required it as part of the liturgy reshapes its halachic and conceptual category. This is a broader principle that will recur as we continue through the masechet: when the Sages fix a text or practice as part of the liturgical sequence, they do not merely add it; they redefine the units of which the liturgy is composed.

### Shema as Parenthesis

The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and the interplay of Shema and Shemoneh Esrei throughout Maariv, illuminate a persistent tension in the masechet between two ways of understanding what we are doing. From one vantage point, Shema is a discrete mitzvah — an act of declaration tied to specific times, morning and evening, with its own berachot and its own requirements of intention and sequence. From another vantage point, Shema is part of an integrated liturgy that shapes the rhythm of the entire day, folding together confession, praise, memory, petition, and love into a structure that brackets human experience.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's position captures something profound about Shema as בְּשָׁכְבְּךָ וּבְקוּמֶךָ: the first thing at waking, the last at sleeping. On this understanding, Shema is a parenthesis around our lives — a framing of each day within the love and covenant that define who we are. The most important thing, the declaration that opens and closes each day, is the relationship itself: the love for God proclaimed in the first paragraph, the commitment to carry that love into every moment of lying down and rising up. The question of whether that declaration is immediately followed by Shemoneh Esrei or not is, in this sense, secondary to what the declaration itself is doing: situating every day, from its first moment to its last, within the foundational reality of אֲהַבְתָּ אֵת ה' אֱלֹקֶיךָ.

The tension between these two understandings — Shema as mitzvah and Shema as the frame of life — is one that the masechet will continue to explore.
