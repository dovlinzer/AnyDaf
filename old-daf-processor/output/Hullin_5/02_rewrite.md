# Chullin 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mumar & Shechita

### Mumar Categories

We begin at the very last line of 5a. The Gemara arrives at תיובתא דרבנן תיובתא — a decisive refutation of the Rabbinic position — in light of a Baraita establishing that a mumar can serve as a shochet. Two explanations had been offered for this Baraita. The first is that the mumar in question is a mumar ochel neveilos leteavon — someone who violates kashrus out of appetite rather than ideological defiance. The second explanation would have accommodated even a genuine mumar, including an idolater. That second explanation was ultimately rejected by the Gemara on the basis of another Baraita that places the public violator of Shabbat in the same categorical exclusion as an idolater — both are treated as fundamentally invalidated in their personal status. We are therefore left with the first explanation: a person who violates in a single area, even if that area is kashrus itself, is not categorically excluded and may, under the right circumstances, be trusted to perform shechita. This is the position that governs halacha.

### Source for Mumar Exclusi…

The Gemara now raises a pointed question: do we actually derive the exclusion of a mumar from bringing sacrifices from the verse about מכם — "from you," excluding those who have departed from the community — or do we derive it from a different source entirely? In fact, the Gemara notes, there is another pasuk: מעם הארץ, "from the people of the land." The letter mem there is read as partitive — not all members of the people of the land qualify — and this serves as the basis for excluding a mumar, a habitual violator and rebel, from bringing a sin offering.

### Willful Violator Doctrine

Rabbi Shimon Yossi then teaches in the name of Rabbi Shimon a distinct principle derived from the verse, "that which should not be done was done in error, and he is guilty." From this verse, Rabbi Shimon derives that only someone who, had he known the facts, would have refrained from the act — הַשָּׁב מִידִּיעָתוֹ, one who returns upon gaining knowledge — brings a korban for his unintentional sin. But someone who would have sinned anyway even with full awareness — אֵינוֹ שָׁב מִידִּיעָתוֹ — does not bring a korban. The logic is stark: if a person habitually eats treifa and on a given occasion eats meat he believed to be kosher that turned out to be treifa, he brings no korban, because even had he known it was treifa, he would have eaten it regardless.

### Rav Hamnuna's Case

The Gemara then asks: what is the practical difference between the two exclusions — the mumar excluded by virtue of his category, and the person excluded because he would have sinned willfully anyway? Rav Hamnuna answers: the difference emerges in the case of a mumar le'echol cheilev — a habitual eater of forbidden fat — who now accidentally consumes blood. As Tosafot notes, cheilev and dam occupy closely related territory: both are prohibited by the Torah in parallel fashion, both are associated with the korbanot, and both carry the penalty of karet. They belong to the same cluster of behaviors, even though the specific violation differs.

The Gemara does not spell out the analysis explicitly, but the distinction between the two positions is as follows. According to Rabbi Shimon's principle — that the issue is whether this person would have done it anyway — a habitual eater of cheilev can still bring a korban for accidentally eating blood, since for blood specifically he would not have sinned willfully. He eats fat out of habit; blood he avoids. Since he would have refrained had he known, the inadvertent transgression is a genuine shgaga, and he brings a korban. But according to the mumar category — the position that a person's status as a habitual violator in a related domain disqualifies him entirely from bringing a sin offering in that cluster of behaviors — even this person does not bring a korban. Since he is a habitual violator in the sphere of cheilev, and dam belongs to the same halachic family, he is excluded from the sin-offering framework for that entire cluster.

Tosafot is careful to point out that we are not speaking here of the categorical disqualification reserved for idolaters and Shabbat violators. A habitual violator of kashrus does not, as a general matter, lose his personal status in the way an idolater does. What he does lose is his standing to bring a sin offering within the halachic sphere in which he is a willful violator. The two frameworks — categorical disqualification and domain-specific exclusion — operate differently, even if both result in the mumar being barred from bringing a korban.

### Two Exclusion Grounds

Having established these two bases for excluding a mumar, the Gemara asks: why do I need both derivations? Why does the Torah need to teach me the mumar exclusion twice — once from מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ and once from the verse about the willful violator? The Gemara answers: חַד בְּחַטָּאת וְחַד בְּעוֹלָה וּצְרִיכָא — one exclusion applies to a chatat, the sin offering, and the other to an olah, the elevation offering, and both are necessary.

Had the Torah only excluded the mumar from a chatat, one might have argued: a chatat is about atonement, and a willful violator simply does not deserve atonement — that is why he is excluded. But an olah is a gift to God; perhaps even a sinner should be permitted to bring a gift, and the exclusion would not apply. Conversely, had the Torah only excluded the mumar from an olah, one might have argued: an olah is a voluntary offering, and since the mumar has no obligation to bring it, we say he is not entitled to. But a chatat is obligatory — the mumar has sinned, even if inadvertently, and incurred an obligation; perhaps we should accept it, and, as Rashi adds, we should not allow the mumar to benefit from his own sinfulness by being released from an obligation. Therefore both exclusions are necessary, each teaching something the other could not.

Returning to the issue at hand: the category of person who is categorically excluded in the general halachic sense — from shechita and other areas — is the idolater and the public Shabbat violator. A person who sins in a particular area, even in kashrus, is not globally invalidated. Given appropriate circumstances, even such a person may be trusted to perform shechita.

## Behemah Terminology

### Behemah & Insult

The Gemara now pauses to examine a lexical question that arises from the Baraita cited earlier. That Baraita had interpreted the word בְּהֵמָה in a verse about those who may bring korbanot as a reference to sinners — people who are as bad as animals. The Gemara asks: does the word behemah always carry this pejorative meaning?

### Humble Behemah

A counter-example is immediately raised: אָדָם וּבְהֵמָה תוֹשִׁיעַ ה' — "man and beast the Lord will save" — and here the term behemah is clearly used in a positive context, since the verse promises salvation. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav explains: this verse refers to people who are clever in their intellect, ערומים בדעת, and yet humble themselves like an animal — מַשִּׂימִים עַצְמָן כִּבְהֵמָה — unpretentious and non-arrogant. In this verse, then, behemah carries an admirable connotation.

### Context of Reference

How do we reconcile the two uses? The Gemara distinguishes on the basis of context: הָתָם כְּתִיב אָדָם וּבְהֵמָה, הָכָא בְּהֵמָה לְחוּדֵיהּ כְּתִיב — there, the verse writes "man and beast" together, and since the two are linked, behemah is interpreted in a positive light, as a type of person who shares the animal's quality of humility while retaining human intelligence. Where behemah appears alone, unlinked from adam, it is interpreted negatively, as referring to someone who has degraded himself to an animal-like level through sin.

### Planting Verse

The Gemara, however, will not let the principle stand unchallenged. It raises another verse: וְזָרַעְתִּי אֶת בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל זֶרַע אָדָם וְזֶרַע בְּהֵמָה — "I will plant the house of Israel, the planting of man and the planting of beast." Here adam and behemah appear together, yet the Gemara, drawing on its interpretation in tractate Sotah, reads זֶרַע בְּהֵמָה as a reference to those who are not learned in Torah — an unflattering characterization. Does this not refute the principle that the paired usage is always positive?

The Gemara resolves the difficulty by a close reading: חַלְּקֵי קְרָא — the verse separates the two. It does not say זֶרַע אָדָם וּבְהֵמָה in a single conjoined phrase, as the earlier verse did; rather, it treats them as distinct categories — the planting of man, and separately, the planting of beast. Because they are not syntactically linked but placed in parallel as separate items, the word behemah in this verse stands alone in meaning, and is thus interpreted negatively. The principle is thereby preserved: genuine linkage of adam and behemah yields a positive interpretation; separation — even in apparent proximity — permits the negative reading.

## Kuti Shechita

### Rabban Gamliel's Ban

The Gemara returns to the central thread of shechita and the question of who qualifies as a valid shochet. The mumar has been addressed; now the Kuti — the Samaritan — is revisited. Rabbi Chanan said in the name of Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi, who said in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi in the name of Bar Kappara: רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וּבֵית דִּינוֹ נִמְנוּ עַל שְׁחִיטַת כּוּתִי וְאֲסָרוּהָ — Rabban Gamliel and his beit din took a formal vote and reassessed the status of the Kuti, and they forbade it. The earlier framework, which had permitted reliance on Kuti shechita under certain conditions, was now overturned by a formal rabbinic ruling.

### Observation Question

Rabbi Zeira addressed Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi directly with a pointed question. He asked: שֶׁמָּא לֹא שָׁמַע רַבִּי אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁאֵין יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבּוֹ — perhaps the ruling you are reporting applies only when there is no Yisrael standing over the shochet, i.e., no direct observation? The earlier framework had always required either direct observation of the Kuti's shechita or ensuring that the Kuti himself would eat from the same meat. Perhaps, Rabbi Zeira suggested, this ruling of Rabban Gamliel did nothing more than reaffirm that without such observation the shechita is invalid — and stopped short of invalidating Kuti shechita even when directly observed.

### Sharp Response

The response was withering. Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi replied: it appears that this rabbi has never properly learned anything in his life. בְּשֶׁאֵין יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבּוֹ לְמֵימְרָא בָּעֵי? — Would that need to be stated at all? The requirement of direct observation when dealing with a Kuti is elementary and universally accepted. If someone is transmitting a ruling attributed all the way back to Rabban Gamliel and his beit din to the effect that Kuti shechita is invalid, the ruling cannot be addressing the already-obvious case of unobserved shechita. It must be addressing direct observation — the only case in which the ruling would tell us something new. The implication is remarkable: Rabban Gamliel and his beit din were declaring the shechita of a Kuti invalid even when a Yisrael stands directly over him and observes every movement. And as the Gemara makes clear, there is really only one category in all of halacha where such a ruling makes sense — a non-Jew, whose shechita is categorically invalid regardless of technical correctness. The implicit conclusion is that Rabban Gamliel's beit din had reclassified the Kutim as non-Jews.

### Later Acceptance

This ruling notwithstanding, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak reported in the name of Rav Assi: אֲנִי רָאִיתִי אֶת רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן שֶׁאָכַל מִשְּׁחִיטַת כּוּתִי — I saw Rabbi Yochanan eating from the shechita of a Kuti. And Rav Assi himself similarly ate from the shechita of a Kuti. These were later Amoraim, operating after the ruling of Rabban Gamliel and his beit din, yet they consumed meat slaughtered by a Kuti without apparent hesitation.

### Zeira's Confusion

Rabbi Zeira was astonished. He proposed two possible explanations for their behavior. Either — לֹא שְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ — they never received the report of Rabban Gamliel's ruling, and had they heard it they would certainly have accepted it and refrained. Or — שְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ וְלֹא קַבְּלוּהָ — they heard the ruling and deliberately rejected it, holding that the shechita of a Kuti remained valid. Rabbi Zeira did not initially know which of these alternatives was correct.

The Gemara then records that Rabbi Zeira resolved the question for himself: מִסְתַּבְּרָא דִּשְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ וְלֹא קַבְּלוּהָ — it is more plausible that they had heard the ruling and rejected it. The reasoning rests on a principle the Gemara now invokes.

### Tzadikim Principle

The Gemara argues as follows. If Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi had never heard the ruling of Rabban Gamliel, and had they heard it they would have accepted it and treated the meat as treif, then they were in fact eating what they themselves would have considered forbidden food — through no fault of their own, simply because the report never reached them. But this leads to a deeply uncomfortable consequence: הֵיכִי מִסְתַּיְּיעָא מִילְּתָא לְמֵיכַל אִיסּוּרָא — how could God have allowed this to happen, that these righteous figures would eat forbidden food?

The Gemara grounds this reasoning in an explicit principle: אֵין הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מֵבִיא תַּקָּלָה עַל יְדֵיהֶם — God does not bring about stumbling through the hands of the righteous. The illustration is the celebrated story of the donkey of Pinchas ben Yair, which refused to eat untithed grain — something the Gemara will return to later on the daf. If God takes such care that even the animals of tzadikim do not consume forbidden food, how much more so — צַדִּיקִים עַצְמָן לֹא כָּל שֶׁכֵּן — would God not allow the tzadikim themselves to inadvertently eat issur. It follows that if Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi did eat from Kuti shechita, it cannot be that they were eating something they would have considered forbidden. They must have heard the ruling and consciously ruled against it — holding that Kuti shechita remained valid.

This principle is significant and, as Tosafot notes, requires careful delimitation. The Gemara does not claim that tzadikim never sin or never err. Tosafot points out that there are cases in the Gemara where tzadikim make mistakes in other domains. The principle as deployed here — and as it will recur later in the daf — is specifically about forbidden food. And this narrow application reflects a broader halachic orientation: that the consumption of forbidden food is not simply a sin like other sins, but carries some additional dimension — physical, spiritual, or both — that makes it a category apart. This is why, for instance, the question of feeding infants non-kosher food is treated differently from allowing infants to perform other prohibited acts before the age of chinuch: treif food may be understood to have a corrupting effect that goes beyond the technical prohibition, an effect that the Gemara treats as an objective concern independent of culpability.

### Final Acceptance

The Gemara then draws the final inference about Rabbi Zeira's own position. Had Rabbi Zeira maintained his initial suggestion — that Rabban Gamliel's ruling only addressed unobserved Kuti shechita — he would have had no difficulty explaining why Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi ate the meat. He could simply have said: they ate it when directly observing, and the ruling says nothing about that case. The very fact that Rabbi Zeira was troubled, that he was forced to consider whether they had never heard the ruling or had rejected it, demonstrates that he had already abandoned his earlier position. He accepted the sharp rebuttal — that Rabban Gamliel's ruling clearly speaks to direct observation as well — and therefore could not simply wave aside the behavior of Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קִבְּלָהּ מִינֵּיהּ — we therefore conclude that Rabbi Zeira accepted the ruling as applying even when a Yisrael stands directly over the Kuti shochet.

The overall picture that emerges from this extended sugya is layered. There is a firm ruling, attributed to Rabban Gamliel and his beit din, that Kuti shechita is invalid under all circumstances — a ruling that implicitly treats Kutim as non-Jews. Rabbi Zeira ultimately accepts this ruling in its full scope. Yet even generations later, prominent Amoraim such as Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Assi ate from Kuti shechita, having apparently concluded that the ruling should not be followed. The sugya thus presents a halachic controversy that did not resolve uniformly across all authorities, even well after Rabban Gamliel's formal decree.
