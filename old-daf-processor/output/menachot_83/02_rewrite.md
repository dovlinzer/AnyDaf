# Menachot 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pesach & Non-Sanctified

### Intro: Pesach Debate

The daf opens in the middle of a sustained debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva concerning the source for the principle that a korban Pesach may only be purchased from non-sanctified, non-ma'aser sheini funds. Rabbi Eliezer derives this rule from the Pesach Mitzrayim, while Rabbi Akiva rejects that inference on the grounds that one cannot apply a principle derived from a ma'aseh she-ee efshar — a situation that was impossible under normal circumstances — to a ma'aseh efshar, a situation that is entirely possible. The Gemara had already established that Rabbi Eliezer's derivation rests on an explicit hekesh linking the two cases, and invokes the famous principle ein meshivin al ha-hekesh: once a biblical linkage is explicit, objections based on logical inconsistency no longer apply.

### Chozer u'Melamed

The Gemara now wraps up this discussion with a question posed u've-tarbitza — in the academy, among the students, in the sense of harbatzat Torah. The question is: ve-chi davar ha-lamed be-hekesh chozer u-melamed be-hekesh? We learned Pesach Dorot from Pesach Mitzrayim through a hekesh; can we then take Pesach Dorot and apply its principles to all other korbanot through another hekesh? The Gemara answers: be-khulala it-mar — it was stated as a general category. Kula chada milta hi: all instances of korban Pesach constitute a single unified category. Even though a hekesh was required to extend the law from Pesach Mitzrayim to Pesach Dorot — because the one applied only once in history while the other is a general ongoing obligation — the derivation does not constitute daisy-chaining from A to B to C. Rather, it is a single category being made whole, and from that unified Pesach category one may legitimately extend principles to all other korbanot.

### Rabbi Akiva's Source

Rabbi Akiva does not dispute the ruling itself — that a Pesach, and by extension all obligatory korbanot, must come from non-ma'aser sheini funds. His disagreement is solely with the source. He cannot learn it from Pesach Mitzrayim, which is a she-ee efshar case. Instead, the Gemara explains, nafka lei me-ha d'amar Shmuel mishum Rabbi Elazar — he derives it from a statement of Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Elazar. That statement will emerge from a general pasuk that simultaneously teaches numerous laws shared across all korbanot: Zot ha-Torah la-olah ve-la-minchah ve-la-chatat ve-la-asham ve-la-miluim u-le-zeivach ha-shelamim. The Gemara will mine each entry in that list for a law that is explicit in one korban and then, by virtue of the pasuk's linkage, applied to all others. Notably, an almost identical exposition of this same pasuk appears near the end of Masechet Zevachim — the same literary phenomenon by which a summing-up verse generates a comprehensive set of shared halakhot toward the close of the tractate.

## Zot HaTorah Pasuk

### Zot HaTorah Intro

The Gemara begins with olah: mah olah te'unah keli — just as an olah requires a vessel, so too every korban requires a vessel. The Gemara first considers whether the vessel in question is a mizrak, a bowl for catching the blood. After Har Sinai, it says, the Torah records that they slaughtered olot and shelamim and placed the blood ba-aganot — in bowls — and from there one might derive that blood-catching requires a vessel. The Gemara objects: but that is written by shelamim as well, not uniquely by the olah. The vessel we are focused on, then, is something specific to the olah: the sakin, the slaughter knife.

### Olah: Knife

The Gemara then asks: ulo'ah gufah minalan — how do we know the olah itself required a knife-vessel? The source is va-yishlach Avraham et yado va-yikach et ha-machelet lishchot et bno: Abraham reached out his hand and took the machelet — a notably graphic term for a knife, one that evokes the act of cutting into flesh — to slaughter his son. The Gemara establishes that Yitzchak was being treated as an olah: after the akeida, the Torah says regarding the ram va-ya'alehu le-olah tachat bno, he offered it as an olah in place of his son, which confirms that the son had been intended as an olah. Since Abraham used a knife for what was being treated as an olah offering, we derive that an olah requires a knife.

This source is striking — and more than a little disturbing. The derivation of korban law from the akeida is unavoidable: olah tachat bno makes the substitutionary logic explicit. The Gemara does not shy away from this, even as we might prefer to bracket it.

A closer look reveals that this passage also bears on the question from Masechet Chulin of whether the slaughter knife must be detached — mechubar le-karka — and we learn from Abraham that it must be. But our Gemara is adding something beyond mere detachment. The word kli suggests that for a korban, the knife must meet the standard of a vessel — something fashioned to function as a knife — as opposed to, say, a sharpened rock. Tosafos raises a further question: does the korban requirement extend to a kli sharet, a sanctified vessel? The Gemara here says only kli, not kli sharet, and it is not obvious from the akeida passage that the machelet was sanctified. Tosafos attempts to derive it from an extra word but acknowledges the difficulty. In practice, there are three distinct levels: the general shechitah requirement that the knife be detached; the korban requirement that it be a proper fashioned vessel; and the debated requirement among Rishonim that it also be a kli sharet.

### Abraham & Isaac

The Gemara proceeds to minchah: mah minchah einah ne'echelet ela le-zichrei kehunah — just as a minchah is eaten only by male kohanim, so too af kol eino ne'echal ela le-zichrei kehunah — anything from among the korbanot that goes to the kohanim is restricted to the male kohanim. This differs from terumah, which the kohen may feed to his entire household including women and children; the portions of the korbanot allocated to kohanim are reserved for the males exclusively.

The Gemara then asks: by which korban is this actually needed? If we are speaking of a chatat or asham, the law is stated explicitly in the relevant pesukim. The one case where this is not explicit is zivchei shalmei tzibbur — the communal shelamim, a category whose only real member in practice is the kivsei atzeret, the lambs of Shavuot, since these go to the kohanim rather than to the owners. The Gemara responds that me-ribuyah de-kra atya — we actually derive this from an extra expression in the pasuk in Parshat Korach, where in reiterating all the priestly entitlements the Torah says kol zachar yochal oto after already having said tochelen — the repetition of "eat it" comes to include shalmei tzibbur among those eaten only by male kohanim. The Gemara therefore concludes tannai hi: it is a tannaic dispute as to which pasuk one learns this from — some derive it from the minchah entry in Zot HaTorah, others from the extra formulation in Parshat Korach.

### Mincha: Male Kohanim

Ve-la-chatat: mah chatat mekadesh be-valua af kol mekadesh be-valua. Just as a chatat sanctifies by absorption, so too any korban sanctifies through absorption. The Torah states by the chatat: kol asher yiga be-visarah yikdash — anything that touches the flesh of the chatat becomes holy. The Gemara interprets this as yikdash lihyot kamoha: it becomes holy in the same mode as the chatat itself. If the chatat was valid, an item that absorbed some of its juices adopts its restrictions — for instance, if the chatat was a one-day sacrifice, the item that absorbed from it may only be eaten for one day. Ve-im pasul, if the chatat was itself invalid, then tipasel — the other item also becomes invalid. This is the principle of mekadesh be-valua: sanctification through being absorbed.

One might ask why this is a special korban principle at all, since the general laws of mixtures already mandate that when something is not batel it conveys the more stringent status. The answer is that by korbanot this applies even in cases where under ordinary mixture principles the forbidden substance would have been batel — the Torah overrides the normal threshold. That expanded stringency is the chiddush of mekadesh be-valua, and af kol mekadesh be-valua extends it to all korbanot.

### Chatat: Sanctifies

Ve-la-asham: mah asham ein shapir ve-shilya kadosh bo — just as by an asham the placenta and embryo carry no sanctity, af kol ein shapir ve-shilya kadosh bo — by no korban do the placenta and embryo carry sanctity. The logic is that an asham must be male, so a placenta and embryo could never exist in an asham in the first place. Applying this to a female korban like a chatat, we learn that even there, if one finds an embryo or placenta inside the animal, it possesses no kedushah: it need not be offered on the mizbeach, and it has none of the attendant restrictions of korban meat. As Rashi notes in Zevachim, the question of whether one must even remove the kidneys and fat of the embryo is itself resolved — the answer is no, since the embryo has no sanctity whatsoever.

The Gemara specifies the two assumptions underlying this derivation: first, that veladot kodashim be-havayatan hen kedoshin — the offspring of a consecrated animal acquires its kedushah only upon birth, not while still in the womb. Second, ve-danin efshar mi-she-ee efshar — one may derive a law applicable in a possible case from a situation that is impossible. This second assumption, notably, is the very principle Rabbi Akiva rejects in the earlier debate. The Gemara is careful to note that the entire braita here is amar Shmuel mishum Rabbi Elazar, i.e., it proceeds from the school of Rabbi Eliezer, who does accept danin efshar mi-she-ee efshar. Rabbi Akiva's own derivations will be addressed separately.

### Asham: No Embryo

Ve-la-miluim: mah miluim mosreihen bi-sreifa — just as by the miluim sacrifices, the leftover meat is burned, ve-ein ba'alei chayim mosreihen bi-sreifa — but live animals are not burned as notar. The Torah says explicitly to Aaron and his sons by the miluim: if anything is left over from the meat, burn it in the fire. While the law of notar applies broadly, the explicit command to burn notar appears not by every case but specifically here by the miluim. By virtue of Zot HaTorah, this applies to all korbanot: af kol mosreihen bi-sreifa — notar meat of any korban is burned.

The second half of the rule — that live animals are not treated as notar subject to burning — arises from the case of a korban that becomes "leftover" not as meat but as a live animal. The classic instance is when one designates a replacement for a lost korban, then recovers the original: one of the two is a kind of leftover. Rashi notes that the Torah never actually addresses the case of burning a live animal by the miluim; shelo matzinu sreifa ba-miluim ela be-vasar le-achar shechitah. The Torah speaks only of burning the remaining meat after shechitah, not of any living animal. So this too is in the category of she-ee efshar — the case simply does not arise. The rule is therefore: notar meat is burned across all korbanot; this does not extend to a live animal that has been rendered "leftover."

### Miluim: Burning

The final term in the pasuk is ul-zeivach ha-shelamim: mah shelamim mifaglim u-mitfaglim — just as shelamim both creates piggul and becomes piggul itself, so too af kol mifaglim u-mitfaglim. The paradigm case of piggul in the Torah is the shelamim, and from there the principle extends to all korbanot.

Tosafos explains the formulation mifaglim u-mitfaglim in light of the fundamental structure of piggul: for piggul to apply, there must be two distinct components — an act being performed (such as zerikah, the sprinkling of the blood) that is matir, i.e., permits, something else (such as the basar, the meat). If a kohen sprinkles the blood with intent to eat the meat outside its proper time, that invalid intent is mefagel the basar. This is why a minchat kohen, which is entirely burned and has no portion to be "permitted" by the burning of the kometz, cannot have piggul — there is no two-part structure. The expression mifaglim u-mitfaglim therefore means: it creates piggul in the remaining portion, and correspondingly that portion itself becomes piggul. This applies to all korbanot that share the two-part structure.

## Rabbi Akiva's Chatat

### Shelamim: Pigul

The Gemara now introduces a parallel braita: be-matnitin tana mishmeih de-Rabbi Akiva — taught in the name of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva proceeds through the same pasuk of Zot HaTorah but draws different lessons from each term. The olah entry is apparently skipped, perhaps because the knife-vessel law is the same; we proceed directly to minchah.

Rabbi Akiva learns mekadesh be-valua — sanctification through absorption — not from the chatat (as Rabbi Eliezer's tradition did) but from the minchah, where it is equally written: kol asher yiga bahem yikdash. The Gemara then asks why the Torah found it necessary to write the rule both by the chatat and by the minchah. The answer is that each case carries a unique rationale that would not have been extended to the other: had the Torah written it only by the minchah, one might have thought the principle applies because the minchah is soft and readily absorbs — mishum de-rachicha balah — but that a chatat, being meat, would not. Had the Torah written it only by the chatat, one might have thought the principle applies because meat is fatty and penetrates — mishum de-vasar agav de-shamein kadir — but that a minchah, being soft and flour-like, would not. Tzricha: both were required.

One difficulty remains in this reasoning. The rationale offered for the minchah is that "it absorbs" — but the direction of transfer in mekadesh be-valua is that the minchah's juices penetrate something else, not that the minchah takes on substance from something else. A minchah's softness would make it more susceptible to absorbing from other things, which is actually the lesser chiddush. The chiddush is precisely that when a drop of minchah gets absorbed into something else, the minchah's kedushahstatus follows it. The Gemara's formulation here — de-rachicha balah — does not map cleanly onto that direction, and the precise logic remains difficult.

### Akiva's Chatat Use

This is the derivation the entire discussion was building toward. Rabbi Akiva reads the chatat entry of Zot HaTorah as follows: machatat einah ba'ah ela min ha-chulin — a chatat comes only from non-sanctified funds. And from the pasuk's linkage, af kol einah ba'ah ela min ha-chulin — so too all obligatory korbanot must come from non-ma'aser sheini funds.

How do we know this specifically of the chatat? Rabbi Chisda teaches: the pasuk in the Yom Kippur passage says ve-hikriv Aharon et par ha-chatat asher lo — "that is his" — lo mishelo, from his own personal funds, ve-lo mishel ma'aser, and not from any other source including ma'aser funds. From the word lo we derive that the chatat must come from the kohen's own non-designated funds. By virtue of Zot HaTorah, this extends to all korbanot.

The Gemara notes that two additional laws were mentioned in conjunction with the chatat: that it must be brought in the daytime, and that the kohen must use his right hand. Both, however, are ultimately superfluous as derivations from this context: the daytime requirement is already learned from the pasuk mi-yom tzavoto — "on the day God commanded" — a general rule for all korbanot; and the right-hand requirement is derived from the principle of Rabba Bar Bar Channah quoting Reish Lakish that wherever the Torah mentions etzba and kehunah together it refers to the right hand. The Gemara concludes regarding both: kidi nasba — they were included gratuitously. The essential and purposeful derivation from the chatat entry is solely the requirement of chulin funds.

### Akiva: Mincha

Ve-la-asham: mah asham atzmotav mutarin — just as by an asham the bones are permitted for personal use, af kol atzmotav mutarin — so too by all korbanot the bones are permitted. This means that the bones, like the hide, are given to the kohanim without any of the restrictions that apply to the korban's meat — no time limit, no geographic restriction, and no obligation to treat them with kedushah. They may be used for whatever purpose one wishes.

This rule is effectively self-evident for most korbanot, since the pesukim speak of the kohanim eating the meat and implicitly exclude non-meat components from the same restrictions. The significant case is the olah, which is entirely burned on the mizbeach. One might have thought that since the entire olah must go up in flames, even the bones are included. This derivation teaches otherwise: even by the olah, the bones are excluded from the korban's sanctity and need not be placed on the mizbeach. The kohen may take the bones home and use them freely.

## Pesach: Shelamim

### Akiva: Chatat Only

The Gemara now turns to Rabbi Akiva's reading of the pasuk in Devarim: u-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar. Rabbi Eliezer had used the word bakar — cattle — to extend the principles of Pesach to all other korbanot, since a Pesach cannot come from cattle. Rabbi Akiva, who cannot accept that usage (it would constitute learning from a she-ee efshar case), instead applies the pasuk mi-bayei lei le-chide-Rav Nachman — to teach the ruling of Rav Nachman.

Rav Nachman, quoting Rabbah bar Avuha, asks: minayin le-motar Pesach she-karev shelamim — how do we know that what is left over from a korban Pesach is brought as a shelamim? Motar Pesach refers to the classic case of a Pesach that was lost, replaced by a substitute, and then recovered — one of the two animals must now be dealt with, and it has the status of "leftover." The answer: she-ne'emar u-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar. Since a Pesach cannot come from bakar, the word bakar must be teaching something else: that the motar Pesach — which can no longer be a Pesach — becomes a korban of the type that comes from both tzon and bakar, namely a shelamim. The Pesach is similar to a shelamim in that it is eaten by its owners, and when it can no longer function as a Pesach it becomes precisely that.

The Gemara then challenges: but do we not already derive this from the pasuk cited by the father of Shmuel? For it is written by the shelamim: im min ha-tzon korbano le-zevach shelamim. The father of Shmuel asks why the Torah says im min ha-tzon rather than im min ha-kevasim — why the broad "flock" rather than the specific "sheep"? Because davar ha-ba min ha-tzon — something that comes only from the flock. What comes only from flock animals? The Pesach — it comes only from sheep or goats. Yehei le-zevach shelamim: if it can no longer be brought as a Pesach, it becomes a shelamim.

A third pasuk is then cited from a braita: the Torah says by the shelamim, im keshev hu makriv — if it is a sheep he brings. Le-rabbot et ha-Pesach la-alyah — this comes to include the Pesach in the requirement of burning the fat tail. And subsequently: ke-she-hu omer im keshev — when it says if it is a sheep — le-havi Pesach she-avrah shenato — this includes a Pesach whose year has passed, and shelamim ha-ba'im machmat Pesach — shelamim that arise on account of the Pesach. Le-khol mitzvot shelamim — they are subject to all the rules of shelamim: semichah, nesachim, tenufat chazeh ve-shok. These are things one does not do with a Pesach itself but does with a Pesach that has transformed into a shelamim. The pasuk then says ve-im ez — if it is a goat — and this interruption, hifsik ha-inyan, teaches she-einah te'unah alyah — a goat's small tail is not burned, unlike a sheep's fat tail.

### Akiva: Asham Bones

The Gemara asks: tlata kra'ei ktivu — there are three pesukim, each apparently teaching that a Pesach becomes a shelamim. Why are all three needed? Chad le-avar zmano ve-avrah shnato — one addresses the case where Pesach has already passed and the animal is also more than a year old, rendering it entirely disqualified from ever serving as a Pesach. Mishum de-idchei lei le-gamrei — since it has been completely rejected from the category of Pesach, its transformation into shelamim seems obvious.

Ve-chad le-avar zmano ve-lo avrah shnato — a second is needed for when Pesach has passed but the animal is still within its first year. Here, d'chazi le-Pesach sheni — it could in theory be used for Pesach Sheni, the makeup Pesach of the fourteenth of Iyyar. Since it has not been fully rejected from potential Pesach use, one might have thought it does not become a shelamim if slaughtered in the interim. The second pasuk teaches that it does.

Ve-chad de-lo avar zmano ve-lo avrah shnato — the third is needed for when it is the right age and Pesach has not yet arrived — it is, say, Purim. The animal is fully eligible to serve as a Pesach in a few weeks. One might have thought that if slaughtered now it carries the status of a Pesach. The third pasuk teaches that even here, if brought before Pesach, it has the status of a shelamim and is not treated as a Pesach. Tzricha: all three cases required their own derivation.

### Pesach Pasuk Issue

The summary of this extended discussion is as follows. The entire debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva ultimately yields the same ruling through different sources: an obligatory korban cannot come from ma'aser sheini funds. Rabbi Eliezer derives it from Pesach Mitzrayim; Rabbi Akiva derives it from the chatat entry of Zot HaTorah. As for the difficult pasuk u-zavachta Pesach la-Hashem Elokecha tzon u-vakar, Rabbi Eliezer reads bakar as extending Pesach principles to all other korbanot; Rabbi Akiva reads it as establishing that a motar Pesach — in any of its three forms — takes on the status of a shelamim.

## Minchot: Sources

### Motar Pesach

A new perek begins, redirecting the discussion back to the laws of menachot. Kol korbanot ha-tzibbur ve-ze ha-yachid — all communal and individual korbanot, by which the mishna means all communal and individual menachot in context — ba'im min ha-aretz u-mi-chutz la-aretz: the grain used for them may come from Israel or from outside Israel. Min ha-chadash u-min ha-yashan: they may come from new grain or old, recently harvested or stored.

### Three Pesukim

Chutz min ha-omer u-shtei ha-lechem — with the exception of the Omer and the Shtei HaLechem brought on Pesach and Shavuot respectively. She-einam ba'im ela min ha-chadash: these two must come from new grain, since their entire purpose is to represent the new barley and wheat harvest. U-min ha-aretz: they must also come from grain grown in Eretz Yisrael, since they are the first harvest of the Land. These are the only two menachot so restricted.

### Pesach Cases

Ve-khulam einam ba'im ela min ha-muvchar — and for all menachot, one must use choice flour. Ve-eizeh u muvchar shelahem? — what counts as the choicest? Machnis ve-Zatcha, two locations in Eretz Yisrael famed for superior grain — some manuscripts read Michmas u-Meyunicha or Machmas ve-Zanocha. Whatever the precise identification, the Mishnah describes them as alfa le-solet — grade A flour, the alpha of fine flour. Shniyah lahem, second to them in quality, is Aforayim ba-bika, from the valley region called Aforayim. Kol ha-aratzot hayu kesherot — all lands were technically valid, ela mi-kan hayu mevi'im — but this is where they would actually bring from, as these produced the best grain. Whether this final specification refers to all menachot or specifically to the Omer and Shtei HaLechem is not entirely clear from the mishna. Rashi reads kol artzot shel Eretz Yisrael kesherot, limiting himself to Eretz Yisrael, which suggests he is speaking of the Omer and Shtei HaLechem specifically, the only menachot required to come from Israeli grain.

## Omer & Shtei HaL

### Grain Sources

The Gemara now challenges the mishna: matnitin de-lo ke-hai tanna — our mishna does not accord with the following tanna. For a braita teaches: Omer ha-ba min ha-yashan kasher — if the Omer is brought from last year's grain, it is valid. Shtei ha-lechem ha-ba'ah min ha-yashan kesherah — and likewise the Shtei HaLechem brought from old grain is valid. Ela she-chisar mitzvah — one has merely failed to fulfill the mitzvah optimally. This stands in clear tension with our mishna, which states that these two must come from new grain.

How does the braita permit this? By the Omer the Torah says takriv et minchat bikurecha — you shall bring your first-fruits offering — and from the additional verb takriv the braita derives that one may bring it even afilu min ha-aliyah, even from stored grain in the attic. Similarly by the Shtei HaLechem, the Torah says mi-moshvoteichem tavi'u — from your dwelling places you shall bring — and from the inclusive word tavi'u (you shall bring) the braita derives that one may bring it from anywhere, even stored grain, though mi-moshvoteichem simultaneously excludes grain from chutz la-aretz.

### Choice Flour

The Gemara presses: ve-ha-kativ reishit — the Torah explicitly says it must be the first of the cutting! And: ha-kativ chadashah — it must be a minchat chadashah, a new grain offering! How can the braita permit old grain in the face of these explicit terms?

The Gemara answers: hai mi-bayei lei li-khe-de-tanya — the word chadashah is needed for what the following tanna teaches.

### Old Grain Braita

Rabbi Natan ve-Rabbi Akiva amru: Shtei ha-lechem ha-ba'ot min ha-yashan kesherot — Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Akiva both hold that Shtei HaLechem brought from old grain is nonetheless valid. U-mani mekayem chadashah — how then do they understand the requirement that it be chadashah? She-tehei chadashah le-khol ha-menachot: it must be new relative to all other menachot — meaning that until the Shtei HaLechem is brought, no other minchahmay be offered from the new grain's harvest. The word chadashah does not mandate that the Shtei HaLechem itself come from new grain; it mandates that it inaugurate the new grain season for all other menachot. Paradoxically, on this view the Shtei HaLechem need not itself consist of new grain, yet once it is brought, all other menachot may now use the new grain. The requirement of chadashah is thus relational — it defines the Shtei HaLechem's role as the gateway to the new grain season — rather than intrinsic to the composition of the offering itself.

A final note on the mishna's opening statement: although it says kol korbanot ha-tzibbur ve-ha-yachid, the context makes clear that menachot are the subject. Tosafos raises the question of analogous rules for animal korbanot — whether there are limitations regarding where the animals come from or distinctions between new and old. He concludes from the Tosefta that kol korbanot ha-tzibbur ve-ha-yachid ba'in me-eretz u-mi-chutz la-aretz afilu mi-ben ovdei kochavim: all animal korbanot may come from Israel, from outside Israel, and even from non-Jews. The notable exceptions are the bechor and ma'aser behemah, where there may be restrictions, a topic that will be addressed more fully in Masechet Bechorot.
