# Berakhot 12 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Emet VeYatziv & Emet

### Emet VeYatziv Emet Ve'em…

The daf opens near the bottom of 12a with a teaching of Rava bar Chinana Sabba in the name of Rav: *Kol shelo amar Emet VeYatziv shachrit ve'Emet Ve'emuna arvit lo yatza yedei chovato* — whoever did not say Emet VeYatziv in the morning and Emet Ve'emuna in the evening has not fulfilled his obligation. The Gemara has been tracing the blessings that surround the Shema — the blessings before it, Yotzer HaMeorot and Ahava Rabba in the morning, Maariv Aravim and Ahavat Olam in the evening, and the distinction between them. Now it turns to the blessings that follow. The point here is not merely that a blessing must be recited after the Shema — that much is known — but that the specific formulation matters. Just as there is a distinction between Ahava Rabba and Ahavat Olam, so too there is a corresponding distinction between the two post-Shema blessings: Emet VeYatziv in the morning and Emet Ve'emuna at night.

The scriptural basis is the verse *lehagid baboker chasdecha ve'emunatecha baleilot* — to declare Your kindness in the morning and Your faithfulness at night. The morning evokes an appreciation of God's manifest beneficence: the light, the world, the tangible gifts of existence — the register of Ahava Rabba. The evening, with its darkness and uncertainty, draws on a deeper register of faith and endurance — the register of Ahavat Olam. Emet Ve'emuna carries that same quality of abiding trust that characterizes the night.

### Rashi: Morning/Evening

Rashi elaborates on this distinction from the angle of content rather than atmosphere. He explains that *birchat Emet VeYatziv kulah al chesed she'asa im avoteinu* — the blessing of Emet VeYatziv is entirely about the chesed God performed for our ancestors: taking us out of Egypt, splitting the sea, bringing them through. By contrast, *birchat Emet Ve'emuna medaberet af al ha'atidot* — the blessing of Emet Ve'emuna speaks also of future hopes, of the anticipation that God will fulfill His promises and redeem us from the dominion of kingdoms. These are not merely parallel formulations; they represent distinct orientations — one looking backward at miraculous history, the other looking forward in faith. Both dimensions, Rashi implies, correspond naturally to their respective times of day: the morning invites gratitude for what has already been given, the night invites trust in what is yet to come.

## Bowing & Rising

### Bow Baruch Rise Shem

The Gemara continues with another teaching from the same tradition — Rava bar Chinana Sabba in the name of Rav: *Hamitpallel kshehu kore'a kore'a beBaruch, ukshehu zokef zokef beShem* — when one prays and bows, one bows at the word *Baruch*, and when one rises, one rises upon pronouncing the divine Name. In practical terms: at *Baruch Ata*, one bends the knees at *Baruch* and is fully bowed at *Ata*, and then as one reaches the divine Name, one straightens upright. Shmuel later conveys this same ruling to Chiya bar Rav — addressing him as *bar Oryan*, either son of a lion or a term evoking Torah scholarship — noting it as a *milta maalya*, a beautiful teaching, that his father Rav had said.

### Shmuel's Reasoning

Shmuel explains the rationale: *dechtiv Hashem zokef kefufim* — as the verse states, God straightens those who are bent. It is therefore the utterance of the divine Name that causes the transition from the bowed position to the erect one. God's Name, in its very pronounciation, lifts the one who has humbled himself.

### Contradiction Resolution

The Gemara raises an apparent contradiction: does not another verse indicate that the divine Name inspires humility — *mipnei shemi nichat hu*, because of My name he is humbled? If God's name causes one to become low, how can it also be what causes one to rise? The Gemara resolves this by noting the precise wording: *mipnei shemi* — because of My name, not through the utterance of My name. The awe and fear that God's name inspires generates the humility that drives a person to bow; but when one actually pronounces the Name in prayer, that very act of reaching out to God is what causes one to stand upright.

There is a beautiful dialectic here that runs through the entire discussion of prayer. We stand in a low place but plant our feet together like an angel, simultaneously expressing smallness and aspiring to connection. Similarly here: the bowing expresses the sense of being nothing in the presence of the Divine, and the pronounciation of the Name — the act of reaching out to connect — is precisely what restores the worshipper to an upright stance. The competing themes of awe and connection, humility and aspiration, are not resolved but held in tension.

### Rav Sheshet's Practice

The Gemara moves to 12b and records the practice of Rav Sheshet: *ki kara, kara kachizra* — when he bowed, he bowed like a rod, meaning all at once, dropping immediately to the ground without hesitation, as though a stick had been thrown down. *Vechi zakif, zakif kachivya* — and when he rose, he rose like a snake, slowly and gradually, the way a snake rises from the ground beginning from its head. Rashi notes that the swift bowing ensures that the act does not look like a burden one is eager to shed. The emphasis falls on the weight given to the bowing down — the immediate, whole-hearted expression of awe — while the rising is a slower, more effortful process of regaining the strength to stand. The asymmetry itself carries the message: the inclination toward humility is swift and instinctive; the return to uprightness requires gradual effort. Here again the tension is not dissolved but given a particular weighting.

## Aseret Yemei Teshuvah

### Year-Round Formula

Following the same chain of tradition — Rabbah bar Chinana Sabba in the name of Rav — the Gemara presents a third ruling, now concerning Shemoneh Esrei rather than the blessings of Shema. Throughout the entire year, in the third blessing of Shemoneh Esrei one says *Ha'El HaKadosh*, and in the blessing of justice in the middle one says *Melech Ohev Tzedakah uMishpat*. These are the standard formulations of the year-round nusach.

### Ten Days Changes

The exception is the ten days encompassing Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur — the Aseret Yemei Teshuvah. The phrase "ten days between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur" is technically imprecise, since there are only seven days between those dates; the expression refers to the ten days that encompass both. During this period, the formulation shifts: instead of *Ha'El HaKadosh* one says *HaMelech HaKadosh*, and instead of *Melech Ohev Tzedakah uMishpat* one says *HaMelech HaMishpat*. The theme of God's kingship and judgment dominates this season, and both formulas reflect that emphasis — *Melech* is foregrounded, and the attribute of justice is stated as identity rather than as a quality God loves.

It should be noted that *HaMelech HaKadosh* was already the accepted formulation specifically on Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur themselves. The question being addressed here is whether that formulation, along with the parallel change to *HaMelech HaMishpat*, extends throughout the Aseret Yemei Teshuvah — and the answer given by Rabbah bar Chinana in the name of Rav is that it does.

### Rabbi Eliezer Opinion

Rabbi Eliezer takes a more lenient position: even if one said *Ha'El HaKadosh* during the Aseret Yemei Teshuvah, he has fulfilled his obligation *bedi'avad*. His proof is from the verse *vayigbah Hashem Tzeva'ot bamishpat veha'El HaKadosh nikdash bitzedakah* — the glory of God is elevated in judgment and the Holy God is sanctified in righteousness. When is God's judgment most prominent? These are precisely the Aseret Yemei Teshuvah. And yet the verse continues by saying *Ha'El HaKadosh*, which shows that even during this period of judgment, *Ha'El HaKadosh* remains an acceptable formulation. By extension, if *Ha'El HaKadosh* is acceptable, *Melech Ohev Tzedakah uMishpat* would presumably be acceptable as well.

### Rav Yosef Rabbah Ruling

The Gemara asks: what is the practical upshot? Rav Yosef rules that the year-round nusach — *Ha'El HaKadosh* and *Melech Ohev Tzedakah uMishpat* — is maintained throughout the Aseret Yemei Teshuvah, and *HaMelech HaKadosh* is reserved for Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur themselves. Rabbah rules that *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat* are indeed required throughout the Aseret Yemei Teshuvah. The Gemara concludes *ve'hilchata keRabbah* — and the halacha follows Rabbah, which is our accepted ruling.

### Tosafot Later Additions

Tosafot raises a further point. Having established that one must say *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat* during the Aseret Yemei Teshuvah — and that failure to do so requires one to repeat the Shemoneh Esrei — Tosafot extends this principle to other insertions of the season: *Zachrenu*, *Mi Chamocha*, *U'chtov L'Chayim*, *U'veSefer Chayim*, and similar additions. The initial position is that since these have become part of the accepted nusach, one who omits them must go back. However, Tosafot then cites a second view — attributed to Rabbeinu Chananel or Rabbeinu Tam — that these additional phrases were introduced later by the Geonim and were never explicitly ruled upon in the Talmud (*d'lo pasik be'Shas befeirush*), and therefore one does not return if one omits them. This is in fact our accepted ruling: one returns for *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat*, but not for the *Zachrenus* and *U'chtavenu* and similar insertions that were added at a later stage.

## Prayer for Others

### Sinner for Not Praying

The Gemara presents yet another teaching from Rabbah bar Chinana Sabba in the name of Rav, this time moving beyond questions of nusach and liturgical form to address the orientation and purpose of prayer itself: *Kol she'efshar lo levakesh rachamim al chaveiro ve'eino mevakesh nikra chotei* — whoever is able to pray for mercy on behalf of his friend and does not do so is called a sinner. The proof-text is Samuel's declaration: *chalila li me'chato la'Hashem mechadol l'hitpalel be'adchem* — God forbid that I should sin against God by ceasing to pray on your behalf. Not to pray for someone when one could is cast as a sin against God.

This teaching carries a double weight. On one level, it expresses a genuine theology of prayer: if one truly believes that God operates in the world and that prayer can effect change, then to withhold prayer from someone who needs it is a form of abandonment. Statutory prayer performed out of obligation speaks less directly to this living faith than responsive prayer — prayer prompted by an actual situation. The emphasis on *chaveiro* — a friend, another person — adds the dimension of interpersonal responsibility. If one has faith in the power of prayer and a friend is in need, the failure to pray combines a failure of faith with a failure of care, and that combination is called a sin.

### Afflict Self Talmid Chac…

The Gemara goes further: *Im talmid chacham hu, tzarich sheyichleh atzmo alav* — if the person in need is a Torah scholar, it is not enough merely to pray; one must make oneself ill over him, investing one's prayer with a depth of personal anguish. The Gemara initially explores whether this is derived from Saul's complaint — *ve'ein choleh mikem alai* — nobody is making themselves ill over me, lamenting that no one was deeply troubled on his behalf. The Gemara questions whether that standard might apply specifically to a king rather than a Torah scholar. The conclusion is instead drawn from the verse *va'ani bechalotam levushi* — and I in their illness wore sackcloth — understood as David afflicting himself in his prayer for others when they became ill. The standard being set is not merely sincere prayer but genuine self-abnegation: prayer that is so deeply felt it registers as a form of personal suffering.

## Shame & Forgiveness

### Sin with Shame Forgiven

Shifting from the topic of prayer to a related register of religious and ethical teaching, the Gemara cites Rabbi Chinana bar Abba in the name of Rav: *Kol oseh devar aveirah umitbayes bo, mochlin lo al kol avonosaiv* — whoever commits a sin and is embarrassed by it has all his sins forgiven. This is a striking statement. The scriptural support comes from the verse in Yechezkel: *lema'an tizkeri uvoshat velo yihyeh lach od pischon peh mipnei kelimasech bechapperi lach lechol asher asit neum Hashem Elokim* — so that you will remember and be ashamed and have no more occasion to speak because of your humiliation, when I have atoned for you for all that you have done, says God. The link is causal: the shame is the condition of the atonement.

The deeper logic here is not simply that suffering generates atonement, but that genuine shame can produce a transformative experience. If a person truly internalizes the wrongness of what he has done and feels it as humiliation, that emotional reckoning can reshape character and redirect future behavior. *Mochlin lo al kol avonosaiv* reflects not a bookkeeping transaction but the possibility of real inner change. The Gemara, however, asks whether this verse applies to the community as a whole — Israel collectively called to account — or also to an individual.

### Shaul Nov Example

The Gemara resolves this question through the case of Shaul. The verse in Shmuel records that Shaul, appealing to the spirit of Shmuel, listed the channels through which he had sought divine guidance: prophets and dreams. Notably, he did not mention the Urim VeTummim. The Gemara observes: *ve'ilu Urim VeTummim lo ka'amar* — and yet he did not say he had tried the Urim VeTummim. The reason, the Gemara infers, is *mishum dekatlei leNov ir hakohanim* — because he had destroyed Nov, the city of the Kohanim, and with it the priestly infrastructure through which one would consult the Urim VeTummim. To mention that avenue would have been to remind everyone of that devastation. His silence, the Gemara reads, reflects shame over what he had done.

This is a relatively oblique expression of shame — not a confession, not an explicit acknowledgment, but merely the omission of a topic he could not comfortably raise. Yet the Gemara treats it as significant. The avoidance itself signals that Shaul recognized the wrongness of his act. From the standpoint of ordinary psychology, one might question whether such silence constitutes genuine shame or merely uncomfortable evasion. Nevertheless, for the Gemara it is enough to establish that Shaul felt the weight of his deed.

### Bas Kol Shaul Chosen

The proof that Shaul was forgiven as a result comes from two sources. The first: Shmuel told Shaul *umachar atah uvanecha immi* — tomorrow you and your sons will be with me. Rabbi Yochanan reads *immi* — with me — as *bimchitzati*, in my portion of the World to Come. This implies that despite his sins, Shaul merited the World to Come, which the Gemara attributes to his shame over Nov Ir Kohanim.

The second source comes from the Rabbis, who cite a different verse. When David asks the Givonim how to atone for Shaul's sins against them, they call for the execution of Shaul's sons and, in that context, the text refers to Shaul as *bechir Hashem* — the chosen of God. The Gemara reads this not as the Givonim's characterization but as the narrator's voice, or more precisely as a divine declaration: a Heavenly voice — *yatzah bas kol ve'amarah* — went forth and declared Shaul to be the chosen of God. The basis for this designation, the Gemara concludes, is precisely that he had been ashamed over the destruction of Nov Ir Kohanim. The degree of shame actually demonstrated was quite minimal — little more than a failure to mention something in a list — and yet the Gemara reads it as sufficient to earn the designation of *bechir Hashem* and full forgiveness of sins.

## Parsha Balak Excluded

### Balak Inclusion Attempt

The Gemara now returns to the structure of the Shema and its surrounding liturgy. Rav Yehuda bar Zvida teaches: *bikshu likvoa parshat Balak bekri'at Shema* — the Sages considered including Parashat Balak as part of the recitation of Shema.

### Torach Tzibur Rejection

The consideration was ultimately rejected for a single practical reason: *mishum torach tzibur* — the burden it would impose on the congregation. The parsha is long, and including it would have made the Shema recitation unacceptably burdensome.

### Yetzias Mitzrayim Sources

The Gemara asks what motivated the proposal. The initial suggestion is that Parashat Balak contains the verse *El motzi'am miMitzrayim* — the God who brought them out of Egypt — and could therefore serve the function currently fulfilled by Parashat Tzitzit, which is the vehicle for the separate mitzvah of remembering the Exodus. But this cannot be the full explanation, since the memory of Yetzias Mitzrayim is mentioned in many places throughout the Torah — the prohibition of *ribbis*, the laws of honest weights — and none of those passages were ever proposed for inclusion in Shema.

Rabbi Yose bar Avin therefore identifies the specific verse that made Parashat Balak a serious candidate: *kara shachav ke'ari u'che'lavia mi yekimenu* — he crouched and lay down like a lion and like a lioness; who will rouse him? Rashi explains that this verse directly echoes the central language of Kri'at Shema — *beshochbecha uvekumecha*, when you lie down and when you rise up. A passage that contains language of lying down and rising up is uniquely suited for inclusion in the context of Shema. The proposal was thus to capture both the theme of Yetzias Mitzrayim and the structural resonance with the act of Kri'at Shema itself through Parashat Balak.

A further question arises: why not select just those two key verses from Balak rather than the whole parsha, thereby avoiding the problem of length? One might say: take *El motzi'am miMitzrayim* and *kara shachav ke'ari* and nothing else.

### Moshe's Divisions Rule

The Gemara dismisses this possibility on the basis of a received tradition: *kol parsha de'paska Moshe Rabbeinu paskhinan, delo paska Moshe Rabbeinu lo paskhinan* — any parsha that Moshe Rabbeinu divided, we may divide; any parsha that Moshe Rabbeinu did not divide, we may not divide. Since Balak is a single undivided unit, we cannot excerpt portions of it for liturgical use. It is all or nothing — and given the length, it is nothing.

This principle is distinct from the related rule that one should not divide a verse when quoting it — a rule reflected in the Gemara's convention of writing *ve'gomer* (implying completion) when citing a pasuk, as opposed to *ve'chulu* (which is used when the citation is not from a verse). *Ve'gomer* signals that the verse should be completed; the reader is meant to continue it to its end. That rule governs individual speech and quotation. The rule being invoked here — not dividing a parsha — applies specifically to liturgical incorporation: when establishing something as part of formal tefillah, one cannot take a fragment of a parsha that Moshe did not himself divide.

## Parsha Tzitzit Selected

### Tzitzit Five Things

Having established why Balak was excluded, the Gemara turns to the affirmative question: *parshat Tzitzit mipnei ma kavua* — why was Parashat Tzitzit chosen? Rav Yehuda bar Chaviva answers: because it contains not one but five things of significance — and the Gemara will note that a careful reading may actually yield six.

### Three Explicit Elements

The first three are explicit in the text. The mitzvah of Tzitzit itself: *ve'asu lahem tzitzit*. The memory of Yetzias Mitzrayim: *asher hotzeiti etchem*. And the acceptance of the yoke of mitzvot: *ure'item oto uzechartem et kol mitzvot Hashem va'asitem otam* — you shall see them and remember all the commandments of God and do them. This last element ties Parashat Tzitzit directly to the themes of the earlier parshiyot of Shema, which emphasize learning Torah and performing mitzvot. The parsha thus does not merely add a discrete obligation; it deepens and reinforces the overarching theme of the Shema recitation.

### Three Hidden Elements

Beyond the three explicit elements, the Gemara derives three further dimensions from the phrase *velo taturu acharei levavchem ve'acharei eineichem asher atem zonim achareihem* — and you shall not stray after your hearts and after your eyes, that which you go lusting after. These three clauses address three distinct dangers: heretical thought, sexual transgression, and idolatry.

### Heretical Thoughts Warni…

*Acharei levavchem* — straying after your hearts — is identified as *minut*, heresy. The heart in this context carries the sense of intellect and conviction, not merely emotion. The supporting verse is *amar naval be'libo ein Elokim* — the degenerate one says in his heart there is no God — where *be'libo*, in his heart, marks the seat of theological denial. The tzitzit serves as a reminder that keeps the mind anchored to true faith, preventing the intellect from drifting into heretical conclusions.

### Lustful Thoughts Warning

*Acharei eineichem* — straying after your eyes — refers to *hirhur aveira*, sinful thoughts of a sexual nature. The supporting verse is from the story of Shimshon: *vayomer Shimshon el aviv otah kach li ki hi yashra be'einai* — and Shimshon said to his father, take her for me, for she is pleasing in my eyes. The visual register — *be'einai*, in my eyes — identifies sight as the gateway to sexual sin. The tzitzit redirects this visual faculty toward the sacred, guarding against the tendency of the eye to lead astray.

### Idolatry Adultery Metaph…

*Asher atem zonim achareihem* — which might initially sound like it refers to sexual sin — is in fact interpreted as *hirhur avoda zara*, idolatrous thought. The key text is *vayiznu acharei ha'be'alim* — and they went lusting after the Baalim — where the metaphor of fornication (*zenut*) is applied to the worship of false gods. The underlying image is the covenantal relationship between Israel and God as a marriage, so that turning to idolatry constitutes a form of adultery or fornication. This metaphor recurs throughout the prophets. Parashat Tzitzit thus addresses, in its compact final verse, the three great temptations that undermine the foundation of Jewish religious life.

Taken as a whole, the six elements embedded in Parashat Tzitzit are not merely a convenient list of important topics. They echo and amplify the central concerns of the earlier parshiyot: the unity and exclusivity of God (*Shema Yisrael*), the commandment to love God and fulfill His mitzvot (*Ve'ahavta*), and the promise of reward and consequence (*Ve'haya im shamoa*). The third paragraph thus rounds out the Shema as a unified declaration of religious commitment, adding the memory of Yetzias Mitzrayim and guarding against the specific temptations most likely to subvert that commitment.

## Yetzias Mitzrayim Baleil…

### Mishna Exodus Night

The Gemara now reaches the final Mishnah of the chapter: *mazkirin Yetzias Mitzrayim baleilos* — we mention the Exodus from Egypt at night. This refers not to the Pesach Seder but to the daily mitzvah of remembering the Exodus, ordinarily associated with Parashat Tzitzit in the Shema. The question is whether this obligation applies only by day or also by night. The Mishnah rules that it applies at night as well.

### Rabbi Elazar Difficulty

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya — speaking of himself as *keven shivim shana*, like one who was seventy years old, a reference to the well-known story of his aged appearance — declared that he never merited understanding how to derive a proof-text for mentioning the Exodus at night, until Ben Zoma came and expounded.

### Ben Zoma Derivation

Ben Zoma's derivation rests on the verse *lema'an tizkor et yom tzeischa me'eretz Mitzrayim kol yemei chayecha* — so that you will remember the day of your going out from Egypt all the days of your life. The phrase *yemei chayecha* alone would denote the days; the inclusion of *kol* — all — expands the obligation to encompass the nights as well. The days of your life are the daytime hours; *all* the days of your life includes the full span of each day, meaning the nights alongside the days.

### Mashiach Era Dispute

The Rabbis read the same verse differently. For them, *yemei chayecha* refers to this world, and the addition of *kol* extends the obligation into the time of Mashiach — meaning that even in the messianic era, the Exodus will still be remembered. Ben Zoma responds with a challenge from the prophet Yirmiyahu: *hinei yamim ba'im... velo yomru od chai Hashem asher he'ela et benei Yisrael me'eretz Mitzrayim* — days are coming when it will no longer be said: by the life of God who brought Israel out of Egypt — *ki im chai Hashem asher he'ela et zera beit Yisrael me'eretz tzafona* — but rather: by the life of God who brought the offspring of Israel from the north and from all the lands to which He had driven them. The ultimate redemption will be so encompassing that it will displace the Exodus as the primary oath-reference. If so, why would the Rabbis say we will still mention Yetzias Mitzrayim in the messianic era?

### Primary Secondary Resolu…

The Sages respond: *lo shetei'aker Yetzias Mitzrayim mimekoma* — it does not mean that the memory of Yetzias Mitzrayim will be uprooted — *ela sheteihei shibud malchuyot ikar veYetzias Mitzrayim tafel lo* — but rather that the redemption from the subjugation of all nations will be primary, and Yetzias Mitzrayim secondary to it. The original redemption will retain its standing as the foundational act of divine salvation; it will simply no longer occupy the primary position in the liturgy of praise and oath-taking.

A parallel case is adduced: *lo yikarei shimcha od Yaakov ki im Yisrael yiheyeh shemecha* — your name shall no longer be called Yaakov, for Yisrael shall be your name. Yet God subsequently addressed him as Yaakov. This shows that the earlier name was not abolished; Yisrael became primary and Yaakov secondary. Similarly, Yetzias Mitzrayim remains part of the vocabulary of redemption, but in the messianic age the larger redemption will be primary and Yetzias Mitzrayim subordinate to it.

Further support comes from Yeshayahu: *al tizkeru rishonot, vekadmoniyot al titbonanu* — do not remember the earlier things; do not reflect on the former ones. The Gemara reads *rishonot* as referring to the subjugation by the nations, and *kadmoniyot* as referring to Yetzias Mitzrayim. In the messianic era, says Rav Yosef, this new reality — identified as the war of Gog and Magog — will so overwhelm the prior ones that they will recede into the background.

### Parable Later Afflictions

The Gemara illustrates this with a parable. A man was walking on the road and encountered a wolf, was saved, and told everyone about the wolf. Later he encountered a lion, was saved, and told everyone about the lion — and forgot about the wolf. Later still he encountered a snake, was saved, and told everyone about the snake — and forgot about both. So too with Israel: later sufferings cause earlier ones to recede from consciousness. The greater the subsequent ordeal, the more it overshadows what came before.

This parable presents something of a tension with the resolution offered by the Rabbis. If the point of the parable is that later afflictions cause people to forget earlier ones, it seems to support Ben Zoma's position that Yetzias Mitzrayim will indeed be forgotten in the light of the ultimate redemption, rather than the Rabbinic position that it will be merely secondary. One possible reading is that the parable is precisely the motivation for the mitzvah: because human nature tends to be overwhelmed by the most recent experience, the Torah specifically commands us never to forget the earlier ones. The obligation to remember Yetzias Mitzrayim, even when greater salvations have occurred, is a counterweight to this natural psychological tendency.

## Supplanting Names

### Avram to Avraham

The Gemara's discussion of Yaakov and Yisrael as names that did not fully supplant one another leads naturally into a broader treatment of names that did change. A verse from Divrei Hayamim states: *Avram hu Avraham* — Avram is Avraham. The Gemara explains: originally he was made a father to Aram alone — *av le'Aram*, reflecting the name *Avram* — but ultimately he became father to the entire world, *av hamon goyim*, father of a multitude of nations, reflected in the addition of the letter *hei* to form *Avraham*.

### Sarai to Sarah

Similarly, *Sarai hi Sarah* — Sarai is Sarah. Originally Sarai was *sharet le'umata*, mistress of her own nation alone; ultimately she became *Sarah*, mistress of all the world.

### Bar Kappara Avram Violat…

Bar Kappara teaches: *kol hakoreh leAvraham Avram over ba'aseh* — whoever calls Avraham by the name Avram transgresses a positive commandment, as the verse states *vehaya shimcha Avraham* — your name shall be Avraham. The new name is not merely descriptive but obligatory; to revert to the old name is to violate a positive mitzvah.

### Rabbi Eliezer Negative

Rabbi Eliezer goes further: it also constitutes a transgression of a negative prohibition, since the verse states *velo yikarei od shimcha Avram* — and your name shall no longer be called Avram. The formulation *velo yikarei* is not merely a statement of fact but a prohibition: do not call him Avram.

### Sarah Different Question

One might ask whether the same rules apply to the name Sarah: should calling her Sarai also constitute a transgression?

### Specific Command Answer

The Gemara answers by noting the precise wording of the divine command regarding Sarah: *Sarai ishtecha lo tikra et shema Sarai* — Sarai your wife, do not call her name Sarai — this is addressed to Avraham in the second person singular. *Lo tikra* — you shall not call her — is a command to Avraham alone, not a universal prohibition for all people. By contrast, the command regarding the name Avraham — *velo yikarei od shimcha Avram* — uses a passive formulation implying a general prohibition against anyone calling him by the old name. Therefore the prohibition applies to the name Avram universally, but the instruction about Sarah was directed only to Avraham himself and does not generate a prohibition for others.

### Jacob Name Change

The Gemara turns to the comparable case of Yaakov. The verse states *lo yikarei shimcha od Yaakov ki im Yisrael yiheyeh shemecha* — your name shall no longer be called Yaakov, for Yisrael shall be your name. By analogy with Avraham, one might expect this too to carry a universal prohibition against using the name Yaakov.

### Scriptural Evidence Reve…

The Gemara rejects this comparison on the basis of a subsequent verse: *vayomer Elokim leYisrael bemarot halayla vayomer Yaakov Yaakov* — and God said to Yisrael in the visions of the night: Yaakov, Yaakov. God Himself reverted to addressing him as Yaakov, which demonstrates that the earlier instruction did not carry the same force as the prohibition regarding Avram.

### Avraham Yaakov Distincti…

The essential distinction is this: by Avraham, the name fully superseded the old one, and reverting to the name Avram constitutes a genuine transgression — both a positive violation according to Bar Kappara and a negative prohibition according to Rabbi Eliezer. By Yaakov, the name Yisrael became primary but the name Yaakov was not abolished; it remains as a secondary name, since God Himself continued to use it.

A practical question that naturally arises is whether one giving a shiur on Parashat Lech Lecha may say "when God spoke to Avram" — using the name that was in effect at the time. The final lines of the Gemara offer some guidance: the Gemara permits a Navi to praise God by recounting *mah dehavei me'ikara*, what happened in the past, including using the name Avram in that context, because the reference is to a historical moment when that was his name. Whether the same latitude extends to other contexts — whether one discussing Avraham's earlier deeds may use the earlier name — remains somewhat ambiguous within the Gemara. The dominant practice of using *Avraham* in all contexts would seem to err on the side of stringency, and most authorities treat Bar Kappara's teaching as having at least the force of an *asmachta*, even if not a fully biblical prohibition.
