# Menachot 95 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tanur Structure

### V-Shape Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כְּמִין כַּוֶּורֶת הָיָה לָהּ בַּתַּנּוּר, וְדוֹמָה כְּמִין טַבְלָא מְרוּבַּעַת! אֵימָא: וּפִיהָ דּוֹמֶה כְּמִין טַבְלָא מְרוּבַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan from another *baraita*: **There was** a mold **in the oven for** the shewbread that was **similar to a barrel** made of reeds, as it was perforated to enable the bread to bake well, **and** in its shape **it resembled a type of rectangular tablet [*tavla*].** This indicates that the shewbread was rectangular. The Gemara answers: **Say** that **the opening,** i.e., the upper section of the mold, **resembled a type of rectangular tablet,** and that the mold tapered down to a point.

We begin at the very top of 95a, with the word *Meitivei* — a challenge to the view that the Tanur was shaped like a boat, a V-like shape. A Baraita is cited: *K'min Kaveiret Haya L'Matnur* — the Tanur was like a *Kaveiret*. A *Kaveiret* literally means a beehive, but it also referred to a kind of wicker basket or hollow tube-like vessel. So the Tanur was not exactly like a *Kaveiret*, but similar to one in shape.

### Kaveiret & Squared

*[Continued from above]*

The Baraita continues: *V'domeh K'min Tavla Meruba'at* — however, it resembled a squared tableau. This sounds very much like a description of a box shape, not a V-shape. The Gemara responds: *Eima Upia Domeh K'min Tavla Meruba'at* — do not read it as saying the entire structure was square; rather, only the opening was square-shaped. This is the first we hear of such a qualification, since the V-shape as previously described would taper at the ends and could not naturally be described as square.

### Tosafot Reconciliation

*[Continued from above]*

Tosafot suggests — not only to explain this Gemara but to reconcile numerous other sources that speak of folding up the edges and having them rise straight up — that after the V-shaped lower portion, the walls did in fact continue vertically upward. This would fit with the squared-opening description. Nevertheless, as we have seen throughout, so many sources point toward the box-like shape, and that remains clearly the dominant position.

### Sideboards Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת, דְּתַנְיָא: אַרְבָּעָה סְנִיפִין שֶׁל זָהָב הָיוּ שָׁם, מְפוּצָּלִין מֵרָאשֵׁיהֶן כְּמִין דֻּקְרָנִין הָיוּ, שֶׁסּוֹמְכִין בָּהֶן אֶת הַלֶּחֶם, שֶׁהוּא דּוֹמֶה כְּמִין סְפִינָה רוֹקֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with the one who said** the shewbread was **like a rocking boat, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **There were four gold panels there,** on the Table, which **split up at their upper ends** so that **they were like forked** reed branches. The panels were forked **because the bread, which resembled a type of rocking boat, was supported by them.**

A second Baraita is now quoted: *Tanya Ke-man De-amar K'min Sefina Rokedet* — we have a Baraita that supports the position of a boat-shaped, V-shaped Tanur. *De-tanya Arba'ah Snifim Shel Zahav Hayu Sham* — there were four golden *Snifim*, sideboards, that supported the bread. *Mifutzalim Merosheihem K'min Dakranim Hayu* — and they spread out at their heads like palm fronds. So there was some manner in which the tops of these sideboards opened and spread outward, making them less like solid walls and more like something resembling a fork whose tines splay open at the top.

### Merosheihem Meaning

*[Continued from above]*

The word *Merosheihem* — meaning "at their heads" or "at their tops" — raises a question about what exactly was spreading and where. One reading is that the sideboards ran the full height of the structure but were not solid; rather, they were lattice-like, resembling sticks with many branches, *K'min Kanim Ha-dokranim Kanim She-yesh Ba-hem Anafim Harbeh*, as Rashi describes. This reading, while somewhat supported by Rashi, is challenged by the word *Merosheihem*, which implies the spreading occurred specifically at the top.

Tosafot therefore suggests that perhaps the sideboards extended only to the height of a single loaf of bread, and then spread out at their tops — *Merosheihem* referring to the top of the sideboards, which reached only to the height of the first bread — in order to support that bottom loaf while the remaining loaves rested above. This reading accounts for why the spreading was needed: it was to stabilize the structure from below upward. The Baraita concludes by stating explicitly that the bread was *she-domeh kemin sefinah rokedet* — like this rocking, unstable boat shape — which strongly supports the V-shaped opinion. The two opinions thus stand unresolved.

### Rambam Box Shape

*[Continued from above]*

As one would expect, the Rambam rules that the box-shaped interpretation is far superior. It simply works better with the sources and is easier to understand physically. The V-shape, while supported by certain Baraitot, creates difficulties that the box-shape does not.

## Lechem HaPanim & Masa'ot

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים נִפְסָל בַּמַּסָּעוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל בַּמַּסָּעוֹת? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי – חַד אָמַר: נִפְסָל, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: During the era of the Tabernacle, was **the shewbread disqualified during the journeys** of the Jewish people in the wilderness, **or was it not disqualified during the journeys?** When the Jewish people would travel from one place to another in the wilderness, the Tabernacle would be dismantled and the Table would be carried with the loaves upon it. The dilemma is about whether or not the loaves were disqualified, since they left the boundaries of the Sanctuary. This matter is the subject of a dispute between **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. One says** the loaves were thereby **disqualified, and one says** they were **not disqualified.**

We now turn to a more *halachic discussion*. *Ibaia lehu* — the following question was raised: *Lechem HaPanim nifsal be-masa'ot o einu nifsal be-masa'ot?* Does the *lechem hapanim* become disqualified during the journeys of the camp or not? This question is obviously pressing: the bread is supposed to remain on the Shulchan throughout the week. Yet whenever the camp moves, if there is no longer a standing Ohel Moed, the bread is effectively outside the Ohel Moed — and bread removed from the Ohel Moed should become *pasul* as *yotzei*. The counterargument is that the bread is still physically on the Shulchan. That tension is precisely what the Gemara now explores.

### Becomes Pasul View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר נִפְסָל, דִּכְתִיב: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יַחֲנוּ כֵּן יִסָּעוּ״. מָה בַּחֲנִיָּיתוֹ – נִפְסָל בְּיוֹצֵא, אַף בִּנְסִיעָתוֹ – נִפְסָל בְּיוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their respective reasons: **The one who says** the shewbread was **disqualified** derives his opinion from a verse, **as it is written** with regard to the journeys: "Then the Tent of Meeting shall journey with the camp of the Levites in the midst of the camps; **as they encamp, so shall they journey,** every man in his place, by their banners" (Numbers 2:17). The verse juxtaposes the encampments with the journeys, indicating that **just as when** the Tabernacle is **encamped** the shewbread is **disqualified when** it **leaves** the Tabernacle courtyard, **so too, when** the Tabernacle **journeys** the shewbread is **disqualified when** it **leaves** the courtyard.

Rebbi Yochanan and Rebbi Yehoshua ben Levi debate this question. The one who says it becomes *pasul* derives it from the verse *Ka'asher yachanu kein yisa'u* — as they encamped, so they traveled. *Mah bi-chaniyatan nifsal be-yotzei af bi-nesiato nifsal be-yotzei* — just as when encamped, removing the bread from its proper place renders it *pasul*, so too when traveling, the same rules apply without special dispensation. Since the Ohel Moed has been dismantled, the bread is effectively outside the Mikdash and thus *pasul*.

### Remains Kosher View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְלֶחֶם הַתָּמִיד עָלָיו יִהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **The one who says** the shewbread was **not disqualified** during the journeys derives his opinion from a verse, **as it is written** with regard to the journeys: "And upon the Table of shewbread they shall spread a cloth of blue…**and the continual bread shall remain upon it"** (Numbers 4:7). The verse refers to the shewbread as "the continual bread" even during the journeys, indicating that as long as the loaves are on the Table they retain their sacred status.

The one who says it does not become *pasul* derives from the verse *Ve-lechem ha-tamid alav yihyeh* — the bread should constantly be upon it. Since the Torah itself commands that the bread remain on the Shulchan, as long as it remains in place, how can we say that following the Torah's command constitutes a problem? And as for the verse *Ka'asher yachanu kein yisa'u*, he reads it *le-idach gisa* — in precisely the opposite direction. *Mah bi-chaniato ki lo yatza mi-mekomo lo nifsal* — just as when encamped, if the bread was not removed from its place, it is not *pasul*, so too *af bi-nesiato ki lo yatza mi-mekomo lo nifsal* — when traveling, if nothing was removed from its place, it is equally not *pasul*. The key question is how one conceptualizes the problem: is it that bread becomes *pasul* whenever it is outside the Mikdash, or only when it has been actively removed from its proper place? According to the second view, no removal occurred here — the Ohel Moed changed around it, but the bread itself remained where it belongs.

This second view is additionally supported by its core proof-text: *Lechem ha-tamid alav yihyeh*, which commands the bread to remain on the Shulchan constantly. Since it does remain there, it is in its proper place and does not become *pasul*.

One might ask how the bread could remain stable while being transported on the Shulchan amid all those elaborate stacking arrangements. This must have been among the miracles associated with the Mishkan. And if the journey lasted beyond a week — a genuine question — we recall that a Baraita already stated *afilu yamim harbeh*, that even for many days the bread on the Shulchan does not become *pasul* through *linah*.

### Rav Dimi Reframe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יַחֲנוּ כֵּן יִסָּעוּ״, לְאִידַּךְ גִּיסָא: מָה בַּחֲנִיָּיתוֹ, כִּי לֹא יָצָא מִמְּקוֹמוֹ – לָא מִיפְּסֵיל, אַף בִּנְסִיעָתוֹ, כִּי לֹא יָצָא מִמְּקוֹמוֹ – לָא מִיפְּסֵיל. וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב ״וְלֶחֶם הַתָּמִיד עָלָיו יִהְיֶה״? אֶלָּא, כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: בִּמְסוּדָּר – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, אֶלָּא כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בִּמְסוּלָּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But also** according to **the other** *amora*, who maintains the shewbread was not disqualified during the journeys, **isn't it written: "As they encamp, so shall they journey,"** indicating that the shewbread is disqualified when it leaves the Tabernacle during the journeys? The Gemara replies that according to this opinion, the juxtaposition of the encampments with the journeys is interpreted **in the other direction: Just as when** the Tabernacle is **encamped, if** the shewbread **does not leave its place** and remains on the Table it is **not disqualified, so too, when** the Tabernacle **journeys, if it does not leave its place** on the Table it is **not disqualified.** The Gemara asks: **But also** according to **the other** *amora*, who maintains the shewbread was disqualified during the journeys, **isn't it written: "And the continual bread shall remain upon it,"** indicating that the shewbread retains its sanctity as long as it is on the Table? **Rather, when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said** that the explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is different. With regard to a case **where** the shewbread **is arranged** on the Table, **everyone agrees** the shewbread is not disqualified during the journey. **Rather, when they disagree** it is in a case **where** the shewbread **is removed** from the Table before the journey.

The Gemara then says: actually, you are right, the verse *Ve-lechem ha-tamid alav yihyeh* is quite persuasive. The back-and-forth has become compelling enough that the Gemara opts to reframe the debate entirely. *Ki ata Rav Dimi* — when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reformulated the debate as follows: *Bimesudar kuli alma lo peligi* — when the bread is arranged on the Shulchan, nobody debates that it does not become *pasul* during travel. It is where it is supposed to be, even if the Ohel Moed has been dismantled. *Ella ki peligi bimesulak* — the debate concerns bread that has already been removed from the Shulchan.

How does this scenario arise? On Shabbat, the old bread is removed and the Kohanim are supposed to eat it that day. The new bread goes on immediately — there is no concern about the new bread. The question is about the bread that was removed: the Kohanim may eat it halachically through Saturday night. If the camp begins traveling Saturday night, when there is no longer an Ohel Moed standing, does that removed bread become *pasul* as *yotzei*? The one who says it becomes *pasul* holds that no special dispensation exists: just as when encamped, bread outside the Mishkan is *pasul*, so too when traveling. The one who says it does not become *pasul* holds that the verse *Venasah Ohel Moed betoch hamachanot* — and the Ohel Moed shall travel in the midst of the camps — indicates that even while traveling and not yet erected, the Ohel Moed retains its status as Ohel Moed.

### Practical Stability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר נִפְסָל, דִּכְתִיב: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יַחֲנוּ כֵּן יִסָּעוּ״, מָה בַּחֲנִיָּיתוֹ נִפְסָל בְּיוֹצֵא, אַף בִּנְסִיעָתוֹ מִיפְּסֵל בְּיוֹצֵא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ נִפְסָל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָסַע אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנָּסַע, אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their respective reasons: **The one who says** the shewbread **is disqualified** derives his opinion from a verse, **as it is written:** "Then the Tent of Meeting shall journey with the camp of the Levites in the midst of the camps; **as they encamp, so shall they journey,** every man in his place, by their banners" (Numbers 2:17). The verse juxtaposes the encampments with the journeys, indicating that **just as when** the Tabernacle is **encamped** the shewbread is **disqualified when** it **leaves** the Tabernacle courtyard, **so too, when** the Tabernacle **journeys** the shewbread is **disqualified when** it **leaves** the courtyard. **According to the one who says** the shewbread **is not disqualified** during the journeys, this is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "Then the Tent of Meeting shall journey,"** indicating that **even though it has journeyed it is** still considered **the Tent of Meeting,** and therefore the shewbread is not disqualified.

This discussion raises further questions. Once we are speaking of bread removed from the Shulchan, the same problem should apply to all *kodshei kodashim*, not just to the *lechem hapanim*. After all, *kodshei kodashim* may not leave the environs of the Ohel Moed — so where exactly is the Ohel Moed when the camp is moving? The Leviim traveled as groups in a particular order: Bnei Gershon and Bnei Merari went ahead to prepare the structure, while Bnei Kehat came later with the actual *keilim*. If they traveled in a line, it becomes quite difficult to identify where, physically, the space of the Ohel Moed would be during transit.

One could argue that if they traveled as a compact unit, as some opinions hold, it would be easier to define where one could eat *kodshei kodashim*. But the plain reading of the verses — which describe first one group traveling, then another — suggests a more linear arrangement. The Gemara itself does not resolve precisely where the physical space of the Ohel Moed is during travel, nor does it specify exactly how the Kohanim would have eaten their bread while on the move, though one might note that the Kohanim were associated with the section of the camp where they traveled and could have eaten during the journey itself.

The Gemara returns to the opposing view — that traveling the Ohel Moed means the status of Ohel Moed persists — and addresses the challenge posed by *Ka'asher yachanu kein yisa'u*. The response is *lishiteih d'idach*: the verse goes the other direction entirely. *Mah bi-chaniyato ki lo mafik lei lo misal* — when encamped, if you did not remove it from its place, it is not *pasul*; *af bi-nesiato ki lo mafik lei lo misal* — so too during travel, if it was not removed from its place, it is not *pasul*. And more fundamentally, since the Ohel Moed is still called Ohel Moed even in transit — *af al pi shenasah Ohel Moed hu* — there is no *yotzei* problem at all.

The two sides then debate the meaning of *Venasah Ohel Moed betoch hamachanot*. One says this verse is merely narrative, describing when the Ohel Moed travels, not granting it halachic status as Ohel Moed during travel. The other responds that the verse *Mimachaneh HaLeviim betoch hamachanot* already covers the narrative; the reason the Torah specifically calls it *Ohel Moed* here rather than simply the Levite camp is precisely to teach that it retains the status of Ohel Moed during travel.

## Kodashim & Masa'ot

### Zevachim Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: בִּשְׁעַת סִילּוּק מַסָּעוֹת, קָדָשִׁים נִפְסָלִין בְּיוֹצֵא, וְזָבִין וּמְצוֹרָעִין מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין חוּץ לִמְחִיצָתָן. מַאי לָאו אֲפִילּוּ לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים? לָא, בַּר מִלֶּחֶם הַפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* to the opinion that the shewbread was not disqualified during the journeys: **At the time of the dismantling** of the Tabernacle in order to commence the **journeys,** when the curtains surrounding the Tabernacle courtyard are removed, the **sacrificial** food **is disqualified** from being consumed, **as** it is considered to have **left** the Tabernacle courtyard. **And *zavim* and lepers are sent out of the partitions of their** camps; the *zavim* are sent out of the Levite camp, while lepers are sent out of the Israelite camp. **What, is it not** referring **even** to **the shewbread,** indicating that it is disqualified during the journeys? The Gemara answers: **No,** the *baraita* means that most sacrificial food is disqualified, **except for the shewbread.**

With the debate framed, the Gemara now introduces a Tosefta from Zevachim: *Besha'at siluk masa'ot* — when the camps were moving — *kodshim nifsalim b'yotzei* — the *kodashim* become *pasul* through *yotzei* — and yet *vezavim u'metzora'im mishtalhin chutz limechitzatam* — Zavim and Metzora'im must still be sent outside their respective boundaries. The Torah commands that a Metzora be sent outside all three camps and a Zav be kept outside *Machaneh Leviyah*, and this remains in effect even while traveling. So the Tosefta is indicating that while the camp retains the status of *Machaneh Leviyah* and Machaneh Yisrael during travel, it does not retain the status of the Mikdash itself — and therefore *kodashim* become *pasul*.

### Apparent Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה נַפְשָׁךְ, אִי ״אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ דַּוְקָא הוּא – אֲפִילּוּ קֳדָשִׁים נָמֵי, אִי ״אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ לָאו דַּוְקָא הוּא – אֲפִילּוּ לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Whichever** way **you** look at it, this explanation is difficult. **If** the phrase: "Then the **Tent of Meeting** shall journey," **is** meant **exactly,** i.e., literally, and it still has the status of the Tent of Meeting even during the journeys, then **even** other **sacrificial** food should not be disqualified during the journeys. **If** "then the **Tent of Meeting** shall journey," **is not** meant **exactly,** and it does not have the status of the Tent of Meeting during the journeys, then **even the shewbread** should be disqualified.

This creates a direct problem. If *kodashim* become *pasul* during travel because there is no longer an Ohel Moed, then there is no room for any debate about whether the *lechem hapanim* remains *kasher* when removed from the Shulchan. The bread is simply another case of *kodshei kodashim*: once the Ohel Moed is dismantled, it is outside its proper place and is *pasul*. So where does the debate go?

### Two Cases Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר: מָר אָמַר בִּמְסוּדָּר, וּמָר אֲמַר בִּמְסוּלָּק, וְלָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said** that there is actually no dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. This **Master,** who stated that the shewbread is not disqualified during the journeys, **stated** his ruling **with regard to** loaves **that are arranged** on the Table; **and** that **Master,** who stated that the shewbread is disqualified during the journeys, **stated** his ruling **with regard to** loaves **that were removed** from the Table. **And** the two *amora'im* **do not disagree,** as each was referring to a different case.

The Gemara answers: *Ella ki ata Ravin amar — mar amar bimesudar u'mar amar bimesulak velo paligi* — when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he reframed the dispute. There is in fact no debate at all. The one who said it does not become *pasul* was discussing the bread still on the Shulchan, where, as we argued from the verse *Lechem ha-tamid alav yihyeh*, it remains in its proper place and does not become *pasul*. The one who said it does become *pasul* was discussing the bread removed from the Shulchan, where, as the Tosefta shows, *kodshei kodashim* in general become *pasul* — and the removed *lechem hapanim* is no different. These were not competing positions but descriptions of two separate cases.

### Night Travel Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ יֵשׁ סִילּוּק מַסָּעוֹת בַּלַּיְלָה, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אֵין סִילּוּק מַסָּעוֹת בַּלַּיְלָה – אֵימַת מִדְּלֵי? לְצַפְרָא? מַאי אִירְיָא מִשּׁוּם יוֹצֵא? תִּיפּוֹק לִי דְּאִיפְּסִיל לֵיהּ בְּלִינָה!
>
> **Translation:** § The aforementioned *baraita* states that when the Tabernacle was dismantled the sacrificial food was disqualified, as it was considered to have left the Tabernacle courtyard. **Abaye said:** One may **conclude from** the *baraita* that **there** could **be** a case where the **dismantling** of the Tabernacle in order to commence the **journeys** would take place **at night.** This is a necessary conclusion, **as if it** should **enter your mind** to say that **there** could **not be** a case where the **dismantling** of the Tabernacle in order to commence the **journeys** would take place **at night, when would** the curtains surrounding the Tabernacle **be removed?** If they were removed right away **in the morning, why** was the sacrificial food disqualified **specifically due to** the fact that it **left** the Tabernacle courtyard? **I may derive that it was disqualified** because of the fact that it was **left overnight.**

From this reframing, Abaye draws an inference: *Shema mina yesh siluk masa'ot balaila* — from this entire debate, we learn that the camps would travel at night. Why? If they never traveled at night, the question about the removed bread would never arise. The bread is taken off the Shulchan on Shabbat; the Kohanim are supposed to eat it that day. If the camp could only begin moving in the morning — Sunday morning at the earliest following a Shabbat departure — the bread would already be *pasul belina*, having remained overnight in a state of *kedushah* without being placed back on the Shulchan. The *yotzei* question would be moot, preempted by the *linah* disqualification. The fact that the Amoraim debated whether the bread becomes *pasul beyotzei* — not *pasul belina* — proves they understood that the camp could depart Saturday night, when the bread was still *kasher* but the Ohel Moed had been dismantled.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, ״לָלֶכֶת יוֹמָם וָלָיְלָה״ כְּתִיב! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דַּעֲקוּר בִּימָמָא, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא עֲקוּר בִּימָמָא – בְּלֵילְיָא לָא מָצוּ עָקְרִי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: It is **obvious** that the Tabernacle could be dismantled at night, as it **is written:** "And the Lord went before them by day in a pillar of cloud, to lead them the way; and by night in a pillar of fire, to give them light; **that they might go by day and by night"** (Exodus 13:21). The Gemara answers that Abaye's observation is necessary **lest you say** that **this statement,** that they would travel at night, applies only in a case **where** the Jewish people **dismantled** their camp and began to journey **by day,** in which case they would continue to travel at night. **But** in a case **where they did not dismantle** their camp **by day they could not dismantle** the camp and leave **at night.** Abaye therefore **teaches us** that they could set out even at night.

The Gemara objects: *Peshita* — obviously they could travel at night! The verse *lalekhet yomam valaila* — to travel by day and by night — says so explicitly. The Gemara replies: one might have thought that the verse means only that once a journey was begun by day, it could be continued through the night; but perhaps the camp could not initiate a journey at night. Therefore, from the debate itself we learn that the Amoraim understood the camp could begin traveling at night as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: הוּגְלְלוּ הַפָּרֹכוֹת, הוּתְּרוּ זָבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִין לִיכָּנֵס לְשָׁם. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הָא – רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The aforementioned *baraita* teaches that even when the Tabernacle was dismantled, *zavim* and lepers were sent outside the camp, as the sanctity of both the Levite camp and the Israelite camp remained intact even while the Tabernacle was dismantled. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita*: Once **the curtain** surrounding the Tabernacle courtyard **was rolled up, the *zavim* and** the **lepers were permitted to enter into that** area where the Tabernacle had stood. This indicates that the camps did not retain their sacred status once the Tabernacle was dismantled. **Rav Ashi said:** This is **not difficult; this** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer** and **that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

The Gemara then raises a further challenge: *Huglelu haparochet* — when the Parochet of the Kodesh HaKodashim was rolled up, *hutar l'zavim u'l'metzora'im likanes lesham* — Zavim and Metzora'im were permitted to enter. This implies that once the Parochet was removed, the Ohel Moed entirely lost its status, and not even the *machaneh* boundaries remained — Zavim and Metzora'im no longer needed to be expelled. Yet the earlier Tosefta said that during travel, even with *kodashim* becoming *pasul*, Zavim and Metzora'im still had to remain outside their respective boundaries. This is a direct contradiction. One might also ask why it is specifically the rolling up of the Parochet that triggers this loss of status — why not the dismantling of the courtyard walls, or the removal of the Aron?

### Rabbi Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל דָּחֲקוּ זָבִין וּמְצוֹרָעִין וְנִכְנְסוּ לַעֲזָרָה בְּפֶסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וִישַׁלְּחוּ מִן הַמַּחֲנֶה כׇּל צָרוּעַ וְכׇל זָב וְכֹל טָמֵא לָנָפֶשׁ״, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁטְּמֵאֵי מֵתִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין – זָבִין וּמְצוֹרָעִין מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין. אֵין טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין – אֵין זָבִים וּמְצוֹרָעִין מִשְׁתַּלְּחִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** One **might** have thought that if ***zavim* and lepers pushed** their way in **and entered the Temple courtyard during** the sacrifice of **a Paschal offering that is brought in** a state of **impurity,** i.e., when the majority of the nation are ritually impure due to contact with a corpse, that perhaps the *zavim* and lepers are liable. In other words, one **might** have thought that since it is prohibited for *zavim* and lepers to enter even under such circumstances, **they would be liable** to receive excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*]. Therefore, **the verse states: "That they send out from the camp any leper and any *zav* and anyone impure by reason of a corpse"** (Numbers 5:2). The verse indicates that **at a time when those who are impure** with impurity imparted **by a corpse are sent out** of the Temple, ***zavim* and lepers are** also **sent out** of the Temple and are liable to receive *karet* if they enter it. But when **those who are impure** with impurity imparted **by a corpse are not sent out** of the Temple, ***zavim* and lepers are** also **not sent out,** i.e., they are not liable to receive *karet* if they enter the Temple.

The Gemara resolves this through Rav Ashi: *Ha Rabbi Eliezer, ha Rabbanan* — these two statements reflect a dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages. A Baraita is cited in which Rabbi Eliezer addresses the following scenario: if, during a *Pesach ha-ba be-tum'ah* — a communal Pesach offering brought in a state of **tum'at meit** — Zavim and Metzora'im forced their way into the Azarah, *dachaku*, are they liable? The Torah did not extend them the license granted to *tamei meit* on such occasions. Rabbi Eliezer derives from the verse *V'yishalhu min ha-machaneh kol tzarua ve-chol zav ve-chol tamei la-nefesh* — send out from the camp all those afflicted with *tzara'at*, every Zav, and every person *tamei* for a dead body — that the expulsion of Zavim and Metzora'im is conditional on the expulsion of *tamei meitim*: *besha'at she-tme'ei meitim mishtalhin zavim u-metzora'im mishtalhin; ein tme'ei meitim mishtalhin ein zavim u-metzora'im mishtalhin* — when *tamei meitim* are sent out, Zavim and Metzora'im must also be sent out; when *tamei meitim* are not sent out, Zavim and Metzora'im need not be sent out either.

This is directly applicable to the *siluk machanot*. When the camp travels, what is missing? Machaneh Shechinah — the Ohel Moed no longer stands. A *tamei meit* is only prohibited from Machaneh Shechinah itself — this is derived from the fact that Moshe carried the bones of Yosef *immo bi-mchitzato*, within the Levite camp, demonstrating that a *tamei meit* may be in *Machaneh Leviyah*. Since during travel there is no Machaneh Shechinah, *tamei meitim* are not being sent anywhere — and by Rabbi Eliezer's principle, therefore Zavim and Metzora'im need not be sent out either. This is precisely the position of the Baraita that says *huglelu haparochet* removes all obligations.

The Sages, by contrast, hold that the obligation to expel Zavim and Metzora'im is not contingent on the status of *tamei meitim* in the same way. Even without Machaneh Shechinah, the status of *Machaneh Leviyah* and Machaneh Yisrael remains intact and the expulsion requirements remain in force — which is why the Tosefta from Zevachim says that even during travel, Zavim and Metzora'im must be sent outside their boundaries.

### Three Purity Levels

*[Continued from above]*

The three levels of *tum'ah* restrictions operate as follows. A *tamei meit*, despite contracting one of the more far-reaching forms of *tum'ah* — lasting seven days, requiring the *Parah Adumah*, and transferring with unusual severity — is nonetheless prohibited only from Machaneh Shechinah, the Mikdash itself. This is because the *tum'ah* is contracted externally, from contact with death, and is therefore seen as less definitively altering the person's status. A Zav, by contrast, is prohibited from *Machaneh Leviyah* — equivalent in contemporary terms to Har HaBayit — but may remain within Machaneh Yisrael, i.e., Yerushalayim. A Metzora is expelled from all three camps entirely.

The deeper question this raises is whether the status of Machaneh Yisrael as a place where *kodashim kalim* may be eaten is itself dependent on the presence of the Mikdash — functioning, as it were, as the suburbs of the Mikdash. If so, once the Ohel Moed is dismantled during travel, perhaps even *kodashim kalim* would become *pasul*, since there are no longer any operative suburbs. On the other hand, the obligation to expel *tme'im* might be an intrinsic feature of the camp's status, independent of the Mikdash's presence. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages turns precisely on this distinction.

## Bread Preparation

### Mixed Location View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַחַת שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, וְאַחַת לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים – לִישָׁתָן וַעֲרִיכָתָן בַּחוּץ, וַאֲפִיָּיתָן בִּפְנִים, וְאֵינָן דּוֹחוֹת אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם בִּפְנִים. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם הֱוֵי רָגִיל לוֹמַר שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים כְּשֵׁרוֹת בַּעֲזָרָה, וּכְשֵׁרוֹת בְּבֵית פָּאגֵי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **both the two loaves and the shewbread, the kneading of their** dough **and the forming of their** loaves take place **outside** the Temple courtyard, **but their baking** takes place **inside** the Temple courtyard. **And their** preparation **does not override Shabbat. Rabbi Yehuda says: All of the procedures** involved in **their** preparation take place **inside** the courtyard. **Rabbi Shimon says:** One **should always be accustomed to say** that **the two loaves and the shewbread are fit** if they were prepared **in the Temple courtyard and they are** also **fit** if they were prepared **in Beit Pagei,** outside the Temple Mount, as he maintains that they may be baked outside the Temple courtyard.

We now turn to the next Mishnah, which addresses both *shtei halechem* and *lechem hapanim* together — two communal *menachos* that share the feature of containing no oil, which becomes significant shortly. The Mishnah states: *Lishatan va'arichatan bachutz* — the kneading and shaping of both were done outside the Beit HaMikdash, meaning they had not yet acquired *kedushat haguf* at that stage. *Ve'afiyatan mibifnim* — but their baking was done inside the Beit HaMikdash. The Mishnah's assumption is that the oven was a *kli sharet* located in the Azarah. Since there is no oil in these offerings, there is no *kli sharet* involved in the measuring — the *midot lach*, the liquid measures, were sanctified, but here only flour and water are used, and water was not measured. Flour uses only dry measures. So the entire process up to baking could be conducted without encountering a *kli sharet*. The baking stage, however, in the Mikdash oven, would confer *kedushat haguf*. *Ve'einan dochot et haShabbat* — and the baking does not override Shabbat, since one simply bakes before Shabbat.

### Lina Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא גּוּפַאּ קַשְׁיָא: אָמַרְתָּ: לִישָׁתָן וַעֲרִיכָתָן בַּחוּץ – אַלְמָא מִדַּת יָבֵשׁ לֹא נִתְקַדְּשָׁה, וַאֲפִיָּיתָן בִּפְנִים – אַלְמָא מִדַּת יָבֵשׁ נִתְקַדְּשָׁה! אָמַר רַבָּה: הִקְשָׁה אָדָם קָשֶׁה, שֶׁהוּא קָשֶׁה כַּבַּרְזֶל, וּמַנּוּ – רַב שֵׁשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that according to the opinion of the first *tanna* the two loaves and the shewbread are kneaded and shaped outside the Temple courtyard, but they are baked inside the courtyard. The Gemara comments: **This itself** is **difficult.** The Gemara elaborates: **You said** initially that **the kneading of their** dough **and the forming of their** loaves take place **outside** the Temple courtyard. One can infer from this that **apparently,** the **dry measure,** i.e., the vessel used in the Temple for measuring dry substances, e.g., flour, **was not consecrated.** If the dry measure sanctified the items placed in them, the flour brought for the two loaves and the shewbread would already be sanctified, and these loaves could not be kneaded and shaped outside the courtyard. And the mishna subsequently states that **their baking** takes place **inside** the Temple courtyard. One can infer from this that **apparently,** the **dry measure was consecrated.** If the flour had not been sanctified by being placed in the measuring vessel, why must the loaves be baked inside the Temple courtyard? **Rabba says: A formidable man, who is** as **tough as iron, raised** this **difficulty. And who is** that Sage? This is referring to **Rav Sheshet.**

This is precisely where the Gemara's problem lies. *Alma tanur mekadesh* — if the oven sanctifies, then baking before Shabbat means the bread acquires *kedushat haguf* on Friday. Now it is Shabbat, the bread has been sanctified, and it has not yet been placed on the Shulchan. It therefore becomes *pasul belina* — disqualified by having remained overnight in its sanctified state without being put in its proper place. Once the oven confers *kedushah*, you cannot bake before Shabbat and then place it on the Shulchan on Shabbat; the bread would be *pasul* by then. So if the oven is a *kli sharet*, the baking must occur on Shabbat — which means the baking *does* override Shabbat, in direct contradiction to the Mishnah's statement. This is the strong question attributed to Rav Sheshet, which Rabbah praises: *Hiksha adam kasheh shehu kasheh kevarzel* — a hard question asked by someone as strong as iron.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִי קַשְׁיָא – הָא קַשְׁיָא: וַאֲפִיָּיתָן בִּפְנִים, אַלְמָא תַּנּוּר מְקַדֵּשׁ, וְאֵין דּוֹחוֹת אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת – אִיפַּסְלָה בְּלִינָה! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הִקְשָׁה אָדָם קָשֶׁה, שֶׁהוּא קָשֶׁה כַּבַּרְזֶל, וּמַנּוּ – רַב שֵׁשֶׁת. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּלְמָא מַאי ״מִבִּפְנִים״ – בִּמְקוֹם זְרִיזִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** the mishna **is difficult, this is difficult:** The mishna states that **the baking** of the two loaves and the shewbread takes place **inside** the courtyard. **Evidently,** the **oven sanctifies** that which is baked inside it. And the mishna subsequently states that kneading, shaping, and baking the loaves **does not override Shabbat.** If these procedures were performed before Shabbat in a vessel that sanctifies them, the loaves would be **disqualified by** virtue of the fact that they were **left overnight** without having been placed on the Table. **Rather, Rava says: A formidable man, who is** as **tough as iron, raised** this **difficulty. And who is** that Sage? This is referring to **Rav Sheshet.** **Rav Ashi said: What** is the **difficulty? Perhaps** the oven does not sanctify the loaves that are baked in it, and therefore the two loaves and the shewbread are not disqualified by being left overnight. And accordingly, **what is** the meaning of the statement: Their baking takes place **inside?** This is not referring to inside the Temple courtyard. Rather, it means they are baked **in a place** where there are priests who are **vigilant** in their efforts, who will supervise the loaves as they are baked and ensure they do not become leavened.

Rav Ashi attempts a resolution: *Dilma mai bifnim? Bemakom zerizim* — perhaps "inside" does not literally mean within the Azarah, but rather in a place where zealous, scrupulous personnel are overseeing the process. On this reading, the oven is not a *kli sharet* and does not confer *kedushah*; "inside" simply means under supervision — perhaps in the *Ezrat Nashim* or a side chamber — to ensure, for instance, that the *lechem hapanim* does not become *chametz*. This would explain why the baking does not override Shabbat: since the oven is not a *kli sharet*, no *kedushat haguf* is conferred at the baking stage, and the bread can be baked before Shabbat without any *linah* concern.

### Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי בְּרוּתָא הִיא, מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי אֲפִיָּיה בָּעֵינַן זְרִיזִין – לִישָׁה וַעֲרִיכָה נָמֵי בָּעֵינַן זְרִיזִין, וְאִי לִישָׁה וַעֲרִיכָה לָא בָּעֵינַן זְרִיזִין – אֲפִיָּיה נָמֵי לָא בָּעֵינַן זְרִיזִין, אֶלָּא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי בְּרוּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And this** statement attributed **to Rav Ashi is an error [*baruta*],** as **whichever** way **you** look at it, it is difficult: **If we require vigilant** priests for the **baking** of the two loaves and the shewbread, **we** should **also require vigilant** priests for the **kneading and forming** of the loaves. **And if we do not require vigilant** priests for the **kneading and forming** of the loaves, **we** should **also not require vigilant** priests for the **baking.** Rav Ashi's explanation does not explain why the mishna differentiates between the kneading and forming of the loaves on the one hand, and their baking on the other hand. **Rather,** the statement attributed **to Rav Ashi is an error.**

The Gemara dismisses this: *Ha deRav Ashi bruta hi* — what Rav Ashi said is simply wrong. *Avi afiyah be'inan zerizim lishvira nami be'inan zerizim* — if zealous overseers are needed during the baking to prevent *chametz* or ensure proper procedure, the same concern applies equally during the kneading and shaping. *Ve'ei l'lisha va'aricha lo be'inan zerizim, afiyah nami lo be'inan zerizim* — and if you trust people without zealous oversight during the kneading, you cannot claim the baking requires a different standard. There is no principled basis for distinguishing the two stages in this way. The Gemara therefore concludes that Rav Ashi's interpretation is untenable; *bifnim* must mean literally inside, and the difficulty remains. Tosafot notes — not in this immediate context, but in an earlier passage — that the Gemara's resolution is essentially to recognize that the line *ve'einan dochot et haShabbat* in the Mishnah represents a different opinion from the voice that said baking occurs inside. Specifically, that line reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon, whose view appears at the end of the Mishnah, as will now become clear. It is, in effect, a misplaced line belonging to Rabbi Shimon's position — *mi sheshana zu lo shana zu*.

### Rabbi Yehuda: All Inside

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Yehuda says *kol ma'aseihen mibifnim* — all stages of preparation were done inside the Mikdash. He presumably holds that even the dry measures were sanctified *klei sharet*, so that the entire process from kneading onward occurred within the Mikdash and conferred *kedushah* from the outset.

### Rabbi Shimon: All Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם הֱוֵי רָגִיל לוֹמַר שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים כְּשֵׁרוֹת בַּעֲזָרָה, וּכְשֵׁרוֹת בְּבֵית פָּאגֵי. וּשְׁנֵיהֶן מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ, ״וְהוּא דֶּרֶךְ חֹל אַף כִּי הַיּוֹם יִקְדַּשׁ בַּכֶּלִי״. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: בַּחוֹל אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ דְּקָא אָפוּ לֵיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: דֶּרֶךְ חוֹל קָא אָפֵיתוּ לֵיהּ? ״אַף כִּי הַיּוֹם יִקְדַּשׁ בַּכֶּלִי״, אִיפְּסִיל לֵיהּ בְּלִינָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר: בְּשַׁבָּת אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ דְּקָא אָפוּ לֵיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא דֶּרֶךְ חוֹל בָּעֵיתוּ לְמִיעְבְּדֵיהּ?! מִידֵּי תַּנּוּר מְקַדֵּשׁ?! שֻׁלְחָן הוּא דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says: All the procedures** involved in the preparation of the two loaves and the shewbread take place **inside** the Temple courtyard, whereas Rabbi Shimon maintains that even their baking may take place outside the courtyard. **Rabbi Abbahu bar Kahana says: Both** Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon derived their opinions from **the same verse,** which **they interpreted homiletically.** When David fled from King Saul he came to Nov, where he requested bread from Ahimelech the priest. Ahimelech replied: "There is no non-sacred bread under my hand, but there is sacred bread" (I Samuel 21:5), i.e., the shewbread. David then said to Ahimelech: **"But it is a non-sacred manner, and yet it shall be consecrated today in the vessel"** (I Samuel 21:6). Both Sages understand David's response to be a halakhic critique: **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that David **found** the priests **baking** the shewbread **on a weekday.** David **said to them:** Why **are you baking** the shewbread in **a non-sacred manner,** i.e., on a weekday, rather than on Shabbat? **"Yet it shall be consecrated today in the vessel,"** i.e., it will be consecrated today when it is baked in the oven, and **it will be disqualified** tomorrow **because** it will have been **left overnight.** Rabbi Yehuda infers from David's criticism that the shewbread must be baked on Shabbat in the Temple courtyard, as it is consecrated in the oven. **Rabbi Shimon holds** that David **found** the priests **baking** the shewbread **on Shabbat.** David **said to them: Aren't you required to prepare it** in **a non-sacred manner,** i.e., on a weekday? **Does the oven consecrate** the shewbread? No, **it is** the **Table that consecrates** it when the shewbread is placed there. The loaves are therefore not disqualified by being left overnight.

Rabbi Shimon states the opposite: *Shtei halechem u'Lechem HaPanim ksheira ba'azarah u'ksheira b'Beit Pagi* — whether prepared in the Azarah or in Beit Pagi, which Rashi explains as referring to all of Yerushalayim, or by some readings even outside Yerushalayim entirely, both were valid. On his view, not even the oven was a *kli sharet*. The sanctification of the *lechem hapanim* occurs only when it is placed on the Shulchan; the sanctification of the *shtei halechem* occurs through the slaughter and blood-sprinkling of the *Kivsei Atzeret*. Until that point, the bread has no *kedushat haguf* regardless of where or how it was prepared. This is why, on Rabbi Shimon's view, there is no *linah* problem and no need for the baking to override Shabbat — the bread acquires no *kedushah* from the oven at all.

We are thus left with three positions: the Mishnah's first opinion holds that the dry measures were not sanctified but the oven was, so the process began outside and was completed inside, and on that view the baking does override Shabbat (the *ve'einan dochot* line not belonging to this voice). Rabbi Yehuda holds that even the dry measures were sanctified and everything was done inside. Rabbi Shimon holds that nothing was sanctified until the bread reached the Shulchan or the *korban*, and the entire preparation could be done outside.

## David & Lechem HaPanim

### Same Verse Source

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Abbahu bar Kahana observes: *Ushneihem mikra echad darshu* — both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon derive their positions from the same verse. The verse is *V'hu *derech chol* af ki hayom yikdash bakeli* — spoken in the episode in which David HaMelech came to Nov, the city of the Kohanim, and asked for bread.

### Biblical Narrative

*[Continued from above]*

The episode unfolds as follows. David says to the Kohen: *Ma yesh tachat yadcha chamisha lechem tena beyadi o hanimtza* — give me five loaves of bread, or whatever you have. *Vaya'an haKohen et David vayomar ein lechem chol el tachat yadi* — the Kohen answers that he has no *lechem chol*, no ordinary unsanctified bread. *Ki im lechem kodesh yesh* — only sacred bread. *Im nishm'ru hane'arim ach me'isha* — if the young men have kept apart from women, they may have it. David responds that his men are pure. *V'hu *derech chol* af ki hayom yikdash bakeli* — and the matter of *derech chol*: even if today it would be sanctified through the vessel. The next verse clarifies: *Vayiten lo haKohen kodesh ki lo hayah sham lechem ki im *lechem hapanim* hamusarim milifnei Hashem lasum lechem cham beyom hilakcho* — the Kohen gave him sacred bread, for there was no bread there except for the *lechem hapanim* that had been removed from before God, to be replaced by hot bread on the day it was taken. This is also the source for the teaching that the *lechem hapanim* remained fresh like the day it was placed on the Shulchan.

### Rav Yehuda Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ דְּבִשְׁעַת אֲפִיָּיה אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתֵּן לוֹ הַכֹּהֵן קֹדֶשׁ כִּי לֹא הָיָה שָׁם לֶחֶם כִּי אִם לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים הַמּוּסָרִים מִלִּפְנֵי ה׳״! אֶלָּא מַאי ״דֶּרֶךְ חֹל״, דְּקָא אָמַר לְהוּ? – הָכִי קָא אָמְרוּ לֵיהּ: לֵיכָּא לֶחֶם, כִּי אִם ״לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים הַמּוּסָרִים מִלִּפְנֵי ה׳״. אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא הַאי, דְּכֵיוָן דִּנְפַק לֵיהּ מִמְּעִילָה דֶּרֶךְ חוֹל הוּא, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ הַאיְךְ נָמֵי דְּ״הַיּוֹם יִקְדַּשׁ בַּכֶּלִי״ – הַבוּ לֵיהּ דְּלֵיכוֹל, מְסוּכָּן הוּא. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּגְמָרָא פְּלִיגִי. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם הֱוֵי רָגִיל לוֹמַר שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים כְּשֵׁרוֹת בַּעֲזָרָה וּכְשֵׁרוֹת אַבֵּית פָּאגֵי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how can you say that** David **found** the priests **at the time of baking? But isn't it** subsequently **written: "And the priest gave him sacred bread, for there was no bread there but the shewbread that was taken from before the Lord"** (I Samuel 21:7)? This indicates that David received shewbread that had already been on the Table, not loaves that had just been baked. **Rather, what** is the meaning of the statement: "But it is **a non-sacred manner,** and yet it shall be consecrated today in the vessel," **which** David **said to** the priests? The Gemara replies: **This is** what the priests **said to him:** There is **no bread** here **except "the shewbread that was taken from before the Lord,"** and the shewbread is prohibited for consumption by non-priests. David **said to** the priests: **It is not necessary** to say that it is permitted for me to eat **this** shewbread, which has already been removed from the Table. This is **because** the frankincense placed in the bowls that were on the Table has been burned. **Since** the shewbread **has been removed from** having the status of items to which the *halakhot* of **misuse** of consecrated property apply, **it is** considered **a non-sacred matter,** i.e., permitted to priests for consumption. **But even the other** shewbread, **which "shall be consecrated today in the vessel,"** i.e., the shewbread placed on the Table today, **you should give him,** i.e., you should give me, **to eat.** This is because **he is,** i.e., I am, **dangerously** ill, being utterly famished, and a non-priest may eat sacrificial food in a life-threatening situation. **And** with regard to **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon,** their dispute does not stem from the interpretation of the verses. Rather, **they disagree with regard to a tradition,** as they had divergent traditions as to whether or not the oven consecrates the shewbread. The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches** that **Rabbi Shimon says:** One **should always be accustomed to say** that **the two loaves and the shewbread are fit** if they were prepared **in the Temple courtyard and they are** also **fit** if they were prepared **in Beit Pagei.** The term: One should always be accustomed to say, indicates that Rabbi Shimon's opinion is based on an oral tradition and not on the interpretation of a verse. The Gemara concludes: One may **conclude from** the mishna that this is the case.

Rabbi Yehuda reads the verse as follows: *Bechol ashkecheinhu d'ka-afei lehu* — David found that they had baked the bread on a weekday, i.e., without it being in the Mikdash. David then said to the Kohen: *Derech chol ka-afeitu leh?* — you baked this as *chol*, in a non-sanctified way? *Af ki hayom *yikdash bakeli* v'ipsulei belinah* — it was already sanctified through the holy vessels when baked, and therefore it has now become *pasul belina*, since it sat overnight after having been sanctified in the oven. David's point is that the bread was always sanctified by the sacred vessel at the time of baking — which is precisely Rabbi Yehuda's position that the oven is a *kli sharet* and confers *kedushah* from the moment of baking.

### Rabbi Shimon Reading

*[Continued from above]*

Rabbi Shimon reads it differently: *BeShabbat ashkecheinhu d'ka-afei lehu* — they had baked it on Shabbat. David said to them: *Lo *derech chol* be'itu lema'avdeih?* — could you not have done it *derech chol*, baked it on a weekday using non-sanctified vessels? Since on Rabbi Shimon's view the bread does not acquire *kedushah* from the oven, there is no need to bake it on Shabbat at all. Baking on a weekday in unsanctified vessels would have been entirely sufficient, with sanctification occurring later upon placement on the Shulchan. David's complaint is thus that they violated Shabbat unnecessarily.

### Me'ila Issue

*[Continued from above]*

The Gemara then questions whether this reading of the narrative is correct. *Umi matsit amart d'beshat afeiyah ashkecheinhu?* — was David present when the bread was being baked? The verse says the bread had already been *hamusarim milifnei Hashem* — removed from before God — which implies it had long since been baked and was no longer on the Shulchan. David was not speaking to a situation of fresh baking. Rather, the Gemara clarifies, what David was addressing was the *me'ila* question. The Kohen had told him he had *lechem kodesh* — sacred bread — that had just come off the Shulchan. David responded: *Lo miba'ei hai* — I have no question about that bread. *Dekivan deka nafik lei mime'ila* — since this bread has left the realm of *me'ila*: once *kodashim* become fit for human consumption by *Kohanim*, the prohibition of *me'ila* — the misappropriation of sacred property — no longer applies. Thus, *derech chol hu* — it is now in the domain of ordinary human use, and giving it to non-Kohanim presents no *me'ila* concern. There remains the separate prohibition of a *zar* eating *kodesh*, but *me'ila* is not at issue.

### Pikuach Nefesh

*[Continued from above]*

Having addressed the bread already removed from the Shulchan, David continues: *Afilu ha'ich nami d'hayom yikdash bakeli* — even the bread that was sanctified today through the vessels and placed on the Shulchan today, which does still carry *me'ila* concerns — *havu li d'lechol*, give it to me as well. Why? *Mesukan hu* — because there is a matter of *sakanat nefashot*, danger to life. *Pikuach nefesh* overrides all. David's statement thus operates on two levels: better to give the bread already off the Shulchan, where no *me'ila* applies; but even the fully sanctified bread currently on the Shulchan may be given when lives are at stake.

The Gemara concludes that the verses in this passage are therefore not about the method of sanctification — whether through the oven, the dry measures, or the Shulchan — but about the *me'ila* question and the principle of *pikuach nefesh*. And as for the actual dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, the Gemara notes: *Tnan nami d'katani, Rabbi Shimon omer, le'olam hevei ragil lomar...* — Rabbi Shimon's formulation, "teach yourself always to say," indicates that he is transmitting a tradition rather than expounding a verse. This in turn confirms that Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon are not engaged in exegetical debate about those verses but are transmitting different received traditions about how the bread was prepared. *Shema mina* — so we learn.

The three positions therefore stand as distinct traditions: the first opinion that the dry measures were not sanctified but the oven was, such that preparation began outside and concluded inside; Rabbi Yehuda that all preparation was conducted inside the Mikdash with sanctified dry measures throughout; and Rabbi Shimon that the entire preparation could be performed outside, with sanctification occurring only upon placement on the Shulchan or the offering of the *Kivsei Atzeret*.
