# Menachot 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kedusha Transfer

### Sanctification Types

We begin slightly back on 108b, at the passage before the mishna: *רצה יביא בדמיהם שנים* — one may bring two animals with the redemption money. The context is the transfer of kedusha: when a person sanctifies an animal and it subsequently develops a mum, the kedusha is transferred through redemption, and the proceeds may then be applied to other animals for an olah.

Rav Menashya bar Zavid said in the name of Rav: this applies only when one says *שור זה עולה* — "this ox is an olah." In that formulation, the kedusha adheres directly to the animal. If the animal develops a mum, one redeems it and may apply the proceeds to any other suitable animal for an olah — even a different species or a different number of animals. However, if one says *שור זה עלי עולה* — "this ox is upon me as an olah" — then *הוקבע*, it is fixed. The phrasing creates a personal obligation, not merely a sanctification of the object, and one would therefore be required to bring a single ox of the same kind when transferring the kedusha.

### Alai Lehavio Read

The Gemara is not satisfied with this distinction. It asks: *ודילמא עלי להביאו קאמר* — perhaps even when one says "upon me," one means "upon me to bring this particular one." That is, the two formulations are being conflated: saying "this ox upon me as an olah" occupies a middle ground between the purely personal obligation ("I obligate myself to bring an ox") and the direct sanctification ("this ox is an olah"). But in either case, once the animal develops a mum and can no longer be brought, the obligation cannot be satisfied by that animal anyway, and thus the flexibility of transfer should apply regardless.

The Gemara therefore reformulates the teaching. Rav Menashya bar Zavid said in the name of Rav: the rule permitting flexible transfer applies whether one says *שור זה עולה* or *שור זה עלי עולה* — in both cases, since the specific animal is named and cannot be brought once blemished, the kedusha may be transferred to whatever one wishes. The process is not a direct exchange but a two-step redemption: one first redeems the blemished animal with money and then designates that money for a new animal.

### Animal vs. Value

The case where transfer is restricted is a different formulation altogether: *שור זה ודמיו עלי עולה* — "this ox and its monetary value I accept upon myself to bring as an olah." Here, by explicitly including the value of the animal in the obligation, one has undertaken to bring a korban equivalent in value to a single ox. If the animal develops a mum, the kedusha must be transferred to something of comparable value — not to two sheep or two oxen of lesser value than the original — because the obligation has been fixed both to the animal and to its value. This concludes the final mishna of the previous discussion, and we move on to a new topic.

## Ambiguous Designation

### Two vs. Three Animals

The next mishna introduces a related but distinct question. If someone says *אחד מכבשי הקדש* — "one of my sheep is hekdesh" — or similarly for oxen, but does not specify which animal, how do we interpret the declaration?

If the person has two animals, the mishna rules: *הגדול שבהן הקדש* — the larger one is hekdesh. The assumption is that a person who sanctifies does so generously, giving the more valuable gift. If the person has three animals, the mishna rules: *בינוני שבהן הקדש* — the middle one is hekdesh. This is initially puzzling. If we assume generous intent, why not always assume the most expensive? The Gemara will need to address this tension.

The mishna then introduces another case: if one explicitly specified which animal was meant but has forgotten — or if one's father, on his deathbed, said he had sanctified one of his animals but died before clarifying which — and the person has a number of animals in the herd, the ruling is *הגדול שבהן הקדש* — the largest is hekdesh. This appears to contradict the three-animal case and requires explanation.

The Gemara observes: *אלמא מקדיש בעין יפה מקדיש* — from the two-animal case we see that one who sanctifies does so with a generous eye, giving the most valuable. But from the three-animal case: *אלמא מקדיש בעין רעה מקדיש* — one who sanctifies does so with a stingy eye, giving only the middle one. How are these reconciled?

### Shmuel's Explanation

Shmuel resolves the tension: *חיישינן אף לבינונית דלגבי קטן עין יפה הוא* — we are also concerned for the possibility that the middle one was intended, because the middle one is generous relative to the worst. The ruling does not mean we are certain the person meant only the middle animal. Rather, when there are three animals, the statement is genuinely ambiguous — we cannot be certain whether the person meant the second or the first. What we can say is that the person meant something better than the worst, and therefore the smallest is excluded. The middle animal and the best animal are both live possibilities.

### Blemish Technique

This raises the practical question: *היכי עביד* — what does one actually do? Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said: *ממתין לו עד שיומם* — one waits until the middle animal develops a blemish. Once it is blemished, one transfers its kedusha to the larger animal: *ומחליא לקדושתיה אגדול*. One then brings the large animal as the korban.

The logic is elegant. If the original declaration referred to the middle animal, then now that it has a mum, one may transfer its kedusha to the large animal and bring the large one. If the original declaration referred to the large animal, then the transfer is superfluous but harmless — one brings the large animal in any case. Either way, the large animal is brought.

One cannot simply exchange one animal directly for another, because the Torah prohibits *תמורה* — direct substitution of a sanctified animal: *לא ימיר אותו טוב ברע ורע בטוב*, and the Gemara understands *רע בטוב* to include even a blemished animal being replaced by an unblemished one. The workaround is indirect: one redeems the blemished animal with money and then designates the money for the new animal. This is entirely permissible.

Rav Nachman said in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: this entire discussion about the middle animal applies only when one says *אחד משורי הקדש* — "one of my oxen." But if one says *שור בשורי הקדש* — "an ox from my oxen" — the ruling is simply that the largest is hekdesh, because the phrase *תורא בתוראי קאמר* — you are saying "the choice ox of my oxen," and the most prominent member of the herd is implied.

The Gemara challenges this: surely the same reasoning should apply to the case of a house? If someone says *בית בביתי אני מוכר לך* — "I am selling you a house from among my houses" — one might think this means the finest house. Yet we rule that one may point to the attic (*עלייה*) and say that is what was sold. An attic is a valid *bayit* but is less desirable than the ground floor. So the phrase "a house from my houses" does not mean the best house. Why then does "an ox from my oxen" mean the best ox?

### Mixed-Up Animals

Before resolving the house case, the Gemara raises a further challenge. A *baraita* discusses a case where an ox designated as hekdesh becomes mixed up among other oxen so that one cannot identify which one was sanctified. The ruling is: *הגדול שבהן הקדש* — the largest is given to hekdesh. This seems analogous to the case of "an ox from my oxen," since in both situations one cannot isolate which animal is meant. Yet in the mixed-up case, additional steps are required: *וכולן ימכרו לצרכי עולות ודמיהן חולין* — all the remaining animals are sold to people who intend to use them for olot, while the proceeds of those sales may be kept by the original owner as ordinary funds.

The mechanism is as follows: suppose the sanctified ox was mixed with ten others. One brings the largest as the designated korban. But perhaps the original sanctified animal was not the largest. In that case, one sells the remaining nine to someone who will bring all of them as olot, ensuring that even if the actual sanctified animal is among the nine, it will be brought as a korban. One is not transferring kedusha away from those animals — they remain hekdesh — so the proceeds from selling them can be used as ordinary funds.

The Gemara resolves the tension by distinguishing between the two cases in the scope of their rulings. When one says "an ox from my oxen," one means the best, and one simply brings that animal without concern for the others. When an animal becomes mixed up in a herd and one cannot identify it, not only does one bring the best, but one must also ensure all the others are used for korbanot. They are textually parallel only in that both involve bringing the largest — not in all the surrounding details.

### House Among Houses

Returning to the house case: the Gemara cites a *baraita* that states if one says "I am selling you a house from my houses" and one of the houses subsequently collapses, one may point to the collapsed ruin and say that was the one sold. Similarly, if one says "I am selling you a slave from my slaves" and one of the slaves dies, one may say that was the slave sold. This demonstrates that in the context of a commercial sale, the seller retains the right to designate the worst item as the one transferred, and the buyer cannot demand better.

This is counterintuitive at first: one might have thought that "a house from my houses" implies the seller is presenting himself as owning many properties and the buyer should receive a proper one. But the Gemara explains:

### Seller's Prerogative

*שאני לוקח דיד בעל השטר על התחתונה* — a purchaser is different, because the one holding the document of sale always has the weaker hand. Until the sale is completed, the property belongs to the seller. The buyer, by producing a document of sale, is making a claim against the seller's property. Any ambiguity in the terms of the contract is resolved against the claimant — against the buyer. The buyer must prove that the contract refers to the better property; failing that, the seller may designate the lesser one.

This principle — *יד בעל השטר על התחתונה* — is a significant rule in contract law. It applies particularly to real estate and slaves, where the transfer is effected by the document or by money rather than by physical possession. Because the transaction can occur without the specific item being present, ambiguities of identity arise, and they are always resolved against the party seeking to extract.

The Gemara then adds: *השתא דאתית להכי* — now that we have established this principle, we can even explain the earlier case of the attic. Even if *עלייה* means the attic — the less desirable upper floor — the seller can legitimately point to it, because *יד בעל השטר על התחתונה*.

This is fundamentally different from hekdesh. In hekdesh, there is no party trying to extract property from another. The question is how we as a legal system choose to interpret the declarant's words. There the Gemara chooses to interpret in the direction of generosity — not necessarily the maximum possible generosity, as the three-animal case shows, but at least something better than the minimum.

This points to a broader theme running through the recent mishnayot: the distinction between trying to recover a speaker's subjective intent and making a legal determination about how to interpret ambiguous language. When we know what was said but not which option was meant, we read the language with a certain generosity — not necessarily maximally, but not minimally either. When no text survives — as in the final case of the mishna, where a father declared something before dying and no one knows what he said — we ask what he probably meant, and since people giving to hekdesh probably give their best, we assume the largest animal.

## Beit Chonyo Vows

### Olah in Beit Chonyo

The next mishna introduces an entirely different subject. *הרי עלי עולה יקריבנה במקדש* — someone says: I accept upon myself an olah, to bring it in the Beit HaMikdash. If such a person brings it in Beit Chonyo instead, *לא יצא* — the vow is not fulfilled. Beit Chonyo was an alternate Jewish place of sacrifice established in Alexandria, Egypt. We have independent historical corroboration of its existence from sources such as Philo and Josephus. Beyond not fulfilling the vow, the person also transgresses the biblical prohibition of *שחוטי חוץ* — bringing korbanot outside the Beit HaMikdash.

### Vow Fulfillment

The more striking part of the mishna follows: *הרי עלי עולה שיקריבנה בבית חוניו* — if someone says "I accept upon myself an olah that I will bring in Beit Chonyo," what then? The mishna says: *יקריבנה במקדש* — one should bring it in the Beit HaMikdash, and: *ואם הקריבה בבית חוניו יצא* — if one brought it in Beit Chonyo, one is yotzei.

This is striking. On one reading, the person has brought a korban outside the Beit HaMikdash, which is an act for which one is *חייב כרת* — yet the mishna frames the outcome with the word *יצא*, suggesting fulfillment. At minimum, the mishna conspicuously omits mentioning the karet liability. The Gemara will become harsher, but the mishna itself speaks very softly.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *אין זה עולה* — this is not an olah at all. One cannot make a binding vow to bring a korban in Beit Chonyo; such a vow is simply invalid, and there is nothing to fulfill and nothing to transgress.

The mishna then discusses the parallel nazirite case. *הריני נזיר יגלח במקדש* — if one vows to be a nazir and stipulates that the concluding rituals will take place in the Beit HaMikdash, but then brings the hair-cutting and korbanot in Beit Chonyo, *ולא יצא* — one has not fulfilled the nezirut. And if one vows nazirut specifying that the concluding rituals will take place in Beit Chonyo, but then performs them in the Beit HaMikdash, *יצא* — the nezirut is ended. But if one actually performs the rituals in Beit Chonyo: *יצא*. This is deeply puzzling, because nezirut can only be concluded by bringing the prescribed korbanot in the legitimate Beit HaMikdash. Rabbi Shimon again says: *אין זה נזיר* — no such vow of nezirut exists at all.

### Rav Hamnuna's Read

The Gemara challenges the Chachamim's ruling that one is *yotzei* by bringing in Beit Chonyo: *המיסת קטליה* — you have killed it, meaning that a korban brought outside the Beit HaMikdash has no status as a korban whatsoever.

Rav Hamnuna answers: *נעשה כאומר הרי עלי עולה על מנת שלא אתחייב באחריותה* — we reinterpret the vow as though the person said "I accept an olah upon myself on condition that I will not be liable for replacing it." Normally, if one sanctifies an animal for a korban and the animal dies or is lost, the obligation remains and one must bring a substitute. Here, by specifying Beit Chonyo, the person is in effect saying: my obligation extends only as far as the act of bringing this animal in Beit Chonyo. Once I have done that, even though halachically it does not constitute bringing a korban, my personal obligation is discharged. We thus reinterpret the word "Beit Chonyo" to delimit the scope of the obligation rather than to identify a legitimate place of sacrifice.

### Nazirite Difference

Rava challenges Rav Hamnuna's interpretation from the nazirite case. For a nazir, it is not just a matter of personal liability — *כמה דלא מייתי קרבנותיו לא מיתכשר* — until one brings the korbanot properly, the nazir status simply does not end. It is a matter of objective halachic standing, not personal obligation. Even if one could argue that a person's liability for the sacrifices ends upon performing the rituals in Beit Chonyo, the nazirite status itself cannot be terminated by an act that has no halachic validity. The mishna's statement that one is *yotzei* in the nazirite case cannot be explained by Rav Hamnuna's approach.

### Rava's Conditional Read

Rava offers an alternative interpretation of the entire mishna: *אדם זה לדורו נתכוון* — this person genuinely intended to give a gift to God. He is not acting in bad faith; he simply believes, perhaps as a diaspora Jew living in Egypt surrounded by fellow Jews who bring sacrifices to Beit Chonyo, that this is a legitimate way to fulfill the obligation. His vow should be interpreted as follows: *אי סגי בבית חוניו טרחנא* — if it works in Beit Chonyo, I will go to the trouble of bringing it there. *טפי לא מצינא לאיצטעורי* — but I am not able to go to the effort of traveling to Jerusalem.

On this reading, the vow is implicitly conditional: I intend this to be binding only if Beit Chonyo constitutes a valid place of sacrifice. Since it does not, the vow was never binding in the first place. When the mishna says *יצא*, it does not mean "fulfilled a binding vow" — it means there was never a vow to fulfill. And when it says that if one brings it in the Beit HaMikdash one is *yotzei*, the meaning is that if one chose to go to Jerusalem, that choice reveals that one was in fact prepared to fulfill the obligation properly, and the vow is retrospectively binding and fulfilled.

The same logic applies to the nazirite case: the person wanted to afflict himself as a nazir, but only conditionally — *אי סגי בבית חוניו טרחנא* — if it works in Beit Chonyo I will take on the nezirut. If told it does not work there, he did not want to be a nazir at all. If he goes to the Beit HaMikdash, that action reveals genuine intent, and the nezirut is binding.

The difference between Rava's reading and Rabbi Shimon's is therefore subtle. Both agree that the specification of Beit Chonyo is halachically void. Rabbi Shimon holds that this voids the entire declaration absolutely: there is no vow at all, and one can never retroactively validate it. The Chachamim, as read by Rava, hold that the choice to bring it in the Beit HaMikdash reveals that the person did fundamentally intend to be obligated, and that intent can be honored.

Rav Hamnuna responds to Rava: in the nazirite case, he concedes that Rava's reading is necessary, since there is no way to dissolve a nazirite status short of saying the vow was never binding. But in the olah case, there is more room to say the animal was genuinely sanctified, and the mention of Beit Chonyo only limits personal liability rather than nullifying the entire vow — which is a stronger reading of the declarant's words.

### Rav Yochanan's Ruling

The Gemara notes that Rav Yochanan holds like Rav Hamnuna. Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rav Yochanan: if one said *הרי עלי עולה שאקריבנה בבית חוניו* and one brought it not in Beit Chonyo but in the Land of Israel outside the Beit HaMikdash — *יצא ואינו בכרת* — one is yotzei and is liable for karet. This formulation makes clear: *yotzei* because on Rav Hamnuna's reading, the animal was genuinely sanctified regardless of the Beit Chonyo specification, and the obligation is discharged whenever the animal is slaughtered — wherever that occurs. Liable for karet because the animal was genuinely sanctified and was slaughtered outside the Beit HaMikdash, constituting shichutei chutz.

A *baraita* confirms this ruling: one who vows to bring an olah in the wilderness brings it in Ever HaYarden — *יצא ואינו בכרת*. Rashi notes that the person perhaps thought the wilderness retained sanctity from the days of the Mishkan. The Gemara interprets: we do not take the specification of the wilderness or of Beit Chonyo literally. The meaningful halachic content of such a declaration is: I am sanctifying this animal but limiting my personal liability to replacing it if it is lost or dies. Any subsequent slaughter of the animal — wherever it occurs — discharges that obligation. But because the animal was genuinely sanctified, slaughtering it outside the Beit HaMikdash carries karet.

This produces a clear three-way map of positions. Rabbi Shimon holds the entire vow void. The Chachamim, on Rava's reading, hold the vow conditionally binding — binding if performed in the Beit HaMikdash, non-binding if performed in Beit Chonyo. The Chachamim, on Rav Hamnuna's and Rav Yochanan's reading, hold the vow unconditionally binding — the animal is genuinely sanctified from the moment of declaration, and Beit Chonyo merely limits personal replacement liability.

## Kohanim Disqualification

### Disqualification Rule

The next mishna rules: *הכהנים ששימשו בבית חוניו לא ישמשו במקדש שבירושלים* — kohanim who served in Beit Chonyo may not serve in the Beit HaMikdash in Jerusalem. *ואין צריך לומר לדבר אחר* — it goes without saying that kohanim who served in avodah zarah are excluded entirely. A pasuk from Yechezkel supports this: *אך לא יעלו כהני הבמות אל מזבח ה' בירושלים* — the kohanim of the *bamot* may not ascend to the altar of Hashem in Jerusalem, but *כי אם אכלו מצות בתוך אחיהם* — they may eat *matzot* among their brothers. The Gemara explains: *הרי אלו כבעלי מומים חולקין ואוכלין ולא מקריבין* — they are like kohanim with physical blemishes: they receive a portion of the sacrificial foods but may not perform the service. Their disqualification is a spiritual *mum*, a religious blemish — not complete exclusion, but permanent removal from active service.

The implicit point of the mishna's formulation — *it goes without saying* regarding avodah zarah — is that Beit Chonyo is not itself a house of avodah zarah. The *baraita* later confirms: *תניא כמאן דאמר בית חוניו לאו עבודה זרה היא* — we taught in accordance with the opinion that Beit Chonyo was not avodah zarah.

### Shechita Exception

Rav Yehuda rules: a kohen who slaughtered an animal for avodah zarah and subsequently did teshuvah — *קרבנו ריח ניחוח* — his subsequent korban in the Beit HaMikdash is accepted. The act of shechita for avodah zarah does not disqualify him. Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi derives the disqualification of kohanim who actually served avodah zarah from a pasuk in Yechezkel: *יען אשר ישרתו אותם לפני גלוליהם* — because they performed service before their abominations — *ולא יגשו אלי לכהן לי* — they may not draw near to Me to serve as priests.

The key word is *ישרתו* — they performed *sheirus*, priestly service. Shechita, however, is *לאו שירות* — it is not categorized as a priestly service, since a non-kohen may also serve as a *shochet*. Therefore, although slaughtering for avodah zarah is a grave sin, it does not besmirch one's kehuna in the specific way that performing priestly service for avodah zarah does. After teshuvah, such a kohen may resume service.

### Zrika Debate

The Gemara now turns to a more complex case: a kohen who performed *zrika* — the sprinkling of blood — for avodah zarah, but did so *bishogeg*, unintentionally. Rav Nachman rules: *קרבנו ריח ניחוח* — his korban is subsequently accepted; he is not disqualified. Rav Sheshet rules: *אין קרבנו ריח ניחוח* — he is disqualified.

Rav Sheshet's source: the pasuk reads *והיו לבית ישראל למכשול עון* — they shall be a stumbling block of sin. *מכשול* refers to an unintentional act (*shogeg*) and *עון* to a willful one (*meizid*). Since both are listed, even a shogeg in zrika disqualifies. Rav Nachman reads *מכשול דעון* as a single phrase — the stumbling that is of a willful kind — meaning only meizid disqualifies.

Rav Nachman adduces support from a *baraita*: *כהן שעבד עבודת כוכבים בשוגג — קרבנו ריח ניחוח* — a kohen who worshipped avodah zarah bishogeg, his korban is subsequently accepted. The baraita adds that he must have done teshuvah. Now: if the case were shogeg, why would teshuvah be needed to restore his status? A shogeg who was never invalidated obviously does not require teshuvah to be re-accepted. Therefore the baraita must be discussing meizid — and yet it says that after teshuvah he is accepted. And if the case were zrika bemeizid — the most severe form — teshuvah would be irrelevant, since he has performed a priestly act in service of avodah zarah and is irrevocably excluded. Therefore the case must be shechita bemeizid: willful slaughter for avodah zarah, after which teshuvah restores full status.

### Rav Nechman Lenient

Rav Nachman's position can now be stated precisely: shechita for avodah zarah — even bemeizid — never invalidates, and teshuvah is required only as a matter of general repentance, not to restore kehuna status. Zrika for avodah zarah bishogeg also does not invalidate. Only zrika bemeizid irrevocably disqualifies.

### Rav Sheshet Strict

Rav Sheshet interprets the baraita differently. He reads the case as shechita bishogeg. Why does teshuvah matter? The baraita is saying: *אם שב מעיקרו* — if he remained in a state of teshuvah from the outset, meaning if his act was genuinely inadvertent throughout, then *קרבנו ריח ניחוח* — he is accepted. If not, *אין קרבנו ריח ניחוח* — he is not. On this reading, even shechita bishogeg can be invalidating unless one maintained genuinely inadvertent intent. Rav Sheshet's consistent position: shechita bemeizid invalidates, not because it is a priestly act, but because one who willfully serves avodah zarah — *נעשה משרת לעבודת כוכבים* — has become identified as a servant of idolatry, and that identity is incompatible with kehuna service regardless of the particular act.

The Gemara notes: *ואזדו לטעמייהו* — they go according to their own reasoning consistently. Rav Nachman says: shechita bemeizid for avodah zarah — *קרבנו ריח ניחוח*, because one did not perform a priestly act. Rav Sheshet says: *אין קרבנו ריח ניחוח*, because one has become a willing servant of avodah zarah.

### Other Avodah Zarah Acts

The Gemara extends the dispute to further cases. If a kohen bowed down — *השתחוה לעבודת כוכבים* — Rav Nachman says *קרבנו ריח ניחוח*: bowing is not a priestly act in the Beit HaMikdash context, and therefore does not besmirch the kehuna. Rav Sheshet says *אין קרבנו ריח ניחוח*: any willful service of avodah zarah disqualifies. If a kohen merely accepted an idol as his deity — *הודה לעבודת כוכבים* — without performing any act of worship, Rav Nachman still says *קרבנו ריח ניחוח* and Rav Sheshet still says *אין קרבנו ריח ניחוח*.

The Gemara explains why all three cases must be stated: had only the zrika case been taught, one might have thought Rav Sheshet is strict only there because it is a genuine priestly act, but would be lenient by shechita. The shechita case teaches that he is strict even there. Had only the shechita case been taught, one might have thought that at least shechita involves an act performed in the Temple context — but hishtachava, which is not a Temple-related act at all, Rav Sheshet might accept. The hishtachava case teaches otherwise. Had only hishtachava been taught, one might have thought that verbal acceptance — *hoda'ah*, which is only words — would not suffice to disqualify. Hence all cases must be stated.

On the other side, Rav Nachman's consistent leniency is clear: only the narrowly defined misuse of priestly service — zrika bemeizid — disqualifies. Every other act, however serious a personal sin, does not carry over into the domain of kehuna invalidation.

The Gemara then notes a discussion in Tosafot relevant to contemporary application. Tosafot cites a work called Sefer HaZahir — a Geonic text, not the Zohar — which holds that a kohen who apostatized and returned should not perform nesi'at kapayim, drawing an analogy from these cases. Rashi, in a responsum, disagrees sharply. Rashi argues that the mishna describes even kohanim who served in Beit Chonyo — which was not avodah zarah — as being like *ba'alei mumim*. A ba'al mum, however, does perform nesi'at kapayim. Moreover, the Gemara's exclusion pertains to service in the Beit HaMikdash; birkat kohanim outside the Temple is a separate matter. There is also a related discussion in Sotah about a kohen who killed someone, where the consideration of *takanas hashavim* — the enactment for penitents — plays a role: to what degree does permanently barring a returnee from his former status push people away rather than bringing them close?

## Chonyo-Shimei Story

### Shimon's Prophecy

The Gemara establishes from the mishna's formulation that Beit Chonyo was not regarded as a house of avodah zarah. A *baraita* then begins the narrative of how it came to be founded. In the year that Shimon HaTzaddik died, he emerged from the Kodesh HaKodashim on Yom Kippur and said to those around him: this year, he himself would die. They asked how he knew. He told them: every Yom Kippur, when I entered the Kodesh HaKodashim, I would encounter an elderly man dressed entirely in white, wrapped in white, who would enter and depart with me. This year I saw an elderly man dressed entirely in black, wrapped in black, who entered with me but did not come out with me — a sign of mourning and departure.

After Sukkot he became ill, lingered seven days, and died. Following his death, his fellow kohanim ceased to pronounce the *Shem HaMeforash* — the explicit divine name — in birkat kohanim, since the level of piety required to utter that name safely was no longer present among them.

### The Garb Trick

At the moment of his death, Shimon said: *חוניו בני ישמש תחתי* — my son Chonyo shall serve in my place as Kohen Gadol. Shimei, Chonyo's brother, was jealous — *שהיה גדול ממנו שתי שנים ומחצה* — because Shimei was two and a half years older and had been passed over. He approached his younger brother and said: come, let me teach you the order of the Temple service. He dressed Chonyo in a cloak (*unkeili*) and girded him with a belt (*tziltzul*) — both garments associated with women — and stationed him at the altar. He then turned to the other kohanim and said: look at what this man vowed and fulfilled for his beloved wife — he said that on the day he would serve as Kohen Gadol he would wear her robe and her belt.

Tosafot notes a difficulty: if Chonyo had been designated as Kohen Gadol, how could he have been duped into thinking this was a normal inauguration ceremony? Tosafot offers two answers. First, he may have been chosen for his exceptional piety rather than his knowledge of Temple procedure, so he was genuinely unfamiliar with the inauguration rites. Second, perhaps he had never witnessed the inauguration of a Kohen Gadol and believed this was the unusual ceremony involved.

### Two Versions

This is Rabbi Meir's version. The fellow kohanim, outraged at the perceived mockery of the kehuna, sought to kill Chonyo. He fled; they pursued him. He went to Alexandria of Egypt. *ובנה שם מזבח והעלה עליו לשם עבודת כוכבים* — and he built an altar there and offered upon it for the sake of avodah zarah. The rabbis, hearing of this, commented: one who was never given the office, and was so consumed by jealousy that it led to this, how much more so one who actually holds a position of power — *המבקש לירד לה על אחת כמה וכמה* — will go to terrible lengths to seize or retain it.

Rabbi Yehuda offers a completely different version. In his account, it was Chonyo who declined the appointment — *לא קיבל עליו חוניו* — out of deference to his older brother Shimei, saying Shimei was the elder and should serve. Shimei became the Kohen Gadol. Nevertheless, Chonyo was jealous of the brother whose advancement he had himself enabled. He approached Shimei and said: come, let me teach you the inauguration procedure. He dressed Shimei in the same female garb, stationed him at the altar, and announced to the assembled kohanim: look at what this man vowed and fulfilled for his beloved wife — on the day he would serve as Kohen Gadol he would wear her robe and her belt.

The other kohanim now wanted to kill Shimei. Shimei explained what had actually happened and turned the anger back toward Chonyo. They then sought to kill Chonyo. *כל הרואה אותו אומר זהו זהו* — everyone who saw him called out that he was the one. He fled to the king's palace, then to Alexandria.

### Alexandria Altar

According to Rabbi Yehuda's version: *ובנה שם מזבח והעלה עליו לשם שמים* — and he built an altar and offered upon it for the sake of Heaven. He was pursuing power through devious means, but his original motivation had been piety — he initially declined the appointment — and he was now fleeing his own deviousness rather than fleeing a rejection of himself. The story reads differently when the founder of Beit Chonyo was someone who set it up as a genuine house of worship, even if driven to Egypt by unworthy jealousy.

Rabbi Yehuda provides a scriptural basis for the founding of an altar in Egypt: *ביום ההוא יהיה מזבח לה' בתוך ארץ מצרים* — on that day there shall be an altar to Hashem in the midst of the land of Egypt; *ומצבה אצל גבולה לה'* — and a pillar at its border to Hashem (Yeshaya 19:19).

When the rabbis heard of it, they commented: *ומה זה שברח ממנה כך* — this person who ran away from the position of greatness, who did not seek it — and yet jealousy still led him to such lengths. *המבקש לירד לה על אחת כמה וכמה* — how much more so will one who actively pursues authority go to improper lengths to acquire or defend it.

### Power Danger Lesson

A *baraita* records Rabbi Yehoshua ben Perachiah's personal statement: *בתחילה כל אומר עלי לה אני כופתו ונותנו לפני אריה* — originally, if anyone had told me to take a position of authority, I would have bound him and thrown him before a lion. He regarded the pursuit of office as a dangerous thing to be avoided at all costs.

### Shaul Parallel

*עתה כל האומר לי לירד ממנה אני מטיל עליו קומקום של חמים* — now, having taken such a position, anyone who tells me to relinquish it, I would throw a flask of hot water upon him. The lesson: *שהרי שאול ברח ממנה* — just as Shaul fled from kingship — as we read in this week's haftarah — *ועלה ביקש להרוג את דוד* — and once he was in the position, he sought to kill David to protect it. The desire for status and the fear of losing it can transform even a person of admirable initial humility.

### Five Cities Alternative

Mar Kashisha son of Rav Chisda asked Abaye: according to Rabbi Meir, who holds that Chonyo built his altar for avodah zarah — what does he do with the verse that Rabbi Yehuda uses to justify it (*ביום ההוא יהיה מזבח לה' בתוך ארץ מצרים*)? A *baraita* answers: after the fall of Sancherib, Chizkiya went out and found royal princes seated in golden wagons. He seized the opportunity to spread recognition of God and made them swear not to worship avodah zarah. This is referenced in the verse: *ביום ההוא יהיו חמש ערים בארץ מצרים מדברות שפת כנען ונשבעות לה' צבאות* — on that day there shall be five cities in Egypt speaking the language of Canaan — Hebrew — and swearing allegiance to Hashem of Hosts. Those princes and their followers went to Alexandria and built an altar and offered upon it *לשם שמים* — for the sake of Heaven.

Thus, according to Rabbi Meir, the verse from Yeshaya refers not to Beit Chonyo but to the altar built in Alexandria by those whom Chizkiya had brought to acknowledge God, centuries before Chonyo's time. For Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that Beit Chonyo was built *leshem shamayim*, the same verse applies to both.
