# Menachot 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Seven Liquid Measures

### Seven Measures Intro

The Mishnah with which we open Daf 88 takes up the subject of liquid measures used in the Beit HaMikdash. Having dealt with dry measures previously, the Mishnah now turns to the lach: שבע מדות של לח היו במקדש — there were seven liquid measures in the Mikdash.

### Log & Revi'it Units

The foundational unit of liquid measure is the log, approximately fourteen ounces. A hin is twelve log. The revi'it — literally "a quarter" — is a quarter of a log, roughly 3.2 to 3.4 ounces, the familiar minimum measure for kiddush. These two units — the log and the revi'it — form the baseline from which the other measures are derived.

### Hin to Quarter-Log

The Mishnah lists the seven measures as follows: a hin, a half-hin (six log), a third of a hin (four log — the portion accompanying a par, as the Torah specifies: שלישית ההין לפר), a quarter of a hin (three log, for an ayil), a log, a half-log, and a quarter-log. Notably, the list moves from the largest measure down to the smallest, and the Torah's language — שלישית ההין לפר ורביעית ההין לכבש — grounds the larger measures in explicit scriptural usage.

### One Hin w/ Lines

Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok offers an alternative to the picture of seven distinct vessels. He holds that שנתות היו בהין — there were graduated markings, or notches, on a single hin vessel, indicating the fill lines for the various sacrifices: עד כאן לפר, עד כאן לאיל, ועד כאן לכבש. Rather than maintaining multiple separate measuring vessels, one vessel served all purposes by means of these calibrated lines. This approach is at least conceptually less precise than having dedicated vessels, though Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok clearly finds it acceptable.

### Rabbi Shimon: No Hin

Rabbi Shimon takes a more radical stance: לא היו שם הין — there was no hin at all in the Beit HaMikdash. His reasoning is functional: וכי מה היה הין משמש? What was the hin ever used for? The largest quantity actually required for any sacrifice was a half-hin (for the par), so a full hin vessel was never actually needed. In its place, Rabbi Shimon says, there was a special measure of a log-and-a-half: מדה יתירה של לוג ומחצה היתה שם, used to divide the oil for the minchat chavitin of the Kohen Gadol — a log-and-a-half in the morning and a log-and-a-half in the afternoon.

The Rabbis push back: but you already have a half-log measure; simply use it three times and you have a log-and-a-half. Why fabricate an entirely new vessel? Rabbi Shimon replies in kind: that argument proves too much. By the same logic, why have a half-log at all — just use the revi'it twice? Why have a full log — just use the half-log twice? The answer is the standing principle of the Mikdash: כלי שמשמש מידה זו אינו משמש מידה אחרת — a vessel designated for one measure is not pressed into service as a substitute for another. If a particular measure is regularly needed, a dedicated vessel is created for it rather than improvising with multiples of a smaller one.

Tosafot notes the Rabbis' implied response: the log-and-a-half measure is needed only twice daily and only for the Kohen Gadol's chavitin, whereas the commonly used measures — a half-log, a log — serve many different purposes throughout the Temple service and therefore clearly warrant dedicated vessels of their own.

## Meir vs. Yehudah Debate

### Baraita Recitation

The Gemara now presents a Baraita that records the same list of seven measures but in ascending order — from smallest to largest: רביעית, לוג, חצי לוג, רביעית ההין, שלישית ההין, חצי ההין, והין. This, the Baraita states, is דברי רבי יהודה — the position of Rabbi Yehudah. Rabbi Meir then gives the exact same list in descending order — hin first, down to the quarter-log. The Baraita also records Rabbi Shimon's position (no hin; the log-and-a-half substitute), which we have already encountered in the Mishnah. The question that immediately presents itself: since Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah appear to list the identical seven vessels in merely different orders, what substantive disagreement underlies the difference in direction?

### Dedicated Vessels

Before reaching that question, the Gemara completes the Baraita's discussion of Rabbi Shimon's position. The Rabbis respond to him: הוי הין דעבד משה לשמן המשחה — the hin that Moshe used when he prepared the shemen hamishcha was preserved. The Torah explicitly states שמן זית הין in that context, so Moshe clearly had a hin vessel. One opinion holds: since it was not needed for later generations, Moshe used it at the time and then it was put away, genzu. The other opinion holds: since it existed, it was retained — כיון דהוה הוה. Furthermore, the Gemara notes that we have a received tradition — גמירי דשבע מדות הלח היו במקדש — that there were precisely seven liquid measures, analogous to the tradition (cited by Ravina in another context) that semicha was performed on precisely two communal sacrifices. If Rabbi Shimon eliminates the hin, he must replace it with something, and he does so with the log-and-a-half.

### Overflow & Tension

Returning to the central question of Rabbi Meir versus Rabbi Yehudah: אמר רבי יוחנן, בירותי מדות איכא בינייהו — Rabbi Yochanan says their dispute concerns the beirutei middot, the overflow that rides atop the surface of a filled measuring vessel due to surface tension. The disagreement is over whether this extra amount — the liquid held above the rim of the vessel — is sanctified by the vessel along with the rest of its contents.

### Transfer Direction

Rashi explains the connection between the ordering of measures and this question of overflow as follows. Rabbi Yehudah, who lists measures from small to large, holds that the beirutei middot are sanctified. According to this view, God gave Moshe the small revi'it as the standard — ורביעית יהב ליה רחמנא למשה — and told him to measure with it, accumulating small measures into larger ones. When pouring from a small vessel into a larger vessel, the overflow on top of the smaller one can be captured: if you tip the small vessel quickly into the larger one, even the tiny amount held by surface tension goes along. Rabbi Meir, who lists measures from large to small, holds that the beirutei middot are not sanctified. According to his view, God gave Moshe the hin — the large measure — and instructed him to divide downward into halves, thirds, and quarters. When transferring from a large vessel into smaller ones, that extra surface liquid simply spills away; it cannot be preserved in the transfer.

The analogy is intuitive: anyone who has filled a kiddush cup to the brim and then tried to pour it into small cups knows that the overflow spills rather than transfers cleanly. By contrast, when pouring from a small vessel into a large one, the extra amount on top rides along into the larger vessel.

One might note a tension here: Rabbi Meir earlier appeared to endorse a גדוש, a heaping measure, for the dry measures — which would suggest he favors counting overflow as part of the sanctified quantity. The relationship between his position on dry measures and his position here on liquid overflow remains to be worked through.

### Sanctification Question

Abaye offers a different analysis entirely. He says that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehudah is not about whether the beirutei middot are sanctified at all — both could hold either position on that question. Rather, their dispute concerns the meaning of מלאים in the verse (שניהם מלאים סלת): what does it mean to fill the measure?

According to Rabbi Meir, who enumerates from large to small, מלאים שלא יחסר ושלא יותיר: the measure must be filled exactly — no less and no more. There is a strict maximum as well as a minimum. According to Rabbi Yehudah, who enumerates from small to large, מלאים means שלא יחסר — do not leave it short — but if you overfill it, אבל יותיר, מלאים קרינא ביה: that too counts as "filled." The direction of enumeration is a kind of סימן — a signal — for this underlying orientation: counting upward suggests that "more" is the direction of acceptability, while counting downward signals that maximums are as binding as minimums.

Tosafot notes that the directional ordering is more of a mnemonic signal for the underlying positions than a logical derivation from them.

This raises the question: is this not identical to the first dispute about beirutei middot? The Acharonim clarify that these are genuinely two distinct questions. One question is whether the liquid that overflows and sits atop the vessel by surface tension is itself sanctified by that vessel. A separate question is whether one is permitted, at the outset, to bring slightly more than the prescribed measure — whether the Torah's requirement functions as a strict maximum or merely as a minimum. One could maintain, for instance, that overfill is halachically permissible to bring, but that the overfill itself does not become sanctified through the measuring vessel; rather, it would only become sanctified when poured into a separate kli sharet. Or one could hold the converse: the overfill is sanctified, but one is nonetheless required to remove it and bring only the exact amount. These are logically independent questions.

## Shimon's Challenge

### Hin Never Used

Having presented the Baraita's full treatment of the debate, the Gemara turns back to examine Rabbi Shimon's position more closely. Rabbi Shimon maintains that the hin was simply never functionally required: לא היו שם הין — no offering demanded a full hin's measure of wine or oil, so why maintain such a vessel?

### Moshe's Hin Preserved

The Rabbis respond that the hin Moshe used for the shemen hamishcha — for which the Torah explicitly writes שמן זית הין — was preserved in the Mikdash. The question then is whether it was preserved permanently or only used for that original occasion. Rabbi Shimon holds: כיון דלדורות לא הוי צריך, לפי שעה הוא דעבד וגנזו — since it was not needed for future generations, Moshe made it for the moment and it was secreted away. The Rabbis hold: כיון דהוה הוה — once it existed, it remained part of the Temple's complement of vessels.

### Seven Measures Tradition

The Gemara then addresses why Rabbi Shimon cannot simply eliminate the hin and leave only six measures. The answer is drawn from a parallel: just as Ravina states elsewhere that גמירי שתי סמיכות בציבור — there is a received tradition that communal semicha was performed on exactly two specific korbanot — so too here, גמירי דשבע מדות הלח היו במקדש: there is a received tradition that the liquid measures in the Mikdash numbered precisely seven. Rabbi Shimon, who accepts this tradition, is therefore compelled to substitute the log-and-a-half measure in place of the hin in order to preserve the count of seven.

### Measures vs. Vessels

The Gemara then addresses Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok, who holds that there was only one hin vessel with graduated lines rather than multiple distinct vessels. Does he reject the tradition of seven measures altogether? The Gemara answers: ואיבעית אימא, מאי שבע מדות? שבע מדידות — he need not reject it. One can distinguish between seven distinct vessels and seven distinct measures. Even if some or all of the measures were marked on a single vessel, the tradition of seven different measures used in the Mikdash remains intact. Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok accepts the seven measures; he simply holds that they were incorporated into a single physical vessel rather than expressed through seven separate keilim.

## Applications of Measures

### Revi'it Uses

The Mishnah now catalogues the specific uses of each liquid measure. The revi'it — approximately 3.2 ounces, the familiar minimum for kiddush — was used for two purposes: רביעית מים למצורע, the water used in the purification process of the metzora (where the bird is slaughtered over mayim chayyim held in a vessel, and the Gemara elsewhere establishes the minimum as a revi'it), and רביעית שמן לנזיר, the revi'it of oil incorporated into the lechem of the nazir when he brings his korban. It is worth noting that this is a standard revi'it — a quarter of a log — and not a revi'it ha-hin, which is three log, a very different quantity.

### Half-Log Uses

The chatzi log served two functions: חצי לוג מים לסוטה — the half-log of water used for the sotah, into which the megillah was scraped and which she then drank — and וחצי לוג שמן לתודה, the half-log of oil incorporated into the bread of the todah offering. The derivations of these specific quantities come from other sugyot; some are learned from pesukim, others rest on tradition alone.

### Log for Menachot

The log was the standard measure for all menachot: ובלוג היה מודד לכל המנחות. Just as the standard unit of flour for a minchah was an isaron — roughly a five-pound measure — the standard amount of oil was one log, approximately fourteen ounces. This is analogous to the rule regarding dry measures: even for a large minchah requiring many isronot, one uses the single isaron measure repeatedly rather than a larger vessel. The same principle applies to oil: אפילו מנחה של שישים עישרון נותנים לה שישים לוג — even a sixty-isaron minchah would receive sixty log of oil, measured out with the log vessel sixty times.

### Large Menachot Debate

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov dissents: אפילו מנחה של שישים עישרון, אין לה אלא לוגה — even a sixty-isaron minchah receives only a single log of oil, no more. In other words, when a donor vows to bring a larger-than-standard minchah, he may increase the flour but not the oil. The oil remains fixed at one log regardless of the quantity of flour. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov derives this from the phrase לַמִּנְחָה וְלֹג שָׁמֶן — the minchah receives a log of oil, full stop.

The reason the Mishnah specifies sixty isronot as the example is deliberate: as a later Mishnah will teach, sixty isronot is the maximum that can be effectively blilah'd — effectively kneaded and mixed. A minchah exceeding sixty isronot must be split into two separate offerings. The familiar rule כל הראוי לבילה אין בילה מעכבת בו — that which is capable of being mixed, failure to mix does not invalidate — applies precisely to a minchah of sixty isronot or fewer. Thus sixty isronot is the largest single minchah possible, and the Mishnah uses it to stake out the dispute's outer limit.

### Sacrifices & Menorah

The Mishnah then assigns specific measures to the libations accompanying animal sacrifices: six log for the par (a half-hin), four log for the ayil (a third-hin), and three log for the keves (a quarter-hin). Although the Mishnah here expresses these in log, the actual measuring vessels used in practice would have been the chatzi ha-hin, shlishit ha-hin, and revi'it ha-hin respectively; the Mishnah is simply clarifying the log equivalencies for completeness.

For the menorah: שלושה ומחצה למנורה — three and a half log in total, with חצי לוג לכל נר — a half-log per lamp. The assumption is that a half-log of oil is sufficient to sustain a lamp through the longest winter night; this standard amount was maintained year-round. Seven lamps at a half-log each yields three and a half log. As for the summer months when nights are shorter, any oil still burning in the morning would be extinguished at the time of the morning service — except for the ner maaravi — and the surplus oil would be poured out.

## Sanctity of Vessels

### Revi'it Sanctity

The Gemara now records a series of questions and answers between Rebbe (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) and his colleagues concerning the status of the liquid measures as כלי שרת — sanctified Temple vessels. Rebbe's starting assumption is that the liquid measures were indeed keilei sharet, as distinct from the dry measures, about which there had been an earlier debate. The question is then: for what purpose would each measure require this sanctified status?

יתיב רבי וקא קשיא ליה — Rebbe sat and was troubled: רביעית למה נשנית? Why was the revi'it designated as a kli sharet? The Mishnah says the revi'it was used for the metzora's water and the nazir's oil. But the metzora's purification took place outside the Mikdash — חוצה הוא — so no kli sharet was needed there. And the nazir's oil accompanies his shlamim, and לחם נזיר בשחיטת איל הוא דקדיש — the lechem of the nazir is sanctified through the slaughtering of his ayil, not through a measuring vessel. So what sanctification role does the revi'it vessel serve?

### Kohen Gadol Oil

Rabbi Chiya resolved Rebbe's question: שבה היה מודד לחביתי כהן גדול — it was used to portion out the oil for each individual challah of the Kohen Gadol's minchat chavitin. The total oil for each half of the minchat chavitin was a log and a half; there were six challot per half; and a log and a half divided among six challot yields a revi'it per challah. In order to apportion the oil individually to each challah — and in that context, one would want the oil itself sanctified before it was applied — the revi'it served as the kli sharet for that purpose.

Rebbe was delighted, and he applied to Rabbi Chiya the verse מארץ מרחק איש עצתי — "from a distant land comes my counselor" — alluding to Rabbi Chiya's origins in Bavel.

### Half-Log Sanctity

The Gemara records the parallel inquiry: יתיב רבי וקא קשיא ליה — Rebbe again sat troubled, this time over the chatzi log. חצי לוג למה נמשח? Why did the half-log require sanctification? The Mishnah says it was used for the sotah's water and the todah's oil. But the water for the sotah was drawn from the kiyor — the laver — and the Torah itself calls it מים קדושים: it was already sanctified through the kiyor, not through a measuring vessel. And the oil for the todah's lechem, like the nazir's, is sanctified through the slaughtering of the todah offering: לחמי תודה בשחיטת תודה הוא דקדשי. So again: why would the chatzi log need to be a kli sharet?

### Menorah Oil Portions

This time it was Rebbe's own son, Rabbi Shimon bar Rebbe, who provided the resolution: שבה היה מחלק חצי לוג שמן לכל נר ונר — the half-log was used to measure and distribute the oil to each individual lamp of the menorah. As the Mishnah stated, each of the seven lamps received a half-log of oil, and that oil needed to be sanctified as it was dispensed. The chatzi log kli sharet was the instrument through which this sanctification was effected.

Rebbe responded: נר ישראל כך היה — "Light of Israel, so it was" — commending his son with this epithet particularly fitting to the subject of the menorah's lamps. Tosafot notes that the phrase נר ישראל appears in another Gemara context as well, not limited to this passage.

## Menorah Lighting

### Lamp Extinction

The discussion of the chatzi log and the menorah's oil naturally leads into a broader treatment of how the menorah was actually maintained. The Gemara cites Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rebbe: נר שכבתה — if one of the lamps of the menorah went out — נדשן השמן ונדשנה הפתילה — and the oil or the wick had become charred and ashy, כיצד עושה? What is the procedure? מטיבה — you clean it out, ונותן בה שמן — you refill it with oil, ומדליקה — and you relight it.

The printed Rashi reads this as a halachic characterization rather than a physical description: once the lamp is extinguished, the oil and wick are treated as rendered inert — like ashes, halachically spent — and must be discarded and replaced regardless of their physical condition. The kesav yad Rashi (the manuscript version), however, takes it literally: the text is discussing a case where the oil and wick have actually become charred and dirty, making reuse inappropriate. Either way, the underlying assumption of this sugya — at least according to the straightforward reading — is that if a lamp goes out during the night, there is an obligation to relight it so that the menorah burns through the night. The Rashba disputes this reading entirely and argues, based on other sugyot, that there is no requirement to relight an extinguished lamp; he therefore construes the Gemara differently. The peshat reading, however, is that the menorah is expected to burn through the night and relighting is required.

### Refill Amount

Once it is established that one must relight an extinguished lamp, a further question presents itself — one posed by Rabbi Zeira: כשהוא נותן בה שמן, כמדת ראשונה או כמה שחסרה? When refilling the oil, does one put in the full original measure — a half-log — or only enough to replace what was actually consumed?

The logic of the second option is not unreasonable: if the lamp went out midway through the night, it only needs to burn through the remaining hours, so perhaps only half a half-log, or some partial amount, is needed rather than the full measure.

### Full Measure Standard

Rabbi Yirmiyah disposes of the question decisively: פשיטא דכמדת ראשונה — obviously you put in the full measure. His reasoning: if you were to put in only the amount consumed, how would you know precisely how much that was? מנא ידעינן מה חסר? And if you say you would measure the remnant oil after removing it, and then replace exactly that amount — אם כן, שבע מידות נפשו להו מידות טובא — you would need measures far beyond the seven established measures, since you would need smaller sub-units to gauge the remnant. Since the Mikdash operated with exactly seven standard liquid measures and no others, there is no mechanism for measuring out a partial amount. The only workable answer is: always put in the full standard half-log.

On this resolution Rebbe applied the verse רכב על דבר אמת וענוה צדק — riding on the matter of truth and righteous humility. The statement is also transmitted in a parallel tradition: Rabbi Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, and in another version Rabbi Abba in the name of Rabbi Chanina in the name of Rebbe himself — indicating this ruling was attributed to Rebbe directly in some traditions.

### Rambam Twice Daily

The Rambam takes a position on the menorah that stands entirely alone among the Rishonim. The near-universal understanding is that the menorah was lit at night and cleaned in the morning — the oil measure of a half-log per lamp being calibrated to last through the longest winter nights — and that the morning service of הטבת הנרות (cleaning the lamps) was distinct from the evening הדלקה (lighting). This is grounded in the verse בבוקר בבוקר בהטיבו את הנרות יקטירנה, which speaks of the morning cleaning in conjunction with the morning incense offering.

The Rambam, however, understands the menorah as burning continuously — through both night and day, tamid in the literal sense. According to him, the procedure of הטבה and הדלקה was performed twice daily, morning and evening, but in each case the process was the same: one checked the lamps, and if a lamp was burning cleanly, one added oil; if a lamp had gone out or needed attention, one cleaned it, refilled it with a half-log, and relit it. Thus for the Rambam, this Gemara's scenario — a lamp that has gone out and must be cleaned and relit — describes not a rare nighttime occurrence but the routine morning procedure.

Under the Rambam's reading, the half-log per lamp need not be calibrated to last a full night; the measure is added at each of the two daily servicings and is sufficient to last until the next one. All other Rishonim disagree and understand the menorah as lit at night and cleaned by day.

## Menorah Construction

### Jointed Lamps

The discussion of how lamps are cleaned leads naturally to a question about how the lamps were physically constructed. Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehudah said in the name of Rav Sheshet: נר שבמקדש, פרקים הוו — the lamps of the menorah were jointed. That is, they were not rigidly fixed but had flexible joints that allowed them to be bent, making it possible to clean them out effectively while they remained attached to the menorah.

The reasoning: the Torah writes ככר זהב טהור יעשה אותה alongside the description of the menorah, and it also says מקשה — hammered from a single piece — applying even to the lamps: המנורה ונרותיה כתיב, the menorah and its lamps are mentioned together as a unit. Since the lamps were part of the menorah itself, they could not simply be removed. Yet since they required regular הטבה, they had to be cleanable in place. The solution was that they were jointed — built with a hinge-like construction that allowed the lamp to be bent or tilted for cleaning without being detached from the menorah's body.

### Removable Lamps

A Baraita challenges this construction. The Baraita describes the cleaning procedure as follows: מסלקן — you remove the lamps, ומניחן באהל — you place them in a vessel or enclosure with a cleansing agent, ומקנחן בספוג — you wipe them out with a sponge, ונותן בהם שמן — you refill them with oil, ומדליקן — and you relight them. This clearly implies that the lamps were removable, not fixed.

The Gemara resolves the contradiction by noting that Rav Sheshet's view accords with a different tanna. The Baraita continues: חכמים אומרים, לא היו מזיזין אותן ממקומן — the Sages say the lamps were not moved from their place. The Gemara initially entertains the possibility that "not moved" means "customarily not moved but capable of being moved." It rejects this reading: the correct formulation is לא היתה זזה ממקומה — they were incapable of being moved, being fixed to the body of the menorah. This Tanna, identified as Rabbi Eliezer, thus agrees with Rav Sheshet that the lamps were fixed and the jointed construction was what enabled cleaning.

### Golden Lid

Rabbi Eliezer bar Tzadok adds a further detail about the lamp construction: כמין תס של זהב היה על גבה — each lamp had a golden lid or cover fitted over it. The oil was protected from contamination by this cover, which had only a small opening through which the wick emerged. When cleaning — כשמטיבה — the lid was pushed toward the lip of the lamp (דוחקו כלפי פיה), allowing access for cleaning. When the oil was refilled — כשנותן בשמן — the lid was repositioned to cover and protect the oil (דוחקו כלפי ראשה). The presence of this golden lid is consistent with the fixed-lamp model: one cleans and refills in place, with the lid serving both protective and operational functions.

### Single Talent

The Gemara now traces this dispute to its tannaitic roots. A Baraita states: מנורה ונרותיה באות מן הככר — the menorah and its lamps both came from the single talent of gold. The tongs and the shovels — the cleaning and maintenance implements — were not part of the talent: ואין מלקחיה ומחתותיה מן הככר.

The derivation: the Torah says ככר זהב טהור יעשה אותה, establishing that the menorah came from a talent. One might think only the menorah itself was included, but the verse then says את כל הכלים האלה — all these vessels — which extends the talent to include the lamps as well. The tongs and shovels are excluded by the limiting term אותה — "it" — which restricts the talent to the structural components of the menorah and its integral lamps, not the external service implements. This is the position of the first Tanna.

### Nechemyah Position

Rabbi Nechemyah disagrees: מנורה היתה באה מן הככר — only the menorah body itself came from the talent — ולא נרותיה ומלקחיה ומחתותיה — not the lamps, not the tongs, not the shovels. He derives his position from the same verses but reads אותה more narrowly, excluding even the lamps from the talent requirement. The practical implication of this position is significant: if the lamps are not part of the talent and not structurally integral to the menorah, they would be detachable rather than fixed — resolving the cleaning problem without requiring a jointed construction. The lamps could simply be removed, cleaned, refilled, and replaced.

The Gemara notes that this produces a pair of tannaitic positions associated with the name Rabbi Nechemyah — תרי תנאי אליבא דרבי נחמיה — since an earlier statement attributed the fixed-lamp view to Rabbi Nechemyah while the Baraita here presents his position as excluding the lamps from the talent, which would imply they are detachable. These are understood as two different tannaim who share the name Rabbi Nechemyah, each holding a different tradition on this question. The underlying issue — whether the lamps came from the same kikar as the menorah body — determines whether they were fixed (and thus required jointed construction for cleaning) or removable (and thus cleanable by detachment).
