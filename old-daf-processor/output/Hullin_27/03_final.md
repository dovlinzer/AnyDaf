# Chullin 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Basic Rules

### Perek Bet Overview

The second perek of *Masechet Chullin* opens on 27a and takes up where many students might have expected the first perek to begin: with the act of shechita itself — what is required, where it must be performed, and how much must be cut. The first perek, as we know, focused on the kasherim and psulim, addressing who may perform shechita, with what instrument, and under what conditions. Here, by contrast, we finally turn to the mechanics of the act itself. That said, the Gemara does not remain purely technical; it uses the occasion as an opportunity for a range of abstract and conceptual inquiries, as we shall see.

### Mishnah: Simanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶחָד בָּעוֹף וּשְׁנַיִם בַּבְּהֵמָה – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, וְרוּבּוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד כָּמוֹהוּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחוֹט אֶת הַוְּורִידִין. חֲצִי אֶחָד בְּעוֹף וְאֶחָד וָחֵצִי בִּבְהֵמָה – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה. רוֹב אֶחָד בָּעוֹף וְרוֹב שְׁנַיִם בַּבְּהֵמָה – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters** by cutting **one** *siman*, i.e., the windpipe or the gullet, **in a bird, and two** *simanim* **in an animal, his slaughter is valid, and** the halakhic status of **the majority of one** *siman* **is like** that of the entire *siman*. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The slaughter is not valid **until he cuts the veins [*haveridin*],** i.e., the major blood vessels in the neck. If one cut **half of one** *siman* **in a bird or one and a half** *simanim* **in an animal, his slaughter is not valid.** If one cut **the majority of one** *siman* **in a bird or the majority of two** *simanim* **in an animal, his slaughter is valid.**

The Mishnah opens with the rule: ha-shochet echad ba-of u-shnayim ba-beheimah — one who slaughters one siman, either the trachea or the esophagus, in birds, or two simanim in animals (more precisely, mammals), shechitato kesherah — the shechita is valid. The Mishnah then adds: v'rubo shel echad kamo-hu — the majority of one siman counts. One need not cut the full trachea or full esophagus; a majority suffices, as has been discussed previously and will be revisited again. The Mishnah continues with the ruling on half: if one cut exactly half — not a majority — of a siman in a bird, or one full siman and half of another in a mammal, shechitato pesulah — the shechita is invalid. This second case is notable: even though cutting one full siman plus half of another amounts to one and a half simanim cut in total, which exceeds the combined majority of each (roughly 1.2), since one did not cut a majority of the second siman it remains invalid. The Mishnah then restates the affirmative: rov echad ba-of v'rov shnayim ba-beheimah — a majority of one in a bird, a majority of two in a mammal — the shechita is valid.

### Rabbi Yehuda & Vridim

Rabbi Yehuda adds: ad she-yishchot et ha-vridim — one must also shecht the blood vessels in the neck, which are presumably the major vessels responsible for draining the blood, either the jugular vein or the carotid arteries. The precise identity of the vridim merits clarification: the word vridim is suggestive of veins, Rashi speaks of there being one on each side of the neck, and various anatomical illustrations identify them as both the jugular veins and the carotid arteries — a contradiction that requires further investigation. What is clear is that they are a major blood vessel in the neck, and it is worth noting that we actually pasken like Rabbi Yehuda, making this not merely an academic question.

The interesting question already in the Mishnah is whether Rabbi Yehuda considers the cutting of the vridim to be part of the act of shechita itself — implied by his use of the verb yishchot and by the apparent consequence that the shechita is invalid without it — or whether he is addressing a separate concern about removing blood from the meat before consumption. The language of the Mishnah strongly suggests that it is part of shechita, but the Gemara will rule otherwise: for Rabbi Yehuda, the cutting of the vridim is not part of the act of shechita but addresses a distinct problem of ensuring adequate removal of blood. The Gemara acknowledges that this reading is not intuitive from the Mishnah's formulation, and it grapples with that tension directly.

Additionally, the Gemara will limit Rabbi Yehuda's requirement to birds, not to mammals — which is somewhat counterintuitive, since one might have supposed that mammals, with their greater volume of blood, would require more rigorous blood removal. The Gemara's discussion of this follows.

### Majority Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״הַשּׁוֹחֵט״ – דִּיעֲבַד אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא. שְׁנַיִם בַּבְּהֵמָה לְכִתְחִלָּה לָא? עַד כַּמָּה לִשְׁחוֹט וְלֵיזִיל? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אַאֶחָד בָּעוֹף, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אַרוּבּוֹ שֶׁל אֶחָד כָּמוֹהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers from the term: **One who slaughters,** that if one slaughtered, then **after the fact, yes,** the slaughter is valid; but ***ab initio*, no,** it is prohibited. The Gemara asks: Is the ruling with regard to the cutting of **two** *simanim* **in an animal** that ***ab initio*, no,** it is prohibited? If so, **how much** is one expected **to continue and cut** the *simanim*, *ab initio*? There are only two relevant *simanim* to be cut. The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that the reference is **to** the cutting of **one** *siman* **in a bird,** as one is required to cut both *simanim* in a bird *ab initio*. **And if you wish, say** instead that the reference is **to** the passage in the mishna that states that the halakhic status of **the majority of one** *siman* **is like** that of the entire *siman*, as one is required to cut the entire *siman ab initio*.

The Gemara opens by noting that the verb ha-shochet — one who slaughters — implies a b'di-eved context: post facto the shechita is valid, but le-chatechila something was lacking. This raises an immediate question: what could be le-chatechila deficient when one has cut the full two simanim in a mammal? One might understand a le-chatechila concern regarding birds — perhaps ideally one should cut both simanim even in a bird. But what more could one have been expected to do when one has cut both the kaneh and the weshet in their entirety?

The Gemara offers two resolutions. The first: eiba'it eima echad ba-of — the implication of b'di-eved is directed at the bird, meaning that le-chatechila one should cut both simanim in a bird as well, even though one suffices. The second resolution, which appears to be the Gemara's primary focus: eiba'it eima a'rubo shel echad kamo-hu — the le-chatechila concern is not with the first clause of the Mishnah at all but with the second, which permits a majority. That is, le-chatechila one should cut the entire siman; b'di-eved a majority suffices.

Rashi explains this le-chatechila requirement as a rabbinic concern: since the boundary between a majority and exactly half can be difficult to gauge with precision, one is asked le-chatechila to cut the entire siman so as to avoid any doubt. There is, however, a deeper conceptual question lurking here that has bearing not only on this halacha but on broader questions in shechita. How do we understand the fundamental obligation: is one's basic duty to cut fifty-one percent, with the majority being the baseline requirement? Or is the baseline obligation to cut the entire siman, with a majority counting as halachically equivalent to the whole — rubo shel echad kamo-hu? If the latter framing is correct, then even the act of cutting more than fifty percent is conceptually a stand-in for cutting the full siman. This in turn bears on the question of le-chatechila, because if conceptually the obligation is to cut the entire siman and the majority merely fulfills it in a diminished form, then of course le-chatechila one should do the full thing. It would also be relevant to cases where a psul — such as shehiya or derasah — occurs in the final forty-nine percent after the majority has already been cut: does that psul affect the shechita, or was the shechita effectively completed at the fifty-one percent mark?

## Location: The Neck

### Rav Kahana: Shach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מִנַּיִן לִשְׁחִיטָה שֶׁהִיא מִן הַצַּוָּאר? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת בֶּן הַבָּקָר״, מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁח חַטֵּהוּ. מִמַּאי דְּהַאי חַטֵּהוּ לִישָּׁנָא דְּדַכּוֹיֵי הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְחִטֵּא אֶת הַבַּיִת״, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״תְּחַטְּאֵנִי בְאֵזוֹב וְאֶטְהָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says: From where** is it derived **with regard to slaughter that it is** performed **from the neck?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And he shall slaughter [*veshaḥat*] the young bull** before the Lord" (Leviticus 1:5), which is interpreted homiletically: **From the place where** the animal **bends [*shaḥ*], purify it [*ḥattehu*]** through slaughter. The Gemara asks: **From where** does one ascertain **that this** term, ***ḥattehu*, is an expression of purification?** The Gemara answers: It is ascertained from a verse, **as it is written: "And he shall purify [*veḥitte*] the house"** (Leviticus 14:52). **And if you wish, say** instead that it is ascertained **from here: "Purge me [*teḥatte'eni*] with hyssop and I will be pure"** (Psalms 51:9).

Having established the basic rules, the Gemara turns to an entirely different question: how do we know that shechita must be performed at the neck or throat? This question has two dimensions: first, why not some other part of the animal entirely — why not stab it in the heart or the back? Second, within the neck, how do we know it must be the front of the throat rather than the back of the neck? Tosafot's reading of the entire subsequent sugya is largely organized around this second question.

Rav Kahana opens: *Minayin lishchita shehi min hatzavar* — from where do we know that shechita is at the neck or throat? He cites the verse: ve-shachat et ben ha-bakar — he shall slaughter the calf, which appears in the context of a korban. From this he derives through a play on words: mi-makom she-shach chata'ehu — from where it bows, there you shall cleanse or validate it. The word shach evokes bowing or inclining, and if one observes a cow standing normally, its neck is angled downward — that is the part of its body that inclines. The word chata'ehu is read as meaning to cleanse or purify, with an analogy to other usages of the root chet-tet-alef in the sense of purification — as in ve-chite et ha-bayit from the purification of a house, or techate'eni be-ezov ve-etaher from the sprinkling of ashes of the *Parah Adumah*. The reading is therefore: from the place where the animal inclines, there you shall make it kasher or cleanse it.

### Shach Challenged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא מִזְּנָבוֹ? שָׁח – מִכְּלָל שֶׁזָּקוּף בָּעֵינַן, וְהָא שָׁח וְעוֹמֵד הוּא. וְאֵימָא מֵאׇזְנוֹ? בָּעֵינַן דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And say** that slaughter is **from its tail,** which is also a place in the animal's body that is bent. The Gemara responds: From the term: **Bends [*shaḥ*],** one can conclude **by inference that we require** a part of the animal's body that can stand **erect** and that bends; **and this,** the tail, **is bent perpetually** and is never erect. The Gemara challenges: **And say** that slaughter is **from its ear,** which is erect and bends. The Gemara explains: **We require** that slaughter be performed on a part of the animal's body from which **blood of the soul** is spilled, **and** when one cuts the ear **there is no** blood of the soul spilled.

The Gemara challenges this derivation on multiple grounds. First: perhaps it refers to the tail, which also hangs downward perpetually. The Gemara responds: sach mi-klal she-zakuf — the word sach, which suggests bending, implies that this is a body part that is sometimes upright and sometimes bent. The tail is always hanging down and therefore cannot be the referent. The Gemara then asks: perhaps the ear? Animals can apparently bend their ears downward. The Gemara responds: be'inan dam ha-nefesh ve-leika — in the context of korbanot, the Torah emphasizes that shechita produces the blood of the soul, and cutting the ear would not produce that effect. The Gemara presses further: perhaps one starts at the ear and continues cutting until one reaches the blood of the soul? Here the Gemara shifts register. Tosafot reads the text as continuing with the word ve-le-ta'ameich — according to your own reasoning — rather than the standard ve-tu. The point becomes: if you challenge this derivation, you will have to concede that it fails. And if it fails, how do you know any of the laws of shechita from the Torah? How do you know the prohibitions of derasah, chaladah, hagramah, and ikkur? The answer: ela Gemara — we know them from tradition. And therefore: shechita min ha-tzavar nami Gemara — the requirement that shechita takes place at the neck is likewise a matter of tradition and cannot be derived from a verse.

### Chateihu & Gistra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: דְּקָרַע וְאָזֵיל עַד דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ! וְתוּ, שְׁהִיָּיה, דְּרָסָה, חֲלָדָה, הַגְרָמָה, וְעִיקּוּר – מְנָלַן? אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא. שְׁחִיטָה מִן הַצַּוָּאר נָמֵי גְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say that one rends** the animal starting from the ear **and continues until the blood of the soul** is spilled. **And furthermore,** with regard to those actions that invalidate slaughter, i.e., **interrupting** the slaughter, **pressing** the knife, **concealing** the knife in the course of an inverted slaughter, **diverting** the knife from the place of slaughter, **and ripping** the *simanim* from their place before cutting them, **from where do we** derive them? **Rather,** these disqualifications are learned through **tradition,** i.e., a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai. The requirement of **slaughter from the neck is also** learned through **tradition,** and has no biblical source.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְרָא לְמַאי אֲתָא? דְּלָא לְשַׁוְּיַיהּ גִּיסְטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if the *halakha* is derived through tradition, **what** *halakha* does **the verse:** "And you shall slaughter," **come to** teach? The Gemara answers: The verse serves to teach **that** one should **not** sever the head completely from the animal's body and **render it a broken** animal. He cuts only the windpipe and the gullet, which are adjacent to the major blood vessels.

If the verse cannot teach us the requirement of the neck from first principles, the Gemara asks: ve-kra le-mai ata — what, then, does the verse teach us? The answer: de-lo le-shaveih gistra — not to sever it completely in two, meaning not to chop through the backbone and divide the animal into two parts. Rashi explains this as a warning not to continue cutting after the simanim until the entire backbone is severed. Tosafot reads this differently and more consistently throughout the sugya: according to Tosafot, once we know from tradition that the shechita takes place at the neck, the verse comes to specify that it must be at the throat — the front of the neck — and not from the back of the neck, i.e., not gistra. Since the verse emphasizes where the animal inclines and connects this to the cleansing or removal of blood, it directs us to the throat, from which blood will flow most directly, rather than to the back of the neck.

It is worth pausing to note a striking irony that runs through all of these drashot: the deriving of the location of shechita is consistently oriented around blood — where the blood is, where it flows, how it is cleansed — and yet shechita in its technical halakhic form is about the trachea and esophagus, not about blood. One could in theory perform a perfectly valid shechita that produces very little blood, if the animal were to die of asphyxiation following the severing of the trachea. The Rabbanan do not make blood a formal requirement of shechita at all. Rabbi Yehuda introduces blood as an additional concern, but the Gemara will even bracket his position as relating not to the act of shechita itself but to a separate concern about preparing the meat. And yet the drashot that locate shechita at the throat are all built around the premise of blood flow. This tension is notable and unresolved.

### Rav Yeimar: Flow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יֵימַר אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְזָבַחְתָּ״, מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁזָּב חַתֵּהוּ. מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי חַתֵּהוּ לִישָּׁנָא דְּמִתְבַּר הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״אַל תִּירָא וְאַל תֵּחָת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar says:** From where is it derived that slaughter is performed from the neck? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "And you shall slaughter [*vezavaḥta*]** of your herd and of your flock" (Deuteronomy 12:21), which is interpreted homiletically: **From the place where** the blood **flows [*shezav*], break it [*ḥattehu*],** i.e., cut it. The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred that this** term, ***ḥattehu*, is an expression of breaking?** The Gemara answers: It is inferred from a verse, **as it is written: "Neither fear nor be dismayed [*teḥat*]"** (Deuteronomy 1:21); ensure that your spirit will not be broken.

Rav Yeimar offers a second derivation. The verse says ve-zavachta — you shall slaughter. He derives from a different play on words: mi-makom she-zav — from where it flows — chateihu — there cut it. Here the word chateihu is spelled with a tav rather than a tet, and the Gemara establishes that this root means to break or sever — as in the verse al tira ve-al techas, do not fear and do not be broken, where the root is read as connoting trembling or collapsing. So: from where the blood flows, there cut it, i.e., the throat. Once again the emphasis is on blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא מֵחוֹטְמוֹ, זָב עַל יְדֵי חִתּוּי בָּעֵינַן, וְהַאי זָב מֵאֵלָיו הוּא. וְאֵימָא מִלִּבּוֹ, וְתוּ: שְׁהִיָּיה, דְּרָסָה, חֲלָדָה, הַגְרָמָה, וְעִיקּוּר – מְנָלַן? אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא. שְׁחִיטָה מִן הַצַּוָּאר נָמֵי גְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And say** that an animal is slaughtered **from its nose,** from which mucus flows, as the verse did not mention blood. The Gemara responds: **We require** a fluid that **flows by means of breaking, and this** mucus **flows on its own.** The Gemara challenges: **And say** that an animal is slaughtered **from its heart** by means of stabbing. **And furthermore,** with regard to those actions that invalidate slaughter, i.e., **interrupting, pressing** the knife during slaughter, **concealing** the knife in the course of an inverted slaughter, **diverting** the knife from the place of slaughter, **and ripping, from where do we** derive them? **Rather,** these disqualifications are learned through **tradition.** The requirement of **slaughter from the neck is also** learned through **tradition.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְרָא לְמַאי אֲתָא? דְּלָא לְשַׁוְּיֵיהּ גִּיסְטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if the *halakha* is derived through tradition, **what** *halakha* does the phrase in **the verse:** "And you shall slaughter" **come to** teach? The Gemara answers: The phrase serves to teach **that** one should **not** sever the head completely from the animal's body and **render it a broken** animal. He cuts only the windpipe and the gullet.

The Gemara challenges this as it challenged the previous derivation. Perhaps the nose — mucus flows from the nose? The Gemara responds: zav al yedei chitui ba'inan — we need something that flows as a result of being cut, not something that flows spontaneously. Perhaps the heart — if you want to ensure blood flows, cut at the heart? Again the Gemara invokes ve-le-ta'ameich and returns to the same conclusion: you cannot derive it from a verse, and we know it from tradition. The verse then teaches de-lo le-shaveih gistra — not to sever the animal from the back, but to proceed from the throat. Tosafot's consistent reading holds here as well: tradition gives us the neck; the verse refines this to the front of the throat, based on the emphasis on blood flow.

### Rabbi Yishmael: Sachat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״וְשָׁחַט״ – אַל תִּקְרֵי ״וְשָׁחַט״ אֶלָּא ״וְסָחַט״, מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁסָּח חַטֵּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** From where is it derived that slaughter is performed from the neck? It is derived from a verse, as it is stated: **"And he shall slaughter [*veshaḥat*]"** (Leviticus 1:5). **Do not read** it as: ***Veshaḥat*; rather,** read it as: ***Vesaḥat*,** which literally means: And he shall squeeze, which is interpreted homiletically: **From the place where** the animal **speaks [*saḥ*], purify it [*ḥattehu*].** The animal's voice emanates from its throat; therefore, it is slaughtered from the neck.

A third derivation is offered from the school of Rabbi Yishmael. The verse says ve-shachat — do not read it as ve-shachat, slaughter, but as ve-sachat. The word sachat can mean squeeze, again evoking the release of blood. Alternatively, the drasha reads: *mi-makom she-sach* — from where the animal speaks, i.e., its throat — chateihu — there cut it. The Gemara challenges this as before: perhaps cut the tongue, since that is even more directly the organ of speech? The Gemara responds: be'inan dam ha-nefesh ve-leika — the context of korbanot demands blood of the soul, and cutting only the tongue will not produce that. The Gemara presses further — begin at the tongue and continue until you reach the blood of the soul? The same conclusion follows: we cannot derive the throat from a verse, and we know it from tradition. The verse teaches only that one must cut from the throat and not from the back of the neck.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא מִלְּשׁוֹנוֹ! בָּעֵינַן דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ וְלֵיכָּא, וְאֵימָא דְּקָרַע וְאָזֵיל עַד דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ! וְתוּ, שְׁהִיָּיה דְּרָסָה חֲלָדָה הַגְרָמָה וְעִיקּוּר מְנָלַן? אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא, שְׁחִיטָה מִן הַצַּוָּאר נָמֵי גְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And say** that slaughter is **from its tongue.** The Gemara explains: **We require** that slaughter be performed on a part of the animal's body from which **blood of the soul** is spilled, **and** when one cuts the tongue **there is no** blood of the soul spilled. The Gemara objects: **And say that one rends** the animal starting from the tongue **and continues until the blood of the soul** is spilled. **And furthermore,** with regard to those actions that invalidate slaughter, i.e., **interrupting, pressing** the knife, **concealing** the knife, **diverting** the knife, **and ripping, from where do we** derive them? **Rather,** these disqualifications are learned through **tradition.** The requirement of **slaughter from the neck is also** learned through **tradition.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּקְרָא לְמַאי אֲתָא? דְּלָא לְשַׁוְּיֵיהּ גִּיסְטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if the *halakha* is derived through tradition, **what** *halakha* does the phrase in **the verse:** "And you shall slaughter," **come to** teach? The Gemara answers: The phrase serves to teach **that** one should **not** sever the head completely from the animal's body and **render it a broken** animal. He cuts only the windpipe and the gullet.

What distinguishes this drasha from the previous two, however, is that it does not rely solely on the theme of blood. The trachea is the organ centrally associated with speech and with the act of shechita — the trachea being one of the two simanim. So at least this drasha, unlike the others, points toward the actual anatomical focus of shechita rather than blood removal, even if it arrives at the same conclusion about the front of the throat.

### Rabbi Chiya: Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי חִיָּיא אָמַר: מִנַּיִן לִשְׁחִיטָה מִן הַצַּוָּאר? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָרְכוּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֲנִים אֵת הַנְּתָחִים״. שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶת הָרֹאשׁ וְאֶת הַפָּדֶר״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶת הָרֹאשׁ וְאֶת הַפָּדֶר״? וַהֲלֹא רֹאשׁ וּפֶדֶר בִּכְלַל כׇּל הַנְּתָחִים הָיוּ, לָמָּה יָצְאוּ? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהִפְשִׁיט אֶת הָעֹלָה וְנִתַּח״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נְתָחִים שֶׁיֶּשְׁנָן בִּכְלַל הַפְשָׁטָה, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָרֹאשׁ שֶׁכְּבָר הוּתַּז? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְאֶת פִּדְרוֹ וְעָרַךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And a *tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall **cites** the source for the *halakha* that slaughter is performed from the neck **from here: As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Ḥiyya says: From where** is it derived **with regard to slaughter that it is** performed **from the neck?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And Aaron's sons, the priests, shall arrange the pieces,** the head, and the fat…upon the altar" (Leviticus 1:8). Rabbi Ḥiyya explains: **As** there is **no** need for **the verse to state: "The head, and the fat." What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "The head, and the fat"? Weren't the head and the fat** included **in** the **category of all the pieces,** mentioned earlier in the verse? **For what** purpose did **they emerge** from the category and warrant individual mention? This is **due to** the fact **that it is stated: "And he shall flay the burnt offering, and cut it** into its pieces" (Leviticus 1:6). One might have thought that **I have** derived that **only the pieces that are** included **in the category of flaying** must be arranged on the altar. **From where** is it derived **to include the head, which was already** partially **severed** when the animal was slaughtered, and is not flayed? It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "With its head and its fat, and** the priest **shall arrange** them" (Leviticus 1:12).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָאָמַר ״אֶת הָרֹאשׁ שֶׁכְּבָר הוּתַּז״, מִכְּלָל דִּשְׁחִיטָה מִן הַצַּוָּאר.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* concludes: **From** the fact **that** Rabbi Ḥiyya **says: The head, which was already** partially **severed,** one learns **by inference that slaughter is from the neck,** as the neck connects the head to the body.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא פָּתַח בְּ״רֹאשׁ וּפֶדֶר״, וּמְסַיֵּים בְּ״רֹאשׁוֹ וּפִדְרוֹ״? הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָרֹאשׁ שֶׁכְּבָר הוּתַּז? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶת הָרֹאשׁ וְאֶת הַפָּדֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the formulation of the *baraita*. **And the *tanna*** of the *baraita* **opened with** a question about the extraneous phrase written with regard to a bull burnt offering: **"The head, and the fat"** (Leviticus 1:8), **and concludes with** an explanation of the phrase written with regard to a sheep burnt offering: **"Its head and its fat"** (Leviticus 1:12). The Gemara answers that **this** is what the *tanna* **is saying: From where** is it derived **to include the head, which was already** partially **severed?** It is derived from a verse, as **the verse states: "The head, and the fat"** (Leviticus 1:8).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְ״רֹאשׁוֹ וּפִדְרוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לְרֹאשׁ וּפֶדֶר שֶׁקּוֹדְמִין לְכׇל הַנְּתָחִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְאֶת פִּדְרוֹ וְעָרַךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if so, **why do I** need the verse cited at the end of the *baraita*: **"Its head and its fat"?** The Gemara answers: The verse **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that the head and the fat precede all the** other **pieces**when the sacrificial portions are sacrificed on the altar? **The verse states: "With its head and its fat, and** the priest **shall arrange** them" (Leviticus 1:12).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפֶדֶר קַמָּא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? חוֹפֶה אֶת הַפֶּדֶר עַל בֵּית הַשְּׁחִיטָה וּמַעֲלֵהוּ, וְזֶהוּ דֶּרֶךְ כָּבוֹד שֶׁל מַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need **the first** mention of **fat that the Merciful One writes:** "The pieces, the head, and the fat" (Leviticus 1:8)? Wasn't the derivation from that verse restricted to the head? The Gemara answers that it is **necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **How does** the priest who elevates the sacrificial portions of the animal to the altar **perform** that task? **He** uses **the fat** to **cover the place of slaughter,** i.e., to conceal the bloody neck, **and elevates** the head to the top of the altar, **and that is a deferential manner** toward **the Most High.**

Rabbi Chiya offers yet another derivation, this time from a *braita says*: minayin lishchita min ha-tzavar — how do we know shechita is from the neck? He cites a verse about the olah: ve-archu bnei Aharon ha-kohanim et ha-netachim et ha-rosh ve-et ha-pader — the kohanim shall arrange the pieces and the head and the fat. The question arises: why does the verse single out the head? The head is merely one of the pieces of the animal — it should be included in the general category of netachim. The *Torah describes* the process: flay the olah, cut it into pieces, place the pieces on the fire. But the head, as Rabbi Chiya argues, was not part of the same process of flaying and dismemberment. The head was already separated in the act of shechita, and therefore it conceptually falls outside the category of netachim produced by the subsequent flaying and severing. Hence the Torah must single it out explicitly to tell us that it too goes on the fire.

The Gemara then draws the inference: mi-klal de-shechita min ha-tzavar — evidently shechita is from the neck, since otherwise the head would not have been treated as already separately processed. The reasoning is not circular, despite initial appearances. The question is: why would the Torah ever need to single out the head from the other pieces? The only compelling explanation is that the head was already in some sense separated prior to the dismemberment process. And the only way that could be true is if shechita occurred at the neck, creating a significant degree of separation between the head and the body. Rashi acknowledges that the head is not fully severed — the backbone is not cut through — but argues that the degree of detachment is sufficient to render it conceptually separate.

The Gemara then resolves an internal tension in the *braita says*: the opening cites one verse about the head and fat, while the conclusion cites a slightly different verse using the possessive form its head and its fat. The resolution is that the two verses each teach something distinct. The first teaches that the head, despite not being dismembered together with the rest of the animal, is nonetheless placed on the fire. The second teaches that the head and fat are placed on the fire first, based on the juxtaposition of et rosho ve-et pidro ve-arach. And why does each verse mention fat alongside the head? A third *braita says* teaches: chofeh et ha-pader al beit ha-shechita uma'alehu — one covers the place of the severing with the fat before placing the head on the fire. The exposed site of the cut is not aesthetically fitting to be offered to God as is, and so the fat is used to cover it. Ve-zeh derech kavod shel ma'alah — this is a respectful manner of presenting it.

This drasha from the olah is more satisfying than the preceding word-plays. It requires no forced etymology; it reasons directly from the structure of the sacrificial procedure. One limitation Tosafot notes is that this drasha does not distinguish between the throat and the back of the neck — it tells us the neck, but not which side. The earlier drashot, for all their awkwardness, at least attempted to address that further specification through the emphasis on blood flow.

## Spectrum Model

### Torah Links Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי תַּנָּא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵהָכָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָעוֹף״, וְכִי בְּאֵיזוֹ תּוֹרָה שָׁוְותָה בְּהֵמָה לָעוֹף וְעוֹף לִבְהֵמָה? בְּהֵמָה מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, עוֹף אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא! עוֹף מְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה, בְּהֵמָה אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה! בְּאֵיזוֹ תּוֹרָה שָׁוְותָה בְּהֵמָה לְעוֹף וְעוֹף לִבְהֵמָה? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה בְּהֵמָה בִּשְׁחִיטָה – אַף עוֹף בִּשְׁחִיטָה. אִי מָה לְהַלָּן בְּרוֹב שְׁנַיִם – אַף כָּאן בְּרוֹב שְׁנַיִם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זֹאת״.
>
> **Translation:** **And this *tanna* cites** proof that slaughter is from the neck **from here: As it is taught** in a *baraita* that the Torah writes with regard to the impurity of carcasses: **"This is the law of the animal, and of the bird"** (Leviticus 11:46), indicating that the two are somehow equated. **But with regard to what law is an animal equal to a bird and a bird to an animal?** The *halakhot* of ritual impurity governing animals and birds are not comparable; **an animal imparts impurity by contact and by carrying,** whereas **a bird does not impart impurity by contact or by carrying.** Furthermore, **a bird renders** the **garments** of one who swallows it **ritually impure** when it is **in the throat; an animal does not render** one's **garments impure** when it is **in the throat. With regard to what law is an animal equal to a bird and a bird to an animal?** The verse comes **to say to you: Just as an animal** avoids the impurity of being an unslaughtered carcass **through slaughter, so too, a bird** avoids the impurity of being an unslaughtered carcass **through slaughter.** The Gemara objects: **If** so, say, based on the same juxtaposition: **Just as there,** in the case of an animal, it avoids the impurity **through** the cutting of **the majority of two** *simanim*, i.e., the windpipe and the gullet, **so too here,** in the case of a bird, it avoids the impurity **through** the cutting of **the majority of two** *simanim*. The Gemara explains that **the verse states: "This** is the law," to restrict the scope of the juxtaposition in the sense that not all of the *halakhot* of birds and animals are equal.

The Gemara now presents a *braita says* concerning the verse zot torat ha-beheimah ve-ha-of — this is the law of the mammal and the bird — which appears in the context of tumah. The *braita says* asks: in what respect are mammals and birds ever comparable in terms of how they impart tumah? In fact, they are not comparable at all. A dead mammal — a nevela — conveys tumah through maga and masa, touch and carrying. A dead bird does not convey tumah in that way. Conversely, a dead bird that has not been slaughtered properly conveys tumah be-vet ha-bli'a — when it is swallowed and passing through the esophagus — and this tumah extends even to the garments one is wearing at the moment of swallowing, making them a rishon. A dead mammal does not convey tumah in this manner.

So the *braita says* asks: since the tumah laws of mammals and birds are so different, why does the Torah use a single heading — this is the law of the mammal and the bird — as if they share a common law? *Lomar lach*: mah beheimah bi-shechita af of bi-shechita — just as mammals require shechita, so too birds require shechita. And this is where the verse connecting them becomes relevant: in the context of tumah, a corpse is no longer a nevela if it has been properly slaughtered. The same holds for both mammals and birds. Shechita is the link.

One might then ask: should the equation be complete? Perhaps birds too require two simanim, exactly as mammals? Talmud lomar: zot — this, the limiting word, indicates that the equation is not total. Shechita is required for birds, but not in the identical manner as for mammals. This is the first of two readings of the verse; it establishes that birds require shechita but does not specify what shechita means for birds and does not provide a new source for the idea of shechita at the neck.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בְּאֵיזוֹ תּוֹרָה שָׁוְותָה בְּהֵמָה לְעוֹף וְעוֹף לִבְהֵמָה? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה עוֹף הֶכְשֵׁרוֹ מִן הַצַּוָּאר, אַף בְּהֵמָה הֶכְשֵׁרָהּ מִן הַצַּוָּאר. אִי מָה לְהַלָּן ״מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף״, אַף כָּאן ״מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף״? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּמָלַק אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ מִמּוּל עׇרְפּוֹ וְלֹא יַבְדִּיל״ – רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל זֶה מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף, וְאֵין רֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל אַחַר מִמּוּל עוֹרֶף.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues. **Rabbi Eliezer says: With regard to what law is an animal equal to a bird and a bird to an animal?** The verse comes **to say to you: Just as** in the case of **a bird, its fitness** for sacrifice and for consumption is accomplished through pinching and slaughter **from the neck,** as the Torah states with regard to bird offerings that one pinches off its head from the neck, **so too,** in the case of **an animal, its fitness** for sacrifice and for consumption is accomplished through slaughter **from the neck. If** so, say, based on the same juxtaposition: **Just as there,** in the case of a bird, the pinching is performed **adjacent to** the **nape** of the neck, **so too here,** with regard to an animal, the slaughter is performed **adjacent to** the **nape** of the neck and not from the throat. The Gemara explains that therefore, **the verse states** with regard to a bird: **"And pinch off its head adjacent to its nape, but shall not divide it asunder"** (Leviticus 5:8), from which it is derived: **Its head,** i.e., the bird's head, is pinched **adjacent** to the **nape, but the head of another,** the animal, **is not** cut **adjacent** to the **nape.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, הַאי ״זֹאת״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? אִי לָאו ״זֹאת״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מָה עוֹף בְּסִימָן אֶחָד, אַף בְּהֵמָה בְּסִימָן אֶחָד, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״זֹאת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Eliezer, what does he do with this** term: **"This** is the law," from which the first *tanna* restricted the scope of the juxtaposition between animals and birds? The Gemara answers: **If not** for the derivation from the term **"This** is the law," **I would say: Just as** the fitness of **a bird** is accomplished **by** cutting **one of the *simanim* that must be severed in ritual slaughter,** i.e., either the windpipe or the gullet, **so too,** the fitness of **an animal** is accomplished **by** cutting **one *siman*.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "This** is the law," to restrict the juxtaposition.

Rabbi Eliezer reads the same verse differently. He too asks: in what way are mammals and birds similar? And he answers: mah of hechshero min ha-tzavar af beheimah hechsheirah min ha-tzavar — just as the validation of a bird is from the neck, so too for a mammal. Where do we know that shechita for a bird is from the neck? From melikah. The Torah explicitly describes an act of ritual slaughter in only one place — the melikah of bird offerings, where it says u-malak oto mi-mul orpo — you shall perform melikah from the back of its neck. This is the single instance in which the Torah overtly describes a killing procedure for a korban. Rabbi Eliezer uses it as a model: since melikah is from the neck, and birds and mammals are linked in the verse, the same applies to mammalian shechita — it too is from the neck. Might we then say it must be from the back of the neck, as in melikah? The Torah distinguishes: the verse about melikah singles out that one — the bird korban — as being from the back of its neck, implying that other forms of slaughter are not from the back of the neck, but from the throat. Rabbi Eliezer's drasha is considered the most compelling of all those presented, because it is the one case in which the Torah does explicitly describe a slaughter procedure, and the derivation by analogy has real force.

The Gemara then asks: what does Rabbi Eliezer do with the limiting word zot in the verse? He uses it to prevent the complete equation from running in the other direction: without zot, one might have said — since we are equating birds and mammals — that just as a bird requires only one siman, so too a mammal requires only one siman. The word zot prevents this: the equation is only for the requirement of shechita at the neck, not for the number of simanim. The mammal remains at two.

### Bar Kappara: Spectrum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָעוֹף״ – הֵטִיל הַכָּתוּב לְעוֹף בֵּין בְּהֵמָה לְדָגִים, לְחַיְּיבוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי סִימָנִין אִי אֶפְשָׁר – שֶׁכְּבָר הוּקַּשׁ לְדָגִים, לְפוֹטְרוֹ בְּלֹא כְּלוּם אִי אֶפְשָׁר – שֶׁכְּבָר הוּקַּשׁ לִבְהֵמָה. הָא כֵּיצַד הֶכְשֵׁרוֹ? בְּסִימָן אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara proceeds to discuss the source for the slaughter of non-sacred birds. **Bar Kappara teaches** that the verse states: **"This is the law of the animal, and of the bird,** and of every living creature that moves in the waters, and of every creature that swarms upon the earth" (Leviticus 11:46). **The verse situated the bird between** the **animal and** the **fish. To require** the cutting of the **two *simanim*** that must be severed in ritual slaughter, i.e., the windpipe and the gullet, for the slaughter of a bird, **is impossible, as it was already juxtaposed to fish,** which do not require slaughter at all. **To exempt it with nothing,** i.e., to exempt the bird from slaughter altogether, **is impossible, as it was already juxtaposed to** the **animal. How,** then, **is fitness** of a bird for consumption accomplished? It is rendered fit **with** the cutting of **one *siman*.**

Bar Kappara taught a sweeping conceptual framework grounded in the same verse: zot torat ha-beheimah ve-ha-of, and the end of the pasuk adds u-ve-chol nefesh ha-chayah ha-romeset ba-mayim — and all living creatures in the water. The verse thus lists three categories: mammals, birds, and fish. Bar Kappara derives from this ordering a spectrum of shechita requirements. The Torah placed birds between mammals and fish. To require two simanim of a bird, as with a mammal, is impossible — shekevar hukash le-dagim — because on one side birds are linked to fish, and fish require no shechita at all. To exempt birds entirely from any shechita is equally impossible — shekevar hukash le-beheimah — because on the other side birds are linked to mammals. *ha keitzad* — how does one resolve this? Hechshiro be-siman echad — one falls in the middle of the spectrum, and thus one siman suffices for a bird.

This is an elegant and systematic piece of reasoning, but it rests on two premises that still need to be established: that fish require zero shechita, and that mammals require two simanim. These endpoints define the spectrum. The Gemara will address the first; the second, as we shall see, remains grounded ultimately in tradition.

### Fish & Asifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּגִים דְּלָאו בְּנֵי שְׁחִיטָה נִינְהוּ, מְנָלַן? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב: ״הֲצֹאן וּבָקָר יִשָּׁחֵט לָהֶם אִם אֶת כׇּל דְּגֵי הַיָּם יֵאָסֵף לָהֶם״, בַּאֲסִיפָה בְּעָלְמָא סַגִּי לְהוּ. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, גַּבֵּי שְׂלָיו דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּאַסְפוּ אֶת הַשְּׂלָיו״, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָאו בִּשְׁחִיטָה? וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: לְפוֹטְרוֹ בִּוְלֹא כְּלוּם אִי אֶפְשָׁר, שֶׁכְּבָר הוּקַּשׁ לִבְהֵמָה! הָתָם לָא כְּתִיבָא אֲסִיפָה בִּמְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה דְּאַחֲרִינֵי, הָכָא כְּתִיבָא אֲסִיפָה בִּמְקוֹם שְׁחִיטָה דְּאַחֲרִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that **fish are not subject to slaughter? If we say** that it is **because it is written: "If flocks and herds be slaughtered for them…or if all the fish of the sea be gathered together for them,** will they suffice them" (Numbers 11:22), which indicates that **mere gathering suffices for them,** that is not a proof. The Gemara clarifies: **But if that is so, with regard to quail** as well, concerning **which it is written:** "And the people rose up…**and gathered the quail"** (Numbers 11:32), **so too,** would one say with regard to birds **that,** like fish, their fitness is **not** accomplished **with slaughter?** The Gemara responds with a question. **But didn't you say: To exempt** birds from slaughter altogether **with nothing is impossible, as it was already juxtaposed to** the **animal?** The Gemara answers: **There, gathering** of quail **is not written in** the **context** of the **slaughter of others;** therefore, gathering is not to be understood as an alternative to slaughtering the birds. **Here, gathering** of fish **is written in** the **context** of the **slaughter of others,** i.e., the flocks and herds, which indicates that gathering is an alternative to slaughter.

How do we know fish require no formal shechita? The Gemara cites the verse in which Moshe speaks to God about feeding Bnei Yisrael: ha-tzon u-vacar yishachet lahem ve-im et kol degei ha-yam ye'asef lahem u-matza lahem — if you slaughter all the cattle and flock for them, and if you gather all the fish of the sea for them, will it be enough? The parallel structure is telling: cattle and flock are slaughtered; fish are merely gathered. Ba-asifa be-alma sagi lehu — gathering alone suffices for fish.

The Gemara challenges this: the word gather is also used in connection with the slav — the quail — as in va-ya'asfu et ha-slav. Would we then say quail requires no shechita? The Gemara distinguishes: in that case, the word gather does not appear in parallel to slaughter performed on other creatures. Here, by contrast, the gathering of fish is explicitly set against the slaughtering of cattle and flock. The parallel is the point; gathering in the verse about fish is the functional equivalent of what slaughter is for mammals. Therefore, ba-asifa be-alma sagi lehu — for fish, mere gathering from the water is sufficient, and no formal shechita is required.

Some have playfully entertained the question of whether the act of asifa — gathering from the water — is itself the required act for fish, raising the theoretical puzzle of what one's status would be if one were to eat a fish directly in the water without ever removing it. The simple reading of the Gemara, however, is unambiguous: lav bnei shechita ninhu — fish are simply not subject to the concept of shechita at all.

### Birds: One Siman

With fish established at zero and mammals understood to require two simanim — a point that, as the Gemara will acknowledge, is itself ultimately rooted in tradition — Bar Kappara's framework is complete. Birds, positioned between these two endpoints by the Torah's own formulation in the verse, require one siman. The framework is clean and appealing, though the Gemara will note tomorrow that the mammals-require-two end of the spectrum is likewise not derivable from a verse and must be learned from tradition.

## Birds from Mud

### Galilean Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ עוֹבֵר גָּלִילָאָה: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁנִּבְרֵאת מִן הַיַּבָּשָׁה – הֶכְשֵׁרָהּ בִּשְׁנֵי סִימָנִים, דָּגִים שֶׁנִּבְרְאוּ מִן הַמַּיִם – הֶכְשֵׁירָן בִּוְלֹא כְּלוּם, עוֹף שֶׁנִּבְרָא מִן הָרְקָק – הֶכְשֵׁרוֹ בְּסִימָן אֶחָד. אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל קַפּוֹטְקָאָה: תֵּדַע, שֶׁהֲרֵי עוֹפוֹת יֵשׁ לָהֶן קַשְׂקֶשֶׂת בְּרַגְלֵיהֶם כַּדָּגִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **a passerby from the Galilee taught: Fitness** for consumption of **animals, which were created from the** dry **land,** is accomplished **through** cutting **two *simanim*,** the gullet and the windpipe. **Fitness** for consumption of **fish, which were created from the water,** is accomplished **with nothing,** as no slaughter is required. **Fitness** for consumption of **birds, which were created from mud [*harekak*],** a combination of dry land and water, is accomplished **through** cutting **one *siman*. Rav Shmuel of Cappadocia says: Know** that **birds** were created from a combination of dry land and water, **as they have scales on their feet like fish.**

An itinerant Galilean preacher — an over Gelila'ah, a transient darshan whose name is not even recorded — offers a theological grounding for the same spectrum. His framework is not hermeneutical but cosmological: the number of simanim required reflects the substance from which each creature was created. Behemah shenivret min ha-yabashah — mammals were created from solid earth — and therefore require two simanim; they possess the most substantial and robust form of life. Dagim shenivru min ha-mayim — fish were created from water — and are the least substantial, and therefore hechshiran be-lo klum — require nothing. Of shenivra min ha-rakak — birds were created from mud, a mixture of earth and water — and therefore occupy a middle position, requiring one siman. The shechita requirement tracks the ontological weight of the creature.

### Scales on Legs

Rav Shmuel Kaputka adds an empirical observation in support of the claim that birds were created from mud: teida she-harei ofot yesh bahem kaskeset be-ragleihem ke-dagim — you can verify this because birds have a type of scales on their legs, similar to fish scales. The legs of a chicken, for instance, exhibit a scaly texture reminiscent of fish scales — a visible remnant of the birds' dual origin from earth and water, the same mud that was their source of creation.

It is worth pausing at this juncture to note another conceptual puzzle raised in passing. The continuum framework — more substantial life requires more simanim — raises a question about the esophagus specifically. Cutting the trachea produces rapid death through asphyxiation. But cutting the esophagus, in isolation, does not directly kill the animal; death would follow only after a prolonged period of starvation. The connection between shechita and immediate killing is therefore not fully symmetrical across both simanim. The Gemara appears to assume that greater vitality demands more cutting, but the mechanism by which the esophagus contributes to death as part of the shechita act is not self-evident from a biological standpoint. This is not a question the Gemara resolves here, but it is worth holding in mind.

### Two Narratives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד שְׁאֵלוֹ, כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֱלֹהִים יִשְׁרְצוּ הַמַּיִם שֶׁרֶץ נֶפֶשׁ חַיָּה וְעוֹף יְעוֹפֵף״, אַלְמָא מִמַּיָּא אִיבְּרוֹ, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּצֶר ה׳ אֱלֹהִים מִן הָאֲדָמָה כׇּל חַיַּת הַשָּׂדֶה וְאֵת כׇּל עוֹף הַשָּׁמַיִם״, אַלְמָא מֵאַרְעָא אִיבְּרוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an excerpt of an exchange between a Roman government official and Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai. **And furthermore,** the official **asked** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai: **One verse states: "And God said: Let the waters swarm with swarms of living creeping animals, and birds will fly"** (Genesis 1:20); **apparently** birds **were created from the water. And it is written: "And from the ground the Lord God formed every beast of the field, and every bird of the air** and brought them unto the man to see what he would call them" (Genesis 2:19); **apparently** birds **were created from the land.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מִן הָרְקָק נִבְרְאוּ. רָאָה תַּלְמִידָיו מִסְתַּכְּלִים זֶה בָּזֶה, אָמַר לָהֶם: קָשֶׁה בְּעֵינֵיכֶם שֶׁדָּחִיתִי אֶת אוֹיְבִי בְּקַשׁ? מִן הַמַּיִם נִבְרְאוּ, וְלָמָּה הֱבִיאָן אֶל הָאָדָם? לִקְרוֹת לָהֶן שֵׁם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **said to him: They were created from the mud. He saw his students looking at each other,** wondering. **He said to them: Does it trouble you that I dismissed my enemy with a flimsy** pretext? Actually, it is **from water** that birds **were created. And why** does the verse state that they were formed from the ground and that God **brought them to Adam?** In other words, why are they mentioned in the second verse? It is not because they were actually formed from the ground, but only because they were brought to Adam so that **he would call them names.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: בְּלָשׁוֹן אַחֵר אָמַר לְאוֹתוֹ הֶגְמוֹן, וּבַלָּשׁוֹן הָרִאשׁוֹן אָמַר לָהֶן לְתַלְמִידָיו, מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב עַל ״וַיִּצֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** that Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai spoke **to that officer with a different formulation,** i.e., he said to him that the birds were created from the water. **And he stated the first formulation,** that the birds were created from the mud, **to his students, because it is written: "And** from the ground the Lord God **formed** every beast of the field, and every bird of the air" (Genesis 2:19). According to this explanation, the birds are mentioned there not only because Adam called them names, but also because they too were created from the ground.

The Gemara now presents the origin of the claim that birds were created from mud, through a story about Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai. A certain Roman official — referred to as a hēgemōn — posed to Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai a contradiction between two verses in Bereishit. Perek 1 states: va-yomer Elokim yishretzu ha-mayim sheretz nefesh chaya ve-of ye'ofef — God said let the waters teem with living creatures, and birds flying above. This verse clearly implies that birds were created from water. Perek 2, however, states: va-yitzer Hashem Elokim min ha-adama kol chayat ha-sadeh ve-et kol of ha-shamayim — God formed from the ground all the animals of the field and all the birds of the sky. This verse attributes the creation of birds to the ground.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai's initial answer was: min ha-rakak nivre — they were created from mud, which is a mixture of water and earth, thereby harmonizing both verses. But when his students looked at one another in astonishment — apparently finding this answer dismissive — Rabbi Yochanan explained himself. He told them: kasha be-eineichem she-dachiti et oyvi be-kaneh — is it troubling to you that I dismissed my antagonist with a reed, a straw? The implication is that he gave the hēgemōn a deflecting answer rather than his real one.

### Mud Resolution

The real answer, which Rabbi Yochanan gave his students, is that the two narratives in Bereishit are serving different purposes. Perek 1 describes the process of creation and is specific: birds emerged from the water. Perek 2, by contrast, is a narrative centered on man — ve-lama hevi'an el ha-adam? *Likro lahem shem* — it describes how God brought the animals and birds to man so that man could name them. Perek 2 is not offering a precise account of how each creature came into being; it is describing the relationship between man and the created world. Therefore, the phrase min ha-adama in perek 2 need not be taken as a precise statement of how birds were formed, and there is no contradiction.

However, the Gemara then records a minority view that reverses the attribution of the two answers: vi-yesh omrim, some say that Rabbi Yochanan gave the mud answer to his students as the genuine resolution, not as a dismissal. According to this view, since perek 2 does say that God formed from the ground — and this cannot simply be ignored with respect to birds — the truth is that both texts are correct in their own way: perek 1 says water, perek 2 says ground. The resolution is mud. Birds were created from the rakak, the muddy swamp, that mixture of earth and water in which the two elements combine. And it is precisely this hybrid origin that places birds in the middle of the ontological spectrum, between the earth-rooted mammals and the water-born fish, and that grounds the requirement of one siman — the middle point between two and zero.
