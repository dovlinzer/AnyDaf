# Menachot 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Asham Metzora Shelo Lish…

### Debate Setup

The Gemara picks up with a fascinating debate that one might have expected to encounter in Masechet Zevachim, yet here we are, near the close of Menachot, finding a key piece of the discussion. The foundational rule from Zevachim is well known: if one shechts an animal shelo lishma, the korban is kosher but the owner does not fulfill his obligation. The question the Gemara now presses is how much the korban retains its distinctive identity when offered shelo lishma. Does it become, in some sense, a generic korban, or does it fully retain the particular character with which it was designated?

The Gemara frames the question concretely: if one shechts the asham of a metzora shelo lishma, must one still bring the accompanying mincha? A normal asham metzora is invalid without its mincha. But if the korban is no longer functioning as his asham metzora — because it was offered shelo lishma — has it shed that identity? The conclusion stated is that yes, one must still bring the mincha, because it retains its identity. The Gemara then pushes further: what about an olah that accompanies the omer, which normally receives a double mincha — if offered shelo lishma, would the double mincha still be required? This question assumes that shelo lishma causes a korban to become more generic, losing its highly particular character.

### Abaye's Position

The first position presented is that of Abaye: even when a korban is offered shelo lishma, it fully retains its distinctive identity in every respect. The owner is simply not yotzei — it functions as a free-will offering in the sense that no obligation is discharged — but the korban itself is treated entirely normally, with all attendant requirements. Nothing changes about how it is handled or what accompanies it.

### Rava's Distinction

Rava draws a different distinction. He holds that when an olah or shelamim is offered shelo lishma, it does in fact lose its particular identity and becomes a generic olah or generic shelamim. Thus the olah accompanying the omer, if offered shelo lishma, no longer requires the double mincha; instead one brings the standard minchat nesachim, because the korban has shed its specific identity and can be understood as a free-will olah. The same logic applies: lo chazi le-olat chova — if it cannot function as the obligatory olah it was designated for, karvi le-olat nedava — it shifts into the category of a generic free-will olah.

### Asham Uniqueness

The asham metzora, however, is a different case entirely, and this is where Rava's analysis becomes pointed. Normally, shelo lishma causes a korban to lose its particular identity because it can then become a generic version of that korban type — a generic olah or generic shelamim. But asham nedava mi ika? There is no such thing as a free-will asham. One cannot bring an asham as a vow or free-will offering. Therefore, if the asham metzora sheds its distinctive identity, it has nowhere to go — it cannot become a generic asham because no such category exists. Consequently, it must retain its identity as an asham metzora. It is still specifically Reuven's asham metzora; he is simply not yotzei his obligation. It remains sufficiently his asham metzora that it may be offered — after all, one cannot bring a generic asham — and yet it falls short of fulfilling his obligation.

This yields a coherent conceptual picture: an asham shelo lishmo is enough his korban that it is valid to bring, since without retaining some identity it could not be brought at all, yet not sufficient to achieve the kapara and discharge his obligation.

One might ask why a chatas shelo lishma cannot be analyzed similarly — that it is still the owner's chatas, just not achieving kapara, yet sufficiently his that it can still be offered. The Gemara learns from a specific pasuk that a chatas does not work this way. However, the Gemara never really supplies a satisfying conceptual distinction between asham and chatas on this point. Indeed, this is not a new difficulty — at the beginning of Zevachim, Reish Lakish is described as falling prostrate in the beis medrash, unable to resolve the question of why asham should differ from chatas. There is a tanna who holds that they are in fact not different, but the Gemara does not arrive at a fully worked-out answer.

### Braita Support

The Gemara notes that a braita supports the view of Rabbi Yochanan on this matter: tanya kevaseih de-Rabbi Yochanan. The braita states that an asham metzora she-shechato shelo lishmo — that was slaughtered shelo lishmo — or where the blood was not applied to the behonot of the metzora, and thus the owner is not yotzei, nevertheless harei zeh oleh legabi mizbeach — it still goes up on the mizbeach — v'ta'un nesachim — it still requires its nesachim — v'tzarich asham acher lehatiro — and another asham is needed to complete the metzora's purification.

It is worth distinguishing the two cases embedded in the braita. The case of shelo nasan mi-damo al gabi ha-metzora — where the blood was not applied to the metzora's extremities — is the more intuitive case. There the korban itself was performed correctly as an asham metzora; it is simply that the tahara ritual was not completed. The asham was fully his, brought normally, but because the blood was not used for the purification process, another asham is needed. That case makes obvious sense as a full asham metzora. The chiddush of the braita is that it inserts shelo lishmo into the same category, teaching that even shelo lishmo the asham metzora retains its full identity — it is brought normally, requires its accompanying mincha, is fully valid as a korban — yet the owner is not yotzei his korban obligation and requires another asham.

### Asham vs. Chatas

The deeper conceptual question — why asham retains its identity shelo lishma while chatas does not — remains unresolved. The Gemara's functional answer is clear: an asham must retain its identity because there is no generic asham to which it could default. But the logical asymmetry with chatas is harder to explain, and the Gemara, as noted, does not provide a fully satisfying account. What we can say is that the asham framework requires the korban to remain anchored to a particular person and circumstance — you are a metzora, you are a nazir, it is your asham — and this anchoring is sufficient to allow it to be offered, even if insufficient to produce the requisite halachic effect of kapara or tahara.

## Measurement Vessels

### Mishna Intro

The Gemara moves to the next mishna, which addresses the measuring vessels of the mikdash. Kol midos she-ba-mikdash hayu nigdashos chutz mi-shel Kohen Gadol she-hayu godesh le-sochah — all the measures in the mikdash were heaped, except for those of the Kohen Gadol, which were leveled. As Rashi explains, the Kohen Gadol's chavitin — the daily pan-offering — must be divided into two halves and poured into separate vessels; a heaped measure would cause spillage, so the measure is leveled. Here the heapingness remains within the vessel walls rather than rising above them.

This brings the Gemara to the broader question that has been developing around whether material that extends beyond the lip of a measuring vessel — the beirutz — acquires kedusha. The mishna distinguishes between liquid and dry measures in this regard. Midat halach beyrotzayan kadosh — for liquid measures, the overage is sanctified. Umidat hayavesh beyrotzayan chol — for dry measures, the overage is not sanctified. This distinction is striking. One way to think about it is that liquid, by its very nature, takes the shape of its container. A liquid measure holds the liquid in a way that a dry measure does not hold its contents to the same degree. A liquid maintains its shape only because of the vessel; remove the vessel and the liquid spills. Solid material, by contrast, retains its form independent of the container. The heap on top of a dry measure does not need the vessel in the same way — it sits on the pile beneath it, not on the vessel walls. Along similar lines, the excess liquid adheres to the vessel through surface tension in a way that excess solid does not. This conceptual difference may explain why liquid measures are more naturally understood as defining and containing their contents, and why the overage of a liquid measure is more intimately connected to the vessel than the overage of a dry measure.

The mishna also raises the question of why liquid measures were considered klei sharet and dry measures were not — a distinction that will be addressed in what follows, though the mishna itself states the fact without explaining its basis.

### Source Attribution

The Gemara asks: mani — whose view underlies our mishna? The difficulty is as follows. Rabbi Meir held that there were two dry measures in the mikdash for the issaron — one for heaping and one for leveling. If so, the mishna's language referring to all the measures being heaped implies multiple vessels. But if it is Rabbi Meir, chad gadush havei — there was only one heaping vessel, not many. Conversely, if the mishna reflects the view of the Rabbis, who do not hold that there were two types of dry measures, then chad umachuk havei — there was only one measure and it was leveled — and the mishna's language of all measures being heaped is even harder to understand.

### Single Vessel Theory

Rav Chisda resolves the difficulty: le'olam Rabbi Meir — the mishna indeed follows Rabbi Meir. And what does kol midos mean? Not that there were many vessels, but that all measurings — every time the vessel was used — were done with heaping, except for the Kohen Gadol's. The mishna is describing a practice, not a plurality of vessels.

Admittedly, this reading has a practical difficulty: if there was only one vessel for the issaron, it is hard to imagine the mikdash operating efficiently with many people bringing minchos at the same time. There is also the matter of residue — dry material leaves traces in a vessel, raising a question of whether one person's flour mingles with the next. These are real practical concerns, though the Gemara does not address them here. The simplest reading of the Gemara does appear to envision a single vessel used for all measurings, which is a somewhat surprising picture practically, whatever its halachic clarity.

## Liquid vs. Dry Measures

### Tanna Kama

The Gemara now explains the three-way debate among the tanna'im that underlies the mishna's distinction between liquid and dry measures regarding the beirutz. The tanna kama holds that when the liquid measures were consecrated — anointed with shemen hamishcha — they were anointed both on the inside and the outside, including the lip. The dry measures, by contrast, were anointed only on the inside, not the outside. The overage of a liquid measure is sanctified because it contacts the outer lip of the vessel, which was itself sanctified; the overage of a dry measure is not sanctified because only the interior was consecrated, and the heap on top does not contact a sanctified surface.

The inner logic of this position — that sanctification travels through the lip to the beirutz — works more naturally for liquids than for solids. With a liquid, the overage forms a meniscus and adheres to the vessel walls, connecting it to the outer lip. With dry material heaped on top, the excess sits on the pile beneath it and has no particular contact with the vessel's rim, so even if the outer surface were anointed, it is not clear how that would sanctify what rests above it.

An alternative conceptual framing that emerged in discussion is worth noting: once the outside of the vessel is sanctified, it is not merely the outer wall that is consecrated but the very concept of "outside" in relation to that vessel — and the beirutz, being outside the measured quantity, falls within that conceptual domain.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva agrees with the tanna kama that liquid measures were anointed inside and out. However, he goes further regarding the dry measures: midas hayavesh lo nimshacha kol ikar — dry measures were not anointed at all and were never klei sharet. For Rabbi Akiva, the absence of sanctification in the overage of a dry measure is not merely because the outside was not anointed; it reflects the fact that the vessel itself never had the status of a kli sharet. The flour placed in it was not thereby sanctified. Sanctification of the flour would occur only at a later stage, when the flour was combined with oil in a liquid vessel — at that point it enters a sanctified vessel of the appropriate type and acquires kedusha. But as dry flour in a dry measuring vessel, it remains unsanctified.

Rabbi Akiva thus expands the scope of the disagreement beyond the beirutz. It is not just that the overage did not acquire kedusha — nothing placed in the dry measure acquired kedusha through the vessel at all.

### Rabbi Yossi

Rabbi Yossi takes yet another position: idi ve'idi nimshacha mibifnim velo nimshacha mibachutz — both the liquid and dry measures were anointed only on the inside, not the outside. Yet the liquid overage is still sanctified, and the reason has nothing to do with the lip. Ve-hacha hainu taama delach ne'ekar — the reason the liquid overage is sanctified is that the liquid within a vessel is displaced. When liquid is poured in, the newly added liquid pushes the existing liquid upward; umigo demana ka'ati — what appears on top as the overage was formerly inside the vessel. It was already sanctified by being in the vessel's interior, and its current position above the rim is simply the result of having been displaced. Vehayavesh eino ne'ekar — with dry material, there is no such displacement. The flour heaped on top was never inside the vessel; it rests on what is below it without ever having entered and been sanctified by the vessel walls.

This is a notably elegant explanation.

### Intent Challenge

The Gemara poses a fundamental challenge applicable to any position that holds the overage is sanctified: ve-chi ne'ekar mai havei — even granting that the liquid was displaced and formerly inside the vessel, why should that matter? Gavra lemai detzarich ka mechabiv — when a person measures out a log of oil, he intends to sanctify a log, not a log and a bit. The overage exceeds his intention. Why should the vessel sanctify more than what the person intended to sanctify?

### Rav Dimi Answer

Rav Dimi bar Shishna answers in the name of Rav: zos omeres — this teaches us that klei shares mekadeshin shelo mada'as — the klei sharet sanctify even without the person's specific intent. One's verbal designation precedes the moment of placing an item into the kli sharet; at the stage of the vessel itself, the vessel operates by its own power and does not require focused intention for each drop that enters. That is why the overage is sanctified — the vessel sanctifies whatever it contains, regardless of whether the person consciously intended to sanctify that precise quantity.

### Ravina Counter

Ravina disagrees: leolam eima lach klei shares ein mekadeshin ella mada'as — klei sharet in truth only sanctify with intention. If one does not intend to sanctify the overage, the overage is not biblically sanctified. Why then does the mishna speak of the beirutz as sanctified? Gezeirah shema yomru motzi'in miklei shares lechol — it is a rabbinic decree. People might observe that the overage is being redeemed or taken out of the context of the klei sharet without restriction, and conclude that items from a kli sharet can generally be treated as non-sacred. To prevent this misimpression, the Rabbis decreed that the overage must be treated as though it were sanctified. The consequence of Ravina's position is that our entire mishna — with its detailed discussion of which beirutz is sanctified and which is not — is not a matter of biblical law but a rabbinic enactment.

This is an unsatisfying conclusion. The simpler and more compelling reading of the mishna is that the klei sharet operate by their own inherent sanctifying power, without requiring a precise mental formulation from the person using them.

This raises a further question: if the beirutz is in truth not biblically sanctified, one might wind up using it directly in a korban — most acutely in the case of wine — without its having proper kedushat haguf. This concern is sharpest for liquids, where the overage might be poured directly onto the mizbeach as a nesach. That the Gemara does not press this question further is itself worth noting.

### Lechem Hapanim Refute

The Gemara challenges Ravina's gezeirah framework from a different direction. Rav Zeira raises a braita: if the lechem hapanim was placed on the shulchan not on Shabbos but on Sunday — perhaps the previous week's arrangements were disrupted — and then the following Shabbos the levona was offered, the bread is invalid. It is akin to mechusar zman — not yet the proper time for offering — since the lechem hapanim must remain on the shulchan for exactly seven days from when it was placed, and a full week has not elapsed from Sunday to the following Shabbos. What should be done? The braita says to leave it until the next Shabbos, because she'afillu hayah al hashulchan yamim rabim ein be'kach klum — even if the bread sat on the shulchan for many days, longer than a week, it does not become pasul. It does not become invalid through linah, staying overnight past its due date.

The Gemara now asks: if we are concerned about appearances — as Ravina's gezeirah claims — then here too we should be concerned. People will see bread resting on the shulchan for eleven, twelve, or more days and conclude that items in a kli sharet never become pasul through linah. Why is there no gezeirah here against this misimpression?

The Gemara answers: penimah chutz ka ramit — you are comparing the inside to the outside. Panim lav kulei alma yadei — the interior of the mikdash, where the shulchan stands, is not public knowledge; not everyone sees what transpires there. Chutz, kulei alma yadei — on the outside, however, everyone sees the measuring vessels being used and sees the overage being handled, and therefore we apply the gezeirah lest the wrong conclusions be drawn.

This remains a notably unsatisfying resolution. The plain sense of the mishna strongly favors the view that the klei sharet sanctify by their own operation, and that the beirutz of a liquid measure is genuinely, biblically sanctified.

## Leftover Nesachim

### Rabbi Chiya

The Gemara cites a mishna elsewhere: motar nesachim lekayitz hamizbeach — the leftover nesachim go toward the kayitz hamizbeach. The kayitz hamizbeach refers to a practice of keeping the mizbeach active during slow periods. On days when few or no obligatory sacrifices are scheduled, it would be unseemly for the mikdash to stand idle; communal funds were therefore used to purchase olos to keep the altar in continuous operation. These are called kayitz hamizbeach — the "summer provision" or "dessert" of the altar, when activity might otherwise languish.

What constitutes the leftover nesachim that fund this practice? Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef says: beirutei midot — the beirutz, the overage of the measuring vessels. The amounts that accumulated above the lips of the vessels over time were collectively redeemed and the proceeds used to purchase these korbanos. An immediate difficulty presents itself: if the beirutz has kedushat haguf, how can it be redeemed? Tosafot addresses this by noting that the chapter opens with a statement that beis din stipulated a condition over these funds, allowing them to be redeemed for the communal purpose of funding the kayitz hamizbeach. Of course, once such a condition is accepted, one of the primary consequences of kedushat haguf — the inability to redeem — has been substantially qualified.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. The overage of the measuring vessels is too small to account meaningfully for the kayitz hamizbeach. Rather, the leftover nesachim refers to ke'ota sheshaninu — to the situation described in another braita involving supply contracts with the mikdash.

### Contract Model

The arrangement worked as follows: a supplier would contract with the mikdash to provide flour at a fixed rate — say, four se'ah per maneh. This was a fixed-price contract for ongoing supply. Rabbi Yochanan understands the surplus funds as originating from profit generated by the mikdash under these arrangements.

### Mikdash Advantage

The governing principle is sheyad hekdesh al haelyona — the mikdash always has the upper hand. If a supplier contracted to provide flour at the rate of four se'ah per maneh, and the market price of flour subsequently rose so that flour now costs only three se'ah per maneh, the supplier is still obligated to deliver four. He cannot profit from market movements against the mikdash. Conversely, if the supplier contracted at three se'ah per maneh and the market shifted so that flour is now cheaper — four se'ah per maneh — the mikdash benefits from the improved rate and receives four. The mikdash always captures the favorable side of any market fluctuation. 

Thus, when the mikdash's fixed-price contract yields more flour than the market rate would require — because the price of flour fell and they are getting four se'ah where they previously contracted for three — the mikdash ends up with a surplus. This surplus flour, or more precisely the surplus funds it represents when sold, is the motar nesachim that goes to fund the kayitz hamizbeach. According to Rabbi Yochanan, then, the leftover nesachim refers not to the physical beirutz of the measuring vessels but to the profit the mikdash captures through the structural advantage it holds in its supply contracts. Notably, on this reading the leftover is dry goods — flour — rather than liquid, so it is more precisely the flour component of the minchat nesachim that generates the surplus, since nesachim in this context refers to the broader package of wine and flour libations accompanying a korban.

### Kayitz Definition

The Gemara clarifies what these kayitz hamizbeach sacrifices are: olos, fully burnt offerings, habasar l'Hashem — the flesh entirely for God — vehaoros l'kohanim — and the hides going to the kohanim. The Gemara then cites braisos supporting each of the two positions — one supporting Rabbi Chiya bar Yosef's view that the beirutz funds the kayitz hamizbeach, and one supporting Rabbi Yochanan's view that it is the flour surplus from supply contracts. The braita supporting Rabbi Chiya speaks of beyotzei middas — the overage of the measures — and what was done with it: if another sacrifice was available, the overage was used for it; if it remained overnight, it became pasul through linah. The mention of linah disqualifying it indicates that it possessed kedushat haguf. If there was neither another sacrifice nor the risk of linah, then makiytzin bahen es hamizbeach — it was used to keep the altar operating.

The tension between possessing kedushat haguf and yet being redeemed for the kayitz hamizbeach is resolved, as Tosafos notes, through the mechanism of lev beis din — the beis din's stipulated condition at the chapter's outset.

## Minchat Nesachim

### Nesachim Inclusion

The discussion now shifts to a new mishna that addresses the obligation of minchat nesachim — the wine and flour libations that accompany a korban — directly. Kol korbanos hatzibbur vehayachid te'unim nesachim — all communal and individual korbanos require the nesachim, the libations of wine plus the mincha of oil and flour described in Parashat Shelach. The exceptions are: the bechor, the ma'aser, the pesach, the chatas, and the asham. One might wonder why the mishna is framed this way — listing everything that does not require nesachim rather than simply stating that only an olah and shelamim require them. The answer likely lies in the structure of the mishna's conclusion, which turns on a contrast that this framing sets up.

### Metzora Exception

The mishna concludes: ella she-chatat metzora ve-ashamo te'unim nesachim — the one exception to the exceptions is that the chatas and asham of the metzora do require nesachim. Normally chatas and asham are excluded; the metzora's are included. The mishna is thus framed to create maximum contrast: chatas and asham as a general rule do not require nesachim — but not by the metzora, whose chatas and asham do. The use of kol at the outset may also reflect the statistical reality that the vast majority of actual korbanos brought are olos and shelamim — the tamidim, the musafim, most individual vow and free-will offerings — so the formulation "all korbanos require nesachim" captures the practical majority even if the list of exceptions is substantial.

### Pesuk Derivation

The Gemara presents a braita that derives these rules from the pesukim in Shelach. The relevant pasuk reads: ve'asitem isheh l'Hashem Olah o Zevach l'fale neder o vindavah o be-moadeichem — and you shall make a fire-offering to God, an olah or a zevach, whether by vow or free-will offering, or on your festivals. The Gemara works through the derivation systematically.

The word "isheh" — fire-offering — might suggest that anything placed on the fire, even a mincha, requires nesachim. The pasuk immediately specifies olah, indicating that the obligation is limited to animal offerings. The word zevach is then used to extend the requirement to shelamim — a slaughtered offering brought as a zevach. The word "o zevach" rather than "ve-zevach" is read as inclusive of the todah, a form of shelamim.

Having extended the requirement broadly, the pasuk then limits it: l'fale neder o vindavah — through a vow or free-will offering. Only something that comes by neder or nedavah requires nesachim. The categories that do not and cannot come through vows — bechor, ma'aser, pesach, chatas, and asham — are thereby excluded.

### Vow Capability

The braita establishes this principle positively: ba b'neder u-vindavah te'un nesachim — something that comes through a vow or free-will offering requires nesachim. She-eino ba b'neder u-vindavah ein te'un nesachim — something that cannot come through a vow or free-will offering does not require nesachim. This cleanly excludes bechor, ma'aser, pesach, chatas, and asham, none of which can arise through a personal vow.

### Free-Will Limit

Having established the principle, a further refinement is needed. If the requirement is tied to korbanot that come through vows, one might reason that even an olah or shelamim brought as an obligation — not through a vow — should be excluded. The most salient examples are the olas re'iyah, the olah every male must bring upon appearing at the mikdash on the regel, and the shalmei chagiga, the shelamim brought as part of the obligation of rejoicing on the festival. These are obligatory, not vow-offerings. Should they not require nesachim?

The braita addresses this through the continuation of the pasuk: o be-moadeichem — or on your festivals. This teaches that anything coming on the regel that is otherwise of the type that comes through vows — an olah or shelamim — does require nesachim even when brought as an obligation. The obligation of the olas re'iyah and shalmei chagiga does not strip them of the requirement of nesachim. The Gemara is not here addressing the musaf offerings, because separate pesukim in Parashat Pinchas make explicit that those communal musafim are accompanied by nesachim; the hava amina under discussion is at the private level.

### Ben Bakar

The braita continues: having established the scope of the requirement, a new question arises from the continuation of the pasuk — la'asot rei'ach nichoach l'Hashem min habakar o min hatzon — as a pleasing aroma to God, from cattle or sheep. Why does the pasuk need to specify cattle and sheep?

One might have thought that since olah has been mentioned and a bird can be brought as an olah, an olas ha'of — a bird olah — would also require nesachim. The specification min habakar u-min hatzon teaches otherwise: only large animals — cattle and sheep — require nesachim. Birds do not. This is the view of Rabbi Yoshiya.

The pasuk had also said ben bakar — specifically a young bull — within this context. Since ben bakar was already included within the general category, its specification invites a derivation: mah ben bakar meyuchad ba b'neder u-vindavah — just as ben bakar is distinctive in that it can come as a neder or nedavah, af kol ba b'neder u-vindavah — so too only korbanot of the type that come through vows require nesachim. The reference to ben bakar here is understood in context as qualifying the broader rule, since the pasuk has associated it with olah o zevach, which are the free-will categories.

### Rabbi Yonasan

Rabbi Yonasan disagrees with Rabbi Yoshiya's derivation: eino tzarich — there is no need to derive the exclusion of birds from min habakar u-min hatzon. The pasuk already says o zevach, and zevach means something slaughtered — an animal whose neck is cut with a knife. Ha'of eino zevach — a bird is not a zevach; bird offerings are performed differently, through melikah with the kohen's fingernail, not through shechita. Therefore birds are already excluded by the word zevach itself, without need of a separate derivation.

### Vav Resolution

If Rabbi Yonasan does not need min habakar u-min hatzon to exclude birds, what then does this phrase teach? The answer lies in resolving an ambiguity in Parashat Vayikra. At the very beginning of Vayikra, the pasuk reads: adam ki yakriv mikem korban l'Hashem min habehema min habakar u-min hatzon takrivu et korbanchem — if a person brings an offering to God from the animals, from the cattle and from the sheep. The word u-min — "and from" — might be read conjunctively: to bring an offering, one must bring both cattle and sheep. Yachol afilu omer harei alai olah yavi mishneihem — perhaps one who undertakes an olah must bring from both?

That is why the pasuk in Shelach specifies min habakar o min hatzon — using o, "or," to teach that back in Vayikra the vav was also meant as "or," not "and." Rotzeh echad meivi, rotzeh shnayim meivi — one who wishes may bring one type; one who wishes may bring both. The vav in Vayikra does not impose an obligation to bring from both cattle and from sheep; it simply describes the range of permissible animals.
