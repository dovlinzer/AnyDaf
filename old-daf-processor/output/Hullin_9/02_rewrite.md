# Chullin 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Knives

### Knives for Meat/Fat

We begin in the middle of 8b, where the Gemara continues its focus on knives. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: the butcher — הטבח — requires three separate knives. The first, אחת ששוחט בה, is used exclusively for shechita, and must be maintained with particular precision. The second, ואחת שמחתך בה בשר, is used for cutting the meat. Clearly one would not use the shechita knife for chopping meat, as that would ruin the precision instrument required for shechita. The third knife, ואחת שמחתך בה חלבין, is used for cutting the chelev — the forbidden fat.

It is worth clarifying what chelev is. The fat one sees marbled throughout a cut of meat is not chelev; that is שומא, shuma, and is permitted. The chelev that the Torah forbids — with the severity of karet, equivalent to the prohibition of blood — is the fat that surrounds the internal organs, remaining distinct and separate from the meat itself. It lays atop or around the organs rather than being integrated into the tissue. This fat, though forbidden for consumption, is permitted for benefit, and so the butcher would cut it up to sell in the marketplace to non-Jews. That is why the Gemara speaks of cutting the chelev rather than merely removing it — it is being processed for sale.

### Cross-Contamination Risk

The Gemara asks: why not use a single knife for both the cutting of meat and the cutting of fat? After all, the shechita knife is obviously kept separate — using it for chopping would ruin it. But why maintain two distinct chopping knives? The Gemara answers: גזירה שמא יחתוך חלבין ואחר כך בשר — we are concerned that the butcher will cut the fat and then, without properly cleaning the knife, proceed to cut the meat, thereby transferring the chelev to the meat and causing cross-contamination.

### Knife Marking

One might ask: if we require two separate knives, won't the butcher simply confuse which is which — מיחלף ליה — and inadvertently use the fat knife for the meat? The Gemara answers: כיון דצריכינו תרי אית ליה היכירא — precisely because we demand that he maintain two distinct knives, he will take care to mark them and keep them identifiable. If he has two knives to track, he will develop a system to distinguish them. The concern about confusion therefore does not override the requirement for separation.

## Two Wash Vessels

### Separate Vessels

Rav Yehuda said again in the name of Rav: the butcher requires two separate vessels of water — שני קיתונין של מים. One, אחד שמדיח בו בשר, is used to rinse the meat itself, and the other, ואחד שמדיח בו חלבים, is used to rinse the fat. The plain reading of the Gemara is that the meat and fat themselves are being washed in these vessels. Once a vessel has been used to rinse one type of item, the water is no longer clean — it contains residue of either meat or chelev — and washing the other type of item in the same water risks transferring the forbidden fat to the meat.

### Order Concern

The Gemara asks: why not maintain a single vessel and establish a careful sequence — first rinse the meat, then the fat? One could even assume that all the meat is processed before any of the fat, or that between animals the vessel is emptied and refilled. The Gemara responds: לא, גזירה שמא ידיח חלבים ואחר כך בשר — no, we are concerned that the butcher will forget the established order, reverse the sequence, or move on to a second animal without cleaning the vessel. The risk is too great to rely on a single vessel and a remembered procedure.

### Vessel Distinction

As with the knives, one might ask whether having two vessels simply doubles the chance for confusion. The Gemara answers identically: כיון דצריכינו תרי אית ליה היכירא — since we require two separate vessels, the butcher will label or otherwise distinguish them, and confusion will be avoided. The underlying principle in both cases is the same: a formal requirement of separation creates the conditions for vigilance, while a single shared implement invites error.

## Fat on Meat

### Kipli Flanks

Meimar said in the name of Rav Pappa: לא ליסחף איניש קיפלי אלווי בשרא — a person should not place the כפלי, kiflei, atop the meat. Rashi identifies the kiflei as the flanks of the animal in the region of the kidneys — the area where the forbidden fat of the kidneys sits. This fat, tarba, is the same chelev forbidden by the Torah. The concern is that when these fat-laden flanks are placed on top of other meat, gravity will cause the fat to flow downward — דדייץ תרבא ובלע בשרא — and be absorbed into the meat below.

### Gravity Concern

The Gemara challenges this ruling: if we are concerned that the fat will flow from the kiflei into the meat when placed on top, then we should equally be concerned when they are placed side by side — כי טריצי נמי דייב תרבא ובא לבישרא — for the fat will dissolve and flow even in that configuration. Why should the prohibition be limited to placing them on top of one another?

### Membrane Barrier

The Gemara answers: קרומא מפסיק מתתאי — there is a membrane surrounding the fat that acts as a barrier. When the fat-laden portions are merely placed adjacent to the meat, the membrane contains the fat and prevents transfer. The Gemara then asks: but if there is a membrane, why is placing them on top more concerning? Even in that position the membrane should provide a barrier. The Gemara answers: איידי דממשמשא ידיה דטבחא מיפתח — because when the butcher handles the portions and lays them atop the meat, the physical handling causes the membrane to rupture. It is the combination of gravity and the pressure of handling that creates the risk of the fat escaping the membrane and flowing into the meat below. Placing portions side by side involves less handling-induced pressure, and the membrane remains intact. Precisely how the act of placing something on top differs from placing it alongside in terms of membrane integrity is not entirely resolved, but the Gemara understands that the combination of factors creates a meaningfully greater risk.

## Three Skills

### Core Skills

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: תלמיד חכם צריך שיהא למוד שלשה דברים — a student of the sages must know three practical skills: כתב, שחיטה, ומילה — writing, shechita, and milah. These are not conventional rabbinic disciplines in the sense of textual analysis, but practical skills essential for a rabbi serving a community, particularly one in a far-flung locale without ready access to specialists. A rabbi must be able to write, since even basic literacy was not universal; he must be able to perform shechita, since a remote community may have no access to a trained shochet; and he must be able to perform milah, since a community may have no mohel.

### Writing/Ktav

The word כתב, ktav, admits of several interpretations among the Rishonim. Rashi understands it simply as the ability to sign one's name — something not everyone knew how to do — since a talmid chacham might sit on a beit din and be required to sign documents. The עיטור, Ittur, interprets ktav as knowledge of שטרות, shtaros — the ability to draft legal documents — which makes considerable practical sense for a communal rabbi. The מהרש"א, Maharsha, understands it as the ability to write a Sefer Torah. The ים של שלמה, Yam Shel Shlomo, suggests it means the ability to write in a literate and articulate style — a reading that is difficult to derive from the word כתב alone. In any case, the list reflects a curriculum of practical rabbinic competence: whatever you know in learning, you must arrive in a community equipped with skills the community will actually need.

## Ritual Skills

### Tfillin/Tzitzit

Rav Chanina bar Shlamya said in the name of Rav: אף קשר של תפילין, וברכת חתנים, וציצית — in addition to the three already mentioned, one must also know how to tie the knot of the tfillin, how to recite the sheva brachos, and how to attach tzitzit. These are three further practical skills that a community might rely upon its rabbi to perform.

### Rabbi's Role

The inclusion of ברכת חתנים, birchas chasanim, is particularly striking. There is a question throughout halachic literature regarding who recites the sheva brachos — whether it is the chasan or members of the community. The Rambam holds that under the chupah the chasan recites the brachos, while at the meals they are recited by community members. The fact that this Gemara specifically requires the rabbi to know birchas chasanim implies that the rabbi is the one who will be reciting them — and further implies that the rabbi serves in the role of mesader kiddushin. This is one of the few places in the Gemara where such a role for the rabbi is suggested.

On the question of the knot of tfillin: there is a debate in Tosafos about whether the knot must be retied daily. One of the Baalei Tosafos reads the verse וקשרתם, vkshartam, literally as requiring the knot to be redone each day. Tosafos itself cites the fact that this Gemara singles out the knot of tfillin as a skill the rabbi must know as proof against that opinion — for if every person were required to redo the knot daily, everyone would already know how to do it, and there would be no special need to mention it as a rabbinic skill.

### Essential vs. Common

The Gemara asks: why does the first authority list only ktav, shechita, and milah, and not the three additional skills of tfillin, birchas chasanim, and tzitzit? The answer: ואידך הני שכיחי — those latter three are commonplace. Everyone knows how to make birchas chasanim, tie tzitzit, and make the knot of tfillin. The first three skills are the greater חידוש, chiddush — they are specifically the skills that are not naturally assumed and that must be deliberately acquired.

## Shochet Knowledge

### Knowledge Required

Returning now to themes central to the beginning of the masechta, Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: כל טבח שאינו יודע הלכות שחיטה אסור לאכול משחיטתו — any shochet who does not know the laws of shechita, one may not eat from his shechita.

### Five Halachos

The Gemara specifies the five primary halachic concerns of shechita: שהייה — pausing in the middle of the shechita; דרסה — applying pressure to the knife rather than drawing it; חלדה — tunneling, meaning inserting the knife between the trachea and esophagus and cutting from the inside rather than from without; הגרמה — shechting in the wrong location, too high or too low; and עיקור — a term whose precise meaning is debated. Rashi understands ikur as the physical tearing out of one of the simanim — the trachea or esophagus — rather than cutting it. Tosafos objects that this would simply mean the siman was not cut, not that a separate psul occurred, and therefore cites another opinion: that ikur refers to a situation in which one of the simanim has been displaced from its normal anatomical position in the animal while still alive — not enough to render the animal a treifa, but such that the shechita requires the simanim to be in their original locations or it is pasul.

### Knowledge Gap

The Gemara asks: is this ruling not obvious? These five categories are all taught in later Mishnayos — of course a shochet must know them. The Gemara responds: לא צריכא — it is necessary in a specific case. The case is where we have witnessed the shochet perform shechita two or three times with evident skill and precision — ושחט שפיר — and one might therefore assume that since he performs so flawlessly in practice, it does not matter whether he possesses the theoretical knowledge. The Gemara teaches that this assumption is wrong. Even if we observe perfect performance, if he does not know the laws — כיון דלא גמר — we cannot trust him. Why? זימנין דשהי ודריס ולא ידע — there will be occasions where he pauses or applies pressure and will not know that he has done anything wrong. A person who does not know the halachic parameters cannot recognize his own deviations from them, and therefore will never report a problem.

### Competence Presumption

The Gemara earlier established two distinct categories: גמיר, gamir — one who has been tested on the laws — and מומחה, mumcheh — one who has demonstrated practical experience. The present ruling addresses the case where we positively know the shochet does not know the laws: in that case, experience alone is insufficient. But there is a third category: what if we simply do not know whether the shochet knows the laws, and had no opportunity to ask or test him? The Gemara earlier established the principle of רוב מצויין אצל שחיטה מומחין הם — those who regularly engage in shechita may be presumed competent, meaning that a person performing shechita is presumed to know what he is doing. Thus: if we have tested the shochet and confirmed knowledge, the shechita is valid; if we have confirmed that he does not know the laws, the shechita is invalid regardless of his practical skill; and if we simply do not know, we may rely on the presumption of competence.

## Inspect Simanim

### Check Organs

Rav Yehuda also said in the name of Shmuel: הטבח צריך שיבדוק בסימנים לאחר שחיטה — after performing shechita, the butcher must inspect the trachea and esophagus to verify that the cut was clean and properly executed. The primary concern is a nick in the knife, which would cause tearing rather than cutting, rendering the shechita invalid.

### Inspection Time

Rav Yosef noted that this obligation is implicit in a Mishnah: רבי שמעון אומר אם שהה כדי ביקור — Rabbi Shimon says that an invalid pause during shechita is defined as a pause long enough to allow for ביקור, inspection. The very fact that a duration called "inspection time" exists implies that there is a recognized practice of inspecting — and Rav Yosef understood this inspection to refer to checking the simanim, the trachea and esophagus.

### Knife Check

Abaye challenged this reading. He cited Rabbi Yochanan: כדי ביקור חכם — the time in question is the time it takes to have a sage check the knife, not the time to check the simanim. The practice was that when a new rabbi arrived in a community, all the shochtim would bring their knives to him for inspection, as an implicit acknowledgment of his authority. The method of checking a knife for nicks is to run one's fingernail along the blade — any hesitation or catch indicates a nick and renders the knife unusable. Those with greater expertise check the blade with the tip of the tongue.

The Gemara then raises a practical objection to the interpretation of ביקור חכם: אם כן נתת דבריך לשיעורין — if the measure of time is how long it takes to have the sage inspect the knife, this is not a fixed duration. It depends on how far away the sage lives, how long the ceremony takes, and so on. The Gemara therefore refines: the measure is not the time to travel to a sage for a formal ceremony, but rather the time required for the shochet himself — who is a knowledgeable practitioner — to check the knife: כדי ביקור טבח חכם. This is a fixed and practical standard.

### Resolution

Although the Gemara could not conclusively prove from the Mishnah that ביקור refers to inspection of the simanim, the halakhic requirement to inspect the simanim after shechita nonetheless stands as stated by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel.

## Uninspected Status

### Treifa Status

The Gemara now asks: לא בדק מאי — if the shochet did not inspect the simanim, what is the halachic status of the animal? Rabbi Eliezer ben Antigonos citing Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Yannai rules: טרפה ואסורה באכילה — it is treifa and forbidden to eat. This is a striking ruling. One might have assumed that the requirement to inspect is only lechatchila and that bedi'avad, after the fact, the animal is permitted — the shochet is experienced, the knife was checked beforehand. Yet the Gemara does not take this position. Without verifying the simanim, we cannot confirm the shechita was valid, and the animal is therefore forbidden.

### Nevela Status

A braita goes further: נבילה ומטמאה במשא — the animal is not merely treifa but nevela, and conveys tumah through carrying. The distinction between treifa and nevela is significant: treifa refers to an animal that was shechted correctly but had an internal defect — a ruptured organ, for example — rendering it non-kosher; nevela refers to an animal that was not shechted correctly at all, whether because it died on its own, was killed in another way, or the shechita was invalid, such as due to a nick in the knife causing tearing. Both are forbidden to eat, but only nevela conveys tumah. If the shechita was not confirmed to be valid, then if it was indeed invalid, the animal would be a nevela — and the braita holds that without confirmation, we treat it as such.

### Chazaka Debate

The Gemara frames the dispute between the treifa ruling and the nevela ruling as reflecting a disagreement about the principle of Rav Huna: בהמה בחייה בחזקת איסור עומדת — while alive, an animal stands in a presumptive status of issur, forbidden to eat. This status persists עד שיודע לך במה נשחטה — until you know how it was shechted. Once a proper shechita is established, the animal moves to a חזקת היתר, a presumptive status of permissibility, and remains there unless you know it became a treifa.

The question is: what does chazaka track? One position holds that chazaka follows the physical facts. The animal was not shechted — that is the factual status quo. Since we cannot confirm it was shechted correctly, we must treat it as though it was not, and an animal not properly shechted that dies is nevela. The other position holds that chazaka follows the halachic status rather than the physical details. The animal's halachic status while alive was: forbidden to eat, but not tamei. Now it is dead and we are uncertain whether the shechita was valid. We retain its prior halachic status: forbidden to eat, not tamei. What category of non-kosher animal is forbidden to eat but does not convey tumah? A treifa — not a nevela. Therefore, without any indication of an internal defect, the animal is categorized as treifa purely on the basis of continuing its prior halachic status. Tosafos notes that this latter reasoning is somewhat strained, and assumes the treifa position is derabbanan — at a biblical level the animal would be considered permitted — which allows the reasoning to operate with greater flexibility.

## Chazaka Principle

### Initial Status

The Gemara now examines Rav Huna's statement in its own right. Rav Huna declares: בהמה בחייה בחזקת איסור עומדת — a live animal stands in a presumptive status of being forbidden. One cannot consume a live animal, and one cannot rip off a limb from a living creature. This forbidden status persists until one establishes that a proper shechita was performed. Chazaka, then, works לחומרא here: the presumption of being forbidden remains until affirmatively overridden by knowledge of a valid shechita.

### Kosher Status

Once proper shechita has been confirmed, the animal acquires a new chazaka — חזקת היתר — a presumptive status of being permitted. This chazaka now works לקולא: the animal is presumed kosher unless there is positive knowledge that it became a treifa. This is why in practice we do not inspect every internal organ after shechita. Most organs present no commonly occurring defects, and relying on the chazaka of heter is sufficient. The only exception is the lungs, where tears and perforations are a relatively common occurrence — a מיעוט המצוי, miyut hamatzui — which obligates inspection. For all other organs, since the defect would be a מיעוט שאינו מצוי, miyut she'eino matzui — a rare occurrence — there is no obligation to investigate.

### Fox Case

The Gemara presents the following scenario, posed by Rabbi Abba to Rav Huna: you shechted an animal, the organs are laid out, and a wolf came and snatched some of them. May we assume the organs were unpunctured? The Gemara immediately clarifies: if the organs were simply carried away, הא ליתנהו — they are not before us, and we need not concern ourselves with them. The question refines itself: suppose the animal grabbed a piece, punctured it, and returned it — or carried it off and returned with a hole evident in it. The concern is: was there a hole in the organ before the animal bit it, perhaps now concealed by the bite mark? The Gemara answers on the basis of chazaka: once proper shechita has been performed, the animal stands in a chazaka of heter. This chazaka persists unless one can positively establish a treifa condition. A doubt raised by a fox's bite — without being able to confirm whether a hole predated it — does not override the chazaka, and the animal remains permitted.

## Hazard Exception

### Snake Concern

The Gemara poses a challenge from a case of real-world danger. If one saw a bird pecking at a fig or a weasel gnawing at a melon, the ruling is חוששין שמא במקום נקב נקב — we are concerned that the bite may be covering a hole left by a snake. The Gemara was consistently concerned that snakes might eat from exposed food and leave behind venom. The fact that you observe the weasel making a hole does not eliminate the concern, because the weasel may have bitten precisely where a snake had bitten previously. The question is: why don't we apply chazaka here? The fig had a chazaka of being intact and unpoisoned; why does a doubt override that chazaka?

### Issur vs. Sakana

The Gemara answers: מי קמידמי איסורא לסכנתא — how can you compare a question of issur, of halachic status, to a question of sakana, of real-world danger? סכנתא שאני — danger is categorically different. The principle חמירא סכנתא מאיסורא captures part of this, but the deeper point is that formal halachic mechanisms like chazaka are appropriate for resolving questions about halachic status. Chazaka is a legal tool — it tells us how to treat something in the halachic framework. It does not actually tell us anything about physical reality. When the question is one of health — is there poison in this food — what matters is the actual state of affairs, not a formal legal presumption. Chazaka is therefore simply irrelevant to health concerns. Real-world danger operates by percentages and probabilities, not by legal status quo.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenged this answer: מאי שנא ספק סכנה לחומרא — why is a doubt regarding danger treated strictly, ספק איסורא נמי לחומרא — if so, should a doubt regarding prohibition not also be treated strictly? Rava does not see a principled distinction; both involve doubt, and strictness seems to apply in both domains.

### Abaye's Answer

Abaye responded: ולא שני בין איסור לסכנתא — are you saying there is no difference between issur and sakana? He demonstrates the difference with a compelling example. The rule is ספק טומאה ברשות הרבים ספיקו טהור — a doubt about tumah in a public domain is resolved leniently, the status is tahor. Yet ספק מים מגולים אסורין — a doubt about uncovered water is treated strictly. Here we have two parallel cases of doubt, and the outcomes are opposite. For halachic tumah, we have a formal rule directing leniency; for health concerns about exposed water, we are strict regardless. This proves that the two domains operate under fundamentally different principles.

## Sota Rules

### Tumah Criteria

Abaye's proof is challenged: perhaps the leniency regarding tumah in the public domain is not based on the general principle of chazaka at all, but on a specific law derived from the case of Sotah. The Torah employs the phrase ונסתרה ונטמאה — she was secluded and she became defiled — in the context of the suspected sotah. From this phrase, the Sages derived that a doubt about tumah is treated as definite tumah only when two criteria are met: it occurred in a private domain, and there was present a party who in theory could know what happened. When either criterion is absent — when the doubt arises in a public domain, or when no party capable of reporting was present — the doubt does not generate a presumption of tumah. This is not chazaka; it is a formal derivation from the laws of sotah.

### Vessel Rules

The Gemara further illustrates this framework with the case of a vessel used to store water for the Parah Adumah purification ritual. If one left a pitcher uncovered and returned to find it covered, one must be concerned that a tamei person entered and covered it — and the vessel may therefore be tamei. Conversely, if one left it covered and returned to find it uncovered, the concern is not about human agency but about animals: could a weasel or snake have drunk from it, rendering the water invalid for use in the Parah Adumah ritual? As Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi explains: מפני שדרכן של שרצים לגלות ואין דרכן לכסות — because the nature of small animals and insects is to uncover vessels, not to cover them. When the vessel was found covered, a human being must have been involved, raising the concern of tumah. When found uncovered, only animal access is suspected, and that raises no tumah concern — only the separate question of whether the water has been invalidated for ritual use.

### Stricter Scrutiny

Rav Ashi concludes the discussion: שמע מינה חמירא סכנתא מאיסורא שמע מינה — conclude from this that danger is treated more strictly than issur. Throughout the extended discussion, the Gemara has demonstrated that doubts resolved by chazaka and formal halachic rules in the domain of issur and tumah are not resolved the same way in the domain of health and danger. When the question is one of halachic status, legal mechanisms — chazaka, formal rules derived from Sotah, the principle of rov — are fully adequate tools. When the question is one of real-world danger, those tools are beside the point. What matters is the actual state of affairs on the ground: is there poison here, did a snake drink from this water. A legal status quo cannot answer that question. Health concerns demand scrutiny of actual probabilities; they cannot be managed by formal legal presumptions.
