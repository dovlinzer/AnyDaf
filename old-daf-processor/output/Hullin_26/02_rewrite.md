# Chullin 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Survey of Six Orders

### Structure Overview

We pick up with the Gemara right after the Mishnah at the bottom of 25b. Before proceeding, it is worth noting something about the structure of the remaining Mishnayot in this perek. Throughout, we have been dealing with propositions of the form "what is true for A is not true for B, and what is true for B is not true for A." The Mishnah we just read — concerning water that has been soaked in grape leaves and is in the process of fermenting — does not quite fit that classical structure. It is not two different objects or two different spheres being compared; it is one thing undergoing a process, with the Mishnah saying that at one stage it is still water and at a later stage it is wine. Rashi points this out. So while there is a structural similarity — here these characteristics apply and not others — it is slightly different from what we have been seeing, and we will encounter that pattern in several of the later Mishnayot as well.

### All Six Orders

There is a second observation worth making about the structure of the perek as a whole. The Mishnayot in this section, taken together, appear to conduct a deliberate survey of all six Sedarim of Shas, and what is striking is that they are not mixed together — each Seder is treated in its own separate unit.

So far, we have covered Kodashim — dealing with which type of bird one brings as a sacrifice, Parah Adumah, Eglah Arufah, and related matters — followed by Taharot, concerning wooden, metal, and pottery vessels. Then came Zeraim, with questions about nuts and the stages at which they are considered bitter, and our present Mishnah about when something is considered wine, which touches on the obligation of Terumos UMa'asros and the eligibility for purchase with Kesef Ma'aser — and though it also touches on Mikvah law, it remains fundamentally a Zeraim question. That accounts for three Sedarim.

Looking ahead, the end of the perek's Mishnayot cover the remaining three. The question of brothers who inherit an estate and when it is treated as one or two estates may at first seem like a stretch, but Achim HaShutafim is a very common question in civil law — so that represents, at some level, Nezikin. The next Mishnah deals straightforwardly with Nashim, concerning at what stage certain legal categories apply to a girl. And the final Mishnah comes from Mo'ed. It appears highly intentional that all of Shisha Sidrei Shas — Kodashim, Taharot, Zeraim, Nezikin, Nashim, Mo'ed — are each dealt with separately and in sequence. It is not by chance.

### Metamed Water-Wine

With that introduction, we turn to the Gemara, which addresses the case of water that has absorbed some grape juice and the question of when it is considered water and when it is considered wine.

## Fermentation Debate

### Maasrot Mishna

The Gemara opens by asking: Mani Matnitin? Who authored our Mishnah? Lo Rebbe Yehuda Velo Rabbanan — it is neither Rabbi Yehuda nor the Rabbanan as we know them from a Mishnah in Maasrot. The Mishnah in Maasrot discusses the following case: HaMetamed VeNatan Mayim BeMidah — someone performing the process of metamed, soaking water in grape skins or leaves to produce a weak wine, puts in a measured quantity of water and UMatza Kedei Midato — finds upon removing the liquid that the quantity is exactly equal to what was put in. The significance is that whatever grape juice entered the water was not detectable through any increase in measured volume, suggesting the grape content is negligible. So is the result considered water or wine?

The Mishnah rules Patur — it is exempt from Terumos UMa'asros, meaning it is treated as water. Rabbi Yehuda Mechayev — Rabbi Yehuda obligates, holding that it is considered wine and therefore Chayav in Terumos UMa'asros regardless of the quantity measurement.

Now, note that neither opinion in the Maasrot Mishnah discusses fermentation at all — they debate whether there has been a measurable increase in quantity. Our Mishnah, however, makes fermentation the determining criterion. So the Gemara asks: how can our Mishnah be reconciled with the positions of Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbanan? Ei Rabbanan — if our Mishnah reflects the Rabbanan, the difficulty is that the Rabbanan seem to require a measurable increase in volume, not merely fermentation; af al gav de-hichmit, even if it fermented, without an increase in quantity they call it water. Ei Rebbi Yehuda — if our Mishnah reflects Rabbi Yehuda, the difficulty runs the other way: af al gav de-lo hichmit, Rabbi Yehuda seems to require no fermentation at all, since he calls it wine even without any measurable increase. So our Mishnah, which pivots entirely on the question of fermentation, appears to fit neither position.

### Rav Nachman: Hichmit

Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha — the first resolution, appearing at the top of 26a, is as follows: Beshe-hichmit machloket — the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbanan applies specifically when the metamed has already fermented. This explains the logic of each position: the Rabbanan can acknowledge that fermentation has occurred, yet maintain that without a measurable increase in volume there is insufficient evidence of genuine grape content to call it wine. Rabbi Yehuda, for his part, does not require that measurable increase — fermentation itself is sufficient for him to call it wine. The Mishnah in Maasrot is therefore about a case that has fermented, and UMatnisin Rebbi Yehuda — our Mishnah, which does not mention any criterion of quantity and focuses solely on fermentation, follows Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yehuda does not add the criterion of increased quantity; he requires only fermentation, and that is precisely what our Mishnah says. Vecho'en amar Rebbi Yose b'Rebbi Chanina — Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina made the same statement: Beshe-hichmit machloket, the dispute applies when it has fermented; otherwise, how could one even understand Rabbi Yehuda's position in calling it wine?

### Potential Fermentation

V'amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Avuha — a further ruling from the same authority: if someone purchased temed with Kesef Ma'aser — as our Mishnah states is permitted once it has fermented — but purchased it before it fermented, and Uv'esof hichmit, in the end it did ferment, then Kana Ma'aser — the Kesef Ma'aser acquired it retroactively, meaning the sanctity of the Ma'aser money transferred validly to this liquid. The implicit reframing here is significant: although our Mishnah speaks of actual fermentation as the criterion, what this ruling suggests is that the real key is the capacity to ferment. If it will ferment in the future, that determination can be made retroactively. As the Gemara explains: Iglay milta lemafrea depeyra hu — the matter has been revealed retroactively that it was fruit, that it was already wine. Since it ultimately fermented, we see that at the time of purchase it was already on the path to fermentation and therefore already carried the status of wine.

The Gemara immediately challenges this. Our Mishnah states: dektani hichmitz in, lo hichmitz lo — if it fermented, yes; if it did not ferment, no. The criterion appears to be actual present fermentation. But according to Rabbah bar Avuha, the Gemara objects, Dilma ishavte havei machmitz — perhaps if you had simply waited, it would have fermented eventually. How then can the Mishnah make any ruling based on whether it has fermented right now? The same problem applies to both legal consequences in our Mishnah: whether the metamed invalidates a Mikvah and whether it can be purchased with Kesef Ma'aser. According to Rabbah bar Avuha, the criterion should be will it ferment, not has it already fermented — so how does our Mishnah permit a determination based solely on its present state?

### Sample Test

Amar Rabba — Rabba resolves this by reinterpreting what our Mishnah is describing. Kesheshayer mimenu bekos velo hichmitz — the Mishnah is addressing a case where one set aside a small sample in a cup in order to monitor whether it will ferment. The determination is not made by looking at the liquid at a single moment and declaring it wine or water on the basis of current fermentation. Rather, one holds over a sample and checks that sample after some time to see whether it ferments. If the sample ferments, the ruling is that the original liquid was wine at the critical moment — when it fell into the Mikvah or was purchased with Kesef Ma'aser. If the sample does not ferment, the ruling is that it was water. The language of the Mishnah — "has it fermented?" — is therefore not asking about the liquid's present state at the moment of the event, but about the result of a prospective test conducted on a retained sample.

The underlying logic, as one participant helpfully articulated, is perhaps best framed not merely as "will it ferment" in some abstract future sense, but "is it currently in the process of fermenting?" — which is a state already underway and measurable, even if not yet apparent to the eye. The result of the sample test reveals what was already happening at the critical moment. This is admittedly not the plain pshat of the Mishnah; the plain reading speaks of has it fermented. But Rabbah bar Avuha reinterprets the criterion to be will it ferment, or more precisely, is it in the fermentation process, and Rabba reads that back into the Mishnah by positing that the Mishnah refers to a test conducted on a held sample, whose result retroactively determines the status at the critical time.

## Appearance & Taste

### Water-Milk Mixture

The Gemara now introduces an entirely different approach to our Mishnah. Rav amar hamani Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri hi — Rav says our Mishnah follows Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri. The basis for this is a Mishnah in Mikva'ot: Gimmel lugin mayim chaser kortov shenafal letochan kortov yayin — three log of water missing a kortov, into which a kortov of wine fell, and umarean kemarei yayin — it looks like wine. The Mishnah rules lo pasul — it does not invalidate a Mikvah. This is the straightforward case: there are two independent reasons not to be concerned. First, it does not actually contain three log of water — only 2.99 log — and the full shiur of drawn water is required to invalidate; anything less is insufficient. Second, it no longer looks like water but like wine.

The Mishnah then presents the more difficult case: Gimmel lugin mayim chaser kortov — the same 2.99 log of water — shenafal letochan kortov chalav — into which a kortov of milk fell. Umarean kemarei mayim — and it looks like water. Now what? In terms of actual composition, it does not contain three log of water; it contains only 2.99 log of water. But it looks like three log of water. The question is whether we determine status by what it actually is or by what it appears to be.

The first opinion rules Shenafal lemikvah lo pasul — it did not invalidate the Mikvah — because actual content determines identity, not appearance. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri omer: Hakol holech achar hamareh — everything follows appearance. If it looks like water, it has the status of water regardless of its actual composition.

### Rav's Position

The principle of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri is that identity is determined not solely by what a substance factually is, but by the characteristics it exhibits — preeminently, its appearance. The Gemara now applies this principle to our Mishnah: Lav amar Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri batar chazuta azlinan? Does not Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri say that we define things based on how they appear?

### Appearance & Taste

Hacha nami zil batar chazuta v'taima — here too, go by appearance and taste. The Gemara here introduces taste alongside appearance, which is worth pausing over. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's Mishnah spoke only of appearance. Why does the Gemara feel comfortable adding taste?

The answer lies in an important structural point. For Kashrut questions, the primary criterion of identity is taste — ta'am. If a drop of chazir falls into a soup, the question is whether its taste is detectable; that is the operative standard for food matters. For non-food identity questions, such as whether something invalidates a Mikvah, appearance is more primary because that is how we ordinarily perceive and categorize things. The metamed case, however, straddles both spheres: whether it can be purchased with Kesef Ma'aser is a food-related determination, while whether it invalidates a Mikvah is a general identity question. One might have said the criteria differ depending on which sphere one is addressing, but the Gemara proceeds on the assumption that the same standard applies across both. Therefore, since Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri bases identity on exhibited characteristics rather than on what something objectively is, the Gemara extends his principle to include taste alongside appearance — both are relevant exhibited characteristics, and the Gemara feels justified in importing taste into his framework.

### Applied to Metamed

V'chazuta d'hai maya ninhu — the appearance of this temed that is going to ferment but has not yet fermented is that of water. The same is true of its taste. On any experiential basis — how we see it and how we taste it — it presents itself as water. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri does not care what something is in some objective, compositional sense; he cares about how it appears, how it tastes, and what characteristics it actually exhibits. Since this metamed does not yet look like wine and does not yet taste like wine, he is not prepared to call it wine. Our Mishnah, which demands that it actually have fermented before receiving the status of wine, is therefore Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri's position.

This stands in contrast to the position attributed to Rabbah bar Avuha, who says the criterion is what the substance objectively is undergoing — and something in the process of fermentation is, in that objective sense, wine, regardless of how it presently appears or tastes. The debate can thus be conceptualized as a fundamental question: is identity determined by what a substance objectively is or by how we experience it? Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri insists that our experienced reality — primarily through appearance but also through taste — defines identity, not some standard that is inaccessible to ordinary perception. The Rabbanan in the water-milk case are, by contrast, measuring actual ingredients, and Rabbah bar Avuha in the fermentation case is measuring what process the substance is undergoing irrespective of perception. What unites Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri across both cases is the consistent appeal to experienced characteristics as the determinant of legal identity.

## Rabbi Elazar

### Before Fermentation

Ufleiga d'Rabbi Elazar — this entire framework, which assumed that the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbanan in Maasrot concerns a case that has already fermented, is disputed by Rabbi Elazar. D'amar Rabbi Elazar: hakol modim she-ein mafrishin alav mi-makom acher ela im ken hechmitz — everyone agrees, both Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbanan, that one cannot take Terumot uMa'asrot from another place to exempt this metamed unless it has actually fermented. The significance of this statement is that Rabbi Elazar assumes the debate in Maasrot takes place when the metamed has not yet fermented — the opposite of what Rav Nachman and Rabbi Yose ben Rabbi Chanina assumed above. On this reading, the question Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbanan are disputing is whether the temed has wine status even before fermentation has occurred.

### Rabbinic Status

This makes Rabbi Yehuda's position considerably more difficult to understand. If it has not yet fermented and the volume has not measurably increased, on what basis does he call it wine? The Gemara explains: Kasavar belo hichmit machloket — Rabbi Elazar holds that the debate applies precisely when it has not yet fermented.

Accordingly, Rabbi Elazar qualifies Rabbi Yehuda's position: Ve'ad kan lo mechayev Rebbe Yehuda ella minei u'vei — Rabbi Yehuda's obligation is only to take Terumot uMa'asrot from the metamed itself, on itself. His position is not that the temed is fully and genuinely wine; it is at most a stringency — perhaps a rabbinic one — to treat it as requiring tithing. Aval me'almah lo — but one cannot take from real wine to exempt this, or from this to exempt real wine. The reason is straightforward: Dilma ati la'aprushei min hachiyuv al haptur min haptur al hachiyuv — genuine wine is fully obligated in Terumot uMa'asrot, whereas this temed that has not fermented is not fully obligated, only rabbinically so at most. Taking from one to exempt the other would therefore involve taking from a patur on a chayav or vice versa, creating miscalculations in every direction. So Rabbi Elazar's contribution is to say: the debate in Maasrot is at the pre-fermentation stage, and even Rabbi Yehuda who calls it wine does not treat it as genuinely and fully wine — only enough to require tithing from itself, rabbinically.

## Mikvah Application

### Metamed Mikvah

Tanu Rabbanan — a Baraita: Hatemed ad shelo hichmis — temed that has not yet fermented — Mashiko bemayim — can be brought to kiss the waters of a Mikvah. This is the process by which tamei water can be purified: since a Mikvah operates on water, tamei water placed in contact with a kosher Mikvah becomes absorbed into the Mikvah's water and thereby becomes tahor — not because the Mikvah purified it in the way it purifies a person or vessel, but because it becomes part of the Mikvah water itself. This process, however, works only for water. For other liquids there is no such mechanism. Therefore, if one's temed became tamei and one wishes to make it tahor, the determination of whether it is still water is essential. As long as it has not yet fermented, it retains the status of water and can be brought into contact with the Mikvah. Mishehichmitz — once it has fermented — ein mashkiko bemayim — it can no longer be brought to kiss the Mikvah water, because it is now wine.

### Pure Water Only

Amar Rava lo shanu ela shetimdo bemayim tehorim venitme'u — Rava qualifies: this ruling — that pre-fermentation temed can be brought to the Mikvah — applies only when the water was originally pure and then became tamei after the grape material was added. Aval temei mei ikara lo — but if the water was tamei from the outset, it cannot be brought to the Mikvah even before fermentation. Tosafot explains the distinction: because it was never in a state of being simply pure water before the process began, it no longer has the straightforward status of pure water that has become tamei; its identity has been complicated from the start.

### Rav Ashi's Q

Ata Rav Gaviha mi-bei Katil amra lishmayta kamei de-Rav Ashi — this ruling was reported before Rav Ashi, who raised a challenge. Mai shna mi-temei mei ikara? What is the difference between water that became tamei originally and water that was pure and only became tamei after the grape material was added? In both cases, before fermentation, it should still have the status of water — so why should the original purity of the water matter?

### Density Separation

De-amrinan idi de-maya yakiri shachni tata'ei u-peira kafi mei-eila — the answer lies in the physics of the mixture: water is denser than the grape juice product and therefore settles at the bottom, while the juice product floats on top. When one brings the vessel of liquid into contact with the Mikvah water, only the surface of the liquid in the vessel actually touches the Mikvah water. If the juice component has separated and risen to the top, then it is only the juice layer that makes contact with the Mikvah — not the water beneath it. Ve-lo salka lah hashkaka le-maya — and the process of hashkakah, of the water kissing the Mikvah water, cannot take effect for the water below, because the water itself never actually touched the Mikvah.

Rav Ashi then asks: Ei hachi tehorim desof mitmu nami — if that is the reason, then even in the case of originally pure water that subsequently became tamei, the same separation problem should apply. The answer is: Mevalbeli — one stirs and mixes the liquid before placing it in the Mikvah, so the water and juice are fully blended and the separation has not yet occurred. Hacha nami mevalbeli — and in the case of originally tamei water, one should equally be able to stir it first and thereby solve the problem. Rava's distinction between originally pure and originally tamei water is thus rejected, and the resolution is simply that in any case one must stir the mixture before bringing it to the Mikvah.

It is worth noting that the Gemara does not pursue the fascinating later portion of the Mishnah dealing with the concept of brothers who inherit an estate and when that estate is treated as a single entity — a striking application of something like a theory of corporate identity in Halacha. The Gemara passes over it, and we move on.

## Nashim: Sale & Fine

### Mutual Exclusion

We have now covered Kodashim, Taharot, Zeraim, and something of Nezikin in the brothers-and-estate question. We turn now to Nashim — the order of women's law — and then to Mo'ed, which will bring the perek to its conclusion as a survey of all of Shas.

Kol makom she-yesh mecher ein knas ve-chol makom she-yesh knas ein mecher — where there is mecher, the right of a father to sell his underage daughter into a form of servitude, there is no knas; and where there is knas, the fine imposed upon a man who seduces or rapes a na'arah, there is no mecher. These two legal categories are mutually exclusive.

The term na'arah requires some unpacking. As the Rabbis understand it, na'arah refers to a young woman who has halachically become an adult — she is twelve years old with two pubic hairs — but who has not yet become a bogeret, a full adult, which occurs six months later. The Torah's obligation of knas for one who rapes or seduces applies to a na'arah specifically. The father's right of mecher, by contrast, applies while she is a ketanah — underage, less than twelve years old.

It is natural to ask: if there is no knas for a ketanah, does she have no legal protection? The Gemara's answer is that knas is not the only consequence. The Rabbis derive that pitui ktanah ones — seduction of an underage girl is treated as rape in any case — and rape carries obligations of personal-injury payments: tza'ar for pain and suffering, boshet for humiliation, and pegam for physical injury. These obligations exist even absent a formal knas. Additionally, the Rambam is explicit: the fifty kesef of knas is for the illicit pleasure of the act itself, and the seducer owes boshet and pegam beyond the knas; the rapist additionally owes tza'ar. As for who receives these payments, the Rambam is clear that the three payments of the seducer and the four of the rapist belong to the father — kol shevach neurim la-av — because all the enhancement of her youth belongs to the father. If there is no father, they go to the girl herself. And if the matter goes to court after she has already become an adult, she receives the payments at that point.

### Rabbi Meir

Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav zu divrei Rabbi Meir — this principle of mutual exclusion between mecher and knas is the position of Rabbi Meir alone. As the Baraita elaborates: Ketanah mi-bat yom echad ad she-tavi shtei se'arot — from one day old until she has two pubic hairs — yesh lah mecher ve-ein lah knas. During this period of being a ketanah, there is mecher but no knas. Mi-she-tavi shtei se'arot ad she-tivager — from the time she has two pubic hairs until she becomes a bogeret, that is, from twelve years and a day to twelve years and six months — yesh lah knas ve-ein lah mecher. She is now a na'arah and no longer a minor, so there is knas but no mecher. Divrei Rabbi Meir — she-hayah Rabbi Meir omer: kol makom she-yesh mecher ein knas ve-chol makom she-yesh knas ein mecher. According to Rabbi Meir, these are strictly non-overlapping windows.

### Sages' Overlap

Va-chachamim omrim yesh knas bimkom mecher — the Sages disagree and hold that there is an overlap: knas applies even while mecher applies. Ketanah mi-bat shalosh shanim ve-yom echad ad she-tivager yesh lah knas — from three years and a day all the way through adulthood, the girl is subject to knas. The lower bound is not birth but age three, because only from that age is the act halachically classified as an act of sexual intercourse. Before age three, even if the same physical events occur, the Rabbis do not consider it a halachic act of sex, and therefore no knas is triggered. So for the Sages, from one day to three years there is only mecher and no knas; from three years to twelve years and a day there is both mecher and knas simultaneously; from twelve years and a day to twelve years and six months there is knas but no mecher; and from twelve years and six months onward, there is neither.

## Miun & Chalitzah

### Mutual Exclusion

Kol makom she-yesh miun ein chalitzah ve-chol makom she-yesh chalitzah ein miun — where there is the right of miun, there is no chalitzah; where there is chalitzah, there is no miun. Chalitzah is the ceremony by which a woman releases her deceased husband's brother from the obligation of yibum, and it requires that both parties be halachic adults; a minor cannot perform chalitzah. Miun is the institution by which a girl who has been married off by her mother and brothers — not her father — can reject that marriage at any time before she becomes an adult. This institution is entirely rabbinic, a takanah enacted because of the social reality of needing to provide for daughters when no father is present. When she reaches adulthood she is no longer a minor, so miun is unavailable — but chalitzah becomes available. The two categories, according to one position, occupy mutually exclusive windows.

### Rabbi Meir

Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav zo divrei Rabbi Meir — this strict mutual exclusion is again Rabbi Meir's position. A Baraita spells it out: ad matai habat mima'enet — until when can a girl reject the marriage? Ad shetavi shtei se'arot divrei Rabbi Meir — until she has two pubic hairs, meaning until she becomes an adult. Once she reaches twelve years and a day, miun is no longer available for Rabbi Meir, and chalitzah begins.

### Sages' Overlap

Aval Chachamim omrim yesh miun bemakom chalitzah — but the Sages say there is a period of overlap, when a girl can perform both miun and chalitzah. That period is when she is a na'arah — twelve years and a day to twelve years and six months. She is halachically an adult and can therefore perform chalitzah, but she can also still exercise miun to reject this rabbinic marriage, as long as she has not confirmed the marriage through sexual relations as an adult.

This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda in the Baraita: ad sheyirbeh hashachor al halavan — until the more developed adult state — understood by the Gemara not as an abundance of pubic hair but as the growth of particularly long pubic hairs, marking her transition to bogeret. According to Rabbi Yehuda, as long as she has not yet become a full bogeret and has not had conjugal relations as an adult in this marriage, she retains the right of miun even though she is already a na'arah and could perform chalitzah. Once she becomes a bogeret or once she has had sexual relations as an adult within the marriage — which constitutes kiddushei biah and fully confirms the marriage — miun is no longer possible.

## Tekiah & Havdalah

### Mutual Exclusion

Kol makom sheyesh tkiyah ein havdalah ve-chol makom sheyesh havdalah ein tkiyah — where there is tekiah there is no havdalah, and where there is havdalah there is no tekiah. The background: on erev Shabbat the shofar was blown to signal to people to cease melachah. On motzei Shabbat, havdalah is recited to formally permit melachah once again. Tekiah marks the transition into a more restrictive state; havdalah marks the transition into a more permissive one. They are therefore categorically opposed.

### Yom Tov Erev

Yom Tov shechal lihyot be'erev Shabbat — when Yom Tov falls on erev Shabbat: Tok'in — one blows the shofar, as one does at the entry of any sanctified day, marking the transition to increased restriction. Lo mavdilin — one does not make havdalah, because one is not moving from a more restricted state to a less restricted one; Yom Tov entering Shabbat moves from one level of restriction to a heavier one.

### Yom Tov Motza'ei

When Yom Tov falls on motzei Shabbat, it is the reverse: Mavdilin v'lo tok'in. Havdalah is recited because one is transitioning out of Shabbat — moving from a more comprehensive prohibition of melachah to a less comprehensive one — and havdalah marks that opening of permission. But tekiah is not performed, because tekiah accompanies the forbidding of melachah, and while Yom Tov is not entirely permissive, one is not moving into a more restricted state.

### Tekiah Method

Heikhi toke'a — how exactly is the tekiah performed when Yom Tov transitions into Shabbat, given that it must be somehow distinguishable from the standard erev Shabbat tekiah, which consists of a tekiah and a teru'ah?

Amar Rav Yehuda: toke'a u'mevi'ah mitoch hateki'ah — one blows the tekiah and it transforms from within itself into a teru'ah. There are not two discrete blows; rather the tekiah flows into the teru'ah without separation.

Rav Assi omer: toke'a u'mevi'ah b'neshimah achat — one produces both sounds in a single breath, so they are distinct sounds but without the pause of taking a new breath between them. Itkin Rav Assi b'Hutzal k'shmaiteh — and Rav Assi enacted in Hutzal to practice as he had ruled.

A challenge is raised from a Baraita: Yom tov shechal lihyot b'erev Shabbat tok'in v'lo mari'in — you blow tekiah but not teru'ah. Does this not mean no teru'ah at all, contradicting both Rav Yehuda and Rav Assi? The Gemara resolves that each Amora defends his position on his own terms: Rav Yehuda metareitz l'taameh — lo mari'in bifnei atzmah, ella mitoch hateki'ah: you do not blow a teru'ah as a separate, independent blow; the teru'ah emerges from within the tekiah. Rav Assi metareitz l'taameh — lo mari'in b'shtei neshimot, ella b'neshimah achat: you do not produce the teru'ah with a separate breath; the two sounds are produced together in one breath.

### Havdalah Text

Uv'motzei Shabbat — when Shabbat ends and Yom Tov begins, and one must recite havdalah, a question arises about the text. The standard havdalah formula contains the phrase hamavdil bein kodesh l'chol — "who separates between holy and mundane." But if Shabbat is being followed by Yom Tov and not an ordinary weekday, the transition is not to chol. One cannot say that God separates between kodesh and chol when the day being entered is itself kodesh. What does one say?

The ruling is hamavdil bein kodesh l'kodesh. Amar Rav Yehuda b'chatimatah — Rav Yehuda rules that this modified phrase is said only in the chatimah, the closing blessing formula, not at the opening. V'chein amar Rav Nachman b'chatimatah — Rav Nachman agrees: only at the close. Rav Sheshet breih d'Rav Idi says af bifitichatah — even at the opening one should say bein kodesh l'kodesh. V'leit hilkhata k'vathieh — and we do not rule like him. The standard text bein kodesh l'chol is retained at the opening, as Rashi explains, because that text reflects biblical language: the Torah itself uses the formulation of separation between the holy and the mundane.

As for Rav Dosa, who would have one say bein kodesh chamur l'kodesh kal — between the heavy holiness of Shabbat and the light holiness of Yom Tov — v'leit hilkhata k'vathieh as well. Rashi explains it is disrespectful to characterize Yom Tov as a kal, a lighter kedushah, and we therefore do not say it.

This brings us to the final ruling of the perek. Amar Rav Zeira: Yom tov shechal lihyot b'emtza Shabbat — when Yom Tov falls in the middle of Shabbat, meaning one is making havdalah on Yom Tov itself as Shabbat ends — what does one say? It might strike one as peculiar to recite bein yom hashevi'i l'sheshet yemei hama'aseh — "between the seventh day and the six days of work" — when there is no weekday in sight. Nevertheless, Rav Zeira rules that one says the standard formula: hamavdil bein kodesh l'chol, bein or l'choshech, bein Yisrael l'amim, u'vein yom hashevi'i l'sheshet yemei hama'aseh.

Mai taama — why? Seder havdalot hu moneh — because one is simply reciting the ordered list of the traditional havdalot, the separations that are mentioned in the Torah itself. Rashi specifies: vayavdeil bein ha'or u'vein hachoshech is the source for bein or l'choshech; v'avdil etchem min ha'amim is the source for bein Yisrael l'amim; and the sanctification of Shabbat in the account of Creation is the source for bein yom hashevi'i l'sheshet yemei hama'aseh. All the clauses of our havdalah are drawn from the Torah. Therefore, even in a context where the transition is not from Shabbat to an ordinary weekday, we retain the biblical nusach in its entirety. And with that, the perek concludes.
